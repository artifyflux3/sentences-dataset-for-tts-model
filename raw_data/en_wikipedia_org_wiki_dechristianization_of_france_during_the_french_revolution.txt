Anti-Christian policy during the French Revolution

"Dechristianization" redirects here. For other uses, see [Dechristianization (disambiguation)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dechristianization_(disambiguation) "Dechristianization (disambiguation)").

[![Image 10](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Jacques_Fran%C3%A7ois_Joseph_Swebach-Desfontaines_-_Pillage_d%27une_%C3%A9glise_pendant_la_r%C3%A9volution_-_P317_-_Mus%C3%A9e_Carnavalet.jpg/330px-Jacques_Fran%C3%A7ois_Joseph_Swebach-Desfontaines_-_Pillage_d%27une_%C3%A9glise_pendant_la_r%C3%A9volution_-_P317_-_Mus%C3%A9e_Carnavalet.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Jacques_Fran%C3%A7ois_Joseph_Swebach-Desfontaines_-_Pillage_d%27une_%C3%A9glise_pendant_la_r%C3%A9volution_-_P317_-_Mus%C3%A9e_Carnavalet.jpg)

_Looting of a church during the Revolution_, by [Swebach-Desfontaines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacques_Fran%C3%A7ois_Joseph_Swebach-Desfontaines "Jacques François Joseph Swebach-Desfontaines") (c. 1793)

The aim of several policies conducted by various governments of France during the [French Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Revolution "French Revolution") ranged from the appropriation by the government of the great landed estates and the large amounts of money held by the [Catholic Church](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catholic_Church_in_France "Catholic Church in France") to the termination of Christian religious practice and of the religion itself.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dechristianization_of_France_during_the_French_Revolution#cite_note-FOOTNOTETallett19911-17-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dechristianization_of_France_during_the_French_Revolution#cite_note-FOOTNOTESpielvogel2006549-2) There has been much scholarly debate over whether the movement was popularly motivated or motivated by a small group of revolutionary radicals.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dechristianization_of_France_during_the_French_Revolution#cite_note-FOOTNOTETallett19911-17-1) These policies, which ended with the [Concordat of 1801](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concordat_of_1801 "Concordat of 1801"), formed the basis of the later and less radical _[laïcité](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/La%C3%AFcit%C3%A9 "Laïcité")_ policies.

The French Revolution initially began with attacks on Church corruption and the wealth of the higher clergy, an action with which even many [Christians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christians "Christians") could identify, since the Gallican Church held a dominant role in [pre-revolutionary France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pre-revolutionary_France "Pre-revolutionary France"). During a one-year period known as the [Reign of Terror](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reign_of_Terror "Reign of Terror"), the episodes of [anti-clericalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anti-clericalism "Anti-clericalism") became some the most violent of any in modern [European history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_history "European history"). The revolutionary authorities suppressed the Church, abolished the Catholic monarchy, nationalized Church property, exiled 30,000 priests, and killed hundreds more.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dechristianization_of_France_during_the_French_Revolution#cite_note-StoryChr-3) In October 1793, the Christian calendar was replaced with one reckoned from the date of the Revolution, and Festivals of Liberty, Reason, and the Supreme Being were scheduled. New forms of moral religion emerged, including the [deistic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deistic "Deistic")[Cult of the Supreme Being](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cult_of_the_Supreme_Being "Cult of the Supreme Being") and the atheistic [Cult of Reason](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cult_of_Reason "Cult of Reason"),[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dechristianization_of_France_during_the_French_Revolution#cite_note-4) with the revolutionary government briefly mandating observance of the former in April 1794.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dechristianization_of_France_during_the_French_Revolution#cite_note-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dechristianization_of_France_during_the_French_Revolution#cite_note-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dechristianization_of_France_during_the_French_Revolution#cite_note-7)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dechristianization_of_France_during_the_French_Revolution#cite_note-8)[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dechristianization_of_France_during_the_French_Revolution#cite_note-FOOTNOTETallett19911-17-1)

Background
----------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Dechristianization_of_France_during_the_French_Revolution&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Background")]

### Before 1789

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Dechristianization_of_France_during_the_French_Revolution&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Before 1789")]

In [18th-century France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Early_modern_France#France_in_the_17th_and_18th_centuries "Early modern France"), the vast majority of the population adhered to the [Catholic Church](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catholic_Church "Catholic Church"), the only religion officially allowed in the kingdom since the [revocation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edict_of_Fontainebleau "Edict of Fontainebleau") of the [Edict of Nantes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edict_of_Nantes "Edict of Nantes") in 1685. Small minorities of French Protestants (mostly [Huguenots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Huguenots "Huguenots") and German [Lutherans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lutheranism "Lutheranism") in [Alsace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alsace "Alsace")) and Jews still lived in France. The [Edict of Versailles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edict_of_Versailles "Edict of Versailles"),[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dechristianization_of_France_during_the_French_Revolution#cite_note-9) commonly known as the Edict of Tolerance, had been signed by [Louis XVI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis_XVI "Louis XVI") on 7 November 1787. It did not give non-Catholics in France the right to openly practice their religions but only the rights to legal and civil status, which included the right to contract marriages without having to convert to the Catholic faith. At the same time, [libertine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Libertine "Libertine") thinkers had popularized atheism and [anti-clericalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anti-clericalism "Anti-clericalism").

The _[ancien régime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancien\_r%C3%A9gime "Ancien régime")_ institutionalised the authority of the clergy in its status as the [First Estate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Estates_of_the_realm "Estates of the realm") of the realm. As the largest landowner in the country, the Catholic Church controlled vast properties and extracted massive revenues from its tenants;[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dechristianization_of_France_during_the_French_Revolution#cite_note-:0-10) the Church also had an enormous income from the collection of compulsory [tithes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tithe "Tithe").[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dechristianization_of_France_during_the_French_Revolution#cite_note-:0-10) Since the Church kept the [registry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civil_registration "Civil registration") of births, deaths, and marriages and was the only institution that provided hospitals and education in most parts of the country, it influenced all citizens.

### Between 1789 and 1792

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Dechristianization_of_France_during_the_French_Revolution&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Between 1789 and 1792")]

[![Image 11](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Pius_VI_%E2%80%93_Collection_generale_des_brefs_et_instructions_relatifs_a_la_revolution_francoise%2C_1798_%E2%80%93_BEIC_14269536.jpg/250px-Pius_VI_%E2%80%93_Collection_generale_des_brefs_et_instructions_relatifs_a_la_revolution_francoise%2C_1798_%E2%80%93_BEIC_14269536.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Pius_VI_%E2%80%93_Collection_generale_des_brefs_et_instructions_relatifs_a_la_revolution_francoise,_1798_%E2%80%93_BEIC_14269536.jpg)

General collection of writs and instructions relating to the French Revolution (_Collection generale des brefs et instructions relatifs a la revolution francoise_) of [Pope Pius VI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pope_Pius_VI "Pope Pius VI"), 1798

A milestone event of the [French Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Revolution "French Revolution") was the abolition of the privileges of the First and Second Estate on the [night of 4 August 1789](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abolition_of_feudalism_in_France "Abolition of feudalism in France"). In particular, it abolished the tithes gathered by the Catholic clergy.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dechristianization_of_France_during_the_French_Revolution#cite_note-11)

The [Declaration of the Rights of Man and Citizen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Declaration_of_the_Rights_of_Man_and_Citizen "Declaration of the Rights of Man and Citizen") of 1789 proclaimed freedom of religion across France in these terms:

> Article IV – Liberty consists of doing anything which does not harm others: thus, the exercise of the natural rights of each man has only those borders which assure other members of the society the enjoyment of these same rights. These borders can be determined only by the law.

> Article X – No one may be disturbed for his opinions, even religious ones, provided that their manifestation does not trouble the public order established by the law.

On 10 October 1789, the [National Constituent Assembly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Constituent_Assembly_(France) "National Constituent Assembly (France)") seized the properties and land held by the Catholic Church and decided to sell them to fund the [assignat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Assignat "Assignat") revolutionary currency. On 12 July 1790, the assembly passed the [Civil Constitution of the Clergy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civil_Constitution_of_the_Clergy "Civil Constitution of the Clergy") that subordinated the Catholic Church in France to the French government. It was never accepted by the [Pope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pope "Pope") and other high-ranking clergy in [Rome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rome "Rome").

Policies of the revolutionary authorities
-----------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Dechristianization_of_France_during_the_French_Revolution&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Policies of the revolutionary authorities")]

The programme of dechristianization waged against Catholicism, and eventually against all forms of Christianity, included:[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dechristianization_of_France_during_the_French_Revolution#cite_note-12)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dechristianization_of_France_during_the_French_Revolution#cite_note-Latreille-13)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dechristianization_of_France_during_the_French_Revolution#cite_note-FOOTNOTESpielvogel2006549-2)[_[need quotation to verify](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability "Wikipedia:Verifiability")_]

*   destruction of statues, plates and other [iconography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iconography "Iconography") from places of worship
*   destruction of [crosses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross "Cross"), bells and other external signs of worship
*   the institution of revolutionary and civic [cults](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cult_(religious_practice) "Cult (religious practice)"), including the [Cult of Reason](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cult_of_Reason "Cult of Reason") and subsequently the [Cult of the Supreme Being](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cult_of_the_Supreme_Being "Cult of the Supreme Being") (spring 1794)
*   the enactment of a law on 21 October 1793 making all [nonjuring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Refractory_bishop "Refractory bishop")[priests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Priest "Priest") and all persons who harbored them liable to death on sight

[![Image 12](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/F%C3%AAte_de_la_Raison_1793.jpg/250px-F%C3%AAte_de_la_Raison_1793.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:F%C3%AAte_de_la_Raison_1793.jpg)

[_Fête de la Raison_ ("Festival of Reason")](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cult_of_Reason#Festival_of_Reason "Cult of Reason"), [Notre Dame, Paris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Notre-Dame_de_Paris "Notre-Dame de Paris"), 10 November 1793

An especially notable event that took place in the course of France’s dechristianization was the [Festival of Reason](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cult_of_Reason#Festival_of_Reason "Cult of Reason"), which was held in [Notre Dame Cathedral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Notre_Dame_de_Paris "Notre Dame de Paris") on 10 November 1793. The dechristianization campaign can be seen as the logical extension[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dechristianization_of_France_during_the_French_Revolution#cite_note-14) of the [materialist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Materialism "Materialism") philosophies of some leaders of the [Enlightenment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment "Age of Enlightenment") such as [Voltaire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voltaire "Voltaire"), while for others with more prosaic concerns it provided an opportunity to unleash resentments against the Catholic Church (in the spirit of conventional [anti-clericalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anti-clericalism "Anti-clericalism")) and its clergy.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dechristianization_of_France_during_the_French_Revolution#cite_note-15)

The Revolution and the Church
-----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Dechristianization_of_France_during_the_French_Revolution&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: The Revolution and the Church")]

In August 1789, the state cancelled the taxing power of the Church. The issue of Church property became central to the policies of the revolutionary government. Declaring that all Church property in France belonged to the nation, confiscations were ordered, and Church properties were sold at public auction. In July 1790 the [National Constituent Assembly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Constituent_Assembly_(France) "National Constituent Assembly (France)") published the [Civil Constitution of the Clergy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civil_Constitution_of_the_Clergy "Civil Constitution of the Clergy") that stripped clerics of their special rights—the clergy were to be made employees of the state, elected by their parish or bishopric, and the number of bishoprics was to be reduced—and required all priests and bishops to swear an oath of fidelity to the new order or face dismissal, deportation or death. French priests had to receive papal approval to sign such an oath, and [Pope Pius VI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pope_Pius_VI "Pope Pius VI") spent almost eight months deliberating on the issue. On 13 April 1791, Pius denounced the constitution, resulting in a split in the [French Catholic Church](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catholic_Church_in_France "Catholic Church in France"). Over 50% became [abjuring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Refractory_bishop "Refractory bishop") priests ("jurors"), also known as "[constitutional clergy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civil_Constitution_of_the_Clergy "Civil Constitution of the Clergy")", and nonjuring priests as "refractory clergy".

[![Image 13](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Carte_des_pr%C3%AAtres_asserment%C3%A9s_en_France_en_1791.svg/250px-Carte_des_pr%C3%AAtres_asserment%C3%A9s_en_France_en_1791.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Carte_des_pr%C3%AAtres_asserment%C3%A9s_en_France_en_1791.svg)

Map of France showing the percentage of juring priests in 1791. The borders of the map are those of 2007, because the data come from archives of the modern [departments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Departments_of_France "Departments of France").

In September 1792, the [Legislative Assembly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legislative_Assembly_(France) "Legislative Assembly (France)") legalized divorce, contrary to Catholic doctrine. At the same time, the state took control of the birth, death, and marriage registers away from the Church. An ever-increasing view that the Church was a counter-revolutionary force exacerbated the social and economic grievances, and violence erupted in towns and cities across France.

In [Paris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paris "Paris"), over a 48-hour period beginning on 2 September 1792, as the [Legislative Assembly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legislative_Assembly_(France) "Legislative Assembly (France)") (successor to the National Constituent Assembly) dissolved into chaos, three Church bishops and more than 200 priests were massacred by angry mobs; this constituted part of what would become known as the [September Massacres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/September_Massacres "September Massacres"). Priests were among those drowned in mass executions (_[noyades](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drownings\_at\_Nantes "Drownings at Nantes")_) for treason under the direction of [Jean-Baptiste Carrier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_Carrier "Jean-Baptiste Carrier"); priests and nuns were among the mass executions at [Lyons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lyon "Lyon") for [separatism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separatism "Separatism"), on the orders of [Joseph Fouché](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Fouch%C3%A9 "Joseph Fouché") and [Collot d'Herbois](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collot_d%27Herbois "Collot d'Herbois"). Hundreds more priests were imprisoned and made to suffer in abominable conditions in the port of [Rochefort](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rochefort,_Charente-Maritime "Rochefort, Charente-Maritime").

### Insurrection of 31 May – 2 June 1793

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Dechristianization_of_France_during_the_French_Revolution&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Insurrection of 31 May – 2 June 1793")]

After the [insurrection of 31 May – 2 June 1793](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Insurrection_of_31_May_%E2%80%93_2_June_1793 "Insurrection of 31 May – 2 June 1793"), there was a decisive turn away from the revolution's original principles of religious freedom, and in the late summer of 1793 dechristianization evolved into what Johnathan Israel describes as a "repressive, vandalistic, inquisitorial movement".[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dechristianization_of_France_during_the_French_Revolution#cite_note-16) A major spasm of dechristianization broke out during the autumn with many of the acts of dechristianization in 1793 being motivated by the seizure of Church gold and silver to finance the war effort.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dechristianization_of_France_during_the_French_Revolution#cite_note-17) In November the _département_ council of [Indre-et-Loire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indre-et-Loire "Indre-et-Loire") abolished the word _dimanche_ (English: Sunday).[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dechristianization_of_France_during_the_French_Revolution#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVovelle1991180,_182-18) The [Gregorian calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gregorian_calendar "Gregorian calendar"), an instrument decreed by [Pope Gregory XIII](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pope_Gregory_XIII "Pope Gregory XIII") in 1582, was replaced by the [French Republican calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Republican_calendar "French Republican calendar") which abolished the [sabbath](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sabbath_in_Christianity "Sabbath in Christianity"), [saints' days](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calendar_of_saints "Calendar of saints") and any references to the Church. The seven-day week became ten days instead.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dechristianization_of_France_during_the_French_Revolution#cite_note-19) It soon became clear, however, that nine consecutive days of work were too much, and that international relations could not be carried out without reverting to the Gregorian system, which was still in use everywhere outside of France. Consequently, the Gregorian calendar was reimplemented in 1795.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dechristianization_of_France_during_the_French_Revolution#cite_note-20)

Anti-clerical parades were held, and the [Archbishop of Paris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Archdiocese_of_Paris "Roman Catholic Archdiocese of Paris"), [Jean-Baptiste-Joseph Gobel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean-Baptiste-Joseph_Gobel "Jean-Baptiste-Joseph Gobel"), was forced to resign his duties and made to replace his [mitre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mitre "Mitre") with the red "[Cap of Liberty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phrygian_cap "Phrygian cap")". Street and place names with any sort of religious connotation were changed, such as the town of [Saint-Tropez](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saint-Tropez "Saint-Tropez"), which became Héraclée. Religious holidays were banned and replaced with holidays to celebrate the harvest and other non-religious symbols. Many churches were converted into "temples of reason", in which deistic services were held.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dechristianization_of_France_during_the_French_Revolution#cite_note-HorneDavidson2013-21)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dechristianization_of_France_during_the_French_Revolution#cite_note-Latreille-13)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dechristianization_of_France_during_the_French_Revolution#cite_note-FOOTNOTESpielvogel2006549-2)[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dechristianization_of_France_during_the_French_Revolution#cite_note-FOOTNOTETallett19911-17-1) Local people often resisted this dechristianisation and forced members of the clergy who had resigned to conduct [mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass_(liturgy) "Mass (liturgy)") again. [Maximilien Robespierre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maximilien_Robespierre "Maximilien Robespierre") and the [Committee of Public Safety](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Committee_of_Public_Safety "Committee of Public Safety") denounced the dechristianizers as foreign enemies of the revolution and established their own religion. This [Cult of the Supreme Being](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cult_of_the_Supreme_Being "Cult of the Supreme Being"), without the alleged "superstitions" of Catholicism,[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dechristianization_of_France_during_the_French_Revolution#cite_note-Censer_and_Hunt-22) supplanted both Catholicism and the rival [Cult of Reason](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cult_of_Reason "Cult of Reason"). Both new religions were short-lived.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dechristianization_of_France_during_the_French_Revolution#cite_note-Fremont-Barnes2007-23)[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dechristianization_of_France_during_the_French_Revolution#cite_note-Censer_and_Hunt-22) Just six weeks before his arrest, on 8 June 1794, the still-powerful Robespierre personally led a vast procession through Paris to the [Tuileries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tuileries_Palace "Tuileries Palace") garden in a ceremony to inaugurate the new faith. His [execution occurred](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermidorian_Reaction "Thermidorian Reaction") shortly afterward, on 28 July 1794.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dechristianization_of_France_during_the_French_Revolution#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVovelle1991180,_182-18)

### Concordat of 1801

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Dechristianization_of_France_during_the_French_Revolution&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Concordat of 1801")]

By early 1795, a return to some form of religion-based faith was beginning to take shape, and a law passed on 21 February 1795 legalized public worship, albeit with strict limitations. The ringing of church bells, religious processions and displays of the Christian cross were still forbidden. As late as 1799, priests were still being imprisoned or deported to penal colonies. Persecution only worsened after the French army led by General [Louis Alexandre Berthier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis_Alexandre_Berthier "Louis Alexandre Berthier") captured Rome in early 1798, declared a new [Roman Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Republic_(1798%E2%80%931799) "Roman Republic (1798–1799)"), and also imprisoned Pope Pius VI, who died in captivity. However, after [Napoleon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Napoleon "Napoleon") seized control of the government in late 1799, France entered into year-long negotiations with [Pope Pius VII](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pope_Pius_VII "Pope Pius VII"), resulting in the [Concordat of 1801](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concordat_of_1801 "Concordat of 1801"). This formally ended the dechristianization period and established the rules for a relationship between the Catholic Church and the French state.

Victims of the [Reign of Terror](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reign_of_Terror "Reign of Terror") totaled somewhere between 20,000 and 40,000. According to one estimate, among those condemned by the [revolutionary tribunals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Revolutionary_Tribunal "Revolutionary Tribunal") about 8 percent were [aristocrats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristocracy "Aristocracy"), 6 percent clergy, 14 percent middle class, and 70 percent were workers or peasants accused of hoarding, evading the draft, desertion, rebellion, and other purported crimes.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dechristianization_of_France_during_the_French_Revolution#cite_note-history.com-24) Of these social groupings, the clergy of the Catholic Church suffered proportionately the greatest loss.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dechristianization_of_France_during_the_French_Revolution#cite_note-history.com-24)

Anti-Church laws were passed by the [Legislative Assembly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legislative_Assembly_(France) "Legislative Assembly (France)") and its successor, the [National Convention](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Convention "National Convention"), as well as by [département](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D%C3%A9partement "Département") councils throughout the country. The Concordat of 1801 endured for more than a century until it was abrogated by the government of the [Third Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Third_Republic "French Third Republic"), which established a policy of [laïcité](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/La%C3%AFcit%C3%A9 "Laïcité") on 11 December 1905.

Toll on the Church
------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Dechristianization_of_France_during_the_French_Revolution&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Toll on the Church")]

Under threat of death, imprisonment, military conscription, and loss of income, about 20,000 constitutional priests were forced to abdicate and hand over their letters of ordination, and 6,000 to 9,000 of them agreed or were [coerced to marry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forced_marriage "Forced marriage"). Many abandoned their pastoral duties altogether.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dechristianization_of_France_during_the_French_Revolution#cite_note-FOOTNOTETallett19911-17-1) Nonetheless, some of those who had abdicated continued covertly to minister to the people.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dechristianization_of_France_during_the_French_Revolution#cite_note-FOOTNOTETallett19911-17-1)

By the end of the decade, approximately 30,000 priests had been forced to leave France, and several hundred who did not leave were executed.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dechristianization_of_France_during_the_French_Revolution#cite_note-FOOTNOTELewis199396-25) Most French parishes were left without the services of a priest and deprived of the [sacraments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sacraments "Sacraments"). Any non-juring priest faced the [guillotine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guillotine "Guillotine") or deportation to [French Guiana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Guiana "French Guiana").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dechristianization_of_France_during_the_French_Revolution#cite_note-FOOTNOTETallett19911-17-1) By Easter 1794, few of France's 40,000 churches remained open; many had been closed, sold, destroyed, or converted to other uses.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dechristianization_of_France_during_the_French_Revolution#cite_note-FOOTNOTETallett19911-17-1)

Victims of revolutionary violence, whether religious or not, were popularly treated as Christian martyrs, and the places where they were killed became pilgrimage destinations.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dechristianization_of_France_during_the_French_Revolution#cite_note-FOOTNOTETallett19911-17-1) Catechising in the home, [folk religion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Folk_religion "Folk religion"), [syncretic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syncretic "Syncretic") and [heterodox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heterodox "Heterodox") practices all became more common.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dechristianization_of_France_during_the_French_Revolution#cite_note-FOOTNOTETallett19911-17-1) The long-term effects on religious practice in France were significant. Many who were dissuaded from their traditional religious practices never resumed them.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dechristianization_of_France_during_the_French_Revolution#cite_note-FOOTNOTETallett19911-17-1)

### Destruction of monasteries

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Dechristianization_of_France_during_the_French_Revolution&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Destruction of monasteries")]

The revolution saw the widespread dissolution and destruction of many French [monasteries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monasteries "Monasteries"), as revolutionary authorities sought to suppress religious institutions and confiscate their wealth.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dechristianization_of_France_during_the_French_Revolution#cite_note-26) Many monastic buildings were seized, looted, repurposed for secular use, or demolished entirely. Orders such as the [Benedictines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benedictines "Benedictines"), [Cistercians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cistercians "Cistercians"), [Franciscans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Franciscans "Franciscans"), and [Carmelites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carmelites "Carmelites") were particularly affected.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dechristianization_of_France_during_the_French_Revolution#cite_note-27)
