_**Venera 1**_ ([Russian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_language "Russian language"): Венера-1 meaning _Venus 1_), also known as **Venera-1VA No.2** and occasionally in the West as _**Sputnik 8**_, was the first spacecraft to perform an [interplanetary flight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_flight "Interplanetary flight") and the first to fly past [Venus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Venus "Venus"), as part of the [Soviet Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Union "Soviet Union")'s [Venera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Venera "Venera") programme.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Venera_1#cite_note-:0-1) Launched in February 1961, it was intended as an impactor, but flew past Venus on 19 May of the same year; however, radio contact with the [probe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_probe "Space probe") was lost before the [flyby](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flyby_(spaceflight) "Flyby (spaceflight)"), resulting in it returning no data.

Spacecraft
----------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Venera_1&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Spacecraft")]

_Venera 1_ was a 643.5-kilogram (1,419 lb) probe consisting of a cylindrical body 1.05 metres (3 ft 5 in) in diameter topped by a dome, totalling 2.035 metres (6 ft 8.1 in) in height. This was pressurized to 1.2 standard atmospheres (120 kPa) with dry nitrogen, with internal fans to maintain even distribution of heat.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] Two solar panels extended from the cylinder, charging a bank of [silver-zinc batteries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silver-zinc_battery "Silver-zinc battery"). A 2-metre (6 ft 7 in) parabolic wire-mesh antenna was designed to send data from Venus to Earth on a frequency of 922.8 MHz. A 2.4-metre (7 ft 10 in) antenna boom was used to transmit short-wave signals during the near-Earth phase of the mission. Semidirectional [quadrupole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrupole "Quadrupole") antennas mounted on the solar panels provided routine telemetry and telecommand contact with Earth during the mission, on a circularly-polarized decimetre radio band.

The probe was equipped with scientific instruments including a flux-gate [magnetometer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetometer "Magnetometer") attached to the antenna boom, two [ion traps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ion_trap "Ion trap") to measure solar wind, [micrometeorite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Micrometeorite "Micrometeorite") detectors, and [Geiger counter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geiger_counter "Geiger counter") tubes and a sodium iodide [scintillator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scintillator "Scintillator") for measurement of cosmic radiation. An experiment attached to one solar panel measured temperatures of experimental coatings. Infrared and/or ultraviolet radiometers may have been included. The dome contained a [KDU-414](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/KDU-414 "KDU-414") engine used for mid-course corrections. Temperature control was achieved by motorized thermal shutters.

During most of its flight, _Venera 1_ was spin stabilized. It was the first spacecraft designed to perform mid-course corrections, by entering a mode of 3-axis stabilization, fixing on the Sun and the star [Canopus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canopus "Canopus"). Had it reached Venus, it would have entered another mode of 3-axis stabilization, fixing on the [Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun "Sun") and [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth "Earth"), and using for the first time a parabolic antenna to relay data.

Launch
------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Venera_1&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Launch")]

[![Image 12](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/The_Soviet_Union_1961_CPA_2557A_stamp_%28Launching_of_the_Venus_space_probe%2C_1961-2-12._Space_probe_and_its_path_to_Venus%29.jpg/250px-The_Soviet_Union_1961_CPA_2557A_stamp_%28Launching_of_the_Venus_space_probe%2C_1961-2-12._Space_probe_and_its_path_to_Venus%29.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:The_Soviet_Union_1961_CPA_2557A_stamp_(Launching_of_the_Venus_space_probe,_1961-2-12._Space_probe_and_its_path_to_Venus).jpg)

Route of Venera 1 on a Soviet stamp

_Venera 1_ was the second of two attempts to launch a probe to Venus in February 1961, immediately following the launch of its sister ship [_Venera-1VA No.1_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tyazhely_Sputnik "Tyazhely Sputnik"),[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Venera_1#cite_note-Chronology-2) which failed to leave Earth orbit due to the failure of a power transformer. The transformer was wrapped in foil and painted black and white for thermal reasons.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Venera_1#cite_note-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Venera_1#cite_note-Soviet_Failures-4) Soviet experts launched Venera-1 using a [Molniya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molniya_(rocket) "Molniya (rocket)") carrier rocket from the [Baikonur Cosmodrome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baikonur_Cosmodrome "Baikonur Cosmodrome"). The launch took place at 00:34:36 GMT on 12 February 1961. Vehicle L1-6V steered downrange into a clear blue winter sky and orbit was successfully achieved.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Venera_1#cite_note-5)

The spacecraft, along with the rocket's [Blok-L](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Blok-L&action=edit&redlink=1 "Blok-L (page does not exist)") upper stage, was initially placed into a 229-by-282-kilometre (142 mi ×175 mi) [low Earth orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit "Low Earth orbit"),[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Venera_1#cite_note-:0-1) before the upper stage fired to place "Venera 1" into a [heliocentric orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heliocentric_orbit "Heliocentric orbit"), directed towards Venus. The 11D33 engine was the world's first staged-combustion-cycle rocket engine, and also the first use of an [ullage engine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ullage_engine "Ullage engine") to allow a liquid-fuel rocket engine to start in space.

Failure
-------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Venera_1&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Failure")]

During the first telemetry session, it was found that Venera 1 failed to enter the constant solar orientation mode, which led to a shortage of electrical power on board. While unfortunate, this was expected and in such a case the station had to conduct a rough orientation to the Sun and turn off non-vital systems. However, the radio transmitters had not been adequately designed and communication was shortly lost. The signal was regained on 17 February but it was still unable to lock onto the Sun properly and again went into protective mode and turned off the transmitter. Three successful telemetry sessions were conducted, gathering solar-wind and cosmic-ray data near Earth, at the Earth's [magnetopause](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetopause "Magnetopause"), and on February 19 at a distance of 1,900,000 km (1,200,000 mi). After discovering the [solar wind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_wind "Solar wind") with _Luna 2_, _Venera 1_ provided the first verification that this plasma was uniformly present in deep space. Seven days later, the next scheduled telemetry session failed to occur. On May 19, 1961, _Venera 1_ passed within 100,000 km (62,000 mi) of Venus. With the help of the British radio telescope at [Jodrell Bank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jodrell_Bank "Jodrell Bank"), some weak signals from _Venera 1_ may have been detected in June. Soviet engineers believed that _Venera 1_ failed due to the overheating of a solar-direction sensor.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Venera_1#cite_note-:1-6)
