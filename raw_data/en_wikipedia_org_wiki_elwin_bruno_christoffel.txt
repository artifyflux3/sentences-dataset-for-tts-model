**Elwin Bruno Christoffel** (German:[[kʁɪˈstɔfl̩]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German "Help:IPA/Standard German"); 10 November 1829 – 15 March 1900) was a German [mathematician](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematician "Mathematician") and [physicist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physicist "Physicist"). He introduced fundamental concepts of [differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry"), opening the way for the development of [tensor calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_calculus "Tensor calculus"), which would later provide the mathematical basis for [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity").

Christoffel was born on 10 November 1829 in Montjoie (now [Monschau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monschau "Monschau")) in [Prussia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Prussia "Kingdom of Prussia") in a family of cloth merchants. He was initially educated at home in languages and mathematics, then attended the Jesuit Gymnasium and the Friedrich-Wilhelms [Gymnasium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gymnasium_(Germany) "Gymnasium (Germany)") in [Cologne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cologne "Cologne"). In 1850 he went to the [University of Berlin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Humboldt_University_of_Berlin "Humboldt University of Berlin"), where he studied mathematics with [Gustav Dirichlet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Gustav_Lejeune_Dirichlet "Peter Gustav Lejeune Dirichlet") (which had a strong influence over him)[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elwin_Bruno_Christoffel#cite_note-Windelband-1) among others, as well as attending courses in physics and chemistry. He received his doctorate in Berlin in 1856 for a thesis on the motion of [electricity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electricity "Electricity") in homogeneous bodies written under the supervision of [Martin Ohm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Martin_Ohm "Martin Ohm"), [Ernst Kummer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ernst_Kummer "Ernst Kummer") and [Heinrich Gustav Magnus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heinrich_Gustav_Magnus "Heinrich Gustav Magnus").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elwin_Bruno_Christoffel#cite_note-Butzer-2)

After receiving his doctorate, Christoffel returned to Montjoie where he spent the following three years in isolation from the academic community. However, he continued to study mathematics (especially mathematical physics) from books by [Bernhard Riemann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bernhard_Riemann "Bernhard Riemann"), Dirichlet and [Augustin-Louis Cauchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Augustin-Louis_Cauchy "Augustin-Louis Cauchy"). He also continued his research, publishing two papers in [differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elwin_Bruno_Christoffel#cite_note-Butzer-2)

In 1859 Christoffel returned to Berlin, earning his [habilitation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habilitation "Habilitation") and becoming a [Privatdozent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Privatdozent "Privatdozent") at the University of Berlin. In 1862 he was appointed to a chair at the [Polytechnic School](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ETH_Z%C3%BCrich "ETH Zürich") in Zürich left vacant by [Dedekind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Dedekind "Richard Dedekind"). He organised a new institute of mathematics at the young institution (it had been established only seven years earlier) that was highly appreciated. He also continued to publish research, and in 1868 he was elected a corresponding member of the [Prussian Academy of Sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prussian_Academy_of_Sciences "Prussian Academy of Sciences") and of the [Istituto Lombardo](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Istituto_Lombardo&action=edit&redlink=1 "Istituto Lombardo (page does not exist)") in Milan. In 1869 Christoffel returned to Berlin as a professor at the Gewerbeakademie (now part of [Technische Universität Berlin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Technische_Universit%C3%A4t_Berlin "Technische Universität Berlin")), with [Hermann Schwarz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Schwarz "Hermann Schwarz") succeeding him in Zürich. However, strong competition from the close proximity to the University of Berlin meant that the Gewerbeakademie could not attract enough students to sustain advanced mathematical courses and Christoffel left Berlin again after three years.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elwin_Bruno_Christoffel#cite_note-Butzer-2)

In 1872 Christoffel became a professor at the [University of Strasbourg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Strasbourg "University of Strasbourg"), a centuries-old institution that was being reorganized into a modern university after Prussia's annexation of [Alsace-Lorraine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alsace-Lorraine "Alsace-Lorraine") in the [Franco-Prussian War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Franco-Prussian_War "Franco-Prussian War"). Christoffel, together with his colleague [Theodor Reye](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theodor_Reye "Theodor Reye"), built a reputable mathematics department at Strasbourg. He continued to publish research and had several doctoral students including [Rikitaro Fujisawa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rikitaro_Fujisawa "Rikitaro Fujisawa"), [Ludwig Maurer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ludwig_Maurer "Ludwig Maurer") and [Paul Epstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Epstein "Paul Epstein"). Christoffel retired from the University of Strasbourg in 1894, being succeeded by [Heinrich Weber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heinrich_Martin_Weber "Heinrich Martin Weber").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elwin_Bruno_Christoffel#cite_note-Butzer-2) After retirement he continued to work and publish, with the last treatise finished just before his death and published posthumously.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elwin_Bruno_Christoffel#cite_note-Windelband-1)

Christoffel died on 15 March 1900 in Strasbourg. He never married and left no family.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elwin_Bruno_Christoffel#cite_note-Butzer-2)

### Differential geometry

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Elwin_Bruno_Christoffel&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Differential geometry")]

Christoffel is mainly remembered for his seminal contributions to [differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry"). In a famous 1869 paper on the equivalence problem for [differential forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_form "Differential form") in _n_ variables, published in [Crelle's Journal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crelle%27s_Journal "Crelle's Journal"),[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elwin_Bruno_Christoffel#cite_note-3) he introduced the fundamental technique later called [covariant differentiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covariant_differentiation "Covariant differentiation") and used it to define the [Riemann–Christoffel tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_curvature_tensor "Riemann curvature tensor") (the most common method used to express the [curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvature "Curvature") of [Riemannian manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_manifolds "Riemannian manifolds")). In the same paper he introduced the [Christoffel symbols](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christoffel_symbols "Christoffel symbols")![Image 2: {\displaystyle \Gamma _{kij}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/32ff1c80086db3b18b1a9d995afd822f63d46421) and ![Image 3: {\displaystyle \Gamma _{ij}^{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54d6c913ed873bfa56bdf2e3dab2b3ea9225638a) which express the components of the [Levi-Civita connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_connection "Levi-Civita connection") with respect to a system of local coordinates. Christoffel's ideas were generalized and greatly developed by [Gregorio Ricci-Curbastro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gregorio_Ricci-Curbastro "Gregorio Ricci-Curbastro") and his student [Tullio Levi-Civita](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tullio_Levi-Civita "Tullio Levi-Civita"), who turned them into the concept of [tensors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor "Tensor") and the [absolute differential calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_differential_calculus "Absolute differential calculus"). The absolute differential calculus, later named [tensor calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_calculus "Tensor calculus"), forms the mathematical basis of the [general theory of relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_theory_of_relativity "General theory of relativity").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elwin_Bruno_Christoffel#cite_note-Butzer-2)

Christoffel contributed to [complex analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_analysis "Complex analysis"), where the [Schwarz–Christoffel mapping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schwarz%E2%80%93Christoffel_mapping "Schwarz–Christoffel mapping") is the first nontrivial constructive application of the [Riemann mapping theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_mapping_theorem "Riemann mapping theorem"). The Schwarz–Christoffel mapping has many applications to the theory of [elliptic functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptic_function "Elliptic function") and to areas of physics.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elwin_Bruno_Christoffel#cite_note-Butzer-2) In the field of elliptic functions he also published results concerning [abelian integrals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abelian_integral "Abelian integral") and [theta functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theta_function "Theta function").

Christoffel generalized the [Gaussian quadrature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaussian_quadrature "Gaussian quadrature") method for integration and, in connection to this, he also introduced the [Christoffel–Darboux formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christoffel%E2%80%93Darboux_formula "Christoffel–Darboux formula") for [Legendre polynomials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legendre_polynomial "Legendre polynomial")[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elwin_Bruno_Christoffel#cite_note-4) (he later also published the formula for general [orthogonal polynomials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_polynomials "Orthogonal polynomials")).

Christoffel also worked on [potential theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potential_theory "Potential theory") and the theory of [differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_equations "Differential equations"), however much of his research in these areas went unnoticed. He published two papers on the propagation of discontinuities in the solutions of partial differential equations which represent pioneering work in the theory of [shock waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shock_waves "Shock waves"). He also studied physics and published research in [optics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optics "Optics"), however his contributions here quickly lost their utility with the abandonment of the concept of the [luminiferous aether](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luminiferous_aether "Luminiferous aether").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elwin_Bruno_Christoffel#cite_note-Butzer-2)

Christoffel was elected as a corresponding member of several academies:

*   [Prussian Academy of Sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prussian_Academy_of_Sciences "Prussian Academy of Sciences") (1868)
*   [Istituto Lombardo](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Istituto_Lombardo&action=edit&redlink=1 "Istituto Lombardo (page does not exist)") (1868)
*   [Göttingen Academy of Sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G%C3%B6ttingen_Academy_of_Sciences "Göttingen Academy of Sciences") (1869)

Christoffel was also awarded two distinctions for his activity by the Kingdom of Prussia:

*   [Order of the Red Eagle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Order_of_the_Red_Eagle "Order of the Red Eagle") 3rd Class with bow (_Schleife_) (1893)
*   [Order of the Crown](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Order_of_the_Crown_(Prussia) "Order of the Crown (Prussia)") 2nd Class (1895)
