[Coordinate surfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_system#Coordinate_surface "Coordinate system") of the bipolar cylindrical coordinates. The yellow crescent corresponds to σ, whereas the red tube corresponds to τ and the blue plane corresponds to _z_=1. The three surfaces intersect at the point **P** (shown as a black sphere).

**Bipolar cylindrical coordinates** are a three-dimensional [orthogonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_coordinates "Orthogonal coordinates")[coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_system "Coordinate system") that results from projecting the two-dimensional [bipolar coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bipolar_coordinates "Bipolar coordinates") in the perpendicular ![Image 2: {\displaystyle z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf368e72c009decd9b6686ee84a375632e11de98)-direction. The two lines of [foci](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Focus_(geometry) "Focus (geometry)")![Image 3: {\displaystyle F_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/100c7fbf174fe8b06eacc2a6b0bb2e1badd1c7ce) and ![Image 4: {\displaystyle F_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0fd17e0779153d765b40ebef91533489b87b2e37) of the projected [Apollonian circles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apollonian_circles "Apollonian circles") are generally taken to be defined by ![Image 5: {\displaystyle x=-a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9f726a9f4cff586cefe8198e5c628a55d70b5571) and ![Image 6: {\displaystyle x=+a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8814caa6e38d62bcfb2abd0536056ce084338746), respectively, (and by ![Image 7: {\displaystyle y=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/094f824655138f6b11d96a0da32e7f0716ba6959)) in the [Cartesian coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system "Cartesian coordinate system").

The term "bipolar" is often used to describe other curves having two singular points (foci), such as [ellipses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse "Ellipse"), [hyperbolas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbola "Hyperbola"), and [Cassini ovals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cassini_oval "Cassini oval"). However, the term _bipolar coordinates_ is never used to describe coordinates associated with those curves, e.g., [elliptic coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptic_coordinates "Elliptic coordinates").

The most common definition of bipolar cylindrical coordinates ![Image 8: {\displaystyle (\sigma ,\tau ,z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b3bd510376be13a47c5f0d984e365cb324833daa) is

![Image 9: {\displaystyle x=a\ {\frac {\sinh \tau }{\cosh \tau -\cos \sigma }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8038c012c5ae8c37a666ae513ef4f2fc1ec3d455)![Image 10: {\displaystyle y=a\ {\frac {\sin \sigma }{\cosh \tau -\cos \sigma }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/04cde3338cb185300dfc6a71418a84e7b1a09869)![Image 11: {\displaystyle z=\ z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5726cdf5e450c25058c02eab8f67c488500c7acf)
where the ![Image 12: {\displaystyle \sigma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/59f59b7c3e6fdb1d0365a494b81fb9a696138c36) coordinate of a point ![Image 13: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a) equals the angle ![Image 14: {\displaystyle F_{1}PF_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9cda32c101507b35c2f715808ecce6b648c0ebff) and the ![Image 15: {\displaystyle \tau }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38a7dcde9730ef0853809fefc18d88771f95206c) coordinate equals the [natural logarithm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_logarithm "Natural logarithm") of the ratio of the distances ![Image 16: {\displaystyle d_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4cccb5a6a2f1acab4ca255e0be86c224ed82282a) and ![Image 17: {\displaystyle d_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9276f8f68c5c23329de74ad76e69f6801358fb1f) to the focal lines

![Image 18: {\displaystyle \tau =\ln {\frac {d_{1}}{d_{2}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/18362215eee1a1e4d0695ab357d50c66cbbd687b)
(Recall that the focal lines ![Image 19: {\displaystyle F_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/100c7fbf174fe8b06eacc2a6b0bb2e1badd1c7ce) and ![Image 20: {\displaystyle F_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0fd17e0779153d765b40ebef91533489b87b2e37) are located at ![Image 21: {\displaystyle x=-a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9f726a9f4cff586cefe8198e5c628a55d70b5571) and ![Image 22: {\displaystyle x=+a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8814caa6e38d62bcfb2abd0536056ce084338746), respectively.)

Surfaces of constant ![Image 23: {\displaystyle \sigma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/59f59b7c3e6fdb1d0365a494b81fb9a696138c36) correspond to cylinders of different radii

![Image 24: {\displaystyle x^{2}+\left(y-a\cot \sigma \right)^{2}={\frac {a^{2}}{\sin ^{2}\sigma }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4f56ee4b3e4fb98725f2f97f9bf4a87960ea27b4)
that all pass through the focal lines and are not concentric. The surfaces of constant ![Image 25: {\displaystyle \tau }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38a7dcde9730ef0853809fefc18d88771f95206c) are non-intersecting cylinders of different radii

![Image 26: {\displaystyle y^{2}+\left(x-a\coth \tau \right)^{2}={\frac {a^{2}}{\sinh ^{2}\tau }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6c9a14fcf9a2e6ada0448d4c344ef89c64d7805c)
that surround the focal lines but again are not concentric. The focal lines and all these cylinders are parallel to the ![Image 27: {\displaystyle z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf368e72c009decd9b6686ee84a375632e11de98)-axis (the direction of projection). In the ![Image 28: {\displaystyle z=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b92bfc06485cc90286474b14a516a68d8bfdd7b3) plane, the centers of the constant-![Image 29: {\displaystyle \sigma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/59f59b7c3e6fdb1d0365a494b81fb9a696138c36) and constant-![Image 30: {\displaystyle \tau }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38a7dcde9730ef0853809fefc18d88771f95206c) cylinders lie on the ![Image 31: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d) and ![Image 32: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) axes, respectively.

The scale factors for the bipolar coordinates ![Image 33: {\displaystyle \sigma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/59f59b7c3e6fdb1d0365a494b81fb9a696138c36) and ![Image 34: {\displaystyle \tau }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38a7dcde9730ef0853809fefc18d88771f95206c) are equal

![Image 35: {\displaystyle h_{\sigma }=h_{\tau }={\frac {a}{\cosh \tau -\cos \sigma }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1f1dde0bb87db13aeafe2e17c842d6fc2d337de2)
whereas the remaining scale factor ![Image 36: {\displaystyle h_{z}=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/21caf494fc4bbb0ac6f8d7fe11be1982f03533e5). Thus, the infinitesimal volume element equals

![Image 37: {\displaystyle dV={\frac {a^{2}}{\left(\cosh \tau -\cos \sigma \right)^{2}}}d\sigma d\tau dz}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c44d55bfc3010f7e9f00793c92c3598beef005b0)
and the Laplacian is given by

![Image 38: {\displaystyle \nabla ^{2}\Phi ={\frac {1}{a^{2}}}\left(\cosh \tau -\cos \sigma \right)^{2}\left({\frac {\partial ^{2}\Phi }{\partial \sigma ^{2}}}+{\frac {\partial ^{2}\Phi }{\partial \tau ^{2}}}\right)+{\frac {\partial ^{2}\Phi }{\partial z^{2}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/31d3697fd259ed55cc7f2cbb04272f6717880e38)
Other differential operators such as ![Image 39: {\displaystyle \nabla \cdot \mathbf {F} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1f4f5b9e6560d7fdaf1a69d9fdeff6beb1f327cc) and ![Image 40: {\displaystyle \nabla \times \mathbf {F} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c6e2429b7e3cb6965d1a6651703ff5ef0c88b378) can be expressed in the coordinates ![Image 41: {\displaystyle (\sigma ,\tau )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c2d60596d44a9bc5043b60b76c1fac959eb65eb0) by substituting the scale factors into the general formulae found in [orthogonal coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_coordinates "Orthogonal coordinates").

The classic applications of bipolar coordinates are in solving [partial differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_differential_equations "Partial differential equations"), e.g., [Laplace's equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laplace%27s_equation "Laplace's equation") or the [Helmholtz equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helmholtz_equation "Helmholtz equation"), for which bipolar coordinates allow a [separation of variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separation_of_variables "Separation of variables") (in 2D). A typical example would be the [electric field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_field "Electric field") surrounding two parallel cylindrical conductors.
