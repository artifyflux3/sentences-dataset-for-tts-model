In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), the **polar coordinate system** specifies a given [point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_(mathematics) "Point (mathematics)") in a [plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_(mathematics) "Plane (mathematics)") by using a distance and an angle as its two [coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_system "Coordinate system"). These are

*   the point's distance from a reference point called the _pole_, and
*   the point's direction from the pole relative to the direction of the _polar axis_, a [ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ray_(geometry) "Ray (geometry)") drawn from the pole.

The distance from the pole is called the _radial coordinate_, _radial distance_ or simply _radius_, and the angle is called the _angular coordinate_, _polar angle_, or _[azimuth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Azimuth "Azimuth")_.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_coordinates#cite_note-brown-1) The pole is analogous to the origin in a [Cartesian coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system "Cartesian coordinate system").

Polar coordinates are most appropriate in any context where the phenomenon being considered is inherently tied to direction and length from a center point in a plane, such as [spirals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spiral "Spiral"). Planar physical systems with bodies moving around a central point, or phenomena originating from a central point, are often simpler and more intuitive to model using polar coordinates.

The polar coordinate system is extended to three dimensions in two ways: the [cylindrical coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylindrical_coordinate_system "Cylindrical coordinate system") adds a second distance coordinate, and the [spherical coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_coordinate_system "Spherical coordinate system") adds a second angular coordinate.

[Grégoire de Saint-Vincent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gr%C3%A9goire_de_Saint-Vincent "Grégoire de Saint-Vincent") and [Bonaventura Cavalieri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bonaventura_Cavalieri "Bonaventura Cavalieri") independently introduced the system's concepts in the mid-17th century, though the actual term _polar coordinates_ has been attributed to [Gregorio Fontana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gregorio_Fontana "Gregorio Fontana") in the 18th century. The initial motivation for introducing the polar system was the study of [circular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circular_motion "Circular motion") and [orbital motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_motion "Orbital motion").

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Head_of_Hipparchus_%28cropped%29.jpg/250px-Head_of_Hipparchus_%28cropped%29.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Head_of_Hipparchus_(cropped).jpg)

Hipparchus

The concepts of angle and radius were already used by ancient peoples of the first millennium [BC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Before_Christ "Before Christ"). The [Greek astronomer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_astronomy "Greek astronomy") and [astrologer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrologer "Astrologer")[Hipparchus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hipparchus "Hipparchus") (190–120 BC) created a table of [chord](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chord_(geometry) "Chord (geometry)") functions giving the length of the chord for each angle, and there are references to his using polar coordinates in establishing stellar positions.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_coordinates#cite_note-2) In _[On Spirals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/On\_Spirals "On Spirals")_, [Archimedes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archimedes "Archimedes") describes the [Archimedean spiral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archimedean_spiral "Archimedean spiral"), a function whose radius depends on the angle. The Greek work, however, did not extend to a full coordinate system.

From the 8th century AD onward, astronomers developed methods for approximating and calculating the direction to [Mecca](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mecca "Mecca") ([qibla](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qibla "Qibla"))—and its distance—from any location on the Earth.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_coordinates#cite_note-3) From the 9th century onward they were using [spherical trigonometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_trigonometry "Spherical trigonometry") and [map projection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Map_projection "Map projection") methods to determine these quantities accurately. The calculation is essentially the conversion of the [equatorial polar coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodetic_coordinates#Coordinates "Geodetic coordinates") of Mecca (i.e. its [longitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Longitude "Longitude") and [latitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latitude "Latitude")) to its polar coordinates (i.e. its qibla and distance) relative to a system whose reference meridian is the [great circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_circle "Great circle") through the given location and the Earth's poles and whose polar axis is the line through the location and its [antipodal point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antipodal_point "Antipodal point").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_coordinates#cite_note-4)

There are various accounts of the introduction of polar coordinates as part of a formal coordinate system. The full history of the subject is described in [Harvard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harvard_University "Harvard University") professor [Julian Lowell Coolidge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Julian_Lowell_Coolidge "Julian Lowell Coolidge")'s _Origin of Polar Coordinates._[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_coordinates#cite_note-coolidge-5) Grégoire de Saint-Vincent and Bonaventura Cavalieri independently introduced the concepts in the mid-seventeenth century. Saint-Vincent wrote about them privately in 1625 and published his work in 1647, while Cavalieri published his in 1635 with a corrected version appearing in 1653. Cavalieri first used polar coordinates to solve a problem relating to the area within an [Archimedean spiral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archimedean_spiral "Archimedean spiral"). [Blaise Pascal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blaise_Pascal "Blaise Pascal") subsequently used polar coordinates to calculate the length of [parabolic arcs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parabola "Parabola").

In _[Method of Fluxions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Method\_of\_Fluxions "Method of Fluxions")_ (written 1671, published 1736), Sir [Isaac Newton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaac_Newton "Isaac Newton") examined the transformations between polar coordinates, which he referred to as the "Seventh Manner; For Spirals", and nine other coordinate systems.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_coordinates#cite_note-6) In the journal _[Acta Eruditorum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acta\_Eruditorum "Acta Eruditorum")_ (1691), [Jacob Bernoulli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacob_Bernoulli "Jacob Bernoulli") used a system with a point on a line, called the _pole_ and _polar axis_ respectively. Coordinates were specified by the distance from the pole and the angle from the _polar axis_. Bernoulli's work extended to finding the [radius of curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radius_of_curvature_(mathematics) "Radius of curvature (mathematics)") of curves expressed in these coordinates.

The actual term _polar coordinates_ has been attributed to [Gregorio Fontana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gregorio_Fontana "Gregorio Fontana") and was used by 18th-century Italian writers. The term appeared in [English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English_language "English language") in [George Peacock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_Peacock_(mathematician) "George Peacock (mathematician)")'s 1816 translation of [Lacroix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sylvestre_Fran%C3%A7ois_Lacroix "Sylvestre François Lacroix")'s _Differential and Integral Calculus_.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_coordinates#cite_note-7)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_coordinates#cite_note-8)[Alexis Clairaut](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexis_Clairaut "Alexis Clairaut") was the first to think of polar coordinates in three dimensions, and [Leonhard Euler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leonhard_Euler "Leonhard Euler") was the first to actually develop them.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_coordinates#cite_note-coolidge-5)

[![Image 3](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Polar_graph_paper.svg/250px-Polar_graph_paper.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Polar_graph_paper.svg)

A polar grid with several angles, increasing in counterclockwise orientation and labelled in degrees

The radial coordinate is often denoted by _r_ or [_ρ_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rho "Rho"), and the angular coordinate by [_φ_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phi "Phi"), [_θ_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theta "Theta"), or _t_. The angular coordinate is specified as _φ_ by [ISO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization "International Organization for Standardization") standard [31-11](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO_31-11 "ISO 31-11"), now [80000-2:2019](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO/IEC_80000 "ISO/IEC 80000"). However, in mathematical literature the angle is often denoted by θ instead.

Angles in polar notation are generally expressed in either [degrees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_(angle) "Degree (angle)") or [radians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radian "Radian") (2[π](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi "Pi") rad being equal to 360°). Degrees are traditionally used in [navigation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Navigation "Navigation"), [surveying](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surveying "Surveying"), and many applied disciplines, while radians are more common in mathematics and mathematical [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics").[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_coordinates#cite_note-9)

The angle _φ_ is defined to start at 0° from a _reference direction_, and to increase for rotations in either [clockwise (cw)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clockwise "Clockwise") or counterclockwise (ccw) orientation. For example, in mathematics, the reference direction is usually drawn as a [ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ray_(geometry) "Ray (geometry)") from the pole horizontally to the right, and the polar angle increases to positive angles for ccw rotations, whereas in navigation ([bearing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bearing_(navigation) "Bearing (navigation)"), [heading](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heading_(navigation) "Heading (navigation)")) the 0°-heading is drawn vertically upwards and the angle increases for cw rotations. The polar angles decrease towards negative values for rotations in the respectively opposite orientations.

### Uniqueness of polar coordinates

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Polar_coordinate_system&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Uniqueness of polar coordinates")]

Adding any number of full [turns](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turn_(geometry) "Turn (geometry)") (360°) to the angular coordinate does not change the corresponding direction. Similarly, any polar coordinate is identical to the coordinate with the negative radial component and the opposite direction (adding 180° to the polar angle). Therefore, the same point (_r_, _φ_) can be expressed with an infinite number of different polar coordinates (_r_, _φ_ + _n_ × 360°) and (−_r_, _φ_ + 180° + _n_ × 360°) = (−_r_, _φ_ + (2 _n_ + 1) × 180°), where _n_ is an arbitrary [integer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer").[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_coordinates#cite_note-10) Moreover, the pole itself can be expressed as (0,_φ_) for any angle _φ_.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_coordinates#cite_note-11)

Where a unique representation is needed for any point besides the pole, it is usual to limit _r_ to positive numbers (_r_> 0) and _φ_ to either the [interval](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interval_(mathematics) "Interval (mathematics)")[0, 360°) or the interval (−180°,180°], which in radians are [0,2π) or (−π,π].[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_coordinates#cite_note-12) Another convention, in reference to the usual [codomain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Codomain "Codomain") of the [arctan function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverse_trigonometric_functions "Inverse trigonometric functions"), is to allow for arbitrary nonzero real values of the radial component and restrict the polar angle to (−90°,90°]. In all cases a unique azimuth for the pole (_r_ = 0) must be chosen, e.g., _φ_=0.

Converting between polar and Cartesian coordinates
--------------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Polar_coordinate_system&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Converting between polar and Cartesian coordinates")]

[![Image 4](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Polar_to_cartesian.svg/250px-Polar_to_cartesian.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Polar_to_cartesian.svg)

A diagram illustrating the relationship between polar and Cartesian coordinates.

The polar coordinates _r_ and _φ_ can be converted to the Cartesian coordinates _x_ and _y_ by using the [trigonometric functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trigonometric_function "Trigonometric function") sine and cosine:

![Image 5: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}x&=r\cos \varphi ,\\y&=r\sin \varphi .\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/32c7f6c81b7b59f338ab20da873bdd8e714f347b)

The Cartesian coordinates _x_ and _y_ can be converted to polar coordinates _r_ and _φ_ with _r_≥0 and _φ_ in the interval (−π, π] by:[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_coordinates#cite_note-13)![Image 6: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}r&={\sqrt {x^{2}+y^{2}}}=\operatorname {hypot} (x,y)\\\varphi &=\operatorname {atan2} (y,x),\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1e3a8927c8a4129125bc15d5b0c62d3f4056aae2) where hypot is the [Pythagorean sum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagorean_addition "Pythagorean addition") and [atan2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atan2 "Atan2") is a common variation on the [arctangent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arctangent "Arctangent") function defined as ![Image 7: {\displaystyle \operatorname {atan2} (y,x)={\begin{cases}\arctan \left({\frac {y}{x}}\right)&{\mbox{if }}x>0\\\arctan \left({\frac {y}{x}}\right)+\pi &{\mbox{if }}x<0{\mbox{ and }}y\geq 0\\\arctan \left({\frac {y}{x}}\right)-\pi &{\mbox{if }}x<0{\mbox{ and }}y<0\\{\frac {\pi }{2}}&{\mbox{if }}x=0{\mbox{ and }}y>0\\-{\frac {\pi }{2}}&{\mbox{if }}x=0{\mbox{ and }}y<0\\{\text{undefined}}&{\mbox{if }}x=0{\mbox{ and }}y=0.\end{cases}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/020a176bd95008f91e938bb68e78eb0f3b4be9c4)

If _r_ is calculated first as above, then this formula for _φ_ may be stated more simply using the [arccosine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arccosine "Arccosine") function: ![Image 8: {\displaystyle \varphi ={\begin{cases}\arccos \left({\frac {x}{r}}\right)&{\mbox{if }}y\geq 0{\mbox{ and }}r\neq 0\\-\arccos \left({\frac {x}{r}}\right)&{\mbox{if }}y<0\\{\text{undefined}}&{\mbox{if }}r=0.\end{cases}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3f358ec57db4fdf72027c6003fe741c65ae335a9)

[![Image 9](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Imaginarynumber2.svg/250px-Imaginarynumber2.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Imaginarynumber2.svg)

An illustration of a complex number _z_ plotted on the complex plane

[![Image 10](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Euler%27s_formula.svg/250px-Euler%27s_formula.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Euler%27s_formula.svg)

An illustration of a complex number plotted on the complex plane using [Euler's formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler%27s_formula "Euler's formula")

Every [complex number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number") can be represented as a point in the [complex plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_plane "Complex plane"), and can therefore be expressed by specifying either the point's Cartesian coordinates (called rectangular or Cartesian form) or the point's polar coordinates (called polar form).

In polar form, the distance and angle coordinates are often referred to as the number's **magnitude** and **argument** respectively. Two complex numbers can be multiplied by adding their arguments and multiplying their magnitudes.

The complex number _z_ can be represented in rectangular form as ![Image 11: {\displaystyle z=x+iy}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/08e90bb6b36fef59c6113eed2a08f10d77240741) where _i_ is the [imaginary unit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imaginary_unit "Imaginary unit"), or can alternatively be written in polar form as ![Image 12: {\displaystyle z=r(\cos \varphi +i\sin \varphi )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9fe097f200e7ea38fe974bf69e6af9a50711f431) and from there, by [Euler's formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler%27s_formula "Euler's formula"),[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_coordinates#cite_note-14) as ![Image 13: {\displaystyle z=re^{i\varphi }=r\exp i\varphi .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fa4851a3817d44e19d4432ddd1d920a750cf299d) where _e_ is [Euler's number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/E_(mathematical_constant) "E (mathematical constant)"), and _φ_, expressed in radians, is the [principal value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principal_value "Principal value") of the complex number function [arg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Argument_(complex_analysis) "Argument (complex analysis)") applied to _x_ + _iy_. To convert between the rectangular and polar forms of a complex number, the conversion formulae given above can be used. Equivalent are the [cis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cis_(mathematics) "Cis (mathematics)") and [angle notations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle_notation "Angle notation"): ![Image 14: {\displaystyle z=r\operatorname {\mathrm {cis} } \varphi =r\angle \varphi .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ac4c954a9291a076a06678cb802ce1f2c091ee0a)

For the operations of [multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication "Multiplication"), [division](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_(mathematics) "Division (mathematics)"), [exponentiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponentiation "Exponentiation"), and [root extraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Root_extraction "Root extraction") of complex numbers, it is generally much simpler to work with complex numbers expressed in polar form rather than rectangular form. From the laws of exponentiation:

Polar equation of a curve
-------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Polar_coordinate_system&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Polar equation of a curve")]

[![Image 19](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Cartesian_to_polar.gif/250px-Cartesian_to_polar.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Cartesian_to_polar.gif)

A curve on the Cartesian plane can be mapped into polar coordinates. In this animation, ![Image 20: {\displaystyle y=\sin(6\!\cdot \!x)+2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf591d693bd935388272e00709852d7bf7546464) is mapped onto ![Image 21: {\displaystyle r=\sin(6\!\cdot \!\theta )+2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ce36e6b7aff8172fdcfd13cba463edd2841036e7). Click on image for details.

The equation defining a [plane curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_curve "Plane curve") expressed in polar coordinates is known as a _polar equation_. In many cases, such an equation can simply be specified by defining _r_ as a [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)") of _φ_. The resulting curve then consists of points of the form (_r_(_φ_),_φ_) and can be regarded as the [graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_of_a_function "Graph of a function") of the polar function _r_. Note that, in contrast to Cartesian coordinates, the independent variable _φ_ is the _second_ entry in the [ordered pair](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordered_pair "Ordered pair").

Different forms of [symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry "Symmetry") can be deduced from the equation of a polar function _r_:

*   If _r_(−_φ_) = _r_(_φ_) the curve will be symmetrical about the horizontal (0°/180°) ray;
*   If _r_(_π_ − _φ_) = _r_(_φ_) it will be symmetric about the vertical (90°/270°) ray:
*   If _r_(_φ_ − α) = _r_(_φ_) it will be [rotationally symmetric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_symmetry "Rotational symmetry") by α clockwise and counterclockwise about the pole.

Because of the circular nature of the polar coordinate system, many curves can be described by a rather simple polar equation, whereas their Cartesian form is much more intricate. Among the best known of these curves are the [polar rose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rose_(mathematics) "Rose (mathematics)"), [Archimedean spiral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archimedean_spiral "Archimedean spiral"), [lemniscate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lemniscate_of_Bernoulli "Lemniscate of Bernoulli"), [limaçon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lima%C3%A7on "Limaçon"), and [cardioid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardioid "Cardioid").

For the circle, line, and polar rose below, it is understood that there are no restrictions on the domain and range of the curve.

[![Image 22](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Circle_r%3D1.svg/250px-Circle_r%3D1.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Circle_r%3D1.svg)

A circle with equation _r_(_φ_) = 1

The general equation for a circle with a center at ![Image 23: {\displaystyle (r_{0},\gamma )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d5ba4978ce2510f55703b6912c4eac28c7b259f5) and radius _a_ is ![Image 24: {\displaystyle r^{2}-2rr_{0}\cos(\varphi -\gamma )+r_{0}^{2}=a^{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a5b2133629adf698f1cbbfb515b96bbf4b98854a)

This can be simplified in various ways, to conform to more specific cases, such as the equation ![Image 25: {\displaystyle r(\varphi )=a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e1bd9d6b55e284e3e39e44b031bab3545e094b8d) for a circle with a center at the pole and radius _a_.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_coordinates#cite_note-ping-15)

When _r_ 0 = _a_ or the origin lies on the circle, the equation becomes ![Image 26: {\displaystyle r=2a\cos(\varphi -\gamma ).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/afe766975258871bd446ee93b07c2e95cc7b4543)

In the general case, the equation can be solved for _r_, giving ![Image 27: {\displaystyle r=r_{0}\cos(\varphi -\gamma )+{\sqrt {a^{2}-r_{0}^{2}\sin ^{2}(\varphi -\gamma )}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d994793bd0b5f0b27d5f44c0ee5c358eea0dcf0e) The solution with a minus sign in front of the square root gives the same curve.

_Radial_ lines (those running through the pole) are represented by the equation ![Image 28: {\displaystyle \varphi =\gamma ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/69719ee225311b045858df8195025e200e1cd201) where ![Image 29: {\displaystyle \gamma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a223c880b0ce3da8f64ee33c4f0010beee400b1a) is the angle of elevation of the line; that is, ![Image 30: {\displaystyle \varphi =\arctan m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e619df44e8373642f59fbbd6624b14b2f8a2b92), where ![Image 31: {\displaystyle m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a07d98bb302f3856cbabc47b2b9016692e3f7bc) is the [slope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slope "Slope") of the line in the Cartesian coordinate system. The non-radial line that crosses the radial line ![Image 32: {\displaystyle \varphi =\gamma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d92fa30c8d0384ef454ffd19bdd96deb59227d03)[perpendicularly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular "Perpendicular") at the point ![Image 33: {\displaystyle (r_{0},\gamma )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d5ba4978ce2510f55703b6912c4eac28c7b259f5) has the equation ![Image 34: {\displaystyle r(\varphi )=r_{0}\sec(\varphi -\gamma ).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ef6da7aade8923fd88ff2e33b0770bacd37e37fc)

Otherwise stated ![Image 35: {\displaystyle (r_{0},\gamma )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d5ba4978ce2510f55703b6912c4eac28c7b259f5) is the point in which the tangent intersects the imaginary circle of radius ![Image 36: {\displaystyle r_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fb12fcfddb65e3d1e6a044215f6e833f0cd4337b)

[![Image 37](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Rose_2sin%284theta%29.svg/250px-Rose_2sin%284theta%29.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Rose_2sin(4theta).svg)

A polar rose with equation _r_(_φ_) = 2 sin 4 _φ_

A [polar rose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rose_(mathematics) "Rose (mathematics)") is a mathematical curve that looks like a petaled flower, and that can be expressed as a simple polar equation, ![Image 38: {\displaystyle r(\varphi )=a\cos \left(k\varphi +\gamma _{0}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a1253e5cbb9692700eebdb3f01dca8bd42d35744)

for any constant γ 0 (including 0). If _k_ is an integer, these equations will produce a _k_-petaled rose if _k_ is [odd](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Even_and_odd_numbers "Even and odd numbers"), or a 2 _k_-petaled rose if _k_ is even. If _k_ is rational, but not an integer, a rose-like shape may form but with overlapping petals. Note that these equations never define a rose with 2, 6, 10, 14, etc. petals. The [variable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variable_(math) "Variable (math)")_a_ directly represents the length or amplitude of the petals of the rose, while _k_ relates to their spatial frequency. The constant γ 0 can be regarded as a phase angle.

[![Image 39](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Spiral_of_Archimedes.svg/250px-Spiral_of_Archimedes.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Spiral_of_Archimedes.svg)

One arm of an Archimedean spiral with equation _r_(_φ_) = _φ_ / 2 _π_ for 0 <_φ_< 6 _π_

The [Archimedean spiral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archimedean_spiral "Archimedean spiral") is a spiral discovered by [Archimedes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archimedes "Archimedes") which can also be expressed as a simple polar equation. It is represented by the equation ![Image 40: {\displaystyle r(\varphi )=a+b\varphi .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b6bc07b3412232dc537a723cc05e6cb670c6bb08) Changing the parameter _a_ will turn the spiral, while _b_ controls the distance between the arms, which for a given spiral is always constant. The Archimedean spiral has two arms, one for _φ_> 0 and one for _φ_< 0. The two arms are smoothly connected at the pole. If _a_ = 0, taking the mirror image of one arm across the 90°/270° line will yield the other arm. This curve is notable as one of the first curves, after the [conic sections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conic_section "Conic section"), to be described in a mathematical treatise, and as a prime example of a curve best defined by a polar equation.

[![Image 41](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Elps-slr.svg/250px-Elps-slr.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Elps-slr.svg)

Ellipse, showing semi-latus rectum

A [conic section](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conic_section "Conic section") with one focus on the pole and the other somewhere on the 0° ray (so that the conic's [major axis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semi-major_axis "Semi-major axis") lies along the polar axis) is given by: ![Image 42: {\displaystyle r={\ell  \over {1-e\cos \varphi }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e9ca91efb44ae1c78c1a9344ec9d9c209cdc98a) where _e_ is the [eccentricity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eccentricity_(mathematics) "Eccentricity (mathematics)") and ![Image 43: {\displaystyle \ell }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f066e981e530bacc07efc6a10fa82deee985929e) is the [semi-latus rectum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semi-latus_rectum "Semi-latus rectum") (the perpendicular distance at a focus from the major axis to the curve). If _e_> 1, this equation defines a [hyperbola](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbola "Hyperbola"); if _e_ = 1, it defines a [parabola](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parabola "Parabola"); and if _e_< 1, it defines an [ellipse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse "Ellipse"). The special case _e_ = 0 of the latter results in a circle of the radius ![Image 44: {\displaystyle \ell }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f066e981e530bacc07efc6a10fa82deee985929e).

[![Image 45](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Quadratrix_animation.gif/250px-Quadratrix_animation.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Quadratrix_animation.gif)

A quadratrix in the first quadrant (_x, y_) is a curve with _y_ = ρ sin θ equal to the fraction of the quarter circle with radius _r_ determined by the radius through the curve point. Since this fraction is ![Image 46: {\displaystyle {\frac {2r\theta }{\pi }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4fd79159404d755ca42a659f176826407d1a2962), the curve is given by ![Image 47: {\displaystyle \rho (\theta )={\frac {2r\theta }{\pi \sin \theta }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5b44eee99d11b7f826a87ceec4d6a00d4683a3e7).[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_coordinates#cite_note-16)

[Calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus "Calculus") can be applied to equations expressed in polar coordinates.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_coordinates#cite_note-17)[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_coordinates#cite_note-18)

The angular coordinate _φ_ is expressed in radians throughout this section, which is the conventional choice when doing calculus.

### Differential calculus

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Polar_coordinate_system&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: Differential calculus")]

Using _x_ = _r_ cos _φ_ and _y_ = _r_ sin _φ_, one can derive a relationship between derivatives in Cartesian and polar coordinates. For a given function, _u_(_x_,_y_), it follows that (by computing its [total derivatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Total_derivative "Total derivative")) or ![Image 60: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}r{\frac {du}{dr}}&=r{\frac {\partial u}{\partial x}}\cos \varphi +r{\frac {\partial u}{\partial y}}\sin \varphi =x{\frac {\partial u}{\partial x}}+y{\frac {\partial u}{\partial y}},\\[2pt]{\frac {du}{d\varphi }}&=-{\frac {\partial u}{\partial x}}r\sin \varphi +{\frac {\partial u}{\partial y}}r\cos \varphi =-y{\frac {\partial u}{\partial x}}+x{\frac {\partial u}{\partial y}}.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/01d876b55918333fa5b3792a72f5254b82b86c63)

Hence, we have the following formula: ![Image 61: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}r{\frac {d}{dr}}&=x{\frac {\partial }{\partial x}}+y{\frac {\partial }{\partial y}}\\[2pt]{\frac {d}{d\varphi }}&=-y{\frac {\partial }{\partial x}}+x{\frac {\partial }{\partial y}}.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9f6d6d1a9e05c093275ecaf66033a4298e41fe1d)

Using the inverse coordinates transformation, an analogous reciprocal relationship can be derived between the derivatives. Given a function _u_(_r_,_φ_), it follows that ![Image 62: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}{\frac {du}{dx}}&={\frac {\partial u}{\partial r}}{\frac {\partial r}{\partial x}}+{\frac {\partial u}{\partial \varphi }}{\frac {\partial \varphi }{\partial x}},\\[2pt]{\frac {du}{dy}}&={\frac {\partial u}{\partial r}}{\frac {\partial r}{\partial y}}+{\frac {\partial u}{\partial \varphi }}{\frac {\partial \varphi }{\partial y}},\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f446e4f0df07ead889d7039cec3301472fc1f9ea) or ![Image 63: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}{\frac {du}{dx}}&={\frac {\partial u}{\partial r}}{\frac {x}{\sqrt {x^{2}+y^{2}}}}-{\frac {\partial u}{\partial \varphi }}{\frac {y}{x^{2}+y^{2}}}\\[2pt]&=\cos \varphi {\frac {\partial u}{\partial r}}-{\frac {1}{r}}\sin \varphi {\frac {\partial u}{\partial \varphi }},\\[2pt]{\frac {du}{dy}}&={\frac {\partial u}{\partial r}}{\frac {y}{\sqrt {x^{2}+y^{2}}}}+{\frac {\partial u}{\partial \varphi }}{\frac {x}{x^{2}+y^{2}}}\\[2pt]&=\sin \varphi {\frac {\partial u}{\partial r}}+{\frac {1}{r}}\cos \varphi {\frac {\partial u}{\partial \varphi }}.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8dcc66ee6a6250cc206f488bb56f976a9a8bd2e8)

Hence, we have the following formulae: ![Image 64: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}{\frac {d}{dx}}&=\cos \varphi {\frac {\partial }{\partial r}}-{\frac {1}{r}}\sin \varphi {\frac {\partial }{\partial \varphi }}\\[2pt]{\frac {d}{dy}}&=\sin \varphi {\frac {\partial }{\partial r}}+{\frac {1}{r}}\cos \varphi {\frac {\partial }{\partial \varphi }}.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a08bfe016a4d8e15a933032d077bcdf8c49d41e)

To find the Cartesian slope of the tangent line to a polar curve _r_(_φ_) at any given point, the curve is first expressed as a system of [parametric equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parametric_equations "Parametric equations"). ![Image 65: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}x&=r(\varphi )\cos \varphi \\y&=r(\varphi )\sin \varphi \end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d57e14dd98cc1b390d69a4f11c9b4086542b45f7)

[Differentiating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative "Derivative") both equations with respect to _φ_ yields ![Image 66: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}{\frac {dx}{d\varphi }}&=r'(\varphi )\cos \varphi -r(\varphi )\sin \varphi \\[2pt]{\frac {dy}{d\varphi }}&=r'(\varphi )\sin \varphi +r(\varphi )\cos \varphi .\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/341713e0b0d484e169fb6cc867ab6a6e31b7ca65)

Dividing the second equation by the first yields the Cartesian slope of the tangent line to the curve at the point (_r_(_φ_),_φ_): ![Image 67: {\displaystyle {\frac {dy}{dx}}={\frac {r'(\varphi )\sin \varphi +r(\varphi )\cos \varphi }{r'(\varphi )\cos \varphi -r(\varphi )\sin \varphi }}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ff730497ec3671286d63415d676817f8f14299e1)

For other useful formulas including divergence, gradient, and Laplacian in polar coordinates, see [curvilinear coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvilinear_coordinates "Curvilinear coordinates").

### Integral calculus (arc length)

The arc length (length of a line segment) defined by a polar function is found by the integration over the curve _r_(_φ_). Let _L_ denote this length along the curve starting from points _A_ through to point _B_, where these points correspond to _φ_ = _a_ and _φ_ = _b_ such that 0 <_b_ − _a_< 2 _π_. The length of _L_ is given by the following integral ![Image 68: {\displaystyle L=\int _{a}^{b}{\sqrt {\left[r(\varphi )\right]^{2}+\left[{\tfrac {dr(\varphi )}{d\varphi }}\right]^{2}}}d\varphi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/34f62c06ac2868b9ac160abe1e75a075cad9e261)

### Integral calculus (area)

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Polar_coordinate_system&action=edit&section=17 "Edit section: Integral calculus (area)")]

[![Image 69](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Polar_coordinates_integration_region.svg/250px-Polar_coordinates_integration_region.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Polar_coordinates_integration_region.svg)

The integration region _R_ is bounded by the curve _r_(_φ_) and the rays _φ_ = _a_ and _φ_ = _b_.

Let _R_ denote the region enclosed by a curve _r_(_φ_) and the rays _φ_ = _a_ and _φ_ = _b_, where 0 <_b_ − _a_ ≤ 2 π. Then, the area of _R_ is ![Image 70: {\displaystyle {\frac {1}{2}}\int _{a}^{b}\left[r(\varphi )\right]^{2}\,d\varphi .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9fc7958a00360514f36fabcac7cab0361c2ae068)

[![Image 71](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Polar_coordinates_integration_Riemann_sum.svg/250px-Polar_coordinates_integration_Riemann_sum.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Polar_coordinates_integration_Riemann_sum.svg)

The region _R_ is approximated by _n_ sectors (here, _n_ = 5).

[![Image 72](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Planimeter.jpg/250px-Planimeter.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Planimeter.jpg)

A [planimeter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planimeter "Planimeter"), which mechanically computes polar integrals

This result can be found as follows. First, the interval [_a_, _b_] is divided into _n_ subintervals, where _n_ is some positive integer. Thus Δ _φ_, the angle measure of each subinterval, is equal to _b_ − _a_ (the total angle measure of the interval), divided by _n_, the number of subintervals. For each subinterval _i_ = 1, 2, ..., _n_, let _φ_ _i_ be the midpoint of the subinterval, and construct a [sector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circular_sector "Circular sector") with the center at the pole, radius _r_(_φ_ _i_), [central angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_angle "Central angle") Δ _φ_ and arc length _r_(_φ_ _i_)Δ _φ_. The area of each constructed sector is therefore equal to ![Image 73: {\displaystyle \left[r(\varphi _{i})\right]^{2}\pi \cdot {\frac {\Delta \varphi }{2\pi }}={\frac {1}{2}}\left[r(\varphi _{i})\right]^{2}\Delta \varphi .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ec0cb32735f6a73076e1db62726c0eace3f000f6) Hence, the total area of all of the sectors is ![Image 74: {\displaystyle \sum _{i=1}^{n}{\tfrac {1}{2}}r(\varphi _{i})^{2}\,\Delta \varphi .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3102375792a188288a60e6138e3eb3497d54eed4)

As the number of subintervals _n_ is increased, the approximation of the area improves. Taking _n_ → ∞, the sum becomes the [Riemann sum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_sum "Riemann sum") for the above integral.

A mechanical device that computes area integrals is the [planimeter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planimeter "Planimeter"), which measures the area of plane figures by tracing them out: this replicates integration in polar coordinates by adding a joint so that the 2-element [linkage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linkage_(mechanical) "Linkage (mechanical)") effects [Green's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green%27s_theorem "Green's theorem"), converting the quadratic polar integral to a linear integral.

Using [Cartesian coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinates "Cartesian coordinates"), an infinitesimal area element can be calculated as _dA_ = _dx_ _dy_. The [substitution rule for multiple integrals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integration_by_substitution#Substitution_for_multiple_variables "Integration by substitution") states that, when using other coordinates, the [Jacobian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacobian_matrix_and_determinant "Jacobian matrix and determinant") determinant of the coordinate conversion formula has to be considered: ![Image 75: {\displaystyle J=\det {\frac {\partial (x,y)}{\partial (r,\varphi )}}={\begin{vmatrix}{\frac {\partial x}{\partial r}}&{\frac {\partial x}{\partial \varphi }}\\[2pt]{\frac {\partial y}{\partial r}}&{\frac {\partial y}{\partial \varphi }}\end{vmatrix}}={\begin{vmatrix}\cos \varphi &-r\sin \varphi \\\sin \varphi &r\cos \varphi \end{vmatrix}}=r\cos ^{2}\varphi +r\sin ^{2}\varphi =r.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/52a1293fcb0e90b77017c0d9176c62aa06615b9b)

Hence, an area element in polar coordinates can be written as ![Image 76: {\displaystyle dA=dx\,dy\ =J\,dr\,d\varphi =r\,dr\,d\varphi .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/204ed02c6ad48093830a86a8f76829a384e16bac)

Now, a function, that is given in polar coordinates, can be integrated as follows: ![Image 77: {\displaystyle \iint _{R}f(x,y)\,dA=\int _{a}^{b}\int _{0}^{r(\varphi )}f(r,\varphi )\,r\,dr\,d\varphi .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/91b80d79c6bc8a4a265145bedfd8cdbc864eba37)

Here, _R_ is the same region as above, namely, the region enclosed by a curve _r_(_φ_) and the rays _φ_ = _a_ and _φ_ = _b_. The formula for the area of _R_ is retrieved by taking _f_ identically equal to 1.

[![Image 78](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/E%5E%28-x%5E2%29.svg/250px-E%5E%28-x%5E2%29.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:E%5E(-x%5E2).svg)

A graph of ![Image 79: {\displaystyle f(x)=e^{-x^{2}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1bed0b77b34cab03996deb42d464becab2f05636) and the area between the function and the ![Image 80: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4)-axis, which is equal to ![Image 81: {\displaystyle {\sqrt {\pi }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7ae18ec124928c74818b516e6350ca9610966c6e).

A more surprising application of this result yields the [Gaussian integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaussian_integral "Gaussian integral"): ![Image 82: {\displaystyle \int _{-\infty }^{\infty }e^{-x^{2}}\,dx={\sqrt {\pi }}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b06d446e3c625f48f318811eabdfe5902b11508a)

[Vector calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_calculus "Vector calculus") can also be applied to polar coordinates. For a planar motion, let ![Image 83: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eca0f46511c4c986c48b254073732c0bd98ae0c1) be the position vector (_r_ cos(_φ_), _r_ sin(_φ_)), with _r_ and _φ_ depending on time _t_.

We define an [orthonormal basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthonormal_basis "Orthonormal basis") with three unit vectors: _radial, transverse, and normal directions_. The _radial direction_ is defined by normalizing ![Image 84: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eca0f46511c4c986c48b254073732c0bd98ae0c1): ![Image 85: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathbf {r} }}=(\cos(\varphi ),\sin(\varphi ))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dea4252337486f32086a8eca580118da61998da8) Radial and velocity directions span the _plane of the motion_, whose normal direction is denoted ![Image 86: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathbf {k} }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/45c643fb60ea71542145705fe801c7ab8c769507): ![Image 87: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathbf {k} }}={\hat {\mathbf {v} }}\times {\hat {\mathbf {r} }}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bb0b0fd52a60edf991ec517a0079d1404c159f84) The _transverse direction_ is perpendicular to both radial and normal directions: ![Image 88: {\displaystyle {\hat {\boldsymbol {\varphi }}}=(-\sin(\varphi ),\cos(\varphi ))={\hat {\mathbf {k} }}\times {\hat {\mathbf {r} }}\ ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38836592838cbd2616f64f824d7f20f76c6a1e28)

Then ![Image 89: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\mathbf {r} &=(x,\ y)=r(\cos \varphi ,\ \sin \varphi )=r{\hat {\mathbf {r} }}\ ,\\[1.5ex]{\dot {\mathbf {r} }}&=\left({\dot {x}},\ {\dot {y}}\right)={\dot {r}}(\cos \varphi ,\ \sin \varphi )+r{\dot {\varphi }}(-\sin \varphi ,\ \cos \varphi )={\dot {r}}{\hat {\mathbf {r} }}+r{\dot {\varphi }}{\hat {\boldsymbol {\varphi }}}\ ,\\[1.5ex]{\ddot {\mathbf {r} }}&=\left({\ddot {x}},\ {\ddot {y}}\right)\\[1ex]&={\ddot {r}}(\cos \varphi ,\ \sin \varphi )+2{\dot {r}}{\dot {\varphi }}(-\sin \varphi ,\ \cos \varphi )+r{\ddot {\varphi }}(-\sin \varphi ,\ \cos \varphi )-r{\dot {\varphi }}^{2}(\cos \varphi ,\ \sin \varphi )\\[1ex]&=\left({\ddot {r}}-r{\dot {\varphi }}^{2}\right){\hat {\mathbf {r} }}+\left(r{\ddot {\varphi }}+2{\dot {r}}{\dot {\varphi }}\right){\hat {\boldsymbol {\varphi }}}\\[1ex]&=\left({\ddot {r}}-r{\dot {\varphi }}^{2}\right){\hat {\mathbf {r} }}+{\frac {1}{r}}\;{\frac {d}{dt}}\left(r^{2}{\dot {\varphi }}\right){\hat {\boldsymbol {\varphi }}}.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/272fcf3c718a928afd6ddced5527376aa6b5e58b)

#### Centrifugal and Coriolis terms

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Polar_coordinate_system&action=edit&section=20 "Edit section: Centrifugal and Coriolis terms")]

[![Image 92](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Position_vector_plane_polar_coords.svg/120px-Position_vector_plane_polar_coords.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Position_vector_plane_polar_coords.svg)

Position vector **r**, always points radially from the origin.

[![Image 93](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Velocity_vector_plane_polar_coords.svg/250px-Velocity_vector_plane_polar_coords.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Velocity_vector_plane_polar_coords.svg)

Velocity vector **v**, always tangent to the path of motion.

[![Image 94](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Acceleration_vector_plane_polar_coords.svg/250px-Acceleration_vector_plane_polar_coords.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Acceleration_vector_plane_polar_coords.svg)

Acceleration vector **a**, not parallel to the radial motion but offset by the angular and Coriolis accelerations, nor tangent to the path but offset by the centripetal and radial accelerations.

Kinematic vectors in plane polar coordinates. Notice the setup is not restricted to 2d space, but a plane in any higher dimension.

The term ![Image 95: {\displaystyle r{\dot {\varphi }}^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4a43d54e4ab6b4e75edf3915099451d1d87ce4cd) is sometimes referred to as the _centripetal acceleration_, and the term ![Image 96: {\displaystyle 2{\dot {r}}{\dot {\varphi }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/31062c81fca84b3560ed160d47ae3b0850001845) as the _Coriolis acceleration_. For example, see Shankar.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_coordinates#cite_note-Shankar-19)

Note: these terms, that appear when acceleration is expressed in polar coordinates, are a mathematical consequence of differentiation; they appear whenever polar coordinates are used. In planar particle dynamics these accelerations appear when setting up Newton's [second law of motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_second_law "Newton's second law") in a rotating frame of reference. Here these extra terms are often called [fictitious forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fictitious_force "Fictitious force"); fictitious because they are simply a result of a change in coordinate frame. That does not mean they do not exist, rather they exist only in the rotating frame.

[![Image 97](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Co-rotating_frame_vector.svg/250px-Co-rotating_frame_vector.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Co-rotating_frame_vector.svg)

Inertial frame of reference _S_ and instantaneous non-inertial co-rotating frame of reference _S′_. The co-rotating frame rotates at angular rate Ω equal to the rate of rotation of the particle about the origin of _S′_ at the particular moment _t_. Particle is located at vector position _r_(_t_) and unit vectors are shown in the radial direction to the particle from the origin, and also in the direction of increasing angle _ϕ_ normal to the radial direction. These unit vectors need not be related to the tangent and normal to the path. Also, the radial distance _r_ need not be related to the radius of curvature of the path.

For a particle in planar motion, one approach to attaching physical significance to these terms is based on the concept of an instantaneous _co-rotating frame of reference_.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_coordinates#cite_note-Taylor-20) To define a co-rotating frame, first an origin is selected from which the distance _r_(_t_) to the particle is defined. An axis of rotation is set up that is perpendicular to the plane of motion of the particle, and passing through this origin. Then, at the selected moment _t_, the rate of rotation of the co-rotating frame Ω is made to match the rate of rotation of the particle about this axis, _dφ_/_dt_. Next, the terms in the acceleration in the inertial frame are related to those in the co-rotating frame. Let the location of the particle in the inertial frame be (_r_(_t_), _φ_(_t_)), and in the co-rotating frame be (_r_′(t), _φ_′(t)). Because the co-rotating frame rotates at the same rate as the particle, _dφ_′/_dt_ = 0. The fictitious centrifugal force in the co-rotating frame is _mr_ Ω 2, radially outward. The velocity of the particle in the co-rotating frame also is radially outward, because _dφ_′/_dt_ = 0. The _fictitious Coriolis force_ therefore has a value −2 _m_(_dr_/_dt_)Ω, pointed in the direction of increasing _φ_ only. Thus, using these forces in Newton's second law we find: ![Image 98: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} +\mathbf {F} _{\text{cf}}+\mathbf {F} _{\text{Cor}}=m{\ddot {\mathbf {r} }}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e05b00a73ed51fd8991ceb02d9ef1711a581355f) where over dots represent derivatives with respect to time, and **F** is the net real force (as opposed to the fictitious forces). In terms of components, this vector equation becomes: ![Image 99: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}F_{r}+mr\Omega ^{2}&=m{\ddot {r}}\\F_{\varphi }-2m{\dot {r}}\Omega &=mr{\ddot {\varphi }}\ ,\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3a86cde193c5bce5cdbac8827bf824f44a6a188) which can be compared to the equations for the inertial frame: ![Image 100: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}F_{r}&=m{\ddot {r}}-mr{\dot {\varphi }}^{2}\\F_{\varphi }&=mr{\ddot {\varphi }}+2m{\dot {r}}{\dot {\varphi }}\ .\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c4a698bd84bbff8bdf676c2a723b63abad1a7f78)

This comparison, plus the recognition that by the definition of the co-rotating frame at time _t_ it has a rate of rotation Ω = _dφ_/_dt_, shows that we can interpret the terms in the acceleration (multiplied by the mass of the particle) as found in the inertial frame as the negative of the centrifugal and Coriolis forces that would be seen in the instantaneous, non-inertial co-rotating frame.

For general motion of a particle (as opposed to simple circular motion), the centrifugal and Coriolis forces in a particle's frame of reference commonly are referred to the instantaneous [osculating circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osculating_circle "Osculating circle") of its motion, not to a fixed center of polar coordinates. For more detail, see [centripetal force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_force#Local_coordinates "Centripetal force").

The polar coordinate system is extended into three dimensions with two different coordinate systems, the [cylindrical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylindrical_coordinate_system "Cylindrical coordinate system") and [spherical coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_coordinate_system "Spherical coordinate system").

Polar coordinates are two-dimensional and thus they can be used only where point positions lie on a single two-dimensional plane. They are most appropriate in any context where the phenomenon being considered is inherently tied to direction and length from a center point. For instance, the examples above show how elementary polar equations suffice to define curves—such as the Archimedean spiral—whose equation in the Cartesian coordinate system would be much more intricate. Moreover, many physical systems—such as those concerned with bodies moving around a central point or with phenomena originating from a central point—are simpler and more intuitive to model using polar coordinates. The initial motivation for the introduction of the polar system was the study of [circular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circular_motion "Circular motion") and [orbital motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_motion "Orbital motion").

### Position and navigation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Polar_coordinate_system&action=edit&section=25 "Edit section: Position and navigation")]

Polar coordinates are used often in [navigation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Navigation "Navigation") as the destination or direction of travel can be given as an angle and distance from the object being considered. For instance, [aircraft](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aircraft "Aircraft") use a slightly modified version of the polar coordinates for navigation. In this system, the one generally used for any sort of navigation, the 0° ray is generally called heading 360, and the angles continue in a clockwise direction, rather than counterclockwise, as in the mathematical system. Heading 360 corresponds to [magnetic north](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_north "Magnetic north"), while headings 90, 180, and 270 correspond to magnetic east, south, and west, respectively.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_coordinates#cite_note-21) Thus, an aircraft traveling 5 nautical miles due east will be traveling 5 units at heading 90 (read [zero-niner-zero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ICAO_spelling_alphabet "ICAO spelling alphabet") by [air traffic control](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Air_traffic_control "Air traffic control")).[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_coordinates#cite_note-22)

Systems displaying [radial symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radial_symmetry "Radial symmetry") provide natural settings for the polar coordinate system, with the central point acting as the pole. A prime example of this usage is the [groundwater flow equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Groundwater_flow_equation "Groundwater flow equation") when applied to radially symmetric wells. Systems with a [radial force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_force "Central force") are also good candidates for the use of the polar coordinate system. These systems include [gravitational fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitation "Gravitation"), which obey the [inverse-square law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverse-square_law "Inverse-square law"), as well as systems with [point sources](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_source "Point source"), such as [radio antennas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antenna_(radio) "Antenna (radio)").

Radially asymmetric systems may also be modeled with polar coordinates. For example, a [microphone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microphone "Microphone")'s [pickup pattern](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microphone_pick_up_patterns "Microphone pick up patterns") illustrates its proportional response to an incoming sound from a given direction, and these patterns can be represented as polar curves. The curve for a standard cardioid microphone, the most common unidirectional microphone, can be represented as _r_ = 0.5 + 0.5sin(_ϕ_) at its target design frequency.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_coordinates#cite_note-23) The pattern shifts toward omnidirectionality at lower frequencies.
