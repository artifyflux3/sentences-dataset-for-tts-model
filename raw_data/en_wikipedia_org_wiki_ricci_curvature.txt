In [differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry"), the **Ricci curvature tensor**, named after [Gregorio Ricci-Curbastro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gregorio_Ricci-Curbastro "Gregorio Ricci-Curbastro"), is a geometric object that is determined by a choice of [Riemannian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_manifold "Riemannian manifold") or [pseudo-Riemannian metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudo-Riemannian_manifold "Pseudo-Riemannian manifold") on a [manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manifold "Manifold"). It can be considered, broadly, as a measure of the degree to which the geometry of a given metric tensor differs locally from that of ordinary [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space") or [pseudo-Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudo-Euclidean_space "Pseudo-Euclidean space").

The Ricci tensor can be characterized by measurement of how a shape is deformed as one moves along [geodesics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic "Geodesic") in the space. In [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity"), which involves the pseudo-Riemannian setting, this is reflected by the presence of the Ricci tensor in the [Raychaudhuri equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raychaudhuri_equation "Raychaudhuri equation"). Partly for this reason, the [Einstein field equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_field_equations "Einstein field equations") propose that spacetime can be described by a pseudo-Riemannian metric, with a strikingly simple relationship between the Ricci tensor and the matter content of the universe.

Like the metric tensor, the Ricci tensor assigns to each [tangent space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_space "Tangent space") of the manifold a [symmetric bilinear form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetric_bilinear_form "Symmetric bilinear form").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_curvature#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBesse198743-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_curvature#cite_note-2) Broadly, one could analogize the role of the Ricci curvature in Riemannian geometry to that of the [Laplacian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laplace_operator "Laplace operator") in the analysis of functions; in this analogy, the [Riemann curvature tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_curvature_tensor "Riemann curvature tensor"), of which the Ricci curvature is a natural by-product, would correspond to the full matrix of second derivatives of a function. However, there are [other ways](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laplace%E2%80%93Beltrami_operator "Laplace–Beltrami operator") to draw the same analogy.

For [three-dimensional manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Low-dimensional_topology "Low-dimensional topology"), the Ricci tensor contains all of the information that in higher dimensions is encoded by the more complicated [Riemann curvature tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_curvature_tensor "Riemann curvature tensor"). In part, this simplicity allows for the application of many geometric and analytic tools, which led to the [solution of the Poincaré conjecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solution_of_the_Poincar%C3%A9_conjecture "Solution of the Poincaré conjecture") through the work of [Richard S. Hamilton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_S._Hamilton "Richard S. Hamilton") and [Grigori Perelman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grigori_Perelman "Grigori Perelman").

In differential geometry, the determination of lower bounds on the Ricci tensor on a Riemannian manifold would allow one to extract global geometric and topological information by comparison (cf. [comparison theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comparison_theorem "Comparison theorem")) with the geometry of a constant curvature [space form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_form "Space form"). This is since lower bounds on the Ricci tensor can be successfully used in studying the length functional in Riemannian geometry, as first shown in 1941 via [Myers's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Myers%27s_theorem "Myers's theorem").

One common source of the Ricci tensor is that it arises whenever one commutes the covariant derivative with the tensor Laplacian. This, for instance, explains its presence in the [Bochner formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bochner%27s_formula "Bochner's formula"), which is used ubiquitously in Riemannian geometry. For example, this formula explains why the gradient estimates due to [Shing-Tung Yau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shing-Tung_Yau "Shing-Tung Yau") (and their developments such as the Cheng–Yau and Li–Yau inequalities) nearly always depend on a lower bound for the Ricci curvature.

In 2007, [John Lott](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Lott_(mathematician) "John Lott (mathematician)"), [Karl-Theodor Sturm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karl-Theodor_Sturm "Karl-Theodor Sturm"), and [Cedric Villani](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cedric_Villani "Cedric Villani") demonstrated decisively that lower bounds on Ricci curvature can be understood entirely in terms of the metric space structure of a Riemannian manifold, together with its volume form.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_curvature#cite_note-FOOTNOTELottVillani2006-3) This established a deep link between Ricci curvature and [Wasserstein geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wasserstein_metric "Wasserstein metric") and [optimal transport](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transportation_theory_(mathematics) "Transportation theory (mathematics)"), which is presently the subject of much research.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

### Comparison of the definitions

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ricci_curvature&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Comparison of the definitions")]

The two above definitions are identical. The formulas defining ![Image 71: {\displaystyle \Gamma _{ij}^{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54d6c913ed873bfa56bdf2e3dab2b3ea9225638a) and ![Image 72: {\displaystyle R_{ij}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/50382df1fd3bebf0baf22e2eab56a7a471d6621a) in the coordinate approach have an exact parallel in the formulas defining the Levi-Civita connection, and the Riemann curvature via the Levi-Civita connection. Arguably, the definitions directly using local coordinates are preferable, since the "crucial property" of the Riemann tensor mentioned above requires ![Image 73: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) to be Hausdorff in order to hold. By contrast, the local coordinate approach only requires a smooth atlas. It is also somewhat easier to connect the "invariance" philosophy underlying the local approach with the methods of constructing more exotic geometric objects, such as [spinor fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spinor_field "Spinor field").

The complicated formula defining ![Image 74: {\displaystyle R_{ij}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/50382df1fd3bebf0baf22e2eab56a7a471d6621a) in the introductory section is the same as that in the following section. The only difference is that terms have been grouped so that it is easy to see that ⁠![Image 75: {\displaystyle R_{ij}=R_{ji}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4e56679840eab3d0ae741cc52d631aed0048f2da)⁠.

As can be seen from the symmetries of the Riemann curvature tensor, the Ricci tensor of a Riemannian manifold is [symmetric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetric_tensor "Symmetric tensor"), in the sense that ![Image 76: {\displaystyle \operatorname {Ric} (X,Y)=\operatorname {Ric} (Y,X)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2e49a83c9124a4f992b8a44d25298e488ab7a89f) for all ⁠![Image 77: {\displaystyle X,Y\in T_{p}M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/680e6192bc908c6811be1ae992313b42136b4b5e)⁠.

It thus follows linear-algebraically that the Ricci tensor is completely determined by knowing the quantity ![Image 78: {\displaystyle \operatorname {Ric} (X,X)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3e1e6dcb3a27e5f47f271c71c7497b4b373093de) for all vectors ![Image 79: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) of unit length. This function on the set of unit tangent vectors is often also called the **Ricci curvature**, since knowing it is equivalent to knowing the Ricci curvature tensor.

The Ricci curvature is determined by the [sectional curvatures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sectional_curvature "Sectional curvature") of a Riemannian manifold, but generally contains less information. Indeed, if ![Image 80: {\displaystyle \xi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e0b461aaf61091abd5d2c808931c48b8ff9647db) is a vector of unit length on a Riemannian ![Image 81: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)-manifold, then ![Image 82: {\displaystyle \operatorname {Ric} (\xi ,\xi )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/88612562a8e20ad82ac10748f7953fbfa06a0ff8) is precisely ![Image 83: {\displaystyle (n-1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/df88c6333caaf6471cf277f24b802ff9931b133e) times the average value of the sectional curvature, taken over all the 2-planes containing ⁠![Image 84: {\displaystyle \xi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e0b461aaf61091abd5d2c808931c48b8ff9647db)⁠. There is an ![Image 85: {\displaystyle (n-2)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3a57c71b5e6c79de3b7fb8d7d0a918b434a160f4)-dimensional family of such 2-planes, and so only in dimensions 2 and 3 does the Ricci tensor determine the full curvature tensor. A notable exception is when the manifold is given a priori as a [hypersurface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypersurface "Hypersurface") of [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space"). The [second fundamental form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_fundamental_form "Second fundamental form"), which determines the full curvature via the [Gauss–Codazzi equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauss%E2%80%93Codazzi_equations "Gauss–Codazzi equations"), is itself determined by the Ricci tensor and the [principal directions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principal_curvature "Principal curvature") of the hypersurface are also the [eigendirections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eigenvalues_and_eigenvectors "Eigenvalues and eigenvectors") of the Ricci tensor. The tensor was introduced by Ricci for this reason.

As can be seen from the second Bianchi identity, one has ![Image 86: {\displaystyle \operatorname {div} \operatorname {Ric} ={\frac {1}{2}}dR,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d35614940a40c16ad0188fba657dbbd0b31b9fbc) where ![Image 87: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) is the [scalar curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_curvature "Scalar curvature"), defined in local coordinates as ⁠![Image 88: {\displaystyle g^{ij}R_{ij}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/89c7df7d5e92b447b61cb1619f166cc66c777cbb)⁠. This is often called the contracted second Bianchi identity.

Direct geometric meaning
------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ricci_curvature&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Direct geometric meaning")]

Near any point ![Image 89: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) in a Riemannian manifold ⁠![Image 90: {\displaystyle \left(M,g\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b487026b0c2aae9757f9fdea4d58704078e98f48)⁠, one can define preferred local coordinates, called [geodesic normal coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic_normal_coordinates "Geodesic normal coordinates"). These are adapted to the metric so that geodesics through ![Image 91: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) correspond to straight lines through the origin, in such a manner that the geodesic distance from ![Image 92: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) corresponds to the Euclidean distance from the origin. In these coordinates, the metric tensor is well-approximated by the Euclidean metric, in the precise sense that ![Image 93: {\displaystyle g_{ij}=\delta _{ij}+O\left(|x|^{2}\right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/09be2fdb293eca37c4c8c189062d89bca1ecd6d5)

In fact, by taking the [Taylor expansion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taylor_expansion "Taylor expansion") of the metric applied to a [Jacobi field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacobi_field "Jacobi field") along a radial geodesic in the normal coordinate system, one has ![Image 94: {\displaystyle g_{ij}=\delta _{ij}-{\frac {1}{3}}R_{ikjl}x^{k}x^{l}+O\left(|x|^{3}\right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7d6d0437fbc011700b8cb28d17a89c015eb37dc2)

In these coordinates, the metric [volume element](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume_element "Volume element") then has the following expansion at p: ![Image 95: {\displaystyle d\mu _{g}=\left[1-{\frac {1}{6}}R_{jk}x^{j}x^{k}+O\left(|x|^{3}\right)\right]d\mu _{\text{Euclidean}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ab57d1e950c7ef3535b9ddfdb905bc09c433d09b) which follows by expanding the square root of the [determinant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Determinant "Determinant") of the metric.

Thus, if the Ricci curvature ![Image 96: {\displaystyle \operatorname {Ric} (\xi ,\xi )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/88612562a8e20ad82ac10748f7953fbfa06a0ff8) is positive in the direction of a vector ⁠![Image 97: {\displaystyle \xi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e0b461aaf61091abd5d2c808931c48b8ff9647db)⁠, the conical region in ![Image 98: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) swept out by a tightly focused family of geodesic segments of length ![Image 99: {\displaystyle \varepsilon }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a30c89172e5b88edbd45d3e2772c7f5e562e5173) emanating from ![Image 100: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36), with initial velocity inside a small cone about ⁠![Image 101: {\displaystyle \xi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e0b461aaf61091abd5d2c808931c48b8ff9647db)⁠, will have smaller volume than the corresponding conical region in Euclidean space, at least provided that ![Image 102: {\displaystyle \varepsilon }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a30c89172e5b88edbd45d3e2772c7f5e562e5173) is sufficiently small. Similarly, if the Ricci curvature is negative in the direction of a given vector ⁠![Image 103: {\displaystyle \xi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e0b461aaf61091abd5d2c808931c48b8ff9647db)⁠, such a conical region in the manifold will instead have larger volume than it would in Euclidean space.

The Ricci curvature is essentially an average of curvatures in the planes including ⁠![Image 104: {\displaystyle \xi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e0b461aaf61091abd5d2c808931c48b8ff9647db)⁠. Thus if a cone emitted with an initially circular (or spherical) cross-section becomes distorted into an ellipse ([ellipsoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipsoid "Ellipsoid")), it is possible for the volume distortion to vanish if the distortions along the [principal axes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principal_axis_theorem "Principal axis theorem") counteract one another. The Ricci curvature would then vanish along ⁠![Image 105: {\displaystyle \xi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e0b461aaf61091abd5d2c808931c48b8ff9647db)⁠. In physical applications, the presence of a nonvanishing [sectional curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sectional_curvature "Sectional curvature") does not necessarily indicate the presence of any mass locally; if an initially circular cross-section of a cone of [worldlines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Worldline "Worldline") later becomes elliptical, without changing its volume, then this is due to tidal effects from a mass at some other location.

Ricci curvature plays an important role in [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity"), where it is the key term in the [Einstein field equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_field_equations "Einstein field equations").

Ricci curvature also appears in the [Ricci flow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_flow "Ricci flow") equation, first introduced by [Richard S. Hamilton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_S._Hamilton "Richard S. Hamilton") in 1982, where certain one-parameter families of Riemannian metrics are singled out as solutions of a geometrically defined partial differential equation. In [harmonic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harmonic_coordinates "Harmonic coordinates") local coordinates the Ricci tensor can be expressed as[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_curvature#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChowKnopf2004Lemma_3.32-4)![Image 106: {\displaystyle R_{ij}=-{\frac {1}{2}}\Delta \left(g_{ij}\right)+{\text{lower-order terms}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3ab0e305f020fe9f002a929daef1b791660f4a85) where ![Image 107: {\displaystyle g_{ij}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e7c1130c3dec178129b287a3672c72f88e773832) are the components of the metric tensor and ![Image 108: {\displaystyle \Delta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/32769037c408874e1890f77554c65f39c523ebe2) is the [Laplace–Beltrami operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laplace%E2%80%93Beltrami_operator "Laplace–Beltrami operator"). This fact motivates the introduction of the [Ricci flow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_flow "Ricci flow") equation as a natural extension of the [heat equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat_equation "Heat equation") for the metric. Since heat tends to spread through a solid until the body reaches an equilibrium state of constant temperature, if one is given a manifold, the Ricci flow may be hoped to produce an 'equilibrium' Riemannian metric that is [Einstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_metric "Einstein metric") or of constant curvature. However, such a clean "convergence" picture cannot be achieved since many manifolds cannot support such metrics. A detailed study of the nature of solutions of the Ricci flow, due principally to Hamilton and [Grigori Perelman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grigori_Perelman "Grigori Perelman"), shows that the types of "singularities" that occur along a Ricci flow, corresponding to the failure of convergence, encodes deep information about 3-dimensional topology. The culmination of this work was a proof of the [geometrization conjecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometrization_conjecture "Geometrization conjecture") first proposed by [William Thurston](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Thurston "William Thurston") in the 1970s, which can be thought of as a classification of compact 3-manifolds.

On a [Kähler manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K%C3%A4hler_manifold "Kähler manifold"), the Ricci curvature determines the first [Chern class](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chern_class "Chern class") of the manifold (mod torsion). However, the Ricci curvature has no analogous topological interpretation on a generic Riemannian manifold.

Global geometry and topology
----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ricci_curvature&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Global geometry and topology")]

Here is a short list of global results concerning manifolds with positive Ricci curvature; see also [classical theorems of Riemannian geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_geometry#Local_to_global_theorems "Riemannian geometry"). Briefly, positive Ricci curvature of a Riemannian manifold has strong topological consequences, while (for dimension at least 3), negative Ricci curvature has _no_ topological implications. (The Ricci curvature is said to be **positive** if the Ricci curvature function ![Image 109: {\displaystyle \operatorname {Ric} (\xi ,\xi )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/88612562a8e20ad82ac10748f7953fbfa06a0ff8) is positive on the set of non-zero tangent vectors ⁠![Image 110: {\displaystyle \xi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e0b461aaf61091abd5d2c808931c48b8ff9647db)⁠.) Some results are also known for pseudo-Riemannian manifolds.

1.   [Myers's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Myers%27s_theorem "Myers's theorem") (1941) states that if the Ricci curvature is bounded from below on a complete Riemannian _n_-manifold by ⁠![Image 111: {\displaystyle (n-1)k>0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5d594888f07e93eacc7a0e0ea0b796d86b1b93e4)⁠, then the manifold has diameter ⁠![Image 112: {\displaystyle \leq \pi /{\sqrt {k}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0df75454e4cbe1555f87cb407af3b208e645c392)⁠. By a covering-space argument, it follows that any compact manifold of positive Ricci curvature must have finite [fundamental group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_group "Fundamental group"). [Cheng](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shiu-Yuen_Cheng "Shiu-Yuen Cheng") (1975) showed that, in this setting, equality in the diameter inequality occurs if only if the manifold is [isometric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isometry "Isometry") to a sphere of a constant curvature ⁠![Image 113: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40)⁠.
2.   The [Bishop–Gromov inequality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bishop%E2%80%93Gromov_inequality "Bishop–Gromov inequality") states that if a complete ![Image 114: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)-dimensional Riemannian manifold has non-negative Ricci curvature, then the volume of a geodesic ball is less than or equal to the volume of a geodesic ball of the same radius in Euclidean ![Image 115: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)-space. Moreover, if ![Image 116: {\displaystyle v_{p}(R)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/166bcd64b415f8a251b485f49cd72d9f4dc869f1) denotes the volume of the ball with center ![Image 117: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) and radius ![Image 118: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) in the manifold and ![Image 119: {\displaystyle V(R)=c_{n}R^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9c251265e7addee9f7a4a25e6db1b3db20b3eec9) denotes the volume of the ball of radius ![Image 120: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) in Euclidean ![Image 121: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)-space then the function ![Image 122: {\displaystyle v_{p}(R)/V(R)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/483f2b095bc555d67dd2969c4aed6054fe4c0c9b) is nonincreasing. This can be generalized to any lower bound on the Ricci curvature (not just nonnegativity), and is the key point in the proof of [Gromov's compactness theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gromov%27s_compactness_theorem_(geometry) "Gromov's compactness theorem (geometry)").
3.   The Cheeger–Gromoll [splitting theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Splitting_theorem "Splitting theorem") states that if a complete Riemannian manifold ![Image 123: {\displaystyle \left(M,g\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b487026b0c2aae9757f9fdea4d58704078e98f48) with ![Image 124: {\displaystyle \operatorname {Ric} \geq 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1ecd307370a45fc4ce369753fbc99c3194d73cd5) contains a _line_, meaning a geodesic ![Image 125: {\displaystyle \gamma :\mathbb {R} \to M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/50077748cd66afc170744f53c721ceef57943fab) such that ![Image 126: {\displaystyle d(\gamma (u),\gamma (v))=\left|u-v\right|}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a578fffb93308f2005a6011e067ec88c0016d828) for all ⁠![Image 127: {\displaystyle u,v\in \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/519ac8331fb1e50912d88886c1746bada455a047)⁠, then it is isometric to a product space ![Image 128: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} \times L}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/154772d05b0df8952faf35876a8cdff2e7e39701). Consequently, a complete manifold of positive Ricci curvature can have at most one topological end. The theorem is also true under some additional hypotheses for complete [Lorentzian manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentzian_manifold "Lorentzian manifold") (of metric signature ⁠![Image 129: {\displaystyle \left(+--\ldots \right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1e441674cb293ee1be429fb8d7e8dff2f78da9ce)⁠) with non-negative Ricci tensor.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_curvature#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGalloway2000-5)
4.   Hamilton's first [convergence theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_flow "Ricci flow") for Ricci flow has, as a corollary, that the only compact 3-manifolds that have Riemannian metrics of positive Ricci curvature are the quotients of the 3-sphere by discrete subgroups of SO(4) that act properly discontinuously. He later extended this to allow for nonnegative Ricci curvature. In particular, the only simply-connected possibility is the 3-sphere itself.

These results, particularly Myers and Hamilton's, show that positive Ricci curvature has strong topological consequences. By contrast, excluding the case of surfaces, negative Ricci curvature is now known to have _no_ topological implications; [Lohkamp (1994)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_curvature#CITEREFLohkamp1994) has shown that any manifold of dimension greater than two admits a complete Riemannian metric of negative Ricci curvature. In the case of two-dimensional manifolds, negativity of the Ricci curvature is synonymous with negativity of the Gaussian curvature, which has very clear [topological implications](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauss-Bonnet_theorem "Gauss-Bonnet theorem"). There are very few two-dimensional manifolds that fail to admit Riemannian metrics of negative Gaussian curvature.

Behavior under conformal rescaling
----------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ricci_curvature&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Behavior under conformal rescaling")]

If the metric ![Image 130: {\displaystyle g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3556280e66fe2c0d0140df20935a6f057381d77) is changed by multiplying it by a conformal factor ⁠![Image 131: {\displaystyle e^{2f}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8f92fd2b29f580f01c37632b30cfcca0ac98855)⁠, the Ricci tensor of the new, conformally-related metric ![Image 132: {\displaystyle {\tilde {g}}=e^{2f}g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af9373624dcb0ce62e854129dbf0717bf3da70bc) is given by[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_curvature#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBesse198759-6)![Image 133: {\displaystyle {\widetilde {\operatorname {Ric} }}=\operatorname {Ric} +(2-n)\left[\nabla df-df\otimes df\right]+\left[\Delta f-(n-2)\|df\|^{2}\right]g,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/152c8cbe3ad9a38657f2d92fb2372e2a4b0036f7) where ![Image 134: {\displaystyle \Delta ={\star }d{\star }d}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bc17fc6560e831421ca91deb1e8e2dede394d42a) is the (positive spectrum) Hodge Laplacian, i.e., the _opposite_ of the usual trace of the Hessian.

In particular, given a point ![Image 135: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) in a Riemannian manifold, it is always possible to find metrics conformal to the given metric ![Image 136: {\displaystyle g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3556280e66fe2c0d0140df20935a6f057381d77) for which the Ricci tensor vanishes at ⁠![Image 137: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36)⁠. Note, however, that this is only pointwise assertion; it is usually impossible to make the Ricci curvature vanish identically on the entire manifold by a conformal rescaling.

For two dimensional manifolds, the above formula shows that if ![Image 138: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) is a [harmonic function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harmonic_function "Harmonic function"), then the conformal scaling ![Image 139: {\displaystyle g\mapsto e^{2f}g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bb6c0924b392208389dba6f91936519bf3968525) does not change the Ricci tensor (although it still changes its trace with respect to the metric unless ⁠![Image 140: {\displaystyle f=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1ee0fdf0f50fcba5afe3e856fcc7dc6acfa61014)⁠.

Trace-free Ricci tensor
-----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ricci_curvature&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Trace-free Ricci tensor")]

In [Riemannian geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_geometry "Riemannian geometry") and [pseudo-Riemannian geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudo-Riemannian_geometry "Pseudo-Riemannian geometry"), the **trace-free Ricci tensor** (also called **traceless Ricci tensor**) of a Riemannian or pseudo-Riemannian ![Image 141: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)-manifold ![Image 142: {\displaystyle \left(M,g\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b487026b0c2aae9757f9fdea4d58704078e98f48) is the tensor defined by ![Image 143: {\displaystyle Z=\operatorname {Ric} -{\frac {1}{n}}Rg,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c0b675ec8830b39bdf96afd7e9697bf5359c05d9) where ![Image 144: {\displaystyle \operatorname {Ric} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/db5affc4fd65ba159e3f58417ed66956201ae098) and ![Image 145: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) denote the Ricci curvature and [scalar curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_curvature "Scalar curvature") of ⁠![Image 146: {\displaystyle g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3556280e66fe2c0d0140df20935a6f057381d77)⁠. The name of this object reflects the fact that its [trace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trace_(linear_algebra) "Trace (linear algebra)") automatically vanishes: ⁠![Image 147: {\displaystyle \operatorname {tr} _{g}Z\equiv g^{ab}Z_{ab}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0c92432201ec6a2cece60ca2b7a04a1103351abb)⁠. However, it is quite an important tensor since it reflects an "orthogonal decomposition" of the Ricci tensor.

### Orthogonal decomposition of the Ricci tensor

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ricci_curvature&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Orthogonal decomposition of the Ricci tensor")]

The following, not so trivial, property is ![Image 148: {\displaystyle \operatorname {Ric} =Z+{\frac {1}{n}}Rg.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/817f2e73658f8c4e1cd381884ff3d503c187266e)

It is less immediately obvious that the two terms on the right hand side are orthogonal to each other: ![Image 149: {\displaystyle \left\langle Z,{\frac {1}{n}}Rg\right\rangle _{g}\equiv g^{ab}\left(R_{ab}-{\frac {1}{n}}Rg_{ab}\right)=0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6eb9335145bbce9efe3bdf10441b4757cd24528d)

An identity that is intimately connected with this (but which could be proved directly) is that ![Image 150: {\displaystyle \left|\operatorname {Ric} \right|_{g}^{2}=|Z|_{g}^{2}+{\frac {1}{n}}R^{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cb01e8a6788708420bd99be69a0ab6e144ecaa48)

### Trace-free Ricci tensor and Einstein metrics

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ricci_curvature&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Trace-free Ricci tensor and Einstein metrics")]

By taking a divergence, and using the contracted Bianchi identity, one sees that ![Image 151: {\displaystyle Z=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/49175243147048b9854d4f1e640e3e0da82c09ff) implies ⁠![Image 152: {\displaystyle \textstyle {\frac {1}{2}}dR-{\frac {1}{n}}dR=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1ad673a854b7532997a77aaac49c17bd7f26fc9e)⁠. So, provided that _n_ ≥ 3 and ![Image 153: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) is connected, the vanishing of ![Image 154: {\displaystyle Z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1cc6b75e09a8aa3f04d8584b11db534f88fb56bd) implies that the scalar curvature is constant. In the Riemannian setting, the above orthogonal decomposition shows that ![Image 155: {\displaystyle R^{2}=n\left|\operatorname {Ric} \right|^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/91fe6e2ab83984a36a1425e5500c18c8263846f3) is also equivalent to these conditions. In the pseudo-Riemmannian setting, by contrast, the condition ![Image 156: {\displaystyle |Z|_{g}^{2}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7f8ff8aeaa9ac591a8422a6445bc73b2f94b8cd1) does not necessarily imply ⁠![Image 157: {\displaystyle Z=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/49175243147048b9854d4f1e640e3e0da82c09ff)⁠, so the most that one can say is that these conditions imply ⁠![Image 158: {\displaystyle R^{2}=n\left\vert \operatorname {Ric} \right\vert _{g}^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b96aa0f10f690ac43e304d46e40fdca81d1ab5cc)⁠.

In particular, the vanishing of trace-free Ricci tensor characterizes [Einstein manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_manifold "Einstein manifold"), as defined by the condition ![Image 159: {\displaystyle \operatorname {Ric} =\lambda g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/59cf9b5649ec062388384a7fd71e67b7eaffe209) for a number ![Image 160: {\displaystyle \lambda .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4bb9c58e3f6b2de892e10ef516f96f07da0423e0) In [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity"), this equation states that ![Image 161: {\displaystyle \left(M,g\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b487026b0c2aae9757f9fdea4d58704078e98f48) is a solution of Einstein's vacuum field equations with [cosmological constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmological_constant "Cosmological constant").

On a [Kähler manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K%C3%A4hler_manifold "Kähler manifold")⁠![Image 162: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab)⁠, the Ricci curvature determines the [curvature form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvature_form "Curvature form") of the [canonical line bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canonical_bundle "Canonical bundle").[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_curvature#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMoroianu2007ch._12-7) The canonical line bundle is the top [exterior power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_power "Exterior power") of the bundle of holomorphic [Kähler differentials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K%C3%A4hler_differential "Kähler differential"): ![Image 163: {\displaystyle \kappa ={\textstyle \bigwedge }^{n}~\Omega _{X}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9abf8ea4e146b520c8aa5f23a63822bc1c543040)

The Levi-Civita connection corresponding to the metric on ![Image 164: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) gives rise to a connection on ⁠![Image 165: {\displaystyle \kappa }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54ddec2e922c5caea4e47d04feef86e782dc8e6d)⁠. The curvature of this connection is the 2-form defined by ![Image 166: {\displaystyle \rho (X,Y)\;{\stackrel {\text{def}}{=}}\;\operatorname {Ric} (JX,Y)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8b64f2c966bd44c8b14ccf639eab2e39a1867017) where ![Image 167: {\displaystyle J}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/359e4f407b49910e02c27c2f52e87a36cd74c053) is the [complex structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_manifold "Complex manifold") map on the tangent bundle determined by the structure of the Kähler manifold. The Ricci form is a [closed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_and_exact_forms "Closed and exact forms") 2-form. Its [cohomology class](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cohomology_class "Cohomology class") is, up to a real constant factor, the first [Chern class](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chern_class "Chern class") of the canonical bundle, and is therefore a topological invariant of ![Image 168: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) (for compact ⁠![Image 169: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab)⁠) in the sense that it depends only on the topology of ![Image 170: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) and the [homotopy class](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homotopy_class "Homotopy class") of the complex structure.

Conversely, the Ricci form determines the Ricci tensor by ![Image 171: {\displaystyle \operatorname {Ric} (X,Y)=\rho (X,JY).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68aed66cf35d2a02b4426c848da1e14b6e00c247)

In local holomorphic coordinates ![Image 172: {\displaystyle z^{\alpha }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c5a0a433cdbf86eab4288d723fa9877945be0def), the Ricci form is given by ![Image 173: {\displaystyle \rho =-i\partial {\overline {\partial }}\log \det \left(g_{\alpha {\overline {\beta }}}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5d87878480fb8e473fc7b39670593bb3875b5ad5) where ∂ is the [Dolbeault operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dolbeault_operator "Dolbeault operator") and ![Image 174: {\displaystyle g_{\alpha {\overline {\beta }}}=g\left({\frac {\partial }{\partial z^{\alpha }}},{\frac {\partial }{\partial {\overline {z}}^{\beta }}}\right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6350d6c6c40e3faf65f3068718958c04962d81ba)

If the Ricci tensor vanishes, then the canonical bundle is flat, so the [structure group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G-structure "G-structure") can be locally reduced to a subgroup of the special linear group ⁠![Image 175: {\displaystyle \mathrm {SL} (n;\mathbb {C} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ce8e271e9b5c60af99c3bf484ba75a876dbe9da0)⁠. However, Kähler manifolds already possess [holonomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holonomy "Holonomy") in ⁠![Image 176: {\displaystyle \mathrm {U} (n)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a32fa84df5de5dfa91b6bdc88fb03fc8792c9f81)⁠, and so the (restricted) holonomy of a Ricci-flat Kähler manifold is contained in ⁠![Image 177: {\displaystyle \mathrm {SU} (n)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e8a205091aabd5690efdfeb7354a55844f2eb31b)⁠. Conversely, if the (restricted) holonomy of a 2![Image 178: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)-dimensional Riemannian manifold is contained in ⁠![Image 179: {\displaystyle \mathrm {SU} (n)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e8a205091aabd5690efdfeb7354a55844f2eb31b)⁠, then the manifold is a Ricci-flat Kähler manifold.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_curvature#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKobayashiNomizu1996IX,_%C2%A74-8)

Generalization to affine connections
------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ricci_curvature&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Generalization to affine connections")]

The Ricci tensor can also be generalized to arbitrary [affine connections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_connection "Affine connection"), where it is an invariant that plays an especially important role in the study of [projective geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_differential_geometry "Projective differential geometry") (geometry associated to unparameterized geodesics).[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_curvature#cite_note-FOOTNOTENomizuSasaki1994-9) If ![Image 180: {\displaystyle \nabla }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a3d0e93b78c50237f9ea83d027e4ebbdaef354b2) denotes an affine connection, then the curvature tensor ![Image 181: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) is the (1,3)-tensor defined by ![Image 182: {\displaystyle R(X,Y)Z=\nabla _{X}\nabla _{Y}Z-\nabla _{Y}\nabla _{X}Z-\nabla _{[X,Y]}Z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6a30a969d5b599e29c8ef7fc0344b823845e5865) for any vector fields ⁠![Image 183: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab)⁠, ⁠![Image 184: {\displaystyle Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/961d67d6b454b4df2301ac571808a3538b3a6d3f)⁠, ⁠![Image 185: {\displaystyle Z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1cc6b75e09a8aa3f04d8584b11db534f88fb56bd)⁠. The Ricci tensor is defined to be the trace: ![Image 186: {\displaystyle \operatorname {ric} (X,Y)=\operatorname {tr} {\big (}Z\mapsto R(Z,X)Y{\big )}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d815f0da09ca9cc4b11f3a4dac53beef7c93f417)

In this more general situation, the Ricci tensor is symmetric if and only if there exists locally a parallel [volume form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume_form "Volume form") for the connection.

Discrete Ricci curvature
------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ricci_curvature&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Discrete Ricci curvature")]

Notions of Ricci curvature on discrete manifolds have been defined on graphs and networks, where they quantify local divergence properties of edges. Ollivier's Ricci curvature is defined using optimal transport theory.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_curvature#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOllivier2009-10) A different (and earlier) notion, Forman's Ricci curvature, is based on topological arguments.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_curvature#cite_note-FOOTNOTEForman2003-11)
