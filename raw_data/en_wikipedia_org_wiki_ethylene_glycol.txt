**Ethylene glycol** ([IUPAC name](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IUPAC_nomenclature "IUPAC nomenclature"): ethane-1,2-diol) is an [organic compound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_compound "Organic compound") (a [vicinal diol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diol#Vicinal_diols "Diol")[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene_glycol#cite_note-7)) with the formula (CH 2 OH)2. It is mainly used for two purposes: as a raw material in the manufacture of polyester fibers and for [antifreeze](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antifreeze "Antifreeze") formulations. It is an odorless, colorless, flammable, viscous liquid. It has a sweet taste but is [toxic in high concentrations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene_glycol_poisoning "Ethylene glycol poisoning"). This molecule has been observed in outer space.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene_glycol#cite_note-8)

Ethylene glycol is produced from [ethylene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene "Ethylene") (ethene), via the intermediate [ethylene oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene_oxide "Ethylene oxide"). Ethylene oxide reacts with [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water "Water") to produce ethylene glycol according to the [chemical equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_equation "Chemical equation")

C 2 H 4 O + H 2 O → HO−CH 2 CH 2−OH
This [reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_reaction "Chemical reaction") can be [catalyzed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catalyst "Catalyst") by either [acids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acid "Acid") or [bases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Base_(chemistry) "Base (chemistry)") or can occur at neutral [pH](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PH "PH") under elevated temperatures. The highest yields of ethylene glycol occur at acidic or neutral pH with a large excess of water. Under these conditions, ethylene glycol yields of 90% can be achieved. The major byproducts are the oligomers [diethylene glycol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diethylene_glycol "Diethylene glycol"), [triethylene glycol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triethylene_glycol "Triethylene glycol"), and [tetraethylene glycol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetraethylene_glycol "Tetraethylene glycol"). The separation of these oligomers and water is energy-intensive. World production of ethylene glycol was ~20 Mt in 2010.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene_glycol#cite_note-YueZhaoMa2012-9)

A higher selectivity is achieved by the use of [Shell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Dutch_Shell "Royal Dutch Shell")'s [OMEGA process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OMEGA_process "OMEGA process"). In the OMEGA process, the ethylene oxide is first converted with [carbon dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_dioxide "Carbon dioxide") (CO 2) to [ethylene carbonate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene_carbonate "Ethylene carbonate"). This ring is then hydrolyzed with a base catalyst in a second step to produce mono-ethylene glycol in 98% selectivity.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene_glycol#cite_note-10) The carbon dioxide is released in this step again and can be fed back into the process circuit. The carbon dioxide comes in part from ethylene oxide production, where a part of the ethylene is completely [oxidized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxidation "Oxidation").

Ethylene glycol is produced from [carbon monoxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide "Carbon monoxide") in countries with large coal reserves and less stringent environmental regulations. The oxidative carbonylation of methanol to [dimethyl oxalate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimethyl_oxalate "Dimethyl oxalate") provides a promising approach to the production of C

1-based ethylene glycol.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene_glycol#cite_note-11) Dimethyl oxalate can be converted into ethylene glycol in high yields (94.7%)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene_glycol#cite_note-12) by [hydrogenation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogenation "Hydrogenation") with a copper catalyst:[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene_glycol#cite_note-13)

[![Image 21](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/MEG_ex_CO.svg/331px-MEG_ex_CO.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:MEG_ex_CO.svg)

Because the methanol is recycled, only carbon monoxide, hydrogen, and oxygen are consumed. One plant with a production capacity of 200 000 tons of ethylene glycol per year is in [Inner Mongolia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inner_Mongolia "Inner Mongolia"), and a second plant in the Chinese province of [Henan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henan "Henan") with a capacity of 250 000 tons per year was scheduled for 2012.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene_glycol#cite_note-14) As of 2015, four plants in China with a capacity of 200 000 t/a each were operating, with at least 17 more to follow.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene_glycol#cite_note-15)

Ethylene glycol can be produced by recycling its polymeric derivatives, such as [polyethylene terephthalate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyethylene_terephthalate "Polyethylene terephthalate").[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene_glycol#cite_note-16)

According to most sources, French chemist [Charles-Adolphe Wurtz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles-Adolphe_Wurtz "Charles-Adolphe Wurtz") (1817–1884) first prepared ethylene glycol in 1856.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene_glycol#cite_note-17) He first treated "ethylene iodide" ([1,2-Diiodoethane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1,2-Diiodoethane "1,2-Diiodoethane")) with silver acetate and then hydrolyzed the resultant "ethylene diacetate" with [potassium hydroxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_hydroxide "Potassium hydroxide"). Wurtz named his new compound "glycol" because it shared qualities with both [ethyl alcohol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethyl_alcohol "Ethyl alcohol") (with one hydroxyl group) and [glycerin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycerin "Glycerin") (with three hydroxyl groups).[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene_glycol#cite_note-18) In 1859, Wurtz prepared ethylene glycol via the [hydration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydration_reaction "Hydration reaction") of [ethylene oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene_oxide "Ethylene oxide").[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene_glycol#cite_note-19) There appears to have been no commercial manufacture or application of ethylene glycol before [World War I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_I "World War I") when it was synthesized from [ethylene dichloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene_dichloride "Ethylene dichloride") in Germany and used as a substitute for [glycerol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycerol "Glycerol") in the [explosives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Explosives "Explosives") industry.

In the United States, semicommercial production of ethylene glycol via [ethylene chlorohydrin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene_chlorohydrin "Ethylene chlorohydrin") started in 1917. The first large-scale commercial glycol plant was erected in 1925 at [South Charleston, West Virginia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Charleston,_West_Virginia "South Charleston, West Virginia"), by Carbide and Carbon Chemicals Co. (now [Union Carbide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Union_Carbide "Union Carbide") Corp.). By 1929, ethylene glycol was being used by almost all [dynamite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynamite "Dynamite") manufacturers. In 1937, Carbide started up the first plant based on Lefort's process for vapor-phase oxidation of ethylene to ethylene oxide. Carbide maintained a monopoly on the direct oxidation process until 1953 when the Scientific Design process was commercialized and offered for licensing.

### Coolant and heat-transfer agent

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ethylene_glycol&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Coolant and heat-transfer agent")]

A major use of ethylene glycol is as an antifreeze agent in [coolants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coolant "Coolant"). This can be useful for automobiles and [air-conditioning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Air-conditioning "Air-conditioning") systems that either have external [chillers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chiller "Chiller") or [air handlers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Air_handler "Air handler") or must cool below the freezing temperature of water. In [geothermal heating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geothermal_heating "Geothermal heating")/cooling systems, ethylene glycol is the [fluid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluid "Fluid") that transports heat through the use of a [geothermal heat pump](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geothermal_heat_pump "Geothermal heat pump"). The ethylene glycol either gains energy from the source (lake, ocean, [water well](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water_well "Water well")) or dissipates heat to the sink, depending on whether the system is being used for heating or cooling.

Pure ethylene glycol has a [specific heat capacity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Specific_heat_capacity "Specific heat capacity") about one-half that of water. So, while providing freeze protection and an increased boiling point, ethylene glycol lowers the specific heat capacity of water mixtures relative to pure water. A 1:1 mix by mass has a specific heat capacity of about 3140 J/(kg·°C) (0.75 BTU/(lb·°F)), three-quarters that of pure water, thus requiring increased flow rates in same-system comparisons with water.

The mixture of ethylene glycol with water provides additional benefits to coolant and antifreeze solutions, such as preventing corrosion and acid degradation, as well as inhibiting the growth of most microbes and fungi.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene_glycol#cite_note-20) Mixtures of ethylene glycol and water are sometimes informally referred to in the industry as glycol concentrates, compounds, mixtures, or solutions.

Pure ethylene glycol freezes at about −12°C (10.4°F) but, when mixed with water, the mixture freezes at a lower temperature. For example, a mixture of 60% ethylene glycol and 40% water freezes at −45°C (−49°F).[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene_glycol#cite_note-Ullmanns-23)[Diethylene glycol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diethylene_glycol "Diethylene glycol") behaves similarly. The freezing point depression of some mixtures can be explained as a [colligative property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colligative_property "Colligative property") of solutions but, in highly concentrated mixtures such as the example, deviations from ideal solution behavior are expected due to the influence of [intermolecular forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intermolecular_forces "Intermolecular forces"). It's important to note that though pure and distilled water will have a greater specific heat capacity than any mixture of antifreeze and water, commercial antifreeze also typically contain an anti-corrosive additive to prevent pure water from corroding coolant passages in the engine block, cylinder head(s), water pump and radiator.

There is a difference in the mixing ratio, depending on whether it is ethylene glycol or propylene glycol. For ethylene glycol, the mixing ratios are typically 30/70 and 35/65, whereas the propylene glycol mixing ratios are typically 35/65 and 40/60. The mixture be frost-proof at the lowest operating temperature.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene_glycol#cite_note-24)

Because of the depressed freezing temperatures, ethylene glycol is used as a [de-icing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De-icing "De-icing") fluid for [windshields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Windshield "Windshield") and aircraft, as an [antifreeze](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antifreeze_(coolant) "Antifreeze (coolant)") in automobile engines, and as a component of [vitrification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cryopreservation#Vitrification "Cryopreservation") (anticrystallization) mixtures for low-temperature preservation of biological tissues and organs.

The use of ethylene glycol not only depresses the freezing point of aqueous mixtures but also elevates their boiling point. This results in the operating temperature range for heat-transfer fluids being broadened on both ends of the temperature scale. The increase in boiling temperature is due to pure ethylene glycol having a much higher boiling point and lower [vapor pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vapor_pressure "Vapor pressure") than pure water.

### Precursor to polymers

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ethylene_glycol&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Precursor to polymers")]

[![Image 22](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/PET.svg/190px-PET.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:PET.svg)

Ethylene glycol is one precursor to [polyethyleneterephthalate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyethyleneterephthalate "Polyethyleneterephthalate"), which is produced on the multimillion ton scale annually.

In the [plastic industry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plastics_industry "Plastics industry"), ethylene glycol is an important precursor to [polyester](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyester "Polyester") fibers and [resins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resin "Resin"). [Polyethylene terephthalate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyethylene_terephthalate "Polyethylene terephthalate"), used to make [plastic bottles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plastic_bottle "Plastic bottle") for [soft drinks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soft_drink "Soft drink"), is prepared from ethylene glycol.

Ethylene glycol is used in the natural gas industry to remove water vapor from natural gas before further processing, in much the same manner as [triethylene glycol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triethylene_glycol "Triethylene glycol") (TEG).

Because of its high boiling point and affinity for water, ethylene glycol is a useful [desiccant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Desiccant "Desiccant"). Ethylene glycol is widely used to inhibit the formation of [natural gas clathrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane_clathrate "Methane clathrate") (hydrates) in long multiphase pipelines that convey natural gas from remote gas fields to a gas processing facility. Ethylene glycol can be recovered from natural gas and reused as an inhibitor after purification treatment that removes water and inorganic salts.

Natural gas is dehydrated by ethylene glycol. In this application, ethylene glycol flows down from the top of a tower and meets a rising mixture of water vapor and [hydrocarbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrocarbon "Hydrocarbon") gases. Dry gas exits from the top of the tower. The glycol and water are separated, and the glycol is recycled. Instead of removing water, ethylene glycol can also be used to depress the temperature at which [hydrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrate "Hydrate") are formed. The purity of glycol used for hydrate suppression (monoethylene glycol) is typically around 80%, whereas the purity of glycol used for dehydration (triethylene glycol) is typically 95 to more than 99%. Moreover, the injection rate for hydrate suppression is much lower than the circulation rate in a [glycol dehydration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycol_dehydration "Glycol dehydration") tower.

#### Precursor to other chemicals

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ethylene_glycol&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Precursor to other chemicals")]

Minor uses of ethylene glycol include the manufacture of capacitors, as a chemical intermediate in the manufacture of [1,4-dioxane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1,4-Dioxane "1,4-Dioxane"), as an additive to prevent [corrosion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corrosion "Corrosion") in liquid cooling systems for [personal computers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Personal_computer "Personal computer"), and inside the lens devices of cathode-ray tube type of rear projection televisions. Ethylene glycol is also used in the manufacture of some [vaccines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vaccine "Vaccine"), but it is not present in these injections. It is used as a minor (1–2%) ingredient in [shoe polish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shoe_polish "Shoe polish") and also in some inks and dyes. Ethylene glycol has seen some use as a rot and fungal treatment for wood, both as a preventative and a treatment after the fact. It has been used in a few cases to treat partially rotted wooden objects to be displayed in museums. It is one of only a few treatments that are successful in dealing with rot in wooden boats and is relatively cheap. Ethylene glycol may also be one of the minor ingredients in screen cleaning solutions, along with the main ingredient [isopropyl alcohol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isopropyl_alcohol "Isopropyl alcohol"). Ethylene glycol is commonly used as a [preservative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Preservative "Preservative") for biological specimens, especially in secondary schools during [dissection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dissection "Dissection") as a safer alternative to [formaldehyde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde "Formaldehyde"). It is also used as part of the water-based hydraulic fluid used to control subsea oil and gas production equipment.

### Organic building block

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ethylene_glycol&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Organic building block")]

Although dwarfed by its use as a precursor to [polyesters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyester "Polyester"), ethylene glycol is useful in more specialized areas of organic chemistry.

It serves as a [protecting group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protecting_group "Protecting group") in [organic synthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_synthesis "Organic synthesis") for the manipulation of [ketones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ketones "Ketones") and aldehydes.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene_glycol#cite_note-25)[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene_glycol#cite_note-greene-26) By reacting with the carbonyl to form an acetal product, it reduces the likelihood of nucleophilic attack at that carbonyl carbon. After the desired reaction is completed, the carbonyl can be regenerated using acid-catalyzed hydrolysis. In one example, [isophorone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isophorone "Isophorone") was protected using ethylene glycol:[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene_glycol#cite_note-27)

[![Image 23](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Ethylene_glycol_protecting_group.png/500px-Ethylene_glycol_protecting_group.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Ethylene_glycol_protecting_group.png)

The glycol-derived [dioxalane](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Dioxalane&action=edit&redlink=1 "Dioxalane (page does not exist)") of [ethyl acetoacetate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethyl_acetoacetate "Ethyl acetoacetate") is a commercial fragrance [fructone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fructone "Fructone").[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene_glycol#cite_note-28)

Miscellaneous chemical reactions
--------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ethylene_glycol&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Miscellaneous chemical reactions")]

Silicon dioxide dissolves slowly in hot ethylene glycol in the presence of [alkali metal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkali_metal "Alkali metal") base to produce silicates.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene_glycol#cite_note-Laine-29)

Ethylene glycol has relatively high mammalian toxicity when ingested, roughly on par with [methanol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol "Methanol"), with an oral [LD Lo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lowest_published_lethal_dose "Lowest published lethal dose") = 786 mg/kg for humans.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene_glycol#cite_note-30) The major danger is due to its sweet [taste](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taste "Taste"), which can attract children and animals. Upon ingestion, ethylene glycol is oxidized to [glycolic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycolic_acid "Glycolic acid"), which is, in turn, oxidized to [oxalic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxalic_acid "Oxalic acid"), which is [toxic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toxicity "Toxicity"). It and its toxic byproducts first affect the [central nervous system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_nervous_system "Central nervous system"), then the heart, and finally the kidneys. Ingestion of sufficient amounts is fatal if untreated.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene_glycol#cite_note-31) Several deaths are recorded annually in the U.S. alone.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene_glycol#cite_note-32)

Antifreeze products for automotive use containing [propylene glycol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propylene_glycol "Propylene glycol") in place of ethylene glycol are available. They are generally considered safer to use, as propylene glycol is not as palatable[[note 1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene_glycol#cite_note-35) and is converted in the body to [lactic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lactic_acid "Lactic acid"), a normal product of metabolism and exercise.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene_glycol#cite_note-36)

Australia, the UK, and seventeen US states (as of 2012) require the addition of a bitter flavoring ([denatonium benzoate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Denatonium "Denatonium")) to antifreeze. In December 2012, US antifreeze manufacturers agreed voluntarily to add a bitter flavoring to all antifreeze that is sold in the consumer market of the US.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene_glycol#cite_note-37)

In 2022, several hundred children died of acute [kidney failure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kidney_failure "Kidney failure") in [Indonesia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indonesia "Indonesia") and [The Gambia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Gambia "The Gambia") because the [paracetamol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paracetamol "Paracetamol") syrup made by [New Delhi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Delhi "New Delhi")-based Maiden Pharmaceuticals contained ethylene glycol and [diethylene glycol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diethylene_glycol "Diethylene glycol"), ingredients that have been linked to child deaths from [acute kidney injury](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acute_kidney_injury "Acute kidney injury") in The Gambia.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene_glycol#cite_note-38) In December 2022, [Uzbekistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uzbekistan "Uzbekistan")'s health ministry said children died as a result of ethylene glycol in [cough syrup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toxic_cough_syrup "Toxic cough syrup") made by [Marion Biotech](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Marion_Biotech&action=edit&redlink=1 "Marion Biotech (page does not exist)"), which is based at [Noida](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noida "Noida"), near New Delhi.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene_glycol#cite_note-39)

Environmental effects
---------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ethylene_glycol&action=edit&section=16 "Edit section: Environmental effects")]

Ethylene glycol is a [high-production-volume chemical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High_production_volume_chemicals "High production volume chemicals"). It breaks down in air in about 10 days and in water or soil in a few weeks. It enters the environment through the dispersal of ethylene glycol-containing products, especially at airports, where it is used in [de-icing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De-icing "De-icing") agents for runways and airplanes.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene_glycol#cite_note-40) While prolonged low doses of ethylene glycol show no toxicity, at near-lethal doses (≥ 1000 mg/kg per day) ethylene glycol acts as a [teratogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teratogen "Teratogen"). "Based on a rather extensive database, it induces skeletal variations and malformations in rats and mice by all routes of exposure."[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene_glycol#cite_note-HC-41)
