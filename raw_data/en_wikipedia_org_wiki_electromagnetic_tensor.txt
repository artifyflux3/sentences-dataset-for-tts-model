In [electromagnetism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetism "Electromagnetism"), the **electromagnetic tensor** or **electromagnetic field tensor** (sometimes called the **field strength tensor**, **Faraday tensor** or **Maxwell bivector**) is a mathematical object that describes the [electromagnetic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_field "Electromagnetic field") in spacetime. The field tensor was developed by [Arnold Sommerfeld](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arnold_Sommerfeld "Arnold Sommerfeld") after the four-dimensional [tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor "Tensor") formulation of [special relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_relativity "Special relativity") was introduced by [Hermann Minkowski](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Minkowski "Hermann Minkowski").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_tensor#cite_note-1): 22  The tensor allows related physical laws to be written concisely, and allows for the [quantization of the electromagnetic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantization_of_the_electromagnetic_field "Quantization of the electromagnetic field") by the Lagrangian formulation described [below](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_tensor#Quantum_electrodynamics_and_field_theory).

The electromagnetic tensor, conventionally labelled _F_, is defined as the [exterior derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_derivative#Exterior_derivative_of_a_k-form "Exterior derivative") of the [electromagnetic four-potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_four-potential "Electromagnetic four-potential"), _A_, a differential 1-form:[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_tensor#cite_note-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_tensor#cite_note-3)

![Image 1: {\displaystyle F\ {\stackrel {\mathrm {def} }{=}}\ \mathrm {d} A.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/991f913e580255b7643ff177d182f8378a5f6cd8)
Therefore, _F_ is a [differential 2-form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_form "Differential form")— an antisymmetric rank-2 tensor field—on Minkowski space. In component form,

![Image 2: {\displaystyle F_{\mu \nu }=\partial _{\mu }A_{\nu }-\partial _{\nu }A_{\mu }.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d57248073b74d1a6dc81e596373dfa61a22178d2)
where ![Image 3: {\displaystyle \partial }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/62b4e7c1cedb9564609aefd2aa2309972f455c24) is the [four-gradient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Four-gradient "Four-gradient") and ![Image 4: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3) is the [four-potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_four-potential "Electromagnetic four-potential").

[SI units for Maxwell's equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxwell%27s_equations#Conventional_formulation_in_SI_units "Maxwell's equations") and the [particle physicist's sign convention](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sign_convention#Metric_signature "Sign convention") for the [signature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_signature "Metric signature") of [Minkowski space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minkowski_space "Minkowski space")(+ − − −), will be used throughout this article.

Note that

![Image 17: {\displaystyle {\begin{cases}dF=0\\{\star }d{\star }F=J\end{cases}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/252be95ad50b8914556ead1a81f3978dc52094df)
where ![Image 18: {\displaystyle d}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e85ff03cbe0c7341af6b982e47e9f90d235c66ab) is the exterior derivative, ![Image 19: {\displaystyle {\star }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dcda0fc423fda8f0a63f8a14aa707e813640b143) is the [Hodge star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hodge_star_operator "Hodge star operator"), ![Image 20: {\displaystyle J=-J_{x}\ dx-J_{y}\ dy-J_{z}\ dz+\rho \ dt}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4e6658a58cbf560c96cfdecf0b913668045a2ed1) (where ![Image 21: {\displaystyle {\vec {J}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f64ab02528d2b80e1df79bc2a8762489a986afa8) is the [electric current density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Current_density "Current density"), and ![Image 22: {\displaystyle \rho }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1f7d439671d1289b6a816e6af7a304be40608d64) is the [electric charge density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charge_density "Charge density")) is the 4-current density 1-form, is the differential forms version of Maxwell's equations.

The [electric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_field "Electric field") and [magnetic fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_field "Magnetic field") can be obtained from the components of the electromagnetic tensor. The relationship is simplest in [Cartesian coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system "Cartesian coordinate system"):

![Image 23: {\displaystyle E_{i}=cF_{0i},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54806634a1705f9ce1ad04ed418eace862e36a7e)
where _c_ is the speed of light, and

![Image 24: {\displaystyle B_{i}=-1/2\epsilon _{ijk}F^{jk},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/440a5ed6a6441da3c3e25986cd27d8fed9d6ec58)
where ![Image 25: {\displaystyle \epsilon _{ijk}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/80cacf43cd468e0f17c0945c0aecaa8d7c57ac99) is the [Levi-Civita tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_tensor "Levi-Civita tensor"). This gives the fields in a particular reference frame; if the reference frame is changed, the components of the electromagnetic tensor will [transform covariantly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covariant_transformation "Covariant transformation"), and the fields in the new frame will be given by the new components.

In contravariant [matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_(mathematics) "Matrix (mathematics)") form with metric signature (+,-,-,-),

![Image 26: {\displaystyle F^{\mu \nu }={\begin{bmatrix}0&-E_{x}/c&-E_{y}/c&-E_{z}/c\\E_{x}/c&0&-B_{z}&B_{y}\\E_{y}/c&B_{z}&0&-B_{x}\\E_{z}/c&-B_{y}&B_{x}&0\end{bmatrix}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/31a955ad5950849ff726ffe46784f60fda6e3f0a)
The covariant form is given by [index lowering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raising_and_lowering_indices#Order-2 "Raising and lowering indices"),

![Image 27: {\displaystyle F_{\mu \nu }=\eta _{\alpha \nu }F^{\beta \alpha }\eta _{\mu \beta }={\begin{bmatrix}0&E_{x}/c&E_{y}/c&E_{z}/c\\-E_{x}/c&0&-B_{z}&B_{y}\\-E_{y}/c&B_{z}&0&-B_{x}\\-E_{z}/c&-B_{y}&B_{x}&0\end{bmatrix}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/30161ff4bf0550721102409e7ba562bb5c84f7d7)
The Faraday tensor's [Hodge dual](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hodge_star_operator "Hodge star operator") is

![Image 28: {\displaystyle {G^{\alpha \beta }={\frac {1}{2}}\epsilon ^{\alpha \beta \gamma \delta }F_{\gamma \delta }={\begin{bmatrix}0&-B_{x}&-B_{y}&-B_{z}\\B_{x}&0&E_{z}/c&-E_{y}/c\\B_{y}&-E_{z}/c&0&E_{x}/c\\B_{z}&E_{y}/c&-E_{x}/c&0\end{bmatrix}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8d3ae0d3a4d2ff256c7921c43fbc79d8e571512d)
From now on in this article, when the electric or magnetic fields are mentioned, a Cartesian coordinate system is assumed, and the electric and magnetic fields are with respect to the coordinate system's reference frame, as in the equations above.

The matrix form of the field tensor yields the following properties:[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_tensor#cite_note-4)

1.   **[Antisymmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skew-symmetric_matrix "Skew-symmetric matrix"):**![Image 29: {\displaystyle F^{\mu \nu }=-F^{\nu \mu }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cc7c5e04daadeef79c5b6c1cc8a98f04649a858e)
2.   **Six independent components:** In Cartesian coordinates, these are simply the three spatial components of the electric field (_E x, E y, E z_) and magnetic field (_B x, B y, B z_).
3.   **Inner product:** If one forms an inner product of the field strength tensor a [Lorentz invariant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentz_invariant "Lorentz invariant") is formed ![Image 30: {\displaystyle F_{\mu \nu }F^{\mu \nu }=2\left(B^{2}-{\frac {E^{2}}{c^{2}}}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ea2def5544bde7fe9e4d4a36e7b612093edd580d) meaning this number does not change from one [frame of reference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frame_of_reference "Frame of reference") to another.
4.   **[Pseudoscalar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudoscalar "Pseudoscalar") invariant:** The product of the tensor ![Image 31: {\displaystyle F^{\mu \nu }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/270a5daa364616f695db1f470f3d341755df881a) with its [Hodge dual](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hodge_dual "Hodge dual")![Image 32: {\displaystyle G^{\mu \nu }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4e467946740615cc2dde83b555083fdc2bb30673) gives a [Lorentz invariant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentz_invariant "Lorentz invariant"): ![Image 33: {\displaystyle G_{\gamma \delta }F^{\gamma \delta }={\frac {1}{2}}\epsilon _{\alpha \beta \gamma \delta }F^{\alpha \beta }F^{\gamma \delta }=-{\frac {4}{c}}\mathbf {B} \cdot \mathbf {E} \,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1f9b608ffcd68f6661af97a6c52a48981c280a66) where ![Image 34: {\displaystyle \epsilon _{\alpha \beta \gamma \delta }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/670a6d080598763bddf814dd66f911eeecdce000) is the rank-4 [Levi-Civita symbol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_symbol "Levi-Civita symbol"). The sign for the above depends on the convention used for the Levi-Civita symbol. The convention used here is ![Image 35: {\displaystyle \epsilon _{0123}=-1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3ea75f4f65a31af50974845858369be44fcdd7c2).
5.   **[Determinant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Determinant "Determinant"):**![Image 36: {\displaystyle \det \left(F\right)={\frac {1}{c^{2}}}\left(\mathbf {B} \cdot \mathbf {E} \right)^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/661fa91500926eb8469ff203d28bd0c86db740d2) which is proportional to the square of the above invariant.
6.   **[Trace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trace_(linear_algebra) "Trace (linear algebra)"):**![Image 37: {\displaystyle F={{F}^{\mu }}_{\mu }=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5fa458716fa7b73ae8cef0ce17b2a9d68e72ee99) which is equal to zero.

This tensor simplifies and reduces [Maxwell's equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxwell%27s_equations "Maxwell's equations") as four vector calculus equations into two tensor field equations. In [electrostatics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrostatic "Electrostatic") and [electrodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrodynamic "Electrodynamic"), [Gauss's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauss%27s_law "Gauss's law") and [Ampère's circuital law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amp%C3%A8re%27s_circuital_law "Ampère's circuital law") are respectively:

![Image 38: {\displaystyle \nabla \cdot \mathbf {E} ={\frac {\rho }{\epsilon _{0}}},\quad \nabla \times \mathbf {B} -{\frac {1}{c^{2}}}{\frac {\partial \mathbf {E} }{\partial t}}=\mu _{0}\mathbf {J} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bd01b2bda23af62c25792217c40f5dcd592b3950)
and reduce to the inhomogeneous Maxwell equation:

![Image 39: {\displaystyle \partial _{\alpha }F^{\beta \alpha }=-\mu _{0}J^{\beta }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/aacf5f3903c30885f00766c74fe1de0b327745a8), where ![Image 40: {\displaystyle J^{\alpha }=(c\rho ,\mathbf {J} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/905d8fc62435108bdfae056ac23feac9b1c56d72) is the [four-current](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Four-current "Four-current").
In [magnetostatics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetostatic "Magnetostatic") and magnetodynamics, [Gauss's law for magnetism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauss%27s_law_for_magnetism "Gauss's law for magnetism") and [Maxwell–Faraday equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Faraday%27s_law_of_induction "Faraday's law of induction") are respectively:

![Image 41: {\displaystyle \nabla \cdot \mathbf {B} =0,\quad {\frac {\partial \mathbf {B} }{\partial t}}+\nabla \times \mathbf {E} =\mathbf {0} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4d3410ceabbc9694bb86f13d9ad4da855d49e307)
which reduce to the [Bianchi identity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bianchi_identity "Bianchi identity"):

![Image 42: {\displaystyle \partial _{\gamma }F_{\alpha \beta }+\partial _{\alpha }F_{\beta \gamma }+\partial _{\beta }F_{\gamma \alpha }=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a574c5f6a069e2b43bc546960ef01b8356834193)
or using the [index notation with square brackets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_calculus#Symmetric_and_antisymmetric_parts "Ricci calculus")[[note 1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_tensor#endnote_antisymmetric) for the antisymmetric part of the tensor:

![Image 43: {\displaystyle \partial _{[\alpha }F_{\beta \gamma ]}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7f11586830935cfd6dce389116d57ecec6cc70d2)
Using the expression relating the Faraday tensor to the four-potential, one can prove that the above antisymmetric quantity turns to zero identically (![Image 44: {\displaystyle \equiv 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/30ccb4758f36908929b0b75a787ded48a5cd33f1)). This tensor equation reproduces the homogeneous Maxwell's equations.

The field tensor derives its name from the fact that the electromagnetic field is found to obey the [tensor transformation law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_transformation_law "Tensor transformation law"), this general property of physical laws being recognised after the advent of [special relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_relativity "Special relativity"). This theory stipulated that all the laws of physics should take the same form in all coordinate systems – this led to the introduction of [tensors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor "Tensor"). The tensor formalism also leads to a mathematically simpler presentation of physical laws.

The inhomogeneous Maxwell equation leads to the [continuity equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuity_equation "Continuity equation"):

![Image 45: {\displaystyle \partial _{\alpha }J^{\alpha }=J^{\alpha }{}_{,\alpha }=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/393de0c99acada59dd8439b21ed695da38dd4562)
implying [conservation of charge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservation_of_charge "Conservation of charge").

Maxwell's laws above can be generalised to [curved spacetime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curved_spacetime "Curved spacetime") by simply replacing [partial derivatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_derivative "Partial derivative") with [covariant derivatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covariant_derivative "Covariant derivative"):

![Image 46: {\displaystyle F_{[\alpha \beta ;\gamma ]}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4f3ad2e8d89e3324c68c1e631262b17e3a265126) and ![Image 47: {\displaystyle F^{\alpha \beta }{}_{;\alpha }=\mu _{0}J^{\beta }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c071e726c99ea2fb5604072f377389cf3762caec)
where the [semicolon notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covariant_derivative#Notation "Covariant derivative") represents a covariant derivative, as opposed to a partial derivative. These equations are sometimes referred to as the [curved space Maxwell equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxwell%27s_equations_in_curved_spacetime "Maxwell's equations in curved spacetime"). Again, the second equation implies charge conservation (in curved spacetime):

![Image 48: {\displaystyle J^{\alpha }{}_{;\alpha }\,=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0c379cf4e2bd833a69c050ec1aa939c1dcfd52d8)
The stress-energy tensor of electromagnetism

Lagrangian formulation of classical electromagnetism
----------------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Electromagnetic_tensor&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Lagrangian formulation of classical electromagnetism")]

[Classical electromagnetism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_electromagnetism "Classical electromagnetism") and [Maxwell's equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxwell%27s_equations "Maxwell's equations") can be derived from the [action](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_(physics) "Action (physics)"): ![Image 51: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {S}}=\int \left(-{\begin{matrix}{\frac {1}{4\mu _{0}}}\end{matrix}}F_{\mu \nu }F^{\mu \nu }-J^{\mu }A_{\mu }\right)\mathrm {d} ^{4}x\,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f9b4848d225f57a7c3be65fed27ade3a672a6f0e) where ![Image 52: {\displaystyle \mathrm {d} ^{4}x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b5c825ac507fba68e7f6c6862e71cf3ae640388a) is over space and time.

This means the [Lagrangian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrangian_(field_theory) "Lagrangian (field theory)") density is

![Image 53: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}{\mathcal {L}}&=-{\frac {1}{4\mu _{0}}}F_{\mu \nu }F^{\mu \nu }-J^{\mu }A_{\mu }\\&=-{\frac {1}{4\mu _{0}}}\left(\partial _{\mu }A_{\nu }-\partial _{\nu }A_{\mu }\right)\left(\partial ^{\mu }A^{\nu }-\partial ^{\nu }A^{\mu }\right)-J^{\mu }A_{\mu }\\&=-{\frac {1}{4\mu _{0}}}\left(\partial _{\mu }A_{\nu }\partial ^{\mu }A^{\nu }-\partial _{\nu }A_{\mu }\partial ^{\mu }A^{\nu }-\partial _{\mu }A_{\nu }\partial ^{\nu }A^{\mu }+\partial _{\nu }A_{\mu }\partial ^{\nu }A^{\mu }\right)-J^{\mu }A_{\mu }\\\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6a8811a9cc997403388cd210ad53cfff4bcc930f)
The two middle terms in the parentheses are the same, as are the two outer terms, so the Lagrangian density is

![Image 54: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {L}}=-{\frac {1}{2\mu _{0}}}\left(\partial _{\mu }A_{\nu }\partial ^{\mu }A^{\nu }-\partial _{\nu }A_{\mu }\partial ^{\mu }A^{\nu }\right)-J^{\mu }A_{\mu }.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3b2ce36a3f848542071f2c0a50ed22baae655516)
Substituting this into the [Euler–Lagrange equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler%E2%80%93Lagrange_equation "Euler–Lagrange equation") of motion for a field:

![Image 55: {\displaystyle \partial _{\mu }\left({\frac {\partial {\mathcal {L}}}{\partial (\partial _{\mu }A_{\nu })}}\right)-{\frac {\partial {\mathcal {L}}}{\partial A_{\nu }}}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/15d04bbf9697915e38803bd1618c316b76e337f6)
So the Euler–Lagrange equation becomes:

![Image 56: {\displaystyle -\partial _{\mu }{\frac {1}{\mu _{0}}}\left(\partial ^{\mu }A^{\nu }-\partial ^{\nu }A^{\mu }\right)+J^{\nu }=0.\,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4fdfbefece31161a3058196a5de57c9f090c2591)
The quantity in parentheses above is just the field tensor, so this finally simplifies to

![Image 57: {\displaystyle \partial _{\mu }F^{\mu \nu }=\mu _{0}J^{\nu }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/64280a1794049909a4b2013fc9757fcac6ecf923)
That equation is another way of writing the two inhomogeneous [Maxwell's equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxwell%27s_equations "Maxwell's equations") (namely, [Gauss's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauss%27s_law "Gauss's law") and [Ampère's circuital law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amp%C3%A8re%27s_circuital_law "Ampère's circuital law")) using the substitutions:

![Image 58: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}{\frac {1}{c}}E^{i}&=-F^{0i}\\\epsilon ^{ijk}B_{k}&=-F^{ij}\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2e13637008119122e02e36b7754ba91d8a59fbc2)
where _i, j, k_ take the values 1, 2, and 3.

### Quantum electrodynamics and field theory

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Electromagnetic_tensor&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Quantum electrodynamics and field theory")]

The [Lagrangian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrangian_(field_theory) "Lagrangian (field theory)") of [quantum electrodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_electrodynamics "Quantum electrodynamics") extends beyond the classical Lagrangian established in relativity to incorporate the creation and annihilation of photons (and electrons):

![Image 73: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {L}}={\bar {\psi }}\left(i\hbar c\,\gamma ^{\alpha }D_{\alpha }-mc^{2}\right)\psi -{\frac {1}{4\mu _{0}}}F_{\alpha \beta }F^{\alpha \beta },}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6522f63a20089ac61a18a80cb20a8d730e7e2186)
where the first part in the right hand side, containing the [Dirac spinor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dirac_spinor "Dirac spinor")![Image 74: {\displaystyle \psi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/45e5789e5d9c8f7c79744f43ecaaf8ba42a8553a), represents the [Dirac field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dirac_field "Dirac field"). In [quantum field theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_field_theory "Quantum field theory") it is used as the template for the gauge field strength tensor. By being employed in addition to the local interaction Lagrangian it reprises its usual role in QED.
