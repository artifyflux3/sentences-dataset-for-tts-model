In [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry"), an **icosahedron** ( or [[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Icosahedron#cite_note-1)) is a [polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyhedron "Polyhedron") with 20 faces. The name comes from [Ancient Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language "Ancient Greek language")εἴκοσι _(eíkosi)_'twenty' and ἕδρα _(hédra)_'seat'. The plural can be either "icosahedra" () or "icosahedrons".

There are infinitely many non-[similar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Similarity_(geometry) "Similarity (geometry)") shapes of icosahedra, some of them being more symmetrical than others. The best known is the ([convex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_polyhedron "Convex polyhedron"), non-[stellated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellation "Stellation")) [regular icosahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron "Regular icosahedron")—one of the [Platonic solids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platonic_solid "Platonic solid")—whose faces are 20 [equilateral triangles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equilateral_triangle "Equilateral triangle").

Regular icosahedra
------------------

[![Image 4](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Icosahedron.png/250px-Icosahedron.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Icosahedron.png)

[![Image 5](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Great_icosahedron.png/250px-Great_icosahedron.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Great_icosahedron.png)

There are two objects, one convex and one nonconvex, that can both be called regular icosahedra. Each has 30 edges and 20 [equilateral triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equilateral_triangle "Equilateral triangle") faces with five meeting at each of its twelve vertices. Both have [icosahedral symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Icosahedral_symmetry "Icosahedral symmetry"). The term "regular icosahedron" generally refers to the convex variety, while the nonconvex form is called a _great icosahedron_.

### Convex regular icosahedron

[![Image 6](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Golden_rectangle_regular_icosahedron.jpg/250px-Golden_rectangle_regular_icosahedron.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Golden_rectangle_regular_icosahedron.jpg)

Three interlocking [golden rectangles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden_rectangle "Golden rectangle") inscribed in a con­vex regular icosahedron

The convex regular icosahedron is usually referred to simply as the _regular icosahedron_, one of the five regular [Platonic solids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platonic_solid "Platonic solid"), and is represented by its [Schläfli symbol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schl%C3%A4fli_symbol "Schläfli symbol") {3, 5}, containing 20 triangular faces, with 5 faces meeting around each vertex.

Its [dual polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_polyhedron "Dual polyhedron") is the [regular dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron "Regular dodecahedron") {5, 3} having three regular pentagonal faces around each vertex.

### Great icosahedron

[![Image 7: A detail of Spinoza monument in Amsterdam](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Icosahedron-spinoza.jpg/250px-Icosahedron-spinoza.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Icosahedron-spinoza.jpg)

A detail of [Spinoza](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baruch_Spinoza "Baruch Spinoza") monument in [Amsterdam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amsterdam "Amsterdam")

The [great icosahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_icosahedron "Great icosahedron") is one of the four regular star [Kepler–Poinsot polyhedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler%E2%80%93Poinsot_polyhedron "Kepler–Poinsot polyhedron"). Its [Schläfli symbol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schl%C3%A4fli_symbol "Schläfli symbol") is {3, ⁠5/2⁠}. Like the convex form, it also has 20 equilateral triangle faces, but its vertex figure is a [pentagram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentagram "Pentagram") rather than a pentagon, leading to geometrically intersecting faces. The intersections of the triangles do not represent new edges.

Its [dual polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_polyhedron "Dual polyhedron") is the [great stellated dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_stellated_dodecahedron "Great stellated dodecahedron") {⁠5/2⁠, 3}, having three regular star pentagonal faces around each vertex.

Stellated icosahedra
--------------------

[Stellation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellation "Stellation") is the process of extending the faces or edges of a polyhedron until they meet to form a new polyhedron. It is done symmetrically so that the resulting figure retains the overall symmetry of the parent figure.

In their book _[The Fifty-Nine Icosahedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The\_Fifty-Nine\_Icosahedra "The Fifty-Nine Icosahedra")_, Coxeter et al. enumerated 59 such stellations of the regular icosahedron.

Of these, many have a single face in each of the 20 face planes and so are also icosahedra. The great icosahedron is among them.

Other stellations have more than one face in each plane or form compounds of simpler polyhedra. These are not strictly icosahedra, although they are often referred to as such.

A _regular icosahedron_ can be distorted or marked up as a lower [pyritohedral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyritohedral "Pyritohedral") symmetry,[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Icosahedron#cite_note-Koca-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Icosahedron#cite_note-Baez-3) and is called a **snub octahedron**, **snub tetratetrahedron**, **snub tetrahedron**, and **pseudo-icosahedron**.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Icosahedron#cite_note-4) This can be seen as an [alternated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alternation_(geometry) "Alternation (geometry)")[truncated octahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Truncated_octahedron "Truncated octahedron"). If all the triangles are [equilateral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equilateral_triangle "Equilateral triangle"), the symmetry can also be distinguished by colouring the 8 and 12 triangle sets differently.

[Pyritohedral symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyritohedral_symmetry "Pyritohedral symmetry") has the symbol (3*2), [3+,4], with order 24. [Tetrahedral symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedral_symmetry "Tetrahedral symmetry") has the symbol (332), [3,3]+, with order 12. These lower symmetries allow geometric distortions from 20 equilateral triangular faces, instead having 8 equilateral triangles and 12 congruent [isosceles triangles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isosceles_triangle "Isosceles triangle").

### Cartesian coordinates

[![Image 57](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Truncated_octahedron_internal_rectangles.png/250px-Truncated_octahedron_internal_rectangles.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Truncated_octahedron_internal_rectangles.png)

Construction from the vertices of a [truncated octahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Truncated_octahedron "Truncated octahedron"), showing internal rectangles.

The [Cartesian coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinates "Cartesian coordinates") of the 12 vertices can be defined by the vectors defined by all the possible cyclic permutations and sign-flips of coordinates of the form (2, 1, 0). These coordinates represent the [truncated octahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Truncated_octahedron "Truncated octahedron") with [alternated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alternation_(geometry) "Alternation (geometry)") vertices deleted.

This construction is called a _snub tetrahedron_ in its regular icosahedron form, generated by the same operations carried out starting with the vector (_ϕ_, 1, 0), where _ϕ_ is the [golden ratio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden_ratio "Golden ratio").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Icosahedron#cite_note-Baez-3)

### Jessen's icosahedron

[![Image 58](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Jessen%27s_icosahedron.svg/250px-Jessen%27s_icosahedron.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Jessen%27s_icosahedron.svg)

Jessen's icosahedron

[![Image 59](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Icosahedral_tensegrity_structure.png/250px-Icosahedral_tensegrity_structure.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Icosahedral_tensegrity_structure.png)

A [tensegrity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensegrity "Tensegrity") Jessen's icosahedron

In Jessen's icosahedron, sometimes called _Jessen's orthogonal icosahedron_, the 12 isosceles faces are arranged differently so that the figure is non-convex and has [right](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_angle "Right angle")[dihedral angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihedral_angle "Dihedral angle").

It is [scissors congruent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert%27s_third_problem "Hilbert's third problem") to a cube, meaning that it can be sliced into smaller polyhedral pieces that can be rearranged to form a solid cube.

### Cuboctahedron

[![Image 60](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/7d/A3-P5-P3.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:A3-P5-P3.gif)

Progressions between an [octahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octahedron "Octahedron"), [pseudoicosahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudoicosahedron "Pseudoicosahedron"), and cuboctahedron. The cuboctahedron can flex this way even if its edges (but not its faces) are rigid.

A regular icosahedron is topologically identical to a [cuboctahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cuboctahedron "Cuboctahedron") with its 6 square faces bisected on diagonals with pyritohedral symmetry. The icosahedra with pyritohedral symmetry constitute an infinite family of polyhedra which include the cuboctahedron, regular icosahedron, Jessen's icosahedron, and [double cover](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orientable_double_cover "Orientable double cover") octahedron. Cyclical kinematic transformations among the members of this family exist.

### Rhombic icosahedron

The [rhombic icosahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhombic_icosahedron "Rhombic icosahedron") is a [zonohedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zonohedron "Zonohedron") made up of 20 congruent rhombs. It can be derived from the [rhombic triacontahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhombic_triacontahedron "Rhombic triacontahedron") by removing 10 middle faces. Even though all the faces are congruent, the rhombic icosahedron is not [face-transitive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Face-transitive "Face-transitive").

### Pyramid and prism symmetries

Common icosahedra with pyramid and prism symmetries include:

*   19-sided [pyramid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyramid "Pyramid") (plus 1 base = 20).
*   18-sided [prism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prism_(geometry) "Prism (geometry)") (plus 2 ends = 20).
*   9-sided [antiprism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiprism "Antiprism") (2 sets of 9 sides + 2 ends = 20).
*   10-sided [bipyramid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bipyramid "Bipyramid") (2 sets of 10 sides = 20).
*   10-sided [trapezohedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezohedron "Trapezohedron") (2 sets of 10 sides = 20).
