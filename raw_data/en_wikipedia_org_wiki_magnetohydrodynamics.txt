In [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics") and [engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineering "Engineering"), **magnetohydrodynamics** (**MHD**; also called **magneto-fluid dynamics** or **hydro­magnetics**) is a model of [electrically conducting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrically_conducting "Electrically conducting")[fluids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluid "Fluid") that treats all interpenetrating [particle species](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Particle_species&action=edit&redlink=1 "Particle species (page does not exist)") together as a single [continuous medium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuum_mechanics "Continuum mechanics"). It is primarily concerned with the low-frequency, large-scale, magnetic behavior in [plasmas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasma_(physics) "Plasma (physics)") and [liquid metals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liquid_metal "Liquid metal") and has applications in multiple fields including [space physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_physics "Space physics"), [geophysics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geophysics "Geophysics"), [astrophysics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrophysics "Astrophysics"), and [engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineering "Engineering").

The word _magneto­hydro­dynamics_ is derived from _\_[magneto-](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/magneto-#English "wikt:magneto-")\__ meaning [magnetic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_field "Magnetic field"), _\_[hydro-](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/hydro-#English "wikt:hydro-")\__ meaning water, and _\_[dynamics](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/dynamics#English "wikt:dynamics")\__ meaning movement. The field of MHD was initiated by [Hannes Alfvén](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hannes_Alfv%C3%A9n "Hannes Alfvén"), for which he received the [Nobel Prize in Physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physics "Nobel Prize in Physics") in 1970.

The MHD description of electrically conducting fluids was first developed by [Hannes Alfvén](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hannes_Alfv%C3%A9n "Hannes Alfvén") in a 1942 paper published in [_Nature_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nature_(journal) "Nature (journal)") titled "Existence of Electromagnetic–Hydrodynamic Waves" which outlined his discovery of what are now referred to as _[Alfvén waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alfv%C3%A9n\_wave "Alfvén wave")_.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamics#cite_note-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamics#cite_note-3) Alfvén initially referred to these waves as "electromagnetic–hydrodynamic waves"; however, in a later paper he noted, "As the term 'electromagnetic–hydrodynamic waves' is somewhat complicated, it may be convenient to call this phenomenon 'magneto–hydrodynamic' waves."[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamics#cite_note-alfven43-4)

In MHD, motion in the fluid is described using linear combinations of the mean motions of the individual [species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_species "Chemical species"): the [current density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Current_density "Current density")![Image 3: {\displaystyle \mathbf {J} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7686846b1a6b756cb514954000004ab5e7b2a5ba) and the [center of mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Center_of_mass "Center of mass") velocity ![Image 4: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35c1866e359fbfd2e0f606c725ba5cc37a5195d6). In a given fluid, each species ![Image 5: {\displaystyle \sigma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/59f59b7c3e6fdb1d0365a494b81fb9a696138c36) has a [number density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_density "Number density")![Image 6: {\displaystyle n_{\sigma }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/138bb83be8821f10b735a381ed38a0b7ddcd6ff4), mass ![Image 7: {\displaystyle m_{\sigma }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/479079231c5624f8fe8a4f577acfe2668b64c8bd), electric charge ![Image 8: {\displaystyle q_{\sigma }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/131b06ce5e46fc3a8b47360a1ccefd262736a473), and a mean velocity ![Image 9: {\displaystyle \mathbf {u} _{\sigma }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1d60241f25c27fd6a1d93f6393058d1d326fc751). MHD can be described by a set of equations consisting of a [continuity equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuity_equation "Continuity equation"), an equation of motion (the [Cauchy momentum equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cauchy_momentum_equation "Cauchy momentum equation")), an [equation of state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equation_of_state "Equation of state"), [Ampère's Law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amp%C3%A8re%27s_Law "Ampère's Law"), [Faraday's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Faraday%27s_law_of_induction#Maxwell%E2%80%93Faraday_equation "Faraday's law of induction"), and [Ohm's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ohm%27s_law "Ohm's law"). As with any fluid description to a kinetic system, a [closure approximation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_closure "Moment closure") must be applied to the highest moment of the particle distribution equation. This is often accomplished with approximations to the heat flux through a condition of [adiabaticity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adiabatic_process "Adiabatic process") or [isothermality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isothermal_process "Isothermal process").

The simplest form of MHD, **ideal MHD**, assumes that the resistive term ![Image 28: {\displaystyle \eta \mathbf {J} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f8aeb5591152235a1f18273452b6e54c531f1810) in [Ohm's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ohm%27s_law "Ohm's law") is small relative to the other terms such that it can be taken to be equal to zero. This occurs in the limit of large [magnetic Reynolds numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_Reynolds_number "Magnetic Reynolds number") during which [magnetic induction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_induction "Electromagnetic induction") dominates over [magnetic diffusion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_diffusion "Magnetic diffusion") at the velocity and [length scales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Length_scale "Length scale") under consideration.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamics#cite_note-bellan06-5) Consequently, processes in ideal MHD that convert magnetic energy into kinetic energy, referred to as _ideal processes_, cannot generate [heat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat "Heat") and raise [entropy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy "Entropy").[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamics#cite_note-priest00-7): 6

A fundamental concept underlying ideal MHD is the [frozen-in flux theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frozen-in_flux_theorem "Frozen-in flux theorem") which states that the bulk fluid and embedded magnetic field are constrained to move together such that one can be said to be "tied" or "frozen" to the other. Therefore, any two points that move with the bulk fluid velocity and lie on the same magnetic field line will continue to lie on the same field line even as the points are [advected](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Advected "Advected") by fluid flows in the system.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamics#cite_note-8)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamics#cite_note-priest00-7): 25  The connection between the fluid and magnetic field fixes the [topology of the magnetic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_topology "Magnetic topology") in the fluid—for example, if a set of magnetic field lines are tied into a knot, then they will remain so as long as the fluid has negligible resistivity. This difficulty in reconnecting magnetic field lines makes it possible to store energy by moving the fluid or the source of the magnetic field. The energy can then become available if the conditions for ideal MHD break down, allowing [magnetic reconnection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_reconnection "Magnetic reconnection") that releases the stored energy from the magnetic field.

### Applicability of ideal MHD to plasmas

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Magnetohydrodynamics&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Applicability of ideal MHD to plasmas")]

Ideal MHD is only strictly applicable when:

1.   The plasma is strongly collisional, so that the time scale of collisions is shorter than the other characteristic times in the system, and the particle distributions are therefore close to [Maxwellian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxwell%E2%80%93Boltzmann_distribution "Maxwell–Boltzmann distribution").
2.   The resistivity due to these collisions is small. In particular, the typical magnetic diffusion times over any scale length present in the system must be longer than any time scale of interest.
3.   Interest in length scales much longer than the ion [skin depth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasma_parameters#Lengths "Plasma parameters") and [Larmor radius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Larmor_radius "Larmor radius") perpendicular to the field, long enough along the field to ignore [Landau damping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Landau_damping "Landau damping"), and time scales much longer than the ion gyration time (system is smooth and slowly evolving).

### Importance of resistivity

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Magnetohydrodynamics&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Importance of resistivity")]

In an imperfectly conducting fluid the magnetic field can generally move through the fluid following a [diffusion law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffusion_equation "Diffusion equation") with the resistivity of the plasma serving as a [diffusion constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffusion_constant "Diffusion constant"). This means that solutions to the ideal MHD equations are only applicable for a limited time for a region of a given size before diffusion becomes too important to ignore. One can estimate the diffusion time across a [solar active region](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_active_region "Solar active region") (from collisional resistivity) to be hundreds to thousands of years, much longer than the actual lifetime of a sunspot—so it would seem reasonable to ignore the resistivity. By contrast, a meter-sized volume of seawater has a magnetic diffusion time measured in milliseconds.

Even in physical systems[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamics#cite_note-9)—which are large and conductive enough that simple estimates of the [Lundquist number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lundquist_number "Lundquist number") suggest that the resistivity can be ignored—resistivity may still be important: many [instabilities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Instability "Instability") exist that can increase the effective resistivity of the plasma by factors of more than 10 9. The enhanced resistivity is usually the result of the formation of small scale structure like current sheets or fine scale magnetic [turbulence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamic_turbulence "Magnetohydrodynamic turbulence"), introducing small spatial scales into the system over which ideal MHD is broken and magnetic diffusion can occur quickly. When this happens, magnetic reconnection may occur in the plasma to release stored magnetic energy as waves, bulk mechanical acceleration of material, [particle acceleration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_acceleration "Particle acceleration"), and heat.

Magnetic reconnection in highly conductive systems is important because it concentrates energy in time and space, so that gentle forces applied to a plasma for long periods of time can cause violent explosions and bursts of radiation.

When the fluid cannot be considered as completely conductive, but the other conditions for ideal MHD are satisfied, it is possible to use an extended model called resistive MHD. This includes an extra term in Ohm's Law which models the collisional resistivity. Generally MHD computer simulations are at least somewhat resistive because their computational grid introduces a [numerical resistivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Numerical_resistivity "Numerical resistivity").

[![Image 33](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Currents.jpg/250px-Currents.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Currents.jpg)

Schematic view of the different current systems which shape the Earth's magnetosphere

In many MHD systems most of the electric current is compressed into thin nearly-two-dimensional ribbons termed [current sheets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Current_sheet "Current sheet").[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamics#cite_note-10) These can divide the fluid into magnetic domains, inside of which the currents are relatively weak. Current sheets in the solar corona are thought to be between a few meters and a few kilometers in thickness, which is quite thin compared to the magnetic domains (which are thousands to hundreds of thousands of kilometers across).[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamics#cite_note-11) Another example is in the Earth's [magnetosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetosphere "Magnetosphere"), where current sheets separate topologically distinct domains, isolating most of the Earth's [ionosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ionosphere "Ionosphere") from the [solar wind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_wind "Solar wind").

The wave modes derived using the MHD equations are called **magnetohydrodynamic waves** or **MHD waves**. There are three MHD wave modes that can be derived from the linearized ideal-MHD equations for a fluid with a uniform and constant magnetic field:

*   Alfvén waves
*   Slow magnetosonic waves
*   Fast magnetosonic waves

The MHD oscillations will be damped if the fluid is not perfectly conducting but has a finite conductivity, or if viscous effects are present.

MHD waves and oscillations are a popular tool for the remote diagnostics of laboratory and astrophysical plasmas, for example, the [corona](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_corona "Solar corona") of the Sun ([Coronal seismology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coronal_seismology "Coronal seismology")).

Resistive Resistive MHD describes magnetized fluids with finite electron diffusivity (_η_ ≠ 0). This diffusivity leads to a breaking in the magnetic topology; magnetic field lines can 'reconnect' when they collide. Usually this term is small and reconnections can be handled by thinking of them as not dissimilar to [shocks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shocks_and_discontinuities_(magnetohydrodynamics) "Shocks and discontinuities (magnetohydrodynamics)"); this process has been shown to be important in the Earth-Solar magnetic interactions.Extended Extended MHD describes a class of phenomena in plasmas that are higher order than resistive MHD, but which can adequately be treated with a single fluid description. These include the effects of Hall physics, electron pressure gradients, finite Larmor Radii in the particle gyromotion, and electron inertia.Two-fluid Two-fluid MHD describes plasmas that include a non-negligible Hall [electric field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_field "Electric field"). As a result, the electron and ion momenta must be treated separately. ### Importance of kinetic effects

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Magnetohydrodynamics&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Importance of kinetic effects")]

Another limitation of MHD (and fluid theories in general) is that they depend on the assumption that the plasma is strongly collisional (this is the first criterion listed above), so that the time scale of collisions is shorter than the other characteristic times in the system, and the particle distributions are [Maxwellian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxwell%E2%80%93Boltzmann_distribution "Maxwell–Boltzmann distribution"). This is usually not the case in fusion, space and astrophysical plasmas. When this is not the case, or the interest is in smaller spatial scales, it may be necessary to use a kinetic model which properly accounts for the non-Maxwellian shape of the distribution function. However, because MHD is relatively simple and captures many of the important properties of plasma dynamics it is often qualitatively accurate and is therefore often the first model tried.

Effects which are essentially kinetic and not captured by fluid models include [double layers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double_layer_(plasma) "Double layer (plasma)"), [Landau damping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Landau_damping "Landau damping"), a wide range of instabilities, chemical separation in space plasmas and electron runaway. In the case of ultra-high intensity laser interactions, the incredibly short timescales of energy deposition mean that hydrodynamic codes fail to capture the essential physics.

Beneath the Earth's mantle lies the core, which is made up of two parts: the solid inner core and liquid outer core.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamics#cite_note-16)[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamics#cite_note-17) Both have significant quantities of [iron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron "Iron"). The liquid outer core moves in the presence of the magnetic field and eddies are set up into the same due to the [Coriolis effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coriolis_effect "Coriolis effect").[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamics#cite_note-18) These eddies develop a magnetic field which boosts Earth's original magnetic field—a process which is self-sustaining and is called the geomagnetic dynamo.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamics#cite_note-pbs-19)

[![Image 55](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/NASA_54559main_comparison1_strip.gif/350px-NASA_54559main_comparison1_strip.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:NASA_54559main_comparison1_strip.gif)

Reversals of [Earth's magnetic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_magnetic_field "Earth's magnetic field")

Based on the MHD equations, Glatzmaier and Paul Roberts have made a supercomputer model of the Earth's interior. After running the simulations for thousands of years in virtual time, the changes in Earth's magnetic field can be studied. The simulation results are in good agreement with the observations as the simulations have correctly predicted that the Earth's magnetic field flips every few hundred thousand years. During the flips, the magnetic field does not vanish altogether—it just gets more complex.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamics#cite_note-glatz-20)

Some monitoring stations have reported that [earthquakes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earthquakes "Earthquakes") are sometimes preceded by a spike in [ultra low frequency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultra_low_frequency "Ultra low frequency") (ULF) activity. A remarkable example of this occurred before the [1989 Loma Prieta earthquake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1989_Loma_Prieta_earthquake "1989 Loma Prieta earthquake") in [California](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/California "California"),[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamics#cite_note-21) although a subsequent study indicates that this was little more than a sensor malfunction.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamics#cite_note-22) On December 9, 2010, geoscientists announced that the [DEMETER](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Demeter_(satellite) "Demeter (satellite)") satellite observed a dramatic increase in ULF radio waves over [Haiti](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haiti "Haiti") in the month before the magnitude 7.0 M w[2010 earthquake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2010_Haiti_earthquake "2010 Haiti earthquake").[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamics#cite_note-23) Researchers are attempting to learn more about this correlation to find out whether this method can be used as part of an early warning system for earthquakes.

The study of space plasmas near [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth "Earth") and throughout the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System") is known as [space physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_physics "Space physics"). Areas researched within space physics encompass a large number of topics, ranging from the [ionosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ionosphere "Ionosphere") to [auroras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aurora "Aurora"), Earth's [magnetosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetosphere "Magnetosphere"), the [Solar wind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_wind "Solar wind"), and [coronal mass ejections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coronal_mass_ejection "Coronal mass ejection").

MHD forms the framework for understanding how populations of plasma interact within the local geospace environment. Researchers have developed global models using MHD to simulate phenomena within Earth's magnetosphere, such as the location of Earth's [magnetopause](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetopause "Magnetopause")[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamics#cite_note-24) (the boundary between the Earth's magnetic field and the solar wind), the formation of the [ring current](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ring_current "Ring current"), [auroral electrojets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrojet "Electrojet"),[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamics#cite_note-25) and [geomagnetically induced currents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geomagnetically_induced_current "Geomagnetically induced current").[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamics#cite_note-26)

One prominent use of global MHD models is in [space weather](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_weather "Space weather") forecasting. [Intense solar storms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_solar_storms "List of solar storms") have the potential to cause extensive damage to satellites[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamics#cite_note-27) and infrastructure, thus it is crucial that such events are detected early. The [Space Weather Prediction Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_Weather_Prediction_Center "Space Weather Prediction Center") (SWPC) runs MHD models to predict the arrival and impacts of space weather events at Earth.

MHD applies to [astrophysics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrophysics "Astrophysics"), including stars, the [interplanetary medium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_medium "Interplanetary medium") (space between the planets), and possibly within the [interstellar medium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_medium "Interstellar medium") (space between the stars) and [jets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_jet "Relativistic jet").[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamics#cite_note-28) Most astrophysical systems are not in local thermal equilibrium, and therefore require an additional kinematic treatment to describe all the phenomena within the system (see [Astrophysical plasma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrophysical_plasma "Astrophysical plasma")).[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamics#cite_note-29)[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamics#cite_note-30)

[Sunspots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sunspot "Sunspot") are caused by the Sun's magnetic fields, as [Joseph Larmor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Larmor "Joseph Larmor") theorized in 1919. The [solar wind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_wind "Solar wind") is also governed by MHD. The differential [solar rotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_rotation "Solar rotation") may be the long-term effect of magnetic drag at the poles of the Sun, an MHD phenomenon due to the [Parker spiral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parker_spiral "Parker spiral") shape assumed by the extended magnetic field of the Sun.

Previously, theories describing the formation of the Sun and planets could not explain how the Sun has 99.87% of the mass, yet only 0.54% of the [angular momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum "Angular momentum") in the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System"). In a [closed system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_system "Closed system") such as the cloud of gas and dust from which the Sun was formed, mass and angular momentum are both [conserved](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservation_law "Conservation law"). That conservation would imply that as the mass concentrated in the center of the cloud to form the Sun, it would spin faster, much like a skater pulling their arms in. The high speed of rotation predicted by early theories would have flung the [proto-Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protostar "Protostar") apart before it could have formed. However, magnetohydrodynamic effects transfer the Sun's angular momentum into the outer solar system, slowing its rotation.

Breakdown of ideal MHD (in the form of magnetic reconnection) is known to be the likely cause of [solar flares](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_flare "Solar flare"). The magnetic field in a solar [active region](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Active_region "Active region") over a sunspot can store energy that is released suddenly as a burst of motion, [X-rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray "X-ray"), and [radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiation "Radiation") when the main current sheet collapses, reconnecting the field.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamics#cite_note-31)[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamics#cite_note-32)

### Magnetic confinement fusion

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Magnetohydrodynamics&action=edit&section=17 "Edit section: Magnetic confinement fusion")]

MHD describes a wide range of physical phenomena occurring in fusion plasmas in devices such as [tokamaks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tokamak "Tokamak") or [stellarators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellarator "Stellarator").

The [Grad-Shafranov equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grad%E2%80%93Shafranov_equation "Grad–Shafranov equation") derived from ideal MHD describes the equilibrium of axisymmetric toroidal plasma in a tokamak. In tokamak experiments, the equilibrium during each discharge is routinely calculated and reconstructed, which provides information on the shape and position of the plasma controlled by currents in external coils.

[MHD stability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasma_stability "Plasma stability") theory is known to govern the operational limits of tokamaks. For example, the ideal MHD kink modes provide hard limits on the achievable plasma beta ([Troyon limit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Troyon_limit "Troyon limit")) and plasma current (set by the ![Image 56: {\displaystyle q>2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a51ff72f66c28051a819a034dea86997914ab599) requirement of the [safety factor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Safety_factor_(plasma_physics) "Safety factor (plasma physics)")).

In a tokamak, instabilities also emerge from resistive MHD. For instance, [tearing modes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tearing_mode "Tearing mode") are instabilities arising within the framework of non-ideal MHD.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamics#cite_note-33) This is an active field of research, since these instabilities are the starting point for disruptions.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamics#cite_note-34)

Magnetohydrodynamic sensors are used for precision measurements of [angular velocities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_velocity "Angular velocity") in [inertial navigation systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inertial_navigation_system "Inertial navigation system") such as in [aerospace engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aerospace_engineering "Aerospace engineering"). Accuracy improves with the size of the sensor. The sensor is capable of surviving in harsh environments.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamics#cite_note-35)

MHD is related to engineering problems such as [plasma confinement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fusion_power "Fusion power"), liquid-metal cooling of [nuclear reactors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_reactor "Nuclear reactor"), and [electromagnetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetism "Electromagnetism") casting (among others).

A [magnetohydrodynamic drive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamic_drive "Magnetohydrodynamic drive") or MHD propulsor is a method for propelling seagoing vessels using only electric and magnetic fields with no moving parts, using magnetohydrodynamics. The working principle involves electrification of the propellant (gas or water) which can then be directed by a magnetic field, pushing the vehicle in the opposite direction. Although some working prototypes exist, MHD drives remain impractical.

The first prototype of this kind of propulsion was built and tested in 1965 by Steward Way, a professor of mechanical engineering at the [University of California, Santa Barbara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_California,_Santa_Barbara "University of California, Santa Barbara"). Way, on leave from his job at [Westinghouse Electric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Westinghouse_Electric_(1886) "Westinghouse Electric (1886)"), assigned his senior-year undergraduate students to develop a submarine with this new propulsion system.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamics#cite_note-36) In the early 1990s, a foundation in Japan (Ship & Ocean Foundation (Minato-ku, Tokyo)) built an experimental boat, the _[Yamato-1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yamato\_1 "Yamato 1")_, which used a magnetohydrodynamic drive incorporating a [superconductor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superconductor "Superconductor") cooled by [liquid helium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium "Helium"), and could travel at 15 km/h.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamics#cite_note-yamato-37)

[MHD power generation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MHD_generator "MHD generator") fueled by potassium-seeded coal combustion gas showed potential for more efficient energy conversion (the absence of solid moving parts allows operation at higher temperatures), but failed due to cost-prohibitive technical difficulties.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamics#cite_note-38) One major engineering problem was the failure of the wall of the primary-coal combustion chamber due to abrasion.

In [microfluidics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microfluidics "Microfluidics"), MHD is studied as a fluid pump for producing a continuous, nonpulsating flow in a complex microchannel design.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamics#cite_note-Nguyen-39)

MHD can be implemented in the [continuous casting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_casting "Continuous casting") process of metals to suppress instabilities and control the flow.[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamics#cite_note-40)[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamics#cite_note-41)

Industrial MHD problems can be modeled using the open-source software EOF-Library.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamics#cite_note-42) Two simulation examples are 3D MHD with a free surface for [electromagnetic levitation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_levitation "Magnetic levitation") melting,[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamics#cite_note-43) and liquid metal stirring by rotating permanent magnets.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamics#cite_note-44)

### Magnetic drug targeting

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Magnetohydrodynamics&action=edit&section=20 "Edit section: Magnetic drug targeting")]

An important task in cancer research is developing more precise methods for delivery of medicine to affected areas. One method involves the binding of medicine to biologically compatible magnetic particles (such as ferrofluids), which are guided to the target via careful placement of permanent magnets on the external body. Magnetohydrodynamic equations and finite element analysis are used to study the interaction between the magnetic fluid particles in the bloodstream and the external magnetic field.[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamics#cite_note-45)
