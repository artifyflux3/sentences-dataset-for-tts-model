Using the Pythagorean theorem to compute two-dimensional Euclidean distance

In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), the **Euclidean distance** between two [points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_(geometry) "Point (geometry)") in [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space") is the [length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Length "Length") of the [line segment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_segment "Line segment") between them. It can be calculated from the [Cartesian coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate "Cartesian coordinate") of the points using the [Pythagorean theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagorean_theorem "Pythagorean theorem"), and therefore is occasionally called the **Pythagorean distance**.

These names come from the ancient [Greek mathematicians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_mathematics "Greek mathematics")[Euclid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid "Euclid") and [Pythagoras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras "Pythagoras"). In the Greek [deductive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deductive "Deductive")[geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry") exemplified by Euclid's [_Elements_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid%27s_Elements "Euclid's Elements"), distances were not represented as numbers but line segments of the same length, which were considered "equal". The notion of distance is inherent in the [compass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compass_(drawing_tool) "Compass (drawing tool)") tool used to draw a [circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle "Circle"), whose points all have the same distance from a common [center point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Center_(geometry) "Center (geometry)"). The connection from the Pythagorean theorem to distance calculation was not made until the 18th century.

The distance between two objects that are not points is usually defined to be the smallest distance among pairs of points from the two objects. Formulas are known for computing distances between different types of objects, such as the [distance from a point to a line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distance_from_a_point_to_a_line "Distance from a point to a line"). In advanced mathematics, the concept of distance has been generalized to abstract [metric spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space "Metric space"), and other distances than Euclidean have been studied. In some applications in [statistics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistics "Statistics") and [optimization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_optimization "Mathematical optimization"), the square of the Euclidean distance is used instead of the distance itself.

The distance between any two points on the [real line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_line "Real line") is the [absolute value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_value "Absolute value") of the numerical difference of their coordinates, their [absolute difference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_difference "Absolute difference"). Thus if ![Image 3: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) and ![Image 4: {\displaystyle q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/06809d64fa7c817ffc7e323f85997f783dbdf71d) are two points on the real line, then the distance between them is given by:[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_distance#cite_note-smith-1)

![Image 5: {\displaystyle d(p,q)=|p-q|.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/358aac90f70a828c1214ac8630e41c13926cae21)

A more complicated formula, giving the same value, but generalizing more readily to higher dimensions, is:[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_distance#cite_note-smith-1)

![Image 6: {\displaystyle d(p,q)={\sqrt {(p-q)^{2}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7d75418dbec9482dbcb70f9063ad66e9cf7b5db9)

In this formula, [squaring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_(algebra) "Square (algebra)") and then taking the [square root](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_root "Square root") leaves any positive number unchanged, but replaces any negative number by its absolute value.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_distance#cite_note-smith-1)

In the [Euclidean plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_plane "Euclidean plane"), let point ![Image 7: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) have [Cartesian coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinates "Cartesian coordinates")![Image 8: {\displaystyle (p_{1},p_{2})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/aaa988e111a7acf37bf178e4a067e7db92a7a9fe) and let point ![Image 9: {\displaystyle q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/06809d64fa7c817ffc7e323f85997f783dbdf71d) have coordinates ![Image 10: {\displaystyle (q_{1},q_{2})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d78a4cde04374e91fd7c7af6c094dad1c0f37f16). Then the distance between ![Image 11: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) and ![Image 12: {\displaystyle q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/06809d64fa7c817ffc7e323f85997f783dbdf71d) is given by:[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_distance#cite_note-cohen-2)

![Image 13: {\displaystyle d(p,q)={\sqrt {(p_{1}-q_{1})^{2}+(p_{2}-q_{2})^{2}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9c0157084fd89f5f3d462efeedc47d3d7aa0b773)

This can be seen by applying the [Pythagorean theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagorean_theorem "Pythagorean theorem") to a [right triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_triangle "Right triangle") with horizontal and vertical sides, having the line segment from ![Image 14: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) to ![Image 15: {\displaystyle q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/06809d64fa7c817ffc7e323f85997f783dbdf71d) as its [hypotenuse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypotenuse "Hypotenuse"). The two squared formulas inside the square root give the areas of squares on the horizontal and vertical sides, and the outer square root converts the area of the square on the hypotenuse into the length of the hypotenuse.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_distance#cite_note-3) In terms of the [Pythagorean addition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagorean_addition "Pythagorean addition") operation ![Image 16: {\displaystyle \oplus }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8b16e2bdaefee9eed86d866e6eba3ac47c710f60), available in many [software libraries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Software_library "Software library") as `hypot`, the same formula can be expressed as:[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_distance#cite_note-4)

### Objects other than points

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Euclidean_distance&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Objects other than points")]

For pairs of objects that are not both points, the distance can most simply be defined as the smallest distance between any two points from the two objects, although more complicated generalizations from points to sets such as [Hausdorff distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hausdorff_distance "Hausdorff distance") are also commonly used.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_distance#cite_note-7) Formulas for computing distances between different types of objects include:

*   The [distance from a point to a line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distance_from_a_point_to_a_line "Distance from a point to a line"), in the Euclidean plane[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_distance#cite_note-baljer-8)
*   The [distance from a point to a plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distance_from_a_point_to_a_plane "Distance from a point to a plane") in three-dimensional Euclidean space[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_distance#cite_note-baljer-8)
*   The [distance between two lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skew_lines#Distance "Skew lines") in three-dimensional Euclidean space[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_distance#cite_note-9)

The distance from a point to a [curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curve "Curve") can be used to define its [parallel curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_curve "Parallel curve"), another curve all of whose points have the same distance to the given curve.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_distance#cite_note-10)

The Euclidean distance is the prototypical example of the distance in a [metric space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space "Metric space"),[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_distance#cite_note-11) and obeys all the defining properties of a metric space:[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_distance#cite_note-strichartz-12)

Another property, [Ptolemy's inequality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ptolemy%27s_inequality "Ptolemy's inequality"), concerns the Euclidean distances among four points ![Image 32: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36), ![Image 33: {\displaystyle q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/06809d64fa7c817ffc7e323f85997f783dbdf71d), ![Image 34: {\displaystyle r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d1ecb613aa2984f0576f70f86650b7c2a132538), and ![Image 35: {\displaystyle s}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/01d131dfd7673938b947072a13a9744fe997e632). It states that

![Image 36: {\displaystyle d(p,q)\cdot d(r,s)+d(q,r)\cdot d(p,s)\geq d(p,r)\cdot d(q,s).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8e97fd9961c4f479bb1528a56f5a5a3cbdcae650)

For points in the plane, this can be rephrased as stating that for every [quadrilateral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral "Quadrilateral"), the products of opposite sides of the quadrilateral sum to at least as large a number as the product of its diagonals. However, Ptolemy's inequality applies more generally to points in Euclidean spaces of any dimension, no matter how they are arranged.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_distance#cite_note-13) For points in metric spaces that are not Euclidean spaces, this inequality may not be true. Euclidean [distance geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distance_geometry "Distance geometry") studies properties of Euclidean distance such as Ptolemy's inequality, and their application in testing whether given sets of distances come from points in a Euclidean space.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_distance#cite_note-14)

According to the [Beckman–Quarles theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beckman%E2%80%93Quarles_theorem "Beckman–Quarles theorem"), any transformation of the Euclidean plane or of a higher-dimensional Euclidean space that preserves unit distances must be an [isometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isometry "Isometry"), preserving all distances.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_distance#cite_note-15)

Squared Euclidean distance
--------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Euclidean_distance&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Squared Euclidean distance")]

[![Image 37](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/3d-function-5.svg/250px-3d-function-5.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:3d-function-5.svg)

A [cone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cone "Cone"), the [graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_of_a_function "Graph of a function") of Euclidean distance from the origin in the plane

[![Image 38](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/3d-function-2.svg/250px-3d-function-2.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:3d-function-2.svg)

A [paraboloid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paraboloid "Paraboloid"), the graph of squared Euclidean distance from the origin

In many applications, and in particular when comparing distances, it may be more convenient to omit the final square root in the calculation of Euclidean distances, as the square root does not change the order (![Image 39: {\displaystyle d_{1}^{2}>d_{2}^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c365fe57a77c5637cf4303bb0c3ef0e1550c851b) if and only if ![Image 40: {\displaystyle d_{1}>d_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9c885929dfa04444c7d9021c9beeedd01b6debb9)). The value resulting from this omission is the [square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_(algebra) "Square (algebra)") of the Euclidean distance, and is called the **squared Euclidean distance**.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_distance#cite_note-spencer-16) For instance, the [Euclidean minimum spanning tree](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_minimum_spanning_tree "Euclidean minimum spanning tree") can be determined using only the ordering between distances, and not their numeric values. Comparing squared distances produces the same result but avoids an unnecessary square-root calculation and sidesteps issues of numerical precision.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_distance#cite_note-17) As an equation, the squared distance can be expressed as a [sum of squares](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sum_of_squares "Sum of squares"):

![Image 41: {\displaystyle d^{2}(p,q)=(p_{1}-q_{1})^{2}+(p_{2}-q_{2})^{2}+\cdots +(p_{n}-q_{n})^{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cb4f9e37dfd3283783b68a02b2af802d49a08e2b)

Beyond its application to distance comparison, squared Euclidean distance is of central importance in [statistics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistics "Statistics"), where it is used in the method of [least squares](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Least_squares "Least squares"), a standard method of fitting statistical estimates to data by minimizing the average of the squared distances between observed and estimated values,[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_distance#cite_note-18) and as the simplest form of [divergence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divergence_(statistics) "Divergence (statistics)") to compare [probability distributions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability_distribution "Probability distribution").[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_distance#cite_note-19) The addition of squared distances to each other, as is done in least squares fitting, corresponds to an operation on (unsquared) distances called [Pythagorean addition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagorean_addition "Pythagorean addition").[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_distance#cite_note-20) In [cluster analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cluster_analysis "Cluster analysis"), squared distances can be used to strengthen the effect of longer distances.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_distance#cite_note-spencer-16)

Squared Euclidean distance does not form a metric space, as it does not satisfy the triangle inequality.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_distance#cite_note-21) However it is a smooth, strictly [convex function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_function "Convex function") of the two points, unlike the distance, which is non-smooth (near pairs of equal points) and convex but not strictly convex. The squared distance is thus preferred in [optimization theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optimization_theory "Optimization theory"), since it allows [convex analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_analysis "Convex analysis") to be used. Since squaring is a [monotonic function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monotonic_function "Monotonic function") of non-negative values, minimizing squared distance is equivalent to minimizing the Euclidean distance, so the optimization problem is equivalent in terms of either, but easier to solve using squared distance.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_distance#cite_note-22)

The collection of all squared distances between pairs of points from a finite set may be stored in a [Euclidean distance matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_distance_matrix "Euclidean distance matrix"), and is used in this form in distance geometry.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_distance#cite_note-23)

In more advanced areas of mathematics, when viewing Euclidean space as a [vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space"), its distance is associated with a [norm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norm_(mathematics) "Norm (mathematics)") called the [Euclidean norm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norm_(mathematics)#Euclidean_norm "Norm (mathematics)"), defined as the distance of each vector from the [origin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Origin_(mathematics) "Origin (mathematics)"). One of the important properties of this norm, relative to other norms, is that it remains unchanged under arbitrary rotations of space around the origin.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_distance#cite_note-24) By [Dvoretzky's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dvoretzky%27s_theorem "Dvoretzky's theorem"), every finite-dimensional [normed vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normed_vector_space "Normed vector space") has a high-dimensional subspace on which the norm is approximately Euclidean; the Euclidean norm is the only norm with this property.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_distance#cite_note-25) It can be extended to infinite-dimensional vector spaces as the [_L_ 2 norm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lp_space "Lp space") or _L_ 2 distance.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_distance#cite_note-26) The Euclidean distance gives Euclidean space the structure of a [topological space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_space "Topological space"), the [Euclidean topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_topology "Euclidean topology"), with the [open balls](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_ball "Open ball") (subsets of points at less than a given distance from a given point) as its [neighborhoods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neighbourhood_(mathematics) "Neighbourhood (mathematics)").[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_distance#cite_note-27)

[![Image 42](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Minkowski_distance_examples.svg/250px-Minkowski_distance_examples.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Minkowski_distance_examples.svg)

Comparison of Chebyshev, Euclidean and taxicab distances for the hypotenuse of a 3-4-5 triangle on a chessboard

Other common distances in [real coordinate spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_coordinate_space "Real coordinate space") and [function spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_space "Function space"):[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_distance#cite_note-28)

*   [Chebyshev distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chebyshev_distance "Chebyshev distance") (_L_∞ distance), which measures distance as the maximum of the distances in each coordinate.
*   [Taxicab distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taxicab_distance "Taxicab distance") (_L_ 1 distance), also called Manhattan distance, which measures distance as the sum of the distances in each coordinate.
*   [Minkowski distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minkowski_distance "Minkowski distance") (_L_ _p_ distance), a generalization that unifies Euclidean distance, taxicab distance, and Chebyshev distance.

For points on surfaces in three dimensions, the Euclidean distance should be distinguished from the [geodesic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic "Geodesic") distance, the length of a shortest curve that belongs to the surface. In particular, for measuring great-circle distances on the Earth or other spherical or near-spherical surfaces, distances that have been used include the [haversine distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haversine_distance "Haversine distance") giving great-circle distances between two points on a sphere from their longitudes and latitudes, and [Vincenty's formulae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vincenty%27s_formulae "Vincenty's formulae") also known as "Vincent distance" for distance on a spheroid.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_distance#cite_note-29)

Euclidean distance is the distance in [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space"). Both concepts are named after ancient Greek mathematician [Euclid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid "Euclid"), whose [_Elements_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid%27s_Elements "Euclid's Elements") became a standard textbook in geometry for many centuries.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_distance#cite_note-30) Concepts of [length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Length "Length") and [distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distance "Distance") are widespread across cultures, can be dated to the earliest surviving "protoliterate" bureaucratic documents from [Sumer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sumer "Sumer") in the fourth millennium BC (far before Euclid),[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_distance#cite_note-31) and have been hypothesized to develop in children earlier than the related concepts of speed and time.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_distance#cite_note-32) But the notion of a distance, as a number defined from two points, does not actually appear in Euclid's _Elements_. Instead, Euclid approaches this concept implicitly, through the [congruence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congruence_(geometry) "Congruence (geometry)") of line segments, through the comparison of lengths of line segments, and through the concept of [proportionality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proportionality_(mathematics) "Proportionality (mathematics)").[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_distance#cite_note-33)

The [Pythagorean theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagorean_theorem "Pythagorean theorem") is also ancient, but it could only take its central role in the measurement of distances after the invention of [Cartesian coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinates "Cartesian coordinates") by [René Descartes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Descartes "René Descartes") in 1637. The distance formula itself was first published in 1731 by [Alexis Clairaut](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexis_Clairaut "Alexis Clairaut").[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_distance#cite_note-34) Because of this formula, Euclidean distance is also sometimes called Pythagorean distance.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_distance#cite_note-35) Although accurate measurements of long distances on the Earth's surface, which are not Euclidean, had again been studied in many cultures since ancient times (see [history of geodesy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_geodesy "History of geodesy")), the idea that Euclidean distance might not be the only way of measuring distances between points in mathematical spaces came even later, with the 19th-century formulation of [non-Euclidean geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-Euclidean_geometry "Non-Euclidean geometry").[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_distance#cite_note-36) The definition of the Euclidean norm and Euclidean distance for geometries of more than three dimensions also first appeared in the 19th century, in the work of [Augustin-Louis Cauchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Augustin-Louis_Cauchy "Augustin-Louis Cauchy").[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_distance#cite_note-37)
