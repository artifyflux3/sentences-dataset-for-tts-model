The _**physics convention**_. Spherical coordinates (**r**, **θ**, **φ**) as commonly used: ([ISO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization "International Organization for Standardization")[80000-2:2019](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO/IEC_80000 "ISO/IEC 80000")): radial distance r ([slant distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slant_distance "Slant distance") to origin), polar angle θ ([theta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theta "Theta")) (angle with respect to positive polar axis), and azimuthal angle φ ([phi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phi "Phi")) (angle of rotation from the initial meridian plane). _**This is the convention followed in this article.**_

In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), a **spherical coordinate system** specifies a given point in [three-dimensional space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_space "Three-dimensional space") by using a distance and two angles as its three [coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_system "Coordinate system"). These are

*   the **radial distance****r** along the line connecting the point to a fixed point called the [origin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Origin_(mathematics) "Origin (mathematics)");
*   the **polar angle****θ** between this radial line and a given _polar axis_;[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_coordinate_system#cite_note-1) and
*   the **azimuthal angle****φ**, which is the [angle of rotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle_of_rotation "Angle of rotation") of the radial line around the polar axis.[[b]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_coordinate_system#cite_note-2)

(See graphic regarding the "physics convention".)

Once the radius is fixed, the three coordinates (_r_, _θ_, _φ_), known as a 3-[tuple](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tuple "Tuple"), provide a coordinate system on a [sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere "Sphere"), typically called the **spherical polar coordinates**. The [plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_(geometry) "Plane (geometry)") passing through the origin and [perpendicular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular "Perpendicular") to the polar axis (where the polar angle is a [right angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_angle "Right angle")) is called the _**reference plane**_ (sometimes _[fundamental plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental\_plane\_(spherical\_coordinates) "Fundamental plane (spherical coordinates)")_).

The physics convention is followed in this article; (See both graphics re "physics convention" and re "mathematics convention".)

The radial distance from the fixed point of origin is also called the _radius_, or _radial line_, or _radial coordinate_. The polar angle may be called _[inclination angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inclination\_angle "Inclination angle")_, _[zenith angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zenith\_angle "Zenith angle")_, _[normal angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal\_vector "Normal vector")_, or the _[colatitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colatitude "Colatitude")_. The user may choose to replace the inclination angle by its [complement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_complement "Angular complement"), the _[elevation angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elevation\_angle "Elevation angle")_ (or _[altitude angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Altitude\_angle "Altitude angle")_), measured upward between the reference plane and the radial line—i.e., from the reference plane upward (towards to the positive z-axis) to the radial line. The _depression angle_ is the negative of the elevation angle. _(See graphic re the "physics convention"—not "mathematics convention".)_

Both the use of symbols and the naming order of tuple coordinates differ among the several sources and disciplines. This article will use the ISO convention[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_coordinate_system#cite_note-3) frequently encountered in _physics_, where the naming tuple gives the order as: radial distance, polar angle, azimuthal angle, or _![Image 2: {\displaystyle (r,\theta ,\varphi )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest\_v1/media/math/render/svg/d0fbf2e667b8c94c169165fc44e0d73e66c901a9)_. (See graphic re the "physics convention".) In contrast, the conventions in many mathematics books and texts give the naming order differently as: radial distance, "azimuthal angle", "polar angle", and ![Image 3: {\displaystyle (\rho ,\theta ,\varphi )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0ea69ccecb586b659206091cb824b2bbf51b5498) or ![Image 4: {\displaystyle (r,\theta ,\varphi )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d0fbf2e667b8c94c169165fc44e0d73e66c901a9)—which switches the uses and meanings of symbols θ and φ. Other conventions may also be used, such as _r_ for a radius from the _z-_ axis that is not from the point of origin. Particular care must be taken to check the meaning of the symbols.

[![Image 5](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/3D_Spherical_2.svg/250px-3D_Spherical_2.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:3D_Spherical_2.svg)

The _**mathematics convention**_. Spherical coordinates (_r_, _θ_, _φ_) as typically used: radial distance r, azimuthal angle θ, and polar angle φ. + _The meanings of θ and φ have been swapped_—compared to the **physics convention**. The 'south'-direction x-axis is depicted but the 'north'-direction x-axis is not. (As in physics, ρ ([rho](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rho "Rho")) is often used instead of r to avoid confusion with the value r in cylindrical and 2D polar coordinates.)

According to the conventions of [geographical coordinate systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geographical_coordinate_system "Geographical coordinate system"), positions are measured by latitude, longitude, and height (altitude). There are a number of [celestial coordinate systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celestial_coordinate_system "Celestial coordinate system") based on different [fundamental planes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_plane_(spherical_coordinates) "Fundamental plane (spherical coordinates)") and with different terms for the various coordinates. The spherical coordinate systems used in mathematics normally use [radians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radian "Radian") rather than [degrees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_(angle) "Degree (angle)"); (note 90 degrees equals π⁄2 radians). And these systems of the _mathematics convention_ may measure the azimuthal angle _counterclockwise_ (i.e., from the south direction x-axis, or 180°, towards the east direction y-axis, or +90°)—rather than measure _clockwise_ (i.e., from the north direction x-axis, or 0°, towards the east direction y-axis, or +90°), as done in the [horizontal coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horizontal_coordinate_system "Horizontal coordinate system").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_coordinate_system#cite_note-4)_(See graphic re "mathematics convention".)_

The spherical coordinate system of the _physics convention_ can be seen as a generalization of the [polar coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_coordinate_system "Polar coordinate system") in [three-dimensional space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_space "Three-dimensional space"). It can be further extended to higher-dimensional spaces, and is then referred to as a [_hyperspherical coordinate system_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/N-sphere#Spherical_coordinates "N-sphere").

To define a spherical coordinate system, one must designate an _origin_ point in space, _O_, and two orthogonal directions: the _zenith reference_ direction and the _azimuth reference_ direction. These choices determine a reference plane that is typically defined as containing the point of origin and the _x– and y–axes_, either of which may be designated as the _azimuth reference_ direction. The reference plane is perpendicular (orthogonal) to the zenith direction, and typically is designated "horizontal" to the zenith direction's "vertical". The spherical coordinates of a point P then are defined as follows:

*   The _radius_ or _radial distance_ is the [Euclidean distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_distance "Euclidean distance") from the origin _O_ to _P_.
*   The _inclination_ (or _polar angle_) is the signed angle from the zenith reference direction to the line segment OP. (_Elevation_ may be used as the polar angle instead of _inclination_; see below.)
*   The _[azimuth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Azimuth "Azimuth")_ (or _azimuthal angle_) is the signed angle measured from the _azimuth reference_ direction to the orthogonal projection of the radial line segment OP on the reference plane.

The sign of the azimuth is determined by designating the rotation that is the _positive_ sense of turning about the zenith. This choice is arbitrary, and is part of the coordinate system definition. (If the inclination is either zero or 180 degrees (= π radians), the azimuth is arbitrary. If the radius is zero, both azimuth and inclination are arbitrary.)

The [_elevation_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horizontal_coordinate_system "Horizontal coordinate system") is the signed angle from the x-y reference plane to the radial line segment OP, where positive angles are designated as upward, towards the zenith reference. _Elevation_ is 90 degrees (= ⁠π/2⁠ radians) _minus inclination_. Thus, if the inclination is 60 degrees (= ⁠π/3⁠ radians), then the elevation is 30 degrees (= ⁠π/6⁠ radians).

In [linear algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_algebra "Linear algebra"), the [vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_vector "Euclidean vector") from the origin O to the point P is often called the _[position vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Position\_vector "Position vector")_ of _P_.

Several different conventions exist for representing spherical coordinates and prescribing the naming order of their symbols. The 3-tuple number set ![Image 6: {\displaystyle (r,\theta ,\varphi )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d0fbf2e667b8c94c169165fc44e0d73e66c901a9) denotes radial distance, the polar angle—"inclination", or as the alternative, "elevation"—and the azimuthal angle. It is the common practice within the physics convention, as specified by [ISO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization "International Organization for Standardization") standard [80000-2:2019](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO_80000-2 "ISO 80000-2"), and earlier in [ISO 31-11](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO_31-11 "ISO 31-11") (1992).

_As stated above, this article describes the ISO "physics convention"—unless otherwise noted._

However, some authors (including mathematicians) use the symbol _ρ_ (rho) for radius, or radial distance, _φ_ for inclination (or elevation) and _θ_ for azimuth—while others keep the use of _r_ for the radius; all which "provides a logical extension of the usual polar coordinates notation".[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_coordinate_system#cite_note-http://mathworld.wolfram.com/SphericalCoordinates.html-5) As to order, some authors list the azimuth _before_ the inclination (or the elevation) angle. Some combinations of these choices result in a [left-handed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right-hand_rule "Right-hand rule") coordinate system. The standard "physics convention" 3-tuple set ![Image 7: {\displaystyle (r,\theta ,\varphi )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d0fbf2e667b8c94c169165fc44e0d73e66c901a9) conflicts with the usual notation for two-dimensional [polar coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_coordinate_system "Polar coordinate system") and three-dimensional [cylindrical coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylindrical_coordinate_system "Cylindrical coordinate system"), where θ is often used for the azimuth.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_coordinate_system#cite_note-http://mathworld.wolfram.com/SphericalCoordinates.html-5)

Angles are typically measured in [degrees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_(angle) "Degree (angle)") (°) or in [radians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radian "Radian") (rad), where 360°=2 π rad. The use of degrees is most common in geography, astronomy, and engineering, where radians are commonly used in mathematics and [theoretical physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theoretical_physics "Theoretical physics"). The unit for radial distance is usually determined by the context, as occurs in applications of the 'unit sphere', see [applications](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_coordinate_system#Applications).

When the system is used to designate physical three-space, it is customary to assign positive to azimuth angles measured in the _counterclockwise_ sense from the reference direction on the reference plane—as seen from the "zenith" side of the plane. This convention is used in particular for geographical coordinates, where the "zenith" direction is [north](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North "North") and the positive azimuth (longitude) angles are measured eastwards from some [prime meridian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_meridian "Prime meridian").

In the case of (_U_, _S_, _E_) the local [azimuth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Azimuth "Azimuth") angle would be measured [counterclockwise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Counterclockwise "Counterclockwise") from S to E.

Any spherical coordinate triplet (or tuple) ![Image 8: {\displaystyle (r,\theta ,\varphi )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d0fbf2e667b8c94c169165fc44e0d73e66c901a9) specifies a single point of three-dimensional space. On the reverse view, any single point has infinitely many equivalent spherical coordinates. That is, the user can add or subtract any number of full turns to the angular measures without changing the angles themselves, and therefore without changing the point. It is convenient in many contexts to use negative radial distances, the convention being ![Image 9: {\displaystyle (-r,\theta ,\varphi )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c4303c15d79785d06c7524a3122212e0b3ae84cf), which is equivalent to ![Image 10: {\displaystyle (r,\theta {+}180^{\circ },\varphi )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cab7e4cba37608e0142734802ddf411f8b8ed421) or ![Image 11: {\displaystyle (r,90^{\circ }{-}\theta ,\varphi {+}180^{\circ })}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d2618130ceec28d3e8fec406305a5f6f6ca2c32a) for any r, θ, and φ. Moreover, ![Image 12: {\displaystyle (r,-\theta ,\varphi )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/beb79911beca5b2e82ba695b1a0b28fa6234141c) is equivalent to ![Image 13: {\displaystyle (r,\theta ,\varphi {+}180^{\circ })}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f7047b347ebddd28dc07756b28171bca656dedf7).

When necessary to define a unique set of spherical coordinates for each point, the user must restrict the [range, aka interval](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interval_(mathematics) "Interval (mathematics)"), of each coordinate. A common choice is:

*   radial distance: _r_ ≥ 0,
*   polar angle: 0° ≤ _θ_ ≤ 180°, or 0 rad ≤ _θ_ ≤ π rad,
*   azimuth: 0° ≤ _φ_< 360°, or 0 rad ≤ _φ_< 2 π rad.

But instead of the interval [0°, 360°), the azimuth φ is typically restricted to the [half-open interval](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interval_(mathematics)#Definitions "Interval (mathematics)")(−180°, +180°], or (−π, +π ] radians, which is the standard convention for geographic longitude.

For the polar angle θ, the range (interval) for inclination is [0°, 180°], which is equivalent to elevation range (interval) [−90°, +90°]. In geography, the latitude is the elevation.

Even with these restrictions, if the polar angle (inclination) is 0° or 180°—elevation is −90° or +90°—then the azimuth angle is arbitrary; and if r is zero, both azimuth and polar angles are arbitrary. To define the coordinates as unique, the user can assert the convention that (in these cases) the arbitrary coordinates are set to zero.

To plot any dot from its spherical coordinates (_r_, _θ_, _φ_), where θ is inclination, the user would: move r units from the origin in the zenith reference direction (z-axis); then rotate by the amount of the azimuth angle (φ) about the origin _from_ the designated _azimuth reference_ direction, (i.e., either the x- or y-axis, see [Definition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_coordinate_system#Definition), above); and then rotate _from_ the z-axis by the amount of the θ angle.

The radial distance upward along the [zenith–axis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zenith "Zenith") from the point of origin to the surface of the sphere is assigned the value unity, or 1. + In this image, r appears to equal 4/6, or .67, (of unity); i.e., four of the six 'nested shells' to the surface. (Note the 'full' rotation, or inclination, from the zenith–axis to the y–axis is 90°).

Just as the two-dimensional [Cartesian coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system "Cartesian coordinate system") is useful—has a wide set of applications—on a planar surface, a two-dimensional spherical coordinate system is useful on the surface of a sphere. For example, one sphere that is described in _Cartesian coordinates_ with the equation _x_ 2 + _y_ 2 + _z_ 2 = _c_ 2 can be described in _spherical coordinates_ by the simple equation _r_ = _c_. (In this system—_shown here in the mathematics convention_—the sphere is adapted as a [unit sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_sphere "Unit sphere"), where the radius is set to unity and then can generally be ignored, see graphic.)

This (unit sphere) simplification is also useful when dealing with objects such as [rotational matrices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation_matrix "Rotation matrix"). Spherical coordinates are also useful in analyzing systems that have some degree of symmetry about a point, including: [volume integrals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiple_integral "Multiple integral") inside a sphere; the potential energy field surrounding a concentrated mass or charge; or global weather simulation in a planet's atmosphere.

[![Image 15](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Bosch_36W_column_loudspeaker_polar_pattern.png/330px-Bosch_36W_column_loudspeaker_polar_pattern.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Bosch_36W_column_loudspeaker_polar_pattern.png)

The output pattern of the industrial [loudspeaker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Loudspeaker "Loudspeaker") shown here uses spherical polar plots taken at six frequencies

Three dimensional modeling of [loudspeaker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Loudspeaker "Loudspeaker") output patterns can be used to predict their performance. A number of polar plots are required, taken at a wide selection of frequencies, as the pattern changes greatly with frequency. Polar plots help to show that many loudspeakers tend toward omnidirectionality at lower frequencies.

An important application of spherical coordinates provides for the [separation of variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separation_of_variables "Separation of variables") in two [partial differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_differential_equations "Partial differential equations")—the [Laplace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laplace_equation "Laplace equation") and the [Helmholtz equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helmholtz_equation "Helmholtz equation")—that arise in many physical problems. The angular portions of the solutions to such equations take the form of [spherical harmonics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_harmonics "Spherical harmonics"). Another application is [ergonomic design](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ergonomic_design "Ergonomic design"), where r is the arm length of a stationary person and the angles describe the direction of the arm as it reaches out. The spherical coordinate system is also commonly used in 3D [game development](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Game_development "Game development") to rotate the camera around the player's position[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_coordinate_system#cite_note-6)

Instead of inclination, the [geographic coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geographic_coordinate_system "Geographic coordinate system") uses elevation angle (or _[latitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latitude "Latitude")_), in the range (aka [domain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interval_(mathematics) "Interval (mathematics)")) −90° ≤ _φ_ ≤ 90° and rotated north from the [equator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equator "Equator") plane. Latitude (i.e., _the angle_ of latitude) may be either _[geocentric latitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geocentric\_latitude "Geocentric latitude")_, measured (rotated) from the Earth's center—and designated variously by _ψ_, _q_, _φ_′, _φ_ c, _φ_ g—or _[geodetic latitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodetic\_latitude "Geodetic latitude")_, measured (rotated) from the observer's [local vertical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_vertical "Local vertical"), and typically designated φ. The polar angle (inclination), which is 90° minus the latitude and ranges from 0 to 180°, is called _[colatitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colatitude "Colatitude")_ in geography.

The azimuth angle (or _[longitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Longitude "Longitude")_) of a given position on Earth, commonly denoted by λ, is measured in degrees east or west from some conventional reference [meridian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meridian_(geography) "Meridian (geography)") (most commonly the [IERS Reference Meridian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IERS_Reference_Meridian "IERS Reference Meridian")); thus its domain (or range) is −180° ≤ _λ_ ≤ 180° and a given reading is typically designated "East" or "West". For positions on the [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth "Earth") or other solid [celestial body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celestial_body "Celestial body"), the reference plane is usually taken to be the plane perpendicular to the [axis of rotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axis_of_rotation "Axis of rotation").

Instead of the radial distance r geographers commonly use _[altitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Altitude "Altitude")_ above or below some local reference surface (_[vertical datum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertical\_datum "Vertical datum")_), which, for example, may be the [mean sea level](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mean_sea_level "Mean sea level"). When needed, the radial distance can be computed from the altitude by adding the [radius of Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radius_of_Earth "Radius of Earth"), which is approximately 6,360±11 km (3,952±7 miles).

However, modern geographical coordinate systems are quite complex, and the positions implied by these simple formulae may be inaccurate by several kilometers. The precise standard meanings of _latitude, longitude_ and _altitude_ are currently defined by the [World Geodetic System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Geodetic_System "World Geodetic System") (WGS), and take into account the flattening of the Earth at the poles (about 21 km or 13 miles) and many other details.

[Planetary coordinate systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_coordinate_system "Planetary coordinate system") use formulations analogous to the geographic coordinate system.

A series of [astronomical coordinate systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_coordinate_systems "Astronomical coordinate systems") are used to measure the elevation angle from several [fundamental planes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_plane_(spherical_coordinates) "Fundamental plane (spherical coordinates)"). These reference planes include: the observer's [horizon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horizontal_coordinate_system "Horizontal coordinate system"), the [galactic equator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galactic_coordinate_system "Galactic coordinate system") (defined by the rotation of the [Milky Way](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milky_Way "Milky Way")), the [celestial equator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celestial_equator "Celestial equator") (defined by [Earth's rotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_rotation "Earth's rotation")), the plane of the [ecliptic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecliptic_coordinate_system "Ecliptic coordinate system") (defined by Earth's orbit around the [Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun "Sun")), and the plane of the earth [terminator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terminator_(solar) "Terminator (solar)") (normal to the instantaneous direction to the [Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun "Sun")).

Coordinate system conversions
-----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Spherical_coordinate_system&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Coordinate system conversions")]

As the spherical coordinate system is only one of many three-dimensional coordinate systems, there exist equations for converting coordinates between the spherical coordinate system and others.

### Cartesian coordinates

The [inverse tangent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverse_tangent "Inverse tangent") denoted in _φ_ = arctan ⁠_y_/_x_⁠ must be suitably defined, taking into account the correct quadrant of (_x_, _y_), as done in the equations above. See the article on [atan2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atan2 "Atan2").

Alternatively, the conversion can be considered as two sequential [rectangular to polar conversions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_coordinate_system#Converting_between_polar_and_Cartesian_coordinates "Polar coordinate system"): the first in the Cartesian xy plane from (_x_, _y_) to (_R_, _φ_), where R is the projection of r onto the xy-plane, and the second in the Cartesian zR-plane from (_z_, _R_) to (_r_, _θ_). The correct quadrants for φ and θ are implied by the correctness of the planar rectangular to polar conversions.

These formulae assume that the two systems have the same origin, that the spherical reference plane is the Cartesian xy plane, that θ is inclination from the z direction, and that the azimuth angles are measured from the Cartesian x axis (so that the y axis has _φ_ = +90°). If _θ_ measures elevation from the reference plane instead of inclination from the zenith the arccos above becomes an arcsin, and the cos _θ_ and sin _θ_ below become switched.

Conversely, the Cartesian coordinates may be retrieved from the spherical coordinates (_radius_ r, _inclination_ θ, _azimuth_ φ), where _r_ ∈ [0, ∞), _θ_ ∈ [0, π], _φ_ ∈ [0, 2 π), by ![Image 17: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}x&=r\sin \theta \,\cos \varphi ,\\y&=r\sin \theta \,\sin \varphi ,\\z&=r\cos \theta .\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/077ab5a9774e05d20497a377bd846f7da9ae95bb)

### Cylindrical coordinates

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Spherical_coordinate_system&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Cylindrical coordinates")]

[Cylindrical coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylindrical_coordinate_system "Cylindrical coordinate system") (_axial_ _radius_ _ρ_, _azimuth_ _φ_, _elevation_ _z_) may be converted into spherical coordinates (_central radius_ _r_, _inclination_ _θ_, _azimuth_ _φ_), by the formulas

![Image 18: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}r&={\sqrt {\rho ^{2}+z^{2}}},\\\theta &=\arctan {\frac {\rho }{z}}=\arccos {\frac {z}{\sqrt {\rho ^{2}+z^{2}}}},\\\varphi &=\varphi .\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/864addcc034827cf2a6448172fc66c10c31bf944)

Conversely, the spherical coordinates may be converted into cylindrical coordinates by the formulae

![Image 19: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\rho &=r\sin \theta ,\\\varphi &=\varphi ,\\z&=r\cos \theta .\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3ee8633ef65e3e0d9ca8070f36cfba5ef231d1a0)

These formulae assume that the two systems have the same origin and same reference plane, measure the azimuth angle φ in the same senses from the same axis, and that the spherical angle θ is inclination from the cylindrical z axis.

Ellipsoidal coordinates
-----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Spherical_coordinate_system&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Ellipsoidal coordinates")]

It is also possible to deal with ellipsoids in Cartesian coordinates by using a modified version of the spherical coordinates.

Let P be an ellipsoid specified by the level set

![Image 20: {\displaystyle ax^{2}+by^{2}+cz^{2}=d.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/827cb48eb0365b2d3fd9836f2240073b7bbd1425)

The modified spherical coordinates of a point in P in the ISO convention (i.e. for physics: _radius_ r, _inclination_ θ, _azimuth_ φ) can be obtained from its [Cartesian coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system "Cartesian coordinate system")(_x_, _y_, _z_) by the formulae

![Image 21: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}x&={\frac {1}{\sqrt {a}}}r\sin \theta \,\cos \varphi ,\\y&={\frac {1}{\sqrt {b}}}r\sin \theta \,\sin \varphi ,\\z&={\frac {1}{\sqrt {c}}}r\cos \theta ,\\r^{2}&=ax^{2}+by^{2}+cz^{2}.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d944a89d434cfc696c3ef19c213eca989079ab10)

An infinitesimal volume element is given by

![Image 22: {\displaystyle \mathrm {d} V=\left|{\frac {\partial (x,y,z)}{\partial (r,\theta ,\varphi )}}\right|\,dr\,d\theta \,d\varphi ={\frac {1}{\sqrt {abc}}}r^{2}\sin \theta \,\mathrm {d} r\,\mathrm {d} \theta \,\mathrm {d} \varphi ={\frac {1}{\sqrt {abc}}}r^{2}\,\mathrm {d} r\,\mathrm {d} \Omega .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ec24746775bfab4e0a5c94c2d7f7423342ea2c20)

The square-root factor comes from the property of the [determinant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Determinant "Determinant") that allows a constant to be pulled out from a column:

![Image 23: {\displaystyle {\begin{vmatrix}ka&b&c\\kd&e&f\\kg&h&i\end{vmatrix}}=k{\begin{vmatrix}a&b&c\\d&e&f\\g&h&i\end{vmatrix}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/924353c1c45455707700cfd0b784365f7e0e9b0d)

Integration and differentiation in spherical coordinates
--------------------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Spherical_coordinate_system&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Integration and differentiation in spherical coordinates")]

[![Image 24](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Kugelkoord-lokb-e.svg/250px-Kugelkoord-lokb-e.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Kugelkoord-lokb-e.svg)

Unit vectors in spherical coordinates

The following equations (Iyanaga 1977) assume that the colatitude θ is the inclination from the positive z axis, as in the _physics convention_ discussed.

The [line element](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_element "Line element") for an infinitesimal displacement from (_r_, _θ_, _φ_) to (_r_ + d _r_, _θ_ + d _θ_, _φ_ + d _φ_) is ![Image 25: {\displaystyle \mathrm {d} \mathbf {r} =\mathrm {d} r\,{\hat {\mathbf {r} }}+r\,\mathrm {d} \theta \,{\hat {\boldsymbol {\theta }}}+r\sin {\theta }\,\mathrm {d} \varphi \,\mathbf {\hat {\boldsymbol {\varphi }}} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b19bc4d661d9cf7cd09027568387e46ee452953f) where ![Image 26: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}{\hat {\mathbf {r} }}&=\sin \theta \cos \varphi \,{\hat {\mathbf {x} }}+\sin \theta \sin \varphi \,{\hat {\mathbf {y} }}+\cos \theta \,{\hat {\mathbf {z} }},\\{\hat {\boldsymbol {\theta }}}&=\cos \theta \cos \varphi \,{\hat {\mathbf {x} }}+\cos \theta \sin \varphi \,{\hat {\mathbf {y} }}-\sin \theta \,{\hat {\mathbf {z} }},\\{\hat {\boldsymbol {\varphi }}}&=-\sin \varphi \,{\hat {\mathbf {x} }}+\cos \varphi \,{\hat {\mathbf {y} }}\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/15c06e5b94e6981470001062b634252d936bbaa3) are the local orthogonal [unit vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_vectors "Unit vectors") in the directions of increasing r, θ, and φ, respectively, and **x̂**, **ŷ**, and **ẑ** are the unit vectors in Cartesian coordinates. The linear transformation to this right-handed coordinate triplet is a [rotation matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation_matrix "Rotation matrix"), ![Image 27: {\displaystyle R={\begin{pmatrix}\sin \theta \cos \varphi &\sin \theta \sin \varphi &{\hphantom {-}}\cos \theta \\\cos \theta \cos \varphi &\cos \theta \sin \varphi &-\sin \theta \\-\sin \varphi &\cos \varphi &{\hphantom {-}}0\end{pmatrix}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/572737b6c535b62bebbff974417fceeb4f7bf035)

This gives the transformation from the Cartesian to the spherical, the other way around is given by its inverse. Note: the matrix is an [orthogonal matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_matrix "Orthogonal matrix"), that is, its inverse is simply its [transpose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transpose "Transpose").

The Cartesian unit vectors are thus related to the spherical unit vectors by: ![Image 28: {\displaystyle {\begin{bmatrix}\mathbf {\hat {x}} \\\mathbf {\hat {y}} \\\mathbf {\hat {z}} \end{bmatrix}}={\begin{bmatrix}\sin \theta \cos \varphi &\cos \theta \cos \varphi &-\sin \varphi \\\sin \theta \sin \varphi &\cos \theta \sin \varphi &{\hphantom {-}}\cos \varphi \\\cos \theta &-\sin \theta &{\hphantom {-}}0\end{bmatrix}}{\begin{bmatrix}{\boldsymbol {\hat {r}}}\\{\boldsymbol {\hat {\theta }}}\\{\boldsymbol {\hat {\varphi }}}\end{bmatrix}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/abdfd135b8aef9bd75094a0ea040705611ee14ea)

The general form of the formula to prove the differential line element, is[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_coordinate_system#cite_note-q74503-7)![Image 29: {\displaystyle \mathrm {d} \mathbf {r} =\sum _{i}{\frac {\partial \mathbf {r} }{\partial x_{i}}}\,\mathrm {d} x_{i}=\sum _{i}\left|{\frac {\partial \mathbf {r} }{\partial x_{i}}}\right|{\frac {\frac {\partial \mathbf {r} }{\partial x_{i}}}{\left|{\frac {\partial \mathbf {r} }{\partial x_{i}}}\right|}}\,\mathrm {d} x_{i}=\sum _{i}\left|{\frac {\partial \mathbf {r} }{\partial x_{i}}}\right|\,\mathrm {d} x_{i}\,{\hat {\boldsymbol {x}}}_{i},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1e6cecacf81c2b593936c0ff6afe6943d5d44bc8) that is, the change in ![Image 30: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eca0f46511c4c986c48b254073732c0bd98ae0c1) is decomposed into individual changes corresponding to changes in the individual coordinates.

To apply this to the present case, one needs to calculate how ![Image 31: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eca0f46511c4c986c48b254073732c0bd98ae0c1) changes with each of the coordinates. In the conventions used, ![Image 32: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} ={\begin{bmatrix}r\sin \theta \,\cos \varphi \\r\sin \theta \,\sin \varphi \\r\cos \theta \end{bmatrix}},x_{1}=r,x_{2}=\theta ,x_{3}=\varphi .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/556087c5124e9c701cf908bd97c4c0f79bdebfc7)

Thus, ![Image 33: {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial \mathbf {r} }{\partial r}}={\begin{bmatrix}\sin \theta \,\cos \varphi \\\sin \theta \,\sin \varphi \\\cos \theta \end{bmatrix}}=\mathbf {\hat {r}} ,\quad {\frac {\partial \mathbf {r} }{\partial \theta }}={\begin{bmatrix}r\cos \theta \,\cos \varphi \\r\cos \theta \,\sin \varphi \\-r\sin \theta \end{bmatrix}}=r\,{\hat {\boldsymbol {\theta }}},\quad {\frac {\partial \mathbf {r} }{\partial \varphi }}={\begin{bmatrix}-r\sin \theta \,\sin \varphi \\{\hphantom {-}}r\sin \theta \,\cos \varphi \\0\end{bmatrix}}=r\sin \theta \,\mathbf {\hat {\boldsymbol {\varphi }}} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2f27d52dcdefa273fb893ae95e21f41075c45a56)

The desired coefficients are the magnitudes of these vectors:[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_coordinate_system#cite_note-q74503-7)![Image 34: {\displaystyle \left|{\frac {\partial \mathbf {r} }{\partial r}}\right|=1,\quad \left|{\frac {\partial \mathbf {r} }{\partial \theta }}\right|=r,\quad \left|{\frac {\partial \mathbf {r} }{\partial \varphi }}\right|=r\sin \theta .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5c0a7b35118ec0b06b2a3072dab425862af68f85)

The [surface element](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_integral "Surface integral") spanning from θ to _θ_ + d _θ_ and φ to _φ_ + d _φ_ on a spherical surface at (constant) radius r is then ![Image 35: {\displaystyle \mathrm {d} S_{r}=\left\|{\frac {\partial {\mathbf {r} }}{\partial \theta }}\times {\frac {\partial {\mathbf {r} }}{\partial \varphi }}\right\|\mathrm {d} \theta \,\mathrm {d} \varphi =\left|r{\hat {\boldsymbol {\theta }}}\times r\sin \theta {\boldsymbol {\hat {\varphi }}}\right|\mathrm {d} \theta \,\mathrm {d} \varphi =r^{2}\sin \theta \,\mathrm {d} \theta \,\mathrm {d} \varphi ~.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d9bace6ed91e600d026364bf6adb830d39575980)

Thus the differential [solid angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_angle "Solid angle") is ![Image 36: {\displaystyle \mathrm {d} \Omega ={\frac {\mathrm {d} S_{r}}{r^{2}}}=\sin \theta \,\mathrm {d} \theta \,\mathrm {d} \varphi .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ef27ca6dea913eae67956940f173d690bcaa71f8)

The surface element in a surface of polar angle θ constant (a cone with vertex at the origin) is ![Image 37: {\displaystyle \mathrm {d} S_{\theta }=r\sin \theta \,\mathrm {d} \varphi \,\mathrm {d} r.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/83704aaf946f20a832c3f4b83e8a6dad0abcc313)

The surface element in a surface of azimuth φ constant (a vertical half-plane) is ![Image 38: {\displaystyle \mathrm {d} S_{\varphi }=r\,\mathrm {d} r\,\mathrm {d} \theta .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b917e1558b487b2650c9546b77dd8bd3911dc878)

The [volume element](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume_element "Volume element") spanning from r to _r_ + d _r_, θ to _θ_ + d _θ_, and φ to _φ_ + d _φ_ is specified by the [determinant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Determinant "Determinant") of the [Jacobian matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacobian_matrix "Jacobian matrix") of [partial derivatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_derivative "Partial derivative"), ![Image 39: {\displaystyle J={\frac {\partial (x,y,z)}{\partial (r,\theta ,\varphi )}}={\begin{pmatrix}\sin \theta \cos \varphi &r\cos \theta \cos \varphi &-r\sin \theta \sin \varphi \\\sin \theta \sin \varphi &r\cos \theta \sin \varphi &{\hphantom {-}}r\sin \theta \cos \varphi \\\cos \theta &-r\sin \theta &{\hphantom {-}}0\end{pmatrix}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/244a10c8233858491f03e21326900c5ef2a24fd4) namely ![Image 40: {\displaystyle \mathrm {d} V=\left|{\frac {\partial (x,y,z)}{\partial (r,\theta ,\varphi )}}\right|\,\mathrm {d} r\,\mathrm {d} \theta \,\mathrm {d} \varphi =r^{2}\sin \theta \,\mathrm {d} r\,\mathrm {d} \theta \,\mathrm {d} \varphi =r^{2}\,\mathrm {d} r\,\mathrm {d} \Omega ~.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/858b36ce1577f1133ecd96c49b15b7a22fd44c29)

Thus, for example, a function _f_(_r_, _θ_, _φ_) can be integrated over every point in **R**3 by the [triple integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiple_integral#Spherical_coordinates "Multiple integral")![Image 41: {\displaystyle \int \limits _{0}^{2\pi }\int \limits _{0}^{\pi }\int \limits _{0}^{\infty }f(r,\theta ,\varphi )r^{2}\sin \theta \,\mathrm {d} r\,\mathrm {d} \theta \,\mathrm {d} \varphi ~.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fc3c4d6cc5aea6c3307480cbd378e8a52b4c771c)

The [del](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Del "Del") operator in this system leads to the following expressions for the [gradient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gradient "Gradient") and [Laplacian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laplacian "Laplacian") for scalar fields, ![Image 42: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\nabla f&={\partial f \over \partial r}{\hat {\mathbf {r} }}+{1 \over r}{\partial f \over \partial \theta }{\hat {\boldsymbol {\theta }}}+{1 \over r\sin \theta }{\partial f \over \partial \varphi }{\hat {\boldsymbol {\varphi }}},\\[8pt]\nabla ^{2}f&={1 \over r^{2}}{\partial  \over \partial r}\left(r^{2}{\partial f \over \partial r}\right)+{1 \over r^{2}\sin \theta }{\partial  \over \partial \theta }\left(\sin \theta {\partial f \over \partial \theta }\right)+{1 \over r^{2}\sin ^{2}\theta }{\partial ^{2}f \over \partial \varphi ^{2}}\\[8pt]&=\left({\frac {\partial ^{2}}{\partial r^{2}}}+{\frac {2}{r}}{\frac {\partial }{\partial r}}\right)f+{1 \over r^{2}\sin \theta }{\partial  \over \partial \theta }\left(\sin \theta {\frac {\partial }{\partial \theta }}\right)f+{\frac {1}{r^{2}\sin ^{2}\theta }}{\frac {\partial ^{2}}{\partial \varphi ^{2}}}f~,\\[8pt]\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c085b5d702755a656ad961a224d007f50c7dfe76)And it leads to the following expressions for the [divergence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divergence "Divergence") and [curl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curl_(mathematics) "Curl (mathematics)") of [vector fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_field "Vector field"),

![Image 43: {\displaystyle \nabla \cdot \mathbf {A} ={\frac {1}{r^{2}}}{\partial  \over \partial r}\left(r^{2}A_{r}\right)+{\frac {1}{r\sin \theta }}{\partial  \over \partial \theta }\left(\sin \theta A_{\theta }\right)+{\frac {1}{r\sin \theta }}{\partial A_{\varphi } \over \partial \varphi },}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a37ea03511d9354ab7d87c6343ca1988cc9f6e21)![Image 44: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\nabla \times \mathbf {A} ={}&{\frac {1}{r\sin \theta }}\left[{\partial  \over \partial \theta }\left(A_{\varphi }\sin \theta \right)-{\partial A_{\theta } \over \partial \varphi }\right]{\hat {\mathbf {r} }}\\[4pt]&{}+{\frac {1}{r}}\left[{1 \over \sin \theta }{\partial A_{r} \over \partial \varphi }-{\partial  \over \partial r}\left(rA_{\varphi }\right)\right]{\hat {\boldsymbol {\theta }}}\\[4pt]&{}+{\frac {1}{r}}\left[{\partial  \over \partial r}\left(rA_{\theta }\right)-{\partial A_{r} \over \partial \theta }\right]{\hat {\boldsymbol {\varphi }}},\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2c2477820afa14dd84fdaba95b49d463fba1ee65)

Further, the inverse Jacobian in Cartesian coordinates is ![Image 45: {\displaystyle J^{-1}={\begin{pmatrix}{\dfrac {x}{r}}&{\dfrac {y}{r}}&{\dfrac {z}{r}}\\\\{\dfrac {xz}{r^{2}{\sqrt {x^{2}+y^{2}}}}}&{\dfrac {yz}{r^{2}{\sqrt {x^{2}+y^{2}}}}}&{\dfrac {-\left(x^{2}+y^{2}\right)}{r^{2}{\sqrt {x^{2}+y^{2}}}}}\\\\{\dfrac {-y}{x^{2}+y^{2}}}&{\dfrac {x}{x^{2}+y^{2}}}&0\end{pmatrix}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b1ee27e9b9d1bde64fc1eb122123c9393cc4aaa6) The [metric tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_tensor "Metric tensor") in the spherical coordinate system is ![Image 46: {\displaystyle g=J^{T}J}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/28fe097305f712f3d16d198eb3c6b954e85e0f21).
