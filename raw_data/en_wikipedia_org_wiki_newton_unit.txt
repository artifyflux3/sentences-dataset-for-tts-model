The **newton** (symbol: **N**) is the unit of [force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Force "Force") in the [International System of Units (SI)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Units "International System of Units"). Expressed in terms of [SI base units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SI_base_units "SI base units"), it is 1 kg⋅m/s 2, the force that accelerates a mass of one kilogram at one metre per second squared.

The unit is named after [Isaac Newton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaac_Newton "Isaac Newton") in recognition of his work on [classical mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_mechanics "Classical mechanics"), specifically [his second law of motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_laws_of_motion#Newton's_second_law "Newton's laws of motion").

A newton is defined as 1 kg⋅m/s 2 (it is a named derived unit defined in terms of the [SI base units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SI_base_unit "SI base unit")).[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton_(unit)#cite_note-SIBrochure9thEd-1): 137  One newton is, therefore, the force needed to [accelerate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acceleration "Acceleration") one [kilogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kilogram "Kilogram") of mass at the rate of one [metre per second squared](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metre_per_second_squared "Metre per second squared") in the direction of the applied force.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton_(unit)#cite_note-:0-2)

The units "metre per second squared" can be understood as measuring a rate of change in [velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity "Velocity") per unit of time, i.e. an increase in velocity by one metre per second every second.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton_(unit)#cite_note-:0-2)

In 1946, the [General Conference on Weights and Measures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Conference_on_Weights_and_Measures "General Conference on Weights and Measures") (CGPM) Resolution 2 standardized the unit of force in the [MKS system of units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MKS_system_of_units "MKS system of units") to be the amount needed to accelerate one kilogram of mass at the rate of one metre per second squared. In 1948, the 9th CGPM Resolution 7 adopted the name _newton_ for this force.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton_(unit)#cite_note-ISU1977-3) The MKS system then became the blueprint for today's SI system of units.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton_(unit)#cite_note-NIST330-4) The newton thus became the standard unit of force in the _Système international d'unités_ (SI), or [International System of Units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Units "International System of Units").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton_(unit)#cite_note-ISU1977-3)

The newton is named after [Isaac Newton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaac_Newton "Isaac Newton"). As with every [SI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Units "International System of Units") unit named after a person, its symbol starts with an [upper case](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Upper_case "Upper case") letter (N), but when written in full, it follows the rules for capitalisation of a [common noun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Common_noun "Common noun"); i.e., _newton_ becomes capitalised at the beginning of a sentence and in titles but is otherwise in lower case.

The connection to Newton comes from [Newton's second law of motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_laws_of_motion "Newton's laws of motion"), which states that the force exerted on an object is directly proportional to the acceleration hence acquired by that object, thus:[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton_(unit)#cite_note-5)![Image 2: {\displaystyle F=ma,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c433d025cc7160cbd654288ea41c37179078fb78) where ![Image 3: {\displaystyle m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a07d98bb302f3856cbabc47b2b9016692e3f7bc) represents the [mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass "Mass") of the object undergoing an acceleration ![Image 4: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc). When using the SI unit of mass, the kilogram (kg), and SI units for distance [metre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metre "Metre") (m), and time, [second](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second "Second") (s) we arrive at the SI definition of the newton: 1 kg⋅m/s 2.

At average [gravity on Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity_of_Earth "Gravity of Earth") (conventionally, ![Image 5: {\displaystyle g_{\text{n}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1b20e15e30e1cdd40cf0099d3d1fe68e91bea089) = 9.806 65 m/s 2), a kilogram mass exerts a force of about 9.81 N.

*   An average-sized [apple](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaac_Newton#Apple_incident "Isaac Newton") with mass 200 g exerts about two newtons of force at Earth's surface, which we measure as the apple's [weight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weight "Weight") on Earth.

![Image 6: {\displaystyle 0.200{\text{ kg}}\times 9.80665{\text{ m/s}}^{2}=1.961{\text{ N}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bed801958154534675890c03e9fc02b06fd2de8c)
*   An [average adult](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_body_weight#Average_weight_around_the_world "Human body weight") exerts a force of about 608 N on Earth.

![Image 7: {\displaystyle 62{\text{ kg}}\times 9.80665{\text{ m/s}}^{2}=608{\text{ N}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/542d481984098a8bc1d8e98306851afbdeef24ee) (where 62 kg is the world average adult mass).[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton_(unit)#cite_note-6)[![Image 8](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Dwire_carabiner.jpg/250px-Dwire_carabiner.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Dwire_carabiner.jpg)

A [carabiner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carabiner "Carabiner") used in [rock climbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rock_climbing "Rock climbing"), with a safety rating of 26 kN when loaded along the spine with the gate closed, 8 kN when loaded perpendicular to the spine, and 10 kN when loaded along the spine with the gate open.

Large forces may be expressed in kilonewtons (kN), where 1 kN = 1000 N. For example, the [tractive effort](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tractive_effort "Tractive effort") of [a Class Y steam train locomotive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NER_Class_Y "NER Class Y") and the [thrust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thrust "Thrust") of an [F100 jet engine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pratt_%26_Whitney_F100 "Pratt & Whitney F100") are both around 130 kN.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

Climbing ropes are tested by assuming a human can withstand a fall that creates 12 kN of force. The ropes must not break when tested against 5 such falls.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton_(unit)#cite_note-7): 11
