Example of a quadrilateral-faced non-convex hexahedron

In [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry"), a **cuboid** is a [hexahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hexahedron "Hexahedron") with [quadrilateral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral "Quadrilateral") faces, meaning it is a [polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyhedron "Polyhedron") with six [faces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Face_(geometry) "Face (geometry)"); it has eight [vertices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_(geometry) "Vertex (geometry)") and twelve [edges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edge_(geometry) "Edge (geometry)"). A _[rectangular cuboid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangular\_cuboid "Rectangular cuboid")_ (sometimes also called a "cuboid") has all [right angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_angle "Right angle") and equal opposite [rectangular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangular "Rectangular") faces. Etymologically, "cuboid" means "like a [cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube "Cube")", in the sense of a [convex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_polyhedron "Convex polyhedron") solid which can be transformed into a cube (by adjusting the lengths of its edges and the [angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihedral_angle "Dihedral angle") between its adjacent faces). A cuboid is a convex polyhedron whose [polyhedral graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyhedral_graph "Polyhedral graph") is the same as that of a cube.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cuboid#cite_note-alexander84-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cuboid#cite_note-grunbaum-2)

General cuboids have many different types. When all of the rectangular cuboid's edges are equal in length, it results in a [cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube "Cube"), with six [square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square "Square") faces and adjacent faces meeting at right angles.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cuboid#cite_note-alexander84-1)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cuboid#cite_note-dupius-3) Along with the rectangular cuboids, _[parallelepiped](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelepiped "Parallelepiped")_ is a cuboid with six [parallelogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram "Parallelogram") faces. _[Rhombohedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhombohedron "Rhombohedron")_ is a cuboid with six [rhombus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhombus "Rhombus") faces. A _[square frustum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square\_frustum "Square frustum")_ is a frustum with a square base, but the rest of its faces are quadrilaterals; the square frustum is formed by [truncating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Truncation_(geometry) "Truncation (geometry)") the [apex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apex_(geometry) "Apex (geometry)") of a [square pyramid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_pyramid "Square pyramid"). In attempting to classify cuboids by their symmetries, [Robertson (1983)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cuboid#CITEREFRobertson1983) found that there were at least 22 different cases, "of which only about half are familiar in the shapes of everyday objects".[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cuboid#cite_note-robertson-4)

There exist quadrilateral-faced hexahedra which are non-[convex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_polyhedron "Convex polyhedron").
