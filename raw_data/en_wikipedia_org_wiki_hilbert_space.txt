Title: Hilbert space

URL Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space

Published Time: 2001-11-02T21:29:08Z

Markdown Content:
[![Image 1](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Standing_waves_on_a_string.gif/250px-Standing_waves_on_a_string.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Standing_waves_on_a_string.gif)

The state of a [vibrating string](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vibrating_string "Vibrating string") can be modeled as a point in a Hilbert space. The decomposition of a vibrating string into its vibrations in distinct [overtones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Overtone "Overtone") is given by the projection of the point onto the [coordinate axes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_axes "Coordinate axes") in the space.

In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), a **Hilbert space** is a [real](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number") or [complex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number")[inner product space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inner_product_space "Inner product space") that is also a [complete metric space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complete_metric_space "Complete metric space") with respect to the metric induced by the inner product. It generalizes the notion of [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space"). The [inner product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inner_product "Inner product") allows lengths and angles to be defined. Furthermore, [completeness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complete_metric_space "Complete metric space") means that there are enough [limits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limit_(mathematics) "Limit (mathematics)") in the space to allow the techniques of calculus to be used. A Hilbert space is a special case of a [Banach space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Banach_space "Banach space").

Hilbert spaces were studied beginning in the first decade of the 20th century by [David Hilbert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Hilbert "David Hilbert"), [Erhard Schmidt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erhard_Schmidt "Erhard Schmidt"), and [Frigyes Riesz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frigyes_Riesz "Frigyes Riesz"). They are indispensable tools in the theories of [partial differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_differential_equation "Partial differential equation"), [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_formulation_of_quantum_mechanics "Mathematical formulation of quantum mechanics"), [Fourier analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourier_analysis "Fourier analysis") (which includes applications to [signal processing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Signal_processing "Signal processing") and [heat transfer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat_transfer "Heat transfer")), and [ergodic theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ergodic_theory "Ergodic theory") (which forms the mathematical underpinning of [thermodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamics "Thermodynamics")). [John von Neumann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_von_Neumann "John von Neumann") coined the term _Hilbert space_ for the abstract concept that underlies many of these diverse applications. The success of Hilbert space methods ushered in a very fruitful era for [functional analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functional_analysis "Functional analysis"). Apart from the classical Euclidean vector spaces, examples of Hilbert spaces include [spaces of square-integrable functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square-integrable_function "Square-integrable function"), [spaces of sequences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sequence_space "Sequence space"), [Sobolev spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sobolev_space "Sobolev space") consisting of [generalized functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generalized_function "Generalized function"), and [Hardy spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hardy_space "Hardy space") of [holomorphic functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holomorphic_function "Holomorphic function").

Geometric intuition plays an important role in many aspects of Hilbert space theory. Exact analogs of the [Pythagorean theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagorean_theorem "Pythagorean theorem") and [parallelogram law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram_law "Parallelogram law") hold in a Hilbert space. At a deeper level, [perpendicular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular "Perpendicular")[projection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projection_(linear_algebra) "Projection (linear algebra)") onto a [linear subspace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_subspace "Linear subspace") plays a significant role in [optimization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_optimization "Mathematical optimization") problems and other aspects of the theory. An element of a Hilbert space can be uniquely specified by its coordinates with respect to an [orthonormal basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthonormal_basis "Orthonormal basis"), in analogy with [Cartesian coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinates "Cartesian coordinates") in classical geometry. When this [basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basis_(linear_algebra) "Basis (linear algebra)") is [countably infinite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Countably_infinite "Countably infinite"), it allows identifying the Hilbert space with the space of the [infinite sequences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinite_sequence "Infinite sequence") that are [square-summable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square-integrable_function "Square-integrable function"). The latter space is often in the older literature referred to as _the_ Hilbert space.

### Motivating example: Euclidean vector space

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hilbert_space&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Motivating example: Euclidean vector space")]

One of the most familiar examples of a Hilbert space is the [Euclidean vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_vector_space "Euclidean vector space") consisting of three-dimensional [vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_vector "Euclidean vector"), denoted by **R**3, and equipped with the [dot product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dot_product "Dot product"). The dot product takes two vectors **x** and **y**, and produces a real number **x** ⋅ **y**. If **x** and **y** are represented in [Cartesian coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinates "Cartesian coordinates"), then the dot product is defined by ![Image 2: {\displaystyle {\begin{pmatrix}x_{1}\\x_{2}\\x_{3}\end{pmatrix}}\cdot {\begin{pmatrix}y_{1}\\y_{2}\\y_{3}\end{pmatrix}}=x_{1}y_{1}+x_{2}y_{2}+x_{3}y_{3}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1a6c73091bcfb40e8ae18ba63c544d4bd4d690b1)

The dot product satisfies the properties[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-1)

1.   It is [symmetric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetric_relation "Symmetric relation") in **x** and **y**: **x** ⋅ **y** = **y** ⋅ **x**.
2.   It is [linear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_function "Linear function") in its first argument: (_a_**x**1 + _b_**x**2) ⋅ **y** = _a_(**x**1 ⋅ **y**) + _b_(**x**2 ⋅ **y**) for any [scalars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_(mathematics) "Scalar (mathematics)")a, b, and vectors **x**1, **x**2, and **y**.
3.   It is [positive definite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Definite_bilinear_form "Definite bilinear form"): for all vectors **x**, **x** ⋅ **x** ≥ 0 , with equality [if and only if](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/If_and_only_if "If and only if")**x** = **0**.

An operation on pairs of vectors that, like the dot product, satisfies these three properties is known as a (real) [inner product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inner_product "Inner product"). A [vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space") equipped with such an inner product is known as a (real) [inner product space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inner_product_space "Inner product space"). Every finite-dimensional inner product space is also a Hilbert space.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-2) The basic feature of the dot product that connects it with Euclidean geometry is that it is related to both the length (or [norm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norm_(mathematics) "Norm (mathematics)")) of a vector, denoted ‖**x**‖, and to the angle θ between two vectors **x** and **y** by means of the formula ![Image 3: {\displaystyle \mathbf {x} \cdot \mathbf {y} =\left\|\mathbf {x} \right\|\left\|\mathbf {y} \right\|\,\cos \theta \,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7ff5157fde2381e9dcd0a006d3e1d0575512afbd)

[![Image 4](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Completeness_in_Hilbert_space.png/250px-Completeness_in_Hilbert_space.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Completeness_in_Hilbert_space.png)

Completeness means that a series of vectors (in blue) results in a [well-defined](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Well_defined "Well defined") net displacement vector (in orange).

[Multivariable calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multivariable_calculus "Multivariable calculus") in Euclidean space relies on the ability to compute [limits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limit_(mathematics) "Limit (mathematics)"), and to have useful criteria for concluding that limits exist. A [mathematical series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Series_(mathematics) "Series (mathematics)")![Image 5: {\displaystyle \sum _{n=0}^{\infty }\mathbf {x} _{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8b3d791a8b023df5a980200e276eec54d69eb2b6) consisting of vectors in **R**3 is [absolutely convergent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_convergence "Absolute convergence") provided that the sum of the lengths converges as an ordinary series of real numbers:[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-3)![Image 6: {\displaystyle \sum _{k=0}^{\infty }\|\mathbf {x} _{k}\|<\infty \,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c49858bb1785370ae3843e6ea5398f612524f806)

Just as with a series of scalars, a series of vectors that converges absolutely also converges to some limit vector **L** in the Euclidean space, in the sense that ![Image 7: {\displaystyle {\Biggl \|}\mathbf {L} -\sum _{k=0}^{N}\mathbf {x} _{k}{\Biggr \|}\to 0\quad {\text{as }}N\to \infty \,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b26b075a3dcc6d237c8c141a155f076338daaaed)

This property expresses the _completeness_ of Euclidean space: that a series that converges absolutely also converges in the ordinary sense.

Hilbert spaces are often taken over the [complex numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number"). The [complex plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_plane "Complex plane") denoted by **C** is equipped with a notion of magnitude, the [complex modulus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_value "Absolute value")|_z_|, which is defined as the square root of the product of z with its [complex conjugate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_conjugate "Complex conjugate"): ![Image 8: {\displaystyle |z|^{2}=z{\overline {z}}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/85f9ec3c8dc777c161389ccaf3e175e8993b4a19)

If _z_ = _x_ + _iy_ is a decomposition of z into its real and imaginary parts, then the modulus is the usual Euclidean two-dimensional length: ![Image 9: {\displaystyle |z|={\sqrt {x^{2}+y^{2}}}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/21888cd022a8dfd5d07d8257d0fd25e2823b5bce)

The inner product of a pair of complex numbers z and w is the product of z with the complex conjugate of w: ![Image 10: {\displaystyle \langle z,w\rangle =z{\overline {w}}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/04c95ae0bc59457f9b44a262586ab7caa2542d11)

This is complex-valued. The real part of ⟨_z_, _w_⟩ gives the usual two-dimensional Euclidean [dot product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dot_product "Dot product").

A second example is the space **C**2 whose elements are pairs of complex numbers _z_ = (_z_ 1, _z_ 2). Then an inner product of z with another such vector _w_ = (_w_ 1, _w_ 2) is given by ![Image 11: {\displaystyle \langle z,w\rangle =z_{1}{\overline {w}}_{1}+z_{2}{\overline {w}}_{2}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47d52131941c1264ba7608aed02459f170c69738)

The real part of ⟨_z_, _w_⟩ is then the four-dimensional Euclidean dot product. This inner product is _Hermitian_ symmetric, which means that the result of interchanging z and w is the complex conjugate: ![Image 12: {\displaystyle \langle w,z\rangle ={\overline {\langle z,w\rangle }}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/72f310d466f226a09af8f8593ff396e1f0401251)

A _Hilbert space_ is a [real](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number") or [complex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number")[inner product space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inner_product_space "Inner product space") that is also a [complete metric space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complete_metric_space "Complete metric space") with respect to the distance function [induced](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Induced_topology "Induced topology") by the inner product.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-General-4)

To say that a complex vector space _H_ is a _complex inner product space_ means that there is an inner product ![Image 13: {\displaystyle \langle x,y\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9df1806ebe1fed1a728b18aed82c30be8b2a0acb) associating a complex number to each pair of elements ![Image 14: {\displaystyle x,y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5ea0abffd33a692ded22accc104515a032851dff) of _H_ that satisfies the following properties:

1.   The inner product is conjugate symmetric; that is, the inner product of a pair of elements is equal to the [complex conjugate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_conjugate "Complex conjugate") of the inner product of the swapped elements: ![Image 15: {\displaystyle \langle y,x\rangle ={\overline {\langle x,y\rangle }}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bb947b57df47d548a0ff388d1af8fb489e90f3ac) Importantly, this implies that ![Image 16: {\displaystyle \langle x,x\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9f081a4822ec7bdff2661ee2070b70a3406cf70f) is a real number.
2.   The inner product is [linear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_functional "Linear functional") in its first[[nb 1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-5) argument. For all complex numbers ![Image 17: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc) and ![Image 18: {\displaystyle b,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bdb96677ba71b937617ca8751955f884f6306b64)![Image 19: {\displaystyle \langle ax_{1}+bx_{2},y\rangle =a\langle x_{1},y\rangle +b\langle x_{2},y\rangle \,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5c1164bfad1024b720cd7262f99f77063676ab12)
3.   The inner product of an element with itself is [positive definite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Definite_bilinear_form "Definite bilinear form"): ![Image 20: {\displaystyle {\begin{alignedat}{4}\langle x,x\rangle >0&\quad {\text{ if }}x\neq 0,\\\langle x,x\rangle =0&\quad {\text{ if }}x=0\,.\end{alignedat}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e04e01914ed2ed909cbfaaa2dafd141857e258e7)

It follows from properties 1 and 2 that a complex inner product is _[antilinear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antilinear\_map "Antilinear map")_, also called _conjugate linear_, in its second argument, meaning that ![Image 21: {\displaystyle \langle x,ay_{1}+by_{2}\rangle ={\bar {a}}\langle x,y_{1}\rangle +{\bar {b}}\langle x,y_{2}\rangle \,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/648a87771f6ed2eae5f1a7867493312b6292ba71)

A _real inner product space_ is defined in the same way, except that _H_ is a real vector space and the inner product takes real values. Such an inner product will be a [bilinear map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bilinear_map "Bilinear map") and ![Image 22: {\displaystyle (H,H,\langle \cdot ,\cdot \rangle )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1bee7511626db6a54e1af12d29ff989d2aff8b7a) will form a [dual system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_system "Dual system").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchaeferWolff1999122%E2%80%93202-6)

The [norm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norm_(mathematics) "Norm (mathematics)") is the real-valued function ![Image 23: {\displaystyle \|x\|={\sqrt {\langle x,x\rangle }}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/23d803bc001c215a1be59acf43e6606b1d774ea8) and the distance ![Image 24: {\displaystyle d}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e85ff03cbe0c7341af6b982e47e9f90d235c66ab) between two points ![Image 25: {\displaystyle x,y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5ea0abffd33a692ded22accc104515a032851dff) in _H_ is defined in terms of the norm by ![Image 26: {\displaystyle d(x,y)=\|x-y\|={\sqrt {\langle x-y,x-y\rangle }}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a4401717fb6ad3702ce9b40126a159d8756a8fed)

That this function is a distance function means firstly that it is symmetric in ![Image 27: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) and ![Image 28: {\displaystyle y,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/99bd9829c9ef4adcb0f9f5d53b27463a873a8e88) secondly that the distance between ![Image 29: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) and itself is zero, and otherwise the distance between ![Image 30: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) and ![Image 31: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d) must be positive, and lastly that the [triangle inequality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle_inequality "Triangle inequality") holds, meaning that the length of one leg of a triangle _xyz_ cannot exceed the sum of the lengths of the other two legs: ![Image 32: {\displaystyle d(x,z)\leq d(x,y)+d(y,z)\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3ef67169190c37bb6b853a1304d1c5ead6bad92b)

[![Image 33](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Triangle_inequality_in_a_metric_space.svg/330px-Triangle_inequality_in_a_metric_space.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Triangle_inequality_in_a_metric_space.svg)

This last property is ultimately a consequence of the more fundamental [Cauchy–Schwarz inequality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cauchy%E2%80%93Schwarz_inequality "Cauchy–Schwarz inequality"), which asserts ![Image 34: {\displaystyle \left|\langle x,y\rangle \right|\leq \|x\|\|y\|}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eea56af9f54f8732fcbe7204ffdcebaeb16a4ba8) with equality if and only if ![Image 35: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) and ![Image 36: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d) are [linearly dependent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_independence "Linear independence").

With a distance function defined in this way, any inner product space is a [metric space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space "Metric space"), and sometimes is known as a _pre-Hilbert space_.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-7) Any pre-Hilbert space that is additionally also a [complete space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complete_space "Complete space") is a Hilbert space.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-8)

The _[completeness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complete\_metric\_space "Complete metric space")_ of _H_ is expressed using a form of the [Cauchy criterion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cauchy_criterion "Cauchy criterion") for sequences in _H_: a pre-Hilbert space _H_ is complete if every [Cauchy sequence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cauchy_sequence "Cauchy sequence")[converges with respect to this norm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limit_(mathematics) "Limit (mathematics)") to an element in the space. Completeness can be characterized by the following equivalent condition: if a series of vectors ![Image 37: {\displaystyle \sum _{k=0}^{\infty }u_{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1eab948361735efa594296bd04d873a8485149c2)[converges absolutely](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_convergence "Absolute convergence") in the sense that ![Image 38: {\displaystyle \sum _{k=0}^{\infty }\|u_{k}\|<\infty \,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9a193b4cda60532c04dc0900d7d30353145ffcab) then the series converges in _H_, in the sense that the [partial sums](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_sums "Partial sums") converge to an element of _H_.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-9)

As a complete normed space, Hilbert spaces are by definition also [Banach spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Banach_space "Banach space"). As such they are [topological vector spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_vector_space "Topological vector space"), in which [topological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology "Topology") notions like the [openness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_set "Open set") and [closedness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_set "Closed set") of subsets are [well defined](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Well-defined_expression "Well-defined expression"). Of special importance is the notion of a closed [linear subspace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_subspace "Linear subspace") of a Hilbert space that, with the inner product induced by [restriction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Restriction_(mathematics) "Restriction (mathematics)"), is also complete (being a closed set in a complete metric space) and therefore a Hilbert space in its own right.

### Second example: sequence spaces

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hilbert_space&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Second example: sequence spaces")]

The [sequence space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sequence_space "Sequence space")![Image 39: {\textstyle \ell ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b58f3717d70c2cbb144ad44658151c595feab54e) consists of all [infinite sequences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sequence_(mathematics) "Sequence (mathematics)")**z** = (_z_ 1, _z_ 2, ...) of complex numbers such that the following series [converges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convergent_series "Convergent series"):[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-Stein_2005-10)![Image 40: {\displaystyle \sum _{n=1}^{\infty }|z_{n}|^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e56a3c8d984a07aea65427989643bcd774fc8ab0)

The inner product on _l_ 2 is defined by: ![Image 41: {\displaystyle \langle \mathbf {z} ,\mathbf {w} \rangle =\sum _{n=1}^{\infty }z_{n}{\overline {w}}_{n}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35b279d630a2c4b451db688f1d1dc5deaf8d7763)

This second series converges as a consequence of the [Cauchy–Schwarz inequality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cauchy%E2%80%93Schwarz_inequality "Cauchy–Schwarz inequality") and the convergence of the previous series.

Completeness of the space holds provided that whenever a series of elements from ![Image 42: {\textstyle \ell ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b58f3717d70c2cbb144ad44658151c595feab54e) converges absolutely (in norm), then it converges to an element of ![Image 43: {\textstyle \ell ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b58f3717d70c2cbb144ad44658151c595feab54e). The proof is basic in [mathematical analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_analysis "Mathematical analysis"), and permits mathematical series of elements of the space to be manipulated with the same ease as series of complex numbers (or vectors in a finite-dimensional Euclidean space).[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-11)

[David Hilbert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Hilbert "David Hilbert")

Prior to the development of Hilbert spaces, other generalizations of Euclidean spaces were known to [mathematicians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematician "Mathematician") and [physicists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physicist "Physicist"). In particular, the idea of an [abstract linear space (vector space)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space") had gained some traction towards the end of the 19th century:[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-12) this is a space whose elements can be added together and multiplied by scalars (such as [real](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_numbers "Real numbers") or [complex numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_numbers "Complex numbers")) without necessarily identifying these elements with ["geometric" vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_(geometric) "Vector (geometric)"), such as position and momentum vectors in physical systems. Other objects studied by mathematicians at the turn of the 20th century, in particular spaces of [sequences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sequence_(mathematics) "Sequence (mathematics)") (including [series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Series_(mathematics) "Series (mathematics)")) and spaces of functions,[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-13) can naturally be thought of as linear spaces. Functions, for instance, can be added together or multiplied by constant scalars, and these operations obey the algebraic laws satisfied by addition and scalar multiplication of spatial vectors.

In the first decade of the 20th century, parallel developments led to the introduction of Hilbert spaces. The first of these was the observation, which arose during [David Hilbert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Hilbert "David Hilbert") and [Erhard Schmidt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erhard_Schmidt "Erhard Schmidt")'s study of [integral equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral_equations "Integral equations"),[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-14) that two [square-integrable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square-integrable "Square-integrable") real-valued functions f and g on an interval [_a_, _b_] have an _inner product_

![Image 45: {\displaystyle \langle f,g\rangle =\int _{a}^{b}f(x)g(x)\,\mathrm {d} x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/15d3b8c1935031c7769ee297cf76cba993af6de5)
that has many of the familiar properties of the Euclidean dot product. In particular, the idea of an [orthogonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonality "Orthogonality") family of functions has meaning. Schmidt exploited the similarity of this inner product with the usual dot product to prove an analog of the [spectral decomposition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectral_theorem "Spectral theorem") for an operator of the form

![Image 46: {\displaystyle f(x)\mapsto \int _{a}^{b}K(x,y)f(y)\,\mathrm {d} y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e0dee2bd40943738c454875f27b51c039baf5ef5)
where K is a continuous function symmetric in x and y. The resulting [eigenfunction expansion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eigenfunction_expansion "Eigenfunction expansion") expresses the function K as a series of the form

![Image 47: {\displaystyle K(x,y)=\sum _{n}\lambda _{n}\varphi _{n}(x)\varphi _{n}(y)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/921e2db751333a14035d636bcf05c6a167344ce1)
where the functions φ n are orthogonal in the sense that ⟨_φ_ _n_, _φ_ _m_⟩ = 0 for all _n_ ≠ _m_. The individual terms in this series are sometimes referred to as elementary product solutions. However, there are eigenfunction expansions that fail to converge in a suitable sense to a square-integrable function: the missing ingredient, which ensures convergence, is completeness.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-15)

The second development was the [Lebesgue integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebesgue_integral "Lebesgue integral"), an alternative to the [Riemann integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_integral "Riemann integral") introduced by [Henri Lebesgue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Lebesgue "Henri Lebesgue") in 1904.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-16) The Lebesgue integral made it possible to integrate a much broader class of functions. In 1907, [Frigyes Riesz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frigyes_Riesz "Frigyes Riesz") and [Ernst Sigismund Fischer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ernst_Sigismund_Fischer "Ernst Sigismund Fischer") independently proved that the space _L_ 2 of square Lebesgue-integrable functions is a [complete metric space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complete_metric_space "Complete metric space").[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-17) As a consequence of the interplay between geometry and completeness, the 19th century results of [Joseph Fourier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Fourier "Joseph Fourier"), [Friedrich Bessel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Bessel "Friedrich Bessel") and [Marc-Antoine Parseval](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marc-Antoine_Parseval "Marc-Antoine Parseval") on [trigonometric series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trigonometric_series "Trigonometric series") easily carried over to these more general spaces, resulting in a geometrical and analytical apparatus now usually known as the [Riesz–Fischer theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riesz%E2%80%93Fischer_theorem "Riesz–Fischer theorem").[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-18)

Further basic results were proved in the early 20th century. For example, the [Riesz representation theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riesz_representation_theorem "Riesz representation theorem") was independently established by [Maurice Fréchet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maurice_Fr%C3%A9chet "Maurice Fréchet") and [Frigyes Riesz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frigyes_Riesz "Frigyes Riesz") in 1907.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-19)[John von Neumann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_von_Neumann "John von Neumann") coined the term _abstract Hilbert space_ in his work on unbounded [Hermitian operators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-adjoint_operator "Self-adjoint operator").[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-20) Although other mathematicians such as [Hermann Weyl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Weyl "Hermann Weyl") and [Norbert Wiener](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norbert_Wiener "Norbert Wiener") had already studied particular Hilbert spaces in great detail, often from a physically motivated point of view, von Neumann gave the first complete and axiomatic treatment of them.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-21) Von Neumann later used them in his seminal work on the foundations of quantum mechanics,[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-22) and in his continued work with [Eugene Wigner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eugene_Wigner "Eugene Wigner"). The name "Hilbert space" was soon adopted by others, for example by Hermann Weyl in his book on quantum mechanics and the theory of groups.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-Weyl31-23)

The significance of the concept of a Hilbert space was underlined with the realization that it offers one of the best [mathematical formulations of quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_formulation_of_quantum_mechanics "Mathematical formulation of quantum mechanics").[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-24) In short, the states of a quantum mechanical system are vectors in a certain Hilbert space, the observables are [hermitian operators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermitian_operator "Hermitian operator") on that space, the [symmetries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry "Symmetry") of the system are [unitary operators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unitary_operator "Unitary operator"), and [measurements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_measurement "Quantum measurement") are [orthogonal projections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_projection "Orthogonal projection"). The relation between quantum mechanical symmetries and unitary operators provided an impetus for the development of the [unitary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unitary_representation "Unitary representation")[representation theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Representation_theory "Representation theory") of [groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_(mathematics) "Group (mathematics)"), initiated in the 1928 work of Hermann Weyl.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-Weyl31-23) On the other hand, in the early 1930s it became clear that classical mechanics can be described in terms of Hilbert space ([Koopman–von Neumann classical mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Koopman%E2%80%93von_Neumann_classical_mechanics "Koopman–von Neumann classical mechanics")) and that certain properties of classical [dynamical systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynamical_systems "Dynamical systems") can be analyzed using Hilbert space techniques in the framework of [ergodic theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ergodic_theory "Ergodic theory").[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-von_Neumann_1932-25)

The algebra of [observables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable "Observable") in quantum mechanics is naturally an algebra of operators defined on a Hilbert space, according to [Werner Heisenberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Werner_Heisenberg "Werner Heisenberg")'s [matrix mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_mechanics "Matrix mechanics") formulation of quantum theory.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-26) Von Neumann began investigating [operator algebras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operator_algebra "Operator algebra") in the 1930s, as [rings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ring_(mathematics) "Ring (mathematics)") of operators on a Hilbert space. The kind of algebras studied by von Neumann and his contemporaries are now known as [von Neumann algebras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Von_Neumann_algebra "Von Neumann algebra").[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-27) In the 1940s, [Israel Gelfand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Israel_Gelfand "Israel Gelfand"), [Mark Naimark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mark_Naimark "Mark Naimark") and [Irving Segal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irving_Segal "Irving Segal") gave a definition of a kind of operator algebras called [C*-algebras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C*-algebra "C*-algebra") that on the one hand made no reference to an underlying Hilbert space, and on the other extrapolated many of the useful features of the operator algebras that had previously been studied. The spectral theorem for self-adjoint operators in particular that underlies much of the existing Hilbert space theory was generalized to C*-algebras.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-28) These techniques are now basic in abstract harmonic analysis and representation theory.

Lebesgue spaces are [function spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_space "Function space") associated to [measure spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Measure_(mathematics) "Measure (mathematics)")(_X_, _M_, _μ_), where _X_ is a set, _M_ is a [σ-algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sigma-algebra "Sigma-algebra") of subsets of _X_, and _μ_ is a [countably additive measure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Countably_additive_measure "Countably additive measure") on _M_. Let _L_ 2(_X_, _μ_) be the space of those complex-valued measurable functions on _X_ for which the [Lebesgue integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebesgue_integration "Lebesgue integration") of the square of the [absolute value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_value "Absolute value") of the function is finite, i.e., for a function _f_ in _L_ 2(_X_, _μ_), ![Image 48: {\displaystyle \int _{X}|f|^{2}\,\mathrm {d} \mu <\infty \,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/52af106413b116885195d66a5ce21446d8da34a3) and where functions are identified if and only if they differ only on a [set of measure zero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Null_set "Null set").

The inner product of functions _f_ and _g_ in _L_ 2(_X_, _μ_) is then defined as ![Image 49: {\displaystyle \langle f,g\rangle =\int _{X}f(t){\overline {g(t)}}\,\mathrm {d} \mu (t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/444efb40cac89ff9e517f21cabefac4b4c461817) or ![Image 50: {\displaystyle \langle f,g\rangle =\int _{X}{\overline {f(t)}}g(t)\,\mathrm {d} \mu (t)\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/759d01e4c6dcc1d76d34c5bf653cf220818a464d)

where the second form (conjugation of the first element) is commonly found in the [theoretical physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theoretical_physics "Theoretical physics") literature. For _f_ and _g_ in _L_ 2, the integral exists because of the Cauchy–Schwarz inequality, and defines an inner product on the space. Equipped with this inner product, _L_ 2 is in fact complete.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-29) The Lebesgue integral is essential to ensure completeness: on domains of real numbers, for instance, not enough functions are [Riemann integrable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_integral "Riemann integral").[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-30)

The Lebesgue spaces appear in many natural settings. The spaces _L_ 2(**R**) and _L_ 2([0,1]) of square-integrable functions with respect to the [Lebesgue measure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebesgue_measure "Lebesgue measure") on the real line and unit interval, respectively, are natural domains on which to define the Fourier transform and Fourier series. In other situations, the measure may be something other than the ordinary Lebesgue measure on the real line. For instance, if _w_ is any positive measurable function, the space of all measurable functions _f_ on the interval [0, 1] satisfying ![Image 51: {\displaystyle \int _{0}^{1}{\bigl |}f(t){\bigr |}^{2}w(t)\,\mathrm {d} t<\infty }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/06662023c777cfa7c3cda7e5fa8e2e3e390f053f) is called the [weighted _L_ 2 space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lp_space#Weighted_Lp_spaces "Lp space")_L_ 2

_w_([0, 1]), and _w_ is called the weight function. The inner product is defined by ![Image 52: {\displaystyle \langle f,g\rangle =\int _{0}^{1}f(t){\overline {g(t)}}w(t)\,\mathrm {d} t\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3305f5510dd0b3311769c19e47bc8f2b0c4e832d)

The weighted space _L_ 2

_w_([0, 1]) is identical with the Hilbert space _L_ 2([0, 1], _μ_) where the measure _μ_ of a Lebesgue-measurable set _A_ is defined by ![Image 53: {\displaystyle \mu (A)=\int _{A}w(t)\,\mathrm {d} t\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/536c89546b2b4876f1c6835d87700c09fd984100)

Weighted _L_ 2 spaces like this are frequently used to study [orthogonal polynomials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_polynomials "Orthogonal polynomials"), because different families of orthogonal polynomials are orthogonal with respect to different weighting functions.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-31)

[Sobolev spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sobolev_space "Sobolev space"), denoted by _H_ _s_ or _W_ _s_,2, are Hilbert spaces. These are a special kind of [function space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_space "Function space") in which [differentiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative "Derivative") may be performed, but that (unlike other [Banach spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Banach_spaces "Banach spaces") such as the [Hölder spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/H%C3%B6lder_space "Hölder space")) support the structure of an inner product. Because differentiation is permitted, Sobolev spaces are a convenient setting for the theory of [partial differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_differential_equations "Partial differential equations").[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-BeJoSc81-32) They also form the basis of the theory of [direct methods in the calculus of variations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direct_method_in_calculus_of_variations "Direct method in calculus of variations").[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-33)

For _s_ a non-negative [integer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer") and Ω ⊂ **R**_n_, the Sobolev space _H_ _s_(Ω) contains _L_ 2 functions whose [weak derivatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weak_derivative "Weak derivative") of order up to _s_ are also _L_ 2. The inner product in _H_ _s_(Ω) is ![Image 54: {\displaystyle \langle f,g\rangle =\int _{\Omega }f(x){\bar {g}}(x)\,\mathrm {d} x+\int _{\Omega }Df(x)\cdot D{\bar {g}}(x)\,\mathrm {d} x+\cdots +\int _{\Omega }D^{s}f(x)\cdot D^{s}{\bar {g}}(x)\,\mathrm {d} x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/50aeb4e11264166dd7a52c8a92ff01274b07ced5) where the dot indicates the dot product in the Euclidean space of partial derivatives of each order. Sobolev spaces can also be defined when _s_ is not an integer.

Sobolev spaces are also studied from the point of view of spectral theory, relying more specifically on the Hilbert space structure. If Ω is a suitable domain, then one can define the Sobolev space _H_ _s_(Ω) as the space of [Bessel potentials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bessel_potential "Bessel potential");[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-34) roughly, ![Image 55: {\displaystyle H^{s}(\Omega )=\left\{(1-\Delta )^{-s/2}f\mathrel {\Big |} f\in L^{2}(\Omega )\right\}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d29c7b84760df017ba43e6ddfaf97c373b67fc92)

Here Δ is the Laplacian and (1 − Δ)−_s_ / 2 is understood in terms of the [spectral mapping theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectral_mapping_theorem "Spectral mapping theorem"). Apart from providing a workable definition of Sobolev spaces for non-integer _s_, this definition also has particularly desirable properties under the [Fourier transform](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourier_transform "Fourier transform") that make it ideal for the study of [pseudodifferential operators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudodifferential_operator "Pseudodifferential operator"). Using these methods on a [compact](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compact_space "Compact space")[Riemannian manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_manifold "Riemannian manifold"), one can obtain for instance the [Hodge decomposition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hodge_decomposition "Hodge decomposition"), which is the basis of [Hodge theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hodge_theory "Hodge theory").[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-35)

### Spaces of holomorphic functions

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hilbert_space&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Spaces of holomorphic functions")]

The [Hardy spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hardy_space "Hardy space") are function spaces, arising in [complex analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_analysis "Complex analysis") and [harmonic analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harmonic_analysis "Harmonic analysis"), whose elements are certain [holomorphic functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holomorphic_function "Holomorphic function") in a complex domain.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-36) Let _U_ denote the [unit disc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_disc "Unit disc") in the complex plane. Then the Hardy space _H_ 2(_U_) is defined as the space of holomorphic functions _f_ on _U_ such that the means ![Image 56: {\displaystyle M_{r}(f)={\frac {1}{2\pi }}\int _{0}^{2\pi }\left|f{\bigl (}re^{i\theta }{\bigr )}\right|^{2}\,\mathrm {d} \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/75d48a7b8ea6e2c4f63b249ef1921e472f615354) remain bounded for _r_< 1. The norm on this Hardy space is defined by ![Image 57: {\displaystyle \left\|f\right\|_{2}=\lim _{r\to 1}{\sqrt {M_{r}(f)}}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/246ff4232c927287a6ed5b599c9fa846a1529085)

Hardy spaces in the disc are related to Fourier series. A function _f_ is in _H_ 2(_U_) if and only if ![Image 58: {\displaystyle f(z)=\sum _{n=0}^{\infty }a_{n}z^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2163356e45f7c36725ccdd94146d0f9a204b4fad) where ![Image 59: {\displaystyle \sum _{n=0}^{\infty }|a_{n}|^{2}<\infty \,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0205b868913347fe8e885d451d575382c60941d4)

Thus _H_ 2(_U_) consists of those functions that are _L_ 2 on the circle, and whose negative frequency Fourier coefficients vanish.

The [Bergman spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bergman_space "Bergman space") are another family of Hilbert spaces of holomorphic functions.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-37) Let _D_ be a bounded open set in the [complex plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_plane "Complex plane") (or a higher-dimensional complex space) and let _L_ 2, _h_(_D_) be the space of holomorphic functions _f_ in _D_ that are also in _L_ 2(_D_) in the sense that ![Image 60: {\displaystyle \|f\|^{2}=\int _{D}|f(z)|^{2}\,\mathrm {d} \mu (z)<\infty \,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7abfa454eccbd44a2f8b837454134d9770b38577) where the integral is taken with respect to the Lebesgue measure in _D_. Clearly _L_ 2, _h_(_D_) is a subspace of _L_ 2(_D_); in fact, it is a [closed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_set "Closed set") subspace, and so a Hilbert space in its own right. This is a consequence of the estimate, valid on [compact](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compact_space "Compact space") subsets _K_ of _D_, that ![Image 61: {\displaystyle \sup _{z\in K}\left|f(z)\right|\leq C_{K}\left\|f\right\|_{2}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7243a8cb764807f9a5d13eb03ada405869f8642d) which in turn follows from [Cauchy's integral formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cauchy%27s_integral_formula "Cauchy's integral formula"). Thus convergence of a sequence of holomorphic functions in _L_ 2(_D_) implies also [compact convergence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compact_convergence "Compact convergence"), and so the limit function is also holomorphic. Another consequence of this inequality is that the linear functional that evaluates a function _f_ at a point of _D_ is actually continuous on _L_ 2,_h_(_D_). The Riesz representation theorem implies that the evaluation functional can be represented as an element of _L_ 2,_h_(_D_). Thus, for every _z_ ∈ _D_, there is a function _η_ _z_ ∈ _L_ 2,_h_(_D_) such that ![Image 62: {\displaystyle f(z)=\int _{D}f(\zeta ){\overline {\eta _{z}(\zeta )}}\,\mathrm {d} \mu (\zeta )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c7ad03af32f07660d72581836b127864b66754ff) for all _f_ ∈ _L_ 2,_h_(_D_). The integrand ![Image 63: {\displaystyle K(\zeta ,z)={\overline {\eta _{z}(\zeta )}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/04b706e60c2651a125a998c77e079cda951306c4) is known as the [Bergman kernel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bergman_kernel "Bergman kernel") of _D_. This [integral kernel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral_kernel "Integral kernel") satisfies a reproducing property ![Image 64: {\displaystyle f(z)=\int _{D}f(\zeta )K(\zeta ,z)\,\mathrm {d} \mu (\zeta )\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/537dd2cecfc6886382c7172984995ebeb0fe6be1)

A Bergman space is an example of a [reproducing kernel Hilbert space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reproducing_kernel_Hilbert_space "Reproducing kernel Hilbert space"), which is a Hilbert space of functions along with a kernel _K_(_ζ_, _z_) that verifies a reproducing property analogous to this one. The Hardy space _H_ 2(_D_) also admits a reproducing kernel, known as the [Szegő kernel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Szeg%C5%91_kernel "Szegő kernel").[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-38) Reproducing kernels are common in other areas of mathematics as well. For instance, in [harmonic analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harmonic_analysis "Harmonic analysis") the [Poisson kernel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poisson_kernel "Poisson kernel") is a reproducing kernel for the Hilbert space of square-integrable [harmonic functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harmonic_function "Harmonic function") in the [unit ball](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_ball "Unit ball"). That the latter is a Hilbert space at all is a consequence of the mean value theorem for harmonic functions.

Many of the applications of Hilbert spaces exploit the fact that Hilbert spaces support generalizations of simple geometric concepts like [projection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projection_operator "Projection operator") and [change of basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Change_of_basis "Change of basis") from their usual finite dimensional setting. In particular, the [spectral theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectral_theory "Spectral theory") of [continuous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_function "Continuous function")[self-adjoint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-adjoint_operator "Self-adjoint operator")[linear operators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_operator "Linear operator") on a Hilbert space generalizes the usual [spectral decomposition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eigendecomposition_of_a_matrix "Eigendecomposition of a matrix") of a [matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_(mathematics) "Matrix (mathematics)"), and this often plays a major role in applications of the theory to other areas of mathematics and physics.

### Sturm–Liouville theory

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hilbert_space&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Sturm–Liouville theory")]

[![Image 65](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Harmonic_partials_on_strings.svg/250px-Harmonic_partials_on_strings.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Harmonic_partials_on_strings.svg)

The [overtones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Overtone "Overtone") of a vibrating string. These are [eigenfunctions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eigenfunction "Eigenfunction") of an associated Sturm–Liouville problem. The eigenvalues 1, ⁠1/2⁠, ⁠1/3⁠, ... form the (musical) [harmonic series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harmonic_series_(music) "Harmonic series (music)").

In the theory of [ordinary differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordinary_differential_equation "Ordinary differential equation"), spectral methods on a suitable Hilbert space are used to study the behavior of eigenvalues and eigenfunctions of differential equations. For example, the [Sturm–Liouville problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sturm%E2%80%93Liouville_theory "Sturm–Liouville theory") arises in the study of the harmonics of waves in a violin string or a drum, and is a central problem in [ordinary differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordinary_differential_equations "Ordinary differential equations").[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-39) The problem is a differential equation of the form ![Image 66: {\displaystyle -{\frac {\mathrm {d} }{\mathrm {d} x}}\left[p(x){\frac {\mathrm {d} y}{\mathrm {d} x}}\right]+q(x)y=\lambda w(x)y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8d4e93c4082311667644e8554f78774b963ffb0e) for an unknown function _y_ on an interval [_a_, _b_], satisfying general homogeneous [Robin boundary conditions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robin_boundary_conditions "Robin boundary conditions")![Image 67: {\displaystyle {\begin{cases}\alpha y(a)+\alpha 'y'(a)&=0\\\beta y(b)+\beta 'y'(b)&=0\,.\end{cases}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/992667418f6db4ca72e98721e1d698be8a90a7e8) The functions _p_, _q_, and _w_ are given in advance, and the problem is to find the function _y_ and constants _λ_ for which the equation has a solution. The problem only has solutions for certain values of _λ_, called eigenvalues of the system, and this is a consequence of the spectral theorem for [compact operators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compact_operator "Compact operator") applied to the [integral operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral_operator "Integral operator") defined by the [Green's function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green%27s_function "Green's function") for the system. Furthermore, another consequence of this general result is that the eigenvalues _λ_ of the system can be arranged in an increasing sequence tending to infinity.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-40)[[nb 2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-41)

### Partial differential equations

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hilbert_space&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Partial differential equations")]

Hilbert spaces form a basic tool in the study of [partial differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_differential_equations "Partial differential equations").[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-BeJoSc81-32) For many classes of partial differential equations, such as linear [elliptic equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptic_partial_differential_equation "Elliptic partial differential equation"), it is possible to consider a generalized solution (known as a [weak](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weak_derivative "Weak derivative") solution) by enlarging the class of functions. Many weak formulations involve the class of [Sobolev functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sobolev_space "Sobolev space"), which is a Hilbert space. A suitable weak formulation reduces to a geometrical problem, the analytic problem of finding a solution or, often what is more important, showing that a solution exists and is unique for given boundary data. For linear elliptic equations, one geometrical result that ensures unique solvability for a large class of problems is the [Lax–Milgram theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lax%E2%80%93Milgram_theorem "Lax–Milgram theorem"). This strategy forms the rudiment of the [Galerkin method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galerkin_method "Galerkin method") (a [finite element method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_element_method "Finite element method")) for numerical solution of partial differential equations.[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-42)

A typical example is the [Poisson equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poisson_equation "Poisson equation")−Δ _u_ = _g_ with [Dirichlet boundary conditions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dirichlet_boundary_conditions "Dirichlet boundary conditions") in a bounded domain Ω in **R**2. The weak formulation consists of finding a function _u_ such that, for all continuously differentiable functions _v_ in Ω vanishing on the boundary: ![Image 68: {\displaystyle \int _{\Omega }\nabla u\cdot \nabla v=\int _{\Omega }gv\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3f8bcf957f73f0f6f2b6c40537741271a5923c38)

This can be recast in terms of the Hilbert space _H_ 1

0(Ω) consisting of functions _u_ such that _u_, along with its weak partial derivatives, are square integrable on Ω, and vanish on the boundary. The question then reduces to finding _u_ in this space such that for all _v_ in this space ![Image 69: {\displaystyle a(u,v)=b(v)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/386fc8bc772c1d357db4e4549e05ad79174118df)

where _a_ is a continuous [bilinear form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bilinear_form "Bilinear form"), and _b_ is a continuous [linear functional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_functional "Linear functional"), given respectively by ![Image 70: {\displaystyle a(u,v)=\int _{\Omega }\nabla u\cdot \nabla v,\quad b(v)=\int _{\Omega }gv\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eda62b2fa76285d30b8ca17da5714caa584b0da4)

Since the Poisson equation is [elliptic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptic_partial_differential_equation "Elliptic partial differential equation"), it follows from Poincaré's inequality that the bilinear form _a_ is [coercive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coercive_function "Coercive function"). The Lax–Milgram theorem then ensures the existence and uniqueness of solutions of this equation.[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-43)

Hilbert spaces allow for many elliptic partial differential equations to be formulated in a similar way, and the Lax–Milgram theorem is then a basic tool in their analysis. With suitable modifications, similar techniques can be applied to [parabolic partial differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parabolic_partial_differential_equation "Parabolic partial differential equation") and certain [hyperbolic partial differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_partial_differential_equation "Hyperbolic partial differential equation").[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-44)

[![Image 71](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/BunimovichStadium.svg/250px-BunimovichStadium.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:BunimovichStadium.svg)

The path of a [billiard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynamical_billiards "Dynamical billiards") ball in the [Bunimovich stadium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bunimovich_stadium "Bunimovich stadium") is described by an ergodic [dynamical system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynamical_system "Dynamical system").

The field of [ergodic theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ergodic_theory "Ergodic theory") is the study of the long-term behavior of [chaotic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chaos_theory "Chaos theory")[dynamical systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynamical_system "Dynamical system"). The protypical case of a field that ergodic theory applies to is [thermodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamics "Thermodynamics"), in which—though the microscopic state of a system is extremely complicated (it is impossible to understand the ensemble of individual collisions between particles of matter)—the average behavior over sufficiently long time intervals is tractable. The [laws of thermodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laws_of_thermodynamics "Laws of thermodynamics") are assertions about such average behavior. In particular, one formulation of the [zeroth law of thermodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zeroth_law_of_thermodynamics "Zeroth law of thermodynamics") asserts that over sufficiently long timescales, the only functionally independent measurement that one can make of a thermodynamic system in equilibrium is its total energy, in the form of [temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperature "Temperature").[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-45)

An ergodic dynamical system is one for which, apart from the energy—measured by the [Hamiltonian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamiltonian_(quantum_mechanics) "Hamiltonian (quantum mechanics)")—there are no other functionally independent [conserved quantities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conserved_quantities "Conserved quantities") on the [phase space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phase_space "Phase space"). More explicitly, suppose that the energy _E_ is fixed, and let Ω _E_ be the subset of the phase space consisting of all states of energy _E_ (an energy surface), and let _T_ _t_ denote the evolution operator on the phase space. The dynamical system is ergodic if every invariant measurable functions on Ω _E_ is constant [almost everywhere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Almost_everywhere "Almost everywhere").[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-46) An invariant function _f_ is one for which ![Image 72: {\displaystyle f(T_{t}w)=f(w)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/edf2a493c10cb88b35c1d4a145926a7ff2cd8842) for all _w_ on Ω _E_ and all time _t_. [Liouville's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liouville%27s_theorem_(Hamiltonian) "Liouville's theorem (Hamiltonian)") implies that there exists a [measure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Measure_theory "Measure theory")_μ_ on the energy surface that is invariant under the [time translation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_translation "Time translation"). As a result, time translation is a [unitary transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unitary_transformation "Unitary transformation") of the Hilbert space _L_ 2(Ω _E_, _μ_) consisting of square-integrable functions on the energy surface Ω _E_ with respect to the inner product ![Image 73: {\displaystyle \left\langle f,g\right\rangle _{L^{2}\left(\Omega _{E},\mu \right)}=\int _{E}f{\bar {g}}\,\mathrm {d} \mu \,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/decbac50d55419a511d0372b6dfad9baa58a20f4)

The von Neumann mean ergodic theorem[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-von_Neumann_1932-25) states the following:

*   If _U_ _t_ is a (strongly continuous) one-parameter [semigroup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semigroup "Semigroup") of unitary operators on a Hilbert space _H_, and _P_ is the orthogonal projection onto the space of common fixed points of _U_ _t_, {_x_ ∈_H_ | _U_ _t_ _x_ = _x_, ∀_t_> 0}, then ![Image 74: {\displaystyle Px=\lim _{T\to \infty }{\frac {1}{T}}\int _{0}^{T}U_{t}x\,\mathrm {d} t\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/58abae503a6878f01de9a3c3517ae8c94ecfbd73)

For an ergodic system, the fixed set of the time evolution consists only of the constant functions, so the ergodic theorem implies the following:[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-47) for any function _f_ ∈ _L_ 2(Ω _E_, _μ_), ![Image 75: {\displaystyle {\underset {T\to \infty }{L^{2}-\lim }}{\frac {1}{T}}\int _{0}^{T}f(T_{t}w)\,\mathrm {d} t=\int _{\Omega _{E}}f(y)\,\mathrm {d} \mu (y)\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b23f47d65d6729b56ee0921737f1840150206835)

That is, the long time average of an observable _f_ is equal to its expectation value over an energy surface.

[![Image 76](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Sawtooth_Fourier_Analysys.svg/250px-Sawtooth_Fourier_Analysys.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Sawtooth_Fourier_Analysys.svg)

Superposition of sinusoidal wave basis functions (bottom) to form a sawtooth wave (top)

[![Image 77](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Harmoniki.png/250px-Harmoniki.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Harmoniki.png)

[Spherical harmonics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_harmonics "Spherical harmonics"), an orthonormal basis for the Hilbert space of square-integrable functions on the sphere, shown graphed along the radial direction

One of the basic goals of [Fourier analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourier_analysis "Fourier analysis") is to decompose a function into a (possibly infinite) [linear combination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_combination "Linear combination") of given basis functions: the associated [Fourier series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourier_series "Fourier series"). The classical Fourier series associated to a function _f_ defined on the interval [0, 1] is a series of the form ![Image 78: {\displaystyle \sum _{n=-\infty }^{\infty }a_{n}e^{2\pi in\theta }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8e2147afcbe4d3a488c888213ccba7a7aff0b3f9) where ![Image 79: {\displaystyle a_{n}=\int _{0}^{1}f(\theta )\;\!e^{-2\pi in\theta }\,\mathrm {d} \theta \,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/929652493606593c89b5d1e2831e236bd4d59912)

The example of adding up the first few terms in a Fourier series for a sawtooth function is shown in the figure. The basis functions are sine waves with wavelengths ⁠_λ_/_n_⁠ (for integer _n_) shorter than the wavelength _λ_ of the sawtooth itself (except for _n_ = 1, the _fundamental_ wave).

A significant problem in classical Fourier series asks in what sense the Fourier series converges, if at all, to the function _f_. Hilbert space methods provide one possible answer to this question.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-48) The functions _e n_(_θ_) = _e_ 2π _inθ_ form an orthogonal basis of the Hilbert space _L_ 2([0, 1]). Consequently, any square-integrable function can be expressed as a series ![Image 80: {\displaystyle f(\theta )=\sum _{n}a_{n}e_{n}(\theta )\,,\quad a_{n}=\langle f,e_{n}\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/180ca7e1463bb5f755ba1377633d8643b9b9d312) and, moreover, this series converges in the Hilbert space sense (that is, in the [_L_ 2 mean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mean_convergence "Mean convergence")).

The problem can also be studied from the abstract point of view: every Hilbert space has an [orthonormal basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthonormal_basis "Orthonormal basis"), and every element of the Hilbert space can be written in a unique way as a sum of multiples of these basis elements. The coefficients appearing on these basis elements are sometimes known abstractly as the Fourier coefficients of the element of the space.[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-49) The abstraction is especially useful when it is more natural to use different basis functions for a space such as _L_ 2([0, 1]). In many circumstances, it is desirable not to decompose a function into trigonometric functions, but rather into [orthogonal polynomials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_polynomials "Orthogonal polynomials") or [wavelets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wavelet "Wavelet") for instance,[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-50) and in higher dimensions into [spherical harmonics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_harmonics "Spherical harmonics").[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-51)

For instance, if _e_ _n_ are any orthonormal basis functions of _L_ 2[0, 1], then a given function in _L_ 2[0, 1] can be approximated as a finite linear combination[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-52)![Image 81: {\displaystyle f(x)\approx f_{n}(x)=a_{1}e_{1}(x)+a_{2}e_{2}(x)+\cdots +a_{n}e_{n}(x)\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e7942e7d3c2d9843987b8382eeae55b7f1f1f04b)

The coefficients {_a_ _j_} are selected to make the magnitude of the difference ‖_f_ − _f_ _n_‖2 as small as possible. Geometrically, the [best approximation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#Best_approximation) is the [orthogonal projection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#Orthogonal_complements_and_projections) of _f_ onto the subspace consisting of all linear combinations of the {_e_ _j_}, and can be calculated by[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-53)![Image 82: {\displaystyle a_{j}=\int _{0}^{1}{\overline {e_{j}(x)}}f(x)\,\mathrm {d} x\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6880852f6c9f4506bca5fc28e0dfdf088fa9ae19)

That this formula minimizes the difference ‖_f_ − _f_ _n_‖2 is a consequence of [Bessel's inequality and Parseval's formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#Bessel's_inequality_and_Parseval's_formula).

In various applications to physical problems, a function can be decomposed into physically meaningful [eigenfunctions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eigenfunction "Eigenfunction") of a [differential operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_operator "Differential operator") (typically the [Laplace operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laplace_operator "Laplace operator")): this forms the foundation for the spectral study of functions, in reference to the [spectrum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectral_theorem "Spectral theorem") of the differential operator.[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-54) A concrete physical application involves the problem of [hearing the shape of a drum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hearing_the_shape_of_a_drum "Hearing the shape of a drum"): given the fundamental modes of vibration that a drumhead is capable of producing, can one infer the shape of the drum itself?[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-55) The mathematical formulation of this question involves the [Dirichlet eigenvalues](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dirichlet_eigenvalue "Dirichlet eigenvalue") of the Laplace equation in the plane, that represent the fundamental modes of vibration in direct analogy with the integers that represent the fundamental modes of vibration of the violin string.

[Spectral theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectral_theory "Spectral theory") also underlies certain aspects of the [Fourier transform](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourier_transform "Fourier transform") of a function. Whereas Fourier analysis decomposes a function defined on a [compact set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compact_set "Compact set") into the discrete spectrum of the Laplacian (which corresponds to the vibrations of a violin string or drum), the Fourier transform of a function is the decomposition of a function defined on all of Euclidean space into its components in the [continuous spectrum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_spectrum "Continuous spectrum") of the Laplacian. The Fourier transformation is also geometrical, in a sense made precise by the [Plancherel theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plancherel_theorem "Plancherel theorem"), that asserts that it is an [isometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isometry "Isometry") of one Hilbert space (the "time domain") with another (the "frequency domain"). This isometry property of the Fourier transformation is a recurring theme in abstract [harmonic analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harmonic_analysis "Harmonic analysis") (since it reflects the conservation of energy for the continuous Fourier Transform), as evidenced for instance by the [Plancherel theorem for spherical functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plancherel_theorem_for_spherical_functions "Plancherel theorem for spherical functions") occurring in [noncommutative harmonic analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noncommutative_harmonic_analysis "Noncommutative harmonic analysis").

[![Image 83](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/HAtomOrbitals.png/250px-HAtomOrbitals.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:HAtomOrbitals.png)

The [orbitals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_orbital "Atomic orbital") of an [electron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron "Electron") in a [hydrogen atom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_atom "Hydrogen atom") are [eigenfunctions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eigenfunction "Eigenfunction") of the [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_(physics) "Energy (physics)").

In the mathematically rigorous formulation of [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics"), developed by [John von Neumann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_von_Neumann "John von Neumann"),[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-56) the possible states (more precisely, the [pure states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pure_state "Pure state")) of a quantum mechanical system are represented by [unit vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_vector "Unit vector") (called _state vectors_) residing in a complex separable Hilbert space, known as the [state space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_state_space "Quantum state space"), well defined up to a complex number of norm 1 (the [phase factor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phase_factor "Phase factor")). In other words, the possible states are points in the [projectivization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_space "Projective space") of a Hilbert space, usually called the [complex projective space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_projective_space "Complex projective space"). The exact nature of this Hilbert space is dependent on the system; for example, the position and momentum states for a single non-relativistic spin zero particle is the space of all [square-integrable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square-integrable "Square-integrable") functions, while the states for the spin of a single proton are unit elements of the two-dimensional complex Hilbert space of [spinors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spinors_in_three_dimensions "Spinors in three dimensions"). Each observable is represented by a [self-adjoint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-adjoint_operator "Self-adjoint operator")[linear operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_operator "Linear operator") acting on the state space. Each eigenstate of an observable corresponds to an [eigenvector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eigenvector "Eigenvector") of the operator, and the associated [eigenvalue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eigenvalue "Eigenvalue") corresponds to the value of the observable in that eigenstate.[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-57)

The inner product between two state vectors is a complex number known as a [probability amplitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability_amplitude "Probability amplitude"). During an ideal measurement of a quantum mechanical system, the probability that a system collapses from a given initial state to a particular eigenstate is given by the square of the [absolute value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_value "Absolute value") of the probability amplitudes between the initial and final states.[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-58) The possible results of a measurement are the eigenvalues of the operator—which explains the choice of self-adjoint operators, for all the eigenvalues must be real. The probability distribution of an observable in a given state can be found by computing the spectral decomposition of the corresponding operator.[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-59)

For a general system, states are typically not pure, but instead are represented as statistical mixtures of pure states, or mixed states, given by [density matrices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Density_matrix "Density matrix"): self-adjoint operators of [trace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trace_of_a_matrix "Trace of a matrix") one on a Hilbert space.[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-60) Moreover, for general quantum mechanical systems, the effects of a single measurement can influence other parts of a system in a manner that is described instead by a [positive operator valued measure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positive_operator_valued_measure "Positive operator valued measure"). Thus the structure both of the states and observables in the general theory is considerably more complicated than the idealization for pure states.[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-61)

In [probability theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability_theory "Probability theory"), Hilbert spaces also have diverse applications. Here a fundamental Hilbert space is the space of [random variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Random_variable "Random variable") on a given [probability space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability_space "Probability space"), having class ![Image 84: {\displaystyle L^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ba162c66ca85776c83557af5088cc6f8584d1912) (finite first and second [moments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_(mathematics) "Moment (mathematics)")). A common operation in statistics is that of centering a random variable by subtracting its [expectation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expected_value "Expected value"). Thus if ![Image 85: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) is a random variable, then ![Image 86: {\displaystyle X-E(X)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0abbac80451543e002ee8914298f66c8cd53a8ea) is its centering. In the Hilbert space view, this is the orthogonal projection of ![Image 87: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) onto the [kernel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kernel_(algebra) "Kernel (algebra)") of the expectation operator, which a [continuous linear functional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_linear_functional "Continuous linear functional") on the Hilbert space (in fact, the inner product with the constant random variable 1), and so this kernel is a closed subspace.

The [conditional expectation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conditional_expectation "Conditional expectation") has a natural interpretation in the Hilbert space.[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-62) Suppose that a probability space ![Image 88: {\displaystyle (\Omega ,P,{\mathcal {B}})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ad2a7e94a34894aec6994e0834db493394c87aa0) is given, where ![Image 89: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {B}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e5622de88a69f68340f8dcb43d0b8bd443ba9e13) is a [sigma algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sigma_algebra "Sigma algebra") on the set ![Image 90: {\displaystyle \Omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/24b0d5ca6f381068d756f6337c08e0af9d1eeb6f), and ![Image 91: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a) is a [probability measure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability_measure "Probability measure") on the measure space ![Image 92: {\displaystyle (\Omega ,{\mathcal {B}})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3c71c3b681b11d672fb4712a121dc140ff810ef). If ![Image 93: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {F}}\leq {\mathcal {B}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/28298e2f52274e8c0bf0e03dbe4e40f15691e2a2) is a sigma subalgebra of ![Image 94: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {B}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e5622de88a69f68340f8dcb43d0b8bd443ba9e13), then the conditional expectation ![Image 95: {\displaystyle E[X\mid {\mathcal {F}}]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/07eba3c083644e9f449c3bf4343af85f68772ac8) is the orthogonal projection of ![Image 96: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) onto the subspace of ![Image 97: {\displaystyle L^{2}(\Omega ,P)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8fcb896d24e39cee28d95a2483f448d3fa231ae1) consisting of the ![Image 98: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {F}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/205d4b91000d9dcf1a5bbabdfa6a8395fa60b676)-measurable functions. If the random variable ![Image 99: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) in ![Image 100: {\displaystyle L^{2}(\Omega ,P)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8fcb896d24e39cee28d95a2483f448d3fa231ae1) is independent of the sigma algebra ![Image 101: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {F}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/205d4b91000d9dcf1a5bbabdfa6a8395fa60b676) then conditional expectation ![Image 102: {\displaystyle E(X\mid {\mathcal {F}})=E(X)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6d98c0092eb22e447f7bea2948250aa9a1313c36), i.e., its projection onto the ![Image 103: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {F}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/205d4b91000d9dcf1a5bbabdfa6a8395fa60b676)-measurable functions is constant. Equivalently, the projection of its centering is zero.

In particular, if two random variables ![Image 104: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) and ![Image 105: {\displaystyle Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/961d67d6b454b4df2301ac571808a3538b3a6d3f) (in ![Image 106: {\displaystyle L^{2}(\Omega ,P)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8fcb896d24e39cee28d95a2483f448d3fa231ae1)) are independent, then the centered random variables ![Image 107: {\displaystyle X-E(X)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0abbac80451543e002ee8914298f66c8cd53a8ea) and ![Image 108: {\displaystyle Y-E(Y)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7d3edaee1c70a145e670d5de5d609b729167ab65) are orthogonal. (This means that the two variables have zero [covariance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covariance "Covariance"): they are [uncorrelated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uncorrelated "Uncorrelated").) In that case, the Pythagorean theorem in the kernel of the expectation operator implies that the [variances](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variance "Variance") of ![Image 109: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) and ![Image 110: {\displaystyle Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/961d67d6b454b4df2301ac571808a3538b3a6d3f) satisfy the identity: ![Image 111: {\displaystyle \operatorname {Var} (X+Y)=\operatorname {Var} (X)+\operatorname {Var} (Y),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7edda73fedaf279c028c7bbf77cd3cb044e301e5) sometimes called the Pythagorean theorem of statistics, and is of importance in [linear regression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_regression "Linear regression").[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-63) As [Stapleton (1995)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#CITEREFStapleton1995) puts it, "the [analysis of variance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analysis_of_variance "Analysis of variance") may be viewed as the decomposition of the squared length of a vector into the sum of the squared lengths of several vectors, using the Pythagorean Theorem."

The theory of [martingales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Martingale_(probability_theory) "Martingale (probability theory)") can be formulated in Hilbert spaces. A martingale in a Hilbert space is a sequence ![Image 112: {\displaystyle x_{1},x_{2},\dots }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e71a0bff46c1d8ad13a271387dcf9c11fb30cee9) of elements of a Hilbert space such that, for each _n_, ![Image 113: {\displaystyle x_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7c5ea190699149306d242b70439e663559e3ffbe) is the orthogonal projection of ![Image 114: {\displaystyle x_{n+1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e76103271a353337f6b3c697209cb19359807df) onto the linear hull of ![Image 115: {\displaystyle x_{1},\dots ,x_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e5afdbc2d248d8fa9ba2c4f5188d946a0537e753).[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-64) If the ![Image 116: {\displaystyle x_{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6d2b88c64c76a03611549fb9b4cf4ed060b56002) are random variables, this reproduces the usual definition of a (discrete) martingale: the expectation of ![Image 117: {\displaystyle x_{n+1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e76103271a353337f6b3c697209cb19359807df), conditioned on ![Image 118: {\displaystyle x_{1},\dots ,x_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e5afdbc2d248d8fa9ba2c4f5188d946a0537e753), is equal to ![Image 119: {\displaystyle x_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7c5ea190699149306d242b70439e663559e3ffbe).

Hilbert spaces are also used throughout the foundations of the [Itô calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/It%C3%B4_calculus "Itô calculus").[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-65) To any square-integrable [martingale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Martingale_(probability_theory) "Martingale (probability theory)"), it is possible to associate a Hilbert norm on the space of equivalence classes of [progressively measurable processes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Progressively_measurable_process "Progressively measurable process") with respect to the martingale (using the [quadratic variation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadratic_variation "Quadratic variation") of the martingale as the measure). The [Itô integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/It%C3%B4_integral "Itô integral") can be constructed by first defining it for [simple processes](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Simple_process&action=edit&redlink=1 "Simple process (page does not exist)"), and then exploiting their density in the Hilbert space. A noteworthy result is then the [Itô isometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/It%C3%B4_isometry "Itô isometry"), which attests that for any martingale _M_ having quadratic variation measure ![Image 120: {\displaystyle d\langle M\rangle _{t}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9d8ebcf606010792fcbc0ce38340f2f2f774daa8), and any progressively measurable process _H_: ![Image 121: {\displaystyle E\left[\left(\int _{0}^{t}H_{s}\,dM_{s}\right)^{2}\right]=E\left[\int _{0}^{t}H_{s}^{2}\,d\langle M\rangle _{s}\right]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81b5afa207e49d4c47140984148e12bc0d683da0) whenever the expectation on the right-hand side is finite.

A deeper application of Hilbert spaces that is especially important in the theory of [Gaussian processes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaussian_process "Gaussian process") is an attempt, due to [Leonard Gross](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leonard_Gross "Leonard Gross") and others, to make sense of certain formal integrals over infinite dimensional spaces like the [Feynman path integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feynman_path_integral "Feynman path integral") from [quantum field theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_field_theory "Quantum field theory"). The problem with integrals like this is that there is no [infinite dimensional Lebesgue measure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinite_dimensional_Lebesgue_measure "Infinite dimensional Lebesgue measure"). The notion of an [abstract Wiener space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abstract_Wiener_space "Abstract Wiener space") allows one to construct a measure on a Banach space _B_ that contains a Hilbert space _H_, called the [Cameron–Martin space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cameron%E2%80%93Martin_space "Cameron–Martin space"), as a dense subset, out of a finitely additive cylinder set measure on _H_. The resulting measure on _B_ is countably additive and invariant under translation by elements of _H_, and this provides a mathematically rigorous way of thinking of the [Wiener measure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wiener_measure "Wiener measure") as a Gaussian measure on the Sobolev space ![Image 122: {\displaystyle H^{1}([0,\infty ))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ac69384c0c142a8bcb92dfa23538dd4f3c8f1f22).[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-66)

Any true physical color can be represented by a combination of pure [spectral colors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectral_color "Spectral color"). As physical colors can be composed of any number of spectral colors, the space of physical colors may aptly be represented by a Hilbert space over spectral colors. Humans have [three types of cone cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trichromacy "Trichromacy") for color perception, so the perceivable colors can be represented by 3-dimensional Euclidean space. The many-to-one linear mapping from the Hilbert space of physical colors to the Euclidean space of human perceivable colors explains why many distinct physical colors may be perceived by humans to be identical (e.g., pure yellow light versus a mix of red and green light, see _[Metamerism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metamerism\_(color) "Metamerism (color)")_).[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-67)[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-68)

### Pythagorean identity

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hilbert_space&action=edit&section=21 "Edit section: Pythagorean identity")]

Two vectors _u_ and _v_ in a Hilbert space _H_ are orthogonal when ⟨_u_, _v_⟩ = 0. The notation for this is _u_ ⊥ _v_. More generally, when _S_ is a subset in _H_, the notation _u_ ⊥ _S_ means that _u_ is orthogonal to every element from _S_.

When _u_ and _v_ are orthogonal, one has ![Image 123: {\displaystyle \|u+v\|^{2}=\langle u+v,u+v\rangle =\langle u,u\rangle +2\,\operatorname {Re} \langle u,v\rangle +\langle v,v\rangle =\|u\|^{2}+\|v\|^{2}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e18db6083346d3b1f8e925f0d2d6a5a1cf02d288)

By induction on _n_, this is extended to any family _u_ 1, ..., _u n_ of _n_ orthogonal vectors, ![Image 124: {\displaystyle \left\|u_{1}+\cdots +u_{n}\right\|^{2}=\left\|u_{1}\right\|^{2}+\cdots +\left\|u_{n}\right\|^{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ac8a2ecc3d4b7abb352ce0887b1eec38f97295f0)

Whereas the Pythagorean identity as stated is valid in any inner product space, completeness is required for the extension of the Pythagorean identity to series.[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-69) A series Σ _u k_ of _orthogonal_ vectors converges in _H_ if and only if the series of squares of norms converges, and ![Image 125: {\displaystyle {\Biggl \|}\sum _{k=0}^{\infty }u_{k}{\Biggr \|}^{2}=\sum _{k=0}^{\infty }\left\|u_{k}\right\|^{2}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ca2cbc044bcb2f7757753e87855fe3ff07aae355) Furthermore, the sum of a series of orthogonal vectors is independent of the order in which it is taken.

### Parallelogram identity and polarization

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hilbert_space&action=edit&section=22 "Edit section: Parallelogram identity and polarization")]

[![Image 126](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Color_parallelogram.svg/250px-Color_parallelogram.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Color_parallelogram.svg)

Geometrically, the parallelogram identity asserts that AC 2 + BD 2 = 2(AB 2 + AD 2). In words, the sum of the squares of the diagonals is twice the sum of the squares of any two adjacent sides.

By definition, every Hilbert space is also a [Banach space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Banach_space "Banach space"). Furthermore, in every Hilbert space the following [parallelogram identity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram_identity "Parallelogram identity") holds:[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-70)![Image 127: {\displaystyle \|u+v\|^{2}+\|u-v\|^{2}=2{\bigl (}\|u\|^{2}+\|v\|^{2}{\bigr )}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/99f0e0121d10601ed32a1acf93b491870eef3bd3)

Conversely, every Banach space in which the parallelogram identity holds is a Hilbert space, and the inner product is uniquely determined by the norm by the [polarization identity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polarization_identity "Polarization identity").[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-71) For real Hilbert spaces, the polarization identity is ![Image 128: {\displaystyle \langle u,v\rangle ={\tfrac {1}{4}}{\bigl (}\|u+v\|^{2}-\|u-v\|^{2}{\bigr )}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bbddb1a3d65ef0456f237dcad6a74b6ce8954e02)

For complex Hilbert spaces, it is ![Image 129: {\displaystyle \langle u,v\rangle ={\tfrac {1}{4}}{\bigl (}\|u+v\|^{2}-\|u-v\|^{2}+i\|u+iv\|^{2}-i\|u-iv\|^{2}{\bigr )}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e178f0bd9f69910d6d29b3159da2a40fc17481fe)

The parallelogram law implies that any Hilbert space is a [uniformly convex Banach space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uniformly_convex_Banach_space "Uniformly convex Banach space").[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-72)

This subsection employs the [Hilbert projection theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_projection_theorem "Hilbert projection theorem"). If _C_ is a non-empty closed convex subset of a Hilbert space _H_ and _x_ a point in _H_, there exists a unique point _y_ ∈ _C_ that minimizes the distance between _x_ and points in _C_,[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-73)![Image 130: {\displaystyle y\in C\,,\quad \|x-y\|=\operatorname {dist} (x,C)=\min {\bigl \{}\|x-z\|\mathrel {\big |} z\in C{\bigr \}}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4d4e7ee5ff6bed2e474dc7061e2a68c1ee67522f)

This is equivalent to saying that there is a point with minimal norm in the translated convex set _D_ = _C_ − _x_. The proof consists in showing that every minimizing sequence (_d n_) ⊂ _D_ is Cauchy (using the parallelogram identity) hence converges (using completeness) to a point in _D_ that has minimal norm. More generally, this holds in any uniformly convex Banach space.[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-74)

When this result is applied to a closed subspace _F_ of _H_, it can be shown that the point _y_ ∈ _F_ closest to _x_ is characterized by[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-75)![Image 131: {\displaystyle y\in F\,,\quad x-y\perp F\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/51abff2fb0fa9975d7b21d7945b9ec722369b5d0)

This point _y_ is the _orthogonal projection_ of _x_ onto _F_, and the mapping _P F_: _x_ → _y_ is linear (see _[§Orthogonal complements and projections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert\_space#Orthogonal\_complements\_and\_projections)_). This result is especially significant in [applied mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Applied_mathematics "Applied mathematics"), especially [numerical analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Numerical_analysis "Numerical analysis"), where it forms the basis of [least squares](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Least_squares "Least squares") methods.[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-76)

In particular, when _F_ is not equal to _H_, one can find a nonzero vector _v_ orthogonal to _F_ (select _x_ ∉ _F_ and _v_ = _x_ − _y_). A very useful criterion is obtained by applying this observation to the closed subspace _F_ generated by a subset _S_ of _H_.

A subset _S_ of _H_ spans a dense vector subspace if (and only if) the vector 0 is the sole vector _v_ ∈ _H_ orthogonal to _S_.
The [dual space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_dual_space "Continuous dual space")_H_* is the space of all [continuous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_function_(topology) "Continuous function (topology)") linear functions from the space _H_ into the base field. It carries a natural norm, defined by ![Image 132: {\displaystyle \|\varphi \|=\sup _{\|x\|=1,x\in H}|\varphi (x)|\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/63ca1b74cb8e720c7d8ebc2a59d67b6427d479ef) This norm satisfies the [parallelogram law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram_law "Parallelogram law"), and so the dual space is also an inner product space where this inner product can be defined in terms of this dual norm by using the [polarization identity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polarization_identity "Polarization identity"). The dual space is also complete so it is a Hilbert space in its own right. If _e_• = (_e_ _i_)_i_ ∈ _I_ is a complete orthonormal basis for H then the inner product on the dual space of any two ![Image 133: {\displaystyle f,g\in H^{*}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35514ab9493e958f4c5de4ebfbaf2c7561222492) is ![Image 134: {\displaystyle \langle f,g\rangle _{H^{*}}=\sum _{i\in I}f(e_{i}){\overline {g(e_{i})}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ad405e9182a274919f6018875a65f20132ced37f) where all but countably many of the terms in this series are zero.

The [Riesz representation theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riesz_representation_theorem "Riesz representation theorem") affords a convenient description of the dual space. To every element _u_ of _H_, there is a unique element _φ u_ of _H_*, defined by ![Image 135: {\displaystyle \varphi _{u}(x)=\langle x,u\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8bb78bcc918808e9e933a0096a2af8211f15a029) where moreover, ![Image 136: {\displaystyle \left\|\varphi _{u}\right\|=\left\|u\right\|.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cb85d4871783b5ce911c1466616a910ea1422cae)

The Riesz representation theorem states that the map from _H_ to _H_* defined by _u_ ↦ _φ u_ is [surjective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surjective_map "Surjective map"), which makes this map an [isometric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isometry "Isometry")[antilinear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antilinear_map "Antilinear map") isomorphism.[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-77) So to every element _φ_ of the dual _H_* there exists one and only one _u φ_ in _H_ such that ![Image 137: {\displaystyle \langle x,u_{\varphi }\rangle =\varphi (x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8e56abca14ae484d69e5eaeaee5d875b3e1f24b8) for all _x_ ∈ _H_. The inner product on the dual space _H_* satisfies ![Image 138: {\displaystyle \langle \varphi ,\psi \rangle =\langle u_{\psi },u_{\varphi }\rangle \,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fe81611f66827f550f48ab0e8d55854e834f8408)

The reversal of order on the right-hand side restores linearity in _φ_ from the antilinearity of _u φ_. In the real case, the antilinear isomorphism from _H_ to its dual is actually an isomorphism, and so real Hilbert spaces are naturally isomorphic to their own duals.

The representing vector _u φ_ is obtained in the following way. When _φ_ ≠ 0, the [kernel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kernel_(algebra) "Kernel (algebra)")_F_ = Ker(_φ_) is a closed vector subspace of _H_, not equal to _H_, hence there exists a nonzero vector _v_ orthogonal to _F_. The vector _u_ is a suitable scalar multiple _λv_ of _v_. The requirement that _φ_(_v_) = ⟨_v_, _u_⟩ yields ![Image 139: {\displaystyle u=\langle v,v\rangle ^{-1}\,{\overline {\varphi (v)}}\,v\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0940b7ef7fe6dfc275ea7ab1961924b9158a047c)

This correspondence _φ_ ↔ _u_ is exploited by the [bra–ket notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bra%E2%80%93ket_notation "Bra–ket notation") popular in [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics").[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-78) It is common in physics to assume that the inner product, denoted by ⟨_x_|_y_⟩, is linear on the right, ![Image 140: {\displaystyle \langle x|y\rangle =\langle y,x\rangle \,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d436ad7973ee47f13d01890d26cc98612fae99c1) The result ⟨_x_|_y_⟩ can be seen as the action of the linear functional ⟨_x_| (the _bra_) on the vector |_y_⟩ (the _ket_).

The Riesz representation theorem relies fundamentally not just on the presence of an inner product, but also on the completeness of the space. In fact, the theorem implies that the [topological dual](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Banach_space "Banach space") of any inner product space can be identified with its completion.[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-79) An immediate consequence of the Riesz representation theorem is also that a Hilbert space _H_ is [reflexive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflexive_space "Reflexive space"), meaning that the natural map from _H_ into its [double dual space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_space "Dual space") is an isomorphism.

### Weakly convergent sequences

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hilbert_space&action=edit&section=25 "Edit section: Weakly convergent sequences")]

In a Hilbert space _H_, a sequence {_x n_} is [weakly convergent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weak_topology#Weak_convergence "Weak topology") to a vector _x_ ∈ _H_ when ![Image 141: {\displaystyle \lim _{n}\langle x_{n},v\rangle =\langle x,v\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fc10be6704806b8ad1d07b418c54115e93863c0c) for every _v_ ∈ _H_.

For example, any orthonormal sequence {_f n_} converges weakly to 0, as a consequence of [Bessel's inequality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#Bessel's_inequality). Every weakly convergent sequence {_x n_} is bounded, by the [uniform boundedness principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uniform_boundedness_principle "Uniform boundedness principle").

Conversely, every bounded sequence in a Hilbert space admits weakly convergent subsequences ([Alaoglu's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alaoglu%27s_theorem "Alaoglu's theorem")).[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-80) This fact may be used to prove minimization results for continuous [convex functionals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_function "Convex function"), in the same way that the [Bolzano–Weierstrass theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bolzano%E2%80%93Weierstrass_theorem "Bolzano–Weierstrass theorem") is used for continuous functions on **R**_d_. Among several variants, one simple statement is as follows:[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-81)

If _f_: _H_ → **R** is a convex continuous function such that _f_(_x_) tends to +∞ when ‖_x_‖ tends to ∞, then _f_ admits a minimum at some point _x_ 0 ∈ _H_.
This fact (and its various generalizations) are fundamental for [direct methods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direct_method_in_the_calculus_of_variations "Direct method in the calculus of variations") in the [calculus of variations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus_of_variations "Calculus of variations"). Minimization results for convex functionals are also a direct consequence of the slightly more abstract fact that closed bounded convex subsets in a Hilbert space _H_ are [weakly compact](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weak_topology "Weak topology"), since _H_ is reflexive. The existence of weakly convergent subsequences is a special case of the [Eberlein–Šmulian theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eberlein%E2%80%93%C5%A0mulian_theorem "Eberlein–Šmulian theorem").

### Banach space properties

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hilbert_space&action=edit&section=26 "Edit section: Banach space properties")]

Any general property of [Banach spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Banach_space "Banach space") continues to hold for Hilbert spaces. The [open mapping theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_mapping_theorem_(functional_analysis) "Open mapping theorem (functional analysis)") states that a [continuous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_function "Continuous function")[surjective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surjective "Surjective") linear transformation from one Banach space to another is an [open mapping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_mapping "Open mapping") meaning that it sends open sets to open sets. A corollary is the [bounded inverse theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bounded_inverse_theorem "Bounded inverse theorem"), that a continuous and [bijective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bijective "Bijective") linear function from one Banach space to another is an isomorphism (that is, a continuous linear map whose inverse is also continuous). This theorem is considerably simpler to prove in the case of Hilbert spaces than in general Banach spaces.[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-82) The open mapping theorem is equivalent to the [closed graph theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_graph_theorem "Closed graph theorem"), which asserts that a linear function from one Banach space to another is continuous if and only if its graph is a [closed set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_set "Closed set").[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-83) In the case of Hilbert spaces, this is basic in the study of [unbounded operators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unbounded_operator "Unbounded operator") (see _[Closed operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed\_operator "Closed operator")_).

The (geometrical) [Hahn–Banach theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hahn%E2%80%93Banach_theorem "Hahn–Banach theorem") asserts that a closed convex set can be separated from any point outside it by means of a [hyperplane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperplane "Hyperplane") of the Hilbert space. This is an immediate consequence of the [best approximation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#Best_approximation) property: if _y_ is the element of a closed convex set _F_ closest to _x_, then the separating hyperplane is the plane perpendicular to the segment _xy_ passing through its midpoint.[[82]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-84)

Operators on Hilbert spaces
---------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hilbert_space&action=edit&section=27 "Edit section: Operators on Hilbert spaces")]

The [continuous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_function_(topology) "Continuous function (topology)")[linear operators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_operator "Linear operator")_A_: _H_ 1 → _H_ 2 from a Hilbert space _H_ 1 to a second Hilbert space _H_ 2 are [_bounded_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bounded_linear_operator "Bounded linear operator") in the sense that they map [bounded sets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bounded_set "Bounded set") to bounded sets.[[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-85) Conversely, if an operator is bounded, then it is continuous. The space of such [bounded linear operators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bounded_linear_operator "Bounded linear operator") has a [norm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norm_(mathematics) "Norm (mathematics)"), the [operator norm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operator_norm "Operator norm") given by ![Image 142: {\displaystyle \lVert A\rVert =\sup {\bigl \{}\|Ax\|\mathrel {\big |} \|x\|\leq 1{\bigr \}}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cfa5bda3c3b0a00fe3f8551b4e4628c4d6288e89)

The sum and the composite of two bounded linear operators is again bounded and linear. For _y_ in _H_ 2, the map that sends _x_ ∈ _H_ 1 to ⟨_Ax_, _y_⟩ is linear and continuous, and according to the [Riesz representation theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riesz_representation_theorem "Riesz representation theorem") can therefore be represented in the form ![Image 143: {\displaystyle \left\langle x,A^{*}y\right\rangle =\langle Ax,y\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b29adafb3f47953e72bb01aad30ad286e8ce98eb) for some vector _A_*_y_ in _H_ 1. This defines another bounded linear operator _A_*: _H_ 2 → _H_ 1, the [adjoint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermitian_adjoint "Hermitian adjoint") of _A_. The adjoint satisfies _A_** = _A_. When the Riesz representation theorem is used to identify each Hilbert space with its continuous dual space, the adjoint of A can be shown to be [identical to](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riesz_representation_theorem#Adjoints_and_transposes "Riesz representation theorem") the [transpose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transpose_of_a_linear_map "Transpose of a linear map")t _A_: _H_ 2* → _H_ 1* of A, which by definition sends ![Image 144: {\displaystyle \psi \in H_{2}^{*}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b567856dbcc5edd986c8a0354ec55b70722ecb8e) to the functional ![Image 145: {\displaystyle \psi \circ A\in H_{1}^{*}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ebde178b2785096195c865497a755ef7b12841e9)

The set B(_H_) of all bounded linear operators on _H_ (meaning operators _H_ → _H_), together with the addition and composition operations, the norm and the adjoint operation, is a [C*-algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C*-algebra "C*-algebra"), which is a type of [operator algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operator_algebra "Operator algebra").

An element _A_ of B(_H_) is called 'self-adjoint' or 'Hermitian' if _A_* = _A_. If _A_ is Hermitian and ⟨_Ax_, _x_⟩ ≥ 0 for every _x_, then _A_ is called 'nonnegative', written _A_ ≥ 0; if equality holds only when _x_ = 0, then _A_ is called 'positive'. The set of self adjoint operators admits a [partial order](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_order "Partial order"), in which _A_ ≥ _B_ if _A_ − _B_ ≥ 0. If _A_ has the form _B_*_B_ for some _B_, then _A_ is nonnegative; if _B_ is invertible, then _A_ is positive. A converse is also true in the sense that, for a non-negative operator _A_, there exists a unique non-negative [square root](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_root_of_a_matrix "Square root of a matrix")_B_ such that ![Image 146: {\displaystyle A=B^{2}=B^{*}B\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e0ca56120d48281eac1949aca5c4a1d18d12d1a5)

In a sense made precise by the [spectral theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectral_theorem "Spectral theorem"), self-adjoint operators can usefully be thought of as operators that are "real". An element _A_ of B(_H_) is called _normal_ if _A_*_A_ = _AA_*. Normal operators decompose into the sum of a self-adjoint operator and an imaginary multiple of a self adjoint operator ![Image 147: {\displaystyle A={\frac {A+A^{*}}{2}}+i{\frac {A-A^{*}}{2i}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/15b7b43f751581832f492a76d21d90aceb87865d) that commute with each other. Normal operators can also usefully be thought of in terms of their real and imaginary parts.

An element _U_ of B(_H_) is called [unitary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unitary_operator "Unitary operator") if _U_ is invertible and its inverse is given by _U_*. This can also be expressed by requiring that _U_ be onto and ⟨_Ux_, _Uy_⟩ = ⟨_x_, _y_⟩ for all _x_, _y_ ∈ _H_. The unitary operators form a [group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_(mathematics) "Group (mathematics)") under composition, which is the [isometry group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isometry_group "Isometry group") of _H_.

An element of B(_H_) is [compact](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compact_operator "Compact operator") if it sends bounded sets to [relatively compact](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relatively_compact "Relatively compact") sets. Equivalently, a bounded operator _T_ is compact if, for any bounded sequence {_x k_}, the sequence {_Tx k_} has a convergent subsequence. Many [integral operators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral_operator "Integral operator") are compact, and in fact define a special class of operators known as [Hilbert–Schmidt operators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert%E2%80%93Schmidt_operator "Hilbert–Schmidt operator") that are especially important in the study of [integral equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral_equation "Integral equation"). [Fredholm operators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fredholm_operator "Fredholm operator") differ from a compact operator by a multiple of the identity, and are equivalently characterized as operators with a finite dimensional [kernel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kernel_(linear_operator) "Kernel (linear operator)") and [cokernel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cokernel "Cokernel"). The index of a Fredholm operator _T_ is defined by ![Image 148: {\displaystyle \operatorname {index} T=\dim \ker T-\dim \operatorname {coker} T\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/77927cb900b809d2100c945d0c0ffcdb9cbf187f)

The index is [homotopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homotopy "Homotopy") invariant, and plays a deep role in [differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry") via the [Atiyah–Singer index theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atiyah%E2%80%93Singer_index_theorem "Atiyah–Singer index theorem").

### Unbounded operators

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hilbert_space&action=edit&section=29 "Edit section: Unbounded operators")]

[Unbounded operators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unbounded_operator "Unbounded operator") are also tractable in Hilbert spaces, and have important applications to [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics").[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-86) An unbounded operator _T_ on a Hilbert space _H_ is defined as a linear operator whose domain _D_(_T_) is a linear subspace of _H_. Often the domain _D_(_T_) is a dense subspace of _H_, in which case _T_ is known as a [densely defined operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Densely_defined_operator "Densely defined operator").

The adjoint of a densely defined unbounded operator is defined in essentially the same manner as for bounded operators. [Self-adjoint unbounded operators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-adjoint_operator "Self-adjoint operator") play the role of the _observables_ in the mathematical formulation of quantum mechanics. Examples of self-adjoint unbounded operators on the Hilbert space _L_ 2(**R**) are:[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-87)

These correspond to the [momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum "Momentum") and [position](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Position_operator "Position operator") observables, respectively. Neither _A_ nor _B_ is defined on all of _H_, since in the case of _A_ the derivative need not exist, and in the case of _B_ the product function need not be square integrable. In both cases, the set of possible arguments form dense subspaces of _L_ 2(**R**).

Two Hilbert spaces _H_ 1 and _H_ 2 can be combined into another Hilbert space, called the [(orthogonal) direct sum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direct_sum_of_modules#Direct_sum_of_Hilbert_spaces "Direct sum of modules"),[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-88) and denoted ![Image 149: {\displaystyle H_{1}\oplus H_{2}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bfae1d22f024f459b7993cb0457aa8575b0292a6)

consisting of the set of all [ordered pairs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordered_pair "Ordered pair")(_x_ 1, _x_ 2) where _x_ _i_ ∈ _H_ _i_, _i_ = 1, 2, and inner product defined by ![Image 150: {\displaystyle {\bigl \langle }(x_{1},x_{2}),(y_{1},y_{2}){\bigr \rangle }_{H_{1}\oplus H_{2}}=\left\langle x_{1},y_{1}\right\rangle _{H_{1}}+\left\langle x_{2},y_{2}\right\rangle _{H_{2}}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/93bc21003f3808137fb4c3f5ada597ecc18dfafc)

More generally, if _H_ _i_ is a family of Hilbert spaces indexed by _i_ ∈ _I_, then the direct sum of the _H_ _i_, denoted ![Image 151: {\displaystyle \bigoplus _{i\in I}H_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/31f61cfad2e924b1c5a4cdfe63d5ec8d7a176ef7) consists of the set of all indexed families ![Image 152: {\displaystyle x=(x_{i}\in H_{i}\mid i\in I)\in \prod _{i\in I}H_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9a199f9cfb236ef57ac07da4f97bd2cfe26de7cf) in the [Cartesian product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_product "Cartesian product") of the _H_ _i_ such that ![Image 153: {\displaystyle \sum _{i\in I}\|x_{i}\|^{2}<\infty \,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a15cfa56f27c36c0d8091110b30eebfdad5c051f)

The inner product is defined by ![Image 154: {\displaystyle \langle x,y\rangle =\sum _{i\in I}\left\langle x_{i},y_{i}\right\rangle _{H_{i}}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e4aacc8f267ce5405bcd8d77550ae773254dc247)

Each of the _H_ _i_ is included as a closed subspace in the direct sum of all of the _H_ _i_. Moreover, the _H_ _i_ are pairwise orthogonal. Conversely, if there is a system of closed subspaces, _V_ _i_, _i_ ∈ _I_, in a Hilbert space _H_, that are pairwise orthogonal and whose union is dense in _H_, then _H_ is canonically isomorphic to the direct sum of _V i_. In this case, _H_ is called the internal direct sum of the _V i_. A direct sum (internal or external) is also equipped with a family of orthogonal projections _E i_ onto the _i_ th direct summand _H i_. These projections are bounded, self-adjoint, [idempotent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Idempotent "Idempotent") operators that satisfy the orthogonality condition ![Image 155: {\displaystyle E_{i}E_{j}=0,\quad i\neq j\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cc313041cf3e24642f4f53b752db11de86c99c99)

The [spectral theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectral_theorem "Spectral theorem") for [compact](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compact_operator "Compact operator") self-adjoint operators on a Hilbert space _H_ states that _H_ splits into an orthogonal direct sum of the eigenspaces of an operator, and also gives an explicit decomposition of the operator as a sum of projections onto the eigenspaces. The direct sum of Hilbert spaces also appears in quantum mechanics as the [Fock space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fock_space "Fock space") of a system containing a variable number of particles, where each Hilbert space in the direct sum corresponds to an additional [degree of freedom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degrees_of_freedom_(mechanics) "Degrees of freedom (mechanics)") for the quantum mechanical system. In [representation theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Representation_theory "Representation theory"), the [Peter–Weyl theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter%E2%80%93Weyl_theorem "Peter–Weyl theorem") guarantees that any [unitary representation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unitary_representation "Unitary representation") of a [compact group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compact_group "Compact group") on a Hilbert space splits as the direct sum of finite-dimensional representations.

An example is provided by the Hilbert space _L_ 2([0, 1]). The Hilbertian tensor product of two copies of _L_ 2([0, 1]) is isometrically and linearly isomorphic to the space _L_ 2([0, 1]2) of square-integrable functions on the square [0, 1]2. This isomorphism sends a simple tensor _f_ 1 ⊗ _f_ 2 to the function ![Image 158: {\displaystyle (s,t)\mapsto f_{1}(s)\,f_{2}(t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bba73ee31532e31ff40fa8a8bb36865c597bb23c) on the square.

The notion of an [orthonormal basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthonormal_basis "Orthonormal basis") from linear algebra generalizes over to the case of Hilbert spaces.[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-91) In a Hilbert space _H_, an orthonormal basis is a family {_e_ _k_}_k_ ∈ _B_ of elements of _H_ satisfying the conditions:

1.   _Orthogonality_: Every two different elements of _B_ are orthogonal: ⟨_e k_, _e j_⟩ = 0 for all _k_, _j_ ∈ _B_ with _k_ ≠ _j_.
2.   _Normalization_: Every element of the family has norm 1: ‖_e_ _k_‖ = 1 for all _k_ ∈ _B_.
3.   _Completeness_: The [linear span](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_span "Linear span") of the family _e_ _k_, _k_ ∈ _B_, is [dense](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dense_set "Dense set") in _H_.

A system of vectors satisfying the first two conditions basis is called an orthonormal system or an orthonormal set (or an orthonormal sequence if _B_ is [countable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Countable_set "Countable set")). Such a system is always [linearly independent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linearly_independent "Linearly independent").

Despite the name, an orthonormal basis is not, in general, a basis in the sense of linear algebra ([Hamel basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamel_basis "Hamel basis")). More precisely, an orthonormal basis is a Hamel basis if and only if the Hilbert space is a finite-dimensional vector space.[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-92)

Completeness of an orthonormal system of vectors of a Hilbert space can be equivalently restated as:

for every _v_ ∈ _H_, if ⟨_v_, _e_ _k_⟩ = 0 for all _k_ ∈ _B_, then _v_ = **0**.
This is related to the fact that the only vector orthogonal to a dense linear subspace is the zero vector, for if _S_ is any orthonormal set and _v_ is orthogonal to _S_, then _v_ is orthogonal to the closure of the linear span of _S_, which is the whole space.

Examples of orthonormal bases include:

*   the set {(1, 0, 0), (0, 1, 0), (0, 0, 1)} forms an orthonormal basis of **R**3 with the [dot product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dot_product "Dot product");
*   the sequence { _f_ _n_ | _n_ ∈ **Z**} with _f_ _n_(_x_) = [exp](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponential_function "Exponential function")(2π _inx_) forms an orthonormal basis of the complex space _L_ 2([0, 1]);

In the infinite-dimensional case, an orthonormal basis will not be a basis in the sense of [linear algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_algebra "Linear algebra"); to distinguish the two, the latter basis is also called a [Hamel basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamel_basis "Hamel basis"). That the span of the basis vectors is dense implies that every vector in the space can be written as the sum of an infinite series, and the orthogonality implies that this decomposition is unique.

The space ![Image 161: {\displaystyle \ell _{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/85a4571ee9be10bd3c9df2480ab3d280f99e801a) of square-summable sequences of complex numbers is the set of infinite sequences[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-Stein_2005-10)![Image 162: {\displaystyle (c_{1},c_{2},c_{3},\dots )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/768f67354e8a3d14402b25c6570e502c4ae57d3a) of real or complex numbers such that ![Image 163: {\displaystyle \left|c_{1}\right|^{2}+\left|c_{2}\right|^{2}+\left|c_{3}\right|^{2}+\cdots <\infty \,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f34d0333aabdeb5ecdd32dff6435c0b21649646b)

This space has an orthonormal basis: ![Image 164: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}e_{1}&=(1,0,0,\dots )\\e_{2}&=(0,1,0,\dots )\\&\ \ \vdots \end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/651d004b8d244263bc4b40ee99daeeaea3c47b83)

This space is the infinite-dimensional generalization of the ![Image 165: {\displaystyle \ell _{2}^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4ac3233a9c36d0e75e9bfc58aab52554a80bbafd) space of finite-dimensional vectors. It is usually the first example used to show that in infinite-dimensional spaces, a set that is [closed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_set "Closed set") and [bounded](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bounded_set "Bounded set") is not necessarily [(sequentially) compact](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sequentially_compact_space "Sequentially compact space") (as is the case in all _finite_ dimensional spaces). Indeed, the set of orthonormal vectors above shows this: It is an infinite sequence of vectors in the unit ball (i.e., the ball of points with norm less than or equal one). This set is clearly bounded and closed; yet, no subsequence of these vectors converges to anything and consequently the unit ball in ![Image 166: {\displaystyle \ell _{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/85a4571ee9be10bd3c9df2480ab3d280f99e801a) is not compact. Intuitively, this is because "there is always another coordinate direction" into which the next elements of the sequence can evade.

One can generalize the space ![Image 167: {\displaystyle \ell _{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/85a4571ee9be10bd3c9df2480ab3d280f99e801a) in many ways. For example, if _B_ is any set, then one can form a Hilbert space of sequences with index set _B_, defined by[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-93)![Image 168: {\displaystyle \ell ^{2}(B)={\biggl \{}x:B\xrightarrow {x} \mathbb {C} \mathrel {\bigg |} \sum _{b\in B}\left|x(b)\right|^{2}<\infty {\biggr \}}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2d626401d25c62bd23b60f8c6947ed391898efbd)

The summation over _B_ is here defined by ![Image 169: {\displaystyle \sum _{b\in B}\left|x(b)\right|^{2}=\sup \sum _{n=1}^{N}\left|x(b_{n})\right|^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/923ebbaa57863084429d6ae2e1a09b598d76cc9c) the [supremum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supremum "Supremum") being taken over all finite subsets of _B_. It follows that, for this sum to be finite, every element of _l_ 2(_B_) has only countably many nonzero terms. This space becomes a Hilbert space with the inner product ![Image 170: {\displaystyle \langle x,y\rangle =\sum _{b\in B}x(b){\overline {y(b)}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ceafc84777c484d7336b63ae19a8373e061443ed)

for all _x_, _y_ ∈ _l_ 2(_B_). Here the sum also has only countably many nonzero terms, and is unconditionally convergent by the Cauchy–Schwarz inequality.

An orthonormal basis of _l_ 2(_B_) is indexed by the set _B_, given by

![Image 171: {\displaystyle e_{b}(b')={\begin{cases}1&{\text{if }}b=b'\\0&{\text{otherwise.}}\end{cases}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0b89bb9d648728d42a2333751eac8a71700c31d8)

### Bessel's inequality and Parseval's formula

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hilbert_space&action=edit&section=35 "Edit section: Bessel's inequality and Parseval's formula")]

Let _f_ 1, ..., _f_ _n_ be a finite orthonormal system in _H_. For an arbitrary vector _x_ ∈ _H_, let ![Image 172: {\displaystyle y=\sum _{j=1}^{n}\langle x,f_{j}\rangle \,f_{j}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1cae76e679dee41086182349e23adc6c0c983d60)

Then ⟨_x_, _f_ _k_⟩ = ⟨_y_, _f_ _k_⟩ for every _k_ = 1, ..., _n_. It follows that _x_ − _y_ is orthogonal to each _f_ _k_, hence _x_ − _y_ is orthogonal to _y_. Using the Pythagorean identity twice, it follows that ![Image 173: {\displaystyle \|x\|^{2}=\|x-y\|^{2}+\|y\|^{2}\geq \|y\|^{2}=\sum _{j=1}^{n}{\bigl |}\langle x,f_{j}\rangle {\bigr |}^{2}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b6914b2f27fcd799379d1324efd6d26a92796afc)

Let {_f_ _i_}, _i_ ∈ _I_, be an arbitrary orthonormal system in _H_. Applying the preceding inequality to every finite subset _J_ of _I_ gives Bessel's inequality:[[92]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-94)![Image 174: {\displaystyle \sum _{i\in I}{\bigl |}\langle x,f_{i}\rangle {\bigr |}^{2}\leq \|x\|^{2},\quad x\in H}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/04cd7cbd400cf024363716e694f90eabfe30a8d9) (according to the definition of the [sum of an arbitrary family](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Series_(mathematics)#Summations_over_general_index_sets "Series (mathematics)") of non-negative real numbers).

Geometrically, Bessel's inequality implies that the orthogonal projection of _x_ onto the linear subspace spanned by the _f i_ has norm that does not exceed that of _x_. In two dimensions, this is the assertion that the length of the leg of a right triangle may not exceed the length of the hypotenuse.

Bessel's inequality is a stepping stone to the stronger result called [Parseval's identity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parseval_identity "Parseval identity"), which governs the case when Bessel's inequality is actually an equality. By definition, if {_e_ _k_}_k_ ∈ _B_ is an orthonormal basis of _H_, then every element _x_ of _H_ may be written as ![Image 175: {\displaystyle x=\sum _{k\in B}\left\langle x,e_{k}\right\rangle \,e_{k}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2998c111fdc5c46c1258e3ec87214b6b8961caef)

Even if _B_ is uncountable, Bessel's inequality guarantees that the expression is well-defined and consists only of countably many nonzero terms. This sum is called the Fourier expansion of _x_, and the individual coefficients ⟨_x_, _e_ _k_⟩ are the Fourier coefficients of _x_. Parseval's identity then asserts that[[93]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-Hewitt_1965-95)![Image 176: {\displaystyle \|x\|^{2}=\sum _{k\in B}|\langle x,e_{k}\rangle |^{2}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e8f86931997645328aefd2451525b8a4b3faf6d8)

Conversely,[[93]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-Hewitt_1965-95) if {_e_ _k_} is an orthonormal set such that Parseval's identity holds for every _x_, then {_e_ _k_} is an orthonormal basis.

As a consequence of [Zorn's lemma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zorn%27s_lemma "Zorn's lemma"), _every_ Hilbert space admits an orthonormal basis; furthermore, any two orthonormal bases of the same space have the same [cardinality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardinal_number "Cardinal number"), called the Hilbert dimension of the space.[[94]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-96) For instance, since _l_ 2(_B_) has an orthonormal basis indexed by _B_, its Hilbert dimension is the cardinality of _B_ (which may be a finite integer, or a countable or uncountable [cardinal number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardinal_number "Cardinal number")).

The Hilbert dimension is not greater than the [Hamel dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamel_dimension "Hamel dimension") (the usual dimension of a vector space).

As a consequence of Parseval's identity,[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-97) if {_e_ _k_}_k_ ∈ _B_ is an orthonormal basis of _H_, then the map Φ: _H_ → _l_ 2(_B_) defined by Φ(_x_) = ⟨x, _e_ _k_⟩_k_∈_B_ is an isometric isomorphism of Hilbert spaces: it is a bijective linear mapping such that ![Image 177: {\displaystyle {\bigl \langle }\Phi (x),\Phi (y){\bigr \rangle }_{l^{2}(B)}=\left\langle x,y\right\rangle _{H}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e124d3427359d497ec8ec6795f810e9692257ab9) for all _x_, _y_ ∈ _H_. The [cardinal number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardinal_number "Cardinal number") of _B_ is the Hilbert dimension of _H_. Thus every Hilbert space is isometrically isomorphic to a sequence space _l_ 2(_B_) for some set _B_.

By definition, a Hilbert space is [separable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separable_space "Separable space") provided it contains a dense countable subset. Along with Zorn's lemma, this means a Hilbert space is separable if and only if it admits a [countable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Countable "Countable") orthonormal basis. All infinite-dimensional separable Hilbert spaces are therefore isometrically isomorphic to the [square-summable sequence space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sequence_space#%E2%84%93p_spaces "Sequence space")![Image 178: {\displaystyle \ell ^{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/11e5f61f7f39a2ef38b3466db6274c425e782404)

In the past, Hilbert spaces were often required to be separable as part of the definition.[[96]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-98)

#### In quantum field theory

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hilbert_space&action=edit&section=38 "Edit section: In quantum field theory")]

Most spaces used in physics are separable, and since these are all isomorphic to each other, one often refers to any infinite-dimensional separable Hilbert space as "_the_ Hilbert space" or just "Hilbert space".[[97]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-99) Even in [quantum field theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_field_theory "Quantum field theory"), most of the Hilbert spaces are in fact separable, as stipulated by the [Wightman axioms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wightman_axioms "Wightman axioms"). However, it is sometimes argued that non-separable Hilbert spaces are also important in quantum field theory, roughly because the systems in the theory possess an infinite number of [degrees of freedom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degrees_of_freedom_(mechanics) "Degrees of freedom (mechanics)") and any infinite [Hilbert tensor product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_product_of_Hilbert_spaces "Tensor product of Hilbert spaces") (of spaces of dimension greater than one) is non-separable.[[98]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-Streater-100) For instance, a [bosonic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bosonic_field "Bosonic field") can be naturally thought of as an element of a tensor product whose factors represent harmonic oscillators at each point of space. From this perspective, the natural state space of a boson might seem to be a non-separable space.[[98]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-Streater-100) However, it is only a small separable subspace of the full tensor product that can contain physically meaningful fields (on which the observables can be defined). Another non-separable Hilbert space models the state of an infinite collection of particles in an unbounded region of space. An orthonormal basis of the space is indexed by the density of the particles, a continuous parameter, and since the set of possible densities is uncountable, the basis is not countable.[[98]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-Streater-100)

Orthogonal complements and projections
--------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hilbert_space&action=edit&section=39 "Edit section: Orthogonal complements and projections")]

If _S_ is a subset of a Hilbert space _H_, the set of vectors orthogonal to _S_ is defined by ![Image 179: {\displaystyle S^{\perp }=\left\{x\in H\mid \langle x,s\rangle =0\ {\text{ for all }}s\in S\right\}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b50d39d82dd2b8976b675f2799be00209c978c0c)

The set _S_⊥ is a [closed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_set "Closed set") subspace of _H_ (can be proved easily using the linearity and continuity of the inner product) and so forms itself a Hilbert space. If _V_ is a closed subspace of _H_, then _V_⊥ is called the _[orthogonal complement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal\_complement "Orthogonal complement")_ of _V_. In fact, every _x_ ∈ _H_ can then be written uniquely as _x_ = _v_ + _w_, with _v_ ∈ _V_ and _w_ ∈ _V_⊥. Therefore, _H_ is the internal Hilbert direct sum of _V_ and _V_⊥.

The linear operator _P V_: _H_ → _H_ that maps _x_ to _v_ is called the _[orthogonal projection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal\_projection "Orthogonal projection")_ onto _V_. There is a [natural](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_transformation "Natural transformation") one-to-one correspondence between the set of all closed subspaces of _H_ and the set of all bounded self-adjoint operators _P_ such that _P_ 2 = _P_. Specifically,

**Theorem**—The orthogonal projection _P V_ is a self-adjoint linear operator on _H_ of norm ≤1 with the property _P_ 2

_V_ = _P V_. Moreover, any self-adjoint linear operator _E_ such that _E_ 2 = _E_ is of the form _P V_, where _V_ is the range of _E_. For every _x_ in _H_, _P V_(_x_) is the unique element _v_ of _V_ that [minimizes the distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_projection_theorem "Hilbert projection theorem")‖_x_ − _v_‖.

This provides the geometrical interpretation of _P V_(_x_): it is the best approximation to _x_ by elements of _V_.[[99]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-101)

Projections _P U_ and _P V_ are called mutually orthogonal if _P_ _U_ _P_ _V_ = 0. This is equivalent to _U_ and _V_ being orthogonal as subspaces of _H_. The sum of the two projections _P_ _U_ and _P_ _V_ is a projection only if _U_ and _V_ are orthogonal to each other, and in that case _P_ _U_ + _P_ _V_ = _P_ _U_+_V_.[[100]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-102) The composite _P_ _U_ _P_ _V_ is generally not a projection; in fact, the composite is a projection if and only if the two projections commute, and in that case _P_ _U_ _P_ _V_ = _P_ _U_∩_V_.[[101]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-103)

By restricting the codomain to the Hilbert space _V_, the orthogonal projection _P_ _V_ gives rise to a projection mapping _π_: _H_ → _V_; it is the adjoint of the [inclusion mapping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inclusion_mapping "Inclusion mapping")![Image 180: {\displaystyle i:V\to H\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eff8dd80c9bda997967981b74b00c080d6d9cb81) meaning that ![Image 181: {\displaystyle \left\langle ix,y\right\rangle _{H}=\left\langle x,\pi y\right\rangle _{V}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7dedd63bfa32beb3519ab13b1feffd305e84c4b9) for all _x_ ∈ _V_ and _y_ ∈ _H_.

The operator norm of the orthogonal projection _P_ _V_ onto a nonzero closed subspace _V_ is equal to 1: ![Image 182: {\displaystyle \|P_{V}\|=\sup _{x\in H,x\neq 0}{\frac {\|P_{V}x\|}{\|x\|}}=1\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6a34fe5aa961ed70c750598d753f451aae687d3b)

Every closed subspace _V_ of a Hilbert space is therefore the image of an operator _P_ of norm one such that _P_ 2 = _P_. The property of possessing appropriate projection operators characterizes Hilbert spaces:[[102]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-104)

*   A Banach space of dimension higher than 2 is (isometrically) a Hilbert space if and only if, for every closed subspace _V_, there is an operator _P_ _V_ of norm one whose image is _V_ such that _P_ 2

_V_ = _P_ _V_.

While this result characterizes the metric structure of a Hilbert space, the structure of a Hilbert space as a [topological vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_vector_space "Topological vector space") can itself be characterized in terms of the presence of complementary subspaces:[[103]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-105)

*   A Banach space _X_ is topologically and linearly isomorphic to a Hilbert space if and only if, to every closed subspace _V_, there is a closed subspace _W_ such that _X_ is equal to the internal direct sum _V_ ⊕ _W_.

The orthogonal complement satisfies some more elementary results. It is a [monotone function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monotone_function "Monotone function") in the sense that if _U_ ⊂ _V_, then _V_⊥ ⊆ _U_⊥ with equality holding if and only if _V_ is contained in the [closure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closure_(topology) "Closure (topology)") of _U_. This result is a special case of the [Hahn–Banach theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hahn%E2%80%93Banach_theorem "Hahn–Banach theorem"). The closure of a subspace can be completely characterized in terms of the orthogonal complement: if _V_ is a subspace of _H_, then the closure of _V_ is equal to _V_⊥⊥. The orthogonal complement is thus a [Galois connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galois_connection "Galois connection") on the [partial order](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_order "Partial order") of subspaces of a Hilbert space. In general, the orthogonal complement of a sum of subspaces is the intersection of the orthogonal complements:[[104]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-106)![Image 183: {\displaystyle {\biggl (}\sum _{i}V_{i}{\biggr )}^{\perp }=\bigcap _{i}V_{i}^{\perp }\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a9630ac35d7d98746b5461804660f51e13861fcf)

If the _V_ _i_ are in addition closed, then ![Image 184: {\displaystyle {\overline {\sum _{i}V_{i}^{\perp }{\vphantom {\Big |}}}}={\biggl (}\bigcap _{i}V_{i}{\biggr )}^{\perp }\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/75c7dc911875bb0fc30d91e4ee1c62f8ae5867f6)

There is a well-developed [spectral theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectral_theory "Spectral theory") for self-adjoint operators in a Hilbert space, that is roughly analogous to the study of [symmetric matrices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetric_matrix "Symmetric matrix") over the reals or self-adjoint matrices over the complex numbers.[[105]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-107) In the same sense, one can obtain a "diagonalization" of a self-adjoint operator as a suitable sum (actually an integral) of orthogonal projection operators.

The [spectrum of an operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectrum_of_an_operator "Spectrum of an operator")_T_, denoted _σ_(_T_), is the set of complex numbers _λ_ such that _T_ − _λ_ lacks a continuous inverse. If _T_ is bounded, then the spectrum is always a [compact set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compact_set "Compact set") in the complex plane, and lies inside the disc |_z_| ≤ ‖_T_‖. If _T_ is self-adjoint, then the spectrum is real. In fact, it is contained in the interval [_m_, _M_] where ![Image 185: {\displaystyle m=\inf _{\|x\|=1}\langle Tx,x\rangle \,,\quad M=\sup _{\|x\|=1}\langle Tx,x\rangle \,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ccc60676b6b2cc94cd85e7447575d20dc96d68cc)

Moreover, _m_ and _M_ are both actually contained within the spectrum.

The eigenspaces of an operator _T_ are given by ![Image 186: {\displaystyle H_{\lambda }=\ker(T-\lambda )\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2822ca54eb778655b2c52f2efa28394566abae91)

Unlike with finite matrices, not every element of the spectrum of _T_ must be an eigenvalue: the linear operator _T_ − _λ_ may only lack an inverse because it is not surjective. Elements of the spectrum of an operator in the general sense are known as _spectral values_. Since spectral values need not be eigenvalues, the spectral decomposition is often more subtle than in finite dimensions.

However, the [spectral theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectral_theorem "Spectral theorem") of a self-adjoint operator _T_ takes a particularly simple form if, in addition, _T_ is assumed to be a [compact operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compact_operator "Compact operator"). The [spectral theorem for compact self-adjoint operators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compact_operator_on_Hilbert_space#Spectral_theorem "Compact operator on Hilbert space") states:[[106]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-108)

This theorem plays a fundamental role in the theory of [integral equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral_equation "Integral equation"), as many integral operators are compact, in particular those that arise from [Hilbert–Schmidt operators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert%E2%80%93Schmidt_operator "Hilbert–Schmidt operator").

The general spectral theorem for self-adjoint operators involves a kind of operator-valued [Riemann–Stieltjes integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann%E2%80%93Stieltjes_integral "Riemann–Stieltjes integral"), rather than an infinite summation.[[107]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-109) The _spectral family_ associated to _T_ associates to each real number λ an operator _E λ_, which is the projection onto the nullspace of the operator (_T_ − _λ_)+, where the positive part of a self-adjoint operator is defined by ![Image 187: {\displaystyle A^{+}={\tfrac {1}{2}}{\Bigl (}{\sqrt {A^{2}}}+A{\Bigr )}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2a99be7cf627f786503cca22294360ada0038c0e)

The operators _E λ_ are monotone increasing relative to the partial order defined on self-adjoint operators; the eigenvalues correspond precisely to the jump discontinuities. One has the spectral theorem, which asserts ![Image 188: {\displaystyle T=\int _{\mathbb {R} }\lambda \,\mathrm {d} E_{\lambda }\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e6337933ee817e34327fbb88c0f4fb5094f5794a)

The integral is understood as a Riemann–Stieltjes integral, convergent with respect to the norm on B(_H_). In particular, one has the ordinary scalar-valued integral representation ![Image 189: {\displaystyle \langle Tx,y\rangle =\int _{\mathbb {R} }\lambda \,\mathrm {d} \langle E_{\lambda }x,y\rangle \,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/266e2f6e28456de2af73cedddfd75bdc5f30c18a)

A somewhat similar spectral decomposition holds for normal operators, although because the spectrum may now contain non-real complex numbers, the operator-valued Stieltjes measure d _E λ_ must instead be replaced by a [resolution of the identity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resolution_of_the_identity "Resolution of the identity").

A major application of spectral methods is the [spectral mapping theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectral_mapping_theorem "Spectral mapping theorem"), which allows one to apply to a self-adjoint operator _T_ any continuous complex function _f_ defined on the spectrum of _T_ by forming the integral ![Image 190: {\displaystyle f(T)=\int _{\sigma (T)}f(\lambda )\,\mathrm {d} E_{\lambda }\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/36bf8ffa0897168c6173ebf83aafd9d1d7735435)

The resulting [continuous functional calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_functional_calculus "Continuous functional calculus") has applications in particular to [pseudodifferential operators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudodifferential_operators "Pseudodifferential operators").[[108]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-110)

The spectral theory of _unbounded_ self-adjoint operators is only marginally more difficult than for bounded operators. The spectrum of an unbounded operator is defined in precisely the same way as for bounded operators: _λ_ is a spectral value if the [resolvent operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resolvent_operator "Resolvent operator")![Image 191: {\displaystyle R_{\lambda }=(T-\lambda )^{-1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8ab87e56c41c0896676a628685b26e77c4dcebe)

fails to be a well-defined continuous operator. The self-adjointness of _T_ still guarantees that the spectrum is real. Thus the essential idea of working with unbounded operators is to look instead at the resolvent _R λ_ where _λ_ is nonreal. This is a _bounded_ normal operator, which admits a spectral representation that can then be transferred to a spectral representation of _T_ itself. A similar strategy is used, for instance, to study the spectrum of the Laplace operator: rather than address the operator directly, one instead looks as an associated resolvent such as a [Riesz potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riesz_potential "Riesz potential") or [Bessel potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bessel_potential "Bessel potential").

A precise version of the spectral theorem in this case is:[[109]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#cite_note-111)

**Theorem**—Given a densely defined self-adjoint operator _T_ on a Hilbert space _H_, there corresponds a unique [resolution of the identity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resolution_of_the_identity "Resolution of the identity")_E_ on the Borel sets of **R**, such that ![Image 192: {\displaystyle \langle Tx,y\rangle =\int _{\mathbb {R} }\lambda \,\mathrm {d} E_{x,y}(\lambda )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/43acc7d3fc1e95faa2b9d5b000516abdaa85f177) for all _x_ ∈ _D_(_T_) and _y_ ∈ _H_. The spectral measure _E_ is concentrated on the spectrum of _T_.

There is also a version of the spectral theorem that applies to unbounded normal operators.

*   [Rigged Hilbert space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rigged_Hilbert_space "Rigged Hilbert space")– Construction linking the study of "bound" and continuous eigenvalues in functional analysis
*   [Topological vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_vector_space "Topological vector space")– Vector space with a notion of nearness
