The altitude from A (dashed line segment) intersects the extended base at D (a point outside the triangle).

In [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry"), an **altitude** of a [triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle "Triangle") is a [line segment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_segment "Line segment") through a given [vertex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_(geometry) "Vertex (geometry)") (called _[apex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apex\_(geometry) "Apex (geometry)")_) and [perpendicular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular "Perpendicular") to a [line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_(geometry) "Line (geometry)") containing the side or [edge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edge_(geometry) "Edge (geometry)") opposite the apex. This (finite) edge and (infinite) line extension are called, respectively, the _[base](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Base\_(geometry) "Base (geometry)")_ and _[extended base](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extended\_side "Extended side")_ of the altitude. The [point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_(geometry) "Point (geometry)") at the intersection of the extended base and the altitude is called the _**foot**_ of the altitude. The length of the altitude, often simply called "the altitude" or "height", symbol h, is the distance between the foot and the apex. The process of drawing the altitude from a vertex to the foot is known as _dropping the altitude_ at that vertex. It is a special case of [orthogonal projection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_projection "Orthogonal projection").

Altitudes can be used in the computation of the [area of a triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area_of_a_triangle "Area of a triangle"): one-half of the product of an altitude's length and its base's length (symbol b) equals the triangle's area: A=h b/2. Thus, the longest altitude is perpendicular to the shortest side of the triangle. The altitudes are also related to the sides of the triangle through the [trigonometric functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trigonometric_functions "Trigonometric functions").

In an [isosceles triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isosceles_triangle "Isosceles triangle") (a triangle with two [congruent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congruence_(geometry) "Congruence (geometry)") sides), the altitude having the incongruent side as its base will have the [midpoint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Midpoint "Midpoint") of that side as its foot. Also the altitude having the incongruent side as its base will be the [angle bisector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle_bisector "Angle bisector") of the vertex angle.

In a [right triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_triangle "Right triangle"), the altitude drawn to the [hypotenuse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypotenuse "Hypotenuse")c divides the hypotenuse into two segments of lengths p and q. If we denote the length of the altitude by h c, we then have the relation

![Image 2: {\displaystyle h_{c}={\sqrt {pq}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c01553f63cacef2e55c7249e51a6a68edc82fac9) ([geometric mean theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_mean_theorem "Geometric mean theorem"); see [special cases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Altitude_(triangle)#Right_triangle), [inverse Pythagorean theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverse_Pythagorean_theorem "Inverse Pythagorean theorem"))[![Image 3](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Rtriangle.svg/250px-Rtriangle.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Rtriangle.svg)

In a right triangle, the altitude from each acute angle coincides with a leg and intersects the opposite side at (has its foot at) the right-angled vertex, which is the orthocenter.

For acute triangles, the feet of the altitudes all fall on the triangle's sides (not extended). In an obtuse triangle (one with an [obtuse angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle "Obtuse angle")), the foot of the altitude to the obtuse-angled vertex falls in the interior of the opposite side, but the feet of the altitudes to the acute-angled vertices fall on the opposite [extended side](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extended_side "Extended side"), exterior to the triangle. This is illustrated in the adjacent diagram: in this obtuse triangle, an altitude dropped perpendicularly from the top vertex, which has an acute angle, intersects the extended horizontal side outside the triangle.

The geometric altitude figures prominently in many important theorems and their proofs. For example, besides those theorems listed below, the altitude plays a central role in proofs of both the [Law of sines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law_of_sines#Proof "Law of sines") and [Law of cosines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law_of_cosines#Using_the_Pythagorean_theorem "Law of cosines").

### Altitude in terms of the sides

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Altitude_(triangle)&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Altitude in terms of the sides")]

For any triangle with sides a, b, c and [semiperimeter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semiperimeter "Semiperimeter")![Image 4: {\displaystyle s={\tfrac {1}{2}}(a+b+c),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f21e731a2e138840d60863a820d6a5cc4e3b18d9) the altitude from side a (the base) is given by

![Image 5: {\displaystyle h_{a}={\frac {2{\sqrt {s(s-a)(s-b)(s-c)}}}{a}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/76991db8cb127b50175bcd1228093fb9526a0075)
This follows from combining [Heron's formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heron%27s_formula "Heron's formula") for the area of a triangle in terms of the sides with the area formula ![Image 6: {\displaystyle {\tfrac {1}{2}}\times {\text{base}}\times {\text{height}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7d26c84bda1f25d87d5a00d454a8708eac57ce1d) where the base is taken as side a and the height is the altitude from the vertex A (opposite side a).

By exchanging a with b or c, this equation can also used to find the altitudes h b and h c, respectively.

Any two altitudes of a triangle are inversely proportional with the sides on which they fall.

Consider an arbitrary triangle with sides a, b, c and with corresponding altitudes h a, h b, h c. #### Equilateral triangle

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Altitude_(triangle)&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Equilateral triangle")]

From any point P within an [equilateral triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equilateral_triangle "Equilateral triangle"), the sum of the perpendiculars to the three sides is equal to the altitude of the triangle. This is [Viviani's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viviani%27s_theorem "Viviani's theorem").

[![Image 16](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Right_angle_altitude.svg/250px-Right_angle_altitude.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Right_angle_altitude.svg)

The altitude of a right triangle from its right angle to its hypotenuse is the geometric mean of the lengths of the segments the hypotenuse is split into. Using [Pythagoras' theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras%27_theorem "Pythagoras' theorem") on the 3 triangles of sides (_p_ + _q_, _r_, _s_ ), (_r_, _p_, _h_ ) and (_s_, _h_, _q_ ),

![Image 17: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}(p+q)^{2}\;\;&=\quad r^{2}\;\;\,+\quad s^{2}\\p^{2}\!\!+\!2pq\!+\!q^{2}&=\overbrace {p^{2}\!\!+\!h^{2}} +\overbrace {h^{2}\!\!+\!q^{2}} \\2pq\quad \;\;\;&=2h^{2}\;\therefore h\!=\!{\sqrt {pq}}\\\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2d87f5b679df7d7f6ff6273b75534348653e00a5)
