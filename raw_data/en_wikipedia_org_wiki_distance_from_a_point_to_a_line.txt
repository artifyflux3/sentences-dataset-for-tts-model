From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

The **distance** (or **perpendicular distance**) **from a point to a line** is the shortest [distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_distance "Euclidean distance") from a fixed [point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_(geometry) "Point (geometry)") to any point on a fixed infinite [line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_(mathematics) "Line (mathematics)") in [Euclidean geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_geometry "Euclidean geometry"). It is the length of the [line segment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_segment "Line segment") which joins the point to the line and is [perpendicular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular "Perpendicular") to the line. The formula for calculating it can be derived and expressed in several ways.

Knowing the shortest distance from a point to a line can be useful in various situations—for example, finding the shortest distance to reach a road, quantifying the scatter on a graph, etc. In [Deming regression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deming_regression "Deming regression"), a type of linear curve fitting, if the dependent and independent variables have equal variance this results in [orthogonal regression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_regression "Orthogonal regression") in which the degree of imperfection of the fit is measured for each data point as the perpendicular distance of the point from the regression line.

Cartesian coordinates
---------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Distance_from_a_point_to_a_line&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Cartesian coordinates")]

### Line defined by an equation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Distance_from_a_point_to_a_line&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Line defined by an equation")]

In the case of a line in the plane given by the equation _ax_ + _by_ + _c_ = 0, where _a_, _b_ and _c_ are [real](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number") constants with _a_ and _b_ not both zero, the distance from the line to a point (_x_ 0,_y_ 0) is[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distance_from_a_point_to_a_line#cite_note-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distance_from_a_point_to_a_line#cite_note-2): p.14

![Image 1: {\displaystyle \operatorname {distance} (ax+by+c=0,(x_{0},y_{0}))={\frac {|ax_{0}+by_{0}+c|}{\sqrt {a^{2}+b^{2}}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/baff35b2a747a59d5a113dcabea8fda7d2c09288)
The point on this line which is closest to (_x_ 0,_y_ 0) has coordinates:[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distance_from_a_point_to_a_line#cite_note-3)

![Image 2: {\displaystyle x={\frac {b(bx_{0}-ay_{0})-ac}{a^{2}+b^{2}}}{\text{ and }}y={\frac {a(-bx_{0}+ay_{0})-bc}{a^{2}+b^{2}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e272ef09915cac216abc89acba3d0eedea77cf30)
**Horizontal and vertical lines**

In the general equation of a line, _ax_ + _by_ + _c_ = 0, _a_ and _b_ cannot both be zero unless _c_ is also zero, in which case the equation does not define a line. If _a_=0 and _b_≠0, the line is horizontal and has equation _y_ = −_c_/_b_. The distance from (_x_ 0, _y_ 0) to this line is measured along a vertical line segment of length |_y_ 0 − (−_c_/_b_)| = |_by_ 0 + _c_| / |_b_| in accordance with the formula. Similarly, for vertical lines (_b_ = 0) the distance between the same point and the line is |_ax_ 0 + _c_| / |_a_|, as measured along a horizontal line segment.

### Line defined by two points

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Distance_from_a_point_to_a_line&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Line defined by two points")]

If the line passes through two points _P_ 1=(_x_ 1,_y_ 1) and _P_ 2=(_x 2_,_y 2_) then the distance of (x 0,y 0) from the line is:

![Image 3: {\displaystyle \operatorname {distance} (P_{1},P_{2},(x_{0},y_{0}))={\frac {|(y_{2}-y_{1})x_{0}-(x_{2}-x_{1})y_{0}+x_{2}y_{1}-y_{2}x_{1}|}{\sqrt {(y_{2}-y_{1})^{2}+(x_{2}-x_{1})^{2}}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/be2ab4a9d9d77f1623a2723891f652028a7a328d)
The denominator of this expression is the distance between _P 1_ and _P 2_. The numerator is twice the area of the triangle with its vertices at the three points, (x 0,y 0), _P 1_ and _P 2_. See: [Area of a triangle §Using coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area_of_a_triangle#Using_coordinates "Area of a triangle"). The expression is equivalent to ![Image 4: {\textstyle h={\frac {2A}{b}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a33ac19f52b384e0c61c9562818531fedd0d9651), which can be obtained by rearranging the standard formula for the area of a triangle: ![Image 5: {\textstyle A={\frac {1}{2}}bh}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/762e972d279a0effe59c7277371a3216408b58c8), where _b_ is the length of a side, and _h_ is the perpendicular height from the opposite vertex.

Line defined by point and angle
-------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Distance_from_a_point_to_a_line&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Line defined by point and angle")]

If the line passes through the point _P_ = (_P_ x, _P_ y) with angle _θ_, then the distance of some point (_x_ 0, _y_ 0) to the line is

![Image 6: {\displaystyle \operatorname {distance} (P,\theta ,(x_{0},y_{0}))=|\cos(\theta )(P_{y}-y_{0})-\sin(\theta )(P_{x}-x_{0})|}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a3af67d8d0f8facbf113306570ecc175284abf1e)
This proof is valid only if the line is neither vertical nor horizontal, that is, we assume that neither _a_ nor _b_ in the equation of the line is zero.

The line with equation _ax_ + _by_ + _c_ = 0 has slope −_a_/_b_, so any line perpendicular to it will have slope _b_/_a_ (the negative reciprocal). Let (_m_, _n_) be the point of intersection of the line _ax_ + _by_ + _c_ = 0 and the line perpendicular to it which passes through the point (_x_ 0, _y_ 0). The line through these two points is perpendicular to the original line, so

![Image 7: {\displaystyle {\frac {y_{0}-n}{x_{0}-m}}={\frac {b}{a}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3db0f24914ca21ebdc4c967c574873c826f05e07)
Thus, ![Image 8: {\displaystyle a(y_{0}-n)-b(x_{0}-m)=0,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/161f8a0292e659f8244ffc56987a9a8f44497a1b) and by squaring this equation we obtain:

![Image 9: {\displaystyle a^{2}(y_{0}-n)^{2}+b^{2}(x_{0}-m)^{2}=2ab(y_{0}-n)(x_{0}-m).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4a829a16b0a73ac5b0802c7a5c291e1536beafca)
Now consider,

![Image 10: {\displaystyle (a(x_{0}-m)+b(y_{0}-n))^{2}=a^{2}(x_{0}-m)^{2}+2ab(y_{0}-n)(x_{0}-m)+b^{2}(y_{0}-n)^{2}=(a^{2}+b^{2})((x_{0}-m)^{2}+(y_{0}-n)^{2})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/982d5646780960d917d6cef171c293f4cf10c93b)
using the above squared equation. But we also have,

![Image 11: {\displaystyle (a(x_{0}-m)+b(y_{0}-n))^{2}=(ax_{0}+by_{0}-am-bn)^{2}=(ax_{0}+by_{0}+c)^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/19963f774e32cb8a862e984caf835ab0496a49a9)
since (_m_, _n_) is on _ax_ + _by_ + _c_ = 0. Thus,

![Image 12: {\displaystyle (a^{2}+b^{2})((x_{0}-m)^{2}+(y_{0}-n)^{2})=(ax_{0}+by_{0}+c)^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4f8ac2e7388e0ba505abc49fa6021589e097c001)
and we obtain the length of the line segment determined by these two points,

![Image 13: {\displaystyle d={\sqrt {(x_{0}-m)^{2}+(y_{0}-n)^{2}}}={\frac {|ax_{0}+by_{0}+c|}{\sqrt {a^{2}+b^{2}}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a8ee1da55aa2e7046aa0c5ab1832a5a42f7d38bc)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distance_from_a_point_to_a_line#cite_note-4)[![Image 14](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Point-to-line2.svg/250px-Point-to-line2.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Point-to-line2.svg)

Diagram for geometric proof

This proof is valid only if the line is not horizontal or vertical.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distance_from_a_point_to_a_line#cite_note-5)

Drop a perpendicular from the point _P_ with coordinates (_x_ 0, _y_ 0) to the line with equation _Ax_ + _By_ + _C_ = 0. Label the foot of the perpendicular _R_. Draw the vertical line through _P_ and label its intersection with the given line _S_. At any point _T_ on the line, draw a right triangle _TVU_ whose sides are horizontal and vertical line segments with hypotenuse _TU_ on the given line and horizontal side of length |_B_| (see diagram). The vertical side of ∆_TVU_ will have length |_A_| since the line has slope −_A_/_B_.

∆_PRS_ and ∆_TVU_ are [similar triangles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Similar_triangles "Similar triangles"), since they are both right triangles and ∠_PSR_ ≅ ∠_TUV_ since they are corresponding angles of a transversal to the parallel lines _PS_ and _UV_ (both are vertical lines).[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distance_from_a_point_to_a_line#cite_note-6) Corresponding sides of these triangles are in the same ratio, so:

![Image 15: {\displaystyle {\frac {|{\overline {PR}}|}{|{\overline {PS}}|}}={\frac {|{\overline {TV}}|}{|{\overline {TU}}|}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bb45959b62f2bd5e9300a43cb7aacef50901cd1b)
If point _S_ has coordinates (_x_ 0,_m_) then |_PS_| = |_y_ 0 − _m_| and the distance from _P_ to the line is:

![Image 16: {\displaystyle |{\overline {PR}}|={\frac {|y_{0}-m||B|}{\sqrt {A^{2}+B^{2}}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/00abd0a03809d493bbb1b22d4f4deae3559da5da)
Since _S_ is on the line, we can find the value of m,

![Image 17: {\displaystyle m={\frac {-Ax_{0}-C}{B}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fa851437803692ec637ffbd40a759f854342488a)
and finally obtain:[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distance_from_a_point_to_a_line#cite_note-7)

![Image 18: {\displaystyle |{\overline {PR}}|={\frac {|Ax_{0}+By_{0}+C|}{\sqrt {A^{2}+B^{2}}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a878404f855da9c4998968ae89db6777d5047375)
A variation of this proof is to place V at P and compute the area of the triangle ∆_UVT_ two ways to obtain that ![Image 19: {\displaystyle D|{\overline {TU}}|=|{\overline {VU}}||{\overline {VT}}|}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/33a5bfebd89bf2dde4a11422aa9ce5ae5bccf8ce) where D is the altitude of ∆_UVT_ drawn to the hypoteneuse of ∆_UVT_ from _P_. The distance formula can then used to express ![Image 20: {\displaystyle |{\overline {TU}}|}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e3f9f32a18a3ce721f8c77470d2e9e9f9c8186f0), ![Image 21: {\displaystyle |{\overline {VU}}|}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/60ebe417ea19236707da8c19a4aa1943688ed897), and ![Image 22: {\displaystyle |{\overline {VT}}|}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0ee154d0f5325196b975f7ffc0c9b7babf79600f)in terms of the coordinates of P and the coefficients of the equation of the line to get the indicated formula.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

### A vector projection proof

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Distance_from_a_point_to_a_line&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: A vector projection proof")]

[![Image 23: Diagram for vector projection proof](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Vectorpoint-to-line.svg/250px-Vectorpoint-to-line.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Vectorpoint-to-line.svg "Diagram for vector projection proof")

Diagram for vector projection proof

Let _P_ be the point with coordinates (_x_ 0, _y_ 0) and let the given line have equation _ax_ + _by_ + _c_ = 0. Also, let _Q_ = (_x_ 1, _y_ 1) be any point on this line and **n** the vector (_a_, _b_) starting at point _Q_. The vector **n** is perpendicular to the line, and the distance _d_ from point _P_ to the line is equal to the length of the orthogonal projection of ![Image 24: {\displaystyle {\overrightarrow {QP}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/29b78737274c81c6169dfd12569caf426f66f0dc) on **n**. The length of this projection is given by:

![Image 25: {\displaystyle d={\frac {|{\overrightarrow {QP}}\cdot \mathbf {n} |}{\|\mathbf {n} \|}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9518b8d989390c2413efe2dac0498da4e0f68573)
Now,

![Image 26: {\displaystyle {\overrightarrow {QP}}=(x_{0}-x_{1},y_{0}-y_{1}),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/121b22e8576044754c245f5459fb25cd8894157b) so ![Image 27: {\displaystyle {\overrightarrow {QP}}\cdot \mathbf {n} =a(x_{0}-x_{1})+b(y_{0}-y_{1})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dfe568e2f25da9e23d05ec2e92a054f52ff6655e) and ![Image 28: {\displaystyle \|\mathbf {n} \|={\sqrt {a^{2}+b^{2}}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a571440ef38438bb71bf85af89a51ee3d31511b6)
thus

![Image 29: {\displaystyle d={\frac {|a(x_{0}-x_{1})+b(y_{0}-y_{1})|}{\sqrt {a^{2}+b^{2}}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2cd047981e9fbc67560953bd90aba010c8607cc8)
Since _Q_ is a point on the line, ![Image 30: {\displaystyle c=-ax_{1}-by_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5244655662da9c8cae50baa07b94394d1cb52dd7), and so,[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distance_from_a_point_to_a_line#cite_note-8)

![Image 31: {\displaystyle d={\frac {|ax_{0}+by_{0}+c|}{\sqrt {a^{2}+b^{2}}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/357e6a175063453f5eff24b8f24d777ae70279c9)
It is possible to produce another expression to find the shortest distance of a point to a line. This derivation also requires that the line is not vertical or horizontal.

The point P is given with coordinates (![Image 32: {\displaystyle x_{0},y_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e75dfe041b2201f4d63e04777fcfd274f271bdec)). The equation of a line is given by ![Image 33: {\displaystyle y=mx+k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a3f5035c65c601a0d22e4fd5a9104deafe8a222f). The equation of the normal of that line which passes through the point P is given ![Image 34: {\displaystyle y={\frac {x_{0}-x}{m}}+y_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a27f704f6af1fa31b02a04f09d143d98a00ded8d).

The point at which these two lines intersect is the closest point on the original line to the point P. Hence:

![Image 35: {\displaystyle mx+k={\frac {x_{0}-x}{m}}+y_{0}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ff0540982c7f6eeacd3c9f6510cffe5c524ea508)
We can solve this equation for _x_,

![Image 36: {\displaystyle x={\frac {x_{0}+my_{0}-mk}{m^{2}+1}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/65fe7821f0dae0a1562701d4816f3502b67ea2b1)
The _y_ coordinate of the point of intersection can be found by substituting this value of _x_ into the equation of the original line,

![Image 37: {\displaystyle y=m{\frac {(x_{0}+my_{0}-mk)}{m^{2}+1}}+k.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dd2e2d024199d150e6ed6cf659d2372570e78fcb)
Using the equation for finding the distance between 2 points, ![Image 38: {\displaystyle d={\sqrt {(X_{2}-X_{1})^{2}+(Y_{2}-Y_{1})^{2}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ecf5afe0940d877801dd597112aff4c4ebfc1f43), we can deduce that the formula to find the shortest distance between a line and a point is the following:

![Image 39: {\displaystyle d={\sqrt {\left({{\frac {x_{0}+my_{0}-mk}{m^{2}+1}}-x_{0}}\right)^{2}+\left({m{\frac {x_{0}+my_{0}-mk}{m^{2}+1}}+k-y_{0}}\right)^{2}}}={\frac {|k+mx_{0}-y_{0}|}{\sqrt {1+m^{2}}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/047d03a828908466bff40bb68c2cd1b3e6f0269a)
Recalling that _m_ = −_a_/_b_ and _k_ = − _c_/_b_ for the line with equation _ax_ + _by_ + c = 0, a little algebraic simplification reduces this to the standard expression.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distance_from_a_point_to_a_line#cite_note-9)

[![Image 40](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Distance_from_a_point_to_a_line.svg/330px-Distance_from_a_point_to_a_line.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Distance_from_a_point_to_a_line.svg)

Illustration of the vector formulation.

The equation of a line can be given in [vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_vector "Euclidean vector") form:

![Image 41: {\displaystyle \mathbf {x} =\mathbf {a} +t\mathbf {n} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1a2530a3ca4e92e83714cf238a678359ffadceee)
Here **a** is the position of a point on the line, and **n** is a [unit vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_vector "Unit vector") in the direction of the line. Then as scalar _t_ varies, **x** gives the [locus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Locus_(mathematics) "Locus (mathematics)") of the line.

The distance of an arbitrary point **p** to this line is given by

![Image 42: {\displaystyle \operatorname {distance} (\mathbf {x} =\mathbf {a} +t\mathbf {n} ,\mathbf {p} )=\|(\mathbf {a} -\mathbf {p} )-((\mathbf {a} -\mathbf {p} )\cdot \mathbf {n} )\mathbf {n} \|.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0df842aba9a43be945b6730c546d32eabe649d6a)
This formula can be derived as follows: ![Image 43: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} -\mathbf {p} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/581e072a6236e1f3808e33314ec3a10a13c296fd) is a vector from **p** to the point **a** on the line. Then ![Image 44: {\displaystyle (\mathbf {a} -\mathbf {p} )\cdot \mathbf {n} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7655090a14dcab3cb8117b0cf617be0bc72abc8b) is the projected length onto the line and so

![Image 45: {\displaystyle ((\mathbf {a} -\mathbf {p} )\cdot \mathbf {n} )\mathbf {n} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35cde79db4bf837a21e3797d1eab50f6a4062fc1)
is a vector that is the [projection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projection_(linear_algebra) "Projection (linear algebra)") of ![Image 46: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} -\mathbf {p} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/581e072a6236e1f3808e33314ec3a10a13c296fd) onto the line. Thus

![Image 47: {\displaystyle (\mathbf {a} -\mathbf {p} )-((\mathbf {a} -\mathbf {p} )\cdot \mathbf {n} )\mathbf {n} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fc001b5665c121d2ec3a0617d76a3402f68011af)
is the component of ![Image 48: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} -\mathbf {p} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/581e072a6236e1f3808e33314ec3a10a13c296fd) perpendicular to the line. The distance from the point to the line is then just the [norm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norm_(mathematics) "Norm (mathematics)") of that vector.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distance_from_a_point_to_a_line#cite_note-10) This more general formula is not restricted to two dimensions.

Another vector formulation
--------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Distance_from_a_point_to_a_line&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Another vector formulation")]

If the line (_l_ ) goes through point A and has a [direction vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_vector "Euclidean vector")![Image 49: {\displaystyle {\vec {u}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/89c41e9cf70c5e5b56e2128a136985a75f90ba43), the distance between point P and line (_l_) is

![Image 50: {\displaystyle d(\mathrm {P} ,(l))={\frac {\left\|{\overrightarrow {\mathrm {AP} }}\times {\vec {u}}\right\|}{\|{\vec {u}}\|}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/40505b1bbbdbcd232c28374dbc355f48b7e614d9)
where ![Image 51: {\displaystyle {\overrightarrow {\mathrm {AP} }}\times {\vec {u}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fe6fb543416ce8ad6fc52f33b0e4ca08ad2d4c54) is the [cross product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product "Cross product") of the vectors ![Image 52: {\displaystyle {\overrightarrow {\mathrm {AP} }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/616a27ab17ea9a9b3ddf433c1ee2f84e45d95a6f) and ![Image 53: {\displaystyle {\vec {u}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/89c41e9cf70c5e5b56e2128a136985a75f90ba43) and where ![Image 54: {\displaystyle \|{\vec {u}}\|}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2ffee8c6e026de4f60495eede0c5e99b2222e2f2) is the vector norm of ![Image 55: {\displaystyle {\vec {u}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/89c41e9cf70c5e5b56e2128a136985a75f90ba43).

![Image 56: {\displaystyle {\overrightarrow {\mathrm {AP} }}=P-A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e425b0584d99fcd101b2a816165e3e5b02be09ab)

Note that cross products only exist in dimensions 3 and 7 and trivially in dimensions 0 and 1 (where the cross product is constant 0).
