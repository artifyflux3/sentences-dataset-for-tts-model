An extreme example of visible light extinction, caused by a [dark nebula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_nebula "Dark nebula")

In [astronomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy "Astronomy"), **extinction** is the [absorption](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absorption_(electromagnetic_radiation) "Absorption (electromagnetic radiation)") and [scattering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_scattering "Light scattering") of [electromagnetic radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation "Electromagnetic radiation") by dust and gas between an emitting [astronomical object](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_object "Astronomical object") and the [observer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observation "Observation"). Interstellar extinction was first documented as such in 1930 by [Robert Julius Trumpler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Julius_Trumpler "Robert Julius Trumpler").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction_(astronomy)#cite_note-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction_(astronomy)#cite_note-Karttunen2016-2) However, its effects had been noted in 1847 by [Friedrich Georg Wilhelm von Struve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Georg_Wilhelm_von_Struve "Friedrich Georg Wilhelm von Struve"),[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction_(astronomy)#cite_note-3) and its effect on the colors of [stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star "Star") had been observed by a number of individuals who did not connect it with the general presence of [galactic dust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_dust "Cosmic dust"). For stars lying near the plane of the [Milky Way](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milky_Way "Milky Way") which are within a few thousand [parsecs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parsec "Parsec") of the Earth, extinction in the [visual band](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visual_band "Visual band") of frequencies ([photometric system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photometric_system "Photometric system")) is roughly 1.8[magnitudes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnitude_(astronomy) "Magnitude (astronomy)") per kiloparsec.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction_(astronomy)#cite_note-4)

For [Earth-bound observers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observatory#Ground-based_observatories "Observatory"), extinction arises both from the [interstellar medium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_medium "Interstellar medium") and the [Earth's atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth "Atmosphere of Earth"); it may also arise from [circumstellar dust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumstellar_dust "Circumstellar dust") around an observed object. Strong extinction in Earth's atmosphere of some [wavelength](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wavelength "Wavelength") regions (such as [X-ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray "X-ray"), [ultraviolet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultraviolet "Ultraviolet"), and [infrared](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infrared "Infrared")) is overcome by the use of [space-based observatories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_telescope "Space telescope"). Since [blue light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blue "Blue") is much more strongly [attenuated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Attenuation "Attenuation") than [red](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red "Red") light, extinction causes objects to appear redder than expected; this phenomenon is called **interstellar reddening**.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction_(astronomy)#cite_note-basicastronomy-5)

Interstellar reddening
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Extinction_(astronomy)&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Interstellar reddening")]

Interstellar reddening is a phenomenon associated with interstellar extinction where the [spectrum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_spectroscopy "Astronomical spectroscopy") of electromagnetic radiation from a [radiation source](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_object "Astronomical object") changes characteristics from that which the object originally [emitted](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emission_spectrum "Emission spectrum"). Reddening occurs due to the light scattering off [dust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_dust "Cosmic dust") and other [matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matter "Matter") in the [interstellar medium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_medium "Interstellar medium"). Interstellar reddening is a different phenomenon from [redshift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift "Redshift"), which is the proportional [frequency shifts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doppler_effect "Doppler effect") of spectra without distortion. Reddening preferentially removes shorter wavelength [photons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon "Photon") from a radiated spectrum while leaving behind the longer wavelength photons, leaving the [spectroscopic lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopy#Atoms "Spectroscopy") unchanged.

In most [photometric systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photometric_system "Photometric system"), filters (passbands) are used from which readings of magnitude of light may take account of latitude and humidity among terrestrial factors. Interstellar reddening equates to the "color excess", defined as the difference between an object's observed color index and its intrinsic color index (sometimes referred to as its normal color index). The latter is the theoretical value which it would have if unaffected by extinction. In the first system, the [UBV photometric system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UBV_photometric_system "UBV photometric system") devised in the 1950s and its most closely related successors, the object's color excess ![Image 2: {\displaystyle E_{B-V}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5092cce6461715463b35866ea3775875ce4f9a99) is related to the object's [B−V color](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/B%E2%88%92V_color "B−V color") (calibrated blue minus calibrated visible) by:

![Image 3: {\displaystyle E_{B-V}=(B-V)_{\textrm {observed}}-(B-V)_{\textrm {intrinsic}}\,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/99d174de237a25ecf477bae84343a6700ebb97de)

For an A0-type main sequence star (these have median wavelength and heat among the main sequence) the color indices are calibrated at 0 based on an intrinsic reading of such a star (± exactly 0.02 depending on which spectral point, i.e. precise passband within the abbreviated color name is in question, see [color index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Color_index "Color index")). At least two and up to five measured passbands in magnitude are then compared by subtraction: U, B, V, I, or R during which the color excess from extinction is calculated and deducted. The name of the four sub-indices (R minus I etc.) and order of the subtraction of recalibrated magnitudes is from right to immediate left within this sequence.

General characteristics
-----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Extinction_(astronomy)&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: General characteristics")]

Interstellar reddening occurs because [interstellar dust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_dust "Cosmic dust") absorbs and scatters blue light waves more than red light waves, making stars appear redder than they are. This is similar to the effect seen when dust particles in the atmosphere of Earth [contribute to red sunsets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sunset#Colors "Sunset").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction_(astronomy)#cite_note-6)

Broadly speaking, interstellar extinction is strongest at short wavelengths, generally observed by using techniques from spectroscopy. Extinction results in a change in the shape of an observed spectrum. Superimposed on this general shape are absorption features (wavelength bands where the intensity is lowered) that have a variety of origins and can give clues as to the chemical composition of the interstellar material, e.g. dust grains. Known absorption features include the 2175[Å](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angstrom "Angstrom") bump, the [diffuse interstellar bands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffuse_interstellar_band "Diffuse interstellar band"), the 3.1[μm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%CE%9Cm "Μm") water ice feature, and the 10 and 18 μm [silicate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicate "Silicate") features.

In the [solar neighborhood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_neighborhood "Solar neighborhood"), the rate of interstellar extinction in the [Johnson–Cousins V-band (visual filter)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UBV_photometric_system "UBV photometric system") averaged at a wavelength of 540 nm is usually taken to be 0.7–1.0 mag/kpc−simply an average due to the _clumpiness_ of interstellar dust.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction_(astronomy)#cite_note-7)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction_(astronomy)#cite_note-8)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction_(astronomy)#cite_note-9) In general, however, this means that a star will have its brightness reduced by about a factor of 2 in the V-band viewed from a good night sky vantage point on earth for every [kiloparsec](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parsec "Parsec") (3,260 light years) it is farther away from us.

The amount of extinction can be significantly higher than this in specific directions. For example, some regions of the [Galactic Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galactic_Center "Galactic Center") are awash with obvious intervening dark dust from our spiral arm (and perhaps others) and themselves in a bulge of dense matter, causing as much as more than 30 magnitudes of extinction in the optical, meaning that less than 1 optical photon in 10 12 passes through.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction_(astronomy)#cite_note-10) This results in the [zone of avoidance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zone_of_Avoidance "Zone of Avoidance"), where our view of the extra-galactic sky is severely hampered, and background galaxies, such as [Dwingeloo 1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dwingeloo_1 "Dwingeloo 1"), were only discovered recently through observations in [radio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radio_astronomy "Radio astronomy") and [infrared](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infrared_astronomy "Infrared astronomy").

The general shape of the ultraviolet through near-infrared (0.125 to 3.5 μm) extinction curve (plotting extinction in magnitude against wavelength, often inverted) looking from our vantage point at other objects in the [Milky Way](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milky_Way "Milky Way"), is fairly well characterized by the stand-alone parameter of relative visibility (of such visible light) R(V) (which is different along different lines of sight),[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction_(astronomy)#cite_note-ca89-11)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction_(astronomy)#cite_note-12) but there are known deviations from this characterization.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction_(astronomy)#cite_note-13) Extending the extinction law into the mid-infrared wavelength range is difficult due to the lack of suitable targets and various contributions by absorption features.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction_(astronomy)#cite_note-14)

R(V) compares aggregate and particular extinctions. It is ![Image 4: {\displaystyle A_{V}/E(B-V)\,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/03af9ef999d9c5072df7196bf1752024a94a1b3f)

Restated, it is the total extinction, A(V) divided by the selective total extinction (A(B)−A(V)) of those two wavelengths (bands). A(B) and A(V) are the _total extinction_ at the [B and V](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UBV_photometric_system "UBV photometric system") filter bands. Another measure used in the literature is the _absolute extinction_ A(λ)/A(V) at wavelength λ, comparing the total extinction at that wavelength to that at the V band.

R(V) is known to be correlated with the average size of the dust grains causing the extinction. For the Milky Way Galaxy, the typical value for R(V) is 3.1,[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction_(astronomy)#cite_note-15) but is found to vary considerably across different lines of sight.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction_(astronomy)#cite_note-ma16-16) As a result, when computing cosmic distances it can be advantageous to move to star data from the near-infrared (of which the filter or passband Ks is quite standard) where the variations and amount of extinction are significantly less, and similar ratios as to R(Ks):[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction_(astronomy)#cite_note-17) 0.49±0.02 and 0.528±0.015 were found respectively by independent groups.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction_(astronomy)#cite_note-ma16-16)[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction_(astronomy)#cite_note-ni09-18) Those two more modern findings differ substantially relative to the commonly referenced historical value ≈0.7.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction_(astronomy)#cite_note-ca89-11)

The relationship between the total extinction, A(V) (measured in [magnitudes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnitude_(astronomy) "Magnitude (astronomy)")), and the [column density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Column_density "Column density") of neutral [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen") atoms column, N H (usually measured in cm−2), shows how the gas and dust in the interstellar medium are related. From studies using ultraviolet spectroscopy of reddened stars and X-ray scattering halos in the Milky Way, Predehl and Schmitt[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction_(astronomy)#cite_note-19) found the relationship between N H and A(V) to be approximately:

![Image 5: {\displaystyle {\frac {N_{H}}{A(V)}}\approx 1.8\times 10^{21}~{\mbox{atoms}}~{\mbox{cm}}^{-2}~{\mbox{mag}}^{-1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e702d56f4085be10149ef6b8f2484d7829c0077b)
(see also:[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction_(astronomy)#cite_note-20)[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction_(astronomy)#cite_note-21)[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction_(astronomy)#cite_note-22)).

Astronomers have determined the three-dimensional distribution of extinction in the "solar circle" (our region of our galaxy), using visible and near-infrared stellar observations and a model of distribution of stars.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction_(astronomy)#cite_note-23)[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction_(astronomy)#cite_note-24) The dust causing extinction mainly lies along the [spiral arms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spiral_galaxy#Spiral_arms "Spiral galaxy"), as observed in other spiral galaxies.

Measuring extinction towards an object
--------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Extinction_(astronomy)&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Measuring extinction towards an object")]

To measure the extinction curve for a [star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star "Star"), the star's spectrum is compared to the observed spectrum of a similar star known not to be affected by extinction (unreddened).[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction_(astronomy)#cite_note-25) It is also possible to use a theoretical spectrum instead of the observed spectrum for the comparison, but this is less common. In the case of [emission nebulae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emission_nebula "Emission nebula"), it is common to look at the ratio of two [emission lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectral_line "Spectral line") which should not be affected by the [temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperature "Temperature") and [density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Density "Density") in the nebula. For example, the ratio of [hydrogen-alpha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen-alpha "Hydrogen-alpha") to [hydrogen-beta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Balmer_series "Balmer series") emission is always around 2.85 under a wide range of conditions prevailing in nebulae. A ratio other than 2.85 must therefore be due to extinction, and the amount of extinction can thus be calculated.

The 2175-angstrom feature
-------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Extinction_(astronomy)&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: The 2175-angstrom feature")]

One prominent feature in measured extinction curves of many objects within the Milky Way is a broad 'bump' at about 2175 [Å](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angstrom "Angstrom"), well into the [ultraviolet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultraviolet "Ultraviolet") region of the electromagnetic spectrum. This feature was first observed in the 1960s,[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction_(astronomy)#cite_note-26)[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction_(astronomy)#cite_note-27) but its origin is still not well understood. Several models have been presented to account for this bump which include [graphitic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graphite "Graphite") grains with a mixture of [PAH](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polycyclic_aromatic_hydrocarbon "Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbon") molecules. Investigations of interstellar grains embedded in interplanetary dust particles (IDP) observed this feature and identified the carrier with organic carbon and amorphous silicates present in the grains.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction_(astronomy)#cite_note-28)

Extinction curves of other galaxies
-----------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Extinction_(astronomy)&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Extinction curves of other galaxies")]

[![Image 6](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Interstellar_extinction_ave_curves_local_group.png/250px-Interstellar_extinction_ave_curves_local_group.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Interstellar_extinction_ave_curves_local_group.png)

Plot showing the average extinction curves for the MW, LMC2, LMC, and SMC Bar.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction_(astronomy)#cite_note-mw_lmc_smc_comp-29) The curves are plotted versus 1/wavelength to emphasize the UV.

The form of the standard extinction curve depends on the composition of the ISM, which varies from galaxy to galaxy. In the [Local Group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_Group "Local Group"), the best-determined extinction curves are those of the Milky Way, the [Small Magellanic Cloud](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Small_Magellanic_Cloud "Small Magellanic Cloud") (SMC) and the [Large Magellanic Cloud](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Large_Magellanic_Cloud "Large Magellanic Cloud") (LMC).

In the LMC, there is significant variation in the characteristics of the ultraviolet extinction with a weaker 2175 Å bump and stronger far-UV extinction in the region associated with the LMC2 supershell (near the 30 Doradus starbursting region) than seen elsewhere in the LMC and in the Milky Way.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction_(astronomy)#cite_note-30)[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction_(astronomy)#cite_note-31) In the SMC, more extreme variation is seen with no 2175 Å bump and very strong far-UV extinction in the star forming Bar and fairly normal ultraviolet extinction seen in the more quiescent Wing.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction_(astronomy)#cite_note-32)[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction_(astronomy)#cite_note-33)[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction_(astronomy)#cite_note-34)

This gives clues as to the composition of the ISM in the various galaxies. Previously, the different average extinction curves in the Milky Way, LMC, and SMC were thought to be the result of the different [metallicities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metallicity "Metallicity") of the three galaxies: the LMC's metallicity is about 40% of that of the [Milky Way](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milky_Way "Milky Way"), while the SMC's is about 10%. Finding extinction curves in both the LMC and SMC which are similar to those found in the Milky Way[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction_(astronomy)#cite_note-mw_lmc_smc_comp-29) and finding extinction curves in the Milky Way that look more like those found in the LMC2 supershell of the LMC[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction_(astronomy)#cite_note-35) and in the SMC Bar[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction_(astronomy)#cite_note-36) has given rise to a new interpretation. The variations in the curves seen in the Magellanic Clouds and Milky Way may instead be caused by processing of the dust grains by nearby star formation. This interpretation is supported by work in starburst galaxies (which are undergoing intense star formation episodes) which shows that their dust lacks the 2175 Å bump.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction_(astronomy)#cite_note-37)[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction_(astronomy)#cite_note-38)

Atmospheric extinction
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Extinction_(astronomy)&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Atmospheric extinction")]

Atmospheric extinction gives the [rising](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sunrise "Sunrise") or [setting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sunset "Sunset") Sun an orange hue and varies with location and [altitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Altitude "Altitude"). Astronomical [observatories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observatory "Observatory") generally are able to characterise the local extinction curve very accurately, to allow observations to be corrected for the effect. Nevertheless, the atmosphere is completely opaque to many wavelengths requiring the use of [satellites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satellite "Satellite") to make observations.

This extinction has three main components: [Rayleigh scattering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rayleigh_scattering "Rayleigh scattering") by air molecules, [scattering by particulates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_scattering_by_particles "Light scattering by particles"), and molecular [absorption](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absorption_(electromagnetic_radiation) "Absorption (electromagnetic radiation)"). Molecular absorption is often referred to as [telluric absorption](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Telluric_contamination "Telluric contamination"), as it is caused by the [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth "Earth") (_telluric_ is a synonym for _terrestrial_). The most important sources of telluric absorption are [molecular oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen "Oxygen") and [ozone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ozone "Ozone"), which strongly absorb radiation near [ultraviolet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultraviolet "Ultraviolet"), and [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water "Water"), which strongly absorbs [infrared](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infrared "Infrared").

The amount of such extinction is lowest at the observer's [zenith](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zenith "Zenith") and highest near the [horizon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horizon "Horizon"). A given star, preferably at solar opposition, reaches its greatest [celestial altitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horizontal_coordinate_system "Horizontal coordinate system") and optimal time for observation when the star is near the local [meridian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meridian_(astronomy) "Meridian (astronomy)") around solar [midnight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Midnight "Midnight") and if the star has a favorable [declination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Declination "Declination") (_i.e._, similar to the observer's [latitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latitude "Latitude")); thus, the seasonal time due to [axial tilt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axial_tilt "Axial tilt") is key. Extinction is approximated by multiplying the standard atmospheric extinction curve (plotted against each wavelength) by the mean [air mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Air_mass_(astronomy) "Air mass (astronomy)") calculated over the duration of the observation. A dry atmosphere reduces infrared extinction significantly.
