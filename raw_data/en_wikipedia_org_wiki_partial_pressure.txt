[![Image 1](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Dalton%27s_law_of_partial_pressures.svg/500px-Dalton%27s_law_of_partial_pressures.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Dalton%27s_law_of_partial_pressures.svg)

The [atmospheric pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure "Atmospheric pressure") is roughly equal to the sum of partial pressures of constituent gases – oxygen, nitrogen, [argon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Argon "Argon"), [water vapor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water_vapor "Water vapor"), carbon dioxide, etc.

In a mixture of [gases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas "Gas"), each constituent gas has a **partial pressure** which is the notional [pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pressure "Pressure") of that constituent gas as if it alone occupied the entire [volume](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume "Volume") of the original mixture at the same [temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperature "Temperature").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_pressure#cite_note-1) The **total pressure** of an [ideal gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ideal_gas "Ideal gas") mixture is the sum of the partial pressures of the gases in the mixture ([Dalton's Law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dalton%27s_Law "Dalton's Law")).

In [respiratory physiology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Respiratory_physiology "Respiratory physiology"), the partial pressure of a dissolved gas in liquid (such as oxygen in arterial blood) is also defined as the partial pressure of that gas as it would be undissolved in gas phase yet in equilibrium with the liquid.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_pressure#cite_note-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_pressure#cite_note-3) This concept is also known as [blood gas tension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_gas_tension "Blood gas tension"). In this sense, the diffusion of a gas liquid is said to be driven by differences in partial pressure (not concentration). In [chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemistry "Chemistry") and [thermodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamics "Thermodynamics"), this concept is generalized to non-ideal gases and instead called [fugacity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fugacity "Fugacity"). The partial pressure of a gas is a measure of its [thermodynamic activity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamic_activity "Thermodynamic activity"). Gases dissolve, diffuse, and react according to their partial pressures and not according to their [concentrations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concentration "Concentration") in a gas mixture or as a solute in solution.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_pressure#cite_note-4) This general property of gases is also true in chemical reactions of gases in biology.

The symbol for pressure is usually _p_ or _pp_ which may use a subscript to identify the pressure, and gas species are also referred to by subscript. When combined, these subscripts are applied recursively.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_pressure#cite_note-Elsevier_guide-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_pressure#cite_note-6)

Dalton's law of partial pressures
---------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Partial_pressure&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Dalton's law of partial pressures")]

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Schematic_Depicting_Dalton%27s_Law-en.svg/250px-Schematic_Depicting_Dalton%27s_Law-en.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Schematic_Depicting_Dalton%27s_Law-en.svg)

Schematic showing the concept of Dalton's Law.

Dalton's law expresses the fact that the total pressure of a mixture of ideal gases is equal to the sum of the partial pressures of the individual gases in the mixture.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_pressure#cite_note-7) This equality arises from the fact that in an ideal gas, the molecules are so far apart that they do not interact with each other. Most actual real-world gases come very close to this ideal. For example, given an ideal gas mixture of [nitrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen "Nitrogen") (N 2), [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen") (H 2) and [ammonia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia "Ammonia") (NH 3):

![Image 3: {\displaystyle p=p_{\mathrm {N} {\vphantom {A}}_{\smash[{t}]{2}}}+p_{\mathrm {H} {\vphantom {A}}_{\smash[{t}]{2}}}+p_{\mathrm {NH} {\vphantom {A}}_{\smash[{t}]{3}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3919abfac7677f06dc8747de2d56f9f3227c94fd) where:

Ideally the ratio of partial pressures equals the ratio of the number of molecules. That is, the [mole fraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mole_fraction "Mole fraction")![Image 4: {\displaystyle x_{\mathrm {i} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/29e34f70cda0c407384c7771503941d7cfcb61a8) of an individual gas component in an [ideal gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ideal_gas "Ideal gas")[mixture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mixture "Mixture") can be expressed in terms of the component's partial pressure or the [moles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mole_(unit) "Mole (unit)") of the component: ![Image 5: {\displaystyle x_{\mathrm {i} }={\frac {p_{\mathrm {i} }}{p}}={\frac {n_{\mathrm {i} }}{n}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e23ebbc56db8bbad2a21c4cd73252f503885d3b7)

and the partial pressure of an individual gas component in an ideal gas can be obtained using this expression: ![Image 6: {\displaystyle p_{\mathrm {i} }=x_{\mathrm {i} }\cdot p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2653898c2a527d5493d490d2ce105c52fb67f8ba)

where:
![Image 7: {\displaystyle x_{\mathrm {i} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/29e34f70cda0c407384c7771503941d7cfcb61a8)= mole fraction of any individual gas component in a gas mixture
![Image 8: {\displaystyle p_{\mathrm {i} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6a9fd82948057ec429dd78a2d23483cba91d8159)= partial pressure of any individual gas component in a gas mixture
![Image 9: {\displaystyle n_{\mathrm {i} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1fe594ca427b31c34faf921f9f8c57224f8bc747)= moles of any individual gas component in a gas mixture
![Image 10: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)= total moles of the gas mixture
![Image 11: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36)= total pressure of the gas mixture

The mole fraction of a gas component in a gas mixture is equal to the volumetric fraction of that component in a gas mixture.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_pressure#cite_note-8)

The ratio of partial pressures relies on the following isotherm relation: ![Image 12: {\displaystyle {\frac {V_{\rm {X}}}{V_{\rm {tot}}}}={\frac {p_{\rm {X}}}{p_{\rm {tot}}}}={\frac {n_{\rm {X}}}{n_{\rm {tot}}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ee6a12b37015e8a79058871d4a9209b16f7589f6)

*   _V_ X is the partial volume of any individual gas component (X)
*   _V_ tot is the total volume of the gas mixture
*   _p_ X is the **partial pressure** of gas X
*   _p_ tot is the total pressure of the gas mixture
*   _n_ X is the [amount of substance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amount_of_substance "Amount of substance") of gas (X)
*   _n_ tot is the total amount of substance in gas mixture

Partial volume (Amagat's law of additive volume)
------------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Partial_pressure&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Partial volume (Amagat's law of additive volume)")]

The partial volume of a particular gas in a mixture is the volume of one component of the gas mixture. It is useful in gas mixtures, e.g. air, to focus on one particular gas component, e.g. oxygen.

It can be approximated both from partial pressure and molar fraction:[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_pressure#cite_note-biophysics200-9)![Image 13: {\displaystyle V_{\rm {X}}=V_{\rm {tot}}\times {\frac {p_{\rm {X}}}{p_{\rm {tot}}}}=V_{\rm {tot}}\times {\frac {n_{\rm {X}}}{n_{\rm {tot}}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/df9b94e910fd3f40cee564b50a101b4b4e2cf5a7)

*   _V_ X is the partial volume of an individual gas component X in the mixture
*   _V_ tot is the total volume of the gas mixture
*   _p_ X is the partial pressure of gas X
*   _p_ tot is the total pressure of the gas mixture
*   _n_ X is the [amount of substance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amount_of_substance "Amount of substance") of gas X
*   _n_ tot is the total amount of substance in the gas mixture

[![Image 14](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Vapor_pressure_chart.svg/250px-Vapor_pressure_chart.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Vapor_pressure_chart.svg)

A log-lin vapor pressure chart for various liquids

[Vapor pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vapor_pressure "Vapor pressure") is the pressure of a [vapor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vapor "Vapor") in equilibrium with its non-vapor phases (i.e., liquid or solid). Most often the term is used to describe a [liquid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liquid "Liquid")'s tendency to [evaporate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evaporate "Evaporate"). It is a measure of the tendency of [molecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecule "Molecule") and [atoms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atom "Atom") to escape from a liquid or a [solid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid "Solid"). A liquid's atmospheric pressure boiling point corresponds to the temperature at which its vapor pressure is equal to the surrounding atmospheric pressure and it is often called the [normal boiling point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_boiling_point "Normal boiling point").

The higher the vapor pressure of a liquid at a given temperature, the lower the normal boiling point of the liquid.

The vapor pressure chart displayed has graphs of the vapor pressures versus temperatures for a variety of liquids.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_pressure#cite_note-10) As can be seen in the chart, the liquids with the highest vapor pressures have the lowest normal boiling points.

For example, at any given temperature, [methyl chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methyl_chloride "Methyl chloride") has the highest vapor pressure of any of the liquids in the chart. It also has the lowest normal boiling point (−24.2°C), which is where the vapor pressure curve of methyl chloride (the blue line) intersects the horizontal pressure line of one atmosphere ([atm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_(unit) "Atmosphere (unit)")) of absolute vapor pressure. At higher altitudes, the atmospheric pressure is less than that at sea level, so boiling points of liquids are reduced. At the top of [Mount Everest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mount_Everest "Mount Everest"), the atmospheric pressure is approximately 0.333 atm, so by using the graph, the boiling point of [diethyl ether](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diethyl_ether "Diethyl ether") would be approximately 7.5°C versus 34.6°C at sea level (1 atm).

For reversible reactions, changes in the total pressure, temperature or reactant concentrations will shift the [equilibrium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_equilibrium "Chemical equilibrium") so as to favor either the right or left side of the reaction in accordance with [Le Chatelier's Principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Le_Chatelier%27s_Principle "Le Chatelier's Principle"). However, the [reaction kinetics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_kinetics "Chemical kinetics") may either oppose or enhance the equilibrium shift. In some cases, the reaction kinetics may be the overriding factor to consider.

Henry's law and the solubility of gases
---------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Partial_pressure&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Henry's law and the solubility of gases")]

Gases will [dissolve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solvation "Solvation") in [liquids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liquid "Liquid") to an extent that is determined by the equilibrium between the undissolved gas and the gas that has dissolved in the liquid (called the _[solvent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solvent "Solvent")_).[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_pressure#cite_note-RolfeSander-11) The equilibrium constant for that equilibrium is:

![Image 38: {\displaystyle k={\frac {p_{x}}{C_{x}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bd60e8e052c3d34420e6daf7086bf25eb9d43443)1

where:

The form of the equilibrium constant shows that **the concentration of a [solute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solute "Solute") gas in a solution is directly proportional to the partial pressure of that gas above the solution**. This statement is known as [Henry's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry%27s_law "Henry's law") and the equilibrium constant ![Image 39: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40) is quite often referred to as the Henry's law constant.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_pressure#cite_note-RolfeSander-11)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_pressure#cite_note-12)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_pressure#cite_note-13)

Henry's law is sometimes written as:[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_pressure#cite_note-UArizona-14)

![Image 40: {\displaystyle k'={\frac {C_{x}}{p_{x}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9b30b016eaf183bbc8f9b2e76ae5e0c61769eaa0)2

where ![Image 41: {\displaystyle k'}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/51a2b832ed184c7b6481b3926bf8172d353fa7de) is also referred to as the Henry's law constant.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_pressure#cite_note-UArizona-14) As can be seen by comparing equations (**[1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_pressure#math_1)**) and (**[2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_pressure#math_2)**) above, ![Image 42: {\displaystyle k'}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/51a2b832ed184c7b6481b3926bf8172d353fa7de) is the reciprocal of ![Image 43: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40). Since both may be referred to as the Henry's law constant, readers of the technical literature must be quite careful to note which version of the Henry's law equation is being used.

Henry's law is an approximation that only applies for dilute, ideal solutions and for solutions where the liquid solvent does not [react chemically](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_reaction "Chemical reaction") with the gas being dissolved.

In diving breathing gases
-------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Partial_pressure&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: In diving breathing gases")]

In [underwater diving](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Underwater_diving "Underwater diving") the physiological effects of individual component gases of [breathing gases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Breathing_gas "Breathing gas") are a function of partial pressure.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_pressure#cite_note-NOAA_Diving_Manual_1979-15)

Using diving terms, partial pressure is calculated as:

**partial pressure = (total absolute pressure) × (volume fraction of gas component)**[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_pressure#cite_note-NOAA_Diving_Manual_1979-15)
For the component gas "i":

**p i = P × F i**[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_pressure#cite_note-NOAA_Diving_Manual_1979-15)
For example, at 50 metres (164 ft) underwater, the total absolute pressure is 6 bar (600 kPa) (i.e., 1 bar of [atmospheric pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure "Atmospheric pressure") + 5 bar of water pressure) and the partial pressures of the main components of [air](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere "Earth's atmosphere"), [oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen "Oxygen") 21% by volume and [nitrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen "Nitrogen") approximately 79% by volume are:

**pN 2** = 6 bar × 0.79 = 4.7 bar absolute**pO 2** = 6 bar × 0.21 = 1.3 bar absolute
where:
p i= partial pressure of gas component i = ![Image 44: {\displaystyle P_{\mathrm {i} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0df8af1c605b5d39a766a26a778832cdbce5b515) in the terms used in this article
P= total pressure = ![Image 45: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a) in the terms used in this article
F i= volume fraction of gas component i = mole fraction, ![Image 46: {\displaystyle x_{\mathrm {i} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/29e34f70cda0c407384c7771503941d7cfcb61a8), in the terms used in this article
pN 2= partial pressure of nitrogen = ![Image 47: {\displaystyle P_{\mathrm {N_{2}} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/75c009c82caa92fd0e9cdb864199c2cbf4ad6651) in the terms used in this article
pO 2= partial pressure of oxygen = ![Image 48: {\displaystyle P_{\mathrm {O_{2}} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b6c83bee2216231f4ad988a9b659057989c41590) in the terms used in this article

The minimum safe lower limit for the partial pressures of oxygen in a breathing gas mixture for diving is 0.16 bars (16 kPa) absolute. [Hypoxia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypoxia_(medical) "Hypoxia (medical)") and sudden unconsciousness can become a problem with an oxygen partial pressure of less than 0.16 bar absolute.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_pressure#cite_note-Sawatzky_2008-16)[Oxygen toxicity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen_toxicity "Oxygen toxicity"), involving convulsions, becomes a problem when oxygen partial pressure is too high. The [NOAA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NOAA "NOAA") Diving Manual recommends a maximum single exposure of 45 minutes at 1.6 bar absolute, of 120 minutes at 1.5 bar absolute, of 150 minutes at 1.4 bar absolute, of 180 minutes at 1.3 bar absolute and of 210 minutes at 1.2 bar absolute. Oxygen toxicity becomes a risk when these oxygen partial pressures and exposures are exceeded. The partial pressure of oxygen also determines the [maximum operating depth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maximum_operating_depth "Maximum operating depth") of a gas mixture.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_pressure#cite_note-NOAA_Diving_Manual_1979-15)

[Narcosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen_narcosis "Nitrogen narcosis") is a problem when breathing gases at high pressure. Typically, the maximum total partial pressure of narcotic gases used when planning for [technical diving](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Technical_diving "Technical diving") may be around 4.5 bar absolute, based on an [equivalent narcotic depth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equivalent_narcotic_depth "Equivalent narcotic depth") of 35 metres (115 ft).

The effect of a toxic contaminant such as [carbon monoxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide "Carbon monoxide") in breathing gas is also related to the partial pressure when breathed. A mixture which may be relatively safe at the surface could be dangerously toxic at the maximum depth of a dive, or a tolerable level of [carbon dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_dioxide "Carbon dioxide") in the breathing loop of a [diving rebreather](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diving_rebreather "Diving rebreather") may become intolerable within seconds during descent when the partial pressure rapidly increases, and could lead to panic or incapacitation of the diver.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_pressure#cite_note-NOAA_Diving_Manual_1979-15)

*   [Blood gas tension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_gas_tension "Blood gas tension")– Partial pressure of blood gases
*   [Breathing gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Breathing_gas "Breathing gas")– Gas used for human respiration
*   [Henry's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry%27s_law "Henry's law")– Gas law regarding proportionality of dissolved gas
*   [Ideal gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ideal_gas "Ideal gas")– Mathematical model which approximates the behavior of real gases 
    *   [Ideal gas law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ideal_gas_law "Ideal gas law")– Equation of the state of a hypothetical ideal gas

*   [Mole fraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mole_fraction "Mole fraction")– Proportion of a constituent in a mixture 
    *   [Mole (unit)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mole_(unit) "Mole (unit)")– SI unit of amount of substance

*   [Vapor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vapor "Vapor")– Substances in the gas phase at a temperature lower than its critical point
