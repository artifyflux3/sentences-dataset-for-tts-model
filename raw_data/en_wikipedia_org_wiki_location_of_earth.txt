Knowledge of the **location of Earth** has been shaped by 400 years of telescopic observations, and has expanded radically since the start of the 20th century. Initially, [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth "Earth") was believed to be the [center of the Universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geocentrism "Geocentrism"), which consisted only of those planets visible with the [naked eye](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_planet "Classical planet") and an outlying sphere of [fixed stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fixed_stars "Fixed stars").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Location_of_Earth#cite_note-1) After the acceptance of the [heliocentric model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heliocentric_model "Heliocentric model") in the 17th century, observations by [William Herschel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Herschel "William Herschel") and others showed that the Sun lay within a vast, disc-shaped [galaxy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy "Galaxy") of stars.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Location_of_Earth#cite_note-2) By the 20th century, observations of [spiral nebulae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spiral_nebula "Spiral nebula") revealed that the [Milky Way](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milky_Way "Milky Way") galaxy was one of billions in an [expanding universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expanding_universe "Expanding universe"),[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Location_of_Earth#cite_note-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Location_of_Earth#cite_note-4) grouped into clusters and [superclusters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supercluster "Supercluster"). By the end of the 20th century, the [overall structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Large-scale_structure_of_the_cosmos "Large-scale structure of the cosmos") of the visible universe was becoming clearer, with superclusters forming into a vast web of [filaments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galactic_filament "Galactic filament") and [voids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Void_(astronomy) "Void (astronomy)").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Location_of_Earth#cite_note-5) Superclusters, filaments and voids are the largest coherent structures in the Universe that we can observe.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Location_of_Earth#cite_note-6) At still larger scales (over 1000 [megaparsecs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Megaparsec "Megaparsec")[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Location_of_Earth#cite_note-parsec-7)) the Universe becomes homogeneous, meaning that all its parts have on average the same density, composition and structure.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Location_of_Earth#cite_note-Kirshner-8)

Since there is believed to be no "center" or "edge" of the Universe, there is no particular reference point with which to plot the overall location of Earth in the universe.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Location_of_Earth#cite_note-Klaus2002-9) Because the [observable universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe "Observable universe") is defined as that region of the Universe visible to terrestrial observers, Earth is, because of the constancy of the speed of light, the center of Earth's observable universe. Reference can be made to Earth's position with respect to specific structures, which exist at various scales. It is still undetermined whether the Universe is [infinite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinity "Infinity"). There have been numerous hypotheses that the known universe may be only one such example within a higher [multiverse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiverse "Multiverse"); however, no direct evidence of any sort of multiverse has been observed, and some have argued that the hypothesis is not [falsifiable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Falsifiable "Falsifiable").[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Location_of_Earth#cite_note-10)[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Location_of_Earth#cite_note-11)

Earth is the third planet from the Sun with an approximate distance of 149.6 million kilometres (93.0 million miles), and is traveling nearly 2.1 million kilometres per hour (1.3 million miles per hour) through [outer space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_space "Outer space").[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Location_of_Earth#cite_note-NASA-HowFast-12)
