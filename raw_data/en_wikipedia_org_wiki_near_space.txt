**Near space** is the upper region of the [Earth's atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere "Earth's atmosphere") between [airspace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Airspace "Airspace") and [outer space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_space "Outer space"). It is sometimes referred to as the "[edge of space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edge_of_space "Edge of space")".[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Near_space#cite_note-Anbound_Jun_2023-1) There is no legal definition for this extent,[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Near_space#cite_note-Su_2013-2) but typically this is the altitude range from 20 to 100 km (12 to 62 mi).[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Near_space#cite_note-Hao_Fabio_2019-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Near_space#cite_note-Wang_2011-4)

The lower limit of this region is set by the [flight envelope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flight_envelope "Flight envelope") of normal [aircraft](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aircraft "Aircraft"). For safety reasons, [commercial aircraft](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commercial_aircraft "Commercial aircraft") are normally limited to altitudes of 12 km (7.5 mi), and [air navigation services](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Air_navigation_service "Air navigation service") only extend to 18 to 20 km (11 to 12 mi).[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Near_space#cite_note-Hao_Fabio_2019-3) The upper limit of the near space range is the [Kármán line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K%C3%A1rm%C3%A1n_line "Kármán line") at 100 km (62 mi), where [astrodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrodynamics "Astrodynamics") must take over from [aerodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aerodynamics "Aerodynamics") in order to achieve flight.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Near_space#cite_note-CAPS_India-5) This range includes the [stratosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stratosphere "Stratosphere"), [mesosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mesosphere "Mesosphere") and lower [thermosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermosphere "Thermosphere") layers of the Earth's atmosphere.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Near_space#cite_note-Luo_et_al_2024-6)

Larger ranges for _near space_ are used by some authors, such as 18 to 160 km (11 to 99 mi). These extend from the [Armstrong limit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Armstrong_limit "Armstrong limit") to the altitudes where [orbital flight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_flight "Orbital flight") in [very low Earth orbits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Very_low_Earth_orbit "Very low Earth orbit") becomes practical.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Near_space#cite_note-Malinowski_2024-7)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Near_space#cite_note-Heiner_2022-8) Spacecraft have entered into a highly elliptical [orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_flight "Orbital flight") with a perigee as low as 80 to 90 km (50 to 56 mi), surviving for multiple orbits. At an altitude of 120 km (75 mi),[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Near_space#cite_note-McDowell_2018-9) descending spacecraft begin [atmospheric entry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmospheric_entry "Atmospheric entry") as [atmospheric drag](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmospheric_drag "Atmospheric drag") becomes noticeable. For [spaceplanes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spaceplane "Spaceplane") such as [NASA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NASA "NASA")'s [Space Shuttle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_Shuttle "Space Shuttle"), this begins the process of switching from steering with thrusters to maneuvering with [aerodynamic control surfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flight_control_surfaces "Flight control surfaces").[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Near_space#cite_note-Petty_2003-10)

Craft that fly in near space include [high-altitude balloons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-altitude_balloon "High-altitude balloon"), [non-rigid airships](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-rigid_airship "Non-rigid airship"), [rockoons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rockoon "Rockoon"), [sounding rockets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sounding_rocket "Sounding rocket"), and the [Lockheed U-2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lockheed_U-2 "Lockheed U-2") aircraft. It is of growing interest for the use of various [high-altitude platform stations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-altitude_platform_station "High-altitude platform station").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Near_space#cite_note-Hao_Fabio_2019-3)[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Near_space#cite_note-Pelton_2020-11) This region can provide long-term sensing of a duration and quality that is unavailable via satellites.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Near_space#cite_note-Wang_2011-4) The potential uses for unmanned near–space vehicles include persistent ground surveillance and communications (including relays), both for military and commercial uses.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Near_space#cite_note-Schmidt_et_al_2007-12) Near-space is a [level flight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Level_flight "Level flight") operating region for [hypersonic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypersonic_flight "Hypersonic flight") vehicles.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Near_space#cite_note-Hu_et_al_2009-13)

Although there are no clouds or storms in near space, wind is still a significant factor that can require [station-keeping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmospheric_station-keeping "Atmospheric station-keeping") of a long-term vehicle. This region is located below the [ionosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ionosphere "Ionosphere"), which can impact electrical signals that pass through it. The ionosphere can significantly degrade microwave signals from orbiting satellites, a disadvantage that near-earth vehicles lack.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Near_space#cite_note-Wang_2011-4)

Near space has been used for scientific ballooning for over two centuries,[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Near_space#cite_note-Vernon_Jones_2005-14) for applications such as [submillimetre astronomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Submillimetre_astronomy#Submillimetre_astronomy_from_near-space "Submillimetre astronomy").[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Near_space#cite_note-Walker_et_al_2014-15) High-altitude balloons are flown by students and [by amateur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amateur_radio_high-altitude_ballooning "Amateur radio high-altitude ballooning") groups, for both scientific and educational purposes.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Near_space#cite_note-Flaten_2012-16) One example are so-called [PongSats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PongSat "PongSat").[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Near_space#cite_note-David_2012-17) Near space is also a potential market for tourism balloon flights.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Near_space#cite_note-Rees_et_al_2023-18)

The stratosphere is a layer of the atmosphere that extends from below near space up to an altitude of 50 km (31 mi),[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Near_space#cite_note-NOAA-19) although this can vary with latitude and seasons. The air here is very dry, but icy [nacreous clouds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nacreous_clouds "Nacreous clouds") can appear near the poles in the lower stratosphere during the winter. Temperature rises with increasing altitude and thus there is very little vertical [convection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convection "Convection"), allowing compounds such as [chlorofluorocarbons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chlorofluorocarbon "Chlorofluorocarbon") to remain in the stratosphere for a long duration. [Ozone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ozone "Ozone") is relatively abundant in this layer, forming an [ozone layer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ozone_layer "Ozone layer") that absorbs [ultraviolet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultraviolet "Ultraviolet") radiation.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Near_space#cite_note-UCAR-20)

Above the stratosphere is the mesosphere, which ranges up to 85 km (53 mi) in altitude. Near the base of this region, the temperature is about −15°C (5°F), and it declines with increasing height. The air pressure is sufficiently dense to decelerate [meteors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteors#Meteor "Meteors"), causing them to burn up.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Near_space#cite_note-NOAA-19) During the summer months at high [latitudes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latitude "Latitude"), [noctilucent clouds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noctilucent_cloud "Noctilucent cloud") form from ice crystals at these altitudes.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Near_space#cite_note-NASA_NC-21) The [ionosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ionosphere "Ionosphere") is a layer of ionized atoms and molecules that spans altitudes of 80 to 600 km (50 to 373 mi). These ions can reflect and modify radio waves passing through the upper atmosphere.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Near_space#cite_note-NOAA_ION-22)

[Airglow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Airglow "Airglow") is a faint energy emission of solar radiation from atmospheric components in the upper part of Earth's atmosphere, starting at around 80 km (50 mi) in altitude. Molecules including NO, OH, and O 2 undergo excitation, dissociation, recombination, and ionization from solar energy, causing the release of radiation. [Aurora](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aurora "Aurora") is an emission caused by interaction between the [Earth's magnetic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_magnetic_field "Earth's magnetic field") and particles carried by the [solar wind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_wind "Solar wind"). These interact with molecular nitrogen and oxygen at altitudes from 90 to 130 km (56 to 81 mi), producing the auroral light. Other sources of energy in the atmosphere include galactic [cosmic rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_ray "Cosmic ray"), [zodiacal light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zodiacal_light "Zodiacal light"), [moonlight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moonlight "Moonlight"), and [lightning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lightning "Lightning") discharge.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Near_space#cite_note-Kren_et_al_2017-23)

[Atmospheric tides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmospheric_tides "Atmospheric tides") are large-scale oscillations created by cycles of solar radiation absorption. These propagate from the lower atmosphere up into the thermosphere, transporting energy and momentum. They couple together the different atmospheric layers through their dynamic behavior and interactions. Temperature variations created by atmospheric tides can control the appearance of ice particles in the mesosphere.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Near_space#cite_note-LIAP-24) Smaller scale oscillations in the atmosphere are caused by lunar tides.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Near_space#cite_note-Kren_et_al_2017-23)
