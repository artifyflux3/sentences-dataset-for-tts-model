**Tullio Levi-Civita**, [ForMemRS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Society#Foreign_Member_of_the_Royal_Society_(ForMemRS) "Fellow of the Royal Society")[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tullio_Levi-Civita#cite_note-1) (; Italian:[[ˈtulljo ˈlɛːvi ˈtʃiːvita]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/Italian "Help:IPA/Italian"); 29 March 1873 – 29 December 1941) was an Italian [mathematician](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematician "Mathematician"), most famous for his work on [absolute differential calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_differential_calculus "Absolute differential calculus") ([tensor calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_calculus "Tensor calculus")) and its applications to the [theory of relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theory_of_relativity "Theory of relativity"), but who also made significant contributions in other areas. He was a pupil of [Gregorio Ricci-Curbastro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gregorio_Ricci-Curbastro "Gregorio Ricci-Curbastro"), the inventor of tensor calculus. His work included foundational papers in both [pure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pure_mathematics "Pure mathematics") and [applied mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Applied_mathematics "Applied mathematics"), [celestial mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celestial_mechanics "Celestial mechanics") (notably on the [three-body problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-body_problem "Three-body problem")), analytic mechanics (the Levi-Civita separability conditions in the [Hamilton–Jacobi equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamilton%E2%80%93Jacobi_equation "Hamilton–Jacobi equation"))[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tullio_Levi-Civita#cite_note-2) and [hydrodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrodynamics "Hydrodynamics").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tullio_Levi-Civita#cite_note-mactutor-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tullio_Levi-Civita#cite_note-mathgene-4)

Tullio Levi-Civita

Born into an Italian [Jewish family](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jewish_family "Jewish family") in [Padua](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Padua "Padua"), Levi-Civita was the son of Giacomo Levi-Civita, a lawyer and former [senator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Senate_of_the_Kingdom_of_Italy "Senate of the Kingdom of Italy"). He graduated in 1892 from the [University of Padua](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Padua "University of Padua") Faculty of Mathematics. In 1894 he earned a teaching diploma after which he was appointed to the Faculty of Science teacher's college in Pavia. In 1898 he was appointed to the Padua Chair of Rational Mechanics (left uncovered by death of [Ernesto Padova](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ernesto_Padova "Ernesto Padova")) where he met and, in 1914, married [Libera Trevisani](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Libera_Trevisani "Libera Trevisani"), one of his pupils.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tullio_Levi-Civita#cite_note-5) He remained in his position at Padua until 1918, when he was appointed to the Chair of Higher Analysis at the [University of Rome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Rome_La_Sapienza "University of Rome La Sapienza"); in another two years he was appointed to the Chair of Mechanics there.

In 1900 he and [Ricci-Curbastro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gregorio_Ricci-Curbastro "Gregorio Ricci-Curbastro") published the theory of [tensors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor "Tensor") in _Méthodes de calcul différentiel absolu et leurs applications_,[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tullio_Levi-Civita#cite_note-6) which [Albert Einstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Einstein "Albert Einstein") used as a resource to master tensor calculus, a critical tool in the development of the theory of [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity"). In 1917 he introduced the notion of parallel transport[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tullio_Levi-Civita#cite_note-7)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tullio_Levi-Civita#cite_note-8) in [Riemannian geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_geometry "Riemannian geometry"), motivated by the will to simplify the computation of the curvature of a [Riemannian manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_manifold "Riemannian manifold").[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tullio_Levi-Civita#cite_note-9) Levi-Civita's series of papers on the problem of a static [gravitational field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_field "Gravitational field") were also discussed in his 1915–1917 correspondence with Einstein. The correspondence was initiated by Levi-Civita, as he found mathematical errors in Einstein's use of tensor calculus to explain the theory of relativity. Levi-Civita methodically kept all of Einstein's replies to him; and even though Einstein had not kept Levi-Civita's, the entire correspondence could be re-constructed from Levi-Civita's archive. It is evident from this that, after numerous letters, the two men had grown to respect each other. In one of the letters, regarding Levi-Civita's new work, Einstein wrote "I admire the elegance of your method of computation; it must be nice to ride through these fields upon the horse of true mathematics while the like of us have to make our way laboriously on foot".[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tullio_Levi-Civita#cite_note-10) In 1933 Levi-Civita contributed to [Paul Dirac](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Dirac "Paul Dirac")'s equations in [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics") as well.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tullio_Levi-Civita#cite_note-11)

His textbook on tensor calculus, _The Absolute Differential Calculus_ (originally a set of lecture notes in Italian co-authored with Ricci-Curbastro), remains one of the standard texts almost a century after its first publication, with several translations available.

In 1936, receiving an invitation from Einstein, Levi-Civita traveled to [Princeton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Princeton_University "Princeton University"), United States and lived there with him for a year. But when the risk of war in Europe again rose, he returned to Italy. The [1938 race laws](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italian_Racial_Laws "Italian Racial Laws") enacted by the Italian Fascist government deprived Levi-Civita of his professorship and of his membership of all scientific societies.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tullio_Levi-Civita#cite_note-:0-12) Isolated from the scientific world, he died in his apartment in Rome on 29 December, 1941.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tullio_Levi-Civita#cite_note-:0-12)

Among his [PhD](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doctor_of_Philosophy "Doctor of Philosophy") students were [Octav Onicescu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octav_Onicescu "Octav Onicescu"), [Attilio Palatini](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Attilio_Palatini "Attilio Palatini"), [Giovanni Lampariello](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Giovanni_Lampariello&action=edit&redlink=1 "Giovanni Lampariello (page does not exist)") and [Gheorghe Vrânceanu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gheorghe_Vr%C3%A2nceanu "Gheorghe Vrânceanu").

Later on, when asked what he liked best about Italy, Einstein said "spaghetti and Levi-Civita".[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tullio_Levi-Civita#cite_note-13)

Other studies and honors
------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tullio_Levi-Civita&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Other studies and honors")]

Analytical dynamics was another aspect of Levi-Civita's studies: many of his articles examine the [three-body problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-body_problem "Three-body problem"). He wrote articles on hydrodynamics and on systems of differential equations. He is credited with improvements to the [Cauchy–Kowalevski theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cauchy%E2%80%93Kowalevski_theorem "Cauchy–Kowalevski theorem"), on which he wrote a book in 1931. In 1933, he contributed to work on the [Dirac equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dirac_equation "Dirac equation"). He developed the [Levi-Civita field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_field "Levi-Civita field"), a system of numbers that includes [infinitesimal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinitesimal "Infinitesimal") quantities.

Levi-Civita was elected an international honorary member of the [American Academy of Arts and Sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Academy_of_Arts_and_Sciences "American Academy of Arts and Sciences") in 1917.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tullio_Levi-Civita#cite_note-14) The [Royal Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Society "Royal Society") awarded him the [Sylvester Medal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sylvester_Medal "Sylvester Medal") in 1922 and elected him as a [foreign member](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Society#Foreign_Member_of_the_Royal_Society_(ForMemRS) "Fellow of the Royal Society") in 1930. He became an honorary member of the [London Mathematical Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/London_Mathematical_Society "London Mathematical Society"), of the [Royal Society of Edinburgh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Society_of_Edinburgh "Royal Society of Edinburgh"), and of the [Edinburgh Mathematical Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edinburgh_Mathematical_Society "Edinburgh Mathematical Society"), following his participation in their colloquium in 1930 at the [University of St Andrews](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_St_Andrews "University of St Andrews"). He was also a member of the [Accademia dei Lincei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accademia_dei_Lincei "Accademia dei Lincei"), the [Pontifical Academy of Sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pontifical_Academy_of_Sciences "Pontifical Academy of Sciences"), and the [American Philosophical Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Philosophical_Society "American Philosophical Society").[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tullio_Levi-Civita#cite_note-15)

All his mathematical works, except for the [monographs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monograph "Monograph"), [treatises](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treatise "Treatise") and [textbooks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Textbook "Textbook"), were posthumously gathered in the six volumes of his "_Collected works_", in a revised typographical form amending both [typographical errors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Typographical_error "Typographical error") and author's oversights.
