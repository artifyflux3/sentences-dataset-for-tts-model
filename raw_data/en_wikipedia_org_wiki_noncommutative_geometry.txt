**Noncommutative geometry** (**NCG**) is a branch of [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics") concerned with a geometric approach to [noncommutative algebras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noncommutative_algebra "Noncommutative algebra"), and with the construction of _spaces_ that are locally presented by noncommutative algebras of functions, possibly in some generalized sense. A noncommutative algebra is an [associative algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Associative_algebra "Associative algebra") in which the multiplication is not [commutative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative "Commutative"), that is, for which ![Image 1: {\displaystyle xy}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c72eb345e496513fb8b2fa4aa8c4d89b855f9a01) does not always equal ![Image 2: {\displaystyle yx}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3e2e367e3fe21533f90bf7db6f03aed1d27d4176); or more generally an [algebraic structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_structure "Algebraic structure") in which one of the principal [binary operations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_operation "Binary operation") is not commutative; one also allows additional structures, e.g. [topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology "Topology") or [norm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norm_(mathematics) "Norm (mathematics)"), to be possibly carried by the noncommutative algebra of functions.

An approach giving deep insight about noncommutative spaces is through [operator algebras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operator_algebra "Operator algebra"), that is, algebras of [bounded linear operators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bounded_linear_operator "Bounded linear operator") on a [Hilbert space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space "Hilbert space").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noncommutative_geometry#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKhalkhaliMarcolli2008171-1) Perhaps one of the typical examples of a noncommutative space is the "[noncommutative torus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noncommutative_torus "Noncommutative torus")", which played a key role in the early development of this field in 1980s and lead to noncommutative versions of [vector bundles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_bundle "Vector bundle"), [connections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connection_(vector_bundle) "Connection (vector bundle)"), [curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvature "Curvature"), etc.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noncommutative_geometry#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKhalkhaliMarcolli200821-2)

The main motivation is to extend the commutative duality between spaces and functions to the noncommutative setting. In mathematics, _[spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space\_(mathematics) "Space (mathematics)")_, which are geometric in nature, can be related to numerical [functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)") on them. In general, such functions will form a [commutative ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_ring "Commutative ring"). For instance, one may take the ring _C_(_X_) of [continuous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_function "Continuous function")[complex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number")-valued functions on a [topological space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_space "Topological space")_X_. In many cases (_e.g._, if _X_ is a [compact](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compact_space "Compact space")[Hausdorff space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hausdorff_space "Hausdorff space")), we can recover _X_ from _C_(_X_), and therefore it makes some sense to say that _X_ has _commutative topology_.

More specifically, in topology, compact [Hausdorff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hausdorff_space "Hausdorff space") topological spaces can be reconstructed from the [Banach algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Banach_algebra "Banach algebra") of functions on the space ([Gelfand–Naimark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gelfand_representation#Statement_of_the_commutative_Gelfand%E2%80%93Naimark_theorem "Gelfand representation")). In commutative [algebraic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_geometry "Algebraic geometry"), [algebraic schemes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scheme_(algebraic_geometry) "Scheme (algebraic geometry)") are locally prime spectra of commutative unital rings ([A. Grothendieck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_Grothendieck "Alexander Grothendieck")), and every quasi-separated scheme ![Image 3: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) can be reconstructed up to isomorphism of schemes from the category of quasicoherent sheaves of ![Image 4: {\displaystyle O_{X}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a816172129d917db5f1bf8e9c091513579890806)-modules ([P. Gabriel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre_Gabriel "Pierre Gabriel")–A. Rosenberg). For [Grothendieck topologies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grothendieck_topologies "Grothendieck topologies"), the cohomological properties of a site are invariants of the corresponding category of sheaves of sets viewed abstractly as a [topos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topos "Topos") (A. Grothendieck). In all these cases, a space is reconstructed from the algebra of functions or its categorified version—some [category of sheaves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sheaf_(mathematics) "Sheaf (mathematics)") on that space.

Functions on a topological space can be multiplied and added pointwise hence they form a commutative algebra; in fact these operations are local in the topology of the base space, hence the functions form a sheaf of commutative rings over the base space.

The dream of noncommutative geometry is to generalize this duality to the duality between noncommutative algebras, or sheaves of noncommutative algebras, or sheaf-like noncommutative algebraic or operator-algebraic structures, and geometric entities of certain kinds, and give an interaction between the algebraic and geometric description of those via this duality.

Regarding that the commutative rings correspond to usual affine schemes, and commutative [C*-algebras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C*-algebras "C*-algebras") to usual topological spaces, the extension to noncommutative rings and algebras requires non-trivial generalization of [topological spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_space "Topological space") as "non-commutative spaces". For this reason there is some talk about [non-commutative topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-commutative_topology "Non-commutative topology"), though the term also has other meanings.

### Applications in mathematical physics

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Noncommutative_geometry&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Applications in mathematical physics")]

There is an influence of physics on noncommutative geometry.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noncommutative_geometry#cite_note-3) The [fuzzy sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fuzzy_sphere "Fuzzy sphere") has been used to study the emergence of [conformal symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conformal_symmetry "Conformal symmetry") in the 3-dimensional [Ising model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ising_model "Ising model").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noncommutative_geometry#cite_note-4)

### Motivation from ergodic theory

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Noncommutative_geometry&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Motivation from ergodic theory")]

Some of the theory developed by [Alain Connes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alain_Connes "Alain Connes") to handle noncommutative geometry at a technical level has roots in older attempts, in particular in [ergodic theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ergodic_theory "Ergodic theory"). The proposal of [George Mackey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_Mackey "George Mackey") to create a _virtual subgroup_ theory, with respect to which ergodic [group actions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_action_(mathematics) "Group action (mathematics)") would become [homogeneous spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homogeneous_space "Homogeneous space") of an extended kind, has by now been subsumed.

Noncommutative C*-algebras, von Neumann algebras
------------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Noncommutative_geometry&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Noncommutative C*-algebras, von Neumann algebras")]

The (formal) duals of [non-commutative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-commutative "Non-commutative")[C*-algebras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C*-algebra "C*-algebra") are often now called non-commutative spaces. This is by analogy with the [Gelfand representation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gelfand_representation "Gelfand representation"), which shows that [commutative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative "Commutative") C*-algebras are [dual](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duality_(mathematics) "Duality (mathematics)") to [locally compact](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Locally_compact "Locally compact")[Hausdorff spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hausdorff_space "Hausdorff space"). In general, one can associate to any C*-algebra _S_ a topological space _Ŝ_; see [spectrum of a C*-algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectrum_of_a_C*-algebra "Spectrum of a C*-algebra").

For the [duality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duality_(mathematics) "Duality (mathematics)") between localizable [measure spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Measure_space "Measure space") and commutative [von Neumann algebras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Von_Neumann_algebra "Von Neumann algebra"), [noncommutative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noncommutative "Noncommutative")[von Neumann algebras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Von_Neumann_algebra "Von Neumann algebra") are called _non-commutative [measure spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Measure\_space "Measure space")_.

Noncommutative differentiable manifolds
---------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Noncommutative_geometry&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Noncommutative differentiable manifolds")]

A smooth [Riemannian manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_manifold "Riemannian manifold")_M_ is a [topological space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_space "Topological space") with a lot of extra structure. From its algebra of continuous functions _C_(_M_), we only recover _M_ topologically. The algebraic invariant that recovers the Riemannian structure is a [spectral triple](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectral_triple "Spectral triple"). It is constructed from a smooth vector bundle _E_ over _M_, e.g. the exterior algebra bundle. The Hilbert space _L_ 2(_M_,_E_) of square integrable sections of _E_ carries a representation of _C_(_M)_ by multiplication operators, and we consider an unbounded operator _D_ in _L_ 2(_M_,_E_) with compact resolvent (e.g. the [signature operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Signature_operator "Signature operator")), such that the commutators [_D_,_f_] are bounded whenever _f_ is smooth. A deep theorem[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noncommutative_geometry#cite_note-5) states that _M_ as a Riemannian manifold can be recovered from this data.

This suggests that one might define a noncommutative Riemannian manifold as a [spectral triple](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectral_triple "Spectral triple") (_A_,_H_,_D_), consisting of a representation of a C*-algebra _A_ on a Hilbert space _H_, together with an unbounded operator _D_ on _H_, with compact resolvent, such that [_D_,_a_] is bounded for all _a_ in some dense subalgebra of _A_. Research in spectral triples is very active, and many examples of noncommutative manifolds have been constructed.

Noncommutative affine and projective schemes
--------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Noncommutative_geometry&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Noncommutative affine and projective schemes")]

In analogy to the [duality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duality_(mathematics) "Duality (mathematics)") between [affine schemes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_scheme "Affine scheme") and [commutative rings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_ring "Commutative ring"), we define a category of **noncommutative affine schemes** as the dual of the category of associative unital rings. There are certain analogues of Zariski topology in that context so that one can glue such affine schemes to more general objects.

There are also generalizations of the Cone and of the Proj of a commutative graded ring, mimicking a theorem of [Serre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean-Pierre_Serre "Jean-Pierre Serre") on Proj. Namely the category of quasicoherent sheaves of O-modules on a Proj of a commutative graded algebra is equivalent to the category of graded modules over the ring localized on Serre's subcategory of graded modules of finite length; there is also analogous theorem for coherent sheaves when the algebra is Noetherian. This theorem is extended as a definition of **noncommutative projective geometry** by [Michael Artin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michael_Artin "Michael Artin") and J. J. Zhang,[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noncommutative_geometry#cite_note-6) who add also some general ring-theoretic conditions (e.g. Artin–Schelter regularity).

Many properties of projective schemes extend to this context. For example, there exists an analog of the celebrated [Serre duality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Serre_duality "Serre duality") for noncommutative projective schemes of Artin and Zhang.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noncommutative_geometry#cite_note-7)

A. L. Rosenberg has created a rather general relative concept of **noncommutative quasicompact scheme** (over a base category), abstracting Grothendieck's study of morphisms of schemes and covers in terms of categories of quasicoherent sheaves and flat localization functors.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noncommutative_geometry#cite_note-8) There is also another interesting approach via localization theory, due to [Fred Van Oystaeyen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fred_Van_Oystaeyen "Fred Van Oystaeyen"), Luc Willaert and Alain Verschoren, where the main concept is that of a **schematic algebra**.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noncommutative_geometry#cite_note-9)[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noncommutative_geometry#cite_note-10)

Invariants for noncommutative spaces
------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Noncommutative_geometry&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Invariants for noncommutative spaces")]

Some of the motivating questions of the theory are concerned with extending known [topological invariants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_invariant "Topological invariant") to formal duals of noncommutative (operator) algebras and other replacements and candidates for noncommutative spaces. One of the main starting points of [Alain Connes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alain_Connes "Alain Connes")' direction in noncommutative geometry is his discovery of a new homology theory associated to noncommutative associative algebras and noncommutative operator algebras, namely the [cyclic homology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclic_homology "Cyclic homology") and its relations to the [algebraic K-theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_K-theory "Algebraic K-theory") (primarily via Connes–Chern character map).

The theory of [characteristic classes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Characteristic_classes "Characteristic classes") of smooth manifolds has been extended to spectral triples, employing the tools of operator [K-theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K-theory "K-theory") and [cyclic cohomology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclic_cohomology "Cyclic cohomology"). Several generalizations of now-classical [index theorems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Index_theorem "Index theorem") allow for effective extraction of numerical invariants from spectral triples. The fundamental characteristic class in cyclic cohomology, the [JLO cocycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/JLO_cocycle "JLO cocycle"), generalizes the classical [Chern character](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chern_character "Chern character").

Examples of noncommutative spaces
---------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Noncommutative_geometry&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Examples of noncommutative spaces")]

*   In the [phase space formulation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phase_space_formulation "Phase space formulation") of quantum mechanics, the [symplectic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symplectic_manifold "Symplectic manifold")[phase space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phase_space "Phase space") of [classical mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamiltonian_mechanics "Hamiltonian mechanics") is [deformed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deformation_quantization "Deformation quantization") into a non-commutative phase space generated by the [position and momentum operators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heisenberg_group "Heisenberg group").
*   The [noncommutative torus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noncommutative_torus "Noncommutative torus"), deformation of the function algebra of the ordinary torus, can be given the structure of a spectral triple. This class of examples has been studied intensively and still functions as a test case for more complicated situations.
*   Snyder space[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noncommutative_geometry#cite_note-11)
*   Noncommutative algebras arising from [foliations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foliation "Foliation").
*   Examples related to [dynamical systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynamical_systems "Dynamical systems") arising from [number theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_theory "Number theory"), such as the [Gauss shift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continued_fractions#Continued_fractions_and_dynamical_systems "Continued fractions") on continued fractions, give rise to noncommutative algebras that appear to have interesting noncommutative geometries.
