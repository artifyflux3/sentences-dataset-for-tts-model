In [classical mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_mechanics "Classical mechanics"), **Euler's rotation equations** are a vectorial quasilinear [first-order ordinary differential equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First-order_ordinary_differential_equation "First-order ordinary differential equation") describing the rotation of a [rigid body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rigid_body "Rigid body"), using a [rotating reference frame](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotating_reference_frame "Rotating reference frame") with [angular velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_velocity "Angular velocity") ω whose axes are fixed to the body. They are named in honour of [Leonhard Euler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leonhard_Euler "Leonhard Euler").

In the absence of applied [torques](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torque "Torque"), one obtains the [Euler top](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler_top "Euler top"). When the torques are due to [gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity "Gravity"), there are special cases when the motion of the top is [integrable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrange_top "Lagrange top").

Their general vector form is

![Image 1: {\displaystyle \mathbf {I} {\dot {\boldsymbol {\omega }}}+{\boldsymbol {\omega }}\times \left(\mathbf {I} {\boldsymbol {\omega }}\right)=\mathbf {M} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e6461dca1b1acca42cfc50177fbbe29395537bdd)
where _M_ is the applied [torques](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torque "Torque") and _I_ is the [inertia matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_of_inertia "Moment of inertia"). The vector ![Image 2: {\displaystyle {\dot {\boldsymbol {\omega }}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/07291380f0c7f97fdc8cd25136a36c77caeecf6e) is the [angular acceleration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_acceleration "Angular acceleration"). Again, note that all quantities are defined in the rotating reference frame.

In [orthogonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_coordinates "Orthogonal coordinates") principal axes of inertia coordinates the equations become

![Image 3: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}I_{1}\,{\dot {\omega }}_{1}+(I_{3}-I_{2})\,\omega _{2}\,\omega _{3}&=M_{1}\\I_{2}\,{\dot {\omega }}_{2}+(I_{1}-I_{3})\,\omega _{3}\,\omega _{1}&=M_{2}\\I_{3}\,{\dot {\omega }}_{3}+(I_{2}-I_{1})\,\omega _{1}\,\omega _{2}&=M_{3}\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5f5e69a3fb3e72238b197705bb307f58e4a1ab71)
where _M k_ are the components of the applied torques, _I k_ are the [principal moments of inertia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_of_inertia "Moment of inertia") and ω _k_ are the components of the angular velocity.

In an [inertial frame of reference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inertial_frame_of_reference "Inertial frame of reference") (subscripted "in"), [Euler's second law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Balance_of_angular_momentum "Balance of angular momentum") states that the [time derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_derivative "Time derivative") of the [angular momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum "Angular momentum")**L** equals the applied [torque](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torque "Torque"):

![Image 4: {\displaystyle {\frac {d\mathbf {L} _{\text{in}}}{dt}}=\mathbf {M} _{\text{in}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/df61ede40b074af3aecb463e6f464d1a847cd8c4)
For point particles such that the internal forces are [central forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_forces "Central forces"), this may be derived using [Newton's second law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_second_law "Newton's second law"). For a rigid body, one has the relation between angular momentum and the [moment of inertia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_of_inertia "Moment of inertia")**I**in given as

![Image 5: {\displaystyle \mathbf {L} _{\text{in}}=\mathbf {I} _{\text{in}}{\boldsymbol {\omega }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f03407a1a32d65fe52e5c21dd1dd4870b02a048a)
In the inertial frame, the differential equation is not always helpful in solving for the motion of a general rotating rigid body, as both **I**in and **ω** can change during the motion. One may instead change to a coordinate frame fixed in the rotating body, in which the moment of inertia tensor is constant. Using a reference frame such as that at the center of mass, the frame's position drops out of the equations. In any rotating reference frame, the time derivative must be replaced so that the equation becomes

![Image 6: {\displaystyle \left({\frac {d\mathbf {L} }{dt}}\right)_{\mathrm {rot} }+{\boldsymbol {\omega }}\times \mathbf {L} =\mathbf {M} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/014536b3f8c0e5327418f57cd8df4b572b89232f)
and so the cross product arises, see [time derivative in rotating reference frame](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotating_reference_frame#Time_derivatives_in_the_two_frames "Rotating reference frame"). The vector components of the torque in the inertial and the rotating frames are related by ![Image 7: {\displaystyle \mathbf {M} _{\text{in}}=\mathbf {Q} \mathbf {M} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/52a2e467f9c7ae2000e3e7f26e37ce0b43f6f334) where ![Image 8: {\displaystyle \mathbf {Q} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132d0144479d6f47c30ad82a65d458966ccbe928) is the rotation tensor (not [rotation matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation_matrix "Rotation matrix")), an [orthogonal tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_matrix "Orthogonal matrix") related to the angular velocity vector by ![Image 9: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {\omega }}\times {\boldsymbol {u}}={\dot {\mathbf {Q} }}\mathbf {Q} ^{-1}{\boldsymbol {u}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/20d0d90f6d4a0e5ff9a399a5e55d38df5dedb8cc) for any vector **u**. Now ![Image 10: {\displaystyle \mathbf {L} =\mathbf {I} {\boldsymbol {\omega }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ecfa955e533da62f024884995efa0f522a6fa3f3) is substituted and the time derivatives are taken in the rotating frame, while realizing that the particle positions and the inertia tensor does not depend on time. This leads to the general vector form of Euler's equations which are valid in such a frame

![Image 11: {\displaystyle \mathbf {I} {\dot {\boldsymbol {\omega }}}+{\boldsymbol {\omega }}\times \left(\mathbf {I} {\boldsymbol {\omega }}\right)=\mathbf {M} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e6461dca1b1acca42cfc50177fbbe29395537bdd)
The equations are also derived from Newton's laws in the discussion of the [resultant torque](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_of_inertia#Resultant_torque "Moment of inertia").

More generally, by the tensor transform rules, any rank-2 tensor ![Image 12: {\displaystyle \mathbf {T} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9593e3b995a1b57c078873a5ea186c7012e1a5ee) has a time-derivative ![Image 13: {\displaystyle \mathbf {\dot {T}} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7e102c28188ceff3121d707f73b0b3368d4d3790) such that for any vector ![Image 14: {\displaystyle \mathbf {u} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/261e20fe101de02a771021d9d4466c0ad3e352d7), one has ![Image 15: {\displaystyle \mathbf {\dot {T}} \mathbf {u} ={\boldsymbol {\omega }}\times (\mathbf {T} \mathbf {u} )-\mathbf {T} ({\boldsymbol {\omega }}\times \mathbf {u} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d647b94b83a93e9a6615d4afa8bc0b71154f0bf3). This yields the Euler's equations by plugging in ![Image 16: {\displaystyle {\frac {d}{dt}}\left(\mathbf {I} {\boldsymbol {\omega }}\right)=\mathbf {M} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/20e822d2ce7b199abaaead68306c97684de05897)

### Principal axes form

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Euler%27s_equations_(rigid_body_dynamics)&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Principal axes form")]

When choosing a frame so that its axes are aligned with the principal axes of the inertia tensor, its component matrix is diagonal, which further simplifies calculations. As described in the [moment of inertia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_of_inertia "Moment of inertia") article, the angular momentum **L** can then be written

![Image 17: {\displaystyle \mathbf {L} =L_{1}\mathbf {e} _{1}+L_{2}\mathbf {e} _{2}+L_{3}\mathbf {e} _{3}=\sum _{i=1}^{3}I_{i}\omega _{i}\mathbf {e} _{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3c8b8b5ea847296f3ed4c4cc6194728b7f7cc6c2)
Also in some frames not tied to the body can it be possible to obtain such simple (diagonal tensor) equations for the rate of change of the angular momentum. Then **ω** must be the angular velocity for rotation of that frames axes instead of the rotation of the body. It is however still required that the chosen axes are still principal axes of inertia. The resulting form of the Euler rotation equations is useful for rotation-symmetric objects that allow some of the principal axes of rotation to be chosen freely.

Special case solutions
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Euler%27s_equations_(rigid_body_dynamics)&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Special case solutions")]

### Torque-free precessions

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Euler%27s_equations_(rigid_body_dynamics)&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Torque-free precessions")]

Torque-free [precessions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Precession "Precession") are non-trivial solution for the situation where the torque on the [right hand side](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Left-hand_side_and_right-hand_side_of_an_equation "Left-hand side and right-hand side of an equation") is zero. When **I** is not constant in the external reference frame (i.e. the body is moving and its inertia tensor is not constantly diagonal) then **I** cannot be pulled through the [derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative "Derivative") operator acting on **L**. In this case **I**(_t_) and **ω**(_t_) do change together in such a way that the derivative of their product is still zero. This motion can be visualized by [Poinsot's construction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poinsot%27s_construction "Poinsot's construction").

Generalized Euler equations
---------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Euler%27s_equations_(rigid_body_dynamics)&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Generalized Euler equations")]

The Euler equations can be generalized to any [simple Lie algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simple_Lie_algebra "Simple Lie algebra").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler's_equations_(rigid_body_dynamics)#cite_note-HSW-1) The original Euler equations come from fixing the Lie algebra to be ![Image 18: {\displaystyle {\mathfrak {so}}(3)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fb4f1d3d3bf3da64b92af1a1018ce00545808b9d), with generators ![Image 19: {\displaystyle {t_{1},t_{2},t_{3}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9b0b456a61ba8b3dc9b531c4fd5573bf33e7ef45) satisfying the relation ![Image 20: {\displaystyle [t_{a},t_{b}]=\epsilon _{abc}t_{c}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/140ff9df94fe2d16ed0bbc878beb138a613e74d7). Then if ![Image 21: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {\omega }}(t)=\sum _{a}\omega _{a}(t)t_{a}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/36b087339a8c7f37a54d3b9af23f067a41fa45f3) (where ![Image 22: {\displaystyle t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/65658b7b223af9e1acc877d848888ecdb4466560) is a time coordinate, not to be confused with basis vectors ![Image 23: {\displaystyle t_{a}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0c1f89579c6b54711128de7bad0f0f760e116180)) is an ![Image 24: {\displaystyle {\mathfrak {so}}(3)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fb4f1d3d3bf3da64b92af1a1018ce00545808b9d)-valued function of time, and ![Image 25: {\displaystyle \mathbf {I} =\mathrm {diag} (I_{1},I_{2},I_{3})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/405f049a1643d7ecdb07468b78a0bcbf6f3cb695) (with respect to the Lie algebra basis), then the (untorqued) original Euler equations can be written[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler's_equations_(rigid_body_dynamics)#cite_note-2)![Image 26: {\displaystyle \mathbf {I} {\dot {\boldsymbol {\omega }}}=[\mathbf {I} {\boldsymbol {\omega }},{\boldsymbol {\omega }}].}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/53098b3fd3b4f5f28210e604f325fa9cc8452fed) To define ![Image 27: {\displaystyle \mathbf {I} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8a458c8aeb096ce732abf346ae8edf3e4f53a126) in a basis-independent way, it must be a self-adjoint map on the Lie algebra ![Image 28: {\displaystyle {\mathfrak {g}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/40a913b1503ed9ec94361b99f7fd59ef60705c28) with respect to the [invariant bilinear form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Killing_form "Killing form") on ![Image 29: {\displaystyle {\mathfrak {g}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/40a913b1503ed9ec94361b99f7fd59ef60705c28). This expression generalizes readily to an arbitrary simple Lie algebra, say in the standard classification of simple Lie algebras.

This can also be viewed as a [Lax pair](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lax_pair "Lax pair") formulation of the generalized Euler equations, suggesting their integrability.
