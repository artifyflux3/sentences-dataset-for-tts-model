In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), an **embedding** (or **imbedding**[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Embedding_(mathematics)#cite_note-1)) is one instance of some [mathematical structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_structure "Mathematical structure") contained within another instance, such as a [group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_(mathematics) "Group (mathematics)") that is a [subgroup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subgroup "Subgroup").

When some object ![Image 1: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) is said to be embedded in another object ![Image 2: {\displaystyle Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/961d67d6b454b4df2301ac571808a3538b3a6d3f), the embedding is given by some [injective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Injective_function "Injective function") and structure-preserving map ![Image 3: {\displaystyle f:X\rightarrow Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b215af1e965d0595a97ad2b21f7d0cbcf6281303). The precise meaning of "structure-preserving" depends on the kind of mathematical structure of which ![Image 4: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) and ![Image 5: {\displaystyle Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/961d67d6b454b4df2301ac571808a3538b3a6d3f) are instances. In the terminology of [category theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category_theory "Category theory"), a structure-preserving map is called a [morphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morphism "Morphism").

The fact that a map ![Image 6: {\displaystyle f:X\rightarrow Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b215af1e965d0595a97ad2b21f7d0cbcf6281303) is an embedding is often indicated by the use of a "hooked arrow" ([U+](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unicode "Unicode")21AAâ†ªRIGHTWARDS ARROW WITH HOOK);[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Embedding_(mathematics)#cite_note-Unicode_Arrows-2) thus: ![Image 7: {\displaystyle f:X\hookrightarrow Y.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b167d232a5c543e1cf462c921ecec9e5a404cf63) (On the other hand, this notation is sometimes reserved for [inclusion maps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inclusion_map "Inclusion map").)

Given ![Image 8: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) and ![Image 9: {\displaystyle Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/961d67d6b454b4df2301ac571808a3538b3a6d3f), several different embeddings of ![Image 10: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) in ![Image 11: {\displaystyle Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/961d67d6b454b4df2301ac571808a3538b3a6d3f) may be possible. In many cases of interest there is a standard (or "canonical") embedding, like those of the [natural numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number "Natural number") in the [integers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer"), the integers in the [rational numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_number "Rational number"), the rational numbers in the [real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number"), and the real numbers in the [complex numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number"). In such cases it is common to identify the [domain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_of_a_function "Domain of a function")![Image 12: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) with its [image](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Image_(mathematics) "Image (mathematics)")![Image 13: {\displaystyle f(X)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b884e2d65b3356219702968b6751485fb8f38570) contained in ![Image 14: {\displaystyle Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/961d67d6b454b4df2301ac571808a3538b3a6d3f), so that ![Image 15: {\displaystyle X\subseteq Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/894a17f6bbae8bb911fb08785e051b353e832ea1).

Topology and geometry
---------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Embedding&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Topology and geometry")]

In [general topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_topology "General topology"), an embedding is a [homeomorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homeomorphism "Homeomorphism") onto its image.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Embedding_(mathematics)#cite_note-3) More explicitly, an injective [continuous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_function_(topology) "Continuous function (topology)") map ![Image 16: {\displaystyle f:X\to Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/abd1e080abef4bbdab67b43819c6431e7561361c) between [topological spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_space "Topological space")![Image 17: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) and ![Image 18: {\displaystyle Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/961d67d6b454b4df2301ac571808a3538b3a6d3f) is a **topological embedding** if ![Image 19: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) yields a homeomorphism between ![Image 20: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) and ![Image 21: {\displaystyle f(X)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b884e2d65b3356219702968b6751485fb8f38570) (where ![Image 22: {\displaystyle f(X)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b884e2d65b3356219702968b6751485fb8f38570) carries the [subspace topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subspace_topology "Subspace topology") inherited from ![Image 23: {\displaystyle Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/961d67d6b454b4df2301ac571808a3538b3a6d3f)). Intuitively then, the embedding ![Image 24: {\displaystyle f:X\to Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/abd1e080abef4bbdab67b43819c6431e7561361c) lets us treat ![Image 25: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) as a [subspace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subspace_topology "Subspace topology") of ![Image 26: {\displaystyle Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/961d67d6b454b4df2301ac571808a3538b3a6d3f). Every embedding is injective and [continuous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_function_(topology) "Continuous function (topology)"). Every map that is injective, continuous and either [open](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_map "Open map") or [closed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_map "Closed map") is an embedding; however there are also embeddings that are neither open nor closed. The latter happens if the image ![Image 27: {\displaystyle f(X)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b884e2d65b3356219702968b6751485fb8f38570) is neither an [open set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_set "Open set") nor a [closed set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_set "Closed set") in ![Image 28: {\displaystyle Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/961d67d6b454b4df2301ac571808a3538b3a6d3f).

For a given space ![Image 29: {\displaystyle Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/961d67d6b454b4df2301ac571808a3538b3a6d3f), the existence of an embedding ![Image 30: {\displaystyle X\to Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/290b16963d52e4a7995aae01ee854b97a6ea10c0) is a [topological invariant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_invariant "Topological invariant") of ![Image 31: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab). This allows two spaces to be distinguished if one is able to be embedded in a space while the other is not.

If the domain of a function ![Image 32: {\displaystyle f:X\to Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/abd1e080abef4bbdab67b43819c6431e7561361c) is a [topological space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_space "Topological space") then the function is said to be _locally injective at a point_ if there exists some [neighborhood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neighbourhood_(mathematics) "Neighbourhood (mathematics)")![Image 33: {\displaystyle U}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/458a728f53b9a0274f059cd695e067c430956025) of this point such that the restriction ![Image 34: {\displaystyle f{\big \vert }_{U}:U\to Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7b3fb3431cf6465ec29420f73a6640c26ffb43f1) is injective. It is called _locally injective_ if it is locally injective around every point of its domain. Similarly, a _local (topological, resp. smooth) embedding_ is a function for which every point in its domain has some neighborhood to which its restriction is a (topological, resp. smooth) embedding.

Every injective function is locally injective but not conversely. [Local diffeomorphisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_diffeomorphism "Local diffeomorphism"), [local homeomorphisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_homeomorphism "Local homeomorphism"), and smooth [immersions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immersion_(mathematics) "Immersion (mathematics)") are all locally injective functions that are not necessarily injective. The [inverse function theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverse_function_theorem "Inverse function theorem") gives a sufficient condition for a continuously differentiable function to be (among other things) locally injective. Every [fiber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiber_(mathematics) "Fiber (mathematics)") of a locally injective function ![Image 35: {\displaystyle f:X\to Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/abd1e080abef4bbdab67b43819c6431e7561361c) is necessarily a [discrete subspace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discrete_space "Discrete space") of its [domain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_of_a_function "Domain of a function")![Image 36: {\displaystyle X.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5ba76c5a460c4a0bb1639a193bc1830f0a773e03)

### Differential topology

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Embedding&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Differential topology")]

In [differential topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_topology "Differential topology"): Let ![Image 37: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) and ![Image 38: {\displaystyle N}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5e3890c981ae85503089652feb48b191b57aae3) be smooth [manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manifold "Manifold") and ![Image 39: {\displaystyle f:M\to N}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1dbd50e2de9728ee14a7c232441137f588b109f6) be a smooth map. Then ![Image 40: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) is called an [immersion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immersion_(mathematics) "Immersion (mathematics)") if its [derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pushforward_(differential) "Pushforward (differential)") is everywhere injective. An **embedding**, or a **smooth embedding**, is defined to be an immersion that is an embedding in the topological sense mentioned above (i.e. [homeomorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homeomorphism "Homeomorphism") onto its image).[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Embedding_(mathematics)#cite_note-4)

In other words, the domain of an embedding is [diffeomorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffeomorphism "Diffeomorphism") to its image, and in particular the image of an embedding must be a [submanifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Submanifold "Submanifold"). An immersion is precisely a **local embedding**, i.e. for any point ![Image 41: {\displaystyle x\in M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9df57d73e9532bb93a1439890bcddbc2806f5859) there is a neighborhood ![Image 42: {\displaystyle x\in U\subset M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/246661f312bbffd5daba7c117684857f5e35abfe) such that ![Image 43: {\displaystyle f:U\to N}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9fb74261ef9cbf82c6b20b6f141b35d6805cb2d7) is an embedding.

When the domain manifold is compact, the notion of a smooth embedding is equivalent to that of an injective immersion.

An important case is ![Image 44: {\displaystyle N=\mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cd9085833545a3c03b03da0ba9589727d02a2d6d). The interest here is in how large ![Image 45: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) must be for an embedding, in terms of the dimension ![Image 46: {\displaystyle m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a07d98bb302f3856cbabc47b2b9016692e3f7bc) of ![Image 47: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd). The [Whitney embedding theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whitney_embedding_theorem "Whitney embedding theorem")[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Embedding_(mathematics)#cite_note-5) states that ![Image 48: {\displaystyle n=2m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d42d6f855a8c9c526b0bebd6a41389ca605c36c8) is enough, and is the best possible linear bound. For example, the [real projective space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_projective_space "Real projective space")![Image 49: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} \mathrm {P} ^{m}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/66b7d68c318b2728cada877e197de3616460741d) of dimension ![Image 50: {\displaystyle m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a07d98bb302f3856cbabc47b2b9016692e3f7bc), where ![Image 51: {\displaystyle m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a07d98bb302f3856cbabc47b2b9016692e3f7bc) is a power of two, requires ![Image 52: {\displaystyle n=2m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d42d6f855a8c9c526b0bebd6a41389ca605c36c8) for an embedding. However, this does not apply to immersions; for instance, ![Image 53: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} \mathrm {P} ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/33a38fcc9fd6f8ab0542458fa54793c578b427fc) can be immersed in ![Image 54: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5) as is explicitly shown by [Boy's surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boy%27s_surface "Boy's surface")â€”which has self-intersections. The [Roman surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_surface "Roman surface") fails to be an immersion as it contains [cross-caps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross-cap "Cross-cap").

An embedding is **proper** if it behaves well with respect to [boundaries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_manifold#Manifolds_with_boundary "Topological manifold"): one requires the map ![Image 55: {\displaystyle f:X\rightarrow Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b215af1e965d0595a97ad2b21f7d0cbcf6281303) to be such that

The first condition is equivalent to having ![Image 56: {\displaystyle f(\partial X)\subseteq \partial Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4778aa1d70d0b169139a3df06168d79410cad40c) and ![Image 57: {\displaystyle f(X\setminus \partial X)\subseteq Y\setminus \partial Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1eec59c0265afaa4747e86211a155e24bf5cddb2). The second condition, roughly speaking, says that ![Image 58: {\displaystyle f(X)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b884e2d65b3356219702968b6751485fb8f38570) is not tangent to the boundary of ![Image 59: {\displaystyle Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/961d67d6b454b4df2301ac571808a3538b3a6d3f).

### Riemannian and pseudo-Riemannian geometry

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Embedding&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Riemannian and pseudo-Riemannian geometry")]

In [Riemannian geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_geometry "Riemannian geometry") and pseudo-Riemannian geometry: Let ![Image 60: {\displaystyle (M,g)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68e27d2e539fd0c3a9a7efab6257abd17de7fc57) and ![Image 61: {\displaystyle (N,h)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/04f1af6aecfdb83f8f264cb0631896fa969081a2) be [Riemannian manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_manifold "Riemannian manifold") or more generally [pseudo-Riemannian manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudo-Riemannian_manifold "Pseudo-Riemannian manifold"). An **isometric embedding** is a smooth embedding ![Image 62: {\displaystyle f:M\rightarrow N}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c6cd11d4656ac58a9d4c9ef09866d91950f6cc7a) that preserves the (pseudo-)[metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_metric "Riemannian metric") in the sense that ![Image 63: {\displaystyle g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3556280e66fe2c0d0140df20935a6f057381d77) is equal to the [pullback](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pullback_(differential_geometry) "Pullback (differential geometry)") of ![Image 64: {\displaystyle h}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b26be3e694314bc90c3215047e4a2010c6ee184a) by ![Image 65: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61), i.e. ![Image 66: {\displaystyle g=f^{*}h}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c219c62031a34374151b1801b606032dedbf358d). Explicitly, for any two tangent vectors ![Image 67: {\displaystyle v,w\in T_{x}(M)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e8dfbcadf8a81dcf7c164a59d3b66d68fabdb2a8) we have

![Image 68: {\displaystyle g(v,w)=h(df(v),df(w)).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cdfef850b0bede889b5341a4e0975e35c7393026)
Analogously, **isometric immersion** is an immersion between (pseudo)-Riemannian manifolds that preserves the (pseudo)-Riemannian metrics.

Equivalently, in Riemannian geometry, an isometric embedding (immersion) is a smooth embedding (immersion) that preserves length of [curves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curve "Curve") (cf. [Nash embedding theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nash_embedding_theorem "Nash embedding theorem")).[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Embedding_(mathematics)#cite_note-6)

In general, for an [algebraic category](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variety_(universal_algebra) "Variety (universal algebra)")![Image 69: {\displaystyle C}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4fc55753007cd3c18576f7933f6f089196732029), an embedding between two ![Image 70: {\displaystyle C}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4fc55753007cd3c18576f7933f6f089196732029)-algebraic structures ![Image 71: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) and ![Image 72: {\displaystyle Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/961d67d6b454b4df2301ac571808a3538b3a6d3f) is a ![Image 73: {\displaystyle C}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4fc55753007cd3c18576f7933f6f089196732029)-morphism ![Image 74: {\displaystyle e:X\rightarrow Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c2e8099bebd692aec622bb2de03c2b0c24ad0fc6) that is injective.

In [field theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_theory_(mathematics) "Field theory (mathematics)"), an **embedding** of a [field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)")![Image 75: {\displaystyle E}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4232c9de2ee3eec0a9c0a19b15ab92daa6223f9b) in a field ![Image 76: {\displaystyle F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/545fd099af8541605f7ee55f08225526be88ce57) is a [ring homomorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ring_homomorphism "Ring homomorphism")![Image 77: {\displaystyle \sigma :E\rightarrow F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5127ee35bdf917745de10e84edd3cf572a73c335).

The [kernel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kernel_(algebra) "Kernel (algebra)") of ![Image 78: {\displaystyle \sigma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/59f59b7c3e6fdb1d0365a494b81fb9a696138c36) is an [ideal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ideal_(ring_theory) "Ideal (ring theory)") of ![Image 79: {\displaystyle E}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4232c9de2ee3eec0a9c0a19b15ab92daa6223f9b), which cannot be the whole field ![Image 80: {\displaystyle E}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4232c9de2ee3eec0a9c0a19b15ab92daa6223f9b), because of the condition ![Image 81: {\displaystyle 1=\sigma (1)=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af78bb8e0d0ba2cd28d5af0d354a2c524373a8cf). Furthermore, any field has as ideals only the zero ideal and the whole field itself (because if there is any non-zero field element in an ideal, it is invertible, showing the ideal is the whole field). Therefore, the kernel is ![Image 82: {\displaystyle 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2aae8864a3c1fec9585261791a809ddec1489950), so any embedding of fields is a [monomorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monomorphism "Monomorphism"). Hence, ![Image 83: {\displaystyle E}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4232c9de2ee3eec0a9c0a19b15ab92daa6223f9b) is [isomorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isomorphic "Isomorphic") to the [subfield](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_extension "Field extension")![Image 84: {\displaystyle \sigma (E)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/625126d54f51953d556e435389f18908bdd5bbd8) of ![Image 85: {\displaystyle F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/545fd099af8541605f7ee55f08225526be88ce57). This justifies the name _embedding_ for an arbitrary homomorphism of fields.

### Universal algebra and model theory

Order theory and domain theory
------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Embedding&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Order theory and domain theory")]

In [order theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Order_theory "Order theory"), an embedding of [partially ordered sets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partially_ordered_set "Partially ordered set") is a function ![Image 94: {\displaystyle F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/545fd099af8541605f7ee55f08225526be88ce57) between partially ordered sets ![Image 95: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) and ![Image 96: {\displaystyle Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/961d67d6b454b4df2301ac571808a3538b3a6d3f) such that

![Image 97: {\displaystyle \forall x_{1},x_{2}\in X:x_{1}\leq x_{2}\iff F(x_{1})\leq F(x_{2}).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/da56e4d9ddfe8ee13b8a605c6641e4acb834e7bd)
Injectivity of ![Image 98: {\displaystyle F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/545fd099af8541605f7ee55f08225526be88ce57) follows quickly from this definition. In [domain theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_theory "Domain theory"), an additional requirement is that

![Image 99: {\displaystyle \forall y\in Y:\{x\mid F(x)\leq y\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/116dec0e81aeb86e60d918697f58851207f1b351) is [directed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Directed_set "Directed set").
A mapping ![Image 100: {\displaystyle \phi :X\to Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b98600ef34bb9c70ab402e2f46de411a750ca3ab) of [metric spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_spaces "Metric spaces") is called an _embedding_ (with [distortion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stretch_factor "Stretch factor")![Image 101: {\displaystyle C>0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c84d4126c6df243734f9355927c026df6b0d3859)) if

![Image 102: {\displaystyle Ld_{X}(x,y)\leq d_{Y}(\phi (x),\phi (y))\leq CLd_{X}(x,y)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eae7e7d0455530cc0e2a05dcb81842d145ddc3aa)
for every ![Image 103: {\displaystyle x,y\in X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6d72f66ab332ed430aa9b34ff18c9723c4fea2a1) and some constant ![Image 104: {\displaystyle L>0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/78d06bfbe00ff463870f868c958b37cbe46ea3a0).

An important special case is that of [normed spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normed_spaces "Normed spaces"); in this case it is natural to consider linear embeddings.

One of the basic questions that can be asked about a finite-dimensional [normed space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normed_space "Normed space")![Image 105: {\displaystyle (X,\|\cdot \|)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/258512fa727a69eec5255c3049ca4f999abd74ab) is, _what is the maximal dimension ![Image 106: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest\_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40) such that the [Hilbert space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert\_space "Hilbert space")![Image 107: {\displaystyle \ell \_{2}^{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest\_v1/media/math/render/svg/85d29620fc91374946bd0b2e171367e6cb6059b6) can be linearly embedded into ![Image 108: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest\_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) with constant distortion?_

The answer is given by [Dvoretzky's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dvoretzky%27s_theorem "Dvoretzky's theorem").

In [category theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category_theory "Category theory"), there is no satisfactory and generally accepted definition of embeddings that is applicable in all categories. One would expect that all isomorphisms and all compositions of embeddings are embeddings, and that all embeddings are monomorphisms. Other typical requirements are: any [extremal monomorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monomorphism#Related_concepts "Monomorphism") is an embedding and embeddings are stable under [pullbacks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pullback_(category_theory) "Pullback (category theory)").

Ideally the class of all embedded [subobjects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subobject "Subobject") of a given object, up to isomorphism, should also be [small](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Small_class "Small class"), and thus an [ordered set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordered_set "Ordered set"). In this case, the category is said to be well powered with respect to the class of embeddings. This allows defining new local structures in the category (such as a [closure operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closure_operator "Closure operator")).

In a [concrete category](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concrete_category "Concrete category"), an **embedding** is a morphism ![Image 109: {\displaystyle f:A\rightarrow B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/aa2fb4d5e9d282ee5442719053c46ad1ad96f2ca) that is an injective function from the underlying set of ![Image 110: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3) to the underlying set of ![Image 111: {\displaystyle B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47136aad860d145f75f3eed3022df827cee94d7a) and is also an **initial morphism** in the following sense: If ![Image 112: {\displaystyle g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3556280e66fe2c0d0140df20935a6f057381d77) is a function from the underlying set of an object ![Image 113: {\displaystyle C}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4fc55753007cd3c18576f7933f6f089196732029) to the underlying set of ![Image 114: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3), and if its composition with ![Image 115: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) is a morphism ![Image 116: {\displaystyle fg:C\rightarrow B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a9161843f4cbfa1dd884b4d2f52eeef787861600), then ![Image 117: {\displaystyle g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3556280e66fe2c0d0140df20935a6f057381d77) itself is a morphism.

A [factorization system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Factorization_system "Factorization system") for a category also gives rise to a notion of embedding. If ![Image 118: {\displaystyle (E,M)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/89ff7b091e376fbd4234eec2f5ebdc1a59783845) is a factorization system, then the morphisms in ![Image 119: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) may be regarded as the embeddings, especially when the category is well powered with respect to ![Image 120: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd). Concrete theories often have a factorization system in which ![Image 121: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) consists of the embeddings in the previous sense. This is the case of the majority of the examples given in this article.

As usual in category theory, there is a [dual](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_(category_theory) "Dual (category theory)") concept, known as quotient. All the preceding properties can be dualized.

An embedding can also refer to an [embedding functor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subcategory#Embeddings "Subcategory").
