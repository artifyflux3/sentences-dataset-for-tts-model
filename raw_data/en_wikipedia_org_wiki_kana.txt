_**Kana**_ ([ä»®å](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E4%BB%AE%E5%90%8D "wikt:ä»®å"); Japanese pronunciation:[[ka.na]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/Japanese "Help:IPA/Japanese")) are [syllabaries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syllabary "Syllabary") used to write [Japanese phonological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japanese_phonology "Japanese phonology") units, [morae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mora_(linguistics) "Mora (linguistics)"). In current usage, _kana_ most commonly refers to _[hiragana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hiragana "Hiragana")_[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kana#cite_note-1) and _[katakana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Katakana "Katakana")_. It can also refer to their ancestor _**magana**_ (çœŸä»®å; lit. 'true kana'),[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kana#cite_note-daijirin-2) which were [Chinese characters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kanji "Kanji") used phonetically to transcribe [Japanese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japanese_language "Japanese language") (e.g. _[man'yÅgana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Man%27y%C5%8Dgana "Man'yÅgana")_); and _[hentaigana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hentaigana "Hentaigana")_, which are historical variants of the now-standard hiragana.

Katakana, with a few additions, are also used to write [Ainu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ainu_language "Ainu language"). A [number of systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Okinawan_scripts "Okinawan scripts") exist to write the [RyÅ«kyÅ«an languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ry%C5%ABky%C5%ABan_languages "RyÅ«kyÅ«an languages"), in particular [Okinawan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Okinawan_language "Okinawan language"), in hiragana. [Taiwanese kana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwanese_kana "Taiwanese kana") were used in [Taiwanese Hokkien](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwanese_Hokkien "Taiwanese Hokkien") as [ruby text](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ruby_text "Ruby text") for Chinese characters in [Taiwan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan "Taiwan") when it was [under Japanese rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan_under_Japanese_rule "Taiwan under Japanese rule").

Each [kana character](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syllabogram "Syllabogram") corresponds to one phoneme or syllable, unlike [kanji](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kanji "Kanji"), which generally each [corresponds to a morpheme](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logogram "Logogram"). Apart from the five vowels, it is always CV (consonant [onset](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syllable_onset "Syllable onset") with vowel [nucleus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syllable_nucleus "Syllable nucleus")), such as _ka_, _ki_, _sa_, _shi_, etc., with the sole exception of the C grapheme for [nasal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nasal_consonant "Nasal consonant")[codas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syllable_coda "Syllable coda") usually romanised as _n_. The structure has led some scholars to label the system _[moraic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mora\_(linguistics) "Mora (linguistics)")_, instead of _syllabic_, because it requires the combination of two syllabograms to represent a CVC syllable with coda (e.g. CV _n_, CV _m_, CV _ng_), a CVV syllable with complex nucleus (i.e. multiple or expressively long vowels), or a CCV syllable with complex onset (i.e. including a [glide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semivowel "Semivowel"), C _y_ V, C _w_ V).

The limited number of [phonemes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phoneme "Phoneme") in Japanese, as well as the relatively rigid syllable structure, makes the kana system a very accurate representation of [spoken Japanese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japanese_phonology "Japanese phonology").

_Kana_ is a compound of _kari_ (ä»®; lit.'borrowed' or 'assumed' or 'false') and _na_ (å; lit.'name'), which eventually collapsed into _kanna_ and ultimately _kana_.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kana#cite_note-daijirin-2)_Kana_ were so called in contrast with _mana_ (çœŸå; lit.'true name') which were kanji used "regularly" (kanji used for their meanings as they are now), or more specifically the [regular script](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_script "Regular script") (æ¥·æ›¸, _kaisho_) writing of such kanji.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kana#cite_note-daijirin-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kana#cite_note-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kana#cite_note-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kana#cite_note-5)

It was not until the 18th century that the early-nationalist _[kokugaku](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kokugaku "Kokugaku")_ movement, which promoted a move away from [Sinocentric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sinocentrism "Sinocentrism") academia, began to reanalyze the script from a phonological point of view.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kana#cite_note-6) In the following centuries, contrary to the traditional Sinocentric view, _kana_ began to be considered a national Japanese writing system that was distinct from Chinese characters, which is the dominant view today.

The difference in usage between hiragana and katakana is stylistic. Usually, hiragana is the default syllabary, and katakana is used in certain special cases. Hiragana is used to write native Japanese words with no [kanji](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kanji "Kanji") representation (or whose kanji is thought obscure or difficult), as well as grammatical elements such as [particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japanese_particles "Japanese particles") and inflections ([okurigana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Okurigana "Okurigana")). Today katakana is most commonly used to write words of foreign origin that do not have kanji representations, as well as foreign personal and place names. Katakana is also used to represent [onomatopoeia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Onomatopoeia "Onomatopoeia") and interjections, emphasis, technical and scientific terms, transcriptions of the [Sino-Japanese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sino-Japanese_vocabulary "Sino-Japanese vocabulary") readings of kanji, and some corporate branding.

Kana can be written in small form above or next to lesser-known kanji in order to show pronunciation; this is called [furigana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Furigana "Furigana"). Furigana is used most widely in children's or learners' books. Literature for young children who do not yet know kanji may dispense with it altogether and instead use hiragana combined with spaces.

Systems supporting only a limited set of characters, such as [Wabun code](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wabun_code "Wabun code") for [Morse code](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morse_code "Morse code") telegrams and [single-byte](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SBCS "SBCS") digital [character encodings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Character_encoding "Character encoding") such as [JIS X 0201](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/JIS_X_0201 "JIS X 0201") or [EBCDIK](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japanese_language_in_EBCDIC#Single-byte_codes "Japanese language in EBCDIC"), likewise dispense with kanji, instead using only katakana. This is not necessary in systems supporting [double-byte](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double-byte_character_set "Double-byte character set") or [variable-width](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variable-width_encoding "Variable-width encoding") encodings such as [Shift JIS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shift_JIS "Shift JIS"), [EUC-JP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/EUC-JP "EUC-JP"), [UTF-8](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UTF-8 "UTF-8") or [UTF-16](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UTF-16 "UTF-16").

Development of hiragana and katakana

Old Japanese was written entirely in kanji, and a set of kanji called _[man'yÅgana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Man%27y%C5%8Dgana "Man'yÅgana")_ were first used to represent the phonetic values of grammatical particles and morphemes. As there was no consistent method of sound representation, a phoneme could be represented by multiple kanji, and even those kana's pronunciations differed in whether they were to be read as _[kungana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kun%27yomi "Kun'yomi")_ (è¨“ä»®å, "meaning kana") or _[ongana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/On%27yomi "On'yomi")_ (éŸ³ä»®å, "sound kana"), making decipherment problematic. The _[man'yÅshÅ«](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Man%27y%C5%8Dsh%C5%AB "Man'yÅshÅ«")_, a poetry anthology assembled sometime after 759 and the eponym of _man'yÅgana_, exemplifies this phenomenon, where as many as almost twenty kanji were used for the mora _ka_. The consistency of the kana used was thus dependent on the style of the writer. [Hiragana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hiragana "Hiragana") developed as a distinct script from [cursive _man'yÅgana_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%C5%8Dgana "SÅgana"), whereas [katakana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Katakana "Katakana") developed from abbreviated parts of [regular script](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_script "Regular script")_man'yÅgana_ as a [glossing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gloss_(annotation) "Gloss (annotation)") system to add readings or explanations to Buddhist [sutras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sutra "Sutra"). Both of these systems were simplified to make writing easier. The shapes of many hiragana resembled the [Chinese cursive script](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cursive_script_(East_Asia) "Cursive script (East Asia)"), as did those of many katakana the Korean _[gugyeol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gugyeol "Gugyeol")_, suggesting that the Japanese followed the continental pattern of their neighbors.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kana#cite_note-Frellesvig-21)

Kana is traditionally said to have been invented by the [Buddhist priest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bhikkhu "Bhikkhu")[KÅ«kai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K%C5%ABkai "KÅ«kai") in the ninth century. KÅ«kai certainly brought the [Siddhaá¹ƒ script](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siddha%E1%B9%83_script "Siddhaá¹ƒ script") of India home on his return from [China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China "China") in 806;[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] his interest in the sacred aspects of [speech](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speech "Speech") and [writing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Writing "Writing") led him to the conclusion that Japanese would be better represented by a phonetic alphabet than by the kanji which had been used up to that point. The modern arrangement of kana reflects that of the [devanagari order](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Devanagari "Devanagari") used for [Sanskrit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sanskrit "Sanskrit") in the [Buddhist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buddhist "Buddhist")[Siddhaá¹ƒ script hybrid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siddha%E1%B9%83_script "Siddhaá¹ƒ script") known by the Japanese at the time,[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kana#cite_note-:0-22) before, the traditional _[iroha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iroha "Iroha")_ arrangement used to follow the order of a poem which uses each kana once.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] Kana's vowel (a-i-u-e-o) and consonant (k, s, t, n, h, m, y, r, w) order coincide with the Sanskrit order, except for the letter s, but that is explained by the properties of Old Japanese and the version of Siddham buddhist monks learnt at the time. However, the first time this order was used in Japanese was during the [Heian period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heian_period "Heian period") by MyÅkaku, a priest and Sanskrit scholar of the 12th century, member of the same order of KÅ«kai and credited as reviving Saskrit studies. In 1695, a priest named KeichÅ« published a 5 volume book that is considered to be fundamental in fixing the sound order to this day; and this work, in turn, was probably based on an earlier 3 volume systematic study of the grammar and the writing system of the Buddhist Hybrid Sanskrit, written by another priest, Kakugen, in 1681, also member of the order of KÅ«kai and MyÅkaku.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kana#cite_note-:0-22)

However, hiragana and katakana did not quickly supplant _man'yÅgana_. It was only in 1900 that the present set of kana was codified. Kana are the basis for [collation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collation "Collation") in Japanese. They are taken in the order given by the _[gojÅ«on](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goj%C5%ABon "GojÅ«on")_ (ã‚ ã„ ã† ãˆ ãŠ ... ã‚ ã‚’ ã‚“), though [iroha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iroha "Iroha") (ã„ ã‚ ã¯ ã« ã» ã¸ ã¨ ... ã› ã™ (ã‚“)) ordering is used for enumeration in some circumstances. Dictionaries differ in the sequence order for long/short vowel distinction, small _tsu_ and diacritics. As Japanese does not use word spaces (except as a tool for children), there can be no word-by-word collation; all collation is kana-by-kana.

The hiragana range in [Unicode](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unicode "Unicode") is U+3040 ... U+309F, and the katakana range is U+30A0 ... U+30FF. The obsolete and rare characters (_wi_ and _we_) also have their proper code points.

U+309E is the voiced hiragana iteration mark, which stands in for the previous hiragana but with the consonant voiced (_k_ becomes _g_, _h_ becomes _b_, etc.).
