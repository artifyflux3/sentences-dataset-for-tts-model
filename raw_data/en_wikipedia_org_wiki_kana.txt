_**Kana**_ ([仮名](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E4%BB%AE%E5%90%8D "wikt:仮名"); Japanese pronunciation:[[ka.na]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/Japanese "Help:IPA/Japanese")) are [syllabaries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syllabary "Syllabary") used to write [Japanese phonological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japanese_phonology "Japanese phonology") units, [morae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mora_(linguistics) "Mora (linguistics)"). In current usage, _kana_ most commonly refers to _[hiragana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hiragana "Hiragana")_[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kana#cite_note-1) and _[katakana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Katakana "Katakana")_. It can also refer to their ancestor _**magana**_ (真仮名; lit. 'true kana'),[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kana#cite_note-daijirin-2) which were [Chinese characters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kanji "Kanji") used phonetically to transcribe [Japanese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japanese_language "Japanese language") (e.g. _[man'yōgana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Man%27y%C5%8Dgana "Man'yōgana")_); and _[hentaigana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hentaigana "Hentaigana")_, which are historical variants of the now-standard hiragana.

Katakana, with a few additions, are also used to write [Ainu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ainu_language "Ainu language"). A [number of systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Okinawan_scripts "Okinawan scripts") exist to write the [Ryūkyūan languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ry%C5%ABky%C5%ABan_languages "Ryūkyūan languages"), in particular [Okinawan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Okinawan_language "Okinawan language"), in hiragana. [Taiwanese kana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwanese_kana "Taiwanese kana") were used in [Taiwanese Hokkien](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwanese_Hokkien "Taiwanese Hokkien") as [ruby text](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ruby_text "Ruby text") for Chinese characters in [Taiwan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan "Taiwan") when it was [under Japanese rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan_under_Japanese_rule "Taiwan under Japanese rule").

Each [kana character](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syllabogram "Syllabogram") corresponds to one phoneme or syllable, unlike [kanji](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kanji "Kanji"), which generally each [corresponds to a morpheme](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logogram "Logogram"). Apart from the five vowels, it is always CV (consonant [onset](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syllable_onset "Syllable onset") with vowel [nucleus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syllable_nucleus "Syllable nucleus")), such as _ka_, _ki_, _sa_, _shi_, etc., with the sole exception of the C grapheme for [nasal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nasal_consonant "Nasal consonant")[codas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syllable_coda "Syllable coda") usually romanised as _n_. The structure has led some scholars to label the system _[moraic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mora\_(linguistics) "Mora (linguistics)")_, instead of _syllabic_, because it requires the combination of two syllabograms to represent a CVC syllable with coda (e.g. CV _n_, CV _m_, CV _ng_), a CVV syllable with complex nucleus (i.e. multiple or expressively long vowels), or a CCV syllable with complex onset (i.e. including a [glide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semivowel "Semivowel"), C _y_ V, C _w_ V).

The limited number of [phonemes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phoneme "Phoneme") in Japanese, as well as the relatively rigid syllable structure, makes the kana system a very accurate representation of [spoken Japanese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japanese_phonology "Japanese phonology").

_Kana_ is a compound of _kari_ (仮; lit.'borrowed' or 'assumed' or 'false') and _na_ (名; lit.'name'), which eventually collapsed into _kanna_ and ultimately _kana_.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kana#cite_note-daijirin-2)_Kana_ were so called in contrast with _mana_ (真名; lit.'true name') which were kanji used "regularly" (kanji used for their meanings as they are now), or more specifically the [regular script](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_script "Regular script") (楷書, _kaisho_) writing of such kanji.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kana#cite_note-daijirin-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kana#cite_note-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kana#cite_note-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kana#cite_note-5)

It was not until the 18th century that the early-nationalist _[kokugaku](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kokugaku "Kokugaku")_ movement, which promoted a move away from [Sinocentric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sinocentrism "Sinocentrism") academia, began to reanalyze the script from a phonological point of view.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kana#cite_note-6) In the following centuries, contrary to the traditional Sinocentric view, _kana_ began to be considered a national Japanese writing system that was distinct from Chinese characters, which is the dominant view today.

Although the term 'kana' is now commonly understood as hiragana and katakana, it actually has broader application as listed below:[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kana#cite_note-daijirin-2)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kana#cite_note-7)

*   _Kana_ (仮名; [[ka.na]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/Japanese "Help:IPA/Japanese"), lit.'false name') or _kana_ (仮字; lit.'false character'): a [syllabary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syllabary "Syllabary"). 
    *   _Magana_ (真仮名; [[ma(ꜜ).ɡa.na,-ŋa.na]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/Japanese "Help:IPA/Japanese"), lit.'true kana') or _otokogana_ (男仮名; [[o.to.koꜜ.ɡa.na,-ŋa.na]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/Japanese "Help:IPA/Japanese"), lit.'men's kana'): phonetic [kanji](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kanji "Kanji") used as syllabary characters, historically used by men (who were more educated). 
        *   _[Man'yōgana/Mannyōgana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Man%27y%C5%8Dgana "Man'yōgana")_ (万葉仮名; [[maɰ̃.joː.ɡa.na,maɰ̃.joꜜː-,-ŋa.na]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/Japanese "Help:IPA/Japanese"), lit.'kana used in the _[Man'yōshū](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Man%27y%C5%8Dsh%C5%AB "Man'yōshū")_'): the most prominent system of magana. 
            *   _[Sōgana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%C5%8Dgana "Sōgana")_ (草仮名; [[soː.ɡa.na,-ŋa.na]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/Japanese "Help:IPA/Japanese"), lit.'[sloppy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cursive_script_(East_Asia) "Cursive script (East Asia)") kana'): cursive man'yōgana. 
                *   _[Hiragana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hiragana "Hiragana")_ (平仮名; [[çi.ɾa.ɡaꜜ.na,-ŋaꜜ.na,-ɡa.na(ꜜ),-ŋa.na(ꜜ)]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/Japanese "Help:IPA/Japanese"), lit.'flat kana'), _onnagana_ (女仮名; [[on.naꜜ.ɡa.na,-ŋa.na]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/Japanese "Help:IPA/Japanese"), lit.'women's kana'), _onnamoji_ (女文字; [[on.na.moꜜ.(d)ʑi]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/Japanese "Help:IPA/Japanese"), lit.'women's script'), _onnade_ (女手; [[on.na.de]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/Japanese "Help:IPA/Japanese"), lit.'women's hands') or _irohagana_ (伊呂波仮名; [[i.ɾo.haꜜ.ɡa.na,-ŋa.na]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/Japanese "Help:IPA/Japanese")): a syllabary derived from simplified sōgana, historically used by women (who were less educated), historically sorted in _[Iroha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iroha "Iroha")_ order. 
                    *   _[Hentaigana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hentaigana "Hentaigana")_ (変体仮名; [[hen.ta(ꜜ)i.ɡa.na,-ŋa.na]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/Japanese "Help:IPA/Japanese"), lit.'variant kana') or _itaigana_ (異体仮名; [[i.taꜜi.ɡa.na,-ŋa.na]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/Japanese "Help:IPA/Japanese")): obsolete variants of hiragana.

            *   _[Katakana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Katakana "Katakana")_ (片仮名; [[ka.ta.kaꜜ.na,-taꜜ.ka-]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/Japanese "Help:IPA/Japanese"), lit.'fragmented kana') or _gojūongana_ (五十音仮名; [[ɡo.(d)ʑɯː.oŋ.ɡa.na,-ŋa.na]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/Japanese "Help:IPA/Japanese"), lit.'fifty-sound kana'): a syllabary derived by using bits of characters in man'yōgana, historically sorted in _[gojūon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goj%C5%ABon "Gojūon")_ order.
            *   _Yamatogana_ (大和仮名; [[ja.ma.toꜜ.ɡa.na,-ŋa.na]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/Japanese "Help:IPA/Japanese"), lit.'[Yamato](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yamato_people "Yamato people")'s kana'): hiragana and katakana, as opposed to kanji.

        *   _Ongana_ (音仮名; [[oŋ.ɡa.na,-ŋa.na]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/Japanese "Help:IPA/Japanese"), lit.'sound kana'): magana for transcribing Japanese words, using, strict or loose, Chinese-derived readings (_[on'yomi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/On%27yomi "On'yomi")_). For example, _yama_ (山; lit.'mountain') would be spelt as 也末, with two magana with on'yomi for _ya_ and _ma_; likewise, _hito_ (人; lit.'human') spelt as 比登 for _hi_ and _to_.
        *   _Kungana_ (訓仮名; [[kɯŋ.ɡa.na,-ŋa.na]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/Japanese "Help:IPA/Japanese"), lit.'learned kana'): magana for transcribing Japanese words, using [native words](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wago "Wago") ascribed to kanji (native "readings" or _[kun'yomi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kun%27yomi "Kun'yomi")_). For example, _Yamato_ (大和) would be spelt as 八間跡, with three magana with kun'yomi for _ya_, _ma_ and _to_; likewise, _natsukashi_ (懐かし; lit.'evoking nostalgia') spelt as 夏樫 for _natsu_ and _kashi_.

*   _Mana_ (真名; [[maꜜ.na]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/Japanese "Help:IPA/Japanese"), lit.'true name'), _mana_ (真字; lit.'true character'), _otokomoji_ (男文字; [[o.to.ko.moꜜ.(d)ʑi]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/Japanese "Help:IPA/Japanese"), lit.'men's script') or _otokode_ (男手; [[o.to.ko.de]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/Japanese "Help:IPA/Japanese"), lit.'men's hands'): kanji used for meanings, historically used by men (who were more educated).
*   _Shinkatakana_ (真片仮名; [[ɕiŋ.ka.ta.kaꜜ.na]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/Japanese "Help:IPA/Japanese"), lit.'mana and katakana'): mixed script including only kanji and katakana.

*   There are presently no kana for _ye_, _yi_ or _wu_, as corresponding syllables do not occur natively in modern Japanese. 
    *   The [jɛ] (_ye_) sound is believed to have existed in pre-Classical Japanese, mostly before the advent of kana, and can be represented by the [man'yōgana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Man%27y%C5%8Dgana "Man'yōgana") kanji 江.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kana#cite_note-yeseely-8)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kana#cite_note-yeslj-9) There was an archaic Hiragana ([![Image 3: 𛀁](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Unicode_Japanese_Hiragana_Ye.svg/20px-Unicode_Japanese_Hiragana_Ye.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Unicode_Japanese_Hiragana_Ye.svg "𛀁"))[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kana#cite_note-katou-10) derived from the [man'yōgana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Man%27y%C5%8Dgana "Man'yōgana")_ye_ kanji 江,[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kana#cite_note-yeseely-8) which is encoded into Unicode at code point U+1B001 (𛀁),[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kana#cite_note-yeunicode-11)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kana#cite_note-12) but it is not widely supported. It is believed that _e_ and _ye_ first merged to _ye_ before shifting back to _e_ during the [Edo period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edo_period "Edo period").[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kana#cite_note-yeslj-9) As demonstrated by 17th century-era European sources, the syllable _we_ (ゑ・ヱ ) also came to be pronounced as [jɛ] (_ye_).[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kana#cite_note-13) If necessary, the modern orthography allows [je] (_ye_) to be written as いぇ (イェ),[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kana#cite_note-14) but this usage is limited and nonstandard.
    *   The modern Katakana _e_, エ, derives from the man'yōgana 江, originally pronounced _ye_;[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kana#cite_note-katou-10) a "Katakana letter Archaic E" ([![Image 4: 𛀀](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Unicode_Japanese_Katakana_Old_E.png/20px-Unicode_Japanese_Katakana_Old_E.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Unicode_Japanese_Katakana_Old_E.png "𛀀")) derived from the man'yōgana 衣 (_e_)[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kana#cite_note-katou-10) is encoded into Unicode at code point U+1B000 (𛀀),[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kana#cite_note-yeunicode-11) due to being used for that purpose in scholarly works on classical Japanese.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kana#cite_note-katou2-15)
    *   Some [gojūon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goj%C5%ABon "Gojūon") tables published during the 19th century list additional Katakana in the _ye_ ([![Image 5: 𛄡](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Katakana_obsolete_ye.svg/20px-Katakana_obsolete_ye.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Katakana_obsolete_ye.svg "𛄡")), _wu_ ([![Image 6: 𛄢](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Katakana_obsolete_wu.svg/20px-Katakana_obsolete_wu.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Katakana_obsolete_wu.svg "𛄢")) and _yi_ ([![Image 7: 𛄠](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Katakana_obsolete_yi.svg/20px-Katakana_obsolete_yi.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Katakana_obsolete_yi.svg "𛄠")) positions.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kana#cite_note-itikun-16) These are not presently used, and the latter two sounds never existed in Japanese.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kana#cite_note-yeslj-9)[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kana#cite_note-17) They were added to Unicode in version 14.0 in 2021.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kana#cite_note-18) These sources also list [![Image 8: 𛀆](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Hiragana_I_01.svg/20px-Hiragana_I_01.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Hiragana_I_01.svg "𛀆") (Unicode U+1B006, 𛀆) in the Hiragana _yi_ position, and [![Image 9: 𛀁](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Unicode_Japanese_Hiragana_Ye.svg/20px-Unicode_Japanese_Hiragana_Ye.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Unicode_Japanese_Hiragana_Ye.svg "𛀁") in the _ye_ position.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kana#cite_note-itikun-16)

*   Although removed from the standard orthography with the _gendai kanazukai_ reforms, _wi_ and _we_ still see stylistic use, as in ウヰスキー for _whisky_ and ヱビス or ゑびす for Japanese kami [Ebisu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ebisu_(mythology) "Ebisu (mythology)"), and [Yebisu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yebisu "Yebisu"), a brand of beer named after Ebisu. Hiragana _wi_ and _we_ are preserved in certain [Okinawan scripts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Okinawan_scripts "Okinawan scripts"), while katakana _wi_ and _we_ are preserved in the [Ainu language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ainu_language "Ainu language").
*   _wo_ is preserved only as the [accusative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accusative_case "Accusative case")[particle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japanese_particles "Japanese particles"), normally occurring only in hiragana.
*   _si_, _ti_, _tu_, _hu_, _wi_, _we_ and _wo_ are usually romanized respectively as _shi_, _chi_, _tsu_, _fu_, _i_, _e_ and _o_ instead, according to contemporary pronunciation.
*   the [sokuon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sokuon "Sokuon") or small _tsu_ (っ/ッ) indicates [gemination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gemination "Gemination") and is romanized by repeating the following consonant. For example, って is romanized _tte_ (exception: っち becomes _tchi_).

Syllables beginning with [p] are spelled with kana from the _h_ column and the half-voicing mark, _[handakuten](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dakuten\_and\_handakuten "Dakuten and handakuten")_.

The difference in usage between hiragana and katakana is stylistic. Usually, hiragana is the default syllabary, and katakana is used in certain special cases. Hiragana is used to write native Japanese words with no [kanji](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kanji "Kanji") representation (or whose kanji is thought obscure or difficult), as well as grammatical elements such as [particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japanese_particles "Japanese particles") and inflections ([okurigana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Okurigana "Okurigana")). Today katakana is most commonly used to write words of foreign origin that do not have kanji representations, as well as foreign personal and place names. Katakana is also used to represent [onomatopoeia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Onomatopoeia "Onomatopoeia") and interjections, emphasis, technical and scientific terms, transcriptions of the [Sino-Japanese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sino-Japanese_vocabulary "Sino-Japanese vocabulary") readings of kanji, and some corporate branding.

Kana can be written in small form above or next to lesser-known kanji in order to show pronunciation; this is called [furigana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Furigana "Furigana"). Furigana is used most widely in children's or learners' books. Literature for young children who do not yet know kanji may dispense with it altogether and instead use hiragana combined with spaces.

Systems supporting only a limited set of characters, such as [Wabun code](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wabun_code "Wabun code") for [Morse code](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morse_code "Morse code") telegrams and [single-byte](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SBCS "SBCS") digital [character encodings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Character_encoding "Character encoding") such as [JIS X 0201](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/JIS_X_0201 "JIS X 0201") or [EBCDIK](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japanese_language_in_EBCDIC#Single-byte_codes "Japanese language in EBCDIC"), likewise dispense with kanji, instead using only katakana. This is not necessary in systems supporting [double-byte](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double-byte_character_set "Double-byte character set") or [variable-width](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variable-width_encoding "Variable-width encoding") encodings such as [Shift JIS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shift_JIS "Shift JIS"), [EUC-JP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/EUC-JP "EUC-JP"), [UTF-8](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UTF-8 "UTF-8") or [UTF-16](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UTF-16 "UTF-16").

Development of hiragana and katakana

Old Japanese was written entirely in kanji, and a set of kanji called _[man'yōgana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Man%27y%C5%8Dgana "Man'yōgana")_ were first used to represent the phonetic values of grammatical particles and morphemes. As there was no consistent method of sound representation, a phoneme could be represented by multiple kanji, and even those kana's pronunciations differed in whether they were to be read as _[kungana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kun%27yomi "Kun'yomi")_ (訓仮名, "meaning kana") or _[ongana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/On%27yomi "On'yomi")_ (音仮名, "sound kana"), making decipherment problematic. The _[man'yōshū](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Man%27y%C5%8Dsh%C5%AB "Man'yōshū")_, a poetry anthology assembled sometime after 759 and the eponym of _man'yōgana_, exemplifies this phenomenon, where as many as almost twenty kanji were used for the mora _ka_. The consistency of the kana used was thus dependent on the style of the writer. [Hiragana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hiragana "Hiragana") developed as a distinct script from [cursive _man'yōgana_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%C5%8Dgana "Sōgana"), whereas [katakana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Katakana "Katakana") developed from abbreviated parts of [regular script](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_script "Regular script")_man'yōgana_ as a [glossing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gloss_(annotation) "Gloss (annotation)") system to add readings or explanations to Buddhist [sutras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sutra "Sutra"). Both of these systems were simplified to make writing easier. The shapes of many hiragana resembled the [Chinese cursive script](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cursive_script_(East_Asia) "Cursive script (East Asia)"), as did those of many katakana the Korean _[gugyeol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gugyeol "Gugyeol")_, suggesting that the Japanese followed the continental pattern of their neighbors.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kana#cite_note-Frellesvig-21)

Kana is traditionally said to have been invented by the [Buddhist priest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bhikkhu "Bhikkhu")[Kūkai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K%C5%ABkai "Kūkai") in the ninth century. Kūkai certainly brought the [Siddhaṃ script](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siddha%E1%B9%83_script "Siddhaṃ script") of India home on his return from [China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China "China") in 806;[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] his interest in the sacred aspects of [speech](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speech "Speech") and [writing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Writing "Writing") led him to the conclusion that Japanese would be better represented by a phonetic alphabet than by the kanji which had been used up to that point. The modern arrangement of kana reflects that of the [devanagari order](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Devanagari "Devanagari") used for [Sanskrit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sanskrit "Sanskrit") in the [Buddhist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buddhist "Buddhist")[Siddhaṃ script hybrid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siddha%E1%B9%83_script "Siddhaṃ script") known by the Japanese at the time,[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kana#cite_note-:0-22) before, the traditional _[iroha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iroha "Iroha")_ arrangement used to follow the order of a poem which uses each kana once.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] Kana's vowel (a-i-u-e-o) and consonant (k, s, t, n, h, m, y, r, w) order coincide with the Sanskrit order, except for the letter s, but that is explained by the properties of Old Japanese and the version of Siddham buddhist monks learnt at the time. However, the first time this order was used in Japanese was during the [Heian period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heian_period "Heian period") by Myōkaku, a priest and Sanskrit scholar of the 12th century, member of the same order of Kūkai and credited as reviving Saskrit studies. In 1695, a priest named Keichū published a 5 volume book that is considered to be fundamental in fixing the sound order to this day; and this work, in turn, was probably based on an earlier 3 volume systematic study of the grammar and the writing system of the Buddhist Hybrid Sanskrit, written by another priest, Kakugen, in 1681, also member of the order of Kūkai and Myōkaku.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kana#cite_note-:0-22)

However, hiragana and katakana did not quickly supplant _man'yōgana_. It was only in 1900 that the present set of kana was codified. All the other forms of hiragana and katakana developed before the 1900 codification are known as _[hentaigana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hentaigana "Hentaigana")_ (変体仮名, "variant kana"). Rules for their usage as per the spelling reforms of 1946, the _[gendai kana-zukai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modern\_kana\_usage "Modern kana usage")_ (現代仮名遣い, "present-day kana usage"), which abolished the kana for _wi_ (ゐ・ヰ), _we_ (ゑ・ヱ), and _wo_ (を・ヲ) (except that the last was reserved as the accusative particle).[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kana#cite_note-Frellesvig-21)

Kana are the basis for [collation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collation "Collation") in Japanese. They are taken in the order given by the _[gojūon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goj%C5%ABon "Gojūon")_ (あ い う え お ... わ を ん), though [iroha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iroha "Iroha") (い ろ は に ほ へ と ... せ す (ん)) ordering is used for enumeration in some circumstances. Dictionaries differ in the sequence order for long/short vowel distinction, small _tsu_ and diacritics. As Japanese does not use word spaces (except as a tool for children), there can be no word-by-word collation; all collation is kana-by-kana.

The hiragana range in [Unicode](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unicode "Unicode") is U+3040 ... U+309F, and the katakana range is U+30A0 ... U+30FF. The obsolete and rare characters (_wi_ and _we_) also have their proper code points.

Characters U+3095 and U+3096 are hiragana [small _ka_ and small _ke_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Small_ke "Small ke"), respectively. U+30F5 and U+30F6 are their katakana equivalents. Characters U+3099 and U+309A are combining [dakuten](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dakuten "Dakuten") and [handakuten](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Handakuten "Handakuten"), which correspond to the spacing characters U+309B and U+309C. U+309D is the hiragana [iteration mark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iteration_mark "Iteration mark"), used to repeat a previous hiragana. U+309E is the voiced hiragana iteration mark, which stands in for the previous hiragana but with the consonant voiced (_k_ becomes _g_, _h_ becomes _b_, etc.). U+30FD and U+30FE are the katakana iteration marks. U+309F is a ligature of _yori_ ([より](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yori_(kana) "Yori (kana)")) sometimes used in vertical writing. U+30FF is a ligature of _koto_ ([コト](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Koto_(kana) "Koto (kana)")), also found in vertical writing.
