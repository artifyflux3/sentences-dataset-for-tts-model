From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Reduction of an object's density

This article is about the physical process. For the ecological technique, see [Rarefaction (ecology)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rarefaction_(ecology) "Rarefaction (ecology)").

[![Image 6](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/62/Onde_compression_impulsion_1d_30_petit.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Onde_compression_impulsion_1d_30_petit.gif)

 An example of rarefaction is also as a [phase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phase_(waves) "Phase (waves)") in a [sound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sound "Sound") wave or [phonon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phonon "Phonon"). Half of a [sound wave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sound_wave "Sound wave") is made up of the compression of the [medium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transmission_medium "Transmission medium"), and the other half is the decompression or _rarefaction_ of the medium.

**Rarefaction** is the reduction of an item's density, the opposite of [compression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compression_(physical) "Compression (physical)").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rarefaction#cite_note-Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica-1) Like compression, which can travel in waves ([sound waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sound_wave "Sound wave"), for instance), rarefaction waves also exist in nature. A common rarefaction wave is the area of low relative pressure following a [shock wave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shock_wave "Shock wave") (see picture).

Rarefaction waves expand with time (much like sea waves spread out as they reach a beach); in most cases rarefaction waves keep the same overall profile ('shape') at all times throughout the wave's movement: it is a _self-similar expansion_. Each part of the wave travels at the local speed of sound, in the local medium. This expansion behaviour contrasts with that of pressure increases, which gets narrower with time until they steepen into shock waves.

Physical examples
-----------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Rarefaction&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Physical examples")]

A natural example of rarefaction occurs in the layers of [Earth's atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere "Earth's atmosphere"). Because the atmosphere has [mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass "Mass"), most atmospheric [matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matter "Matter") is nearer to the Earth due to the Earth's [gravitation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitation "Gravitation"). Therefore, air at higher layers of the atmosphere is less dense, or _rarefied_, relative to air at lower layers. Thus, rarefaction can refer either to a reduction in density over space at a single point of time, or a reduction of density over time for one particular area.

Rarefaction can be easily observed by compressing a [spring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spring_(device) "Spring (device)") and releasing it.

In manufacturing
----------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Rarefaction&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: In manufacturing")]

Modern construction of guitars is an example of using rarefaction in manufacturing. By forcing the reduction of density (loss of oils and other impurities) in the cellular structure of the soundboard, a rarefied guitar top produces a tonal decompression affecting the sound of the instrument, mimicking aged wood.

In philosophy
-------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Rarefaction&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: In philosophy")]

Distinctions between rarefaction and condensation, or between "the rare" and "the dense", were important in the early development of western philosophy, for example in the thinking of the [ancient philosopher](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_philosophy "Ancient philosophy")[Anaximenes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anaximenes_of_Miletus "Anaximenes of Miletus"),[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rarefaction#cite_note-2) and in [Aristotle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotle "Aristotle")'s _Physics_.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rarefaction#cite_note-3)
