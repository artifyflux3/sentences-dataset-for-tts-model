The **hydroxyl radical**, denoted as **•OH** or **HO•**,[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroxyl_radical#cite_note-3) is the neutral form of the hydroxide ion (OH–). As a free radical, it is highly reactive and consequently short-lived, making it a pivotal species in [radical chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radical_(chemistry) "Radical (chemistry)").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroxyl_radical#cite_note-4)

In nature, hydroxyl radicals are most notably produced from the decomposition of [hydroperoxides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_peroxide "Organic peroxide") (ROOH) or, in [atmospheric chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmospheric_chemistry "Atmospheric chemistry"), by the reaction of [excited](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Excited_state "Excited state")[atomic oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_oxygen "Atomic oxygen") with water. They are also significant in radiation chemistry, where their formation can lead to hydrogen peroxide and oxygen, which in turn can accelerate [corrosion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corrosion "Corrosion") and [stress corrosion cracking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stress_corrosion_cracking "Stress corrosion cracking") in environments such as nuclear reactor coolant systems. Other important formation pathways include the UV-light dissociation of [hydrogen peroxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_peroxide "Hydrogen peroxide") (H 2 O 2) and the [Fenton reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fenton%27s_reagent "Fenton's reagent"), where trace amounts of reduced transition metals catalyze the breakdown of peroxide.

In [organic synthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_synthesis "Organic synthesis"), hydroxyl radicals are most commonly generated by [photolysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photolysis "Photolysis") of _[1-Hydroxy-2(1H)-pyridinethione](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1-Hydroxy-2(1H)-pyridinethione "1-Hydroxy-2(1H)-pyridinethione")_.

The hydroxyl radical is often referred to as the "detergent" of the [troposphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Troposphere "Troposphere") because it reacts with many pollutants, often acting as the first step to their removal. It also has an important role in eliminating some [greenhouse gases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenhouse_gas "Greenhouse gas") like [methane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane "Methane") and [ozone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ozone "Ozone").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroxyl_radical#cite_note-5) The rate of reaction with the hydroxyl radical often determines how long many pollutants last in the atmosphere, if they do not undergo [photolysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photolysis "Photolysis") or are [rained out](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Precipitation "Precipitation"). For instance, methane, which reacts relatively slowly with hydroxyl radicals, has an average lifetime of >5 years and many [CFCs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chlorofluorocarbon "Chlorofluorocarbon") have lifetimes of 50+ years. Pollutants, such as larger [hydrocarbons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrocarbons "Hydrocarbons"), can have very short average lifetimes of less than a few hours.

The first reaction with many [volatile organic compounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volatile_organic_compounds "Volatile organic compounds") (VOCs) is the removal of a hydrogen atom, forming water and an [alkyl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkyl "Alkyl") radical (R•):

•OH + RH → H 2 O + R•
The alkyl radical will typically react rapidly with [oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen "Oxygen") forming a [peroxy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peroxy "Peroxy") radical:

R• + O 2 → RO 2•
The fate of this radical in the [troposphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Troposphere "Troposphere") is dependent on factors such as the amount of sunlight, pollution in the atmosphere and the nature of the [alkyl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkyl "Alkyl") radical that formed it (see chapters 12 & 13 in External Links "University Lecture notes on Atmospheric chemistry").

Biological significance
-----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hydroxyl_radical&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Biological significance")]

Hydroxyl radicals can occasionally be produced as a byproduct of [immune action](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system "Immune system"). [Macrophages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macrophage "Macrophage") and [microglia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microglia "Microglia") most frequently generate this compound when exposed to very specific [pathogens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pathogen "Pathogen"), such as certain bacteria. The destructive action of hydroxyl radicals has been implicated in several [neurological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neurological "Neurological")[autoimmune diseases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autoimmune_disorder "Autoimmune disorder") such as [HIV-associated dementia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HIV-associated_dementia "HIV-associated dementia"), when immune cells become over-activated and toxic to neighboring healthy cells.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroxyl_radical#cite_note-6)

The hydroxyl radical can damage virtually all types of macromolecules: carbohydrates, nucleic acids ([mutations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mutation "Mutation")), lipids ([lipid peroxidation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lipid_peroxidation "Lipid peroxidation")) and amino acids (e.g. conversion of [Phe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phenylalanine "Phenylalanine") to m-[Tyrosine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tyrosine "Tyrosine") and o-[Tyrosine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tyrosine "Tyrosine")). The hydroxyl radical has a very short _[in vivo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/In\_vivo "In vivo")_[half-life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Half-life "Half-life") of approximately 10−9 seconds and a high reactivity.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroxyl_radical#cite_note-7) This makes it a very dangerous compound to the organism.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroxyl_radical#cite_note-Reiter_RJ,_Melchiorri_D,_Sewerynek_E,_et_al._1995_1%E2%80%9311-8)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroxyl_radical#cite_note-9)

Unlike [superoxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superoxide "Superoxide"), which can be detoxified by [superoxide dismutase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superoxide_dismutase "Superoxide dismutase"), the hydroxyl radical cannot be eliminated by an [enzymatic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enzyme "Enzyme") reaction. Mechanisms for scavenging peroxyl radicals for the protection of [cellular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_(biology) "Cell (biology)") structures include endogenous [antioxidants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antioxidants "Antioxidants") such as [melatonin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Melatonin "Melatonin") and [glutathione](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glutathione "Glutathione"), and dietary [antioxidants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antioxidants "Antioxidants") such as [mannitol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mannitol "Mannitol") and [vitamin E](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vitamin_E "Vitamin E").[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroxyl_radical#cite_note-Reiter_RJ,_Melchiorri_D,_Sewerynek_E,_et_al._1995_1%E2%80%9311-8)

Importance in the Earth's atmosphere
------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hydroxyl_radical&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Importance in the Earth's atmosphere")]

The hydroxyl radical (•OH) is one of the main chemical species controlling the oxidizing capacity of the Earth's atmosphere, having a major impact on the concentrations and distribution of greenhouse gases and pollutants. It is the most widespread oxidizer in the [troposphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Troposphere "Troposphere"), the lowest part of the atmosphere. Understanding •OH variability is important to evaluating human impacts on the atmosphere and climate. The •OH species has a lifetime in the Earth's atmosphere of less than one second.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroxyl_radical#cite_note-10) Understanding the role of •OH in the oxidation process of methane (CH 4) present in the atmosphere to first carbon monoxide (CO) and then carbon dioxide (CO 2) is important for assessing the residence time of this greenhouse gas, the overall carbon budget of the troposphere, and its influence on the process of global warming.

The lifetime of •OH radicals in the Earth's atmosphere is very short; therefore, •OH concentrations in the air are very low and very sensitive techniques are required for its direct detection.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroxyl_radical#cite_note-11) Global average hydroxyl radical concentrations have been measured indirectly by analyzing [methyl chloroform](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methyl_chloroform "Methyl chloroform") (CH 3 CCl 3) present in the air. The results obtained by Montzka _et al._ (2011)[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroxyl_radical#cite_note-12) show that the interannual variability in •OH estimated from CH 3 CCl 3 measurements is small, indicating that global •OH is generally well buffered against perturbations. This small variability is consistent with measurements of [methane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane "Methane") and other trace gases primarily oxidized by •OH, as well as global photochemical model calculations.

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hydroxyl_radical&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: First detection of interstellar •HO")]

The first experimental evidence for the presence of 18 cm absorption lines of the hydroxyl (•HO) radical in the radio absorption spectrum of [Cassiopeia A](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cassiopeia_A "Cassiopeia A") was obtained by Weinreb et al. (Nature, Vol. 200, pp.829, 1963) based on observations made during the period October 15–29, 1963.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroxyl_radical#cite_note-DieterEwen1964-13)

•HO is a diatomic molecule. The electronic angular momentum along the molecular axis is +1 or −1, and the electronic spin angular momentum S=1/2. Because of the orbit-spin coupling, the spin angular momentum can be oriented in parallel or anti-parallel directions to the orbital angular momentum, producing the splitting into Π 1/2 and Π 3/2 states. The 2 Π 3/2 ground state of •HO is split by lambda doubling interaction (an interaction between the nuclei rotation and the unpaired electron motion around its orbit). Hyperfine interaction with the unpaired spin of the proton further splits the levels.

### Chemistry of the molecule •HO

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hydroxyl_radical&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Chemistry of the molecule •HO")]

In order to study gas phase interstellar chemistry, it is convenient to distinguish two types of interstellar clouds: diffuse clouds, with T=30–100 K, and n=10–1000 cm−3, and dense clouds with T=10–30K and density n=10 4–10 3 cm−3. Ion-chemical routes in both dense and diffuse clouds have been established for some works (Hartquist 1990).

#### •HO production pathways

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hydroxyl_radical&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: •HO production pathways")]

The •HO radical is linked with the production of H 2 O in molecular clouds. Studies of •HO distribution in Taurus Molecular Cloud-1 (TMC-1)[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroxyl_radical#cite_note-HarjuWinnberg2000-22) suggest that in dense gas, •HO is mainly formed by dissociative recombination of H 3 O+. Dissociative recombination is the reaction in which a molecular ion recombines with an electron and dissociates into neutral fragments. Important formation mechanisms for •HO are:

H 3 O+ + e− → •HO + H 2 (1a) Dissociative recombination H 3 O+ + e− → •HO + •H + •H (1b) Dissociative recombination HCO+2 + e− → •HO + CO (2a) Dissociative recombination •O + HCO → •HO + CO (3a) Neutral-neutral H− + H 3 O+ → •HO + H 2 + •H (4a) Ion-molecular ion neutralization

#### •HO destruction pathways

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hydroxyl_radical&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: •HO destruction pathways")]

Experimental data on association reactions of •H and •HO suggest that radiative association involving atomic and diatomic neutral radicals may be considered as an effective mechanism for the production of small neutral molecules in the interstellar clouds.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroxyl_radical#cite_note-FieldAdams1980-23) The formation of O 2 occurs in the gas phase via the neutral exchange reaction between •O and •HO, which is also the main sink for •HO in dense regions.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroxyl_radical#cite_note-HarjuWinnberg2000-22)

We can see that atomic oxygen takes part both in the production and destruction of •HO, so the abundance of •HO depends mainly on the abundance of [H+3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trihydrogen_cation "Trihydrogen cation"). Then, important chemical pathways leading from •HO radicals are:

•HO + •O → O 2 + •H (1A) Neutral-neutral •HO + C+ → CO+ + •H (2A) Ion-neutral •HO + •N → NO + •H (3A) Neutral-neutral •HO + C → CO + •H (4A) Neutral-neutral •HO + •H → H 2 O + ≡ext{photon} (5A) Neutral-neutral

### Importance of interstellar •HO observations

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hydroxyl_radical&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Importance of interstellar •HO observations")]

Discoveries of the microwave spectra of a considerable number of molecules prove the existence of rather complex molecules in the interstellar clouds and provide the possibility to study dense clouds, which are obscured by the dust they contain.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroxyl_radical#cite_note-25) The •HO molecule has been observed in the interstellar medium since 1963 through its 18-cm transitions.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroxyl_radical#cite_note-26) In the subsequent years, •HO was observed by its rotational transitions at far-infrared wavelengths, mainly in the Orion region. Because each rotational level of •HO is split by lambda doubling, astronomers can observe a wide variety of energy states from the ground state.

#### •HO as a tracer of shock conditions

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hydroxyl_radical&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: •HO as a tracer of shock conditions")]

Very high densities are required to thermalize the rotational transitions of •HO,[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroxyl_radical#cite_note-27) so it is difficult to detect far-infrared emission lines from a quiescent molecular cloud. Even at H 2 densities of 10 6 cm−3, dust must be optically thick at infrared wavelengths. But the passage of a shock wave through a molecular cloud is precisely the process which can bring the molecular gas out of equilibrium with the dust, making observations of far-infrared emission lines possible. A moderately fast shock may produce a transient raise in the •HO abundance relative to hydrogen. So, it is possible that far-infrared emission lines of •HO can be a good diagnostic of shock conditions.

Diffuse clouds are of astronomical interest because they play a primary role in the evolution and thermodynamics of the ISM. Observation of the abundant atomic hydrogen in 21 cm has shown good signal-to-noise ratio in both emission and absorption. Nevertheless, HI observations have a fundamental difficulty when they are directed to low-mass regions of the hydrogen nucleus, such as the center part of a diffuse cloud: the thermal width of hydrogen lines are of the same order as the internal velocity structures of interest, so cloud components of various temperatures and central velocities are indistinguishable in the spectrum. Molecular line observations in principle do not suffer from these problems. Unlike HI, molecules generally have an [excitation temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Excitation_temperature "Excitation temperature") T ex<< T kin, so that emission is very weak even from abundant species. CO and •HO are considered to be the most easily studied candidate molecules. CO has transitions in a region of the spectrum (wavelength < 3 mm) where there are not strong background continuum sources, but •HO has the 18 cm emission line, convenient for absorption observations.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroxyl_radical#cite_note-DickeyCrovisier1981-18) Observation studies provide the most sensitive means of detection for molecules with sub-thermal excitation, and can give the opacity of the spectral line, which is a central issue to model the molecular region.

Studies based in the kinematic comparison of •HO and HI absorption lines from diffuse clouds are useful in determining their physical conditions, especially because heavier elements provide higher velocity resolution.

•HO [masers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maser "Maser"), a type of [astrophysical maser](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrophysical_maser "Astrophysical maser"), were the first masers to be discovered in space and have been observed in more environments than any other type of maser.

In the [Milky Way](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milky_Way "Milky Way"), •HO masers are found in stellar masers (evolved stars), interstellar masers (regions of massive star formation), or in the interface between supernova remnants and molecular material. Interstellar HO masers are often observed from molecular material surrounding ultracompact [H II regions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/H_II_region "H II region") (UC H II). But there are masers associated with very young stars that have yet to create UC H II regions.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroxyl_radical#cite_note-28) This class of •HO masers appears to form near the edges of very dense material, places where H 2 O masers form, and where total densities drop rapidly and UV radiation from young stars can dissociate H 2 O molecules. So, observations of •HO masers in these regions can be an important way to probe the distribution of the important H 2 O molecule in interstellar shocks at high [spatial resolutions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spatial_resolution "Spatial resolution").
