From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

**Stellar molecules** are [molecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecule "Molecule") that exist or form in [stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star "Star"). Such formations can take place when the [temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperature "Temperature") is low enough for molecules to form – typically around 6,000 K (5,730°C; 10,340°F) or cooler.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecules_in_stars#cite_note-Masseron2015-1) Otherwise the stellar [matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matter "Matter") is restricted to [atoms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atom "Atom") and [ions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ion "Ion") in the forms of [gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas "Gas") or – at very high temperatures – [plasma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasma_(physics) "Plasma (physics)").

[Matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matter "Matter") is made up by [atoms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atom "Atom") (formed by [protons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton "Proton") and other [subatomic particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subatomic_particle "Subatomic particle")). When the environment is right, atoms can join together and form [molecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecules "Molecules"), which give rise to most materials studied in [materials science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Materials_science "Materials science"). But certain environments, such as high temperatures, don't allow atoms to form molecules, as the environmental energy exceeds that of the dissociation energy of the bonds within the molecule. Stars have very high temperatures, primarily in their interior, and therefore there are few molecules formed in stars.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecules_in_stars#cite_note-smol-2)

By the mid-18th century, scientists surmised that the source of the Sun's light was [incandescence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Incandescence "Incandescence"), rather than [combustion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Combustion "Combustion").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecules_in_stars#cite_note-sa-3)

Evidence and research
---------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Molecules_in_stars&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Evidence and research")]

Although the [Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun "Sun") is a star, its [photosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photosphere "Photosphere") has a low enough temperature of 6,000 K (5,730°C; 10,340°F), and therefore molecules can form. [Water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water "Water") has been found on the Sun, and there is evidence of [H 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_hydrogen "Molecular hydrogen") in [white dwarf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_dwarf "White dwarf") stellar atmospheres.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecules_in_stars#cite_note-smol-2)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecules_in_stars#cite_note-XuJura2013-4)

Cooler stars include [absorption band](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absorption_band "Absorption band") spectra that are characteristic of molecules. Similar absorption bands can be found through observation of solar [sun spots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun_spot "Sun spot"), which are cool enough to allow persistence of stellar molecules. Molecules found in the Sun include [MgH](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnesium_monohydride "Magnesium monohydride"), [CaH](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calcium_monohydride "Calcium monohydride"), [FeH](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron_monohydride "Iron monohydride"), [CrH](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chromium_monohydride "Chromium monohydride"), [NaH](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NaH "NaH"), [OH](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroxyl_radical "Hydroxyl radical"), [SiH](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_monohydride "Silicon monohydride"), [VO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vanadium_monoxide "Vanadium monoxide"), and [TiO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Titanium_monoxide "Titanium monoxide"). Others include [CN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyano_radical "Cyano radical"), [CH](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methylidyne "Methylidyne"), [MgF](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=MgF&action=edit&redlink=1 "MgF (page does not exist)"), [NH](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imidogen "Imidogen"), [C 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dicarbon "Dicarbon"), [SrF](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=SrF&action=edit&redlink=1 "SrF (page does not exist)"), [ZrO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zirconium_monoxide "Zirconium monoxide"), [YO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yttrium_monoxide "Yttrium monoxide"), [ScO](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Scandium_monoxide&action=edit&redlink=1 "Scandium monoxide (page does not exist)"), and [BH](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boron_monohydride "Boron monohydride").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecules_in_stars#cite_note-pam-5)

Stars of most types can contain molecules, even the Ap category of [A-type stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A-type_main-sequence_star "A-type main-sequence star"). Only the hottest O-, B-, and A-type stars have no detectable molecules. Carbon-rich white dwarfs, even though very hot, have spectral lines of [C 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dicarbon "Dicarbon") and [CH](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methylidyne "Methylidyne").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecules_in_stars#cite_note-astroc-6)

### Laboratory measurements

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Molecules_in_stars&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Laboratory measurements")]

Measurements of simple molecules that may be found in stars are performed in laboratories to determine the wavelengths of the spectra lines. Also, it is important to measure the [dissociation energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dissociation_energy "Dissociation energy") and [oscillator strengths](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oscillator_strength "Oscillator strength") (how strongly the molecule interacts with electromagnetic radiation). These measurements are inserted into formula that can calculate the spectrum under different conditions of pressure and temperature. However, man-made conditions are often different from those in stars, because it is hard to achieve the temperatures, and also [local thermal equilibrium](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Local_thermal_equilibrium&action=edit&redlink=1 "Local thermal equilibrium (page does not exist)"), as found in stars, is unlikely. Accuracy of oscillator strengths and actual measurement of dissociation energy is usually only approximate.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecules_in_stars#cite_note-astroc-6)

A numerical model of a star's atmosphere will calculate pressures and temperatures at different depths, and can predict the spectrum for different elemental concentrations.

The molecules in stars can be used to determine some characteristics of the star. The isotopic composition can be determined if the lines in the molecular spectrum are observed. The different masses of different isotopes cause vibration and rotation frequencies to significantly vary. Secondly the temperature can be determined, as the temperature will change the numbers of molecules in the different vibrational and rotational states. Some molecules are sensitive to the ratio of elements, and so indicate elemental composition of the star.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecules_in_stars#cite_note-astroc-6) Different molecules are characteristic of different kinds of stars, and are used to classify them.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecules_in_stars#cite_note-pam-5) Because there can be numerous spectral lines of different strength, conditions at different depths in the star can be determined. These conditions include temperature and speed towards or away from the observer.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecules_in_stars#cite_note-astroc-6)

The spectrum of molecules has advantages over atomic spectral lines, as atomic lines are often very strong, and therefore only come from high in the atmosphere. Also the profile of the atomic spectral line can be distorted due to isotopes or overlaying of other spectral lines.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecules_in_stars#cite_note-astroc-6) The molecular spectrum is much more sensitive to temperature than atomic lines.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecules_in_stars#cite_note-astroc-6)
