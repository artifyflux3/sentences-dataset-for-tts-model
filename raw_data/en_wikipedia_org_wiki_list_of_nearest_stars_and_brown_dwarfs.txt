A radar map of all known stellar and [substellar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Substellar "Substellar") objects within 9 light years (ly), arranged clockwise in [hours](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hour_angle "Hour angle") of [right ascension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_ascension "Right ascension"), and marked by distance (▬) and position (◆). Distances are marked outward from the [Sun (Sol)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun "Sun"), with concentric circles indicating the distance in one ly steps. Positions are marked inward from their distance markings, connected by lines according to their [declinations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Declination "Declination") (dotted when positive), representing the [arcs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circular_arc "Circular arc") of the declinations viewed edge-on. For within 12 ly see [this map](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Angular_map_of_fusors_around_Sol_within_12ly_(large).png "commons:File:Angular map of fusors around Sol within 12ly (large).png").

This list covers all known [stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star "Star"), [white dwarfs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_dwarf "White dwarf"), [brown dwarfs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brown_dwarf "Brown dwarf"), and [sub-brown dwarfs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sub-brown_dwarf "Sub-brown dwarf") within 20 [light-years](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light-year "Light-year") (6.13 [parsecs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parsec "Parsec")) of the [Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun "Sun"). So far, 131 such objects have been found. Only [22 are bright enough](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_nearest_bright_stars "List of nearest bright stars") to be visible without a telescope, for which the star's visible light needs to reach or exceed the dimmest brightness visible to the [naked eye](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naked_eye "Naked eye") from Earth, which is typically around 6.5 [apparent magnitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apparent_magnitude "Apparent magnitude").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_nearest_stars_and_brown_dwarfs#cite_note-Weaver1947-1)

The known 131 objects are bound in 94 [stellar systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_systems "Stellar systems"). Of those, 103 are [main sequence stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Main_sequence_stars "Main sequence stars"): 80 [red dwarfs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_dwarf "Red dwarf") and 23 "typical" stars having greater mass. Additionally, astronomers have found 6 [white dwarfs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_dwarf "White dwarf") (stars that have exhausted all fusible hydrogen), 21 brown dwarfs, as well as 1 sub-brown dwarf, [WISE 0855−0714](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WISE_0855%E2%88%920714 "WISE 0855−0714") (possibly a [rogue planet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rogue_planet "Rogue planet")). The closest system is [Alpha Centauri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri "Alpha Centauri"), with [Proxima Centauri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proxima_Centauri "Proxima Centauri") as the closest star in that system, at 4.2465 light-years from Earth. The brightest, most massive and most luminous object among those 131 is [Sirius A](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sirius_A "Sirius A"), which is also the brightest star in Earth's [night sky](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Night_sky "Night sky"); its white dwarf companion Sirius B is the hottest object among them. The largest object within the 20 light-years is [Procyon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Procyon "Procyon").

The [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System"), and the other stars/dwarfs listed here, are currently moving within (or near) the [Local Interstellar Cloud](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_Interstellar_Cloud "Local Interstellar Cloud"), roughly 30 [light-years](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light-year "Light-year") (9.2[pc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parsec "Parsec")) across. The Local Interstellar Cloud is, in turn, contained inside the [Local Bubble](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_Bubble "Local Bubble"), a cavity in the interstellar medium about 300 [light-years](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light-year "Light-year") (92.0[pc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parsec "Parsec")) across. It contains [Ursa Major](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ursa_Major_Moving_Group "Ursa Major Moving Group") and the [Hyades](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyades_(star_cluster) "Hyades (star cluster)")[star cluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_cluster "Star cluster"), among others. The Local Bubble also contains the neighboring [G-Cloud](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G-Cloud "G-Cloud"), which contains the stars [Alpha Centauri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri "Alpha Centauri") and [Altair](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Altair "Altair"). In the [galactic context](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System#Galactic_context "Solar System"), the Local Bubble is a small part of the [Orion Arm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_Arm "Orion Arm"), which contains most stars that we can see without a telescope. The Orion Arm is one of the [spiral arms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spiral_arms "Spiral arms") of our [Milky Way galaxy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milky_Way_galaxy "Milky Way galaxy").

The easiest way to determine stellar distance to the Sun for objects at these distances is [parallax](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallax "Parallax"), which measures how much stars appear to move against background objects over the course of Earth's orbit around the Sun. As a [parsec](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parsec "Parsec") (parallax-second) is defined by the distance of an object that would appear to move exactly one [second of arc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_of_arc "Second of arc") against background objects, stars less than 5 parsecs away will have measured parallaxes of over 0.2 arcseconds, or 200 milliarcseconds. Determining past and future positions relies on accurate [astrometric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrometric "Astrometric") measurements of their parallax and total [proper motions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proper_motion "Proper motion") (how far they move across the sky due to their actual velocity relative to the Sun), along with [spectroscopically](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic "Spectroscopic") determined [radial velocities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radial_velocities "Radial velocities") (their speed directly towards or away from us, which combined with proper motion defines their true movement through the sky relative to the Sun). Both of these measurements are subject to increasing and significant errors over very long time spans, especially over the several thousand-year time spans it takes for stars to noticeably move relative to each other.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_nearest_stars_and_brown_dwarfs#cite_note-Matthews1994-2)

Based on results from the [Gaia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaia_(spacecraft) "Gaia (spacecraft)") telescope's second data release from April 2018, an estimated 694 stars will approach the Solar System to less than 5 [parsecs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parsec "Parsec") in the next 15 million years. Of these, 26 have a good probability to come within 1.0 parsec (3.3 light-years) and another 7 within 0.5 parsecs (1.6 light-years).[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_nearest_stars_and_brown_dwarfs#cite_note-Bailer2018-3) This number is likely much higher, due to the sheer number of stars needed to be surveyed; a star approaching the Solar System 10 million years ago, moving at a typical Sun-relative 20–200 kilometers per second, would be 600–6,000 light-years from the Sun at present day, with millions of stars closer to the Sun. The closest encounter to the Sun so far predicted is the low-mass orange dwarf star [Gliese 710](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gliese_710 "Gliese 710") / HIP 89825 with roughly 60% the mass of the Sun.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_nearest_stars_and_brown_dwarfs#cite_note-Hall2018-4) It is currently predicted to pass 0.1696±0.0065 ly (10 635±500[au](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_unit "Astronomical unit")) from the Sun in 1.290±0.04 million years from the present, close enough to significantly disturb the Solar System's [Oort cloud](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud "Oort cloud").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_nearest_stars_and_brown_dwarfs#cite_note-resultsGaiaDR3-5)

The [classes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_classification "Stellar classification") of the stars and brown dwarfs are shown in the color of their spectral types (these colors are derived from conventional names for the spectral types and do not necessarily represent the star's observed color). Many brown dwarfs are not listed by [visual magnitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visible_spectrum "Visible spectrum") but are listed by near-infrared [J band](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J_band_(infrared) "J band (infrared)")[apparent magnitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnitude_(astronomy) "Magnitude (astronomy)") due to how dim (and often invisible) they are in visible color bands (U, B or V). Absolute magnitude (with electromagnetic wave, 'light' band denoted in subscript) is a measurement at a 10-parsec distance across imaginary empty space devoid of all its sparse dust and gas. Some of the [parallaxes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallax "Parallax") and resultant distances are rough measurements.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_nearest_stars_and_brown_dwarfs#cite_note-RECONS-6)

Distant future and past encounters
----------------------------------

[![Image 7](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Sun_in_orbit_around_Galactic_Centre.gif/500px-Sun_in_orbit_around_Galactic_Centre.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Sun_in_orbit_around_Galactic_Centre.gif)

Visualisation of the [orbit of the Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbit_of_the_Sun "Orbit of the Sun") (yellow dot and white curve) around the [Galactic Centre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galactic_Centre "Galactic Centre") (GC) in the last [galactic year](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galactic_year "Galactic year"). The red dots correspond to the positions of the stars studied by the [European Southern Observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Southern_Observatory "European Southern Observatory") in a monitoring programme.[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_nearest_stars_and_brown_dwarfs#cite_note-74)

Over long periods of time, the slow independent motion of stars change in both relative position and in their distance from the observer. This can cause other currently distant stars to fall within a stated range, which may be readily calculated and predicted using accurate [astrometric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrometric "Astrometric") measurements of [parallax](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallax "Parallax") and total [proper motions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proper_motion "Proper motion"), along with [spectroscopically](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic "Spectroscopic") determined [radial velocities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radial_velocities "Radial velocities"). Although extrapolations can be made into the past or future, they are subject to increasingly significant cumulative errors over very long periods.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_nearest_stars_and_brown_dwarfs#cite_note-Matthews1994-2) Inaccuracies of these measured parameters make determining the true minimum distances of any encountering stars or brown dwarfs fairly difficult.[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_nearest_stars_and_brown_dwarfs#cite_note-75)

One of the first stars known to approach the Sun particularly close is [Gliese 710](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gliese_710 "Gliese 710"). The star, whose mass is roughly half that of the Sun, is currently 62 light-years from the Solar System. It was first noticed in 1999 using data from the [Hipparcos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hipparcos "Hipparcos") satellite, and was estimated to pass less than 1.3 light-years (0.40 pc) from the Sun in 1.4 million years.[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_nearest_stars_and_brown_dwarfs#cite_note-76) With the release of [_Gaia_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaia_(spacecraft) "Gaia (spacecraft)")'s observations of the star, it has since been refined to a much closer 0.178 light-years (0.055 pc), close enough to significantly disturb objects in the [Oort cloud](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud "Oort cloud"), which extends 1.2 light-years (0.37 pc) from the Sun.[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_nearest_stars_and_brown_dwarfs#cite_note-77)
