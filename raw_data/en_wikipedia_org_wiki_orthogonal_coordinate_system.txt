From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), **orthogonal coordinates** are defined as a set of d coordinates ![Image 1: {\displaystyle \mathbf {q} =(q^{1},q^{2},\dots ,q^{d})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/66e437acb5460dd773e107ff0050b4216e6854c5) in which the [coordinate hypersurfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_system#Coordinate_surface "Coordinate system") all meet at [right angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_angle "Right angle") (note that superscripts are [indices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_notation "Einstein notation"), not [exponents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponent "Exponent")). A coordinate surface for a particular coordinate q k is the [curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curve "Curve"), [surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface "Surface"), or [hypersurface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypersurface "Hypersurface") on which q k is a constant. For example, the three-dimensional [Cartesian coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system "Cartesian coordinate system")(_x_, _y_, _z_) is an orthogonal coordinate system, since its coordinate surfaces _x_ = constant, _y_ = constant, and _z_ = constant are planes that meet at right angles to one another, i.e., are perpendicular. Orthogonal coordinates are a special but extremely common case of [curvilinear coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvilinear_coordinates "Curvilinear coordinates").

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Conformal_map.svg/250px-Conformal_map.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Conformal_map.svg)

A [conformal map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conformal_map "Conformal map") acting on a rectangular grid. Note that the orthogonality of the curved grid is retained.

While vector operations and physical laws are normally easiest to derive in [Cartesian coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinates "Cartesian coordinates"), non-Cartesian orthogonal coordinates are often used instead for the solution of various problems, especially [boundary value problems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boundary_value_problem "Boundary value problem"), such as those arising in field theories of [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics"), [fluid flow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluid_flow "Fluid flow"), [electrodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrodynamics "Electrodynamics"), [plasma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasma_(physics) "Plasma (physics)") physics and the [diffusion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffusion "Diffusion") of [chemical species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_species "Chemical species") or [heat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat "Heat").

The chief advantage of non-Cartesian coordinates is that they can be chosen to match the symmetry of the problem. For example, the pressure wave due to an explosion far from the ground (or other barriers) depends on 3D space in Cartesian coordinates, however the pressure predominantly moves away from the center, so that in [spherical coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_coordinates "Spherical coordinates") the problem becomes very nearly one-dimensional (since the pressure wave dominantly depends only on time and the distance from the center). Another example is (slow) fluid in a straight circular pipe: in Cartesian coordinates, one has to solve a (difficult) two dimensional boundary value problem involving a partial differential equation, but in [cylindrical coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylindrical_coordinates "Cylindrical coordinates") the problem becomes one-dimensional with an [ordinary differential equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordinary_differential_equation "Ordinary differential equation") instead of a [partial differential equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_differential_equation "Partial differential equation").

The reason to prefer orthogonal coordinates instead of general [curvilinear coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvilinear_coordinates "Curvilinear coordinates") is simplicity: many complications arise when coordinates are not orthogonal. For example, in orthogonal coordinates many problems may be solved by [separation of variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separation_of_variables#pde "Separation of variables"). Separation of variables is a mathematical technique that converts a complex _d_-dimensional problem into _d_ one-dimensional problems that can be solved in terms of known functions. Many equations can be reduced to [Laplace's equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laplace%27s_equation "Laplace's equation") or the [Helmholtz equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helmholtz_equation "Helmholtz equation"). [Laplace's equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laplace%27s_equation "Laplace's equation") is separable in 13 orthogonal coordinate systems (the 14 listed [in the table below](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_coordinate_system#Table_of_orthogonal_coordinates) with the exception of [toroidal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toroidal_coordinates "Toroidal coordinates")), and the [Helmholtz equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helmholtz_equation "Helmholtz equation") is separable in 11 orthogonal coordinate systems.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_coordinate_system#cite_note-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_coordinate_system#cite_note-2)

Orthogonal coordinates never have off-diagonal terms in their [metric tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_tensor "Metric tensor"). In other words, the infinitesimal squared distance _ds_ 2 can always be written as a scaled sum of the squared infinitesimal coordinate displacements:

![Image 3: {\displaystyle ds^{2}=\sum _{k=1}^{d}\left(h_{k}\,dq^{k}\right)^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/963fd3560911131a642227ec11d7d23e5ba57058)
where _d_ is the dimension and the scaling functions (or scale factors):

![Image 4: {\displaystyle h_{k}(\mathbf {q} )\ {\stackrel {\mathrm {def} }{=}}\ {\sqrt {g_{kk}(\mathbf {q} )}}=|\mathbf {e} _{k}|}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a7aa6402e8d1977866039597d3cd2e3df2d35168)
equal the square roots of the diagonal components of the metric tensor, or the lengths of the local basis vectors ![Image 5: {\displaystyle \mathbf {e} _{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/afc2926ab5c390c48c2abe97d1cc7bf00e972677) described below. These scaling functions _h_ _i_ are used to calculate differential operators in the new coordinates, e.g., the [gradient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gradient "Gradient"), the [Laplacian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_Laplacian "Vector Laplacian"), the [divergence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divergence "Divergence") and the [curl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curl_(mathematics) "Curl (mathematics)").

A simple method for generating orthogonal coordinates systems in two dimensions is by a [conformal mapping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conformal_mapping "Conformal mapping") of a standard two-dimensional grid of [Cartesian coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinates "Cartesian coordinates")(_x_, _y_). A [complex number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number")_z_ = _x_ + _iy_ can be formed from the real coordinates _x_ and _y_, where _i_ represents the [imaginary unit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imaginary_unit "Imaginary unit"). Any [holomorphic function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holomorphic_function "Holomorphic function")_w_ = _f_(_z_) with non-zero complex derivative will produce a [conformal mapping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conformal_mapping "Conformal mapping"); if the resulting complex number is written _w_ = _u_ + _iv_, then the curves of constant _u_ and _v_ intersect at right angles, just as the original lines of constant _x_ and _y_ did.

Orthogonal coordinates in three and higher dimensions can be generated from an orthogonal two-dimensional coordinate system, either by projecting it into a new dimension (_cylindrical coordinates_) or by rotating the two-dimensional system about one of its symmetry axes. However, there are other orthogonal coordinate systems in three dimensions that cannot be obtained by projecting or rotating a two-dimensional system, such as the [ellipsoidal coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipsoidal_coordinates "Ellipsoidal coordinates"). More general orthogonal coordinates may be obtained by starting with some necessary coordinate surfaces and considering their [orthogonal trajectories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_trajectories "Orthogonal trajectories").

In [Cartesian coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinates "Cartesian coordinates"), the [basis vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basis_vectors "Basis vectors") are fixed (constant). In the more general setting of [curvilinear coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvilinear_coordinates "Curvilinear coordinates"), a point in space is specified by the coordinates, and at every such point there is bound a set of basis vectors, which generally are not constant: this is the essence of curvilinear coordinates in general and is a very important concept. What distinguishes orthogonal coordinates is that, though the basis vectors vary, they are always [orthogonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal "Orthogonal") with respect to each other. In other words,

![Image 6: {\displaystyle \mathbf {e} _{i}\cdot \mathbf {e} _{j}=0\quad {\text{if}}\quad i\neq j}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7dad6b387eef3880aef60bd770fc6d3995683c76)
These basis vectors are by definition the [tangent vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry_of_curves#Tangent_vector "Differential geometry of curves") of the curves obtained by varying one coordinate, keeping the others fixed:

[![Image 7](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/OrthogonalCoordinates.png/330px-OrthogonalCoordinates.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:OrthogonalCoordinates.png)

Visualization of 2D orthogonal coordinates. Curves obtained by holding all but one coordinate constant are shown, along with basis vectors. Note that the basis vectors aren't of equal length: they need not be, they only need to be orthogonal.

![Image 8: {\displaystyle \mathbf {e} _{i}={\frac {\partial \mathbf {r} }{\partial q^{i}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7cd0683dd8f31ac78435173748f38be09e1e984a)
where **r** is some point and _q_ _i_ is the coordinate for which the basis vector is extracted. In other words, a curve is obtained by fixing all but one coordinate; the unfixed coordinate is varied as in a [parametric curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parametric_curve "Parametric curve"), and the derivative of the curve with respect to the parameter (the varying coordinate) is the basis vector for that coordinate.

Note that the vectors are not necessarily of equal length. The useful functions known as scale factors of the coordinates are simply the lengths ![Image 9: {\displaystyle h_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d535f210cbd9b9fe6689e61427b3e213e5b2d547) of the basis vectors ![Image 10: {\displaystyle {\mathbf {e} }_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35f9df09c5d2a9a2f830f1acb268cba7ee2f2830) (see table below). The scale factors are sometimes called [Lamé coefficients](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lam%C3%A9_coefficients "Lamé coefficients"), not to be confused with [Lamé parameters (solid mechanics)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lam%C3%A9_parameters_(solid_mechanics) "Lamé parameters (solid mechanics)").

The [normalized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_vector "Unit vector") basis vectors are notated with a hat and obtained by dividing by the length:

![Image 11: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathbf {e} }}_{i}={\frac {{\mathbf {e} }_{i}}{h_{i}}}={\frac {{\mathbf {e} }_{i}}{\left|{\mathbf {e} }_{i}\right|}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d20aebc3fd0c808e3d55f7922c3915acb68df359)
A [vector field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_field "Vector field") may be specified by its components with respect to the basis vectors or the normalized basis vectors, and one must be sure which case is meant. Components in the normalized basis are most common in applications for clarity of the quantities (for example, one may want to deal with tangential velocity instead of tangential velocity times a scale factor); in derivations the normalized basis is less common since it is more complicated.

### Contravariant basis

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Orthogonal_coordinates&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Contravariant basis")]

The basis vectors shown above are [covariant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covariance_and_contravariance_of_vectors "Covariance and contravariance of vectors") basis vectors (because they "co-vary" with vectors). In the case of orthogonal coordinates, the contravariant basis vectors are easy to find since they will be in the same direction as the covariant vectors but [reciprocal length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reciprocal_length "Reciprocal length") (for this reason, the two sets of basis vectors are said to be reciprocal with respect to each other):

![Image 12: {\displaystyle \mathbf {e} ^{i}={\frac {{\hat {\mathbf {e} }}_{i}}{h_{i}}}={\frac {\mathbf {e} _{i}}{h_{i}^{2}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/98fc5c6a693224ea013a8716736438199748ba66)
this follows from the fact that, by definition, ![Image 13: {\displaystyle \mathbf {e} _{i}\cdot \mathbf {e} ^{j}=\delta _{i}^{j}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d23a1d02908738d5687878450861ed889182804d), using the [Kronecker delta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kronecker_delta "Kronecker delta"). Note that:

![Image 14: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathbf {e} }}_{i}={\frac {\mathbf {e} _{i}}{h_{i}}}=h_{i}\mathbf {e} ^{i}\equiv {\hat {\mathbf {e} }}^{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e3a9b2475ebf49649a736ebb905bf8037cc45c4a)
We now face three different basis sets commonly used to describe vectors in orthogonal coordinates: the covariant basis **e**_i_, the contravariant basis **e**_i_, and the normalized basis **ê**_i_. While a vector is an _objective quantity_, meaning its identity is independent of any coordinate system, the components of a vector depend on what basis the vector is represented in.

To avoid confusion, the components of the vector **x** with respect to the **e**_i_ basis are represented as _x_ _i_, while the components with respect to the **e**_i_ basis are represented as _x_ _i_:

![Image 15: {\displaystyle \mathbf {x} =\sum _{i}x^{i}\mathbf {e} _{i}=\sum _{i}x_{i}\mathbf {e} ^{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b56a8db45d45e1e87ac3f61cb17c61aa7c13c249)
The position of the indices represent how the components are calculated (upper indices should not be confused with [exponentiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponentiation "Exponentiation")). Note that the [summation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Summation "Summation") symbols Σ (capital [Sigma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sigma_(letter) "Sigma (letter)")) and the summation range, indicating summation over all basis vectors (_i_ = 1, 2, ..., _d_), are often [omitted](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_notation "Einstein notation"). The components are related simply by:

![Image 16: {\displaystyle h_{i}^{2}x^{i}=x_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fa11758074e081917fe300f88cf0d3ef29a2514a)
There is no distinguishing widespread notation in use for vector components with respect to the normalized basis; in this article we'll use subscripts for vector components and note that the components are calculated in the normalized basis.

Vector addition and negation are done component-wise just as in Cartesian coordinates with no complication. Extra considerations may be necessary for other vector operations.

Note however, that all of these operations assume that two vectors in a [vector field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_field "Vector field") are bound to the same point (in other words, the tails of vectors coincide). Since basis vectors generally vary in orthogonal coordinates, if two vectors are added whose components are calculated at different points in space, the different basis vectors require consideration.

The [dot product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dot_product "Dot product") in [Cartesian coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinates "Cartesian coordinates") ([Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space") with an [orthonormal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthonormal "Orthonormal") basis set) is simply the sum of the products of components. In orthogonal coordinates, the dot product of two vectors **x** and **y** takes this familiar form when the components of the vectors are calculated in the normalized basis:

![Image 17: {\displaystyle \mathbf {x} \cdot \mathbf {y} =\sum _{i}x_{i}{\hat {\mathbf {e} }}_{i}\cdot \sum _{j}y_{j}{\hat {\mathbf {e} }}_{j}=\sum _{i}x_{i}y_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/516e333be5e06718b9ba14802ef917070541295f)
This is an immediate consequence of the fact that the normalized basis at some point can form a Cartesian coordinate system: the basis set is [orthonormal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthonormal "Orthonormal").

For components in the covariant or contravariant bases,

![Image 18: {\displaystyle \mathbf {x} \cdot \mathbf {y} =\sum _{i}h_{i}^{2}x^{i}y^{i}=\sum _{i}{\frac {x_{i}y_{i}}{h_{i}^{2}}}=\sum _{i}x^{i}y_{i}=\sum _{i}x_{i}y^{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7d1a2637fdb5e6f6eb3d759f1de245fa2699d186)
This can be readily derived by writing out the vectors in component form, normalizing the basis vectors, and taking the dot product. For example, in 2D:

![Image 19: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\mathbf {x} \cdot \mathbf {y} &=\left(x^{1}\mathbf {e} _{1}+x^{2}\mathbf {e} _{2}\right)\cdot \left(y_{1}\mathbf {e} ^{1}+y_{2}\mathbf {e} ^{2}\right)\\[10pt]&=\left(x^{1}h_{1}{\hat {\mathbf {e} }}_{1}+x^{2}h_{2}{\hat {\mathbf {e} }}_{2}\right)\cdot \left(y_{1}{\frac {{\hat {\mathbf {e} }}^{1}}{h_{1}}}+y_{2}{\frac {{\hat {\mathbf {e} }}^{2}}{h_{2}}}\right)=x^{1}y_{1}+x^{2}y_{2}\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b967298ef8aa5d1b20f98c99698d74ed639bbcae)
where the fact that the normalized covariant and contravariant bases are equal has been used.

The [cross product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product "Cross product") in 3D Cartesian coordinates is:

![Image 20: {\displaystyle \mathbf {x} \times \mathbf {y} =(x_{2}y_{3}-x_{3}y_{2}){\hat {\mathbf {e} }}_{1}+(x_{3}y_{1}-x_{1}y_{3}){\hat {\mathbf {e} }}_{2}+(x_{1}y_{2}-x_{2}y_{1}){\hat {\mathbf {e} }}_{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8b33fbef51f4deeb81350400cb34e7f26c8d1184)
The above formula then remains valid in orthogonal coordinates if the components are calculated in the normalized basis.

To construct the cross product in orthogonal coordinates with covariant or contravariant bases we again must simply normalize the basis vectors, for example:

![Image 21: {\displaystyle \mathbf {x} \times \mathbf {y} =\sum _{i}x^{i}\mathbf {e} _{i}\times \sum _{j}y^{j}\mathbf {e} _{j}=\sum _{i}x^{i}h_{i}{\hat {\mathbf {e} }}_{i}\times \sum _{j}y^{j}h_{j}{\hat {\mathbf {e} }}_{j}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c1e73d241f0b945208a9529ff29b17ef130ec0e9)
which, written expanded out,

![Image 22: {\displaystyle \mathbf {x} \times \mathbf {y} =\left(x^{2}y^{3}-x^{3}y^{2}\right){\frac {h_{2}h_{3}}{h_{1}}}\mathbf {e} _{1}+\left(x^{3}y^{1}-x^{1}y^{3}\right){\frac {h_{1}h_{3}}{h_{2}}}\mathbf {e} _{2}+\left(x^{1}y^{2}-x^{2}y^{1}\right){\frac {h_{1}h_{2}}{h_{3}}}\mathbf {e} _{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c726bfe933511ce59d5c5e2d105b5c90419e8c8b)
Terse notation for the cross product, which simplifies generalization to non-orthogonal coordinates and higher dimensions, is possible with the [Levi-Civita tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_tensor "Levi-Civita tensor"), which will have components other than zeros and ones if the scale factors are not all equal to one.

Looking at an infinitesimal displacement from some point, it's apparent that

![Image 23: {\displaystyle d\mathbf {r} =\sum _{i}{\frac {\partial \mathbf {r} }{\partial q^{i}}}\,dq^{i}=\sum _{i}\mathbf {e} _{i}\,dq^{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9624522eba955927dd5d2c70fcac31465f233421)
By [definition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gradient#Gradient_and_the_derivative_or_differential "Gradient"), the gradient of a function must satisfy (this definition remains true if _ƒ_ is any [tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor "Tensor"))

![Image 24: {\displaystyle df=\nabla f\cdot d\mathbf {r} \quad \Rightarrow \quad df=\nabla f\cdot \sum _{i}\mathbf {e} _{i}\,dq^{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/febcf27959a74444848ecdb1368e5c6fa480bcf4)
It follows then that [del operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Del_operator "Del operator") must be:

![Image 25: {\displaystyle \nabla =\sum _{i}\mathbf {e} ^{i}{\frac {\partial }{\partial q^{i}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e39e1f0c310cced188b274b96d681d0a0bccf09e)
and this happens to remain true in general curvilinear coordinates. Quantities like the [gradient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gradient "Gradient") and [Laplacian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laplacian "Laplacian") follow through proper application of this operator.

![Image 33: {\displaystyle \int _{\mathcal {P}}\mathbf {F} \cdot d\mathbf {r} =\int _{\mathcal {P}}\sum _{i}F_{i}\mathbf {e} ^{i}\cdot \sum _{j}\mathbf {e} _{j}\,dq^{j}=\sum _{i}\int _{\mathcal {P}}F_{i}\,dq^{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e9dee1460a939333835157da75959a183895010f)
An infinitesimal element of area for a surface described by holding one coordinate _q k_ constant is:

![Image 34: {\displaystyle dA_{k}=\prod _{i\neq k}ds_{i}=\prod _{i\neq k}h_{i}\,dq^{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7fecb256a6c4a15d6d12ac7a8afd1babe8d7e607)
Similarly, the volume element is:

![Image 35: {\displaystyle dV=\prod _{i}ds_{i}=\prod _{i}h_{i}\,dq^{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6d7191dc2c23ead4505b38a263b236c83b80d18d)
where the large symbol Π (capital [Pi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi_(letter) "Pi (letter)")) indicates a [product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Product_(mathematics) "Product (mathematics)") the same way that a large Σ indicates summation. Note that the product of all the scale factors is the [Jacobian determinant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacobian_determinant "Jacobian determinant").

As an example, the [surface integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_integral "Surface integral") of a vector function **F** over a _q_ 1 = _constant_ surface ![Image 36: {\displaystyle \scriptstyle {\mathcal {S}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4968bf40c4785e9ae26190998b4b27cee3379090) in 3D is:

![Image 37: {\displaystyle \int _{\mathcal {S}}\mathbf {F} \cdot d\mathbf {A} =\int _{\mathcal {S}}\mathbf {F} \cdot {\hat {\mathbf {n} }}\ dA=\int _{\mathcal {S}}\mathbf {F} \cdot {\hat {\mathbf {e} }}_{1}\ dA=\int _{\mathcal {S}}F^{1}{\frac {h_{2}h_{3}}{h_{1}}}\,dq^{2}\,dq^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4101e61c87e53bac6f4debabe0b537d9c1b6d353)
Note that **F**1/_h_ 1 is the component of **F** normal to the surface.
