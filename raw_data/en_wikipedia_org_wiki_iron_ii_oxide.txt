Chemical compound

**Iron(II) oxide** or **ferrous oxide** is the [inorganic compound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inorganic_compound "Inorganic compound") with the formula FeO. Its mineral form is known as [wüstite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/W%C3%BCstite "Wüstite").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron(II)_oxide#cite_note-Mindat-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron(II)_oxide#cite_note-IMA-4) One of several [iron oxides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron_oxide "Iron oxide"), it is a black-colored powder that is sometimes confused with [rust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rust "Rust"), the latter of which consists of hydrated [iron(III) oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron(III)_oxide "Iron(III) oxide") (ferric oxide). Iron(II) oxide also refers to a family of related [non-stoichiometric compounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-stoichiometric_compound "Non-stoichiometric compound"), which are typically iron deficient with compositions ranging from Fe 0.84 O to Fe 0.95 O.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron(II)_oxide#cite_note-Greenwood-5)

-----------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Iron(II)_oxide&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Preparation")]

FeO can be prepared by the thermal decomposition of [iron(II) oxalate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron(II)_oxalate "Iron(II) oxalate").

FeC 2 O 4 → FeO + CO 2 + CO
The procedure is conducted under an inert atmosphere to avoid the formation of iron(III) oxide (Fe 2 O 3). A similar procedure can also be used for the synthesis of [manganous oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manganous_oxide "Manganous oxide") and [stannous oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stannous_oxide "Stannous oxide").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron(II)_oxide#cite_note-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron(II)_oxide#cite_note-7)

Stoichiometric FeO can be prepared by heating Fe 0.95 O with metallic iron at 770°C and 36 kbar.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron(II)_oxide#cite_note-Wells-8)

Reactions
---------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Iron(II)_oxide&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Reactions")]

FeO is thermodynamically unstable below 575°C, tending to disproportionate to metal and [Fe 3 O 4](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron(II,III)_oxide "Iron(II,III) oxide"):[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron(II)_oxide#cite_note-Greenwood-5)

4 FeO → Fe + Fe 3 O 4

Structure
---------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Iron(II)_oxide&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Structure")]

[Iron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron "Iron")(II) oxide adopts the cubic, [rock salt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride "Sodium chloride") structure, where iron atoms are octahedrally coordinated by oxygen atoms and the oxygen atoms octahedrally coordinated by iron atoms. The non-stoichiometry occurs because of the ease of oxidation of Fe II to Fe III effectively replacing a small portion of Fe II with two-thirds their number of Fe III, which take up tetrahedral positions in the close packed oxide lattice.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron(II)_oxide#cite_note-Wells-8)

In contrast to the crystalline solid, in the molten state iron atoms are coordinated by predominantly 4 or 5 oxygen atoms.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron(II)_oxide#cite_note-ShiFeOx2020-9)

Below 200 K there is a minor change to the structure which changes the symmetry to rhombohedral and samples become [antiferromagnetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiferromagnetism "Antiferromagnetism").[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron(II)_oxide#cite_note-Wells-8)[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron(II)_oxide#cite_note-10)

Occurrence in nature
--------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Iron(II)_oxide&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Occurrence in nature")]

Iron(II) oxide makes up approximately 9% of the Earth's [mantle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mantle_(geology) "Mantle (geology)"). Within the mantle, it may be electrically conductive, which is a possible explanation for perturbations in Earth's rotation not accounted for by accepted models of the mantle's properties.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron(II)_oxide#cite_note-11)

Uses
----

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Iron(II)_oxide&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Uses")]

Iron(II) oxide is used as a [pigment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pigment "Pigment"). It is [FDA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Food_and_Drug_Administration "Food and Drug Administration")-approved for use in cosmetics and it is used in some [tattoo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tattoo "Tattoo") inks. It can also be used as a phosphate remover from home aquaria.
