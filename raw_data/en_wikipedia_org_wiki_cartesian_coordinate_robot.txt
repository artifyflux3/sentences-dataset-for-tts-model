A [plotter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plotter "Plotter") is a type of Cartesian coordinate robot.

A **Cartesian coordinate robot** (also called **linear robot**) is an [industrial robot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Industrial_robot "Industrial robot") whose three [principal axes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principal_axis_(mechanics) "Principal axis (mechanics)") of control are linear (i.e. they move in a straight line rather than rotate) and are at [right angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_angle "Right angle") to each other.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_robot#cite_note-1) The three [sliding joints](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prismatic_joint "Prismatic joint") correspond to moving the wrist up-down, in-out, back-forth. Among other advantages, this mechanical arrangement simplifies the [robot control](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robot_control "Robot control")[arm solution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arm_solution "Arm solution"). It has high reliability and precision when operating in three-dimensional space.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_robot#cite_note-2) As a robot coordinate system, it is also effective for horizontal travel and for stacking bins.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_robot#cite_note-3)

Gantry robot

Robots have [mechanisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanism_(engineering) "Mechanism (engineering)") consisting of rigid links connected together by [joints](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_pair "Kinematic pair") with either linear (prismatic _P_) or rotary (revolute _R_) motion, or combinations of the two.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_robot#cite_note-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_robot#cite_note-5)Active prismatic _P_ and active revolute _R_ joints are driven by motors under programmable control to manipulate objects to perform complex automated tasks. The linear motion of active prismatic _P_ joints may be driven by rotary motors through gears or pulleys. Cartesian coordinate robots are controlled by mutually [perpendicular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular#:~:text=In_elementary_geometry,_the_property,intersect_at_a_right_angle. "Perpendicular") active prismatic _P_ joints that are aligned with the _X, Y, Z_ axes of a [Cartesian coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system "Cartesian coordinate system").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_robot#cite_note-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_robot#cite_note-7)Although not strictly ‘robots’, other types of [manipulators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manipulator_(device) "Manipulator (device)"), such as computer [numerically controlled](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Numerical_control "Numerical control") (CNC) machines, [3D printers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3D_printing#Processes_and_printers "3D printing") or [pen plotters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plotter "Plotter"), also have the same mechanical arrangement of mutually perpendicular active prismatic _P_ joints.

A single chain of links and joints connects a moving object to a base of [serial manipulators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Serial_manipulator "Serial manipulator"). Multiple chains (limbs) connect the moving object to the base of [parallel manipulators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_manipulator "Parallel manipulator").[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_robot#cite_note-8) Most Cartesian coordinate robots are fully serial or a combination of serial and parallel connected linkages. However, there are some Cartesian coordinate robots that are [fully parallel-connected](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_parallel_manipulators "Cartesian parallel manipulators").[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_robot#cite_note-9)[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_robot#cite_note-10)[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_robot#cite_note-11)

Since they are driven by linear active prismatic _P_ joints, Cartesian coordinate robots typically manipulate objects with only linear translation _T_[degrees of freedom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degrees_of_freedom_(mechanics) "Degrees of freedom (mechanics)"). However, some Cartesian coordinate robots also have [rotational _R_ degrees of freedom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_parallel_manipulators "Cartesian parallel manipulators").[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_robot#cite_note-12)

Each axis of a Cartesian coordinate robot typically is a [linear stage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_stage "Linear stage") consisting of a linear [actuator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Actuator "Actuator") geometrically parallel with [linear bearings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear-motion_bearing "Linear-motion bearing"). The linear actuator is typically between two linear bearings spaced apart from each other to support [moment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_(physics) "Moment (physics)") loads. Two perpendicular linear stages stacked on top of each other form a [XY table](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-Y_table "X-Y table"). Examples of XY tables include the XY axes of [milling machines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milling_(machining) "Milling (machining)") or precision positioning stages. At least one of the linear stages of [cantilevered](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cantilever "Cantilever") Cartesian coordinate robots is supported at only one end. Cantilevered construction provides accessibility to parts for pick-and-place applications such as [laboratory automation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laboratory_automation "Laboratory automation") for example. Cartesian coordinate robots with the horizontal member supported at both ends are sometimes called gantry robots; mechanically, they resemble [gantry cranes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gantry_crane "Gantry crane"), although the latter are not generally robots. Gantry robots are often quite large and may support heavy loads.

Popular applications for Cartesian coordinate robots are computer numerical control machines ([CNC machine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CNC_machine "CNC machine")) and [3D printing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3D_printing "3D printing"). The simplest application is used in milling machines and [plotters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plotter "Plotter") where a tool such as a router or pen translates across an _X-Y_ plane and is raised and lowered onto a surface to create a precise design.

[Pick and place](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Automated_storage_and_retrieval_system "Automated storage and retrieval system") machines are another application for Cartesian coordinate robots. For example, overhead gantry Cartesian robots are [applied](https://robot.cfp.co.ir/en/robots/cartesian-gantry) for continuous parts loading and unloading on [CNC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CNC "CNC") lathes production lines, performing 3-axis _(X, Y, Z)_ pick and place operations of heavy loads with high speed performance and high positioning accuracy. In general, overhead gantry Cartesian robots are suitable for many [automation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Automation "Automation") systems.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_robot#cite_note-13)
