A green angle formed by two red [rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ray_(geometry) "Ray (geometry)") on the [Cartesian coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system "Cartesian coordinate system")

In [Euclidean geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_geometry "Euclidean geometry"), an **angle** is the opening between two [lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_(geometry) "Line (geometry)") in the same [plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_plane "Euclidean plane") that meet at a point.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-1) The term _angle_ is used to denote both geometric figures and their size or [magnitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnitude_(mathematics) "Magnitude (mathematics)"). **Angular measure** or **measure of angle** are sometimes used to distinguish between the measurement and figure itself. The measurement of angles is intrinsically linked with circles and rotation. For an ordinary angle, this is often visualized or defined using the [arc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arc_(geometry) "Arc (geometry)") of a [circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle "Circle") centered at the vertex and lying between the sides.

An angle is defined as a figure where two rays share a common endpoint. The two rays are called the _[sides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Side\_(plane\_geometry) "Side (plane geometry)")_ of the angle, and the point they share is called the _[vertex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex\_(geometry) "Vertex (geometry)")_ of the angle.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-:0-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-3) More generally, angles are also formed wherever two [line segments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_segment "Line segment") come together, such as at the corners of triangles and other polygons,[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-:0-2) or at the intersection of two planes or [curves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curve "Curve"), in which case the rays lying [tangent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent "Tangent") to each curve at the point of intersection define the angle.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911-4) The sides divide the plane of the angle into two regions: the _interior of the angle_ and the _exterior of the angle_. The interior of the angle is also referred to as an **angular sector**.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-6)[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-10)

### Units of measurement

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Angle&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Units of measurement")]

Angles are measured in various units, the most common being the [degree](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_(angle) "Degree (angle)") (denoted by the symbol **¬∞**), [radian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radian "Radian") (denoted by the symbol **rad**) and [turn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turn_(angle) "Turn (angle)"). These units differ in the way they divide up a _full angle_, an angle where one ray, initially congruent to the other, performs a compete rotation about the vertex to return back to its starting position.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-14)

Degrees and turns are defined directly with reference to a full angle, which measures 1 turn or 360¬∞.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-15) A measure in turns gives an angle's size as a proportion of a full angle and a degree can be considered as a subdivision of a turn. Radians are not defined directly in relation to a full angle (see _[¬ßMeasuring angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse\_angle#Measuring\_angles)_), but in such a way that its measure is 2 œÄ rad, approximately 6.28 rad.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-16)

[![Image 17](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Angle_types.svg/250px-Angle_types.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Angle_types.svg)

Common types of angles

*   An angle equal to 0¬∞ or not turned is called a _zero angle_.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMoser197141-17)
*   An angle smaller than a right angle (less than 90¬∞) is called an _acute angle_.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGodfreySiddons19199-18)
*   An angle equal to ‚Å†1/4‚Å†[turn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turn_(angle) "Turn (angle)") (90¬∞ or ‚Å†œÄ/2‚Å†rad) is called a _[right angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right\_angle "Right angle")_. Two lines that form a right angle are said to be _[normal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal\_(geometry) "Normal (geometry)")_, _[orthogonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonality "Orthogonality")_, or _[perpendicular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular "Perpendicular")_.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMoser197171-19)
*   An angle larger than a right angle and smaller than a straight angle (between 90¬∞ and 180¬∞) is called an _obtuse angle_[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGodfreySiddons19199-18) ("obtuse" meaning "blunt").
*   An angle equal to ‚Å†1/2‚Å†turn (180¬∞ or œÄ rad) is called a _straight angle_.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMoser197141-17)
*   An angle larger than a straight angle but less than 1 turn (between 180¬∞ and 360¬∞) is called a _reflex angle_.
*   An angle equal to 1 turn (360¬∞ or 2 œÄ rad) is called a _full angle_, _complete angle_, _round angle_ or _perigon_.
*   An angle that is not a multiple of a right angle is called an _oblique angle_.

### Addition and subtraction

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Angle&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Addition and subtraction")]

[![Image 18](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Angle_addition.svg/250px-Angle_addition.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Angle_addition.svg)

The angle addition postulate defines addition and subtraction of angles: _Œ∏_ + _Œ±_ = _œÜ_; _œÜ_ ‚àí _Œ±_ = _Œ∏_.

The **angle addition postulate** states that if D is a point lying in the interior of ![Image 19: {\displaystyle \angle {\text{BAC}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4c60e50d75b3faea7f9b5b509ed40876d17d6f9a) then:[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-:1-20)![Image 20: {\displaystyle m\angle {\text{BAC}}=m\angle {\text{BAD}}+m\angle {\text{DAC}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b66a144a7d9dfb5300336893b096fa9273c84381) This relationship _defines_ what it means add any two angles: their vertices are placed together while sharing a side to create a new larger angle. The measure of the new larger angle is the sum of the measures of the two angles. Subtraction follows from rearrangement of the formula.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-:1-20)

### Adjacent and vertical angles

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Angle&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Adjacent and vertical angles")]

[![Image 21](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Adjacentangles.svg/250px-Adjacentangles.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Adjacentangles.svg)

Angles A and B are adjacent.

"Vertical angle" redirects here; not to be confused with [Zenith angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zenith_angle "Zenith angle").

_**Adjacent angles**_ (abbreviated _adj. ‚à†s_), are angles that share a common vertex and edge but do not share any interior points. In other words, they are angles side by side or adjacent, sharing an "arm".

[![Image 22](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Vertical_Angles.svg/250px-Vertical_Angles.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Vertical_Angles.svg)

Angles A and B are a pair of vertical angles; angles C and D are a pair of vertical angles. [Hatch marks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hatch_mark#Congruency_notation "Hatch mark") are used here to show angle equality.

_**Vertical angles**_ are formed when two straight lines intersect at a point producing four angles. A pair of angles opposite each other are called _vertical angles_, _opposite angles_ or _vertically opposite angles_ (abbreviated _vert. opp. ‚à†s_),[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-tb-21) where "vertical" refers to the sharing of a vertex, rather than an up-down orientation. A theorem states that vertical angles are always congruent or equal to each other.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-FOOTNOTEShuteShirkPorter196025%E2%80%9327-22) A [_transversal_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transversal_(geometry) "Transversal (geometry)") is a line that intersects a pair of (often parallel) lines and is associated with _exterior angles_, _interior angles_, _alternate exterior angles_, _alternate interior angles_, _corresponding angles_, and _consecutive interior angles_.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJacobs1974255-23)

### Combining angle pairs

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Angle&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Combining angle pairs")]

When summing two angles (either adjacent or separated in space), three special cases are named _complementary_, _supplementary_, and _explementary_ angles.

[![Image 23](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Complement_angle.svg/250px-Complement_angle.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Complement_angle.svg)

Angles a and b are _complementary_ angles

_**Complementary angles**_ are angle pairs whose measures sum to a right angle (‚Å†1/4‚Å† turn, 90¬∞, or ‚Å†œÄ/2‚Å† rad).[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-24) If the two complementary angles are adjacent, their non-shared sides form a right angle. In a [right-angle triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_triangle "Right triangle") the two acute angles are complementary as the sum of the internal angles of a [triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle "Triangle") is 180¬∞. The difference between an angle and a right angle is termed the _complement_ of the angle.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911-4)

[![Image 24](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Angle_obtuse_acute_straight.svg/250px-Angle_obtuse_acute_straight.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Angle_obtuse_acute_straight.svg)

Angles a and b are _supplementary_ angles

_**Supplementary angles**_ sum to a straight angle (‚Å†1/2‚Å† turn, 180¬∞, or œÄ rad).[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-25) If the two supplementary angles are [adjacent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#adjacent), their non-shared sides form a straight angle or [straight line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_(geometry) "Line (geometry)") and are called a _linear pair of angles_.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJacobs197497-26) The difference between an angle and a straight angle is termed the _supplement_ of the angle.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-27)

Examples of non-adjacent complementary angles include the consecutive angles of a [parallelogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram "Parallelogram") and opposite angles of a [cyclic quadrilateral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclic_quadrilateral "Cyclic quadrilateral"). For a circle with center O, and [tangent lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_lines_to_circles "Tangent lines to circles") from an exterior point P touching the circle at points T and Q, the resulting angles ‚à†TPQ and ‚à†TOQ are supplementary.

[![Image 25](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Conjugate_Angles.svg/250px-Conjugate_Angles.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Conjugate_Angles.svg)

Angles AOB and COD are _explementary_ _or conjugate_ angles

_**Explementary angles**_ or _**conjugate angles**_ sum to a full angle (1 turn, 360¬∞, or 2 œÄ radians).[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-28) The difference between an angle and a full angle is termed the _explement_ or _conjugate_ of the angle.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-29)[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-30)

[![Image 26](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/ExternalAngles.svg/250px-ExternalAngles.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:ExternalAngles.svg)

Internal and external angles

*   An angle that is part of a [simple polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simple_polygon "Simple polygon") is called an _[interior angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interior\_angle "Interior angle")_ if it lies on the inside of that simple polygon. A simple [concave polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concave_polygon "Concave polygon") has at least one interior angle, that is, a reflex angle. In [Euclidean geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_geometry "Euclidean geometry"), the measures of the interior angles of a [triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle "Triangle") add up to œÄ radians, 180¬∞, or ‚Å†1/2‚Å† turn; the measures of the interior angles of a simple [convex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_polygon "Convex polygon")[quadrilateral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral "Quadrilateral") add up to 2 œÄ radians, 360¬∞, or 1 turn. In general, the measures of the interior angles of a simple convex [polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon "Polygon") with _n_ sides add up to (_n_‚àí2)œÄ radians, or (_n_‚àí2)180 degrees, (_n_‚àí2)2 right angles, or (_n_‚àí2)‚Å†1/2‚Å†turn.
*   The supplement of an interior angle is called an _[exterior angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior\_angle "Exterior angle")_; that is, an interior angle and an exterior angle form a [linear pair of angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#Linear_pair_of_angles). There are two exterior angles at each vertex of the polygon, each determined by extending one of the two sides of the polygon that meet at the vertex; these two angles are vertical and hence are equal. An exterior angle measures the amount of rotation one must make at a vertex to trace the polygon.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHendersonTaimina2005104-31) If the corresponding interior angle is a reflex angle, the exterior angle should be considered [negative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Negative_number "Negative number"). Even in a non-simple polygon, it may be possible to define the exterior angle. Still, one will have to pick an [orientation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orientation_(space) "Orientation (space)") of the [plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_(mathematics) "Plane (mathematics)") (or [surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_(mathematics) "Surface (mathematics)")) to decide the sign of the exterior angle measure. In Euclidean geometry, the sum of the exterior angles of a simple convex polygon, if only one of the two exterior angles is assumed at each vertex, will be one full turn (360¬∞). The exterior angle here could be called a _supplementary exterior angle_. Exterior angles are commonly used in [Logo Turtle programs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logo_(programming_language) "Logo (programming language)") when drawing regular polygons.
*   In a [triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle "Triangle"), the [bisectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bisection "Bisection") of two exterior angles and the bisector of the other interior angle are [concurrent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concurrent_lines "Concurrent lines") (meet at a single point).[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-Johnson-32): 149 
*   In a triangle, three intersection points, each of an external angle bisector with the opposite [extended side](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extended_side "Extended side"), are [collinear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collinearity "Collinearity").[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-Johnson-32): 149 
*   In a triangle, three intersection points, two between an interior angle bisector and the opposite side, and the third between the other exterior angle bisector and the opposite side extended are collinear.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-Johnson-32): 149 
*   Some authors use the name _exterior angle_ of a simple polygon to mean the _explement exterior angle_ (_not_ supplement!) of the interior angle.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-33) This conflicts with the above usage.

*   The angle between two [planes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_(mathematics) "Plane (mathematics)") (such as two adjacent faces of a [polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyhedron "Polyhedron")) is called a _[dihedral angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihedral\_angle "Dihedral angle")_.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911-4) It may be defined as the acute angle between two lines [normal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_(geometry) "Normal (geometry)") to the planes.
*   The angle between a plane and an intersecting straight line is complementary to the angle between the intersecting line and the [normal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_(geometry) "Normal (geometry)") to the plane.

Angle measurement encompasses both direct physical measurement using a measuring instrument such as a [protractor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protractor "Protractor"), as well as the theoretical calculation of angle size from other known quantities. While the measurement of angles is intrinsically linked with rotation and circles, there are various perspectives as to exactly _what_ is being measured, including amongst others: the amount of rotation about the vertex of one ray to the other[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-34); the amount of opening between the rays[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-35); or the length of the arc that subtends the angle at the centre of a unit circle.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-SIBrochure9thEd-36)

The measurement of angles is inherently different from the measurement of other physical quantities such as length.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-37) Angles of special significance (such as the right angle) inform the systems and units of angular measurement, which is not the case for length where the units of measurement (metres, feet) are arbitrary.

Broadly there are two approaches to measuring angles: relative to a reference angle (such as a right angle); and circular measurement.

A chosen reference angle (right angle, straight angle or full angle) can be divided into equal parts, and the size of one part used as a unit for measurement of other angles.

In the most common method of practical angular measurement a right angle is divided into 90 equal parts called [degrees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_(angle) "Degree (angle)"), while in the rarely used _centesimal_ system, a right angle is divided into 100 equal parts called [gradians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gradian "Gradian").[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-38)[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-:2-39)

### Circular measurement

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Angle&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Circular measurement")]

[![Image 27](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Basic_angle_in_circle.svg/250px-Basic_angle_in_circle.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Basic_angle_in_circle.svg)

The angle size ![Image 28: {\displaystyle \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e5ab2664b422d53eb0c7df3b87e1360d75ad9af) can be measured as _s_/_r_ radians or _s_/_C_ turns

With circular measurement an angle is placed within a circle of any size, with the vertex at the circle's centre and the sides intersecting the perimeter.

An [arc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circular_arc "Circular arc") with length s is formed as the perimeter between the two points of intersection, which is said to [subtend](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subtended_angle "Subtended angle") the angle. The length _s_ can be used to measure the angle's size _Œ∏_, however as _s_ is dependent on the size of the circle chosen, the measure must be scaled. This can be done by taking the ratio of _s_ to either the radius _r_ or circumference _C_ of the circle.

The ratio of the length s by the radius r is the number of [radians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radian "Radian") in the angle,[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-SIBrochure9thEd-36) while the ratio of length s by the circumference C is the number of [turns](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turn_(angle) "Turn (angle)"): ![Image 29: {\displaystyle \theta ={\frac {s}{r}}\,\mathrm {rad} ={\frac {s}{C}}\,\mathrm {turn} ={\frac {s}{2\pi r}}\,\mathrm {turn} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5fa34ca426126312b44744309c348b69fced0ce1)

[![Image 30](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Angle_measure.svg/250px-Angle_measure.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Angle_measure.svg)

The measure of angle _Œ∏_ is ‚Å†_s_/_r_‚Å† radians.

The value of _Œ∏_ thus defined is independent of the size of the circle: if the length of the radius is changed, then both the circumference and the arc length change in the same proportion, so the ratios ‚Å†_s_/_r_‚Å† and ‚Å†_s_/_C_‚Å† are unaltered.[[nb 1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-40)

The ratio ‚Å†_s_/_r_‚Å† is sometimes called the "circular measure" of an angle,[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-41)[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-:2-39) a phrase commonly used prior to the widespread adoption of the name "radian".[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-42)

Angles of the same size are said to be _equal_, _congruent_ or _equal in measure_.

### Dimensional analysis

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Angle&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: Dimensional analysis")]

In mathematics and the [International System of Quantities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Quantities "International System of Quantities"), an angle is defined as a dimensionless quantity,[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-43) and in particular, the [radian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radian "Radian") is defined as dimensionless in the [International System of Units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Units "International System of Units").[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-44) This convention prevents angles providing information for [dimensional analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimensional_analysis "Dimensional analysis"). For example, when one measures an angle in radians by dividing the arc length by the radius, one is essentially dividing a length by another length, and the units of length cancel each other out. Therefore the result‚Äîthe angle‚Äîdoesn't have a physical "dimension" like meters or seconds.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-45) This holds true with all angle units, such as radians, degrees, or turns‚Äîthey all represent a pure number quantifying how much something has turned.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-46) This is why, in many equations, angle units seem to "disappear" during calculations, which feels inconsistent and can lead to mixing up angle units.[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-47)[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-48)

This has led to significant discussion among scientists and educators. Some scientists have suggested treating the angle as having its own fundamental dimension, similar to length or time.[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-Quincey-49) This would mean that angle units like radians would always be explicitly present in calculations, facilitating dimensional analysis. However, this approach would also require changing many well-known mathematical and physics formulas, making them longer and perhaps a bit less familiar.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-50) For now, the established practice is to write angle units where appropriate but consider them dimensionless, understanding that these units are important but behave differently from meters or kilograms.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-51)

[![Image 31](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Angles_on_the_unit_circle.svg/250px-Angles_on_the_unit_circle.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Angles_on_the_unit_circle.svg)

Measuring from the [x-axis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-axis "X-axis"), angles on the [unit circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_circle "Unit circle") count as positive in the [counterclockwise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Counterclockwise "Counterclockwise") direction, and negative in the [clockwise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clockwise "Clockwise") direction.

An angle denoted as ‚à†BAC might refer to any of four angles: the clockwise angle from B to C about A, the anticlockwise angle from B to C about A, the clockwise angle from C to B about A, or the anticlockwise angle from C to B about A, It is therefore frequently helpful to impose a convention that allows positive and negative angular values to represent [orientations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orientation_(geometry) "Orientation (geometry)") and/or [rotations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation_(mathematics) "Rotation (mathematics)") in opposite directions or "sense" relative to some reference.

In a two-dimensional [Cartesian coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system "Cartesian coordinate system"), an angle is typically defined by its two sides, with its vertex at the origin. The _initial side_ is on the positive [x-axis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-axis "X-axis"), while the other side or _terminal side_ is defined by the measure from the initial side in radians, degrees, or turns, with _positive angles_ representing rotations toward the positive [y-axis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Y-axis "Y-axis") and _negative angles_ representing rotations toward the negative _y_-axis. When Cartesian coordinates are represented by _standard position_, defined by the _x_-axis rightward and the _y_-axis upward, positive rotations are [anticlockwise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anticlockwise "Anticlockwise"), and negative cycles are [clockwise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clockwise "Clockwise").

In many contexts, an angle of ‚àí_Œ∏_ is effectively equivalent to an angle of "one full turn minus _Œ∏_". For example, an orientation represented as ‚àí45¬∞ is effectively equal to an orientation defined as 360¬∞ ‚àí 45¬∞ or 315¬∞. Although the final position is the same, a physical rotation (movement) of ‚àí45¬∞ is not the same as a rotation of 315¬∞ (for example, the rotation of a person holding a broom resting on a dusty floor would leave visually different traces of swept regions on the floor).

In three-dimensional geometry, "clockwise" and "anticlockwise" have no absolute meaning, so the direction of positive and negative angles must be defined in terms of an [orientation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orientability "Orientability"), which is typically determined by a [normal vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_(geometry) "Normal (geometry)") passing through the angle's vertex and perpendicular to the plane in which the rays of the angle lie.

In [navigation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Navigation "Navigation"), [bearings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bearing_(navigation) "Bearing (navigation)") or [azimuth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Azimuth "Azimuth") are measured relative to north. By convention, viewed from above, bearing angles are positive clockwise, so a bearing of 45¬∞ corresponds to a north-east orientation. Negative bearings are not used in navigation, so a north-west orientation corresponds to a bearing of 315¬∞.

*   Angles that have the same measure (i.e., the same magnitude) are said to be _equal_ or _[congruent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congruence\_(geometry) "Congruence (geometry)")_. An angle is defined by its measure and is not dependent upon the lengths of the sides of the angle (e.g., all _right angles_ are equal in measure).
*   Two angles that share terminal sides, but differ in size by an integer multiple of a turn, are called _coterminal angles_.
*   The _reference angle_ (sometimes called _related angle_) for any angle _Œ∏_ in standard position is the positive acute angle between the terminal side of _Œ∏_ and the x-axis (positive or negative).[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-52)[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-53) Procedurally, the magnitude of the reference angle for a given angle may determined by taking the angle's magnitude [modulo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modulo "Modulo")‚Å†1/2‚Å† turn, 180¬∞, or œÄ radians, then stopping if the angle is acute, otherwise taking the supplementary angle, 180¬∞ minus the reduced magnitude. For example, an angle of 30 degrees is already a reference angle, and an angle of 150 degrees also has a reference angle of 30 degrees (180¬∞ ‚àí 150¬∞). Angles of 210¬∞ and 510¬∞ correspond to a reference angle of 30 degrees as well (210¬∞ mod 180¬∞ = 30¬∞, 510¬∞ mod 180¬∞ = 150¬∞ whose supplementary angle is 30¬∞).

For an angular unit, it is definitional that the [angle addition postulate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle_addition_postulate "Angle addition postulate") holds, however some measurements or quantities related to angles are in use that do not satisfy this postulate:

*   The _[slope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slope "Slope")_ or _gradient_ is equal to the [tangent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_(trigonometric_function) "Tangent (trigonometric function)") of the angle and is often expressed as a percentage ("rise" over "run"). For very small values (less than 5%), the slope of a line is approximately the measure in radians of its angle with the horizontal direction. An [elevation grade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grade_(slope) "Grade (slope)") is a slope used to indicate the steepness of roads, paths and railway lines.
*   The _[spread](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spread\_(rational\_trigonometry) "Spread (rational trigonometry)")_ between two lines is defined in [rational geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_geometry "Rational geometry") as the square of the sine of the angle between the lines. As the sine of an angle and the sine of its supplementary angle are the same, any angle of rotation that maps one of the lines into the other leads to the same value for the spread between the lines.
*   Although done rarely, one can report the direct results of [trigonometric functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trigonometric_functions "Trigonometric functions"), such as the [sine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sine "Sine") of the angle.

Bisecting and trisecting angles
-------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Angle&action=edit&section=20 "Edit section: Bisecting and trisecting angles")]

The [ancient Greek mathematicians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_mathematics "Greek mathematics") knew how to bisect an angle (divide it into two angles of equal measure) using only a [compass and straightedge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compass_and_straightedge "Compass and straightedge") but could only trisect certain angles. In 1837, [Pierre Wantzel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre_Wantzel "Pierre Wantzel") showed that this construction could not be performed for most angles.

Dot product and generalisations
-------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Angle&action=edit&section=21 "Edit section: Dot product and generalisations")]

In the [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space"), the angle _Œ∏_ between two [Euclidean vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_vector "Euclidean vector")**u** and **v** is related to their [dot product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dot_product "Dot product") and their lengths by the formula ![Image 33: {\displaystyle \mathbf {u} \cdot \mathbf {v} =\cos(\theta )\left\|\mathbf {u} \right\|\left\|\mathbf {v} \right\|.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9a18878af580581a1a8c122ab324e722efd5a12f)

This formula supplies an easy method to find the angle between two planes (or curved surfaces) from their [normal vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_vector "Normal vector") and between [skew lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skew_lines "Skew lines") from their vector equations.

To define angles in an abstract real [inner product space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inner_product_space "Inner product space"), we replace the Euclidean dot product ( **¬∑** ) by the inner product ![Image 34: {\displaystyle \langle \cdot ,\cdot \rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9a50080b735975d8001c9552ac2134b49ad534c0), i.e. ![Image 35: {\displaystyle \langle \mathbf {u} ,\mathbf {v} \rangle =\cos(\theta )\ \left\|\mathbf {u} \right\|\left\|\mathbf {v} \right\|.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9c2ca506e5af6ea05b0ce72517cabaae9b31df82)

In a complex [inner product space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inner_product_space "Inner product space"), the expression for the cosine above may give non-real values, so it is replaced with ![Image 36: {\displaystyle \operatorname {Re} \left(\langle \mathbf {u} ,\mathbf {v} \rangle \right)=\cos(\theta )\left\|\mathbf {u} \right\|\left\|\mathbf {v} \right\|.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/39e31b9f9e22e6be0fde65116719d8a11b185ef6) or, more commonly, using the absolute value, with ![Image 37: {\displaystyle \left|\langle \mathbf {u} ,\mathbf {v} \rangle \right|=\left|\cos(\theta )\right|\left\|\mathbf {u} \right\|\left\|\mathbf {v} \right\|.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fcb6028c88ccd8fe54d81c245a6e17a49c711e94)

The latter definition ignores the direction of the vectors. It thus describes the angle between one-dimensional subspaces ![Image 38: {\displaystyle \operatorname {span} (\mathbf {u} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/627f7185adb8f4ba1802de7db03ef9f42474ac5a) and ![Image 39: {\displaystyle \operatorname {span} (\mathbf {v} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/00563c64123d9bffafc970072a0654965a408045) spanned by the vectors ![Image 40: {\displaystyle \mathbf {u} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/261e20fe101de02a771021d9d4466c0ad3e352d7) and ![Image 41: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35c1866e359fbfd2e0f606c725ba5cc37a5195d6) correspondingly.

### Angles in Riemannian geometry

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Angle&action=edit&section=24 "Edit section: Angles in Riemannian geometry")]

In [Riemannian geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_geometry "Riemannian geometry"), the [metric tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_tensor "Metric tensor") is used to define the angle between two [tangents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent "Tangent"). Where _U_ and _V_ are tangent vectors and _g_ _ij_ are the components of the metric tensor _G_, ![Image 49: {\displaystyle \cos \theta ={\frac {g_{ij}U^{i}V^{j}}{\sqrt {\left|g_{ij}U^{i}U^{j}\right|\left|g_{ij}V^{i}V^{j}\right|}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a9685961ef6102dc031001a0c97cd7953eeee7b3)

A [hyperbolic angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_angle "Hyperbolic angle") is an [argument](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Argument_of_a_function "Argument of a function") of a [hyperbolic function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_function "Hyperbolic function") just as the _circular angle_ is the argument of a [circular function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circular_function "Circular function"). The comparison can be visualized as the size of the openings of a [hyperbolic sector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_sector "Hyperbolic sector") and a [circular sector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circular_sector "Circular sector") since the [areas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area "Area") of these sectors correspond to the angle magnitudes in each case.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-55) Unlike the circular angle, the hyperbolic angle is unbounded. When the circular and hyperbolic functions are viewed as [infinite series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinite_series "Infinite series") in their angle argument, the circular ones are just [alternating series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alternating_series "Alternating series") forms of the hyperbolic functions. This comparison of the two series corresponding to functions of angles was described by [Leonhard Euler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leonhard_Euler "Leonhard Euler") in _[Introduction to the Analysis of the Infinite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Introduction\_to\_the\_Analysis\_of\_the\_Infinite "Introduction to the Analysis of the Infinite")_ (1748).

History and etymology
---------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Angle&action=edit&section=26 "Edit section: History and etymology")]

The word _angle_ comes from the [Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin "Latin") word _angulus_, meaning "corner". [Cognate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cognate "Cognate") words include the [Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_language "Greek language")·ºÄŒ≥Œ∫œçŒªŒøœÇ (ankylŒøs) meaning "crooked, curved" and the [English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English_language "English language") word "[ankle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ankle "Ankle")". Both are connected with the [Proto-Indo-European](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language "Proto-Indo-European language") root _*ank-_, meaning "to bend" or "bow".[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-56)

Philosophers have debated the nature of angles for millennia, with some arguing that angles are a measure (quantity), and others saying they are a kind of shape defined by the lines that bound it (qualitative relation), and still others saying an angle is both.[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-57) Pedagogically, the accepted answer is that angles are defined as figures, and the measure of an angle is defined as the number of congruent non-overlapping copies of a unit angle necessary to cover the angle and its interior. Angles are said to be equal in measure and [similar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Similarity_(geometry) "Similarity (geometry)") or [congruent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congruence_(geometry) "Congruence (geometry)") in shape.[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-58)

[Euclid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid "Euclid") defines a plane angle as the inclination to each other, in a plane, of two lines that meet each other and do not lie straight with respect to each other. According to the Neoplatonic metaphysician [Proclus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proclus "Proclus"), an angle must be either a quality, a quantity, or a relationship. The first concept, angle as quality, was used by [Eudemus of Rhodes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eudemus_of_Rhodes "Eudemus of Rhodes"), who regarded an angle as a deviation from a [straight line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Straight_line "Straight line"); the second, angle as quantity, by [Carpus of Antioch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carpus_of_Antioch "Carpus of Antioch"), who regarded it as the interval or space between the intersecting lines; Euclid adopted the third: angle as a relationship.[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-59)

### Vertical angle theorem

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Angle&action=edit&section=27 "Edit section: Vertical angle theorem")]

The equality of vertically opposite angles is called the _vertical angle theorem_. [Eudemus of Rhodes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eudemus_of_Rhodes "Eudemus of Rhodes") attributed the proof to [Thales of Miletus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thales "Thales").[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-60)[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-FOOTNOTEShuteShirkPorter196025%E2%80%9327-22) The proposition showed that since both of a pair of vertical angles are supplementary to both of the adjacent angles, the vertical angles are equal in measure. According to a historical note,[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-FOOTNOTEShuteShirkPorter196025%E2%80%9327-22) when Thales visited Egypt, he observed that whenever the Egyptians drew two intersecting lines, they would measure the vertical angles to make sure that they were equal. Thales concluded that one could prove that all vertical angles are equal if one accepted some general notions such as:

*   All straight angles are equal.
*   Equals added to equals are equal.
*   Equals subtracted from equals are equal.

When two adjacent angles form a straight line, they are supplementary. Therefore, if we assume that the measure of angle _A_ equals _x_, the measure of angle _C_ would be 180¬∞ ‚àí _x_. Similarly, the measure of angle _D_ would be 180¬∞ ‚àí _x_. Both angle _C_ and angle _D_ have measures equal to 180¬∞ ‚àí _x_ and are congruent. Since angle _B_ is supplementary to both angles _C_ and _D_, either of these angle measures may be used to determine the measure of Angle _B_. Using the measure of either angle _C_ or angle _D_, we find the measure of angle _B_ to be 180¬∞ ‚àí (180¬∞ ‚àí _x_) = _x_. Therefore, both angle _A_ and angle _B_ have measures equal to _x_ and are equal in measure.

Angles in geography and astronomy
---------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Angle&action=edit&section=28 "Edit section: Angles in geography and astronomy")]

In [geography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography "Geography"), the location of any point on the Earth can be identified using a _[geographic coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geographic\_coordinate\_system "Geographic coordinate system")_. This system specifies the [latitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latitude "Latitude") and [longitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Longitude "Longitude") of any location in terms of angles subtended at the center of the Earth, using the [equator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equator "Equator") and (usually) the [Greenwich meridian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenwich_meridian "Greenwich meridian") as references.

In [astronomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy "Astronomy"), a given point on the [celestial sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celestial_sphere "Celestial sphere") (that is, the apparent position of an astronomical object) can be identified using any of several _[astronomical coordinate systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical\_coordinate\_systems "Astronomical coordinate systems")_, where the references vary according to the particular system. Astronomers measure the _[angular separation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular\_separation "Angular separation")_ of two [stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star "Star") by imagining two lines through the center of the [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth "Earth"), each intersecting one of the stars. The angle between those lines and the angular separation between the two stars can be measured.

In both geography and astronomy, a sighting direction can be specified in terms of a [vertical angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertical_angle "Vertical angle") such as [altitude angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Altitude_angle "Altitude angle") or [elevation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elevation_angle "Elevation angle") with respect to the [horizon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horizon "Horizon") as well as the [azimuth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Azimuth "Azimuth") with respect to [north](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North "North").

Astronomers also measure objects' _apparent size_ as an [angular diameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_diameter "Angular diameter"). For example, the [full moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Full_moon "Full moon") has an angular diameter of approximately 0.5¬∞ when viewed from Earth. One could say, "The Moon's diameter subtends an angle of half a degree." The [small-angle formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Small-angle_formula "Small-angle formula") can convert such an angular measurement into a distance/size ratio.

Other astronomical approximations include:

*   0.5¬∞ is the approximate angular diameter of the [Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun "Sun") and of the [Moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moon "Moon") as viewed from Earth.
*   1¬∞ is the approximate angular width of the [little finger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Little_finger "Little finger") at arm's length.
*   10¬∞ is the approximate angular width of a closed fist at arm's length.
*   20¬∞ is the approximate angular width of a handspan at arm's length.

These measurements depend on the individual subject, and the above should be treated as rough [rule of thumb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rule_of_thumb "Rule of thumb") approximations only.

In astronomy, [right ascension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_ascension "Right ascension") is usually measured in angular units that are expressed in terms of time based on a 24-hour day.[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle#cite_note-61)
