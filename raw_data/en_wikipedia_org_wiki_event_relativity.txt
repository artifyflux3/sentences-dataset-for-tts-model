In [relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theory_of_relativity "Theory of relativity"), an **event** is anything that happens that has a specific time and place in [spacetime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spacetime "Spacetime"). For example, a glass breaking on the floor is an event; it occurs at a unique place and a unique time.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Event_(relativity)#cite_note-1) Strictly speaking, the notion of an event is an [idealization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Idealization_(philosophy_of_science) "Idealization (philosophy of science)"), in the sense that it specifies a definite time and place, whereas any actual event is bound to have a finite extent, both in time and in space.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Event_(relativity)#cite_note-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Event_(relativity)#cite_note-3)

The [spacetime interval](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spacetime#Spacetime_interval "Spacetime") between two events: (interval)2=[event time separation]2−[event space separation]2{\displaystyle ({\text{interval}})^{2}=\left[{\frac {\text{event time}}{\text{separation}}}\right]^{2}-\left[{\frac {\text{event space}}{\text{separation}}}\right]^{2}}![Image 14: {\displaystyle ({\text{interval}})^{2}=\left[{\frac {\text{event time}}{\text{separation}}}\right]^{2}-\left[{\frac {\text{event space}}{\text{separation}}}\right]^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f208f213bcd1c1d3961dec1426c31610bb3b9b0e)

is an [invariant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invariance_(physics) "Invariance (physics)").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Event_(relativity)#cite_note-Taylor_1992-4): 9

An event in the universe is caused by the set of events in its causal past. An event contributes to the occurrence of events in its causal future.

Upon choosing a frame of reference, one can assign coordinates to the event: three spatial coordinates x→=(x,y,z){\displaystyle {\vec {x}}=(x,y,z)}![Image 15: {\displaystyle {\vec {x}}=(x,y,z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2ecdc4cb0c225b2fdf59c9107da4a83004d7d007) to describe the location and one time coordinate t{\displaystyle t}![Image 16: {\displaystyle t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/65658b7b223af9e1acc877d848888ecdb4466560) to specify the moment at which the event occurs. These four coordinates (x→,t){\displaystyle ({\vec {x}},t)}![Image 17: {\displaystyle ({\vec {x}},t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/75b5a668b21420131ed56f44130efcf94b87a6db) together form a [four-vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Four-vector "Four-vector") associated to the event.

One of the goals of relativity is to specify the possibility of one event influencing another. This is done by means of the [metric tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_tensor "Metric tensor"), which allows for determining the [causal structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Causal_structure "Causal structure") of spacetime. The difference (or interval) between two events can be classified into [spacelike](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spacelike "Spacelike"), [lightlike](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lightlike "Lightlike") and [timelike](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timelike "Timelike") separations. Only if two events are separated by a lightlike or timelike interval can one influence the other.

[P. W. Bridgman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/P._W._Bridgman "P. W. Bridgman") found the event concept insufficient for operational physics in his book _[The Logic of Modern Physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The\_Logic\_of\_Modern\_Physics "The Logic of Modern Physics")_.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Event_(relativity)#cite_note-5)
