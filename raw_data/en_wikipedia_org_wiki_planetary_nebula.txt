A **planetary nebula** is a type of [emission nebula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emission_nebula "Emission nebula") consisting of an expanding, glowing shell of [ionized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ionization "Ionization") gas ejected from [red giant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_giant "Red giant") stars late in their lives.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-Frankowskietal2009-4)

The term "planetary nebula" is a [misnomer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Misnomer "Misnomer") because they are unrelated to [planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planet "Planet"). The term originates from the planet-like round shape of these [nebulae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nebula "Nebula") observed by astronomers through early telescopes. The first usage may have occurred during the 1780s with the English astronomer [William Herschel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Herschel "William Herschel") who described these nebulae as resembling planets; however, as early as January 1779, the French astronomer [Antoine Darquier de Pellepoix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antoine_Darquier_de_Pellepoix "Antoine Darquier de Pellepoix") described in his observations of the [Ring Nebula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ring_Nebula "Ring Nebula"), "very dim but perfectly outlined; it is as large as Jupiter and resembles a fading planet".[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-Darquier-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-Olsen2017-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-Steinicke2018-7) Though the modern interpretation is different, the old term is still used.

All planetary nebulae form at the end of the life of a star of intermediate mass, about 1-8 solar masses. It is expected that the [Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun "Sun") will form a planetary nebula at the end of its life cycle.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-8) They are relatively short-lived phenomena, lasting perhaps a few tens of millennia, compared to considerably longer phases of [stellar evolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_evolution "Stellar evolution").[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-FrewParker2010-9) Once all of the red giant's atmosphere has been dissipated, energetic [ultraviolet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultraviolet "Ultraviolet")[radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiation "Radiation") from the exposed hot luminous core, called a planetary nebula nucleus (P.N.N.), ionizes the ejected material.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-Frankowskietal2009-4) Absorbed ultraviolet light then energizes the shell of nebulous gas around the central star, causing it to appear as a brightly coloured planetary nebula.

Planetary nebulae probably play a crucial role in the [chemical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemistry "Chemistry")[evolution of the Milky Way](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_formation_and_evolution "Galaxy formation and evolution") by expelling [elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_element "Chemical element") into the [interstellar medium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_medium "Interstellar medium") from stars where those elements were created. Planetary nebulae are observed in more distant [galaxies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy "Galaxy"), yielding useful information about their chemical abundances.

Starting from the 1990s, [Hubble Space Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope "Hubble Space Telescope") images revealed that many planetary nebulae have extremely complex and varied morphologies. About one-fifth are roughly spherical, but the majority are not spherically symmetric. The mechanisms that produce such a wide variety of shapes and features are not yet well understood, but [binary central stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_star "Binary star"), stellar winds and [magnetic fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_field "Magnetic field") may play a role.

The first planetary nebula discovered (though not yet termed as such) was the [Dumbbell Nebula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dumbbell_Nebula "Dumbbell Nebula") in the constellation of [Vulpecula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vulpecula "Vulpecula"). It was observed by [Charles Messier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Messier "Charles Messier") on July 12, 1764 and listed as M27 in his [catalogue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Messier_object "Messier object") of nebulous objects.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-Kwok1-10) To early observers with low-resolution telescopes, M27 and subsequently discovered planetary nebulae resembled the giant planets like [Uranus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uranus "Uranus"). As early as January 1779, the French astronomer [Antoine Darquier de Pellepoix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antoine_Darquier_de_Pellepoix "Antoine Darquier de Pellepoix") described in his observations of the [Ring Nebula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ring_Nebula "Ring Nebula"), "a very dull nebula, but perfectly outlined; as large as Jupiter and looks like a fading planet".[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-Darquier-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-Olsen2017-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-Steinicke2018-7)

The nature of these objects remained unclear. In 1782, [William Herschel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Herschel "William Herschel"), discoverer of Uranus, found the [Saturn Nebula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saturn_Nebula "Saturn Nebula") (NGC 7009) and described it as "A curious nebula, or what else to call it I do not know". He later described these objects as seeming to be planets "of the starry kind".[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-11) As noted by Darquier before him, Herschel found that the disk resembled a planet but it was too faint to be one. In 1785, Herschel wrote to [Jérôme Lalande](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J%C3%A9r%C3%B4me_Lalande "Jérôme Lalande"):

> These are celestial bodies of which as yet we have no clear idea and which are perhaps of a type quite different from those that we are familiar with in the heavens. I have already found four that have a visible diameter of between 15 and 30 seconds. These bodies appear to have a disk that is rather like a planet, that is to say, of equal brightness all over, round or somewhat oval, and about as well defined in outline as the disk of the planets, of a light strong enough to be visible with an ordinary telescope of only one foot, yet they have only the appearance of a star of about ninth magnitude.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-12)

He assigned these to Class IV of his catalogue of "nebulae", eventually listing 78 "planetary nebulae", most of which are in fact galaxies.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-13)

Herschel used the term "planetary nebulae" for these objects. The origin of this term not known.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-Kwok1-10)[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-Moore2007-14) The label "planetary nebula" became ingrained in the terminology used by astronomers to categorize these types of nebulae, and is still in use by astronomers today.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-seds2013-15)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-hubbleSite1997-16)

The nature of planetary nebulae remained unknown until the first [spectroscopic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_spectroscopy "Astronomical spectroscopy") observations were made in the mid-19th century. Using a [prism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prism_(optics) "Prism (optics)") to disperse their light, [William Huggins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Huggins "William Huggins") was one of the earliest astronomers to study the [optical spectra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optical_spectrum "Optical spectrum") of astronomical objects.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-Moore2007-14)

On August 29, 1864, Huggins was the first to analyze the spectrum of a planetary nebula when he observed [Cat's Eye Nebula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cat%27s_Eye_Nebula "Cat's Eye Nebula").[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-Kwok1-10) His observations of stars had shown that their spectra consisted of a [continuum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuum_(theory) "Continuum (theory)") of radiation with many [dark lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absorption_line "Absorption line") superimposed. He found that many nebulous objects such as the [Andromeda Nebula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andromeda_Galaxy "Andromeda Galaxy") (as it was then known) had spectra that were quite similar. However, when Huggins looked at the Cat's Eye Nebula, he found a very different spectrum. Rather than a strong continuum with absorption lines superimposed, the Cat's Eye Nebula and other similar objects showed a number of [emission lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emission_spectrum "Emission spectrum").[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-Moore2007-14) Brightest of these was at a wavelength of 500.7[nanometres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nanometre "Nanometre"), which did not correspond with a line of any known element.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-Huggins1864-17)

At first, it was hypothesized that the line might be due to an unknown element, which was named [nebulium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nebulium "Nebulium"). A similar idea had led to the discovery of [helium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium "Helium") through analysis of the [Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun "Sun")'s spectrum in 1868.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-Kwok1-10) While helium was isolated on Earth soon after its discovery in the spectrum of the Sun, "nebulium" was not. In the early 20th century, [Henry Norris Russell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry_Norris_Russell "Henry Norris Russell") proposed that, rather than being a new element, the line at 500.7 nm was due to a familiar element in unfamiliar conditions.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-Kwok1-10)

Physicists showed in the 1920s that in gas at extremely low densities, [electrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron "Electron") can occupy [excited](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Excited_state "Excited state")[metastable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metastability "Metastability")[energy levels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_level "Energy level") in atoms and ions that would otherwise be de-excited by collisions that would occur at higher densities.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-Bowen1927-18) Electron transitions from these levels in [nitrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen "Nitrogen") and [oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen "Oxygen") ions (O+, [O 2+](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doubly_ionized_oxygen "Doubly ionized oxygen") (a.k.a. O iii), and N+) give rise to the 500.7 nm emission line and others.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-Kwok1-10) These spectral lines, which can only be seen in very low-density gases, are called _[forbidden lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forbidden\_line "Forbidden line")_. Spectroscopic observations thus showed that nebulae were made of extremely rarefied gas.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-Gurzadyan-19)

[![Image 9](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Potw1550a.tif/lossy-page1-250px-Potw1550a.tif.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Potw1550a.tif)

Planetary nebula NGC 3699 is distinguished by an irregular mottled appearance and a dark rift.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-20)

The central stars of planetary nebulae are very hot.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-Frankowskietal2009-4) Only when a star has exhausted most of its nuclear fuel can it collapse to a small size. Planetary nebulae are understood as a final stage of [stellar evolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_evolution "Stellar evolution"). Spectroscopic observations show that all planetary nebulae are expanding. This led to the idea that planetary nebulae were caused by a star's outer layers being thrown into space at the end of its life.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-Kwok1-10)

### Modern observations

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Planetary_nebula&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Modern observations")]

Towards the end of the 20th century, technological improvements helped to further the study of planetary nebulae.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-KwokJun2005-21)[Space telescopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_telescope "Space telescope") allowed astronomers to study light wavelengths outside those that the Earth's atmosphere transmits. The first UV observations of PNe ([IC 2149](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IC_2149 "IC 2149")) were performed from space, with the [Orion 2 Space Observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_2_Space_Observatory "Orion 2 Space Observatory") (see [Orion 1 and Orion 2 Space Observatories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_1_and_Orion_2_Space_Observatories "Orion 1 and Orion 2 Space Observatories")) on board the [Soyuz 13](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soyuz_13 "Soyuz 13") spacecraft in December 1973,[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-22) two photon emission from nebulae was detected for the first time.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-23)[Infrared](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infrared "Infrared") and ultraviolet studies of planetary nebulae allowed much more accurate determinations of nebular [temperatures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperature "Temperature"), [densities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Density "Density") and elemental abundances.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-Hora2004-24)[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-Kwoketal2006-25)[Charge-coupled device](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charge-coupled_device "Charge-coupled device") technology allowed much fainter spectral lines to be measured accurately than had previously been possible. The Hubble Space Telescope also showed that while many nebulae appear to have simple and regular structures when observed from the ground, the very high [optical resolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optical_resolution "Optical resolution") achievable by telescopes above the [Earth's atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere "Earth's atmosphere") reveals extremely complex structures.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-Reed1999-26)[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-Alleretal2003-27)

Under the [Morgan-Keenan spectral classification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morgan-Keenan_spectral_classification "Morgan-Keenan spectral classification") scheme, planetary nebulae are classified as _Type-**P**_, although this notation is seldom used in practice.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-Krause-28)

A butterfly-like area surrounds the S shape with the S shape corresponding to the body of the butterfly.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Stellar_nebula_simulation.jpg/250px-Stellar_nebula_simulation.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Stellar_nebula_simulation.jpg)

Stars greater than 8[solar masses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_mass "Solar mass") (M⊙) will probably end their lives in dramatic [supernovae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supernova "Supernova") explosions, while planetary nebulae seemingly only occur at the end of the lives of intermediate and low mass stars between 0.8 M⊙ to 8.0 M⊙.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-Macieletal2009-29) Progenitor stars that form planetary nebulae will spend most of their lifetimes converting their [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen") into [helium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium "Helium") in the star's core by [nuclear fusion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_fusion "Nuclear fusion") at about 15 million [K](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvin "Kelvin"). This generates energy in the core, which creates outward pressure that balances the crushing inward pressures of gravity.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-Harpaz4-30) This state of equilibrium is known as the [main sequence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Main_sequence "Main sequence"), which can last for tens of millions to billions of years, depending on the mass.

When the hydrogen in the core starts to run out, nuclear fusion generates less energy and gravity starts compressing the core, causing a rise in temperature to about 100 million K.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-Harpaz6-31) Such high core temperatures then make[_[how?](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Please\_clarify "Wikipedia:Please clarify")_] the star's cooler outer layers expand to create much larger red giant stars. This end phase causes a dramatic rise in stellar luminosity, where the released energy is distributed over a much larger surface area, which in fact causes the average surface temperature to be lower. In [stellar evolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_evolution "Stellar evolution") terms, stars undergoing such increases in luminosity are known as [asymptotic giant branch stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asymptotic_giant_branch "Asymptotic giant branch") (AGB).[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-Harpaz6-31) During this phase, the star can lose 50–70% of its total mass from its [stellar wind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_wind "Stellar wind").[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-wood-32)

For the more massive asymptotic giant branch stars that form planetary nebulae, whose progenitors exceed about 0.6M⊙, their cores will continue to contract. When temperatures reach about 100 million K, the available [helium nuclei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium "Helium") fuse into [carbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon "Carbon") and [oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen "Oxygen"), so that the star again resumes radiating energy, temporarily stopping the core's contraction. This new helium burning phase (fusion of helium nuclei) forms a growing inner core of inert carbon and oxygen. Above it is a thin helium-burning shell, surrounded in turn by a hydrogen-burning shell. However, this new phase lasts only 20,000 years or so, a very short period compared to the entire lifetime of the star.

The venting of atmosphere continues unabated into interstellar space, but when the outer surface of the exposed core reaches temperatures exceeding about 30,000 K, there are enough emitted [ultraviolet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultraviolet "Ultraviolet")[photons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon "Photon") to [ionize](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ionisation "Ionisation") the ejected atmosphere, causing the gas to shine as a planetary nebula.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-Harpaz6-31)

[![Image 11](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Necklace_Nebula.jpg/250px-Necklace_Nebula.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Necklace_Nebula.jpg)

 The [Necklace Nebula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Necklace_Nebula "Necklace Nebula") consists of a bright ring, measuring about two light-years across, dotted with dense, bright knots of gas that resemble diamonds in a necklace. The knots glow brightly due to absorption of ultraviolet light from the central stars.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-33)

After a star passes through the [asymptotic giant branch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asymptotic_giant_branch "Asymptotic giant branch") (AGB) phase, the short planetary nebula phase of stellar evolution begins[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-KwokJun2005-21) as gases blow away from the central star at speeds of a few kilometers per second. The central star is the remnant of its AGB progenitor, an electron-degenerate carbon-oxygen core that has lost most of its hydrogen envelope due to mass loss on the AGB.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-KwokJun2005-21) As the gases expand, the central star undergoes a two-stage evolution, first growing hotter as it continues to contract and hydrogen fusion reactions occur in the shell around the core and then slowly cooling when the hydrogen shell is exhausted through fusion and mass loss.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-KwokJun2005-21) In the second phase, it radiates away its energy and fusion reactions cease, as the central star is not heavy enough to generate the core temperatures required for carbon and oxygen to fuse.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-Kwok1-10)[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-KwokJun2005-21) During the first phase, the central star maintains constant luminosity,[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-KwokJun2005-21) while at the same time it grows ever hotter, eventually reaching temperatures around 100,000 K. In the second phase, it cools so much that it does not give off enough ultraviolet radiation to ionize the increasingly distant gas cloud. The star becomes a [white dwarf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_dwarf "White dwarf"), and the expanding gas cloud becomes invisible to us, ending the planetary nebula phase of evolution.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-KwokJun2005-21) For a typical planetary nebula, about 10,000 years[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-KwokJun2005-21) passes between its formation and recombination of the resulting [plasma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasma_(physics) "Plasma (physics)").[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-Kwok1-10)

Role in galactic enrichment
---------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Planetary_nebula&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Role in galactic enrichment")]

[![Image 12](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/An_Interstellar_Distributor.jpg/250px-An_Interstellar_Distributor.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:An_Interstellar_Distributor.jpg)

 ESO 455-10 is a [planetary nebula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebulae "Planetary nebulae") located in the constellation of [Scorpius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scorpius "Scorpius") (The Scorpion).[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-34)

Planetary nebulae may play a very important role in galactic evolution. Newly born stars consist almost entirely of [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen") and [helium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium "Helium"),[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-35) but as stars evolve through the [asymptotic giant branch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asymptotic_giant_branch "Asymptotic giant branch") phase,[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-36) they create heavier elements via [nuclear fusion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_fusion "Nuclear fusion") which are eventually expelled by strong [stellar winds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_wind "Stellar wind").[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-37) Planetary nebulae usually contain larger proportions of elements such as [carbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon "Carbon"), [nitrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen "Nitrogen") and [oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen "Oxygen"), and these are recycled into the interstellar medium via these powerful winds. In this way, planetary nebulae greatly enrich the [Milky Way](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milky_Way "Milky Way") and their [nebulae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nebula "Nebula") with these heavier elements – collectively known by astronomers as _metals_ and specifically referred to by the [metallicity parameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metallicity "Metallicity")_Z_.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-Kwok19-38)

Subsequent generations of stars formed from such nebulae also tend to have higher metallicities. Although these metals are present in stars in relatively tiny amounts, they have marked effects on [stellar evolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_evolution "Stellar evolution") and fusion reactions. When stars formed earlier in the [universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe "Universe") they theoretically contained smaller quantities of heavier elements.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-PopIII-39) Known examples are the metal poor [Population II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Population_II "Population II") stars. (See [Stellar population](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_population "Stellar population").)[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-Marochnik-40)[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-Gregory-41) Identification of stellar metallicity content is found by [spectroscopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_spectroscopy "Astronomical spectroscopy").

### Physical characteristics

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Planetary_nebula&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Physical characteristics")]

[![Image 13: Elliptical shell with fine red outer edge surrounding region of yellow and then pink around a nearly circular blue area with the central star at its center. A few background stars are visible.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/M57_The_Ring_Nebula.JPG/250px-M57_The_Ring_Nebula.JPG)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:M57_The_Ring_Nebula.JPG)

NGC 6720, the [Ring Nebula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ring_Nebula "Ring Nebula")

[![Image 14](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/IC3568.jpg/250px-IC3568.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:IC3568.jpg)

[Lemon slice nebula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lemon_slice_nebula "Lemon slice nebula") (IC 3568)

A typical planetary nebula is roughly one [light year](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_year "Light year") across, and consists of extremely rarefied gas, with a density generally from 100 to 10,000 particles per cm 3.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-Osterbrock1-42) (The Earth's atmosphere, by comparison, contains 2.5×10 19 particles per cm 3.) Young planetary nebulae have the highest densities, sometimes as high as 10 6 particles per cm 3. As nebulae age, their expansion causes their density to decrease. The masses of planetary nebulae range from 0.1 to 1[solar masses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_mass "Solar mass").[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-Osterbrock1-42)

Radiation from the central star heats the gases to temperatures of about 10,000[K](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvin "Kelvin").[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-Gurzadyan2-43) The gas temperature in central regions is usually much higher than at the periphery reaching 16,000–25,000 K.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-Gurzadyan3-44) The volume in the vicinity of the central star is often filled with a very hot (coronal) gas having the temperature of about 1,000,000 K. This gas originates from the surface of the central star in the form of the fast stellar wind.[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-Osterbrock261-45)

Nebulae may be described as _matter bounded_ or _radiation bounded_. In the former case, there is not enough matter in the nebula to absorb all the UV photons emitted by the star, and the visible nebula is fully ionized. In the latter case, there are not enough UV photons being emitted by the central star to ionize all the surrounding gas, and an ionization front propagates outward into the circumstellar envelope of neutral atoms.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-Osterbrock2-46)

### Numbers and distribution

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Planetary_nebula&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Numbers and distribution")]

About 3000 planetary nebulae are now known to exist in our galaxy,[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-Parkeretal2006-47) out of 200 billion stars. Their very short lifetime compared to total stellar lifetime accounts for their rarity. They are found mostly near the plane of the [Milky Way](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milky_Way "Milky Way"), with the greatest concentration near the [Galactic Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galactic_Center "Galactic Center").[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-majaess2007-48)

This animation shows how the two stars at the heart of a planetary nebula like [Fleming 1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fleming_1 "Fleming 1") can control the creation of the spectacular jets of material ejected from the object.

Only about 20% of planetary nebulae are spherically symmetric (for example, see [Abell 39](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abell_39 "Abell 39")).[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-Jacoby2001-49) A wide variety of shapes exist with some very complex forms seen. Planetary nebulae are classified by different authors into: stellar, disk, ring, irregular, helical, [bipolar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bipolar_nebula "Bipolar nebula"), quadrupolar,[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-KwoketalDec2005-50) and other types,[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-Kwok8-51) although the majority of them belong to just three types: spherical, elliptical and bipolar. Bipolar nebulae are concentrated in the [galactic plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galactic_plane "Galactic plane"), probably produced by relatively young massive progenitor stars; and bipolars in the [galactic bulge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galactic_bulge "Galactic bulge") appear to prefer orienting their orbital axes parallel to the galactic plane.[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-52) On the other hand, spherical nebulae are probably produced by old stars similar to the Sun.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-Osterbrock-1)

The huge variety of the shapes is partially the projection effect—the same nebula when viewed under different angles will appear different.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-53) Nevertheless, the reason for the huge variety of physical shapes is not fully understood.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-Kwok8-51) Gravitational interactions with companion stars if the central stars are [binary stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_star "Binary star") may be one cause. Another possibility is that planets disrupt the flow of material away from the star as the nebula forms. It has been determined that the more massive stars produce more irregularly shaped nebulae.[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-Morris-54) In January 2005, astronomers announced the first detection of magnetic fields around the central stars of two planetary nebulae,[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-Jordanetal2005-55) predicted already in 1960s,[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-56) and hypothesized that the fields might be partly or wholly responsible for their remarkable shapes.[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-57)

Membership in clusters
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Planetary_nebula&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Membership in clusters")]

[![Image 15](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Abell_78.jpg/250px-Abell_78.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Abell_78.jpg)

[Abell 78](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abell_78 "Abell 78"), 24 inch telescope on Mt. Lemmon, Arizona.

Planetary nebulae have been detected as members in four Galactic [globular clusters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Globular_clusters "Globular clusters"): [Messier 15](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Messier_15 "Messier 15"), [Messier 22](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Messier_22 "Messier 22"), [NGC 6441](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NGC_6441 "NGC 6441") and [Palomar 6](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palomar_6 "Palomar 6"). Evidence also points to the potential discovery of planetary nebulae in globular clusters in the galaxy [M31](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andromeda_Galaxy "Andromeda Galaxy").[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-ja2013-58) However, there is currently only one case of a planetary nebula discovered in an [open cluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_cluster "Open cluster") that is agreed upon by independent researchers.[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-fr2008-59)[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-parker2011-60)[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-ma2014-61) That case pertains to the planetary nebula PHR 1315-6555 and the open cluster Andrews-Lindsay 1. Indeed, through cluster membership, PHR 1315-6555 possesses among the most precise distances established for a planetary nebula (i.e., a 4% distance solution). The cases of [NGC 2818](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NGC_2818 "NGC 2818") and NGC 2348 in [Messier 46](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Messier_46 "Messier 46"), exhibit mismatched velocities between the planetary nebulae and the clusters, which indicates they are line-of-sight coincidences.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-majaess2007-48)[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-kiss2008-62)[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-mermilliod2001-63) A subsample of _tentative_ cases that may potentially be cluster/PN pairs includes Abell 8 and Bica 6,[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-bo2008-64)[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-tu2011-65) and He 2-86 and NGC 4463.[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-mo2014-66)

Theoretical models predict that planetary nebulae can form from [main-sequence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Main-sequence "Main-sequence") stars of between one and eight solar masses, which puts the progenitor star's age at greater than 40 million years. Although there are a few hundred known open clusters within that age range, a variety of reasons limit the chances of finding a planetary nebula within.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-majaess2007-48) For one reason, the planetary nebula phase for more massive stars is on the order of millennia, which is a blink of the eye in astronomic terms. Also, partly because of their small total mass, open clusters have relatively poor gravitational cohesion and tend to disperse after a relatively short time, typically from 100 to 600 million years.[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-Allison-67)

Current issues in planetary nebula studies
------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Planetary_nebula&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Current issues in planetary nebula studies")]

The distances to planetary nebulae are generally poorly determined,[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-68) but the _[Gaia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaia\_(spacecraft) "Gaia (spacecraft)")_ mission is now measuring direct [parallactic distances](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_parallax "Stellar parallax") between their central stars and neighboring stars.[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-69) It is also possible to determine distances to nearby planetary nebula by measuring their expansion rates. High resolution observations taken several years apart will show the expansion of the nebula perpendicular to the line of sight, while spectroscopic observations of the [Doppler shift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doppler_shift "Doppler shift") will reveal the velocity of expansion in the line of sight. Comparing the angular expansion with the derived velocity of expansion will reveal the distance to the nebula.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-Reed1999-26)

The issue of how such a diverse range of nebular shapes can be produced is a debatable topic. It is theorised that interactions between material moving away from the star at different speeds gives rise to most observed shapes.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-Kwok8-51) However, some astronomers postulate that close binary central stars might be responsible for the more complex and extreme planetary nebulae.[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-Soker2002-70) Several have been shown to exhibit strong magnetic fields,[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-Gurzadyan4-71) and their interactions with ionized gas could explain some planetary nebulae shapes.[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-Jordanetal2005-55)

There are two main methods of determining [metal abundances](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metallicity "Metallicity") in nebulae. These rely on recombination lines and collisionally excited lines. Large discrepancies are sometimes seen between the results derived from the two methods. This may be explained by the presence of small temperature fluctuations within planetary nebulae. The discrepancies may be too large to be caused by temperature effects, and some hypothesize the existence of cold knots containing very little hydrogen to explain the observations. However, such knots have yet to be observed.[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula#cite_note-Liuetal2000-72)
