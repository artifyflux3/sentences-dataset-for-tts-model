In [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry"), a **geodesic** ()[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic#cite_note-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic#cite_note-2) is a [curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curve "Curve") representing in some sense the locally[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic#cite_note-3) shortest[[b]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic#cite_note-pseudo-4) path ([arc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arc_(geometry) "Arc (geometry)")) between two points in a [surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry_of_surfaces "Differential geometry of surfaces"), or more generally in a [Riemannian manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_manifold "Riemannian manifold"). The term also has meaning in any [differentiable manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differentiable_manifold "Differentiable manifold") with a [connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connection_(mathematics) "Connection (mathematics)"). It is a generalization of the notion of a "[straight line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_(geometry) "Line (geometry)")".

The noun _[geodesic](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/geodesic "wikt:geodesic")_ and the adjective _[geodetic](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/geodetic "wikt:geodetic")_ come from _[geodesy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesy "Geodesy")_, the science of measuring the size and shape of [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth "Earth"), though many of the underlying principles can be applied to any [ellipsoidal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipsoidal_geodesic "Ellipsoidal geodesic") geometry. In the original sense, a geodesic was the shortest route between two points on the Earth's [surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_surface "Planetary surface"). For a [spherical Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_Earth "Spherical Earth"), it is a [segment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_segment "Line segment") of a [great circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_circle "Great circle") (see also [great-circle distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great-circle_distance "Great-circle distance")). The term has since been generalized to more abstract mathematical spaces; for example, in [graph theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_theory "Graph theory"), one might consider a [geodesic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distance_(graph_theory) "Distance (graph theory)") between two [vertices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_(graph_theory) "Vertex (graph theory)")/nodes of a [graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_(discrete_mathematics) "Graph (discrete mathematics)").

In a [Riemannian manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_manifold "Riemannian manifold") or submanifold, geodesics are characterised by the property of having vanishing [geodesic curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic_curvature "Geodesic curvature"). More generally, in the presence of an [affine connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_connection "Affine connection"), a geodesic is defined to be a curve whose [tangent vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_space "Tangent space") remain parallel if they are [transported](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_transport "Parallel transport") along it. Applying this to the [Levi-Civita connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_connection "Levi-Civita connection") of a [Riemannian metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_metric "Riemannian metric") recovers the previous notion.

Geodesics are of particular importance in [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity"). Timelike [geodesics in general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesics_in_general_relativity "Geodesics in general relativity") describe the motion of [free falling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_fall "Free fall")[test particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Test_particles "Test particles").

A locally shortest path between two given points in a curved space, assumed[[b]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic#cite_note-pseudo-4) to be a [Riemannian manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_manifold "Riemannian manifold"), can be defined by using the [equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equation "Equation") for the [length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arc_length "Arc length") of a [curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curve "Curve") (a function _f_ from an [open interval](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_interval "Open interval") of **[R](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number_line "Real number line")** to the space), and then minimizing this length between the points using the [calculus of variations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus_of_variations "Calculus of variations"). This has some minor technical problems because there is an infinite-dimensional space of different ways to parameterize the shortest path. It is simpler to restrict the set of curves to those that are parameterized "with constant speed" 1, meaning that the distance from _f_(_s_) to _f_(_t_) along the curve equals |_s_−_t_|. Equivalently, a different quantity may be used, termed the energy of the curve; minimizing the energy leads to the same equations for a geodesic (here "constant velocity" is a consequence of minimization).[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] Intuitively, one can understand this second formulation by noting that an [elastic band](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elastic_band "Elastic band") stretched between two points will contract its width, and in so doing will minimize its energy. The resulting shape of the band is a geodesic.

It is possible that several different curves between two points minimize the distance, as is the case for two diametrically opposite points on a sphere. In such a case, any of these curves is a geodesic.

A contiguous segment of a geodesic is again a geodesic.

In general, geodesics are not the same as "shortest curves" between two points, though the two concepts are closely related. The difference is that geodesics are only _locally_ the shortest distance between points, and are parameterized with "constant speed". Going the "long way round" on a [great circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_circle "Great circle") between two points on a sphere is a geodesic but not the shortest path between the points. The map ![Image 2: {\displaystyle t\to t^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cae66039269f72a16ad4cf708b56b7f45d67d9cf) from the unit interval on the real number line to itself gives the shortest path between 0 and 1, but is not a geodesic because the velocity of the corresponding motion of a point is not constant.

Geodesics are commonly seen in the study of [Riemannian geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_geometry "Riemannian geometry") and more generally [metric geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_geometry "Metric geometry"). In [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity"), geodesics in [spacetime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spacetime "Spacetime") describe the motion of [point particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_particle "Point particle") under the influence of gravity alone. In particular, the path taken by a falling rock, an orbiting [satellite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satellite "Satellite"), or the shape of a [planetary orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_orbit "Planetary orbit") are all geodesics[[c]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic#cite_note-5) in curved spacetime. More generally, the topic of [sub-Riemannian geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sub-Riemannian_geometry "Sub-Riemannian geometry") deals with the paths that objects may take when they are not free, and their movement is constrained in various ways.

This article presents the mathematical formalism involved in defining, finding, and proving the existence of geodesics, in the case of [Riemannian manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_manifold "Riemannian manifold"). The article [Levi-Civita connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_connection "Levi-Civita connection") discusses the more general case of a [pseudo-Riemannian manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudo-Riemannian_manifold "Pseudo-Riemannian manifold") and [geodesic (general relativity)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic_(general_relativity) "Geodesic (general relativity)") discusses the special case of general relativity in greater detail.

If an insect is placed on a surface and continually walks "forward", by definition it will trace out a geodesic.

The most familiar examples are the straight lines in [Euclidean geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_geometry "Euclidean geometry"). On a [sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere "Sphere"), the images of geodesics are the [great circles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_circle "Great circle"). The shortest path from point _A_ to point _B_ on a sphere is given by the shorter [arc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arc_(geometry) "Arc (geometry)") of the great circle passing through _A_ and _B_. If _A_ and _B_ are [antipodal points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antipodal_point "Antipodal point"), then there are _infinitely many_ shortest paths between them. [Geodesics on an ellipsoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesics_on_an_ellipsoid "Geodesics on an ellipsoid") behave in a more complicated way than on a sphere; in particular, they are not closed in general (see figure).

[![Image 5](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Spherical_triangle.svg/250px-Spherical_triangle.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Spherical_triangle.svg)

A geodesic triangle on the sphere.

A **geodesic triangle** is formed by the geodesics joining each pair out of three points on a given surface. On the sphere, the geodesics are [great circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_circle "Great circle") arcs, forming a [spherical triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_triangle "Spherical triangle").

Geodesic triangles in spaces of positive (top), negative (middle) and zero (bottom) curvature.

In [metric geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_geometry "Metric geometry"), a geodesic is a curve which is everywhere [locally](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Locally "Locally") a [distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distance "Distance") minimizer. More precisely, a [curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curve "Curve")_γ_: _I_ → _M_ from an interval _I_ of the reals to the [metric space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space "Metric space")_M_ is a **geodesic** if there is a [constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_constant "Mathematical constant")_v_ ≥ 0 such that for any _t_ ∈ _I_ there is a neighborhood _J_ of _t_ in _I_ such that for any _t_ 1, _t_ 2 ∈ _J_ we have

![Image 7: {\displaystyle d(\gamma (t_{1}),\gamma (t_{2}))=v\left|t_{1}-t_{2}\right|.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b56a4e4454cc0827d267bae9a964c33585e768fb)
This generalizes the notion of geodesic for Riemannian manifolds. However, in metric geometry the geodesic considered is often equipped with [natural parameterization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curve#Lengths_of_curves "Curve"), i.e. in the above identity _v_=1 and

![Image 8: {\displaystyle d(\gamma (t_{1}),\gamma (t_{2}))=\left|t_{1}-t_{2}\right|.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/11287438f7e92605867ca1c2db5038c59ee15413)
If the last equality is satisfied for all _t_ 1, _t_ 2 ∈ _I_, the geodesic is called a **minimizing geodesic** or **shortest path**.

In general, a metric space may have no geodesics, except constant curves. At the other extreme, any two points in a [length metric space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Length_metric_space "Length metric space") are joined by a minimizing sequence of [rectifiable paths](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectifiable_path "Rectifiable path"), although this minimizing sequence need not converge to a geodesic. The [metric Hopf-Rinow theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hopf%E2%80%93Rinow_theorem#Variations_and_generalizations "Hopf–Rinow theorem") provides situations where a length space is automatically a geodesic space.

Common examples of geodesic metric spaces that are often not manifolds include [metric graphs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_graph "Metric graph"), (locally compact) metric [polyhedral complexes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyhedral_complex "Polyhedral complex"), infinite-dimensional [pre-Hilbert spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pre-Hilbert_space "Pre-Hilbert space"), and [real trees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_tree "Real tree").

In Riemannian geometry, all geodesics are locally distance-minimizing paths, but the converse is not true. In fact, only paths that are both locally distance minimizing and parameterized proportionately to arc-length are geodesics.

Another equivalent way of defining geodesics on a Riemannian manifold, is to define them as the minima of the following [action](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_(physics) "Action (physics)") or [energy functional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_functional "Energy functional")

### Calculus of variations

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Geodesic&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Calculus of variations")]

Techniques of the classical [calculus of variations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus_of_variations "Calculus of variations") can be applied to examine the energy functional ![Image 40: {\displaystyle E}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4232c9de2ee3eec0a9c0a19b15ab92daa6223f9b). The [first variation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_variation "First variation") of energy is defined in local coordinates by

![Image 41: {\displaystyle \delta E(\gamma )(\varphi )=\left.{\frac {\partial }{\partial t}}\right|_{t=0}E(\gamma +t\varphi ).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/37ab1157c6fecbaf96ca0d505e3fbbf1319233d1)
The [critical points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Critical_point_(mathematics) "Critical point (mathematics)") of the first variation are precisely the geodesics. The [second variation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_variation "Second variation") is defined by

![Image 42: {\displaystyle \delta ^{2}E(\gamma )(\varphi ,\psi )=\left.{\frac {\partial ^{2}}{\partial s\,\partial t}}\right|_{s=t=0}E(\gamma +t\varphi +s\psi ).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3791963dc218251f64e22f4efec4f3bbb9709338)
In an appropriate sense, zeros of the second variation along a geodesic ![Image 43: {\displaystyle \gamma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a223c880b0ce3da8f64ee33c4f0010beee400b1a) arise along [Jacobi fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacobi_field "Jacobi field"). Jacobi fields are thus regarded as variations through geodesics.

By applying variational techniques from [classical mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_mechanics "Classical mechanics"), one can also regard [geodesics as Hamiltonian flows](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesics_as_Hamiltonian_flows "Geodesics as Hamiltonian flows"). They are solutions of the associated [Hamilton equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamilton_equation "Hamilton equation"), with [(pseudo-)Riemannian metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudo_Riemannian_metric "Pseudo Riemannian metric") taken as [Hamiltonian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamiltonian_mechanics "Hamiltonian mechanics").

![Image 47: {\displaystyle \nabla _{\dot {\gamma }}{\dot {\gamma }}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/998d786a63d948705e7212f53917b10213171b02)1

at each point along the curve, where ![Image 48: {\displaystyle {\dot {\gamma }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c250a2cd0b68db26e9663686345f6cee8074d5f0) is the derivative with respect to ![Image 49: {\displaystyle t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/65658b7b223af9e1acc877d848888ecdb4466560). More precisely, in order to define the covariant derivative of ![Image 50: {\displaystyle {\dot {\gamma }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c250a2cd0b68db26e9663686345f6cee8074d5f0) it is necessary first to extend ![Image 51: {\displaystyle {\dot {\gamma }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c250a2cd0b68db26e9663686345f6cee8074d5f0) to a continuously differentiable [vector field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_field "Vector field") in an [open set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_set "Open set"). However, the resulting value of (**[1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic#math_1)**) is independent of the choice of extension.

Using [local coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_coordinates "Local coordinates") on ![Image 52: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd), we can write the **geodesic equation** (using the [summation convention](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Summation_convention "Summation convention")) as

![Image 53: {\displaystyle {\frac {d^{2}\gamma ^{\lambda }}{dt^{2}}}+\Gamma _{\mu \nu }^{\lambda }{\frac {d\gamma ^{\mu }}{dt}}{\frac {d\gamma ^{\nu }}{dt}}=0\ ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/18bad7ac47f0475ca9ec38a077a1e69adc8e10d7)
where ![Image 54: {\displaystyle \gamma ^{\mu }=x^{\mu }\circ \gamma (t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/89889b02694dae9b5f264b3d005100d8063d2890) are the coordinates of the curve ![Image 55: {\displaystyle \gamma (t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54fa4a5d64e164410e4a18106677bebefe1a1f1b) and ![Image 56: {\displaystyle \Gamma _{\mu \nu }^{\lambda }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b2ed711a5120b51b00a8bb4bdd696b6f38655ee2) are the [Christoffel symbols](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christoffel_symbol "Christoffel symbol") of the connection ![Image 57: {\displaystyle \nabla }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a3d0e93b78c50237f9ea83d027e4ebbdaef354b2). This is an [ordinary differential equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordinary_differential_equation "Ordinary differential equation") for the coordinates. It has a unique solution, given an initial position and an initial velocity. Therefore, from the point of view of [classical mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_mechanics "Classical mechanics"), geodesics can be thought of as trajectories of [free particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_particle "Free particle") in a manifold. Indeed, the equation ![Image 58: {\displaystyle \nabla _{\dot {\gamma }}{\dot {\gamma }}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/998d786a63d948705e7212f53917b10213171b02) means that the [acceleration vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acceleration_(differential_geometry) "Acceleration (differential geometry)") of the curve has no components in the direction of the surface (and therefore it is perpendicular to the tangent plane of the surface at each point of the curve). So, the motion is completely determined by the bending of the surface. This is also the idea of general relativity where particles move on geodesics and the bending is caused by gravity.

### Existence and uniqueness

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Geodesic&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Existence and uniqueness")]

The _local existence and uniqueness theorem_ for geodesics states that geodesics on a smooth manifold with an [affine connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_connection "Affine connection") exist, and are unique. More precisely:

For any point _p_ in _M_ and for any vector _V_ in _T p M_ (the [tangent space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_space "Tangent space") to _M_ at _p_) there exists a unique geodesic ![Image 59: {\displaystyle \gamma \,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/65da7961fee8269d576e5d06e838bf8695fc5179): _I_ → _M_ such that ![Image 60: {\displaystyle \gamma (0)=p\,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/04dd0f3aedbf7f138571de442c02f5d10fc4a2ce) and![Image 61: {\displaystyle {\dot {\gamma }}(0)=V,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ce8f1fe7c7944140a317a9f37a43ebaabb9d0994)where _I_ is a maximal [open interval](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_interval "Open interval") in **R** containing 0.
The proof of this theorem follows from the theory of [ordinary differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordinary_differential_equation "Ordinary differential equation"), by noticing that the geodesic equation is a second-order ODE. Existence and uniqueness then follow from the [Picard–Lindelöf theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Picard%E2%80%93Lindel%C3%B6f_theorem "Picard–Lindelöf theorem") for the solutions of ODEs with prescribed initial conditions. γ depends [smoothly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smooth_function "Smooth function") on both _p_ and _V_.

In general, _I_ may not be all of **R** as for example for an open disc in **R**2. Any γ extends to all of ℝ if and only if M is [geodesically complete](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic_manifold "Geodesic manifold").

**Geodesic [flow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flow_(mathematics) "Flow (mathematics)")** is a local **R**-[action](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_action_(mathematics) "Group action (mathematics)") on the [tangent bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_bundle "Tangent bundle")_TM_ of a manifold _M_ defined in the following way

![Image 62: {\displaystyle G^{t}(V)={\dot {\gamma }}_{V}(t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0ba0cb3eb1fec6233de4124531c25c1b57652f09)
where _t_∈**R**, _V_∈_TM_ and ![Image 63: {\displaystyle \gamma _{V}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3e564896baedd0fbdb5645daa0e467d4c7efb412) denotes the geodesic with initial data ![Image 64: {\displaystyle {\dot {\gamma }}_{V}(0)=V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/903b229c4679111a13875213b13b0c2b9c3262e1). Thus, _![Image 65: {\displaystyle G^{t}(V)=\exp(tV)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest\_v1/media/math/render/svg/b655902cadaf052c0ac81b65ca6b83c8d98a4ca2) is the [exponential map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponential\_map\_(Riemannian\_geometry) "Exponential map (Riemannian geometry)") of the vector_ tV _. A closed orbit of the geodesic flow corresponds to a [closed geodesic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed\_geodesic "Closed geodesic") on_ M _._

On a (pseudo-)Riemannian manifold, the geodesic flow is identified with a [Hamiltonian flow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamiltonian_flow "Hamiltonian flow") on the cotangent bundle. The [Hamiltonian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamiltonian_mechanics "Hamiltonian mechanics") is then given by the inverse of the (pseudo-)Riemannian metric, evaluated against the [canonical one-form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canonical_one-form "Canonical one-form"). In particular the flow preserves the (pseudo-)Riemannian metric ![Image 66: {\displaystyle g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3556280e66fe2c0d0140df20935a6f057381d77), i.e.

![Image 67: {\displaystyle g(G^{t}(V),G^{t}(V))=g(V,V).\,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2a9387876b13605e6f4f5e98ce29f38347920b82)
In particular, when _V_ is a unit vector, ![Image 68: {\displaystyle \gamma _{V}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3e564896baedd0fbdb5645daa0e467d4c7efb412) remains unit speed throughout, so the geodesic flow is tangent to the [unit tangent bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_tangent_bundle "Unit tangent bundle"). [Liouville's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liouville%27s_theorem_(Hamiltonian) "Liouville's theorem (Hamiltonian)") implies invariance of a kinematic measure on the unit tangent bundle.

The geodesic flow defines a family of curves in the [tangent bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_bundle "Tangent bundle"). The derivatives of these curves define a [vector field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_field "Vector field") on the [total space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Total_space "Total space") of the tangent bundle, known as the **geodesic [spray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spray_(mathematics) "Spray (mathematics)")**.

More precisely, an affine connection gives rise to a splitting of the [double tangent bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double_tangent_bundle "Double tangent bundle") TT _M_ into [horizontal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horizontal_bundle "Horizontal bundle") and [vertical bundles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertical_bundle "Vertical bundle"):

![Image 69: {\displaystyle TTM=H\oplus V.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/334c0c4fde35c86a0328b5d4e2e3022b0003d502)
The geodesic spray is the unique horizontal vector field _W_ satisfying

![Image 70: {\displaystyle \pi _{*}W_{v}=v\,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4f0c75ac8678799b53cb17ce904bb310f9144ad6)
at each point _v_∈T _M_; here π∗:TT _M_→T _M_ denotes the [pushforward (differential)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pushforward_(differential) "Pushforward (differential)") along the projection π:T _M_→_M_ associated to the tangent bundle.

More generally, the same construction allows one to construct a vector field for any [Ehresmann connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ehresmann_connection "Ehresmann connection") on the tangent bundle. For the resulting vector field to be a spray (on the deleted tangent bundle T _M_\{0}) it is enough that the connection be equivariant under positive rescalings: it need not be linear. That is, (cf. [Ehresmann connection#Vector bundles and covariant derivatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ehresmann_connection#Vector_bundles_and_covariant_derivatives "Ehresmann connection")) it is enough that the horizontal distribution satisfy

![Image 71: {\displaystyle H_{\lambda X}=d(S_{\lambda })_{X}H_{X}\,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/808d3f4094599f4cb669dac4d360039fa3fc379b)
for every _X_∈T _M_\{0} and λ>0. Here _d_(_S_ λ) is the [pushforward](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pushforward_(differential) "Pushforward (differential)") along the scalar homothety ![Image 72: {\displaystyle S_{\lambda }:X\mapsto \lambda X.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5429ef549abb15665109e613f45595cfd87978b7) A particular case of a non-linear connection arising in this manner is that associated to a [Finsler manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finsler_manifold "Finsler manifold").

### Affine and projective geodesics

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Geodesic&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Affine and projective geodesics")]

Equation (**[1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic#math_1)**) is invariant under affine reparameterizations; that is, parameterizations of the form

![Image 73: {\displaystyle t\mapsto at+b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dd2fda512d4d28d7d6fd88e0f4375677ff59c0c8)
where _a_ and _b_ are constant real numbers. Thus apart from specifying a certain class of embedded curves, the geodesic equation also determines a preferred class of parameterizations on each of the curves. Accordingly, solutions of (**[1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic#math_1)**) are called geodesics with **affine parameter**.

An affine connection is _determined by_ its family of affinely parameterized geodesics, up to [torsion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torsion_tensor "Torsion tensor") ([Spivak 1999](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic#CITEREFSpivak1999), Chapter 6, Addendum I). The torsion itself does not, in fact, affect the family of geodesics, since the geodesic equation depends only on the symmetric part of the connection. More precisely, if ![Image 74: {\displaystyle \nabla ,{\bar {\nabla }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/401c936af8fa2f02701289eb7fb27fd86e74cbda) are two connections such that the difference tensor

![Image 75: {\displaystyle D(X,Y)=\nabla _{X}Y-{\bar {\nabla }}_{X}Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/73a0284ec3aa869ef64bb4b57a3fc06073179636)
is [skew-symmetric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skew-symmetric_matrix "Skew-symmetric matrix"), then ![Image 76: {\displaystyle \nabla }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a3d0e93b78c50237f9ea83d027e4ebbdaef354b2) and ![Image 77: {\displaystyle {\bar {\nabla }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/72c8395cfd87dab30d574e954445752b3bafc2c6) have the same geodesics, with the same affine parameterizations. Furthermore, there is a unique connection having the same geodesics as ![Image 78: {\displaystyle \nabla }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a3d0e93b78c50237f9ea83d027e4ebbdaef354b2), but with vanishing torsion.

Geodesics without a particular parameterization are described by a [projective connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_connection "Projective connection").

Computational methods
---------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Geodesic&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Computational methods")]

Efficient solvers for the minimal geodesic problem on surfaces have been proposed by Mitchell,[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic#cite_note-6) Kimmel,[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic#cite_note-7) Crane,[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic#cite_note-8) and others.

A ribbon "test" is a way of finding a geodesic on a physical surface.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic#cite_note-9) The idea is to fit a bit of paper around a straight line (a ribbon) onto a curved surface as closely as possible without stretching or squishing the ribbon (without changing its internal geometry).

For example, when a ribbon is wound as a ring around a cone, the ribbon would not lie on the cone's surface but stick out, so that circle is not a geodesic on the cone. If the ribbon is adjusted so that all its parts touch the cone's surface, it would give an approximation to a geodesic.

Mathematically the ribbon test can be formulated as finding a mapping ![Image 79: {\displaystyle f:N(\ell )\to S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/69b0fb1f3ce903c3368a80fe809c8b1a6e9d6de0) of a [neighborhood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neighborhood_(mathematics) "Neighborhood (mathematics)")![Image 80: {\displaystyle N}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5e3890c981ae85503089652feb48b191b57aae3) of a line ![Image 81: {\displaystyle \ell }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f066e981e530bacc07efc6a10fa82deee985929e) in a plane into a surface ![Image 82: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2) so that the mapping ![Image 83: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) "doesn't change the distances around ![Image 84: {\displaystyle \ell }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f066e981e530bacc07efc6a10fa82deee985929e) by much"; that is, at the distance ![Image 85: {\displaystyle \varepsilon }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a30c89172e5b88edbd45d3e2772c7f5e562e5173) from ![Image 86: {\displaystyle l}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/829091f745070b9eb97a80244129025440a1cfac) we have ![Image 87: {\displaystyle g_{N}-f^{*}(g_{S})=O(\varepsilon ^{2})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0813580dcdb64027dc21fe46c233d0b86989f5fc) where ![Image 88: {\displaystyle g_{N}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54e484cdd4542405ed69cbc921f9f39cddbe9e46) and ![Image 89: {\displaystyle g_{S}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fdab3e338698b6e41fb3889764ea6f3e2302acf5) are [metrics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_tensor "Metric tensor") on ![Image 90: {\displaystyle N}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5e3890c981ae85503089652feb48b191b57aae3) and ![Image 91: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2).

Examples of applications
------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Geodesic&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Examples of applications")]

[![Image 92: [icon]](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/20px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg)This section **needs expansion**. While geometric in nature, the idea of a shortest path is so general that it easily finds extensive use in nearly all sciences, and in some other disciplines as well.

### Topology and geometric group theory

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Geodesic&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: Topology and geometric group theory")]

*   In a surface with negative [Euler characteristic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler_characteristic "Euler characteristic"), any (free) homotopy class determines a unique (closed) geodesic for a [hyperbolic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_surface "Hyperbolic surface") metric. These geodesics contribute significantly to the geometric understanding of the action of [mapping classes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mapping_class_group_of_a_surface "Mapping class group of a surface").
*   [Geodesic metric spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic_metric_space "Geodesic metric space") and [length spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Length_space "Length space") behave particularly well with isometric [group actions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_action "Group action") ([Švarc-Milnor lemma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C5%A0varc%E2%80%93Milnor_lemma "Švarc–Milnor lemma"), [Hopf-Rinow theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hopf%E2%80%93Rinow_theorem#Variations_and_generalizations "Hopf–Rinow theorem"), [Morse lemma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasi-isometry#Quasigeodesics_and_the_Morse_lemma "Quasi-isometry")...). They are often an adequate framework for generalizing results from Riemannian geometry to constructions that reflect the geometry of a group. For instance, [Gromov-hyperbolicity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_metric_space "Hyperbolic metric space") can be understood in terms of geodesic triangle thinness, and [CAT(0)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CAT(k)_space "CAT(k) space") can be stated in terms of angles between geodesics.

### Probability, statistics and machine learning

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Geodesic&action=edit&section=16 "Edit section: Probability, statistics and machine learning")]

*   [Optimal transport](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optimal_transport "Optimal transport") can be understood as the problem of finding geodesic paths in spaces of measures.
*   In [information geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information_geometry "Information geometry"), [divergences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divergence_(statistics) "Divergence (statistics)") such as the [Kullback-Leibler divergence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kullback%E2%80%93Leibler_divergence "Kullback–Leibler divergence") play a role analogous to that of a Riemannian metric, allowing analogies for [connections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_connection "Metric connection") and geodesics.

*   In [classical mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_mechanics "Classical mechanics"), [trajectories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trajectory "Trajectory") minimize an energy according to the [Hamilton-Jacobi equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamilton%E2%80%93Jacobi_equation "Hamilton–Jacobi equation"), which can be regarded as a similar idea to geodesics. In some special cases, [the two notions actually coincide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesics_as_Hamiltonian_flows "Geodesics as Hamiltonian flows").
*   [Relativity theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theory_of_relativity "Theory of relativity") models [spacetime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spacetime "Spacetime") as a [Lorentzian manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentzian_manifold "Lorentzian manifold"), where light follows Lorentzian geodesics.

*   The study of how the [nervous system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nervous_system "Nervous system") optimizes muscular movement may be approached by endowing a [configuration space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Configuration_space_(physics) "Configuration space (physics)") of the body with a [Riemannian metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_metric "Riemannian metric") that measures the effort, so that the problem can be stated in terms of geodesy.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic#cite_note-10)
*   [Geodesic distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic_distance "Geodesic distance") is often used to measure the length of paths for signal propagation in neurons.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic#cite_note-11)
*   The structures of geodesics in large molecules plays a role in the study of [protein folds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protein_folds "Protein folds").[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic#cite_note-12)
*   The structure of [compound eyes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compound_eye "Compound eye"), many parts of which are being held together and supported by a geodesic dome grid on the outside surface of the eye.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic#cite_note-13)

Geodesics serve as the basis to calculate:

*   geodesic airframes; see [geodesic airframe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic_airframe "Geodesic airframe") or [geodetic airframe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodetic_airframe "Geodetic airframe")
*   geodesic structures – for example [geodesic domes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic_domes "Geodesic domes")
*   horizontal distances on or near Earth; see [Earth geodesics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth_geodesics "Earth geodesics")
*   mapping images on surfaces, for rendering; see [UV mapping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UV_mapping "UV mapping")
*   robot [motion planning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Motion_planning "Motion planning") (e.g., when painting car parts); see [Shortest path problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shortest_path_problem "Shortest path problem")
*   geodesic shortest path (GSP) correction over [Poisson surface reconstruction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poisson_surface_reconstruction "Poisson surface reconstruction") (e.g. in [digital dentistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digital_dentistry "Digital dentistry")); without GSP reconstruction often results in self-intersections within the surface

*   [Introduction to the mathematics of general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Introduction_to_the_mathematics_of_general_relativity "Introduction to the mathematics of general relativity")
*   [Clairaut's relation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clairaut%27s_relation "Clairaut's relation")– Formula in classical differential geometry
*   [Differentiable curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differentiable_curve "Differentiable curve")– Study of curves from a differential point of view
*   [Differential geometry of surfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry_of_surfaces "Differential geometry of surfaces")
*   [Geodesic circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic_circle "Geodesic circle")
*   [Hopf–Rinow theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hopf%E2%80%93Rinow_theorem "Hopf–Rinow theorem")– Gives equivalent statements about the geodesic completeness of Riemannian manifolds
*   [Intrinsic metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intrinsic_metric "Intrinsic metric")– Concept in geometry/topology
*   [Isotropic line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotropic_line "Isotropic line")– Line along which a quadratic form applied to any two points' displacement is zero
*   [Jacobi field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacobi_field "Jacobi field")– Vector field in Riemannian geometry
*   [Morse theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morse_theory "Morse theory")– Analyzes the topology of a manifold by studying differentiable functions on that manifold
*   [Zoll surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zoll_surface "Zoll surface")– Surface homeomorphic to a sphere

2.   ^ [_**a**_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic#cite_ref-pseudo_4-0)[_**b**_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic#cite_ref-pseudo_4-1)For a [pseudo-Riemannian manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudo-Riemannian_manifold "Pseudo-Riemannian manifold"), e.g., a [Lorentzian manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentzian_manifold "Lorentzian manifold"), the definition is more complicated.
3.   **[^](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic#cite_ref-5)**The path is a local maximum of the interval _k_ rather than a local minimum.
