The giant elliptical galaxy [ESO 325-4](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ESO_325-4 "ESO 325-4")

An **elliptical galaxy** is a type of [galaxy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy "Galaxy") with an approximately [ellipsoidal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipsoid "Ellipsoid") shape and a smooth, nearly featureless image. They are one of the three main [classes of galaxy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_morphological_classification "Galaxy morphological classification") described by [Edwin Hubble](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edwin_Hubble "Edwin Hubble") in his [Hubble sequence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble_sequence#Physical_significance "Hubble sequence") and 1936 work _The Realm of the Nebulae_,[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptical_galaxy#cite_note-Hubble-1936-1) along with [spiral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spiral_galaxy "Spiral galaxy") and [lenticular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lenticular_galaxy "Lenticular galaxy") galaxies. Elliptical (E) galaxies are, together with lenticular galaxies (S0) with their large-scale disks, and ES galaxies[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptical_galaxy#cite_note-adsabs.harvard.edu-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptical_galaxy#cite_note-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptical_galaxy#cite_note-4) with their intermediate scale disks, a subset of the "early-type" galaxy population.

Most elliptical galaxies are composed of older, [low-mass stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_evolution#Low-mass_stars "Stellar evolution"), with a sparse [interstellar medium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_medium "Interstellar medium"), and they tend to be surrounded by large numbers of [globular clusters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Globular_cluster "Globular cluster"). [Star formation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_formation "Star formation") activity in elliptical galaxies is typically minimal; they may, however, undergo brief periods of star formation when merging with other galaxies.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptical_galaxy#cite_note-5) Elliptical galaxies are believed to make up approximately 10–15% of galaxies in the [Virgo Supercluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgo_Supercluster "Virgo Supercluster"), and they are not the dominant type of galaxy in the universe overall.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptical_galaxy#cite_note-6) They are preferentially found close to the centers of [galaxy clusters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_cluster "Galaxy cluster").[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptical_galaxy#cite_note-7)

Elliptical galaxies range in size from dwarf ellipticals with tens of millions of stars, to [supergiants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Type-cD_galaxy "Type-cD galaxy") of over one hundred trillion [stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star "Star") that dominate their galaxy clusters. Originally, Edwin Hubble hypothesized that elliptical galaxies evolved into spiral galaxies, which was later discovered to be false,[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptical_galaxy#cite_note-author-8) although the accretion of gas and smaller galaxies may build a disk around a pre-existing ellipsoidal structure.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptical_galaxy#cite_note-9)[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptical_galaxy#cite_note-10) Stars found inside of elliptical galaxies are on average much older than stars found in spiral galaxies.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptical_galaxy#cite_note-author-8)

General characteristics
-----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Elliptical_galaxy&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: General characteristics")]

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Elliptical_galaxy_IC_2006.jpg/250px-Elliptical_galaxy_IC_2006.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Elliptical_galaxy_IC_2006.jpg)

Elliptical galaxy [IC 2006](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IC_2006 "IC 2006")[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptical_galaxy#cite_note-13)

Elliptical galaxies are characterized by several properties that make them distinct from other classes of galaxy. They are spherical or ovoid masses of stars, starved of star-making gases. Furthermore, there is very little [interstellar matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_matter "Interstellar matter") (neither gas nor dust), which results in low rates of [star formation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_formation "Star formation"), few [open star clusters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_cluster "Open cluster"), and few young stars; rather elliptical galaxies are dominated by [old stellar populations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Population_II "Population II"), giving them red colors. Large elliptical galaxies typically have an extensive system of [globular clusters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Globular_cluster "Globular cluster"). They generally have two distinct populations of globular clusters: one that is redder and metal-rich, and another that is bluer and metal-poor.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptical_galaxy#cite_note-14)

[![Image 3](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Galactic_fireflies.jpg/250px-Galactic_fireflies.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Galactic_fireflies.jpg)

The galaxy located in the image is [4C 73.08](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=4C_73.08&action=edit&redlink=1 "4C 73.08 (page does not exist)"), a giant elliptical galaxy.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptical_galaxy#cite_note-15)

The dynamical properties of elliptical galaxies and the [bulges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bulge_(astronomy) "Bulge (astronomy)") of [disk galaxies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disc_galaxy "Disc galaxy") are similar, suggesting that they may be formed by the same physical processes, although this remains controversial. The [luminosity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luminosity#In_astronomy "Luminosity") profiles of both elliptical galaxies and bulges are well fit by [Sersic's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sersic_profile "Sersic profile"), and a range of scaling relations between the elliptical galaxies' structural parameters unify the population.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptical_galaxy#cite_note-16)

Every massive elliptical galaxy contains a [supermassive black hole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supermassive_black_hole "Supermassive black hole") at its center. Observations of 46 elliptical galaxies, 20 classical bulges, and 22 pseudobulges show that each contain a black hole at the center.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptical_galaxy#cite_note-17) The mass of the black hole is tightly correlated with the mass of the galaxy,[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptical_galaxy#cite_note-18) evidenced through correlations such as the [M–sigma relation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M%E2%80%93sigma_relation "M–sigma relation") which relates the [velocity dispersion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity_dispersion "Velocity dispersion") of the surrounding stars to the mass of the black hole at the center.

Elliptical galaxies are preferentially found in [galaxy clusters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_clusters "Galaxy clusters") and in compact [groups of galaxies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_group "Galaxy group").

Unlike flat [spiral galaxies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spiral_galaxy "Spiral galaxy") with organization and structure, elliptical galaxies are more three-dimensional, without much structure, and their stars are in somewhat random orbits around the center.

[![Image 4](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/A_Multi-Wavelength_View_of_Radio_Galaxy_Hercules_A.jpg/250px-A_Multi-Wavelength_View_of_Radio_Galaxy_Hercules_A.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:A_Multi-Wavelength_View_of_Radio_Galaxy_Hercules_A.jpg)

[Hercules A](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hercules_A "Hercules A"), a supergiant elliptical galaxy and also a [radio galaxy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radio_galaxy "Radio galaxy"). The radio lobes shown here in pink are over a million light-years across.

The [largest galaxies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_largest_galaxies "List of largest galaxies") are supergiant ellipticals, or [type-cD galaxies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Type-cD_galaxy "Type-cD galaxy"). Elliptical galaxies vary greatly in both size and mass with diameters ranging from 3,000 [light years](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_year "Light year") to more than 700,000 light years, and masses from 10 5 to nearly 10 13 solar masses.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptical_galaxy#cite_note-19) This range is much broader for this galaxy type than for any other. The smallest, the [dwarf elliptical galaxies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dwarf_elliptical_galaxy "Dwarf elliptical galaxy"), may be no larger than a typical [globular cluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Globular_cluster "Globular cluster"), but contain a considerable amount of [dark matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter "Dark matter") not present in clusters. Most of these small galaxies may not be related to other ellipticals.

[![Image 5](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/MACSJ1423.8%2B2404.jpg/250px-MACSJ1423.8%2B2404.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:MACSJ1423.8%2B2404.jpg)

The brilliant central object is the supergiant elliptical galaxy SDSS J142347.87+240442.4, the dominant member of the [galaxy cluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_cluster "Galaxy cluster") MACS J1423.8+2404. It has a diameter of 380,000 light-years.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptical_galaxy#cite_note-NED_SDSSJ1423-20) Note the [gravitational lensing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_lensing "Gravitational lensing").

The [Hubble classification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_morphological_classification "Galaxy morphological classification") of elliptical galaxies contains an integer that describes how elongated the galaxy image is. The classification is determined by the ratio of the major (_a_) to the minor (_b_) axes of the galaxy's [isophotes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contour_line#Other_phenomena "Contour line"):

![Image 6: {\displaystyle 10\times \left(1-{\frac {b}{a}}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/17443047f34992a07b0a3b19ea2aa0152f7f338f)
Thus for a spherical galaxy with _a_ equal to _b_, the number is 0, and the Hubble type is E0. While the limit in the literature is about E7, it has been known since 1966[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptical_galaxy#cite_note-adsabs.harvard.edu-2) that the E4 to E7 galaxies are misclassified lenticular galaxies with disks inclined at different angles to our line of sight. This has been confirmed through spectral observations revealing the rotation of their stellar disks.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptical_galaxy#cite_note-21)[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptical_galaxy#cite_note-22) Hubble recognized that his shape classification depends both on the intrinsic shape of the galaxy, as well as the angle with which the galaxy is observed. Hence, some galaxies with Hubble type E0 are actually elongated.

It is sometimes said that there are two physical types of ellipticals: the giant ellipticals with slightly "boxy"-shaped isophotes, whose shapes result from random motion which is greater in some directions than in others (anisotropic random motion); and the "disky" normal and [dwarf ellipticals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dwarf_elliptical "Dwarf elliptical"), which contain disks.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptical_galaxy#cite_note-23)[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptical_galaxy#cite_note-24) This is, however, an abuse of the nomenclature, as there are two types of early-type galaxy, those with disks and those without. Given the existence of ES galaxies with intermediate-scale disks, it is reasonable to expect that there is a continuity from E to ES, and onto the S0 galaxies with their large-scale stellar disks that dominate the light at large radii.

[Dwarf spheroidal galaxies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dwarf_spheroidal_galaxy "Dwarf spheroidal galaxy") appear to be a distinct class: their properties are more similar to those of irregulars and late spiral-type galaxies.

At the large end of the elliptical spectrum, there is further division, beyond Hubble's classification. Beyond _gE_ giant ellipticals, lies [D-galaxies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Type-D_galaxy "Type-D galaxy") and [cD-galaxies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Type-cD_galaxy "Type-cD galaxy"). These are similar to their smaller brethren, but more diffuse, with large haloes that may as much belong to the galaxy cluster within which they reside than the centrally located giant galaxy.

[![Image 7](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/A_galactic_mega-merger.jpg/250px-A_galactic_mega-merger.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:A_galactic_mega-merger.jpg)

[NGC 3597](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NGC_3597 "NGC 3597") is the product of a collision between two galaxies. It is evolving into a giant elliptical galaxy.

In recent years, evidence has shown that a reasonable proportion (~25%) of early-type (E, ES and S0) galaxies have residual gas reservoirs[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptical_galaxy#cite_note-Young2011-25) and low-level star formation.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptical_galaxy#cite_note-Crocker2011-26)

[Herschel Space Observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herschel_Space_Observatory "Herschel Space Observatory") researchers have speculated that the central black holes in elliptical galaxies keep the gas from cooling enough for star formation.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptical_galaxy#cite_note-27)
