In physics , a spherical pendulum is a higher dimensional analogue of the pendulum . It consists of a mass m moving without friction on the surface of a sphere . The only forces acting on the mass are the reaction from the sphere and gravity .

Owing to the spherical geometry of the problem, spherical coordinates are used to describe the position of the mass in terms of ( r , θ θ , ϕ ϕ ) {\displaystyle (r,\theta ,\phi )} , where r is fixed such that r = l {\displaystyle r=l} .

The second order differential equation determining the evolution of ϕ ϕ {\displaystyle \phi } is thus ϕ ϕ ¨ ¨ sin ⁡ ⁡ θ θ = − − 2 θ θ ˙ ˙ ϕ ϕ ˙ ˙ cos ⁡ ⁡ θ θ {\displaystyle {\ddot {\phi }}\,\sin \theta =-2\,{\dot {\theta }}\,{\dot {\phi }}\,\cos \theta } .

The conical pendulum refers to the special solutions where θ θ ˙ ˙ = 0 {\displaystyle {\dot {\theta }}=0} and ϕ ϕ ˙ ˙ {\displaystyle {\dot {\phi }}} is a constant not depending on time.
