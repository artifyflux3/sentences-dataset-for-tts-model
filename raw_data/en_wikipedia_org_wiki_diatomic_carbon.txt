**Diatomic carbon** (systematically named **dicarbon** and **1λ 2,2λ 2-ethene**), is a green, gaseous [inorganic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inorganic_chemistry "Inorganic chemistry")[chemical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical "Chemical") with the [chemical formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_formula "Chemical formula") C=C (also written [C 2] or C 2). It is kinetically unstable at ambient temperature and pressure, being removed through [autopolymerisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polymerization "Polymerization"). It occurs in carbon vapor, for example in [electric arcs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_arc "Electric arc"); in [comets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comet "Comet"), [stellar atmospheres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_atmosphere "Stellar atmosphere"), and the [interstellar medium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_medium "Interstellar medium"); and in blue [hydrocarbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrocarbon "Hydrocarbon")[flames](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flame "Flame").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diatomic_carbon#cite_note-Hoffman1995-1) Diatomic carbon is the second simplest of the [allotropes of carbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allotropes_of_carbon "Allotropes of carbon") (after [atomic carbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_carbon "Atomic carbon")), and is an intermediate participant in the genesis of [fullerenes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fullerenes "Fullerenes").

C 2 is a component of carbon vapor. One paper estimates that carbon vapor is around 28% diatomic,[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diatomic_carbon#cite_note-singlet-2) but theoretically this depends on the temperature and pressure.

### Electromagnetic properties

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Diatomic_carbon&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Electromagnetic properties")]

The electrons in diatomic carbon are distributed among the molecular orbitals according to the [Aufbau principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aufbau_principle "Aufbau principle") to produce unique quantum states, with corresponding energy levels. The state with the lowest energy level, or ground state, is a singlet state (1 Σ+

_g_), which is systematically named ethene-1,2-diylidene or dicarbon(0•). There are several excited singlet and triplet states that are relatively close in energy to the ground state, which form significant proportions of a sample of dicarbon under ambient conditions. When most of these excited states undergo photochemical relaxation, they emit in the infrared region of the electromagnetic spectrum. However, one state in particular emits in the green region. That state is a triplet state (3 Π _g_), which is systematically named ethene-μ,μ-diyl-μ-ylidene or dicarbon(2•). In addition, there is an excited state somewhat further in energy from the ground state, which only form a significant proportion of a sample of dicarbon under mid-ultraviolet irradiation. Upon relaxation, this excited state fluoresces in the violet region and phosphoresces in the blue region. This state is also a singlet state (1 Π _g_), which is also named ethene-μ,μ-diyl-μ-ylidene or dicarbon(2•).

[Molecular orbital theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_orbital_theory "Molecular orbital theory") shows that there are two sets of paired electrons in a degenerate pi bonding set of orbitals. This gives a bond order of 2, meaning that there should exist a double bond between the two carbon atoms in a C 2 molecule.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diatomic_carbon#cite_note-Zhong-3) One analysis suggested instead that a [quadruple bond](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadruple_bond "Quadruple bond") exists,[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diatomic_carbon#cite_note-4) an interpretation that was disputed.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diatomic_carbon#cite_note-5)[CASSCF](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CASSCF "CASSCF") calculations indicate that the quadruple bond based on molecular orbital theory is also reasonable.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diatomic_carbon#cite_note-Zhong-3)[Bond dissociation energies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bond_dissociation_energy "Bond dissociation energy") (BDE) of [B 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diboron "Diboron"), C 2, and [N 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dinitrogen "Dinitrogen") show increasing BDE, indicating [single](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Single_bond "Single bond"), [double](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double_bond "Double bond"), and [triple bonds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triple_bond "Triple bond"), respectively.

In certain forms of crystalline carbon, such as [diamond](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diamond "Diamond") and [graphite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graphite "Graphite"), a saddle point or "hump" occurs at the bond site in the charge density. The triplet state of C 2 does follow this trend. However, the singlet state of C 2 acts more like [silicon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon "Silicon") or [germanium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germanium "Germanium"); that is, the charge density has a maximum at the bond site.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diatomic_carbon#cite_note-high-6)

Diatomic carbon will react with [acetone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone "Acetone") and [acetaldehyde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde "Acetaldehyde") to produce [acetylene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene "Acetylene") by two different pathways.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diatomic_carbon#cite_note-singlet-2)

*   [Triplet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triplet_state "Triplet state") C 2 molecules will react through an intermolecular pathway, which is shown to exhibit diradical character. The intermediate for this pathway is the ethylene radical. Its abstraction is correlated with bond energies.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diatomic_carbon#cite_note-singlet-2)
*   [Singlet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Singlet_state "Singlet state") C 2 molecules will react through an intramolecular, nonradical pathway in which two hydrogen atoms will be taken away from one molecule. The intermediate for this pathway is singlet [vinylidene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vinylidene "Vinylidene"). The singlet reaction can happen through a 1,1-diabstraction or a 1,2-diabstraction. This reaction is insensitive to isotope substitution. The different abstractions are possibly due to the spatial orientations of the collisions rather than the bond energies.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diatomic_carbon#cite_note-singlet-2)
*   Singlet C 2 will also react with [alkenes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkene "Alkene"). Acetylene is a main product; however, it appears C 2 will insert into carbon-hydrogen bonds.
*   C 2 is 2.5 times more likely to insert into a [methyl group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methyl_group "Methyl group") as into [methylene groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methylene_group "Methylene group").[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diatomic_carbon#cite_note-alkanes-7)
*   There is a disputed possible room-temperature chemical synthesis via alkynyl-λ 3-iodane.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diatomic_carbon#cite_note-8)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diatomic_carbon#cite_note-9)

[C/2022 E3 (ZTF)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C/2022_E3_(ZTF) "C/2022 E3 (ZTF)") also looks green.

The light of gas-rich comets mainly originates from the emission of diatomic carbon. An example is [C/2014 Q2 (Lovejoy)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C/2014_Q2_(Lovejoy) "C/2014 Q2 (Lovejoy)"), where there are several lines of C 2 light, mostly in the [visible spectrum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visible_spectrum "Visible spectrum"),[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diatomic_carbon#cite_note-C2014Q2Spec-10) forming the [Swan bands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swan_band "Swan band").[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diatomic_carbon#cite_note-CrniVrhObservatory-11)[C/2022 E3 (ZTF)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C/2022_E3_(ZTF) "C/2022 E3 (ZTF)"), visible in early 2023, also exhibits green color due to the presence of diatomic carbon.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diatomic_carbon#cite_note-12)
