A [torus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torus "Torus") as a square revolved around an axis parallel to one of its diagonals.

A **surface of revolution** is a [surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_(mathematics) "Surface (mathematics)") in [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space") created by [rotating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotating "Rotating") a [curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curve "Curve") (the _[generatrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generatrix "Generatrix")_) one full [revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Revolution_(unit) "Revolution (unit)") around an _[axis of rotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axis\_of\_rotation "Axis of rotation")_ (normally not [intersecting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intersection_(geometry) "Intersection (geometry)") the generatrix, except at its endpoints).[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_of_revolution#cite_note-1) The volume bounded by the surface created by this revolution is the _[solid of revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid\_of\_revolution "Solid of revolution")_.

Examples of surfaces of revolution generated by a straight line are [cylindrical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylinder_(geometry) "Cylinder (geometry)") and [conical surfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conical_surface "Conical surface") depending on whether or not the line is parallel to the axis. A circle that is rotated around any diameter generates a sphere of which it is then a [great circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_circle "Great circle"), and if the circle is rotated around an axis that does not intersect the interior of a circle, then it generates a [torus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torus "Torus") which does not intersect itself (a [ring torus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ring_torus "Ring torus")).

The sections of the surface of revolution made by planes through the axis are called _meridional sections_. Any meridional section can be considered to be the generatrix in the plane determined by it and the axis.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_of_revolution#cite_note-2)

The sections of the surface of revolution made by planes that are perpendicular to the axis are circles.

Some special cases of [hyperboloids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperboloids "Hyperboloids") (of either one or two sheets) and [elliptic paraboloids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptic_paraboloid "Elliptic paraboloid") are surfaces of revolution. These may be identified as those quadratic surfaces all of whose [cross sections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_section_(geometry) "Cross section (geometry)") perpendicular to the axis are circular.

If the curve is described by the [parametric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parametric_curve "Parametric curve") functions _x_(_t_), _y_(_t_), with t ranging over some interval [_a_,_b_], and the axis of revolution is the y-axis, then the [surface area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_area "Surface area")A y is given by the [integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral "Integral")![Image 3: {\displaystyle A_{y}=2\pi \int _{a}^{b}x(t)\,{\sqrt {\left({dx \over dt}\right)^{2}+\left({dy \over dt}\right)^{2}}}\,dt,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/789ba02b689da27cf9d900e991f9ef455e61f1a9) provided that _x_(_t_) is never negative between the endpoints a and b. This formula is the calculus equivalent of [Pappus's centroid theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pappus%27s_centroid_theorem "Pappus's centroid theorem").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_of_revolution#cite_note-3) The quantity ![Image 4: {\displaystyle {\sqrt {\left({dx \over dt}\right)^{2}+\left({dy \over dt}\right)^{2}}}\,dt}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6aa3ac547f02d1bfd7520bed2f7368e0a93bc135) comes from the [Pythagorean theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagorean_theorem "Pythagorean theorem") and represents a small segment of the arc of the curve, as in the [arc length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arc_length "Arc length") formula. The quantity 2π _x_(_t_) is the path of (the centroid of) this small segment, as required by Pappus' theorem.

Likewise, when the axis of rotation is the x-axis and provided that _y_(_t_) is never negative, the area is given by[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_of_revolution#cite_note-4)![Image 5: {\displaystyle A_{x}=2\pi \int _{a}^{b}y(t)\,{\sqrt {\left({dx \over dt}\right)^{2}+\left({dy \over dt}\right)^{2}}}\,dt.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a4d41e5f365b92ddcb921c46e6e7067dd4ffc01d)

If the continuous curve is described by the function _y_ = _f_(_x_), _a_ ≤ _x_ ≤ _b_, then the integral becomes ![Image 6: {\displaystyle A_{x}=2\pi \int _{a}^{b}y{\sqrt {1+\left({\frac {dy}{dx}}\right)^{2}}}\,dx=2\pi \int _{a}^{b}f(x){\sqrt {1+{\big (}f'(x){\big )}^{2}}}\,dx}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d676c180a8fea4c5bd8b092685b5cb18f130513b) for revolution around the x-axis, and ![Image 7: {\displaystyle A_{y}=2\pi \int _{a}^{b}x{\sqrt {1+\left({\frac {dy}{dx}}\right)^{2}}}\,dx}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a7d4b3bc0cc16765e3e8430fcb2e21837bfc6299) for revolution around the _y_-axis (provided _a_ ≥ 0). These come from the above formula.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_of_revolution#cite_note-5)

This can also be derived from multivariable integration. If a plane curve is given by ![Image 8: {\displaystyle \langle x(t),y(t)\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c2e03187084d9229dc5fc9fa10a6ea639f138990) then its corresponding surface of revolution when revolved around the x-axis has Cartesian coordinates given by ![Image 9: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} (t,\theta )=\langle y(t)\cos(\theta ),y(t)\sin(\theta ),x(t)\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e00610dc8a7ae8d2eb7652bca9fabdb3447f7b77) with ![Image 10: {\displaystyle 0\leq \theta \leq 2\pi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f318de7eb365815ca54d8b958a846dd196ab283f). Then the surface area is given by the [surface integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_integral "Surface integral")![Image 11: {\displaystyle A_{x}=\iint _{S}dS=\iint _{[a,b]\times [0,2\pi ]}\left\|{\frac {\partial \mathbf {r} }{\partial t}}\times {\frac {\partial \mathbf {r} }{\partial \theta }}\right\|\ d\theta \ dt=\int _{a}^{b}\int _{0}^{2\pi }\left\|{\frac {\partial \mathbf {r} }{\partial t}}\times {\frac {\partial \mathbf {r} }{\partial \theta }}\right\|\ d\theta \ dt.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9b6e20c212de99b253a52f3442b8f74ea73d61ac)

Computing the partial derivatives yields ![Image 12: {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial \mathbf {r} }{\partial t}}=\left\langle {\frac {dy}{dt}}\cos(\theta ),{\frac {dy}{dt}}\sin(\theta ),{\frac {dx}{dt}}\right\rangle ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/71aff3f6ee87ae9473de0690e3dde2377c3d13ae)![Image 13: {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial \mathbf {r} }{\partial \theta }}=\langle -y\sin(\theta ),y\cos(\theta ),0\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/52daf0c56d0246d1ebe07ba7eb10ef2808aced60) and computing the [cross product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product "Cross product") yields ![Image 14: {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial \mathbf {r} }{\partial t}}\times {\frac {\partial \mathbf {r} }{\partial \theta }}=\left\langle y\cos(\theta ){\frac {dx}{dt}},y\sin(\theta ){\frac {dx}{dt}},y{\frac {dy}{dt}}\right\rangle =y\left\langle \cos(\theta ){\frac {dx}{dt}},\sin(\theta ){\frac {dx}{dt}},{\frac {dy}{dt}}\right\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/51d9fcc7d8050e6553c301195a968c13e9ded5ee) where the trigonometric identity ![Image 15: {\displaystyle \sin ^{2}(\theta )+\cos ^{2}(\theta )=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47f3203bca6dc55c36d94ee525c44dac9e1716f1) was used. With this cross product, we get ![Image 16: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}A_{x}&=\int _{a}^{b}\int _{0}^{2\pi }\left\|{\frac {\partial \mathbf {r} }{\partial t}}\times {\frac {\partial \mathbf {r} }{\partial \theta }}\right\|\ d\theta \ dt\\[1ex]&=\int _{a}^{b}\int _{0}^{2\pi }\left\|y\left\langle y\cos(\theta ){\frac {dx}{dt}},y\sin(\theta ){\frac {dx}{dt}},y{\frac {dy}{dt}}\right\rangle \right\|\ d\theta \ dt\\[1ex]&=\int _{a}^{b}\int _{0}^{2\pi }y{\sqrt {\cos ^{2}(\theta )\left({\frac {dx}{dt}}\right)^{2}+\sin ^{2}(\theta )\left({\frac {dx}{dt}}\right)^{2}+\left({\frac {dy}{dt}}\right)^{2}}}\ d\theta \ dt\\[1ex]&=\int _{a}^{b}\int _{0}^{2\pi }y{\sqrt {\left({\frac {dx}{dt}}\right)^{2}+\left({\frac {dy}{dt}}\right)^{2}}}\ d\theta \ dt\\[1ex]&=\int _{a}^{b}2\pi y{\sqrt {\left({\frac {dx}{dt}}\right)^{2}+\left({\frac {dy}{dt}}\right)^{2}}}\ dt\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/67ad143220b39defd5b72caa82d8beb7b47d4946) where the same trigonometric identity was used again. The derivation for a surface obtained by revolving around the y-axis is similar.

For example, the [spherical surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere "Sphere") with unit radius is generated by the curve _y_(_t_) = sin(_t_), _x_(_t_) = cos(_t_), when t ranges over [0,π]. Its area is therefore ![Image 17: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}A&{}=2\pi \int _{0}^{\pi }\sin(t){\sqrt {{\big (}\cos(t){\big )}^{2}+{\big (}\sin(t){\big )}^{2}}}\,dt\\&{}=2\pi \int _{0}^{\pi }\sin(t)\,dt\\&{}=4\pi .\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d80e9fcf18385aff0fed736af8e606cf1307dbfd)

For the case of the spherical curve with radius r, _y_(_x_) = √_r_ 2 − _x_ 2 rotated about the x-axis ![Image 18: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}A&=2\pi \int _{-r}^{r}{\sqrt {r^{2}-x^{2}}}\,{\sqrt {1+{\frac {x^{2}}{r^{2}-x^{2}}}}}\,dx\\&=2\pi r\int _{-r}^{r}\,{\sqrt {r^{2}-x^{2}}}\,{\sqrt {\frac {1}{r^{2}-x^{2}}}}\,dx\\&=2\pi r\int _{-r}^{r}\,dx\\&=4\pi r^{2}\,\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5611e37ee5e46a49d5f00ab82740d1c2b79dfe5f)

A [minimal surface of revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minimal_surface_of_revolution "Minimal surface of revolution") is the surface of revolution of the curve between two given points which [minimizes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_optimization "Mathematical optimization")[surface area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_area "Surface area").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_of_revolution#cite_note-Mathworld:_Minimal_Surface_of_Revolution-6) A basic problem in the [calculus of variations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus_of_variations "Calculus of variations") is finding the curve between two points that produces this minimal surface of revolution.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_of_revolution#cite_note-Mathworld:_Minimal_Surface_of_Revolution-6)

There are only two [minimal surfaces of revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minimal_surfaces_of_revolution "Minimal surfaces of revolution") ([surfaces of revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surfaces_of_revolution "Surfaces of revolution") which are also minimal surfaces): the [plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_(geometry) "Plane (geometry)") and the [catenoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catenoid "Catenoid").[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_of_revolution#cite_note-7)

A surface of revolution given by rotating a curve described by ![Image 19: {\displaystyle y=f(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2311a6a75c54b0ea085a381ba472c31d59321514) around the x-axis may be most simply described by ![Image 20: {\displaystyle y^{2}+z^{2}=f(x)^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5c2e8e9be62f3332f0de9cad53b48d020f766625). This yields the parametrization in terms of ![Image 21: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) and ![Image 22: {\displaystyle \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e5ab2664b422d53eb0c7df3b87e1360d75ad9af) as ![Image 23: {\displaystyle (x,f(x)\cos(\theta ),f(x)\sin(\theta ))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/56a09e910fe55297ce35a8208117e2aef5c8fc02). If instead we revolve the curve around the y-axis, then the curve is described by ![Image 24: {\displaystyle y=f({\sqrt {x^{2}+z^{2}}})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/618f40328a32f0a7d49d2969e7a2f226cc3ba8ca), yielding the expression ![Image 25: {\displaystyle (x\cos(\theta ),f(x),x\sin(\theta ))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/89ffc8e7e04f644a81739ebb48cf11899ebac76c) in terms of the parameters ![Image 26: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) and ![Image 27: {\displaystyle \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e5ab2664b422d53eb0c7df3b87e1360d75ad9af).

If x and y are defined in terms of a parameter ![Image 28: {\displaystyle t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/65658b7b223af9e1acc877d848888ecdb4466560), then we obtain a parametrization in terms of ![Image 29: {\displaystyle t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/65658b7b223af9e1acc877d848888ecdb4466560) and ![Image 30: {\displaystyle \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e5ab2664b422d53eb0c7df3b87e1360d75ad9af). If ![Image 31: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) and ![Image 32: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d) are functions of ![Image 33: {\displaystyle t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/65658b7b223af9e1acc877d848888ecdb4466560), then the surface of revolution obtained by revolving the curve around the x-axis is described by ![Image 34: {\displaystyle (x(t),y(t)\cos(\theta ),y(t)\sin(\theta ))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/384175b23e0f9acc81b1287d84f6d246fe00ab98), and the surface of revolution obtained by revolving the curve around the y-axis is described by ![Image 35: {\displaystyle (x(t)\cos(\theta ),y(t),x(t)\sin(\theta ))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/be1673d3f4277826ca005759f1bce06c1eb3c5fc).

[Meridians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meridian_(geography) "Meridian (geography)") are always geodesics on a surface of revolution. Other geodesics are governed by [Clairaut's relation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clairaut%27s_relation "Clairaut's relation").[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_of_revolution#cite_note-8)

[![Image 36](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Toroid_by_Zureks.svg/250px-Toroid_by_Zureks.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Toroid_by_Zureks.svg)

A toroid generated from a square

A surface of revolution with a hole in, where the axis of revolution does not intersect the surface, is called a toroid.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_of_revolution#cite_note-9) For example, when a rectangle is rotated around an axis parallel to one of its edges, then a hollow square-section ring is produced. If the revolved figure is a [circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle "Circle"), then the object is called a [torus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torus "Torus").
