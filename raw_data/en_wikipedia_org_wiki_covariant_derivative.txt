Title: Covariant derivative

URL Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covariant_derivative

Published Time: 2004-01-15T17:59:35Z

Markdown Content:
In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), the **covariant derivative** is a way of specifying a [derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative "Derivative") along [tangent vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_vector "Tangent vector") of a [manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manifold "Manifold"). Alternatively, the covariant derivative is a way of introducing and working with a [connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connection_(mathematics) "Connection (mathematics)") on a manifold by means of a [differential operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_operator "Differential operator"), to be contrasted with the approach given by a [principal connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connection_(principal_bundle) "Connection (principal bundle)") on the [frame bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frame_bundle "Frame bundle") – see [affine connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_connection "Affine connection"). In the special case of a manifold [isometrically](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isometry "Isometry") embedded into a higher-dimensional [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space"), the covariant derivative can be viewed as the [orthogonal projection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_projection "Orthogonal projection") of the Euclidean [directional derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Directional_derivative "Directional derivative") onto the manifold's tangent space. In this case the Euclidean derivative is broken into two parts, the extrinsic normal component (dependent on the embedding) and the intrinsic covariant derivative component.

The name is motivated by the importance of [changes of coordinate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_covariance "General covariance") in [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics"): the covariant derivative transforms [covariantly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covariant_transformation "Covariant transformation") under a general coordinate transformation, that is, linearly via the [Jacobian matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacobian_matrix_and_determinant "Jacobian matrix and determinant") of the transformation.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covariant_derivative#cite_note-1)

This article presents an introduction to the covariant derivative of a [vector field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_field "Vector field") with respect to a vector field, both in a coordinate-free language and using a local [coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_system "Coordinate system") and the traditional index notation. The covariant derivative of a [tensor field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_field "Tensor field") is presented as an extension of the same concept. The covariant derivative generalizes straightforwardly to a notion of differentiation associated to a [connection on a vector bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connection_on_a_vector_bundle "Connection on a vector bundle"), also known as a **Koszul connection**.

Historically, at the turn of the 20th century, the covariant derivative was introduced by [Gregorio Ricci-Curbastro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gregorio_Ricci-Curbastro "Gregorio Ricci-Curbastro") and [Tullio Levi-Civita](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tullio_Levi-Civita "Tullio Levi-Civita") in the theory of [Riemannian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_geometry "Riemannian geometry") and [pseudo-Riemannian geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudo-Riemannian_manifold "Pseudo-Riemannian manifold").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covariant_derivative#cite_note-2) Ricci and Levi-Civita (following ideas of [Elwin Bruno Christoffel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elwin_Bruno_Christoffel "Elwin Bruno Christoffel")) observed that the [Christoffel symbols](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christoffel_symbols "Christoffel symbols") used to define the [curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_tensor "Riemann tensor") could also provide a notion of [differentiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative "Derivative") which generalized the classical [directional derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Directional_derivative "Directional derivative") of [vector fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_fields "Vector fields") on a manifold.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covariant_derivative#cite_note-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covariant_derivative#cite_note-4) This new derivative – the [Levi-Civita connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_connection "Levi-Civita connection") – was _[covariant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covariance\_and\_contravariance\_of\_vectors "Covariance and contravariance of vectors")_ in the sense that it satisfied Riemann's requirement that objects in geometry should be independent of their description in a particular coordinate system.

It was soon noted by other mathematicians, prominent among these being [Hermann Weyl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Weyl "Hermann Weyl"), [Jan Arnoldus Schouten](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jan_Arnoldus_Schouten "Jan Arnoldus Schouten"), and [Élie Cartan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%89lie_Cartan "Élie Cartan"),[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covariant_derivative#cite_note-5) that a covariant derivative could be defined abstractly without the presence of a [metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_tensor "Metric tensor"). The crucial feature was not a particular dependence on the metric, but that the Christoffel symbols satisfied a certain precise second-order transformation law. This transformation law could serve as a starting point for defining the derivative in a covariant manner. Thus the theory of covariant differentiation forked off from the strictly Riemannian context to include a wider range of possible geometries.

In the 1940s, practitioners of [differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry") began introducing other notions of covariant differentiation in general [vector bundles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_bundle "Vector bundle") which were, in contrast to the classical bundles of interest to geometers, not part of the [tensor analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_analysis "Tensor analysis") of the manifold. By and large, these generalized covariant derivatives had to be specified _ad hoc_ by some version of the connection concept. In 1950, [Jean-Louis Koszul](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean-Louis_Koszul "Jean-Louis Koszul") unified these new ideas of covariant differentiation in a vector bundle by means of what is known today as a [Koszul connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Koszul_connection "Koszul connection") or a connection on a vector bundle.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covariant_derivative#cite_note-6) Using ideas from [Lie algebra cohomology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_algebra_cohomology "Lie algebra cohomology"), Koszul successfully converted many of the analytic features of covariant differentiation into algebraic ones. In particular, Koszul connections eliminated the need for awkward manipulations of [Christoffel symbols](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christoffel_symbols "Christoffel symbols") (and other analogous non-[tensorial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor "Tensor") objects) in differential geometry. Thus they quickly supplanted the classical notion of covariant derivative in many post-1950 treatments of the subject.

[![Image 1](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/%D0%9A%D0%BE%D0%B2%D0%B0%D1%80%D0%B8%D0%B0%D0%BD%D1%82%D0%BD%D0%B0%D1%8F_%D0%BF%D1%80%D0%BE%D0%B8%D0%B7%D0%B2%D0%BE%D0%B4%D0%BD%D0%B0%D1%8F_c.jpg/250px-%D0%9A%D0%BE%D0%B2%D0%B0%D1%80%D0%B8%D0%B0%D0%BD%D1%82%D0%BD%D0%B0%D1%8F_%D0%BF%D1%80%D0%BE%D0%B8%D0%B7%D0%B2%D0%BE%D0%B4%D0%BD%D0%B0%D1%8F_c.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:%D0%9A%D0%BE%D0%B2%D0%B0%D1%80%D0%B8%D0%B0%D0%BD%D1%82%D0%BD%D0%B0%D1%8F_%D0%BF%D1%80%D0%BE%D0%B8%D0%B7%D0%B2%D0%BE%D0%B4%D0%BD%D0%B0%D1%8F_c.jpg)

The **covariant derivative** is a generalization of the [directional derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Directional_derivative "Directional derivative") from [vector calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_calculus "Vector calculus"). As with the directional derivative, the covariant derivative is a rule, ![Image 2: {\displaystyle \nabla _{\mathbf {u} }{\mathbf {v} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d297323577f94584c1e2b227f87803fb9f88632), which takes as its inputs: (1) a vector, **u**, defined at a point P, and (2) a [vector field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_field "Vector field")**v** defined in a [neighborhood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neighborhood_(mathematics) "Neighborhood (mathematics)") of P.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covariant_derivative#cite_note-7) The output is the vector ![Image 3: {\displaystyle \nabla _{\mathbf {u} }{\mathbf {v} }(P)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c26ad13c324c92d47c98ab15d083e63859670a9e), also at the point P. The primary difference from the usual directional derivative is that ![Image 4: {\displaystyle \nabla _{\mathbf {u} }{\mathbf {v} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d297323577f94584c1e2b227f87803fb9f88632) must, in a certain precise sense, be _independent_ of the manner in which it is expressed in a [coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_system "Coordinate system").

A vector may be _described_ as a list of numbers in terms of a [basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basis_(mathematics) "Basis (mathematics)"), but as a geometrical object the vector retains its identity regardless of how it is described. For a geometric vector written in components with respect to one basis, when the basis is changed the components transform according to a [change of basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Change_of_basis "Change of basis") formula, with the coordinates undergoing a [covariant transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covariant_transformation "Covariant transformation"). The covariant derivative is required to transform, under a change in coordinates, by a covariant transformation in the same way as a basis does (hence the name).

In the case of [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space"), one usually defines the directional derivative of a vector field in terms of the difference between two vectors at two nearby points. In such a system one [translates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Translation_(geometry) "Translation (geometry)") one of the vectors to the origin of the other, keeping it parallel, then takes their difference within the same vector space. With a Cartesian (fixed [orthonormal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthonormal "Orthonormal")) coordinate system "keeping it parallel" amounts to keeping the components constant. This ordinary directional derivative on Euclidean space is the first example of a covariant derivative.

Next, one must take into account changes of the coordinate system. For example, if the Euclidean plane is described by polar coordinates, "keeping it parallel" does _not_ amount to keeping the polar components constant under translation, since the coordinate grid itself "rotates". Thus, the same covariant derivative written in [polar coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinates_(elementary_mathematics) "Coordinates (elementary mathematics)") contains extra terms that describe how the coordinate grid itself rotates, or how in more general coordinates the grid expands, contracts, twists, interweaves, etc.

Consider the example of a particle moving along a curve _γ_(_t_) in the Euclidean plane. In polar coordinates, γ may be written in terms of its radial and angular coordinates by _γ_(_t_) = (_r_(_t_), _θ_(_t_)). A vector at a particular time t[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covariant_derivative#cite_note-8) (for instance, a constant acceleration of the particle) is expressed in terms of ![Image 5: {\displaystyle (\mathbf {e} _{r},\mathbf {e} _{\theta })}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1c0a6e3fa3fcd2ce23a8fa64d5b49105e58e9ea7), where ![Image 6: {\displaystyle \mathbf {e} _{r}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/887d07a86b50894ee5e5d16b76e8fbaf5f5eaa11) and ![Image 7: {\displaystyle \mathbf {e} _{\theta }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c8df567db25da5831ccd6615329591bf02901893) are unit tangent vectors for the polar coordinates, serving as a basis to decompose a vector in terms of radial and [tangential components](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangential_component "Tangential component"). At a slightly later time, the new basis in polar coordinates appears slightly rotated with respect to the first set. The covariant derivative of the basis vectors (the [Christoffel symbols](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christoffel_symbols "Christoffel symbols")) serve to express this change.

In a curved space, such as the surface of the Earth (regarded as a sphere), the [translation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Translation_(geometry) "Translation (geometry)") of tangent vectors between different points is not well defined, and its analog, [parallel transport](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_transport "Parallel transport"), depends on the path along which the vector is translated. A vector on a globe on the equator at point Q is directed to the north. Suppose we transport the vector (keeping it parallel) first along the equator to the point P, then drag it along a meridian to the N pole, and finally transport it along another meridian back to Q. Then we notice that the parallel-transported vector along a closed circuit does not return as the same vector; instead, it has another orientation. This would not happen in Euclidean space and is caused by the _curvature_ of the surface of the globe. The same effect occurs if we drag the vector along an infinitesimally small closed surface subsequently along two directions and then back. This infinitesimal change of the vector is a measure of the [curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvature_of_Riemannian_manifolds "Curvature of Riemannian manifolds"), and can be defined in terms of the covariant derivative.

*   The definition of the covariant derivative does not use the metric in space. However, for each metric there is a unique [torsion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torsion_tensor "Torsion tensor")-free covariant derivative called the [Levi-Civita connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_connection "Levi-Civita connection") such that the covariant derivative of the metric is zero.
*   The properties of a derivative imply that ![Image 8: {\displaystyle \nabla _{\mathbf {v} }\mathbf {u} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/44967125134581a5c711f8f516d2184e34beaeec) depends on the values of u in a neighborhood of a point p in the same way as e.g. the derivative of a scalar function f along a curve at a given point p depends on the values of f in a neighborhood of p.
*   The information in a neighborhood of a point p in the covariant derivative can be used to define [parallel transport](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_transport "Parallel transport") of a vector. Also the [curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvature_of_Riemannian_manifolds "Curvature of Riemannian manifolds"), [torsion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torsion_tensor "Torsion tensor"), and [geodesics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic "Geodesic") may be defined only in terms of the covariant derivative or other related variation on the idea of a [linear connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connection_(vector_bundle) "Connection (vector bundle)").

For a very simple example that captures the essence of the description above, draw a circle on a flat sheet of paper. Travel around the circle at a constant speed. The derivative of your velocity, your acceleration vector, always points radially inward. Roll this sheet of paper into a cylinder. Now the (Euclidean) derivative of your velocity has a component that sometimes points inward toward the axis of the cylinder depending on whether you're near a solstice or an equinox. (At the point of the circle when you are moving parallel to the axis, there is no inward acceleration. Conversely, at a point (1/4 of a circle later) when the velocity is along the cylinder's bend, the inward acceleration is maximum.) This is the (Euclidean) normal component. The covariant derivative component is the component parallel to the cylinder's surface, and is the same as that before you rolled the sheet into a cylinder.

A covariant derivative is a [(Koszul) connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connection_(vector_bundle) "Connection (vector bundle)") on the [tangent bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_bundle "Tangent bundle") and other [tensor bundles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_bundle "Tensor bundle"): it differentiates vector fields in a way analogous to the usual differential on functions. The definition extends to a differentiation on the dual of vector fields (i.e. [covector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cotangent_space "Cotangent space") fields) and to arbitrary [tensor fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_field "Tensor field"), in a unique way that ensures compatibility with the tensor product and trace operations (tensor contraction).

Given a point ![Image 32: {\displaystyle p\in M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35ad2c18a15749505c928763cd4fdb56f4982816) of the manifold M, a real function ![Image 33: {\displaystyle f:M\to \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3f7c129dfb3446a2251aabc1cb3eba0b13ee7b21) on the manifold and a tangent vector ![Image 34: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} \in T_{p}M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9d0da5d57a542a6ab57433debbb48cd1e4b2543f), the covariant derivative of f at p along **v** is the scalar at p, denoted ![Image 35: {\displaystyle \left(\nabla _{\mathbf {v} }f\right)_{p}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b73a7d1dc6e8cb9a9cbf083383be3df6d9afeb09), that represents the [principal part](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principal_part#Calculus "Principal part") of the change in the value of f when the argument of f is changed by the infinitesimal displacement vector **v**. (This is the [differential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_of_a_function "Differential of a function") of f evaluated against the vector **v**.) Formally, there is a differentiable curve ![Image 36: {\displaystyle \phi :[-1,1]\to M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7e5d89fc45414df51e7c7100dd8a5034886d0f05) such that ![Image 37: {\displaystyle \phi (0)=p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cd00d10763ae974c85ed282db09bcbe65ec6761d) and ![Image 38: {\displaystyle \phi '(0)=\mathbf {v} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/04ce61fa9d114c9c242f18e262e2e238c6235264), and the covariant derivative of f at p is defined by ![Image 39: {\displaystyle \left(\nabla _{\mathbf {v} }f\right)_{p}=\left(f\circ \phi \right)^{\prime }\left(0\right)=\lim _{t\to 0}{\frac {f(\phi \left(t\right))-f(p)}{t}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cbd7b6197090bd588790e7e4262ad2866ed33817)

When ![Image 40: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} :M\to T_{p}M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ab8573f23f6a5ed765a38fd537a355d42b2f1bde) is a vector field on M, the covariant derivative ![Image 41: {\displaystyle \nabla _{\mathbf {v} }f:M\to \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a3d699728ba63767a9addbbf907d4efbb51cfe95) is the function that associates with each point p in the common domain of f and **v** the scalar ![Image 42: {\displaystyle \left(\nabla _{\mathbf {v} }f\right)_{p}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b73a7d1dc6e8cb9a9cbf083383be3df6d9afeb09).

For a scalar function f and vector field **v**, the covariant derivative ![Image 43: {\displaystyle \nabla _{\mathbf {v} }f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7a5c1a9b537c696a19c9a3ae520218d4419878f9) coincides with the [Lie derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_derivative "Lie derivative")![Image 44: {\displaystyle L_{\mathbf {v} }(f)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8c967df13cb70afe4849b88349c898426195ed9e), and with the [exterior derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_derivative "Exterior derivative")![Image 45: {\displaystyle df(\mathbf {v} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/376a822302fad3502564782c5f706e42b140b5f1).

Given a point p of the manifold M, a vector field ![Image 46: {\displaystyle \mathbf {u} :M\to T_{p}M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/046154ee560c9f43190906de56181d73dfe49be2) defined in a neighborhood of p and a tangent vector ![Image 47: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} \in T_{p}M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9d0da5d57a542a6ab57433debbb48cd1e4b2543f), the covariant derivative of **u** at p along **v** is the tangent vector at p, denoted ![Image 48: {\displaystyle (\nabla _{\mathbf {v} }\mathbf {u} )_{p}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b01e907c29d1e95676da974602582ef9b3fcad8d), such that the following properties hold (for any tangent vectors **v**, **x** and **y** at p, vector fields **u** and **w** defined in a neighborhood of p, scalar values g and h at p, and scalar function f defined in a neighborhood of p):

1.   ![Image 49: {\displaystyle \left(\nabla _{\mathbf {v} }\mathbf {u} \right)_{p}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/aa0ec958d18d14ea97ec73659f32a1450a2cb7e5) is linear in ![Image 50: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35c1866e359fbfd2e0f606c725ba5cc37a5195d6) so ![Image 51: {\displaystyle \left(\nabla _{g\mathbf {x} +h\mathbf {y} }\mathbf {u} \right)_{p}=g(p)\left(\nabla _{\mathbf {x} }\mathbf {u} \right)_{p}+h(p)\left(\nabla _{\mathbf {y} }\mathbf {u} \right)_{p}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/848b0d5c7df482da743a2419ba5e39b97c96ec29)
2.   ![Image 52: {\displaystyle \left(\nabla _{\mathbf {v} }\mathbf {u} \right)_{p}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/aa0ec958d18d14ea97ec73659f32a1450a2cb7e5) is additive in ![Image 53: {\displaystyle \mathbf {u} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/261e20fe101de02a771021d9d4466c0ad3e352d7) so: ![Image 54: {\displaystyle \left(\nabla _{\mathbf {v} }\left[\mathbf {u} +\mathbf {w} \right]\right)_{p}=\left(\nabla _{\mathbf {v} }\mathbf {u} \right)_{p}+\left(\nabla _{\mathbf {v} }\mathbf {w} \right)_{p}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5b778432f3325c9ea994ac1c4e9ccdd347033fe)
3.   ![Image 55: {\displaystyle (\nabla _{\mathbf {v} }\mathbf {u} )_{p}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b01e907c29d1e95676da974602582ef9b3fcad8d) obeys the [product rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Product_rule "Product rule"); i.e., where ![Image 56: {\displaystyle \nabla _{\mathbf {v} }f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7a5c1a9b537c696a19c9a3ae520218d4419878f9) is defined above, ![Image 57: {\displaystyle \left(\nabla _{\mathbf {v} }\left[f\mathbf {u} \right]\right)_{p}=f(p)\left(\nabla _{\mathbf {v} }\mathbf {u} )_{p}+(\nabla _{\mathbf {v} }f\right)_{p}\mathbf {u} _{p}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d4936abae65ff637d6b70b22780c065462e76fb7)

Note that ![Image 58: {\displaystyle \left(\nabla _{\mathbf {v} }\mathbf {u} \right)_{p}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/aa0ec958d18d14ea97ec73659f32a1450a2cb7e5) depends not only on the value of **u** at p but also on values of **u** in a neighborhood of p, because the last property, the product rule, involves the directional derivative of f (by the vector **v**).

If **u** and **v** are both vector fields defined over a common domain, then ![Image 59: {\displaystyle \nabla _{\mathbf {v} }\mathbf {u} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/44967125134581a5c711f8f516d2184e34beaeec) denotes the vector field whose value at each point p of the domain is the tangent vector ![Image 60: {\displaystyle \left(\nabla _{\mathbf {v} }\mathbf {u} \right)_{p}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/aa0ec958d18d14ea97ec73659f32a1450a2cb7e5).

Given a field of [covectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cotangent_space "Cotangent space") (or [one-form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One-form "One-form")) ![Image 61: {\displaystyle \alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b79333175c8b3f0840bfb4ec41b8072c83ea88d3) defined in a neighborhood of p, its covariant derivative ![Image 62: {\displaystyle (\nabla _{\mathbf {v} }\alpha )_{p}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cee666cbcfb8f72b71fc2d5142a86c5d8b431a64) is defined in a way to make the resulting operation compatible with tensor contraction and the product rule. That is, ![Image 63: {\displaystyle (\nabla _{\mathbf {v} }\alpha )_{p}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cee666cbcfb8f72b71fc2d5142a86c5d8b431a64) is defined as the unique one-form at p such that the following identity is satisfied for all vector fields **u** in a neighborhood of p![Image 64: {\displaystyle \left(\nabla _{\mathbf {v} }\alpha \right)_{p}\left(\mathbf {u} _{p}\right)=\nabla _{\mathbf {v} }\left[\alpha \left(\mathbf {u} \right)\right]_{p}-\alpha _{p}\left[\left(\nabla _{\mathbf {v} }\mathbf {u} \right)_{p}\right].}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f99f0886345bebb1123ad6cb5d6810b8795e3a36)

The covariant derivative of a covector field along a vector field **v** is again a covector field.

Once the covariant derivative is defined for fields of vectors and covectors it can be defined for arbitrary [tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_(intrinsic_definition) "Tensor (intrinsic definition)") fields by imposing the following identities for every pair of tensor fields ![Image 65: {\displaystyle \varphi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/33ee699558d09cf9d653f6351f9fda0b2f4aaa3e) and ![Image 66: {\displaystyle \psi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/45e5789e5d9c8f7c79744f43ecaaf8ba42a8553a) in a neighborhood of the point p: ![Image 67: {\displaystyle \nabla _{\mathbf {v} }\left(\varphi \otimes \psi \right)_{p}=\left(\nabla _{\mathbf {v} }\varphi \right)_{p}\otimes \psi (p)+\varphi (p)\otimes \left(\nabla _{\mathbf {v} }\psi \right)_{p},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e9a82cad853597c0a25518011e4d7d03151b0527) and for ![Image 68: {\displaystyle \varphi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/33ee699558d09cf9d653f6351f9fda0b2f4aaa3e) and ![Image 69: {\displaystyle \psi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/45e5789e5d9c8f7c79744f43ecaaf8ba42a8553a) of the same valence ![Image 70: {\displaystyle \nabla _{\mathbf {v} }(\varphi +\psi )_{p}=(\nabla _{\mathbf {v} }\varphi )_{p}+(\nabla _{\mathbf {v} }\psi )_{p}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dcbd7e70703e3ac67c3e17d27ae27396ce78587f) The covariant derivative of a tensor field along a vector field **v** is again a tensor field of the same type.

Explicitly, let T be a tensor field of type (_p_, _q_). Consider T to be a differentiable [multilinear map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multilinear_map "Multilinear map") of [smooth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smooth_function "Smooth function")[sections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Section_(fiber_bundle) "Section (fiber bundle)")_α_ 1, _α_ 2, ..., _α_ _q_ of the cotangent bundle _T_∗_M_ and of sections _X_ 1, _X_ 2, ..., _X_ _p_ of the [tangent bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_bundle "Tangent bundle")_TM_, written _T_(_α_ 1, _α_ 2, ..., _X_ 1, _X_ 2, ...) into **R**. The covariant derivative of T along Y is given by the formula ![Image 71: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}(\nabla _{Y}T)\left(\alpha _{1},\alpha _{2},\ldots ,X_{1},X_{2},\ldots \right)=&{}\nabla _{Y}\left(T\left(\alpha _{1},\alpha _{2},\ldots ,X_{1},X_{2},\ldots \right)\right)\\&{}-T\left(\nabla _{Y}\alpha _{1},\alpha _{2},\ldots ,X_{1},X_{2},\ldots \right)-T\left(\alpha _{1},\nabla _{Y}\alpha _{2},\ldots ,X_{1},X_{2},\ldots \right)-\cdots \\&{}-T\left(\alpha _{1},\alpha _{2},\ldots ,\nabla _{Y}X_{1},X_{2},\ldots \right)-T\left(\alpha _{1},\alpha _{2},\ldots ,X_{1},\nabla _{Y}X_{2},\ldots \right)-\cdots \end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/006b54e2c5e5945a6fc8e6bfc2cefaf97cc432f5)

Coordinate description
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Covariant_derivative&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Coordinate description")]

Given coordinate functions ![Image 72: {\displaystyle x^{i},\ i=0,1,2,\dots ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5adc0e6d04014ddafe356638108687e5953d5a1) any [tangent vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_vector "Tangent vector") can be described by its components in the basis ![Image 73: {\displaystyle \mathbf {e} _{i}={\frac {\partial }{\partial x^{i}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/911fcdb9705d9e50bb8fcd03e832ccc853972a44)

The covariant derivative of a basis vector along a basis vector is again a vector and so can be expressed as a linear combination ![Image 74: {\displaystyle \Gamma ^{k}\mathbf {e} _{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/31ab1d1e875a06d40f513d65516543d0a8695b28). To specify the covariant derivative it is enough to specify the covariant derivative of each basis vector field ![Image 75: {\displaystyle \mathbf {e} _{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ba77c5a75ef9e230d1a36183785477a2eb3c5c1e) along ![Image 76: {\displaystyle \mathbf {e} _{j}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b7742f3851d608848056eab437b32e8b753dd5b). ![Image 77: {\displaystyle \nabla _{\mathbf {e} _{j}}\mathbf {e} _{i}={\Gamma ^{k}}_{ij}\mathbf {e} _{k},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/34e96f9b2a4df1ac6e79a74d4dd4c625a79c49de)

the coefficients ![Image 78: {\displaystyle \Gamma ^{k}{}_{ij}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a7e4ec7aee48f357858d3b563fdb772cc6857ffe) are the components of the connection with respect to a system of local coordinates. In the theory of Riemannian and pseudo-Riemannian manifolds, the components of the Levi-Civita connection with respect to a system of local coordinates are called [Christoffel symbols](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christoffel_symbols "Christoffel symbols").

Then using the rules in the definition, we find that for general vector fields ![Image 79: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} =v^{j}\mathbf {e} _{j}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e153a27bb09fff10021e47fa6a412dce3bf9e439) and ![Image 80: {\displaystyle \mathbf {u} =u^{i}\mathbf {e} _{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a1e8b53e76b7ca5d369f9d4b42d723f18f304120) we get ![Image 81: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\nabla _{\mathbf {v} }\mathbf {u} &=\nabla _{v^{j}\mathbf {e} _{j}}u^{i}\mathbf {e} _{i}\\&=v^{j}\nabla _{\mathbf {e} _{j}}u^{i}\mathbf {e} _{i}\\&=v^{j}u^{i}\nabla _{\mathbf {e} _{j}}\mathbf {e} _{i}+v^{j}\mathbf {e} _{i}\nabla _{\mathbf {e} _{j}}u^{i}\\&=v^{j}u^{i}{\Gamma ^{k}}_{ij}\mathbf {e} _{k}+v^{j}{\partial u^{i} \over \partial x^{j}}\mathbf {e} _{i}\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bff311c04dcab23f7733455253a6e4477fa9829c) so ![Image 82: {\displaystyle \nabla _{\mathbf {v} }\mathbf {u} =\left(v^{j}u^{i}{\Gamma ^{k}}_{ij}+v^{j}{\partial u^{k} \over \partial x^{j}}\right)\mathbf {e} _{k}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/18a0cb2ed255cb3bafe19962bcb9c37b777d467d)

The first term in this formula is responsible for "twisting" the coordinate system with respect to the covariant derivative and the second for changes of components of the vector field u. In particular ![Image 83: {\displaystyle \nabla _{\mathbf {e} _{j}}\mathbf {u} =\nabla _{j}\mathbf {u} =\left({\frac {\partial u^{i}}{\partial x^{j}}}+u^{k}{\Gamma ^{i}}_{kj}\right)\mathbf {e} _{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b3d3bdddbaa79ee5adb0ed915bc94fdb8f617c50)

In words: the covariant derivative is the usual derivative along the coordinates with correction terms which tell how the coordinates change.

For covectors similarly we have ![Image 84: {\displaystyle \nabla _{\mathbf {e} _{j}}{\mathbf {\theta } }=\left({\frac {\partial \theta _{i}}{\partial x^{j}}}-\theta _{k}{\Gamma ^{k}}_{ij}\right){\mathbf {e} ^{*}}^{i},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d039bc037194c47f94e2117f0a5192927e39ea4a) where ![Image 85: {\displaystyle {\mathbf {e} ^{*}}^{i}(\mathbf {e} _{j})={\delta ^{i}}_{j}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ad68203e6b8ed59d21856f6fb275f31d6102a540).

The covariant derivative of a type (_r_, _s_) tensor field along ![Image 86: {\displaystyle e_{c}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bff4dd814a40eac238f8dd5db4ac12a3ba36bb47) is given by the expression:

![Image 87: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}{(\nabla _{e_{c}}T)^{a_{1}\ldots a_{r}}}_{b_{1}\ldots b_{s}}={}&{\frac {\partial }{\partial x^{c}}}{T^{a_{1}\ldots a_{r}}}_{b_{1}\ldots b_{s}}\\&+\,{\Gamma ^{a_{1}}}_{dc}{T^{da_{2}\ldots a_{r}}}_{b_{1}\ldots b_{s}}+\cdots +{\Gamma ^{a_{r}}}_{dc}{T^{a_{1}\ldots a_{r-1}d}}_{b_{1}\ldots b_{s}}\\&-\,{\Gamma ^{d}}_{b_{1}c}{T^{a_{1}\ldots a_{r}}}_{db_{2}\ldots b_{s}}-\cdots -{\Gamma ^{d}}_{b_{s}c}{T^{a_{1}\ldots a_{r}}}_{b_{1}\ldots b_{s-1}d}.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6732f7bb7f9f4d4a3b27a3d4741cd59844f5dcdf) Or, in words: take the partial derivative of the tensor and add: ![Image 88: {\displaystyle +{\Gamma ^{a_{i}}}_{dc}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5a943f29746ccb9eda876b6b9ef8040662ec65be) for every upper index ![Image 89: {\displaystyle a_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0bc77764b2e74e64a63341054fa90f3e07db275f), and ![Image 90: {\displaystyle -{\Gamma ^{d}}_{b_{i}c}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/213ea32d0dc3ed8fc1e446f5aa47e8f681f1d6fe) for every lower index ![Image 91: {\displaystyle b_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/40a8c2db2990a53c683e75961826167c5adac7c3).

If instead of a tensor, one is trying to differentiate a _[tensor density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor\_density "Tensor density")_ (of weight +1), then one also adds a term ![Image 92: {\displaystyle -{\Gamma ^{d}}_{dc}{T^{a_{1}\ldots a_{r}}}_{b_{1}\ldots b_{s}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/411045a7dab5c672da11a92c84cd3ce0de3c5d75) If it is a tensor density of weight W, then multiply that term by W. For example, ![Image 93: {\textstyle {\sqrt {-g}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8446fb32de221bcd0b8679ad7034987375ca4f2a) is a scalar density (of weight +1), so we get: ![Image 94: {\displaystyle \left({\sqrt {-g}}\right)_{;c}=\left({\sqrt {-g}}\right)_{,c}-{\sqrt {-g}}\,{\Gamma ^{d}}_{dc}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c41034557db08866fdd6dcc98e428434145281ee) where the semicolon ";" indicates covariant differentiation and the comma "," indicates partial differentiation. Incidentally, this particular expression is equal to zero, because the covariant derivative of a function solely of the metric is always zero.

In textbooks on physics, the covariant derivative is sometimes simply stated in terms of its components in this equation.

Often a notation is used in which the covariant derivative is given with a [semicolon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semicolon "Semicolon"), while a normal [partial derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_derivative "Partial derivative") is indicated by a [comma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comma "Comma"). In this notation we write the same as: ![Image 95: {\displaystyle \nabla _{e_{j}}\mathbf {v} \ {\stackrel {\mathrm {def} }{=}}\ {v^{s}}_{;j}\mathbf {e} _{s}\;\;\;\;\;\;{v^{i}}_{;j}={v^{i}}_{,j}+v^{k}{\Gamma ^{i}}_{kj}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/34b0ac6bc9031d4e6e3bd06e61668fbf795d129c) In case two or more indexes appear after the semicolon, all of them must be understood as covariant derivatives: ![Image 96: {\displaystyle \nabla _{e_{k}}\left(\nabla _{e_{j}}\mathbf {v} \right)\ {\stackrel {\mathrm {def} }{=}}\ {v^{s}}_{;jk}\mathbf {e} _{s}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c1b7105539069b9db8414a5a684ce1cd247bb78e)

In some older texts (notably Adler, Bazin & Schiffer, _Introduction to General Relativity_), the covariant derivative is denoted by a double pipe and the partial derivative by single pipe: ![Image 97: {\displaystyle \nabla _{e_{j}}\mathbf {v} \ {\stackrel {\mathrm {def} }{=}}\ {v^{i}}_{||j}={v^{i}}_{|j}+v^{k}{\Gamma ^{i}}_{kj}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7810d20698b5cce4f60925f328dd3887486c3bd6)

Covariant derivative by field type
----------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Covariant_derivative&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Covariant derivative by field type")]

For a scalar field ![Image 98: {\displaystyle \phi \,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c69f1c4a95b2d750b30fa4cf5d5d068a573ac0d8), covariant differentiation is simply partial differentiation: ![Image 99: {\displaystyle \phi _{;a}\equiv \partial _{a}\phi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5152f530a48e239e088c15873fe1b96af1cb011c)

For a contravariant vector field ![Image 100: {\displaystyle \lambda ^{a}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/89e7dfb6af28964a99ec1ff1d96eef8057de4f3d), we have: ![Image 101: {\displaystyle {\lambda ^{a}}_{;b}\equiv \partial _{b}\lambda ^{a}+{\Gamma ^{a}}_{bc}\lambda ^{c}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3b4a5db6b509ea3c83a8512986bf0700a086cef0)

For a covariant vector field ![Image 102: {\displaystyle \lambda _{a}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b7687dadd30027695922f307162cfea498553bc8), we have: ![Image 103: {\displaystyle \lambda _{a;c}\equiv \partial _{c}\lambda _{a}-{\Gamma ^{b}}_{ca}\lambda _{b}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ca74564002435c767f8d41285495f142b9064349)

For a type (2,0) tensor field ![Image 104: {\displaystyle \tau ^{ab}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e5d2079cb5dd9a1ca81cdcc61ff92385b4b21105), we have: ![Image 105: {\displaystyle {\tau ^{ab}}_{;c}\equiv \partial _{c}\tau ^{ab}+{\Gamma ^{a}}_{cd}\tau ^{db}+{\Gamma ^{b}}_{cd}\tau ^{ad}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c867ba71fc55563bd49d1ed1a393c56ab83d028d)

For a type (0,2) tensor field ![Image 106: {\displaystyle \tau _{ab}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/696d50933746fb30555ecdfe94e102148ab3bd1d), we have: ![Image 107: {\displaystyle \tau _{ab;c}\equiv \partial _{c}\tau _{ab}-{\Gamma ^{d}}_{ca}\tau _{db}-{\Gamma ^{d}}_{cb}\tau _{ad}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e8b43fc1a79d0c25705906b2f4b16c71e9860e4f)

For a type (1,1) tensor field ![Image 108: {\displaystyle {\tau ^{a}}_{b}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0c4dd29e4b76239f90464b193b6d9ff3c799b410), we have: ![Image 109: {\displaystyle {\tau ^{a}}_{b;c}\equiv \partial _{c}{\tau ^{a}}_{b}+{\Gamma ^{a}}_{cd}{\tau ^{d}}_{b}-{\Gamma ^{d}}_{cb}{\tau ^{a}}_{d}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/96c69cbcd69bca8d7e76b95fe1f3a3fbd9634d84)

The notation above is meant in the sense ![Image 110: {\displaystyle {\tau ^{ab}}_{;c}\equiv \left(\nabla _{\mathbf {e} _{c}}\tau \right)^{ab}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/82c3a3860fc950f5e7a79357f252529f71ddda36)

In general, covariant derivatives do not commute. By example, the covariant derivatives of vector field ![Image 111: {\displaystyle \lambda _{a;bc}\neq \lambda _{a;cb}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/610a74619948aa3c1244c08a02491a2a230f0b3f). The [Riemann tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_tensor "Riemann tensor")![Image 112: {\displaystyle {R^{d}}_{abc}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5d80a1b247a900bb226f4981af6bc1bfa4cfbcbe) is defined such that: ![Image 113: {\displaystyle \lambda _{a;bc}-\lambda _{a;cb}={R^{d}}_{abc}\lambda _{d}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1121d09a7c3115130b280c6a316ec6aba89977bd) or, equivalently, ![Image 114: {\displaystyle {\lambda ^{a}}_{;bc}-{\lambda ^{a}}_{;cb}=-{R^{a}}_{dbc}\lambda ^{d}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/668a20ad82381db307f920236c9a1a99f4d9f860)

The covariant derivative of a (2,0)-tensor field fulfills: ![Image 115: {\displaystyle {\tau ^{ab}}_{;cd}-{\tau ^{ab}}_{;dc}=-{R^{a}}_{ecd}\tau ^{eb}-{R^{b}}_{ecd}\tau ^{ae}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2cae4682f6d2cefc58b03cd467da3c2c6c8c7f19)

The latter can be shown by taking (without loss of generality) that ![Image 116: {\displaystyle \tau ^{ab}=\lambda ^{a}\mu ^{b}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/51d3b4d8743f2f8dc192169a5ea08a784cda8987).

Derivative along a curve
------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Covariant_derivative&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Derivative along a curve")]

Since the covariant derivative ![Image 117: {\displaystyle \nabla _{X}T}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/afbdf75173b1357b66434d693dc4ac665a8c4694) of a tensor field T at a point p depends only on the value of the vector field X at p one can define the covariant derivative along a smooth curve ![Image 118: {\displaystyle \gamma (t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54fa4a5d64e164410e4a18106677bebefe1a1f1b) in a manifold: ![Image 119: {\displaystyle D_{t}T=\nabla _{{\dot {\gamma }}(t)}T.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d829a764fbb50380e6117e18438a28530ae00903) Note that the tensor field T only needs to be defined on the curve ![Image 120: {\displaystyle \gamma (t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54fa4a5d64e164410e4a18106677bebefe1a1f1b) for this definition to make sense.

In particular, ![Image 121: {\displaystyle {\dot {\gamma }}(t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9dc803b9b734185548ca3c18bb50e78bc44c1b35) is a vector field along the curve ![Image 122: {\displaystyle \gamma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a223c880b0ce3da8f64ee33c4f0010beee400b1a) itself. If ![Image 123: {\displaystyle \nabla _{{\dot {\gamma }}(t)}{\dot {\gamma }}(t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c8ffd33a5ccb2dbc006858fb4aa0249531392466) vanishes then the curve is called a geodesic of the covariant derivative. If the covariant derivative is the [Levi-Civita connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_connection "Levi-Civita connection") of a [positive-definite metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_tensor "Metric tensor") then the geodesics for the connection are precisely the [geodesics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesics "Geodesics") of the metric that are parametrized by [arc length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arc_length#Generalization_to_(pseudo-)Riemannian_manifolds "Arc length").

The derivative along a curve is also used to define the [parallel transport](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_transport "Parallel transport") along the curve.

Sometimes the covariant derivative along a curve is called **absolute** or **intrinsic derivative**.

Relation to Lie derivative
--------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Covariant_derivative&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: Relation to Lie derivative")]

A covariant derivative introduces an extra geometric structure on a manifold that allows vectors in neighboring tangent spaces to be compared: there is no canonical way to compare vectors from different tangent spaces because there is no canonical coordinate system.

There is however another generalization of directional derivatives which _is_ canonical: the [Lie derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_derivative "Lie derivative"), which evaluates the change of one vector field along the flow of another vector field. Thus, one must know both vector fields in a neighborhood, not merely at a single point. The covariant derivative on the other hand introduces its own change for vectors in a given direction, and it only depends on the vector direction at a single point, rather than a vector field in a neighborhood of a point. In other words, the covariant derivative is linear (over _C_∞(_M_)) in the direction argument, while the Lie derivative is linear in neither argument.

Note that the antisymmetrized covariant derivative ∇_u_ _v_ − ∇_v_ _u_, and the Lie derivative _L_ _u_ _v_ differ by the [torsion of the connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torsion_of_connection "Torsion of connection"), so that if a connection is torsion free, then its antisymmetrization _is_ the Lie derivative.
