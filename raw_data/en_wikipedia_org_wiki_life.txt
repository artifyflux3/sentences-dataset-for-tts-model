**Life**, also known as **biota**, refers to [matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matter "Matter") that has [biological processes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biological_process "Biological process"), such as [signaling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_signaling "Cell signaling") and self-sustaining processes. It is defined descriptively by the capacity for [homeostasis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homeostasis "Homeostasis"), [organisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Structure#Biological "Structure"), [metabolism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metabolism "Metabolism"), [growth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_growth "Cell growth"), [adaptation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adaptation "Adaptation"), response to [stimuli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stimulus_(physiology) "Stimulus (physiology)"), and [reproduction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reproduction "Reproduction"). All life over time eventually reaches a state of [death](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Death "Death"), and none is [immortal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immortality "Immortality"). Many philosophical definitions of [living systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Living_systems "Living systems") have been proposed, such as [self-organizing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-organizing "Self-organizing") systems. [Viruses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virus "Virus") in particular make definition difficult as they replicate only in [host](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Host_(biology) "Host (biology)") cells. Life exists all over the Earth in air, water, and [soil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soil "Soil"), with many [ecosystems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem "Ecosystem") forming the [biosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere "Biosphere"). Some of these are harsh environments occupied only by [extremophiles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extremophile "Extremophile").

Life has been studied since ancient times, with theories such as [Empedocles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empedocles "Empedocles")'s [materialism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Materialism "Materialism") asserting that it was composed of [four eternal elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_element "Classical element"), and [Aristotle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotle "Aristotle")'s [hylomorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hylomorphism "Hylomorphism") asserting that living things have [souls](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soul "Soul") and embody both [form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Form_(Plato) "Form (Plato)") and matter. [Life originated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Origin_of_life "Origin of life") at least 3.5 billion years ago, resulting in a [universal common ancestor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universal_common_ancestor "Universal common ancestor"). This evolved into all the [species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Species "Species") that exist now, by way of many [extinct](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinct "Extinct") species, some of which have left traces as [fossils](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fossil "Fossil"). Attempts to classify living things, too, [began with Aristotle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotle%27s_biology "Aristotle's biology"). Modern [classification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taxonomy "Taxonomy") began with [Carl Linnaeus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus "Carl Linnaeus")'s system of [binomial nomenclature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature "Binomial nomenclature") in the 1740s.

Living things are composed of [biochemical molecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biochemistry "Biochemistry"), formed mainly from a few core [chemical elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_element "Chemical element"). All living things contain two types of large molecule, [proteins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protein "Protein") and [nucleic acids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleic_acid "Nucleic acid"), the latter usually both [DNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNA "DNA") and [RNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RNA "RNA"): these carry the information needed by each species, including the instructions to make each type of protein. The proteins, in turn, serve as the machinery which carries out the many chemical processes of life. The [cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_(biology) "Cell (biology)") is the structural and functional unit of life. Smaller organisms, including [prokaryotes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prokaryote "Prokaryote") (bacteria and [archaea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archaea "Archaea")), consist of small single cells. Larger [organisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism "Organism"), mainly [eukaryotes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eukaryote "Eukaryote"), can consist of single cells or may be [multicellular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multicellular "Multicellular") with more complex structure. Life is only known to exist on Earth but [extraterrestrial life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extraterrestrial_life "Extraterrestrial life") is [thought probable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermi_paradox "Fermi paradox"). [Artificial life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artificial_life "Artificial life") is being simulated and explored by scientists and engineers.

Definitions
-----------

### Challenge

The definition of life has long been a challenge for scientists and philosophers.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-Definitions_2009-6)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-Emmeche1997-7)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-McKay-8) This is partially because life is a process, not a substance.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-DefinitionMotivation-9)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-SeedingBook-10)[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-11) This is complicated by a lack of knowledge of the characteristics of living entities, if any, that may have developed outside Earth.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-12)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-Bioethics-13) Philosophical definitions of life have also been put forward, with similar difficulties on how to distinguish living things from the non-living.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-Jeuken1975-14)[Legal definitions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legal_death "Legal death") of life have been debated, though these generally focus on the decision to declare a human dead, and the legal ramifications of this decision.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-Capron1978-15) At least 123 definitions of life have been compiled.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-JBSD-20110317-16)

### Descriptive

Since there is no consensus for a definition of life, most current definitions in biology are descriptive. Life is considered a characteristic of something that preserves, furthers or reinforces its existence in the given environment. This implies all or most of the following traits:[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-McKay-8)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-Koshland-17)[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-18)[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-merriamwebster-19)[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-20)[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-JBS-2012Feb-21)

1.   [Homeostasis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homeostasis "Homeostasis"): regulation of the internal environment to maintain a constant state; for example, [sweating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sweating "Sweating") to reduce temperature.
2.   [Organisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organisation "Organisation"): being structurally composed of one or more [cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_(biology) "Cell (biology)")– the basic units of life.
3.   [Metabolism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metabolism "Metabolism"): transformation of energy, used to convert chemicals into cellular components ([anabolism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anabolism "Anabolism")) and to decompose organic matter ([catabolism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catabolism "Catabolism")). Living things [require energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bioenergetics "Bioenergetics") for homeostasis and other activities.
4.   Growth: maintenance of a higher rate of anabolism than catabolism. A growing organism increases in size and structure.
5.   [Adaptation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adaptation "Adaptation"): the evolutionary process whereby an organism becomes better able to live in its [habitat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habitat "Habitat").[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-22)[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-23)[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-24)
6.   Response to [stimuli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stimulus_(physiology) "Stimulus (physiology)"): such as the contraction of a [unicellular organism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unicellular_organism "Unicellular organism") away from external chemicals, the complex reactions involving all the senses of [multicellular organisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multicellular_organisms "Multicellular organisms"), or the motion of the leaves of a plant turning toward the sun ([phototropism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phototropism "Phototropism")), and [chemotaxis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemotaxis "Chemotaxis").
7.   [Reproduction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reproduction "Reproduction"): the ability to produce new individual organisms, either [asexually](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asexual_reproduction "Asexual reproduction") from a single parent organism or [sexually](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sexual_reproduction "Sexual reproduction") from two parent organisms.

### Physics

From a [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics") perspective, an organism is a [thermodynamic system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamic_system "Thermodynamic system") with an organised molecular structure that can reproduce itself and evolve as survival dictates.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-Luttermoser-1-25)[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-Luttermoser-2-26) Thermodynamically, life has been described as an open system which makes use of gradients in its surroundings to create imperfect copies of itself.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-Review_2009-27) Another way of putting this is to define life as "a self-sustained chemical system capable of undergoing [Darwinian evolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Darwinian_evolution "Darwinian evolution")", a definition adopted by a [NASA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NASA "NASA") committee attempting to define life for the purposes of [exobiology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exobiology "Exobiology"), based on a suggestion by [Carl Sagan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Sagan "Carl Sagan").[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-28)[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-29) This definition, however, has been widely criticised because according to it, a single sexually reproducing individual is not alive as it is incapable of evolving on its own.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-30)

### Living systems

Others take a [living systems theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Living_systems_theory "Living systems theory") viewpoint that does not necessarily depend on molecular chemistry. One systemic definition of life is that living things are [self-organizing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-organizing "Self-organizing") and [autopoietic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autopoietic "Autopoietic") (self-producing). Variations of this include [Stuart Kauffman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stuart_Kauffman "Stuart Kauffman")'s definition as an [autonomous agent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autonomous_agent "Autonomous agent") or a [multi-agent system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multi-agent_system "Multi-agent system") capable of reproducing itself, and of completing at least one [thermodynamic work cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamic_cycle "Thermodynamic cycle").[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-31) This definition is extended by the evolution of novel functions over time.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-32) Living systems are characterized by a multiscale, [hierarchical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hierarchy "Hierarchy") organization, spanning from molecular machines to cells, organs, tissues, organisms, populations, ecosystems, up to the whole biosphere.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-33)

### Death

[![Image 3](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Male_Lion_and_Cub_Chitwa_South_Africa_Luca_Galuzzi_2004.JPG/250px-Male_Lion_and_Cub_Chitwa_South_Africa_Luca_Galuzzi_2004.JPG)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Male_Lion_and_Cub_Chitwa_South_Africa_Luca_Galuzzi_2004.JPG)

Animal corpses, like this [African buffalo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/African_buffalo "African buffalo"), are recycled by the [ecosystem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem "Ecosystem"), providing energy and nutrients for living organisms.

Death is the termination of all vital functions or life processes in an organism or cell.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-34)[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-define_death-35) One of the challenges in defining death is in distinguishing it from life. Death would seem to refer to either the moment life ends, or when the state that follows life begins.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-define_death-35) However, determining when death has occurred is difficult, as cessation of life functions is often not simultaneous across organ systems.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-36) Such determination, therefore, requires drawing conceptual lines between life and death. This is problematic because there is little consensus over how to define life. The nature of death has for millennia been a central concern of the world's religious traditions and of philosophical inquiry. Many religions maintain faith in either a kind of [afterlife](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Afterlife "Afterlife") or [reincarnation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reincarnation "Reincarnation") for the [soul](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soul "Soul"), or [resurrection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resurrection "Resurrection") of the body at a later date.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-37)

### Viruses

[![Image 4](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Adenovirus_transmission_electron_micrograph_B82-0142_lores.jpg/250px-Adenovirus_transmission_electron_micrograph_B82-0142_lores.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Adenovirus_transmission_electron_micrograph_B82-0142_lores.jpg)

[Adenoviruses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adenovirus "Adenovirus") as seen under an electron microscope

Whether or not viruses should be considered as alive is controversial.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-38)[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-39) They are most often considered as just [gene coding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gene_coding "Gene coding")[replicators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNA_replication "DNA replication") rather than forms of life.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-40) They have been described as "organisms at the edge of life"[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-41) because they possess [genes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gene "Gene"), evolve by natural selection,[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-pmid17914905-42)[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-Forterre_2010-43) and replicate by making multiple copies of themselves through self-assembly. However, viruses do not metabolise and they require a host cell to make new products. Virus self-assembly within host cells has implications for the study of the [origin of life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Origin_of_life "Origin of life"), as it may support the hypothesis that life could have started as self-assembling [organic molecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_molecules "Organic molecules").[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-pmid16984643-44)[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-45)

History of study
----------------

### Materialism

Some of the earliest theories of life were materialist, holding that all that exists is matter, and that life is merely a complex form or arrangement of matter. [Empedocles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empedocles "Empedocles") (430 BC) argued that everything in the universe is made up of a combination of [four eternal "elements"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_element "Classical element") or "roots of all": earth, water, air, and fire. All change is explained by the arrangement and rearrangement of these four elements. The various forms of life are caused by an appropriate mixture of elements.[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-46)[Democritus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democritus "Democritus") (460 BC) was an [atomist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomist "Atomist"); he thought that the essential characteristic of life was having a [soul](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soul "Soul") (_psyche_), and that the soul, like everything else, was composed of fiery atoms. He elaborated on fire because of the apparent connection between life and heat, and because fire moves.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-democritus-47)[Plato](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plato "Plato"), in contrast, held that the world was organised by permanent [forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theory_of_Forms "Theory of Forms"), reflected imperfectly in matter; forms provided direction or intelligence, explaining the regularities observed in the world.[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-48)

The [mechanistic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanism_(philosophy) "Mechanism (philosophy)") materialism that originated in [ancient Greece](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greece "Ancient Greece") was revived and revised by the French philosopher [René Descartes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Descartes "René Descartes") (1596–1650), who held that animals and humans were assemblages of parts that together functioned as a machine. [Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gottfried_Wilhelm_Leibniz "Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz") emphasised the [hierarchical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hierarchical_organization "Hierarchical organization") organization of living machines, noting in his book _[Monadology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monadology "Monadology")_ (1714) that "...the machines of nature, that is living bodies, are still machines in their smallest parts, to infinity."[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-49) This idea was developed further by [Julien Offray de La Mettrie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Julien_Offray_de_La_Mettrie "Julien Offray de La Mettrie") (1709–1750) in his book _L'Homme Machine_.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-50) In the 19th century the advances in [cell theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_theory "Cell theory") in biological science encouraged this view. The [evolutionary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evolution "Evolution") theory of [Charles Darwin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Darwin "Charles Darwin") (1859) is a mechanistic explanation for the origin of species by means of [natural selection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_selection "Natural selection").[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-51) At the beginning of the 20th century [Stéphane Leduc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/St%C3%A9phane_Leduc "Stéphane Leduc") (1853–1939) promoted the idea that biological processes could be understood in terms of physics and chemistry, and that their growth resembled that of inorganic crystals immersed in solutions of sodium silicate. His ideas, set out in his book _La biologie synthétique_,[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-52) were widely dismissed during his lifetime, but has incurred a resurgence of interest in the work of Russell, Barge and colleagues.[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-53)

### Hylomorphism

[![Image 5](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Aristotelian_Soul.png/500px-Aristotelian_Soul.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Aristotelian_Soul.png)

The [structure of the souls](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soul#Aristotle "Soul") of plants, animals, and humans, according to [Aristotle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotle "Aristotle")

Hylomorphism is a theory first expressed by the Greek philosopher [Aristotle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotle "Aristotle") (322 BC). The application of hylomorphism to biology was important to Aristotle, and [biology is extensively covered in his extant writings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotle%27s_biology "Aristotle's biology"). In this view, everything in the material universe has both matter and form, and the form of a living thing is its [soul](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soul "Soul") (Greek _psyche_, Latin _anima_). There are three kinds of souls: the _vegetative soul_ of plants, which causes them to grow and decay and nourish themselves, but does not cause motion and sensation; the _animal soul_, which causes animals to move and feel; and the _rational soul_, which is the source of consciousness and reasoning, which (Aristotle believed) is found only in man.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-54) Each higher soul has all of the attributes of the lower ones. Aristotle believed that while matter can exist without form, form cannot exist without matter, and that therefore the soul cannot exist without the body.[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-55)

This account is consistent with [teleological explanations of life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teleology_in_biology "Teleology in biology"), which account for phenomena in terms of purpose or goal-directedness. Thus, the whiteness of the polar bear's coat is explained by its purpose of camouflage. The direction of causality (from the future to the past) is in contradiction with the scientific evidence for natural selection, which explains the consequence in terms of a prior cause. Biological features are explained not by looking at future optimal results, but by looking at the past [evolutionary history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evolutionary_history "Evolutionary history") of a species, which led to the natural selection of the features in question.[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-stewert_williams2010-56)

### Spontaneous generation

Spontaneous generation was the belief that living organisms can form without descent from similar organisms. Typically, the idea was that certain forms such as fleas could arise from inanimate matter such as dust or the supposed seasonal generation of mice and insects from mud or garbage.[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-57)

The theory of spontaneous generation was proposed by [Aristotle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotle "Aristotle"),[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-58) who compiled and expanded the work of prior natural philosophers and the various ancient explanations of the appearance of organisms; it was considered the best explanation for two millennia. It was decisively dispelled by the experiments of [Louis Pasteur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis_Pasteur "Louis Pasteur") in 1859, who expanded upon the investigations of predecessors such as [Francesco Redi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Francesco_Redi "Francesco Redi").[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-59)[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-60) Disproof of the traditional ideas of spontaneous generation is no longer controversial among biologists.[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-Bernal_1967-61)[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-62)[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-Szathmary-63)

### Vitalism

Vitalism is the belief that there is a non-material life-principle. This originated with [Georg Ernst Stahl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georg_Ernst_Stahl "Georg Ernst Stahl") (17th century), and remained popular until the middle of the 19th century. It appealed to philosophers such as [Henri Bergson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Bergson "Henri Bergson"), [Friedrich Nietzsche](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Nietzsche "Friedrich Nietzsche"), and [Wilhelm Dilthey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilhelm_Dilthey "Wilhelm Dilthey"),[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-64) anatomists like [Xavier Bichat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xavier_Bichat "Xavier Bichat"), and chemists like [Justus von Liebig](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Justus_von_Liebig "Justus von Liebig").[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-Wilkinson-65) Vitalism included the idea that there was a fundamental difference between organic and inorganic material, and the belief that [organic material](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_material "Organic material") can only be derived from living things. This was disproved in 1828, when [Friedrich Wöhler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_W%C3%B6hler "Friedrich Wöhler") prepared [urea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urea "Urea") from inorganic materials.[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-66) This [Wöhler synthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/W%C3%B6hler_synthesis "Wöhler synthesis") is considered the starting point of modern [organic chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_chemistry "Organic chemistry"). It is of historical significance because for the first time an [organic compound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_compound "Organic compound") was produced in [inorganic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inorganic "Inorganic") reactions.[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-Wilkinson-65)

During the 1850s [Hermann von Helmholtz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_von_Helmholtz "Hermann von Helmholtz"), anticipated by [Julius Robert von Mayer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Julius_Robert_von_Mayer "Julius Robert von Mayer"), demonstrated that no energy is lost in muscle movement, suggesting that there were no "vital forces" necessary to move a muscle.[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-67) These results led to the abandonment of scientific interest in vitalistic theories, especially after [Eduard Buchner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eduard_Buchner "Eduard Buchner")'s demonstration that alcoholic fermentation could occur in cell-free extracts of yeast.[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-68) Nonetheless, belief still exists in [pseudoscientific](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudoscientific "Pseudoscientific") theories such as [homoeopathy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homoeopathy "Homoeopathy"), which interprets diseases and sickness as caused by disturbances in a hypothetical vital force or life force.[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-69)

Development
-----------

### Origin of life

The [age of Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Age_of_Earth "Age of Earth") is about 4.54 [billion years](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bya "Bya").[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-70) Life on Earth has existed for at least 3.5 billion years,[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-PNAS-20151014-pdf-71)[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-72)[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-RavenJohnson2002-73)[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-74) with the oldest physical [traces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trace_fossil "Trace fossil") of life dating back 3.7 billion years.[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-NG-20131208-75)[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-AST-20131108-76) Estimates from molecular clocks, as summarised in the [TimeTree](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TimeTree "TimeTree") public database, place the origin of life around 4.0 billion years ago.[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-77) Hypotheses on the origin of life attempt to explain the formation of a [universal common ancestor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universal_common_ancestor "Universal common ancestor") from simple [organic molecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_molecule "Organic molecule") via pre-cellular life to [protocells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protocell "Protocell") and metabolism.[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-78) In 2016, a set of 355 [genes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gene "Gene") from the [last universal common ancestor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Last_universal_common_ancestor "Last universal common ancestor") was tentatively identified.[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-NYT-20160725-79)

The biosphere is postulated to have developed, from the origin of life onwards, at least some 3.5 billion years ago.[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-Campbell_2006-80) The earliest evidence for life on Earth includes [biogenic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biogenic "Biogenic")[graphite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graphite "Graphite") found in 3.7 billion-year-old [metasedimentary rocks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metasediment "Metasediment") from [Western Greenland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_Greenland "Western Greenland")[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-NG-20131208-75) and [microbial mat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microbial_mat "Microbial mat")[fossils](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fossils "Fossils") found in 3.48 billion-year-old [sandstone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sandstone "Sandstone") from [Western Australia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_Australia "Western Australia").[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-AST-20131108-76) More recently, in 2015, "remains of [biotic life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biotic_life "Biotic life")" were found in 4.1 billion-year-old rocks in Western Australia.[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-PNAS-20151014-pdf-71) In 2017, putative fossilised [microorganisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism "Microorganism") (or [microfossils](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Micropaleontology#Microfossils "Micropaleontology")) were announced to have been discovered in [hydrothermal vent precipitates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrothermal_vent "Hydrothermal vent") in the [Nuvvuagittuq Belt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuvvuagittuq_Belt "Nuvvuagittuq Belt") of Quebec, Canada that were as old as 4.28 billion years, the oldest record of life on Earth, suggesting "an almost instantaneous emergence of life" after [ocean formation 4.4 billion years ago](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Origin_of_water_on_Earth#History_of_water_on_Earth "Origin of water on Earth"), and not long after the [formation of the Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Age_of_the_Earth "Age of the Earth") 4.54 billion years ago.[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-NAT-20170301-81)

### Evolution

[Evolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evolution "Evolution") is the change in [heritable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heritable "Heritable")[characteristics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phenotypic_trait "Phenotypic trait") of biological populations over successive generations. It results in the appearance of new species and often the disappearance of old ones.[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-82)[[82]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-83) Evolution occurs when evolutionary processes such as [natural selection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_selection "Natural selection") (including [sexual selection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sexual_selection "Sexual selection")) and [genetic drift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genetic_drift "Genetic drift") act on genetic variation, resulting in certain characteristics increasing or decreasing in frequency within a population over successive generations.[[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-Scott-Phillips-84) The process of evolution has given rise to [biodiversity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity "Biodiversity") at every level of [biological organisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biological_organisation "Biological organisation").[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-85)[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-Voet2016a-86)

### Fossils

Fossils are the preserved remains or [traces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trace_fossil "Trace fossil") of organisms from the remote past. The totality of fossils, both discovered and undiscovered, and their placement in layers ([strata](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strata "Strata")) of [sedimentary rock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sedimentary_rock "Sedimentary rock") is known as the _fossil record_. A preserved specimen is called a fossil if it is older than the arbitrary date of 10,000 years ago.[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-87) Hence, fossils range in age from the youngest at the start of the [Holocene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holocene "Holocene") Epoch to the oldest from the [Archaean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archean "Archean") Eon, up to 3.4 [billion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1000000000_(number) "1000000000 (number)") years old.[[87]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-88)[[88]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-89)

### Extinction

Extinction is the process by which a [species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Species "Species") dies out.[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-90) The moment of extinction is the death of the last individual of that species. Because a species' potential [range](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Range_(biology) "Range (biology)") may be very large, determining this moment is difficult, and is usually done retrospectively after a period of apparent absence. Species become extinct when they are no longer able to survive in changing [habitat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habitat "Habitat") or against superior competition. Over 99% of all the species that have ever lived are now extinct.[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-91)[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-Book-Biology-92)[[92]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-StearnsStearns2000-93)[[93]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-NYT-20141108-MJN-94)[Mass extinctions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass_extinction "Mass extinction") may have accelerated evolution by providing opportunities for new groups of organisms to diversify.[[94]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-95)

Environmental conditions
------------------------

[![Image 6](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/20100422_235222_Cyanobacteria.jpg/250px-20100422_235222_Cyanobacteria.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:20100422_235222_Cyanobacteria.jpg)

[Cyanobacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyanobacteria "Cyanobacteria")[dramatically changed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen_catastrophe "Oxygen catastrophe") the composition of life forms on Earth by leading to the near-extinction of [oxygen-intolerant organisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anaerobic_organism "Anaerobic organism").

The diversity of life on Earth is a result of the dynamic interplay between [genetic opportunity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genetic_opportunity "Genetic opportunity"), metabolic capability, [environmental](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Environment_(biophysical) "Environment (biophysical)") challenges,[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-astrobiology-96) and [symbiosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symbiosis "Symbiosis").[[96]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-97)[[97]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-98)[[98]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-99) For most of its existence, Earth's habitable environment has been dominated by [microorganisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism "Microorganism") and subjected to their metabolism and evolution. As a consequence of these microbial activities, the physical-chemical environment on Earth has been changing on a [geologic time scale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geologic_time_scale "Geologic time scale"), thereby affecting the path of evolution of subsequent life.[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-astrobiology-96) For example, the release of molecular [oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen "Oxygen") by [cyanobacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyanobacteria "Cyanobacteria") as a by-product of [photosynthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photosynthesis "Photosynthesis") induced global changes in the Earth's environment. Because oxygen was toxic to most life on Earth at the time, this posed novel evolutionary challenges, and ultimately resulted in the formation of Earth's major animal and plant species. This interplay between organisms and their environment is an inherent feature of living systems.[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-astrobiology-96)

### Biosphere

[![Image 7](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Deinococcus_geothermalis_cells.jpg/250px-Deinococcus_geothermalis_cells.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Deinococcus_geothermalis_cells.jpg)

_[Deinococcus geothermalis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deinococcus\_geothermalis "Deinococcus geothermalis")_, a bacterium that thrives in [geothermal springs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geothermal_springs "Geothermal springs") and deep ocean subsurfaces[[99]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-100)

The [biosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere "Biosphere") is the global sum of all ecosystems. It can also be termed as the zone of life on Earth, a closed system (apart from solar and cosmic radiation and heat from the interior of the Earth), and largely self-regulating.[[100]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-101) Organisms exist in every part of the biosphere, including [soil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soil "Soil"), [hot springs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hot_spring "Hot spring"), [inside rocks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endolith "Endolith") at least 19 km (12 mi) deep underground, the deepest parts of the ocean, and at least 64 km (40 mi) high in the atmosphere.[[101]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-SD-19980625-UG-102)[[102]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-ABM-20150112-103)[[103]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-BBC-20151124-104) For example, spores of _[Aspergillus niger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aspergillus\_niger "Aspergillus niger")_ have been detected in the [mesosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mesosphere "Mesosphere") at an altitude of 48 to 77 km.[[104]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-105) Under test conditions, life forms have been observed to survive in the vacuum of space.[[105]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-Dose-106)[[106]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-Horneck-107) Life forms thrive in the deep [Mariana Trench](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mariana_Trench "Mariana Trench"),[[107]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-NG-20130317-108) and inside rocks up to 580 m (1,900 ft; 0.36 mi) below the sea floor under 2,590 m (8,500 ft; 1.61 mi) of ocean off the coast of the northwestern United States,[[108]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-LS-20130317-109)[[109]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-LS-20130314-110) and 2,400 m (7,900 ft; 1.5 mi) beneath the seabed off Japan.[[110]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-BBC-20141215-RM-111) In 2014, life forms were found living 800 m (2,600 ft; 0.50 mi) below the ice of Antarctica.[[111]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-NAT-20140820-112)[[112]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-FRB-20140820-113) Expeditions of the [International Ocean Discovery Program](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Ocean_Discovery_Program "International Ocean Discovery Program") found [unicellular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unicellular "Unicellular") life in 120°C sediment 1.2 km below seafloor in the [Nankai Trough](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nankai_Trough "Nankai Trough")[subduction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subduction "Subduction") zone.[[113]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-114) According to one researcher, "You can find [microbes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microbe "Microbe") everywhere—they're extremely adaptable to conditions, and survive wherever they are."[[108]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-LS-20130317-109)

### Range of tolerance

The inert components of an ecosystem are the physical and chemical factors necessary for life—energy (sunlight or [chemical energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biochemistry "Biochemistry")), water, heat, [atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere "Earth's atmosphere"), [gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_biology "Gravitational biology"), [nutrients](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nutrient "Nutrient"), and [ultraviolet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultraviolet "Ultraviolet")[solar radiation protection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ozone_layer "Ozone layer").[[114]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-115) In most ecosystems, the conditions vary during the day and from one season to the next. To survive in these ecosystems, organisms must be able to tolerate a range of conditions defined as the "range of tolerance".[[115]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-tolerance-116) Outside this range are the "zones of physiological stress", where the survival and reproduction are possible but not optimal. Beyond these zones are the "zones of intolerance", where survival and reproduction of that organism is unlikely or impossible. Organisms that have a wide range of tolerance are more widely distributed than organisms with a narrow range of tolerance.[[115]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-tolerance-116)

### Extremophiles

[![Image 8](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Deinococcus_radiodurans.jpg/250px-Deinococcus_radiodurans.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Deinococcus_radiodurans.jpg)

_[Deinococcus radiodurans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deinococcus\_radiodurans "Deinococcus radiodurans")_ is an [extremophile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extremophile "Extremophile") that can resist extremes of cold, dehydration, vacuum, acid, and radiation exposure.

To survive, some microorganisms have evolved to withstand [freezing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Psychrophile "Psychrophile"), [complete desiccation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xerophile "Xerophile"), [starvation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oligotroph "Oligotroph"), high levels of [radiation exposure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radioresistance "Radioresistance"), and other physical or chemical challenges. These [extremophile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extremophile "Extremophile") microorganisms may survive exposure to such conditions for long periods.[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-astrobiology-96)[[116]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-NYT-20160912-117) They excel at exploiting uncommon sources of energy. Characterization of the [structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morphology_(biology) "Morphology (biology)") and metabolic diversity of microbial communities in such [extreme environments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extreme_environment "Extreme environment") is ongoing.[[117]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-118)

Classification
--------------

### Antiquity

The first classification of organisms was made by the Greek philosopher Aristotle (384–322 BC), who grouped living things as either plants or animals, based mainly on their ability to move. He distinguished animals with blood from animals without blood, which can be compared with the concepts of [vertebrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertebrate "Vertebrate") and [invertebrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invertebrate "Invertebrate") respectively, and divided the blooded animals into five groups: viviparous quadrupeds ([mammals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mammal "Mammal")), oviparous quadrupeds (reptiles and [amphibians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amphibian "Amphibian")), birds, fishes and [whales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cetacea "Cetacea"). The bloodless animals were divided into five groups: [cephalopods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cephalopod "Cephalopod"), [crustaceans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crustacean "Crustacean"), insects (which included the spiders, [scorpions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scorpion "Scorpion"), and [centipedes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centipede "Centipede")), shelled animals (such as most [molluscs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mollusc "Mollusc") and [echinoderms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Echinoderm "Echinoderm")), and "[zoophytes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zoophyte "Zoophyte")" (animals that resemble plants). This theory remained dominant for more than a thousand years.[[118]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-119)

### Linnaean

In the late 1740s, [Carl Linnaeus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus "Carl Linnaeus") introduced his system of [binomial nomenclature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature "Binomial nomenclature") for the classification of species. Linnaeus attempted to improve the composition and reduce the length of the previously used many-worded names by abolishing unnecessary rhetoric, introducing new descriptive terms and precisely defining their meaning.[[119]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-120)

The fungi were originally treated as plants. For a short period Linnaeus had classified them in the taxon [Vermes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vermes "Vermes") in Animalia, but later placed them back in Plantae. [Herbert Copeland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herbert_Copeland_(biologist) "Herbert Copeland (biologist)") classified the Fungi in his [Protoctista](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protoctista "Protoctista"), including them with single-celled organisms and thus partially avoiding the problem but acknowledging their special status.[[120]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-121) The problem was eventually solved by [Whittaker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Whittaker_(ecologist) "Robert Whittaker (ecologist)"), when he gave them their own [kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_(biology) "Kingdom (biology)") in his [five-kingdom system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Five-kingdom_system "Five-kingdom system"). [Evolutionary history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evolutionary_history "Evolutionary history") shows that the fungi are more closely related to animals than to plants.[[121]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-122)

As advances in [microscopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microscopy "Microscopy") enabled detailed study of [cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_(biology) "Cell (biology)") and microorganisms, new groups of life were revealed, and the fields of [cell biology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_biology "Cell biology") and [microbiology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microbiology "Microbiology") were created. These new organisms were originally described separately in [protozoa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protozoa "Protozoa") as animals and [protophyta/thallophyta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thallophyte "Thallophyte") as plants, but were united by [Ernst Haeckel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ernst_Haeckel "Ernst Haeckel") in the kingdom [Protista](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protista "Protista"); later, the [prokaryotes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prokaryote "Prokaryote") were split off in the kingdom [Monera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monera "Monera"), which would eventually be divided into two separate groups, the Bacteria and the [Archaea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archaea "Archaea"). This led to the [six-kingdom system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Six-kingdom_system "Six-kingdom system") and eventually to the current [three-domain system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-domain_system "Three-domain system"), which is based on evolutionary relationships.[[122]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-Woese1990-123) However, the classification of eukaryotes, especially of protists, is still controversial.[[123]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-Adl_05-124)

As microbiology developed, viruses, which are non-cellular, were discovered. Whether these are considered alive has been a matter of debate; viruses lack characteristics of life such as cell membranes, metabolism and the ability to grow or respond to their environments. Viruses have been classed into "species" based on their [genetics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genetics "Genetics"), but many aspects of such a classification remain controversial.[[124]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-125)

The original Linnaean system has been modified many times, for example as follows:

### Metagenomic

The ability to sequence large numbers of complete [genomes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genome "Genome") has allowed biologists to take a [metagenomic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metagenomic "Metagenomic") view of the [phylogeny](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phylogeny "Phylogeny") of the whole [tree of life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tree_of_life_(biology) "Tree of life (biology)"). This has led to the realisation that the majority of living things are bacteria, and that all have a common origin.[[122]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-Woese1990-123)[[134]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-NM-20160411-135)

Composition
-----------

### Chemical elements

All life forms require certain core [chemical elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_element "Chemical element") for their [biochemical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biochemical "Biochemical") functioning. These include [carbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon "Carbon"), [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen"), [nitrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen "Nitrogen"), [oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen "Oxygen"), [phosphorus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphorus "Phosphorus"), and [sulfur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur "Sulfur")—the elemental [macronutrients](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nutrient "Nutrient") for all organisms.[[135]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-wsj20101203-136) Together these make up [nucleic acids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleic_acid "Nucleic acid"), proteins and [lipids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lipid "Lipid"), the bulk of living matter. Five of these six elements comprise the chemical components of DNA, the exception being sulfur. The latter is a component of the amino acids [cysteine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cysteine "Cysteine") and [methionine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methionine "Methionine"). The most abundant of these elements in organisms is carbon, which has the desirable attribute of forming multiple, stable [covalent bonds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covalent_bond "Covalent bond"). This allows carbon-based (organic) molecules to form the immense variety of chemical arrangements described in [organic chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_chemistry "Organic chemistry").[[136]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-Lipkus_Yuan_Lucas_Funk_2008_pp._4443%E2%80%934451-137) Alternative [hypothetical types of biochemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypothetical_types_of_biochemistry "Hypothetical types of biochemistry") have been proposed that eliminate one or more of these elements, swap out an element for one not on the list, or change required [chiralities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chirality_(chemistry) "Chirality (chemistry)") or other chemical properties.[[137]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-138)[[138]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-139)

### DNA

Main article: [DNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNA "DNA")

Deoxyribonucleic acid or [DNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNA "DNA") is a [molecule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecule "Molecule") that carries most of the [genetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genetics "Genetics") instructions used in the growth, development, functioning and [reproduction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reproduction "Reproduction") of all known living [organisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism "Organism") and many viruses. DNA and [RNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RNA "RNA") are [nucleic acids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleic_acid "Nucleic acid"); alongside [proteins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protein "Protein") and [complex carbohydrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polysaccharide "Polysaccharide"), they are one of the three major types of [macromolecule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macromolecule "Macromolecule") that are essential for all known forms of life. Most DNA molecules consist of two [biopolymer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biopolymer "Biopolymer") strands coiled around each other to form a [double helix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double_helix "Double helix"). The two DNA strands are known as [polynucleotides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynucleotide "Polynucleotide") since they are composed of [simpler units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monomer "Monomer") called [nucleotides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleotide "Nucleotide").[[139]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-140) Each nucleotide is composed of a [nitrogen-containing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogenous_base "Nitrogenous base")[nucleobase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleobase "Nucleobase")—either [cytosine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cytosine "Cytosine") (C), [guanine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guanine "Guanine") (G), [adenine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adenine "Adenine") (A), or [thymine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thymine "Thymine") (T)—as well as a [sugar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monosaccharide "Monosaccharide") called [deoxyribose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deoxyribose "Deoxyribose") and a [phosphate group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphate_group "Phosphate group").

The nucleotides are joined to one another in a chain by [covalent bonds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covalent_bond "Covalent bond") between the sugar of one nucleotide and the phosphate of the next, resulting in an alternating [sugar-phosphate backbone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Backbone_chain "Backbone chain"). According to [base pairing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Base_pair "Base pair") rules (A with T, and C with G), [hydrogen bonds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_bond "Hydrogen bond") bind the nitrogenous bases of the two separate polynucleotide strands to make double-stranded DNA. This has the key property that each strand contains all the information needed to recreate the other strand, enabling the information to be preserved during reproduction and cell division.[[140]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-NYT-20150718-rn-141) Within cells, DNA is organised into long structures called [chromosomes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chromosome "Chromosome"). During [cell division](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_division "Cell division") these chromosomes are duplicated in the process of [DNA replication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNA_replication "DNA replication"), providing each cell its own complete set of chromosomes. Eukaryotes store most of their DNA inside the [cell nucleus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_nucleus "Cell nucleus").[[141]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-142)

### Cells

Cells are the basic unit of structure in every living thing, and all cells arise from pre-existing cells by [division](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_division "Cell division").[[142]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-143)[[143]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-144)[Cell theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_theory "Cell theory") was formulated by [Henri Dutrochet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Dutrochet "Henri Dutrochet"), [Theodor Schwann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theodor_Schwann "Theodor Schwann"), [Rudolf Virchow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rudolf_Virchow "Rudolf Virchow") and others during the early nineteenth century, and subsequently became widely accepted.[[144]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-sapp2003-145) The activity of an organism depends on the total activity of its cells, with [energy flow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cellular_respiration "Cellular respiration") occurring within and between them. Cells contain hereditary information that is carried forward as a [genetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genetics "Genetics") code during cell division.[[145]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-146)

There are two primary types of cells, reflecting their evolutionary origins. [Prokaryote](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prokaryote "Prokaryote") cells lack a [nucleus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_nucleus "Cell nucleus") and other membrane-bound [organelles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organelle "Organelle"), although they have circular DNA and [ribosomes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ribosome "Ribosome"). Bacteria and [Archaea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archaea "Archaea") are two [domains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_(biology) "Domain (biology)") of prokaryotes. The other primary type is the [eukaryote](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eukaryote "Eukaryote") cell, which has a distinct nucleus bound by a [nuclear membrane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_membrane "Nuclear membrane") and membrane-bound organelles, including [mitochondria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mitochondria "Mitochondria"), [chloroplasts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chloroplasts "Chloroplasts"), [lysosomes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lysosomes "Lysosomes"), rough and smooth [endoplasmic reticulum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endoplasmic_reticulum "Endoplasmic reticulum"), and [vacuoles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vacuoles "Vacuoles"). In addition, their DNA is organised into [chromosomes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chromosome "Chromosome"). All species of large complex organisms are eukaryotes, including animals, plants and fungi, though with a wide diversity of [protist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protist "Protist")[microorganisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism "Microorganism").[[146]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-147) The conventional model is that eukaryotes evolved from prokaryotes, with the main organelles of the eukaryotes forming through [endosymbiosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endosymbiosis "Endosymbiosis") between bacteria and the progenitor eukaryotic cell.[[147]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-148)

The molecular mechanisms of [cell biology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_biology "Cell biology") are based on [proteins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protein "Protein"). Most of these are synthesised by the ribosomes through an [enzyme-catalyzed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enzyme_catalysis "Enzyme catalysis") process called [protein biosynthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protein_biosynthesis "Protein biosynthesis"). A sequence of amino acids is assembled and joined based upon [gene expression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gene_expression "Gene expression") of the cell's nucleic acid.[[148]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-149) In eukaryotic cells, these proteins may then be transported and processed through the [Golgi apparatus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golgi_apparatus "Golgi apparatus") in preparation for dispatch to their destination.[[149]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-pmid20605430-150)

Cells reproduce through a process of [cell division](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_division "Cell division") in which the parent cell divides into two or more daughter cells. For prokaryotes, cell division occurs through a process of [fission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fission_(biology) "Fission (biology)") in which the DNA is replicated, then the two copies are attached to parts of the cell membrane. In [eukaryotes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eukaryote "Eukaryote"), a more complex process of [mitosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mitosis "Mitosis") is followed. However, the result is the same; the resulting cell copies are identical to each other and to the original cell (except for [mutations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mutations "Mutations")), and both are capable of further division following an [interphase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interphase "Interphase") period.[[150]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-151) Most species of multicellular [plants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant "Plant"), [animals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Animal "Animal") and [fungi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fungus "Fungus") as well as many [protists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protist "Protist") are capable of [sexual reproduction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sexual_reproduction "Sexual reproduction"). Sexual reproduction, involving a [meiotic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meiosis "Meiosis") process, is considered to have arisen very early in the evolution of [eukaryotes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eukaryote "Eukaryote").[[151]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-152)[[152]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-153)

### Multicellular structure

[Multicellular organisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multicellular_organism "Multicellular organism") may have first evolved through the formation of [colonies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colony_(biology) "Colony (biology)") of identical cells. These cells can form group organisms through [cell adhesion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_adhesion "Cell adhesion"). The individual members of a colony are capable of surviving on their own, whereas the members of a true multi-cellular organism have developed specialisations, making them dependent on the remainder of the organism for survival. Such organisms are formed [clonally](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clone_(cell_biology) "Clone (cell biology)") or from a single [germ cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germ_cell "Germ cell") that is capable of forming the various specialised cells that form the adult organism. This specialisation allows multicellular organisms to exploit resources more efficiently than single cells.[[153]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-154) About 800 million years ago, a minor genetic change in a single molecule, the [enzyme](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enzyme "Enzyme")[GK-PID](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GK-PID "GK-PID"), may have allowed organisms to go from a single cell organism to one of many cells.[[154]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-NYT-20160107-155)

Cells have evolved methods to perceive and respond to their microenvironment, thereby enhancing their adaptability. [Cell signaling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_signaling "Cell signaling") coordinates cellular activities, and hence governs the basic functions of multicellular organisms. Signaling between cells can occur through direct cell contact using [juxtacrine signalling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Juxtacrine_signalling "Juxtacrine signalling"), or indirectly through the exchange of agents as in the [endocrine system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endocrine_system "Endocrine system"). In more complex organisms, coordination of activities can occur through a dedicated [nervous system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nervous_system "Nervous system").[[155]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-alberts2002-156)

In the universe
---------------

Though life is confirmed only on Earth, many think that [extraterrestrial life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extraterrestrial_life "Extraterrestrial life") is not only plausible, but probable or inevitable,[[156]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-RaceRandolph2002-157)[[157]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-158) possibly resulting in a [biophysical cosmology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biophysical_cosmology "Biophysical cosmology") instead of a mere [physical cosmology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_cosmology "Physical cosmology").[[158]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-v237-159) Other planets and [moons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons "Moons") in the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System") and other [planetary systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_system "Planetary system") are being examined for evidence of having once supported simple life, and projects such as [SETI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SETI "SETI") are trying to detect radio transmissions from possible alien civilisations. Other locations within the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System") that may host [microbial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microbial "Microbial") life include the subsurface of [Mars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life_on_Mars_(planet) "Life on Mars (planet)"), the upper atmosphere of [Venus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life_on_Venus "Life on Venus"),[[159]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-160) and subsurface oceans on some of the [moons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_satellite_habitability "Natural satellite habitability") of the [giant planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giant_planet "Giant planet").[[160]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-WRD-20150127-161)[[161]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-162)

Investigation of the tenacity and versatility of life on Earth,[[116]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-NYT-20160912-117) as well as an understanding of the molecular systems that some organisms utilise to survive such extremes, is important for the search for extraterrestrial life.[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-astrobiology-96) For example, [lichen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lichen "Lichen") could survive for a month in a [simulated Martian environment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life_on_Earth_under_Martian_conditions "Life on Earth under Martian conditions").[[162]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-Skymania-20120426-163)[[163]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-EGU-20120426-164)

Beyond the Solar System, the region around another [main-sequence star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Main-sequence_star "Main-sequence star") that could support Earth-like life on an Earth-like planet is known as the [habitable zone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habitable_zone "Habitable zone"). The inner and outer radii of this zone vary with the luminosity of the star, as does the time interval during which the zone survives. Stars more massive than the Sun have a larger habitable zone, but remain on the Sun-like "main sequence" of [stellar evolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_evolution "Stellar evolution") for a shorter time interval. Small [red dwarfs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_dwarf "Red dwarf") have the opposite problem, with a smaller habitable zone that is subject to higher levels of magnetic activity and the effects of [tidal locking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tidal_locking "Tidal locking") from close orbits. Hence, stars in the intermediate mass range such as the Sun may have a greater likelihood for Earth-like life to develop.[[164]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-selis2006-165)

The location of the star within a galaxy may also affect the likelihood of life forming. Stars in regions with a greater abundance of heavier elements that can form planets, in combination with a low rate of potentially [habitat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habitat "Habitat")-damaging [supernova](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supernova "Supernova") events, are predicted to have a higher probability of hosting planets with complex life.[[165]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-science303_5654_59-166) The variables of the [Drake equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drake_equation "Drake equation") are used to discuss the conditions in planetary systems where civilisation is most likely to exist, within wide bounds of uncertainty.[[166]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-vakoch_harrison2011-167) A "Confidence of Life Detection" scale (CoLD) for reporting evidence of life beyond Earth has been proposed.[[167]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-NAT-20211027-168)[[168]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-COS-20211030-169)

Artificial
----------

Artificial life is the [simulation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simulation "Simulation") of any aspect of life, as through computers, [robotics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robotics "Robotics"), or [biochemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biochemistry "Biochemistry").[[169]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-170)[Synthetic biology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synthetic_biology "Synthetic biology") is a new area of [biotechnology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biotechnology "Biotechnology") that combines science and [biological engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biological_engineering "Biological engineering"). The common goal is the design and construction of new biological functions and systems not found in nature. Synthetic biology includes the broad redefinition and expansion of biotechnology, with the ultimate goals of being able to design and build engineered biological systems that process information, manipulate chemicals, fabricate materials and structures, produce energy, provide food, and maintain and enhance human health and the environment.[[170]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#cite_note-171)
