A cylindrical [hairbrush](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hairbrush "Hairbrush") showing the intuition behind the term _fiber bundle_. This hairbrush is like a fiber bundle in which the base space is a cylinder and the fibers ([bristles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bristle "Bristle")) are line segments. The mapping ![Image 2: {\displaystyle \pi :E\to B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b3b6680121ed6dbd1f69c871093712b1de5bd4ac) would take a point on any bristle and map it to its root on the cylinder.

In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), and particularly [topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology "Topology"), a **fiber bundle** ([_Commonwealth English_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English_in_the_Commonwealth_of_Nations "English in the Commonwealth of Nations"): **fibre bundle**) is a [space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_(mathematics) "Space (mathematics)") that is _locally_ a [product space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Product_space "Product space"), but _globally_ may have a different [topological structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_structure "Topological structure"). Specifically, the similarity between a space ![Image 3: {\displaystyle E}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4232c9de2ee3eec0a9c0a19b15ab92daa6223f9b) and a product space ![Image 4: {\displaystyle B\times F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/05e0fcb90530d44152eea7eb002ee9b1278c7165) is defined using a [continuous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_function_(topology) "Continuous function (topology)")[surjective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surjective_function "Surjective function")[map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Map_(mathematics) "Map (mathematics)"), ![Image 5: {\displaystyle \pi :E\to B,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4858e5871a48062c0d5e3fe6b11b999f1b646459) that in small regions of ![Image 6: {\displaystyle E}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4232c9de2ee3eec0a9c0a19b15ab92daa6223f9b) behaves just like a projection from corresponding regions of ![Image 7: {\displaystyle B\times F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/05e0fcb90530d44152eea7eb002ee9b1278c7165) to ![Image 8: {\displaystyle B.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0eccf5bca7cdc1fa4439af2d31831db6bde00473) The map ![Image 9: {\displaystyle \pi ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47495546dd4b7607bbcb5658efe66abaf1955034) called the **[projection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projection_(mathematics) "Projection (mathematics)")** or [**submersion**](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Submersion_(mathematics) "Submersion (mathematics)") of the bundle, is regarded as part of the structure of the bundle. The space ![Image 10: {\displaystyle E}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4232c9de2ee3eec0a9c0a19b15ab92daa6223f9b) is known as the **total space** of the fiber bundle, ![Image 11: {\displaystyle B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47136aad860d145f75f3eed3022df827cee94d7a) as the **base space**, and ![Image 12: {\displaystyle F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/545fd099af8541605f7ee55f08225526be88ce57) the **fiber**.

In the _[trivial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triviality\_(mathematics) "Triviality (mathematics)")_ case, ![Image 13: {\displaystyle E}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4232c9de2ee3eec0a9c0a19b15ab92daa6223f9b) is just ![Image 14: {\displaystyle B\times F,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/11bba3d0eb03fd90cbd6c61a167fc0bac7ff2cbe) and the map ![Image 15: {\displaystyle \pi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9be4ba0bb8df3af72e90a0535fabcc17431e540a) is just the projection from the product space to the first factor. This is called a **trivial bundle**. Examples of non-trivial fiber bundles include the [Möbius strip](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M%C3%B6bius_strip "Möbius strip") and [Klein bottle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Klein_bottle "Klein bottle"), as well as nontrivial [covering spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covering_space "Covering space"). Fiber bundles, such as the [tangent bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_bundle "Tangent bundle") of a [manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manifold "Manifold") and other more general [vector bundles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_bundle "Vector bundle"), play an important role in [differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry") and [differential topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_topology "Differential topology"), as do [principal bundles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principal_bundle "Principal bundle").

Mappings between total spaces of fiber bundles that "commute" with the projection maps are known as [bundle maps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bundle_map "Bundle map"), and the [class](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Class_(set_theory) "Class (set theory)") of fiber bundles forms a [category](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category_theory "Category theory") with respect to such mappings. A bundle map from the base space itself (with the [identity mapping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Identity_mapping "Identity mapping") as projection) to ![Image 16: {\displaystyle E}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4232c9de2ee3eec0a9c0a19b15ab92daa6223f9b) is called a [section](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Section_(fiber_bundle) "Section (fiber bundle)") of ![Image 17: {\displaystyle E.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4a2566d01f104ef084ea424b8b35c2534f7f902b) Fiber bundles can be specialized in a number of ways, the most common of which is requiring that the [transition maps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transition_map "Transition map") between the local trivial patches lie in a certain [topological group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_group "Topological group"), known as the **structure group**, acting on the fiber ![Image 18: {\displaystyle F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/545fd099af8541605f7ee55f08225526be88ce57).

In [topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology "Topology"), the terms _**fiber**_ (German: _Faser_) and _**fiber space**_ (_gefaserter Raum_) appeared for the first time in a paper by [Herbert Seifert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herbert_Seifert "Herbert Seifert") in 1933,[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiber_bundle#cite_note-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiber_bundle#cite_note-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiber_bundle#cite_note-3) but his definitions are limited to a very special case. The main difference from the present day conception of a fiber space, however, was that for Seifert what is now called the **base space** (topological space) of a fiber (topological) space _E_ was not part of the structure, but derived from it as a quotient space of _E_. The first definition of **fiber space** was given by [Hassler Whitney](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hassler_Whitney "Hassler Whitney") in 1935[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiber_bundle#cite_note-4) under the name **sphere space**, but in 1940 Whitney changed the name to **sphere bundle**.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiber_bundle#cite_note-5)

The theory of fibered spaces, of which [vector bundles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_bundle "Vector bundle"), [principal bundles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principal_bundle "Principal bundle"), topological [fibrations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fibration "Fibration") and [fibered manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fibered_manifold "Fibered manifold") are a special case, is attributed to [Herbert Seifert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herbert_Seifert "Herbert Seifert"), [Heinz Hopf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heinz_Hopf "Heinz Hopf"), [Jacques Feldbau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacques_Feldbau "Jacques Feldbau"),[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiber_bundle#cite_note-6) Whitney, [Norman Steenrod](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norman_Steenrod "Norman Steenrod"), [Charles Ehresmann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Ehresmann "Charles Ehresmann"),[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiber_bundle#cite_note-7)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiber_bundle#cite_note-8)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiber_bundle#cite_note-9)[Jean-Pierre Serre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean-Pierre_Serre "Jean-Pierre Serre"),[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiber_bundle#cite_note-10) and others.

Fiber bundles became their own object of study in the period 1935–1940. The first general definition appeared in the works of Whitney.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiber_bundle#cite_note-11)

Whitney came to the general definition of a fiber bundle from his study of a more particular notion of a [sphere bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere_bundle "Sphere bundle"),[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiber_bundle#cite_note-12) that is a fiber bundle whose fiber is a sphere of arbitrary [dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension "Dimension").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiber_bundle#cite_note-13)

A fiber bundle is a structure ![Image 19: {\displaystyle (E,\,B,\,\pi ,\,F),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/008aed5ce4bb2642b27842b3faeac4987723f75e) where ![Image 20: {\displaystyle E,B,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/56e02ad19af4010c156476e1afaee7c54935c8a8) and ![Image 21: {\displaystyle F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/545fd099af8541605f7ee55f08225526be88ce57) are [topological spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_spaces "Topological spaces") and ![Image 22: {\displaystyle \pi :E\to B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b3b6680121ed6dbd1f69c871093712b1de5bd4ac) is a [continuous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_(topology) "Continuous (topology)")[surjection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surjection "Surjection") satisfying a _local triviality_ condition outlined below. The space ![Image 23: {\displaystyle B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47136aad860d145f75f3eed3022df827cee94d7a) is called the **base space** of the bundle, ![Image 24: {\displaystyle E}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4232c9de2ee3eec0a9c0a19b15ab92daa6223f9b) the **total space**, and ![Image 25: {\displaystyle F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/545fd099af8541605f7ee55f08225526be88ce57) the **fiber**. The map ![Image 26: {\displaystyle \pi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9be4ba0bb8df3af72e90a0535fabcc17431e540a) is called the **projection map** (or **bundle projection**). We shall assume in what follows that the base space ![Image 27: {\displaystyle B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47136aad860d145f75f3eed3022df827cee94d7a) is [connected](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connected_space "Connected space").

We require that for every ![Image 28: {\displaystyle x\in B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9aac01724708de4e1d41423bc64b35e9d94c9009), there is an open [neighborhood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neighborhood_(topology) "Neighborhood (topology)")![Image 29: {\displaystyle U\subseteq B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/858638a6c9ba36e8aa68ffaf90eb9b38c4b20fcd) of ![Image 30: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) (which will be called a trivializing neighborhood) such that there is a [homeomorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homeomorphism "Homeomorphism")![Image 31: {\displaystyle \varphi :\pi ^{-1}(U)\to U\times F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bbed031bbcb0ad117fb39e66ecc9d0cc7a58e0da) (where ![Image 32: {\displaystyle \pi ^{-1}(U)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/52414f17b55ee456b0b6cabc0da7f5c141981c09) is given the [subspace topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subspace_topology "Subspace topology"), and ![Image 33: {\displaystyle U\times F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f9f06bc8785fae8deb989c1a480d8084d092f66c) is the product space) in such a way that ![Image 34: {\displaystyle \pi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9be4ba0bb8df3af72e90a0535fabcc17431e540a) agrees with the projection onto the first factor. Thus for any ![Image 39: {\displaystyle p\in B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e9667526cc706fea40d816f40349a48ca47fa339), the [preimage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Preimage "Preimage")![Image 40: {\displaystyle \pi ^{-1}(\{p\})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5bcaa81afb068608175e6a1f8dd1cfec19378329) is homeomorphic to ![Image 41: {\displaystyle F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/545fd099af8541605f7ee55f08225526be88ce57) (since this is true of ![Image 42: {\displaystyle \operatorname {proj} _{1}^{-1}(\{p\})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b0f3eb75d2b109a3a3a61e1b5ec10695b536629f)) and is called the **fiber over ![Image 43: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36)**. Every fiber bundle ![Image 44: {\displaystyle \pi :E\to B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b3b6680121ed6dbd1f69c871093712b1de5bd4ac) is an [open map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_map "Open map"), since projections of products are open maps. Therefore ![Image 45: {\displaystyle B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47136aad860d145f75f3eed3022df827cee94d7a) carries the [quotient topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quotient_topology "Quotient topology") determined by the map ![Image 46: {\displaystyle \pi .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c94b721b560eaa34cbf1e346505aca908d473be5)

A fiber bundle ![Image 47: {\displaystyle (E,\,B,\,\pi ,\,F)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6005627016d5a28b8324b0a1dea2781bccef342a) is often denoted ![Image 48: {\displaystyle {\begin{matrix}{}\\F\longrightarrow E\ {\xrightarrow {\,\ \pi \ }}\ B\\{}\end{matrix}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1040aa1555c6953616f09de8c69b6f074239421a) that, in analogy with a [short exact sequence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Short_exact_sequence "Short exact sequence"), indicates which space is the fiber, total space and base space, as well as the map from total to base space.

A **smooth fiber bundle** is a fiber bundle in the [category](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category_(mathematics) "Category (mathematics)") of [smooth manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smooth_manifold "Smooth manifold"). That is, ![Image 49: {\displaystyle E}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4232c9de2ee3eec0a9c0a19b15ab92daa6223f9b), ![Image 50: {\displaystyle B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47136aad860d145f75f3eed3022df827cee94d7a), and ![Image 51: {\displaystyle F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/545fd099af8541605f7ee55f08225526be88ce57) are required to be smooth manifolds and all the [functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)") above are required to be [smooth maps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smooth_map "Smooth map").

Let ![Image 52: {\displaystyle E=B\times F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e8aa627b0a478850e41c73b0eb70cadd831feac3) and let ![Image 53: {\displaystyle \pi :E\to B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b3b6680121ed6dbd1f69c871093712b1de5bd4ac) be the projection onto the first factor. Then ![Image 54: {\displaystyle \pi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9be4ba0bb8df3af72e90a0535fabcc17431e540a) is a fiber bundle (of ![Image 55: {\displaystyle F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/545fd099af8541605f7ee55f08225526be88ce57)) over ![Image 56: {\displaystyle B.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0eccf5bca7cdc1fa4439af2d31831db6bde00473) Here ![Image 57: {\displaystyle E}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4232c9de2ee3eec0a9c0a19b15ab92daa6223f9b) is not just locally a product but _globally_ one. Any such fiber bundle is called a **trivial bundle**. Any fiber bundle over a [contractible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contractible_space "Contractible space")[CW-complex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CW-complex "CW-complex") is trivial.

[![Image 58](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/71/Moebius_Surface_1_Display_Small.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Moebius_Surface_1_Display_Small.png)

The Möbius strip is a nontrivial bundle over the circle.

Perhaps the simplest example of a nontrivial bundle ![Image 59: {\displaystyle E}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4232c9de2ee3eec0a9c0a19b15ab92daa6223f9b) is the [Möbius strip](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M%C3%B6bius_strip "Möbius strip"). It has the [circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle "Circle") that runs lengthwise along the center of the strip as a base ![Image 60: {\displaystyle B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47136aad860d145f75f3eed3022df827cee94d7a) and a [line segment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_segment "Line segment") for the fiber ![Image 61: {\displaystyle F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/545fd099af8541605f7ee55f08225526be88ce57), so the Möbius strip is a bundle of the line segment over the circle. A [neighborhood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neighbourhood_(mathematics) "Neighbourhood (mathematics)")![Image 62: {\displaystyle U}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/458a728f53b9a0274f059cd695e067c430956025) of ![Image 63: {\displaystyle \pi (x)\in B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/268040b306dd6d0d763083be8cd6bec2c15390c2) (where ![Image 64: {\displaystyle x\in E}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/30b1971b01bc31d5b816f03cc7e1d9215d6c2ad8)) is an [arc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circular_arc "Circular arc"); in the picture, this is the [length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Length "Length") of one of the squares. The [preimage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Image_(mathematics) "Image (mathematics)")![Image 65: {\displaystyle \pi ^{-1}(U)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/52414f17b55ee456b0b6cabc0da7f5c141981c09) in the picture is a (somewhat twisted) slice of the strip four squares wide and one long (i.e. all the points that project to ![Image 66: {\displaystyle U}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/458a728f53b9a0274f059cd695e067c430956025)).

A homeomorphism (![Image 67: {\displaystyle \varphi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/33ee699558d09cf9d653f6351f9fda0b2f4aaa3e) in [§Formal definition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiber_bundle#Formal_definition)) exists that maps the preimage of ![Image 68: {\displaystyle U}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/458a728f53b9a0274f059cd695e067c430956025) (the trivializing neighborhood) to a slice of a cylinder: curved, but not twisted. This pair locally trivializes the strip. The corresponding trivial bundle ![Image 69: {\displaystyle B\times F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/05e0fcb90530d44152eea7eb002ee9b1278c7165) would be a [cylinder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylinder_(geometry) "Cylinder (geometry)"), but the Möbius strip has an overall "twist". This twist is visible only globally; locally the Möbius strip and the cylinder are identical (making a single vertical cut in either gives the same space).

A similar nontrivial bundle is the [Klein bottle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Klein_bottle "Klein bottle"), which can be viewed as a "twisted" circle bundle over another circle. The corresponding non-twisted (trivial) bundle is the 2-[torus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torus "Torus"), ![Image 70: {\displaystyle S^{1}\times S^{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/120ffc4a9b57e92089797fa472099abd3e1099db).

A **[covering space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covering_map "Covering map")** is a fiber bundle such that the bundle projection is a [local homeomorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_homeomorphism "Local homeomorphism"). It follows that the fiber is a [discrete space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discrete_space "Discrete space").

### Vector and principal bundles

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Fiber_bundle&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Vector and principal bundles")]

A special class of fiber bundles, called **[vector bundles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_bundle "Vector bundle")**, are those whose fibers are [vector spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space") (to qualify as a vector bundle the structure group of the bundle—see below—must be a [linear group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_linear_group "General linear group")). Important examples of vector bundles include the [tangent bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_bundle "Tangent bundle") and [cotangent bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cotangent_bundle "Cotangent bundle") of a smooth manifold. From any vector bundle, one can construct the [frame bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frame_bundle "Frame bundle") of [bases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basis_(mathematics) "Basis (mathematics)"), which is a principal bundle (see below).

Another special class of fiber bundles, called **[principal bundles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principal_bundle "Principal bundle")**, are bundles on whose fibers a [free](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_action "Free action") and [transitive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transitive_action "Transitive action")[action](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_action_(mathematics) "Group action (mathematics)") by a group ![Image 71: {\displaystyle G}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5f3c8921a3b352de45446a6789b104458c9f90b) is given, so that each fiber is a [principal homogeneous space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principal_homogeneous_space "Principal homogeneous space"). The bundle is often specified along with the group by referring to it as a principal ![Image 72: {\displaystyle G}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5f3c8921a3b352de45446a6789b104458c9f90b)-bundle. The group ![Image 73: {\displaystyle G}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5f3c8921a3b352de45446a6789b104458c9f90b) is also the structure group of the bundle. Given a [representation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_representation "Group representation")![Image 74: {\displaystyle \rho }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1f7d439671d1289b6a816e6af7a304be40608d64) of ![Image 75: {\displaystyle G}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5f3c8921a3b352de45446a6789b104458c9f90b) on a vector space ![Image 76: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845), a vector bundle with ![Image 77: {\displaystyle \rho (G)\subseteq {\text{Aut}}(V)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/46713850bda63f6d201483bca6aa6d8d9d4d81f8) as a structure group may be constructed, known as the [associated bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Associated_bundle "Associated bundle").

A **sphere bundle** is a fiber bundle whose fiber is an [_n_-sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypersphere "Hypersphere"). Given a vector bundle ![Image 78: {\displaystyle E}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4232c9de2ee3eec0a9c0a19b15ab92daa6223f9b) with a [metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_tensor "Metric tensor") (such as the tangent bundle to a [Riemannian manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_manifold "Riemannian manifold")) one can construct the associated **unit sphere bundle**, for which the fiber over a point ![Image 79: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) is the set of all [unit vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_vector "Unit vector") in ![Image 80: {\displaystyle E_{x}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/029e49fbec18ece71cdd1e68bc478444e2c99d30). When the vector bundle in question is the tangent bundle ![Image 81: {\displaystyle TM}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ea000afb5769206ddd5fd43f458430d04422ddeb), the unit sphere bundle is known as the **[unit tangent bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_tangent_bundle "Unit tangent bundle")**.

A sphere bundle is partially characterized by its [Euler class](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler_class "Euler class"), which is a degree ![Image 82: {\displaystyle n+1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2a135e65a42f2d73cccbfc4569523996ca0036f1)[cohomology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cohomology "Cohomology") class in the total space of the bundle. In the case ![Image 83: {\displaystyle n=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d9ec7e1edc2e6d98f5aec2a39ae5f1c99d1e1425) the sphere bundle is called a [circle bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle_bundle "Circle bundle") and the Euler class is equal to the first [Chern class](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chern_class "Chern class"), which characterizes the topology of the bundle completely. For any ![Image 84: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b), given the Euler class of a bundle, one can calculate its cohomology using a [long exact sequence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Long_exact_sequence "Long exact sequence") called the [Gysin sequence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gysin_sequence "Gysin sequence").

If ![Image 85: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) is a [topological space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_space "Topological space") and ![Image 86: {\displaystyle f:X\to X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/672b7e48a9c7c9f7b0be9a75555e0dbd87c64aaa) is a [homeomorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homeomorphism "Homeomorphism") then the [mapping torus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mapping_torus "Mapping torus")![Image 87: {\displaystyle M_{f}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/be53b2e05e15a7c85c9be0f4ba32d7f3ef28b98b) has a natural structure of a fiber bundle over the [circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle "Circle") with fiber ![Image 88: {\displaystyle X.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5ba76c5a460c4a0bb1639a193bc1830f0a773e03) Mapping tori of homeomorphisms of [surfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_(topology) "Surface (topology)") are of particular importance in [3-manifold topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3-manifold "3-manifold").

If ![Image 89: {\displaystyle G}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5f3c8921a3b352de45446a6789b104458c9f90b) is a [topological group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_group "Topological group") and ![Image 90: {\displaystyle H}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/75a9edddcca2f782014371f75dca39d7e13a9c1b) is a [closed subgroup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_subgroup "Closed subgroup"), then under some circumstances, the [quotient space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quotient_space_(topology) "Quotient space (topology)")![Image 91: {\displaystyle G/H}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/21e7e9d6e3072ec8dd48200d755847154ea5d35c) together with the quotient map ![Image 92: {\displaystyle \pi :G\to G/H}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f882e309ca7251052f39ff2bf96bf09e26899a0a) is a fiber bundle, whose fiber is the topological space ![Image 93: {\displaystyle H}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/75a9edddcca2f782014371f75dca39d7e13a9c1b). A [necessary and sufficient condition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Necessary_and_sufficient_condition "Necessary and sufficient condition") for (![Image 94: {\displaystyle G,\,G/H,\,\pi ,\,H}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5e758ed31860cd5bc2d31049948f8b98f6e9c3ff)) to form a fiber bundle is that the mapping ![Image 95: {\displaystyle \pi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9be4ba0bb8df3af72e90a0535fabcc17431e540a) admits [local cross-sections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiber_bundle#Sections) ([Steenrod 1951](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiber_bundle#CITEREFSteenrod1951), §7).

The most general conditions under which the [quotient map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quotient_map "Quotient map") will admit local cross-sections are not known, although if ![Image 96: {\displaystyle G}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5f3c8921a3b352de45446a6789b104458c9f90b) is a [Lie group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_group "Lie group") and ![Image 97: {\displaystyle H}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/75a9edddcca2f782014371f75dca39d7e13a9c1b) a closed subgroup (and thus a [Lie subgroup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_subgroup "Lie subgroup") by [Cartan's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_subgroup_theorem "Closed subgroup theorem")), then the quotient map is a fiber bundle. One example of this is the [Hopf fibration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hopf_fibration "Hopf fibration"), ![Image 98: {\displaystyle S^{3}\to S^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5a7181b48a46cdabea1bcd19d37b03ab6d1b5f0d), which is a fiber bundle over the sphere ![Image 99: {\displaystyle S^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3b6401d5d0155afb1406770d1eb80badce4e08ce) whose total space is ![Image 100: {\displaystyle S^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/01e57c690f890937838c10ba57853ff21bf30ec8). From the perspective of Lie groups, ![Image 101: {\displaystyle S^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/01e57c690f890937838c10ba57853ff21bf30ec8) can be identified with the [special unitary group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_unitary_group "Special unitary group")![Image 102: {\displaystyle SU(2)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/27f8cd5de228a45abf34210c1666cd46dd87bc12). The [abelian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abelian_group "Abelian group") subgroup of [diagonal matrices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diagonal_matrices "Diagonal matrices") is [isomorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isomorphic_group "Isomorphic group") to the [circle group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle_group "Circle group")![Image 103: {\displaystyle U(1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e62b00d74ee0cefb86cc052365625abff56d43e6), and the quotient ![Image 104: {\displaystyle SU(2)/U(1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bc4ad0dd7f39bfc5bdc2761f146760c9a691aeb1) is [diffeomorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffeomorphism "Diffeomorphism") to the sphere.

More generally, if ![Image 105: {\displaystyle G}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5f3c8921a3b352de45446a6789b104458c9f90b) is any topological group and ![Image 106: {\displaystyle H}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/75a9edddcca2f782014371f75dca39d7e13a9c1b) a closed subgroup that also happens to be a Lie group, then ![Image 107: {\displaystyle G\to G/H}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/590a254045ce35aef27818ba4b389df2816b685c) is a fiber bundle.

A **section** (or **cross section**) of a fiber bundle ![Image 108: {\displaystyle \pi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9be4ba0bb8df3af72e90a0535fabcc17431e540a) is a continuous map ![Image 109: {\displaystyle f:B\to E}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/65a5c096e4ba10a60a2a376ce357cfa12b8681a8) such that ![Image 110: {\displaystyle \pi (f(x))=x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f1447d0e6557d3e738a272b5d4a60601154794a2)[for all](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/For_all "For all")_x_ in _B_. Since bundles do not in general have globally defined sections, one of the purposes of the theory is to account for their existence. The [obstruction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obstruction_theory "Obstruction theory") to the existence of a section can often be measured by a cohomology class, which leads to the theory of [characteristic classes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Characteristic_class "Characteristic class") in [algebraic topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_topology "Algebraic topology").

The most well-known example is the [hairy ball theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hairy_ball_theorem "Hairy ball theorem"), where the [Euler class](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler_class "Euler class") is the obstruction to the [tangent bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_bundle "Tangent bundle") of the [2-sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2-sphere "2-sphere") having a nowhere vanishing section.

Often one would like to define sections only locally (especially when global sections do not exist). A **local section** of a fiber bundle is a continuous map ![Image 111: {\displaystyle f:U\to E}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ee5792e9fade5dd22f0bf1f330eb9f0f94a7cf0b) where _U_ is an [open set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_set "Open set") in _B_ and ![Image 112: {\displaystyle \pi (f(x))=x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f1447d0e6557d3e738a272b5d4a60601154794a2) for all _x_ in _U_. If ![Image 113: {\displaystyle (U,\,\varphi )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/042ff86941f95f3565886d06e9a5d81ed5a3acaa) is a local trivialization [chart](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chart_(mathematics) "Chart (mathematics)") then local sections always exist over _U_. Such sections are in [1-1 correspondence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1-1_Correspondence "1-1 Correspondence") with continuous maps ![Image 114: {\displaystyle U\to F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9a39672c2717f34a6d4f56a8a2dd4674236a776a). Sections form a [sheaf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sheaf_(mathematics) "Sheaf (mathematics)").

Structure groups and transition functions
-----------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Fiber_bundle&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Structure groups and transition functions")]

Fiber bundles often come with a [group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_(mathematics) "Group (mathematics)") of symmetries that describe the matching conditions between overlapping local trivialization charts. Specifically, let _G_ be a [topological group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_group "Topological group") that [acts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_action_(mathematics) "Group action (mathematics)") continuously on the fiber space _F_ on the left. We lose nothing if we require _G_ to act [faithfully](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Faithful_action "Faithful action") on _F_ so that it may be thought of as a group of [homeomorphisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homeomorphism "Homeomorphism") of _F_. A **_G_-[atlas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atlas_(topology) "Atlas (topology)")** for the bundle ![Image 115: {\displaystyle (E,B,\pi ,F)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/363fe17ec08d0de5e0e563fcbf103e619a4c0ec3) is a set of local trivialization charts ![Image 116: {\displaystyle \{(U_{k},\,\varphi _{k})\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cc9c61bdd172fd75a4269c9d41ae3ca1be2278ef) such that for any ![Image 117: {\displaystyle \varphi _{i},\varphi _{j}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/89bfa525439fad361d0b36318eac5372af259d09) for the overlapping charts ![Image 118: {\displaystyle (U_{i},\,\varphi _{i})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0be63dd2b73da00338ba52639cbfa2ee7da4a040) and ![Image 119: {\displaystyle (U_{j},\,\varphi _{j})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/165a4fc9d26022db917708de50c81ddbfbb53296) the function ![Image 120: {\displaystyle \varphi _{i}\varphi _{j}^{-1}:\left(U_{i}\cap U_{j}\right)\times F\to \left(U_{i}\cap U_{j}\right)\times F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/92b9ebeee52f49129fe1dd6de035c2753a73bd78) is given by ![Image 121: {\displaystyle \varphi _{i}\varphi _{j}^{-1}(x,\,\xi )=\left(x,\,t_{ij}(x)\xi \right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/25469cd7288b1b9755106fbbf1d59230084103a2) where ![Image 122: {\displaystyle t_{ij}:U_{i}\cap U_{j}\to G}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fc499251de96f82583ec0b06827cf6a08b3c9bbf) is a continuous map called a **transition function**. Two _G_-atlases are equivalent if their union is also a _G_-atlas. A **_G_-bundle** is a fiber bundle with an equivalence class of _G_-atlases. The group _G_ is called the **structure group** of the bundle. This is related to the [gauge group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauge_group "Gauge group") in [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics"), which is the group of automorphisms of the principal _G_-bundle that leave the base space unchanged.

In the smooth category, a _G_-bundle is a smooth fiber bundle where _G_ is a [Lie group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_group "Lie group") and the corresponding action on _F_ is smooth and the transition functions are all smooth maps.

The transition functions ![Image 123: {\displaystyle t_{ij}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fac4d83b980aeee694196ea954d449e2db972135) satisfy the following conditions

1.   ![Image 124: {\displaystyle t_{ii}(x)=1\,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e9b142d2a28df5707a659582dc3d02ad1acfca4a)
2.   ![Image 125: {\displaystyle t_{ij}(x)=t_{ji}(x)^{-1}\,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/991935e10053858cdc35f4d35a944fc9d2616fdf)
3.   ![Image 126: {\displaystyle t_{ik}(x)=t_{ij}(x)t_{jk}(x).\,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8c601ccfdeaa8457267da1d7ee7d17b1c752252f)

The third condition applies on triple overlaps _U i_ ∩ _U j_ ∩ _U k_ and is called the **[cocycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C4%8Cech_cohomology#Cocycle "Čech cohomology") condition** (see [Čech cohomology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C4%8Cech_cohomology "Čech cohomology")). The importance of this is that the transition functions determine the fiber bundle (if one assumes the Čech cocycle condition).

A [principal _G_-bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principal_bundle "Principal bundle") is a _G_-bundle where the fiber _F_ is a [principal homogeneous space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principal_homogeneous_space "Principal homogeneous space") for the left action of _G_ itself (equivalently, one can specify that the action of _G_ on the fiber _F_ is free and transitive, i.e. [regular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_action_(mathematics)#Regular "Group action (mathematics)")). In this case, it is often a matter of convenience to identify _F_ with _G_ and so obtain a (right) action of _G_ on the principal bundle.

It is useful to have notions of a mapping between two fiber bundles. Suppose that ![Image 127: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) and ![Image 128: {\displaystyle N}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5e3890c981ae85503089652feb48b191b57aae3) are base spaces, and ![Image 129: {\displaystyle \pi _{E}:E\to M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7ec2f359188ecf04334dc8d114ee73d2d804064b) and ![Image 130: {\displaystyle \pi _{F}:F\to N}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d56259dbc4afff4398f29d4bfc05d00add2f071d) are fiber bundles over ![Image 131: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) and ![Image 132: {\displaystyle N}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5e3890c981ae85503089652feb48b191b57aae3), respectively. A **bundle map** or **bundle morphism** consists of a pair of continuous[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiber_bundle#cite_note-14) functions ![Image 133: {\displaystyle \varphi :E\to F,\quad f:M\to N}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ba8b4ca83fd977e39c0d06791011d0be8d14c810) such that ![Image 134: {\displaystyle \pi _{F}\circ \varphi =f\circ \pi _{E}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/74ab46bf528265f45017c5b9f3310d8b4a7e6d7b). That is, the following diagram is [commutative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_diagram "Commutative diagram"):

[![Image 135](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/BundleMorphism-04.svg/150px-BundleMorphism-04.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:BundleMorphism-04.svg)

For fiber bundles with structure group ![Image 136: {\displaystyle G}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5f3c8921a3b352de45446a6789b104458c9f90b) and whose total spaces are (right) ![Image 137: {\displaystyle G}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5f3c8921a3b352de45446a6789b104458c9f90b)-spaces (such as a principal bundle), bundle [morphisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morphism "Morphism") are also required to be ![Image 138: {\displaystyle G}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5f3c8921a3b352de45446a6789b104458c9f90b)-[equivariant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equivariant "Equivariant") on the fibers. This means that ![Image 139: {\displaystyle \varphi :E\to F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5eb41436c30bc4a610765fa03e643ecbe252b19a) is also ![Image 140: {\displaystyle G}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5f3c8921a3b352de45446a6789b104458c9f90b)-morphism from one ![Image 141: {\displaystyle G}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5f3c8921a3b352de45446a6789b104458c9f90b)-space to another, that is, ![Image 142: {\displaystyle \varphi (xs)=\varphi (x)s}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ce9899b443db8b2499e816876f3a1c3f907529d8) for all ![Image 143: {\displaystyle x\in E}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/30b1971b01bc31d5b816f03cc7e1d9215d6c2ad8) and ![Image 144: {\displaystyle s\in G}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f3669b548485e062ed616d490e37d9294f6e57dd).

In case the base spaces ![Image 145: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) and ![Image 146: {\displaystyle N}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5e3890c981ae85503089652feb48b191b57aae3) coincide, then a bundle morphism over ![Image 147: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) from the fiber bundle ![Image 148: {\displaystyle \pi _{E}:E\to M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7ec2f359188ecf04334dc8d114ee73d2d804064b) to ![Image 149: {\displaystyle \pi _{F}:F\to M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b0f78e659e36dda269a5dcdf0ccede19c26dd7f0) is a map ![Image 150: {\displaystyle \varphi :E\to F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5eb41436c30bc4a610765fa03e643ecbe252b19a) such that ![Image 151: {\displaystyle \pi _{E}=\pi _{F}\circ \varphi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c6a8d781850a9dfa217bc5c00cf53181a2219936). Differentiable fiber bundles
----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Fiber_bundle&action=edit&section=16 "Edit section: Differentiable fiber bundles")]

In the category of [differentiable manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differentiable_manifold "Differentiable manifold"), fiber bundles arise naturally as [submersions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Submersion_(mathematics) "Submersion (mathematics)") of one manifold to another. Not every (differentiable) submersion ![Image 164: {\displaystyle f:M\to N}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1dbd50e2de9728ee14a7c232441137f588b109f6) from a differentiable manifold _M_ to another differentiable manifold _N_ gives rise to a differentiable fiber bundle. For one thing, the map must be surjective, and ![Image 165: {\displaystyle (M,N,f)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f44fea4832b971dd1e528fce9d40179627e345af) is called a [fibered manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fibered_manifold "Fibered manifold"). However, this necessary condition is not quite sufficient, and there are a variety of sufficient conditions in common use.

If _M_ and _N_ are [compact](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compact_space "Compact space") and [connected](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connection_(mathematics) "Connection (mathematics)"), then any submersion ![Image 166: {\displaystyle f:M\to N}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1dbd50e2de9728ee14a7c232441137f588b109f6) gives rise to a fiber bundle in the sense that there is a fiber space _F_ diffeomorphic to each of the fibers such that ![Image 167: {\displaystyle (E,B,\pi ,F)=(M,N,f,F)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5b3bb7e2220d9c621a7ee2b3e1f9fa5e281322ef) is a fiber bundle. (Surjectivity of ![Image 168: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) follows by the assumptions already given in this case.) More generally, the assumption of compactness can be relaxed if the submersion ![Image 169: {\displaystyle f:M\to N}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1dbd50e2de9728ee14a7c232441137f588b109f6) is assumed to be a surjective [proper map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proper_map "Proper map"), meaning that ![Image 170: {\displaystyle f^{-1}(K)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/269e80d51c926fe296775d04e8a657538c1e7fca) is compact for every compact [subset](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subset "Subset")_K_ of _N_. Another sufficient condition, due to [Ehresmann (1951)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiber_bundle#CITEREFEhresmann1951), is that if ![Image 171: {\displaystyle f:M\to N}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1dbd50e2de9728ee14a7c232441137f588b109f6) is a surjective [submersion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Submersion_(mathematics) "Submersion (mathematics)") with _M_ and _N_[differentiable manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differentiable_manifold "Differentiable manifold") such that the preimage ![Image 172: {\displaystyle f^{-1}\{x\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5bf4d843f3d77d6b980d7f4b25dd659e99472166) is compact and connected for all ![Image 173: {\displaystyle x\in N,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e3c8d7fd864ef9386a936b3bf5ea31b82c595bb0) then ![Image 174: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) admits a [compatible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compatible_(algebra) "Compatible (algebra)") fiber bundle structure ([Michor 2008](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiber_bundle#CITEREFMichor2008), §17).

*   The notion of a [bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bundle_(mathematics) "Bundle (mathematics)") applies to many more categories in mathematics, at the expense of appropriately modifying the local triviality condition; cf. [principal homogeneous space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principal_homogeneous_space "Principal homogeneous space") and [torsor (algebraic geometry)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torsor_(algebraic_geometry) "Torsor (algebraic geometry)").
*   In topology, a [fibration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fibration "Fibration") is a mapping ![Image 175: {\displaystyle \pi :E\to B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b3b6680121ed6dbd1f69c871093712b1de5bd4ac) that has certain [homotopy-theoretic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homotopy_theory "Homotopy theory") properties in common with fiber bundles. Specifically, under mild technical assumptions a fiber bundle always has the [homotopy lifting property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homotopy_lifting_property "Homotopy lifting property") or homotopy covering property (see [Steenrod (1951](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiber_bundle#CITEREFSteenrod1951), 11.7) for details). This is the defining property of a fibration.
*   A section of a fiber bundle is a "function whose output [range](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Range_of_a_function "Range of a function") is continuously dependent on the input." This property is formally captured in the notion of [dependent type](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dependent_type "Dependent type").
