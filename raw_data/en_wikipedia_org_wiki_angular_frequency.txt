In [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics"), **angular frequency** (symbol _**ω**_), also called **angular speed** and **angular rate**, is a [scalar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_(physics) "Scalar (physics)") measure of the [angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle "Angle")[rate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rate_(mathematics) "Rate (mathematics)") (the angle per unit time) or the [temporal rate of change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temporal_rate_of_change "Temporal rate of change") of the [phase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phase_(waves) "Phase (waves)")[argument](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_argument "Function argument") of a [sinusoidal waveform](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sinusoidal_waveform "Sinusoidal waveform") or [sine function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sine_function "Sine function") (for example, in oscillations and waves). Angular frequency (or angular speed) is the magnitude of the [pseudovector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudovector "Pseudovector") quantity _[angular velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular\_velocity "Angular velocity")_.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_frequency#cite_note-UP1-1)

Angular frequency can be obtained multiplying _[rotational frequency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational\_frequency "Rotational frequency")_, _ν_ (or ordinary _[frequency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frequency "Frequency")_, _f_) by a full [turn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turn_(unit) "Turn (unit)") (2[π](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi "Pi")[radians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radians "Radians")): _ω_ = 2 π rad⋅_ν_. It can also be formulated as _ω_ = d _θ_/d _t_, the [instantaneous rate of change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Instantaneous_rate_of_change "Instantaneous rate of change") of the [angular displacement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_displacement "Angular displacement"), _θ_, with respect to time,_t_.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_frequency#cite_note-ISO80000-3_2019-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_frequency#cite_note-3)

In [SI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SI "SI")[units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Units_of_measurement "Units of measurement"), angular frequency is normally presented in the unit [radian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radian "Radian") per [second](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second "Second"). The unit [hertz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hertz "Hertz") (Hz) is dimensionally equivalent, but by convention it is only used for frequency _f_, never for angular frequency _ω_. This convention is used to help avoid the confusion[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_frequency#cite_note-4) that arises when dealing with quantities such as frequency and angular quantities because the units of measure (such as cycle or radian) are considered to be one and hence may be omitted when expressing quantities in terms of SI units.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_frequency#cite_note-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_frequency#cite_note-6)

In [digital signal processing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digital_signal_processing "Digital signal processing"), the frequency may be normalized by the [sampling rate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sampling_rate "Sampling rate"), yielding the [normalized frequency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normalized_frequency_(digital_signal_processing) "Normalized frequency (digital signal processing)").

In a rotating or orbiting object, there is a relation between distance from the axis, ![Image 4: {\displaystyle r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d1ecb613aa2984f0576f70f86650b7c2a132538), [tangential speed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangential_speed "Tangential speed"), ![Image 5: {\displaystyle v}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e07b00e7fc0847fbd16391c778d65bc25c452597), and the angular frequency of the rotation. During one period, ![Image 6: {\displaystyle T}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ec7200acd984a1d3a3d7dc455e262fbe54f7f6e0), a body in circular motion travels a distance ![Image 7: {\displaystyle vT}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/91f0891d99694fcff4556e2d759cb1cdfd3ea58d). This distance is also equal to the circumference of the path traced out by the body, ![Image 8: {\displaystyle 2\pi r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/71e811131a9c6c5f45e6657e0fc506e7e2a37f06). Setting these two quantities equal, and recalling the link between period and angular frequency we obtain: ![Image 9: {\displaystyle \omega =v/r.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fa48e2b86620756f9f30cce003602f7820f0232e) Circular motion on the unit circle is given by ![Image 10: {\displaystyle \omega ={\frac {2\pi }{T}}={2\pi f},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c9c43359ce05dc206a7430d3a4b5714b0d1d8b5e) where:

*   _ω_ is the angular frequency (SI unit: [radians per second](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radians_per_second "Radians per second")),
*   _T_ is the [period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frequency "Frequency") (SI unit: [seconds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second "Second")),
*   _f_ is the [ordinary frequency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordinary_frequency "Ordinary frequency") (SI unit: [hertz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hertz "Hertz")).

### Oscillations of a spring

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Angular_frequency&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Oscillations of a spring")]

An object attached to a spring can [oscillate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oscillation "Oscillation"). If the spring is assumed to be ideal and massless with no damping, then the motion is [simple and harmonic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harmonic_oscillator "Harmonic oscillator") with an angular frequency given by[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_frequency#cite_note-PoP1-7)![Image 11: {\displaystyle \omega ={\sqrt {\frac {k}{m}}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/de4b590d6aa119660a7f9898dffe27a0c1a4b8ba) where

*   _k_ is the [spring constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spring_constant "Spring constant"),
*   _m_ is the mass of the object.

_ω_ is referred to as the natural angular frequency (sometimes be denoted as _ω_ 0).

As the object oscillates, its acceleration can be calculated by [![Image 12: {\displaystyle a=-\omega ^{2}x,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a33373c9bb4d91af5b292e084f5f33857459461b)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:MathWikibase&qid=Q11376) where _x_ is displacement from an equilibrium position.

Using standard frequency _f_, this equation would be ![Image 13: {\displaystyle a=-(2\pi f)^{2}x.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/635c14f73ed04adf883369eb877d1d66ea67bdd3)

The resonant angular frequency in a series [LC circuit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LC_circuit "LC circuit") equals the square root of the [reciprocal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplicative_inverse "Multiplicative inverse") of the product of the [capacitance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capacitance "Capacitance") (_C_, with SI unit [farad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Farad "Farad")) and the [inductance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inductance "Inductance") of the circuit (_L_, with SI unit [henry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry_(unit) "Henry (unit)")):[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_frequency#cite_note-LC1-8)![Image 14: {\displaystyle \omega ={\sqrt {\frac {1}{LC}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8cf8762d2a406077bb2221f5f8519288af83964a)

Adding series resistance (for example, due to the resistance of the wire in a coil) does not change the resonant frequency of the series LC circuit. For a parallel tuned circuit, the above equation is often a useful approximation, but the resonant frequency does depend on the losses of parallel elements.

Although angular frequency is often loosely referred to as frequency, it differs from frequency by a factor of 2 π, which potentially leads confusion when the distinction is not made clear.
