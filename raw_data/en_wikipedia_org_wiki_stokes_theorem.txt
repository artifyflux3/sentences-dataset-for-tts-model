From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

[![Image 1](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Stokes%27_Theorem.svg/250px-Stokes%27_Theorem.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Stokes%27_Theorem.svg)

An illustration of Stokes' theorem, with surface Σ, its boundary ∂Σ and the normal vector n. The direction of positive circulation of the bounding contour ∂Σ, and the direction n of positive flux through the surface Σ, are related by a [right-hand-rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right-hand_rule "Right-hand rule") (i.e., the right hand the fingers circulate along ∂Σ and the thumb is directed along n).

**Stokes' theorem**,[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stokes'_theorem#cite_note-1) also known as the **Kelvin–Stokes theorem**[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stokes'_theorem#cite_note-iwahori-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stokes'_theorem#cite_note-fujimno-3) after [Lord Kelvin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lord_Kelvin "Lord Kelvin") and [George Stokes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sir_George_Stokes,_1st_Baronet "Sir George Stokes, 1st Baronet"), the **fundamental theorem for curls**, or simply the **curl theorem**,[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stokes'_theorem#cite_note-4) is a [theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theorem "Theorem") in [vector calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_calculus "Vector calculus") on ![Image 2: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5). Given a [vector field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_field "Vector field"), the theorem relates the [integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_integral "Surface integral") of the [curl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curl_(mathematics) "Curl (mathematics)") of the vector field over some surface, to the [line integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_integral "Line integral") of the vector field around the boundary of the surface. The classical theorem of Stokes can be stated in one sentence:

The [line integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_integral "Line integral") of a vector field over a loop is equal to the [surface integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_integral "Surface integral") of its _[curl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curl\_(mathematics) "Curl (mathematics)")_ over the enclosed surface.
Stokes' theorem is a special case of the [generalized Stokes theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generalized_Stokes_theorem "Generalized Stokes theorem").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stokes'_theorem#cite_note-DTPO-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stokes'_theorem#cite_note-lee-6) In particular, a vector field on ![Image 3: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5) can be considered as a [1-form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_form "Differential form") in which case its curl is its [exterior derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_derivative "Exterior derivative"), a 2-form.

Let ![Image 4: {\displaystyle \Sigma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9e1f558f53cda207614abdf90162266c70bc5c1e) be a smooth oriented surface in ![Image 5: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5) with boundary ![Image 6: {\displaystyle \partial \Sigma \equiv \Gamma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d40747f680b82cf02cc1d84182b9c7918f543420). If a vector field ![Image 7: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} (x,y,z)=(F_{x}(x,y,z),F_{y}(x,y,z),F_{z}(x,y,z))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/90a102266ee6f8fe369849ac7d4452a8dbdc47b4) is defined and has continuous first order [partial derivatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_derivatives "Partial derivatives") in a region containing ![Image 8: {\displaystyle \Sigma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9e1f558f53cda207614abdf90162266c70bc5c1e), then ![Image 9: {\displaystyle \iint _{\Sigma }(\nabla \times \mathbf {F} )\cdot \mathrm {d} \mathbf {\Sigma } =\oint _{\partial \Sigma }\mathbf {F} \cdot \mathrm {d} \mathbf {\Gamma } .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4af4baba7bbcf358b4e9316aba382b786e182a6b) More explicitly, the equality says that ![Image 10: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}&\iint _{\Sigma }\left(\left({\frac {\partial F_{z}}{\partial y}}-{\frac {\partial F_{y}}{\partial z}}\right)\,\mathrm {d} y\,\mathrm {d} z+\left({\frac {\partial F_{x}}{\partial z}}-{\frac {\partial F_{z}}{\partial x}}\right)\,\mathrm {d} z\,\mathrm {d} x+\left({\frac {\partial F_{y}}{\partial x}}-{\frac {\partial F_{x}}{\partial y}}\right)\,\mathrm {d} x\,\mathrm {d} y\right)\\&=\oint _{\partial \Sigma }{\Bigl (}F_{x}\,\mathrm {d} x+F_{y}\,\mathrm {d} y+F_{z}\,\mathrm {d} z{\Bigr )}.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81303bfc0500af5077351098c0e04c8f53e46719)

The main challenge in a precise statement of Stokes' theorem is in defining the notion of a boundary. Surfaces such as the [Koch snowflake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Koch_snowflake "Koch snowflake"), for example, are well-known not to exhibit a Riemann-integrable boundary, and the notion of surface measure in [Lebesgue theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebesgue_integration "Lebesgue integration") cannot be defined for a non-[Lipschitz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lipschitz_function "Lipschitz function") surface. One (advanced) technique is to pass to a [weak formulation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weak_formulation "Weak formulation") and then apply the machinery of [geometric measure theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_measure_theory "Geometric measure theory"); for that approach see the [coarea formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coarea_formula "Coarea formula"). In this article, we instead use a more elementary definition, based on the fact that a boundary can be discerned for full-dimensional subsets of ![Image 11: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e150115ab9f63023215109595b76686a1ff890fd).

A more detailed statement will be given for subsequent discussions. Let ![Image 12: {\displaystyle \gamma :[a,b]\to \mathbb {R} ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f1d326a550ef41246054e0311de07e1375d535f3) be a [piecewise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Piecewise "Piecewise") smooth [Jordan plane curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jordan_curve "Jordan curve"): a simple closed curve in the plane. The [Jordan curve theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jordan_curve_theorem "Jordan curve theorem") implies that ![Image 13: {\displaystyle \gamma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a223c880b0ce3da8f64ee33c4f0010beee400b1a) divides ![Image 14: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e150115ab9f63023215109595b76686a1ff890fd) into two components, a [compact](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compact_space "Compact space") one and another that is non-compact. Let ![Image 15: {\displaystyle D}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f34a0c600395e5d4345287e21fb26efd386990e6) denote the compact part; then ![Image 16: {\displaystyle D}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f34a0c600395e5d4345287e21fb26efd386990e6) is bounded by ![Image 17: {\displaystyle \gamma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a223c880b0ce3da8f64ee33c4f0010beee400b1a). It now suffices to transfer this notion of boundary along a continuous map to our surface in ![Image 18: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5). But we already have such a map: the [parametrization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parametrization_(geometry) "Parametrization (geometry)") of ![Image 19: {\displaystyle \Sigma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9e1f558f53cda207614abdf90162266c70bc5c1e).

Suppose ![Image 20: {\displaystyle \psi :D\to \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ab1d2309af5137f60b98d7f7e4b1e36ceb0bebda) is [piecewise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Piecewise "Piecewise") smooth at the [neighborhood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neighbourhood_(mathematics) "Neighbourhood (mathematics)") of ![Image 21: {\displaystyle D}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f34a0c600395e5d4345287e21fb26efd386990e6), with ![Image 22: {\displaystyle \Sigma =\psi (D)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/641d3ff168d493a310cc25d3b81371a46228794e).[[note 1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stokes'_theorem#cite_note-imgg-7) If ![Image 23: {\displaystyle \Gamma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4cfde86a3f7ec967af9955d0988592f0693d2b19) is the [space curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_curve "Space curve") defined by ![Image 24: {\displaystyle \Gamma (t)=\psi (\gamma (t))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2450d275b156d6e508371e0df0f05504575bb728)[[note 2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stokes'_theorem#cite_note-cgamma-8) then we call ![Image 25: {\displaystyle \Gamma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4cfde86a3f7ec967af9955d0988592f0693d2b19) the boundary of ![Image 26: {\displaystyle \Sigma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9e1f558f53cda207614abdf90162266c70bc5c1e), written ![Image 27: {\displaystyle \partial \Sigma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a8b6112fefd0ffc1007f8dbfc95e374e12acf6e7).

With the above notation, if ![Image 28: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/da18bef8c979f3548bb0d8976f5844012d7b8256) is any smooth vector field on ![Image 29: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5), then[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stokes'_theorem#cite_note-Jame-9)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stokes'_theorem#cite_note-bath-10)![Image 30: {\displaystyle \oint _{\partial \Sigma }\mathbf {F} \,\cdot \,\mathrm {d} {\mathbf {\Gamma } }=\iint _{\Sigma }\nabla \times \mathbf {F} \,\cdot \,\mathrm {d} \mathbf {\Sigma } .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/73a4b30418738b50fba062e5f61bab17b8183c97)

Here, the "![Image 31: {\displaystyle \cdot }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ba2c023bad1bd39ed49080f729cbf26bc448c9ba)" represents the [dot product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dot_product "Dot product") in ![Image 32: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5).

### Special case of a more general theorem

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Stokes%27_theorem&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Special case of a more general theorem")]

Stokes' theorem can be viewed as a special case of the following identity:[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stokes'_theorem#cite_note-11)![Image 33: {\displaystyle \oint _{\partial \Sigma }(\mathbf {F} \,\cdot \,\mathrm {d} {\mathbf {\Gamma } })\,\mathbf {g} =\iint _{\Sigma }\left[\mathrm {d} \mathbf {\Sigma } \cdot \left(\nabla \times \mathbf {F} -\mathbf {F} \times \nabla \right)\right]\mathbf {g} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0ac2bcf8b2c9f7e19c5032ec19f45de6cd5ea845) where ![Image 34: {\displaystyle \mathbf {g} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8cdf843789e9564a867aee3ff184453b72ecbafe) is any smooth vector or scalar field in ![Image 35: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5). When ![Image 36: {\displaystyle \mathbf {g} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8cdf843789e9564a867aee3ff184453b72ecbafe) is a uniform scalar field, the standard Stokes' theorem is recovered.

The proof of the theorem consists of 4 steps. We assume [Green's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green%27s_theorem "Green's theorem"), so what is of concern is how to boil down the three-dimensional complicated problem (Stokes' theorem) to a two-dimensional rudimentary problem (Green's theorem).[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stokes'_theorem#cite_note-12) When proving this theorem, mathematicians normally deduce it as a special case of a [more general result](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generalized_Stokes_theorem "Generalized Stokes theorem"), which is stated in terms of [differential forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_form "Differential form"), and proved using more sophisticated machinery. While powerful, these techniques require substantial background, so the proof below avoids them, and does not presuppose any knowledge beyond a familiarity with basic vector calculus and linear algebra.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stokes'_theorem#cite_note-bath-10) At the end of this section, a short alternative proof of Stokes' theorem is given, as a corollary of the generalized Stokes' theorem.

#### First step of the elementary proof (parametrization of integral)

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Stokes%27_theorem&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: First step of the elementary proof (parametrization of integral)")]

As in _[§Theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stokes'\_theorem#Theorem)_, we reduce the dimension by using the natural parametrization of the surface. Let _**ψ**_ and γ be as in that section, and note that by change of variables ![Image 37: {\displaystyle \oint _{\partial \Sigma }{\mathbf {F} (\mathbf {x} )\cdot \,\mathrm {d} \mathbf {\Gamma } }=\oint _{\gamma }{\mathbf {F} ({\boldsymbol {\psi }}(\mathbf {\gamma } ))\cdot \,\mathrm {d} {\boldsymbol {\psi }}(\mathbf {\gamma } )}=\oint _{\gamma }{\mathbf {F} ({\boldsymbol {\psi }}(\mathbf {y} ))\cdot J_{\mathbf {y} }({\boldsymbol {\psi }})\,\mathrm {d} \gamma }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ee1131d6dfbb3d20af4c39eac40dfd9cbe38da67) where J _y_ ψ stands for the [Jacobian matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacobian_matrix_and_determinant "Jacobian matrix and determinant") of ψ at _y_ = _γ_(_t_).

Now let {**e**_u_, **e**_v_} be an orthonormal basis in the coordinate directions of **R**2.[[note 3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stokes'_theorem#cite_note-orf2-13)

Recognizing that the columns of _J_**y**_**ψ**_ are precisely the partial derivatives of _**ψ**_ at **y**, we can expand the previous equation in coordinates as ![Image 38: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\oint _{\partial \Sigma }{\mathbf {F} (\mathbf {x} )\cdot \,\mathrm {d} \mathbf {\Gamma } }&=\oint _{\gamma }{\mathbf {F} ({\boldsymbol {\psi }}(\mathbf {y} ))\cdot J_{\mathbf {y} }({\boldsymbol {\psi }})\mathbf {e} _{u}(\mathbf {e} _{u}\cdot \,\mathrm {d} \mathbf {y} )+\mathbf {F} ({\boldsymbol {\psi }}(\mathbf {y} ))\cdot J_{\mathbf {y} }({\boldsymbol {\psi }})\mathbf {e} _{v}(\mathbf {e} _{v}\cdot \,\mathrm {d} \mathbf {y} )}\\&=\oint _{\gamma }{\left(\left(\mathbf {F} ({\boldsymbol {\psi }}(\mathbf {y} ))\cdot {\frac {\partial {\boldsymbol {\psi }}}{\partial u}}(\mathbf {y} )\right)\mathbf {e} _{u}+\left(\mathbf {F} ({\boldsymbol {\psi }}(\mathbf {y} ))\cdot {\frac {\partial {\boldsymbol {\psi }}}{\partial v}}(\mathbf {y} )\right)\mathbf {e} _{v}\right)\cdot \,\mathrm {d} \mathbf {y} }\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7d9b6044d71d07d39563ee2c63a59cd373e5ff64)

#### Second step in the elementary proof (defining the pullback)

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Stokes%27_theorem&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Second step in the elementary proof (defining the pullback)")]

The previous step suggests we define the function ![Image 39: {\displaystyle \mathbf {P} (u,v)=\left(\mathbf {F} ({\boldsymbol {\psi }}(u,v))\cdot {\frac {\partial {\boldsymbol {\psi }}}{\partial u}}(u,v)\right)\mathbf {e} _{u}+\left(\mathbf {F} ({\boldsymbol {\psi }}(u,v))\cdot {\frac {\partial {\boldsymbol {\psi }}}{\partial v}}(u,v)\right)\mathbf {e} _{v}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/53fe1894fe8c6a9af96c6b6f19b1f8d7fdae8d88)

Now, if the scalar value functions ![Image 40: {\displaystyle P_{u}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ddff09f5647bf3a8e9f36dd56d07aa1c4b95a1b0) and ![Image 41: {\displaystyle P_{v}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a5ca3e8cb08e2700ccc8b6988533df691de0761) are defined as follows, ![Image 42: {\displaystyle {P_{u}}(u,v)=\left(\mathbf {F} ({\boldsymbol {\psi }}(u,v))\cdot {\frac {\partial {\boldsymbol {\psi }}}{\partial u}}(u,v)\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a0619304a6787f528c7e203be89a67ce2c9baf48)![Image 43: {\displaystyle {P_{v}}(u,v)=\left(\mathbf {F} ({\boldsymbol {\psi }}(u,v))\cdot {\frac {\partial {\boldsymbol {\psi }}}{\partial v}}(u,v)\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/00e2ef1e85eba2f865ec2acf5c7ebd4ae56ab3b8) then, ![Image 44: {\displaystyle \mathbf {P} (u,v)={P_{u}}(u,v)\mathbf {e} _{u}+{P_{v}}(u,v)\mathbf {e} _{v}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54b851c7a884ef27b2ce5e838b39baa263a6db49)

This is the [pullback](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pullback_(differential_geometry) "Pullback (differential geometry)") of **F** along _**ψ**_, and, by the above, it satisfies ![Image 45: {\displaystyle \oint _{\partial \Sigma }{\mathbf {F} (\mathbf {x} )\cdot \,\mathrm {d} \mathbf {l} }=\oint _{\gamma }{\mathbf {P} (\mathbf {y} )\cdot \,\mathrm {d} \mathbf {l} }=\oint _{\gamma }{({P_{u}}(u,v)\mathbf {e} _{u}+{P_{v}}(u,v)\mathbf {e} _{v})\cdot \,\mathrm {d} \mathbf {l} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3cfb17521b7f2e725a7d08433f5b6c80afc6166d)

We have successfully reduced one side of Stokes' theorem to a 2-dimensional formula; we now turn to the other side.

#### Third step of the elementary proof (second equation)

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Stokes%27_theorem&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Third step of the elementary proof (second equation)")]

First, calculate the partial derivatives appearing in [Green's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green%27s_theorem "Green's theorem"), via the [product rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Leibniz_rule "General Leibniz rule"): ![Image 46: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}{\frac {\partial P_{u}}{\partial v}}&={\frac {\partial (\mathbf {F} \circ {\boldsymbol {\psi }})}{\partial v}}\cdot {\frac {\partial {\boldsymbol {\psi }}}{\partial u}}+(\mathbf {F} \circ {\boldsymbol {\psi }})\cdot {\frac {\partial ^{2}{\boldsymbol {\psi }}}{\partial v\,\partial u}}\\[5pt]{\frac {\partial P_{v}}{\partial u}}&={\frac {\partial (\mathbf {F} \circ {\boldsymbol {\psi }})}{\partial u}}\cdot {\frac {\partial {\boldsymbol {\psi }}}{\partial v}}+(\mathbf {F} \circ {\boldsymbol {\psi }})\cdot {\frac {\partial ^{2}{\boldsymbol {\psi }}}{\partial u\,\partial v}}\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f51dcdb39953b46773127bb038799e610b8672eb)

Conveniently, the second term vanishes in the difference, by [equality of mixed partials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equality_of_mixed_partials "Equality of mixed partials"). So,[[note 4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stokes'_theorem#cite_note-naiseki-14)![Image 47: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}{\frac {\partial P_{v}}{\partial u}}-{\frac {\partial P_{u}}{\partial v}}&={\frac {\partial (\mathbf {F} \circ {\boldsymbol {\psi }})}{\partial u}}\cdot {\frac {\partial {\boldsymbol {\psi }}}{\partial v}}-{\frac {\partial (\mathbf {F} \circ {\boldsymbol {\psi }})}{\partial v}}\cdot {\frac {\partial {\boldsymbol {\psi }}}{\partial u}}\\[5pt]&={\frac {\partial {\boldsymbol {\psi }}}{\partial v}}\cdot (J_{{\boldsymbol {\psi }}(u,v)}\mathbf {F} ){\frac {\partial {\boldsymbol {\psi }}}{\partial u}}-{\frac {\partial {\boldsymbol {\psi }}}{\partial u}}\cdot (J_{{\boldsymbol {\psi }}(u,v)}\mathbf {F} ){\frac {\partial {\boldsymbol {\psi }}}{\partial v}}&&{\text{(chain rule)}}\\[5pt]&={\frac {\partial {\boldsymbol {\psi }}}{\partial v}}\cdot \left(J_{{\boldsymbol {\psi }}(u,v)}\mathbf {F} -{(J_{{\boldsymbol {\psi }}(u,v)}\mathbf {F} )}^{\mathsf {T}}\right){\frac {\partial {\boldsymbol {\psi }}}{\partial u}}\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5aaaa6a4a22c19ada2efe9544e29878feaa55378)

But now consider the matrix in that quadratic form—that is, ![Image 48: {\displaystyle J_{{\boldsymbol {\psi }}(u,v)}\mathbf {F} -(J_{{\boldsymbol {\psi }}(u,v)}\mathbf {F} )^{\mathsf {T}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ef4cd95ddfb3d25550e19b94b82dbba997ce37b0). We claim this matrix in fact describes a cross product. Here the superscript "![Image 49: {\displaystyle {}^{\mathsf {T}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/de0c33e315a8e2cbf2daec6394ccc13017d41fd1)" represents the [transposition of matrices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transpose "Transpose").

To be precise, let ![Image 50: {\displaystyle A=(A_{ij})_{ij}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d1bb64b99ddd408ee9ca72fed64aef27bcaf151a) be an arbitrary 3 × 3 matrix and let ![Image 51: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} ={\begin{bmatrix}a_{1}\\a_{2}\\a_{3}\end{bmatrix}}={\begin{bmatrix}A_{32}-A_{23}\\A_{13}-A_{31}\\A_{21}-A_{12}\end{bmatrix}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1dfa65c332dc32feee0556a801af696986fe5ffa)

Note that **x** ↦ **a** × **x** is linear, so it is determined by its action on basis elements. But by direct calculation ![Image 52: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\left(A-A^{\mathsf {T}}\right)\mathbf {e} _{1}&={\begin{bmatrix}0\\a_{3}\\-a_{2}\end{bmatrix}}=\mathbf {a} \times \mathbf {e} _{1}\\\left(A-A^{\mathsf {T}}\right)\mathbf {e} _{2}&={\begin{bmatrix}-a_{3}\\0\\a_{1}\end{bmatrix}}=\mathbf {a} \times \mathbf {e} _{2}\\\left(A-A^{\mathsf {T}}\right)\mathbf {e} _{3}&={\begin{bmatrix}a_{2}\\-a_{1}\\0\end{bmatrix}}=\mathbf {a} \times \mathbf {e} _{3}\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/669bc58a7cdca60b1b58eeb0fff211675abdbce2) Here, {**e**_1_, **e**_2_, **e**_3_} represents an orthonormal basis in the coordinate directions of ![Image 53: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5).[[note 5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stokes'_theorem#cite_note-orf3-15)

Thus (_A_ − _A_ T)**x** = **a** × **x** for any **x**.

Substituting ![Image 54: {\displaystyle {(J_{{\boldsymbol {\psi }}(u,v)}\mathbf {F} )}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f6652a1b2a6f6cea6ad98c9cfe1992839487748f) for A, we obtain ![Image 55: {\displaystyle \left({(J_{{\boldsymbol {\psi }}(u,v)}\mathbf {F} )}-{(J_{{\boldsymbol {\psi }}(u,v)}\mathbf {F} )}^{\mathsf {T}}\right)\mathbf {x} =(\nabla \times \mathbf {F} )\times \mathbf {x} ,\quad {\text{for all}}\,\mathbf {x} \in \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a114f389df6050193c250e82679d6bddea4ae3a7)

We can now recognize the difference of partials as a [(scalar) triple product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triple_product "Triple product"): ![Image 56: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}{\frac {\partial P_{v}}{\partial u}}-{\frac {\partial P_{u}}{\partial v}}&={\frac {\partial {\boldsymbol {\psi }}}{\partial v}}\cdot (\nabla \times \mathbf {F} )\times {\frac {\partial {\boldsymbol {\psi }}}{\partial u}}=(\nabla \times \mathbf {F} )\cdot {\frac {\partial {\boldsymbol {\psi }}}{\partial u}}\times {\frac {\partial {\boldsymbol {\psi }}}{\partial v}}\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4ddf1375517db32016285d678d65cf71e8b65cbe)

On the other hand, the definition of a [surface integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_integral "Surface integral") also includes a triple product—the very same one! ![Image 57: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\iint _{\Sigma }(\nabla \times \mathbf {F} )\cdot \,d\mathbf {\Sigma } &=\iint _{D}{(\nabla \times \mathbf {F} )({\boldsymbol {\psi }}(u,v))\cdot {\frac {\partial {\boldsymbol {\psi }}}{\partial u}}(u,v)\times {\frac {\partial {\boldsymbol {\psi }}}{\partial v}}(u,v)\,\mathrm {d} u\,\mathrm {d} v}\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7be21dad22c3cef7c9bcead1c59346d5f40a1cc8)

So, we obtain ![Image 58: {\displaystyle \iint _{\Sigma }(\nabla \times \mathbf {F} )\cdot \,\mathrm {d} \mathbf {\Sigma } =\iint _{D}\left({\frac {\partial P_{v}}{\partial u}}-{\frac {\partial P_{u}}{\partial v}}\right)\,\mathrm {d} u\,\mathrm {d} v}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/afe72669c261928c970191762732862aa9de32c9)

#### Fourth step of the elementary proof (reduction to Green's theorem)

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Stokes%27_theorem&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Fourth step of the elementary proof (reduction to Green's theorem)")]

Combining the second and third steps and then applying [Green's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green%27s_theorem "Green's theorem") completes the proof. Green's theorem asserts the following: for any region D bounded by the Jordans closed curve γ and two scalar-valued smooth functions ![Image 59: {\displaystyle P_{u}(u,v),P_{v}(u,v)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e606c2f39cd5cc473a022790d807057c538f5328) defined on D;

![Image 60: {\displaystyle \oint _{\gamma }{({P_{u}}(u,v)\mathbf {e} _{u}+{P_{v}}(u,v)\mathbf {e} _{v})\cdot \,\mathrm {d} \mathbf {l} }=\iint _{D}\left({\frac {\partial P_{v}}{\partial u}}-{\frac {\partial P_{u}}{\partial v}}\right)\,\mathrm {d} u\,\mathrm {d} v}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ebdcd243c647fe864c1d3cacc0a137f4e589aa6f)

We can substitute the conclusion of STEP2 into the left-hand side of Green's theorem above, and substitute the conclusion of STEP3 into the right-hand side. [Q.E.D.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Q.E.D. "Q.E.D.")

### Irrotational fields

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Stokes%27_theorem&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Irrotational fields")]

In this section, we will discuss the irrotational field ([lamellar vector field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lamellar_vector_field "Lamellar vector field")) based on Stokes' theorem.

**Definition 2-1 (irrotational field).** A smooth vector field **F** on an [open](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_set "Open set")![Image 67: {\displaystyle U\subseteq \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e49d1f1833ec676f13374b867fdb0af643877203) is _irrotational_ ([lamellar vector field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lamellar_vector_field "Lamellar vector field")) if ∇ × **F** = 0.

This concept is very fundamental in mechanics; as we'll prove later, if **F** is _irrotational_ and the domain of **F** is [simply connected](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simply_connected "Simply connected"), then **F** is a [conservative vector field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservative_vector_field "Conservative vector field").

#### Helmholtz's theorem

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Stokes%27_theorem&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Helmholtz's theorem")]

In this section, we will introduce a theorem that is derived from Stokes' theorem and characterizes vortex-free vector fields. In classical mechanics and fluid dynamics it is called [Helmholtz's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helmholtz_theorem_(classical_mechanics) "Helmholtz theorem (classical mechanics)").

**Theorem 2-1 (Helmholtz's theorem in fluid dynamics).**[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stokes'_theorem#cite_note-DTPO-5)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stokes'_theorem#cite_note-fujimno-3): 142  Let ![Image 68: {\displaystyle U\subseteq \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e49d1f1833ec676f13374b867fdb0af643877203) be an [open](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_set "Open set")[subset](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subset "Subset") with a lamellar vector field **F** and let _c_ 0, _c_ 1: [0, 1] → _U_ be piecewise smooth loops. If there is a function _H_: [0, 1] × [0, 1] → _U_ such that

*   **[TLH0]**H is piecewise smooth,
*   **[TLH1]**_H_(_t_, 0) = _c_ 0(_t_) for all _t_ ∈ [0, 1],
*   **[TLH2]**_H_(_t_, 1) = _c_ 1(_t_) for all _t_ ∈ [0, 1],
*   **[TLH3]**_H_(0, _s_) = _H_(1, _s_) for all _s_ ∈ [0, 1].

Then, ![Image 69: {\displaystyle \int _{c_{0}}\mathbf {F} \,\mathrm {d} c_{0}=\int _{c_{1}}\mathbf {F} \,\mathrm {d} c_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d3f7f7e3422c8978f976a292ae8f02f30c02a45)

Some textbooks such as Lawrence[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stokes'_theorem#cite_note-DTPO-5) call the relationship between _c_ 0 and _c_ 1 stated in theorem 2-1 as "homotopic" and the function _H_: [0, 1] × [0, 1] → _U_ as "homotopy between _c_ 0 and _c_ 1". However, "homotopic" or "homotopy" in above-mentioned sense are different (stronger than) [typical definitions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homotopy "Homotopy") of "homotopic" or "homotopy"; the latter omit condition [TLH3]. So from now on we refer to homotopy (homotope) in the sense of theorem 2-1 as a _tubular homotopy (resp. tubular-homotopic)_.[[note 6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stokes'_theorem#cite_note-TLH-17)

##### Proof of Helmholtz's theorem

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Stokes%27_theorem&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Proof of Helmholtz's theorem")]

[![Image 70](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Domain_of_singular_2_cube.svg/250px-Domain_of_singular_2_cube.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Domain_of_singular_2_cube.svg)

The definitions of _γ_ 1, ..., _γ_ 4

In what follows, we [abuse notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abuse_of_notation "Abuse of notation") and use "![Image 71: {\displaystyle \oplus }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8b16e2bdaefee9eed86d866e6eba3ac47c710f60)" for concatenation of paths in the [fundamental groupoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_groupoid "Fundamental groupoid") and "![Image 72: {\displaystyle \ominus }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8a5d20fe7e97d90a5d1afd1054c89385364be553)" for reversing the orientation of a path.

Let _D_ = [0, 1] × [0, 1], and split ∂_D_ into four line segments _γ_ _j_. ![Image 73: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\gamma _{1}:[0,1]\to D;\quad &\gamma _{1}(t)=(t,0)\\\gamma _{2}:[0,1]\to D;\quad &\gamma _{2}(s)=(1,s)\\\gamma _{3}:[0,1]\to D;\quad &\gamma _{3}(t)=(1-t,1)\\\gamma _{4}:[0,1]\to D;\quad &\gamma _{4}(s)=(0,1-s)\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b440cc87d1805ebd2adafff5286e09cd7aae8677) so that ![Image 74: {\displaystyle \partial D=\gamma _{1}\oplus \gamma _{2}\oplus \gamma _{3}\oplus \gamma _{4}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f39b6f033108fd389ab59978d51089463733af44)

By our assumption that _c_ 0 and _c_ 1 are piecewise smooth homotopic, there is a piecewise smooth homotopy _H_: _D_ → _M_![Image 75: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\Gamma _{i}(t)&=H(\gamma _{i}(t))&&i=1,2,3,4\\\Gamma (t)&=H(\gamma (t))=(\Gamma _{1}\oplus \Gamma _{2}\oplus \Gamma _{3}\oplus \Gamma _{4})(t)\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/be9c22d2bee180bcee3fceccec6aa86792cd5d95)

Let S be the image of D under H. That ![Image 76: {\displaystyle \iint _{S}\nabla \times \mathbf {F} \,\mathrm {d} S=\oint _{\Gamma }\mathbf {F} \,\mathrm {d} \Gamma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/48c4db5ffe2d4107078db02115370a90619356be) follows immediately from Stokes' theorem. **F** is lamellar, so the left side vanishes, i.e. ![Image 77: {\displaystyle 0=\oint _{\Gamma }\mathbf {F} \,\mathrm {d} \Gamma =\sum _{i=1}^{4}\oint _{\Gamma _{i}}\mathbf {F} \,\mathrm {d} \Gamma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fc80f33cb1b0126c1d4df61fec0cde748de314ce)

As H is tubular(satisfying [TLH3]),![Image 78: {\displaystyle \Gamma _{2}=\ominus \Gamma _{4}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b2dd48f4b95e72c2e7177b15dc49c97bb372e1c) and ![Image 79: {\displaystyle \Gamma _{2}=\ominus \Gamma _{4}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b2dd48f4b95e72c2e7177b15dc49c97bb372e1c). Thus the line integrals along Γ 2(_s_) and Γ 4(_s_) cancel, leaving ![Image 80: {\displaystyle 0=\oint _{\Gamma _{1}}\mathbf {F} \,\mathrm {d} \Gamma +\oint _{\Gamma _{3}}\mathbf {F} \,\mathrm {d} \Gamma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7bbcafcefb522726a597626452a6949b5f9af4bc)

On the other hand, _c_ 1 = Γ 1, ![Image 81: {\displaystyle c_{3}=\ominus \Gamma _{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/13d448303a4e42fa67021a88672085400c54b4fa), so that the desired equality follows almost immediately.

### Conservative forces

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Stokes%27_theorem&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Conservative forces")]

Above Helmholtz's theorem gives an explanation as to why the work done by a conservative force in changing an object's position is path independent. First, we introduce the Lemma 2-2, which is a corollary of and a special case of Helmholtz's theorem.

**Lemma 2-2.**[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stokes'_theorem#cite_note-DTPO-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stokes'_theorem#cite_note-lee-6) Let ![Image 82: {\displaystyle U\subseteq \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e49d1f1833ec676f13374b867fdb0af643877203) be an [open](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_set "Open set")[subset](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subset "Subset"), with a Lamellar vector field **F** and a piecewise smooth loop _c_ 0: [0, 1] → _U_. Fix a point **p** ∈ _U_, if there is a homotopy _H_: [0, 1] × [0, 1] → _U_ such that

*   **[SC0]**H is _piecewise smooth_,
*   **[SC1]**_H_(_t_, 0) = _c_ 0(_t_) for all _t_ ∈ [0, 1],
*   **[SC2]**_H_(_t_, 1) = **p** for all _t_ ∈ [0, 1],
*   **[SC3]**_H_(0, _s_) = _H_(1, _s_) = **p** for all _s_ ∈ [0, 1].

Then, ![Image 83: {\displaystyle \int _{c_{0}}\mathbf {F} \,\mathrm {d} c_{0}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3e0fd704f18ab41163401bdc9d90cc1dd94e2e9a)

Above Lemma 2-2 follows from theorem 2–1. In Lemma 2-2, the existence of H satisfying [SC0] to [SC3] is crucial;the question is whether such a homotopy can be taken for arbitrary loops. If U is simply connected, such H exists. The definition of [simply connected space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simply_connected_space "Simply connected space") follows:

**Definition 2-2 (simply connected space).**[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stokes'_theorem#cite_note-DTPO-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stokes'_theorem#cite_note-lee-6) Let ![Image 84: {\displaystyle M\subseteq \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/07a641533ae6e2f2cd1a4da05e0721ade2b47eba) be non-empty and [path-connected](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connected_space#Path_connectedness "Connected space"). M is called [simply connected](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simply_connected "Simply connected") if and only if for any continuous loop, _c_: [0, 1] → _M_ there exists a continuous tubular homotopy _H_: [0, 1] × [0, 1] → _M_ from c to a fixed point _p_ ∈ _c_; that is,

*   **[SC0']**H is _continuous_,
*   **[SC1]**_H_(_t_, 0) = _c_(_t_) for all _t_ ∈ [0, 1],
*   **[SC2]**_H_(_t_, 1) = **p** for all _t_ ∈ [0, 1],
*   **[SC3]**_H_(0, _s_) = _H_(1, _s_) = **p** for all _s_ ∈ [0, 1].

The claim that "for a conservative force, the work done in changing an object's position is path independent" might seem to follow immediately if the M is simply connected. However, recall that simple-connection only guarantees the existence of a _continuous_ homotopy satisfying [SC1-3]; we seek a piecewise smooth homotopy satisfying those conditions instead.

Fortunately, the gap in regularity is resolved by the [Whitney's approximation theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Whitney%27s_approximation_theorem&action=edit&redlink=1 "Whitney's approximation theorem (page does not exist)").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stokes'_theorem#cite_note-lee-6): 136, 421 [[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stokes'_theorem#cite_note-ptr-18) In other words, the possibility of finding a continuous homotopy, but not being able to integrate over it, is actually eliminated with the benefit of higher mathematics. We thus obtain the following theorem.

**Theorem 2-2.**[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stokes'_theorem#cite_note-DTPO-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stokes'_theorem#cite_note-lee-6) Let ![Image 85: {\displaystyle U\subseteq \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e49d1f1833ec676f13374b867fdb0af643877203) be [open](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_set "Open set") and simply connected with an irrotational vector field **F**. For all piecewise smooth loops _c_: [0, 1] → _U_![Image 86: {\displaystyle \int _{c_{0}}\mathbf {F} \,\mathrm {d} c_{0}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3e0fd704f18ab41163401bdc9d90cc1dd94e2e9a)

6.   **[^](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stokes'_theorem#cite_ref-TLH_17-0)**There do exist textbooks that use the terms "homotopy" and "homotopic" in the sense of Theorem 2-1.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stokes'_theorem#cite_note-DTPO-5) Indeed, this is very convenient _for the specific problem_ of conservative forces. However, both uses of homotopy appear sufficiently frequently that some sort of terminology is necessary to disambiguate, and the term "tubular homotopy" adopted here serves well enough for that end.
