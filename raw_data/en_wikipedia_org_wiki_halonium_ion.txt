From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Any onium ion containing a halogen atom carrying a positive charge

[![Image 5](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Cyclopentene-bromonium-3D-balls.png/250px-Cyclopentene-bromonium-3D-balls.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Cyclopentene-bromonium-3D-balls.png)

A [ball-and-stick model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ball-and-stick_model "Ball-and-stick model") of a bromonium ion formed from [cyclopentene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclopentene "Cyclopentene")

A **halonium ion** is any [onium ion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Onium_ion "Onium ion") containing a [halogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halogen "Halogen") atom carrying a positive charge. This [cation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cation "Cation") has the general structure R−+X−R′ where X is any halogen and no restrictions on R,[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halonium_ion#cite_note-Goldbook-1) this structure can be cyclic or an open chain molecular structure. Halonium ions formed from [fluorine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluorine "Fluorine"), [chlorine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chlorine "Chlorine"), [bromine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bromine "Bromine"), and [iodine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iodine "Iodine") are called **[fluoronium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluoronium "Fluoronium")**, **chloronium**, **bromonium**, and **iodonium**, respectively.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halonium_ion#cite_note-Goldbook-1) The 3-membered cyclic variety commonly proposed as intermediates in electrophilic halogenation may be called haliranium ions, using the [Hantzsch-Widman nomenclature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hantzsch%E2%80%93Widman_nomenclature "Hantzsch–Widman nomenclature") system.

Structure
---------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Halonium_ion&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Structure")]

The simplest halonium ions are of the structure H−+X−H (X = F, Cl, Br, I). Many halonium ions have a three-atom cyclic structure, similar to that of an [epoxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epoxide "Epoxide"), resulting from the formal addition of a halogenium ion X+ to a C=C [double bond](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double_bond "Double bond"), as when a halogen is added to an [alkene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkene "Alkene").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halonium_ion#cite_note-Goldbook-1) The formation of 5-membered halonium ions (e.g., chlorolanium, bromolanium ions) via [neighboring group participation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neighbouring_group_participation "Neighbouring group participation") is also well studied.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halonium_ion#cite_note-2)

Diaryliodonium ions ([Ar 2 I]+X−) are generally stable, isolable salts which exhibit a T-shaped geometry with the aryl groups at ~90 degrees apart;[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halonium_ion#cite_note-3) for more details, see [hypervalent iodine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypervalent_iodine "Hypervalent iodine").

The tendency to form bridging halonium ions is in the order I > Br > Cl > F. Whereas iodine and bromine readily form bridged iodonium and bromonium ions, fluoronium ions have only recently been characterized in designed systems that force close encounter of the fluorine lone pair and a carbocationic center. In practice, structurally, there is a continuum between a symmetrically bridged halonium, to an unsymmetrical halonium with a long weak bond to one of the carbon centers, to a true β-halocarbocation with no halonium character. The equilibrium structure depends on the ability of the carbon atoms and the halogen to accommodate positive charge. Thus, a bromonium ion that bridges a primary and tertiary carbon will often exhibit a skewed structure, with a weak bond to the tertiary center (with significant carbocation character) and stronger bond to the primary carbon. This is due to the increased stability of tertiary carbons to stabilize positive charge. In the more extreme case, if the tertiary center is doubly benzylic for instance, then the open form may be favored. Similarly, switching from bromine to chlorine also weakens bridging character, due to the higher electronegativity of chlorine and lower propensity to share electron density compared to bromine.

Reactivity
----------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Halonium_ion&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Reactivity")]

These ions are usually only short-lived [reaction intermediates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reaction_intermediate "Reaction intermediate"); they are very reactive, owing to high [ring strain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ring_strain "Ring strain") in the three-membered ring and the positive charge on the halogen; this positive charge makes them great [electrophiles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrophile "Electrophile"). In almost all cases, the halonium ion is attacked by a [nucleophile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleophile "Nucleophile") within a very short time. Even a weak nucleophile, such as [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Properties_of_water "Properties of water") will attack the halonium ion; this is how [halohydrins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halohydrin "Halohydrin") can be made.

On occasion, a halonium atom will rearrange to a [carbocation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbocation "Carbocation"). This usually occurs only when that carbocation is an allylic or a benzylic carbocation.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halonium_ion#cite_note-4)

History
-------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Halonium_ion&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: History")]

Halonium ions were first postulated in 1937 by Roberts and Kimball[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halonium_ion#cite_note-5) to account for observed _anti_[diastereoselectivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diastereoselectivity "Diastereoselectivity") in [halogen addition reactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halogen_addition_reaction "Halogen addition reaction") to [alkenes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkene "Alkene"). They correctly argued that if the initial reaction intermediate in bromination is the open-chain X–C–C+ species, rotation around the C–C [single bond](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Single_bond "Single bond") would be possible leading to a mixture of equal amounts of dihalogen [_syn_ isomer and _anti_ isomer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syn_and_anti_addition "Syn and anti addition"), which is not the case. They also asserted that a positively charged halogen atom is [isoelectronic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isoelectronic "Isoelectronic") with oxygen and that carbon and bromine have comparable [ionization potentials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ionization_potential "Ionization potential"). For certain aryl substituted alkenes, the _anti_ stereospecificity is diminished or lost, as a result of weakened or absent halonium character in the cationic intermediate.

In 1970 [George A. Olah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_A._Olah "George A. Olah") succeeded in preparing and isolating halonium [salts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salt_(chemistry) "Salt (chemistry)")[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halonium_ion#cite_note-6) by adding a [methyl halide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methyl_halide "Methyl halide") such as [methyl bromide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methyl_bromide "Methyl bromide") or [methyl chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methyl_chloride "Methyl chloride") in [sulfur dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide "Sulfur dioxide") at −78°C to a complex of [antimony pentafluoride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antimony_pentafluoride "Antimony pentafluoride") and [tetrafluoromethane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrafluoromethane "Tetrafluoromethane") in sulfur dioxide. After evaporation of [sulfur dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide "Sulfur dioxide") this procedure left crystals of [H 3 C–+X–CH 3][SbF 6]–, stable at [room temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Room_temperature "Room temperature") but not to moisture. A fluoronium ion was recently characterized in solution phase (dissolved in sulfur dioxide or [sulfuryl chloride fluoride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfuryl_chloride_fluoride "Sulfuryl chloride fluoride")) at low temperature.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halonium_ion#cite_note-7)

Cyclic and acyclic chloronium,[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halonium_ion#cite_note-8) bromonium, and iodonium ions have been structurally characterised by [X-ray crystallography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray_crystallography "X-ray crystallography"), such as the bi(adamantylidene)-derived bromonium cation shown below.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halonium_ion#cite_note-9)

Compounds containing trivalent or tetravalent halonium ions do not exist but for some hypothetical compounds stability has been computationally tested.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halonium_ion#cite_note-10)
