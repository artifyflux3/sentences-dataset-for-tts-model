In [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics"), **relativistic quantum mechanics** (**RQM**) is any [Poincaré](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poincar%C3%A9_group "Poincaré group")-[covariant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principle_of_covariance "Principle of covariance") formulation of [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics") (QM). This theory is applicable to [massive particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Massive_particle "Massive particle") propagating at all [velocities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity "Velocity") up to those comparable to the [speed of light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light "Speed of light")_c_, and can accommodate [massless particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Massless_particle "Massless particle"). The theory has application in [high-energy physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-energy_physics "High-energy physics"),[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-1)[particle physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_physics "Particle physics") and [accelerator physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerator_physics "Accelerator physics"),[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-Martin,_Shaw,_p_3-2) as well as [atomic physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_physics "Atomic physics"), [chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemistry "Chemistry")[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-3) and [condensed matter physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics "Condensed matter physics").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-5)_Non-relativistic quantum mechanics_ refers to the [mathematical formulation of quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_formulation_of_quantum_mechanics "Mathematical formulation of quantum mechanics") applied in the context of [Galilean relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galilean_relativity "Galilean relativity"), more specifically quantizing the equations of [classical mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_mechanics "Classical mechanics") by replacing dynamical variables by [operators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operator_(physics) "Operator (physics)"). _Relativistic quantum mechanics_ (RQM) is quantum mechanics applied with [special relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_relativity "Special relativity"). Although the earlier formulations, like the [Schrödinger picture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schr%C3%B6dinger_picture "Schrödinger picture") and [Heisenberg picture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heisenberg_picture "Heisenberg picture") were originally formulated in a non-relativistic background, a few of them (e.g. the Dirac or path-integral formalism) also work with special relativity.

Key features common to all RQMs include: the prediction of [antimatter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antimatter "Antimatter"), [spin magnetic moments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_magnetic_moment "Spin magnetic moment") of [elementary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementary_particle "Elementary particle")[spin-1/2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin-1/2 "Spin-1/2")[fermions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermion "Fermion"), [fine structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fine_structure "Fine structure"), and quantum dynamics of [charged particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charged_particle "Charged particle") in [electromagnetic fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_field "Electromagnetic field").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-Martin,_Shaw,_pp._5%E2%80%936-6) The key result is the [Dirac equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dirac_equation "Dirac equation"), from which these predictions emerge automatically. By contrast, in non-relativistic quantum mechanics, terms have to be introduced artificially into the [Hamiltonian operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamiltonian_operator "Hamiltonian operator") to achieve agreement with experimental observations.

The most successful (and most widely used) RQM is _relativistic [quantum field theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum\_field\_theory "Quantum field theory")_ (QFT), in which elementary particles are interpreted as _field quanta_. A unique consequence of QFT that has been tested against other RQMs is the failure of conservation of particle number, for example, in [matter creation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matter_creation "Matter creation") and [annihilation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Annihilation "Annihilation").[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-7)

[Paul Dirac](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Dirac "Paul Dirac")'s work between 1927 and 1933 shaped the synthesis of special relativity and quantum mechanics.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-8) His work was instrumental, as he formulated the Dirac equation and also originated [quantum electrodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_electrodynamics "Quantum electrodynamics"), both of which were successful in combining the two theories.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-:5-9)

In this article, the equations are written in familiar 3D [vector calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_calculus "Vector calculus") notation and use hats for [operators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operator_(physics) "Operator (physics)") (not necessarily in the literature), and where space and time components can be collected, [tensor index notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_index_notation "Tensor index notation") is shown also (frequently used in the literature), in addition the [Einstein summation convention](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_notation "Einstein notation") is used. [SI units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SI_units "SI units") are used here; [Gaussian units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaussian_units "Gaussian units") and [natural units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_units "Natural units") are common alternatives. All equations are in the position representation; for the momentum representation the equations have to be [Fourier-transformed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourier_transform "Fourier transform")– see [position and momentum space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Position_and_momentum_space "Position and momentum space").

Combining special relativity and quantum mechanics
--------------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Relativistic_quantum_mechanics&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Combining special relativity and quantum mechanics")]

One approach is to modify the Schrödinger picture to be consistent with special relativity.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-Martin,_Shaw,_p_3-2)

A [postulate of quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_formulation_of_quantum_mechanics "Mathematical formulation of quantum mechanics") is that the [time evolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_evolution "Time evolution") of any quantum system is given by the [Schrödinger equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schr%C3%B6dinger_equation "Schrödinger equation"):

![Image 1: {\displaystyle i\hbar {\frac {\partial }{\partial t}}\psi ={\hat {H}}\psi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d7393923eac53291ede3fa210a57318731a3f8f4)
using a suitable [Hamiltonian operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamiltonian_operator "Hamiltonian operator")_Ĥ_ corresponding to the system. The solution is a [complex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number")-valued [wavefunction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wavefunction "Wavefunction")_ψ_(**r**, _t_), a [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)") of the [3D](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_space "Three-dimensional space")[position vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Position_vector "Position vector")**r** of the particle at time _t_, describing the behavior of the system.

Every particle has a non-negative [spin quantum number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_quantum_number "Spin quantum number")_s_. The number 2 _s_ is an integer, odd for [fermions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermion "Fermion") and even for [bosons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boson "Boson"). Each _s_ has 2 _s_ + 1 _z_-projection quantum numbers; _σ_=_s_, _s_−1, ... , −_s_+1, −_s_.[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-10) This is an additional discrete variable the wavefunction requires; _ψ_(**r**,_t_,_σ_).

Historically, in the early 1920s [Pauli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Pauli "Wolfgang Pauli"), [Kronig](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ralph_Kronig "Ralph Kronig"), [Uhlenbeck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_Uhlenbeck "George Uhlenbeck") and [Goudsmit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samuel_Goudsmit "Samuel Goudsmit") were the first to propose the concept of spin. The inclusion of spin in the wavefunction incorporates the [Pauli exclusion principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pauli_exclusion_principle "Pauli exclusion principle") (1925) and the more general [spin–statistics theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin%E2%80%93statistics_theorem "Spin–statistics theorem") (1939) due to [Fierz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Markus_Fierz "Markus Fierz"), rederived by Pauli a year later. This is the explanation for a diverse range of [subatomic particle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subatomic_particle "Subatomic particle") behavior and phenomena: from the [electronic configurations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronic_configuration "Electronic configuration") of atoms, nuclei (and therefore all [elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_element "Chemical element") on the [periodic table](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Periodic_table "Periodic table") and their [chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemistry "Chemistry")), to the quark configurations and [colour charge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colour_charge "Colour charge") (hence the properties of [baryons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baryon "Baryon") and [mesons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meson "Meson")).

A fundamental prediction of special relativity is the relativistic [energy–momentum relation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy%E2%80%93momentum_relation "Energy–momentum relation"); for a particle of [rest mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rest_mass "Rest mass")_m_, and in a particular [frame of reference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frame_of_reference "Frame of reference") with [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy "Energy")_E_ and 3-[momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum "Momentum")**p** with [magnitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norm_(mathematics) "Norm (mathematics)") in terms of the [dot product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dot_product "Dot product")![Image 2: {\displaystyle p={\sqrt {\mathbf {p} \cdot \mathbf {p} }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3b16974dd6e36500145d0889dba355a27cd85ffb), it is:[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-11)

![Image 3: {\displaystyle E^{2}=c^{2}\mathbf {p} \cdot \mathbf {p} +(mc^{2})^{2}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/13d24b23c27ea4433f4e6ab0452e7a75a5aecdc2)
These equations are used together with the [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_operator "Energy operator") and [momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum_operator "Momentum operator")[operators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operator_(physics)#Operators_in_quantum_mechanics "Operator (physics)"), which are respectively:

![Image 4: {\displaystyle {\hat {E}}=i\hbar {\frac {\partial }{\partial t}}\,,\quad {\hat {\mathbf {p} }}=-i\hbar \nabla \,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47d4158a68890b8fd4e5d7bc1b590adb4dd83627)
to construct a [relativistic wave equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_wave_equation "Relativistic wave equation") (RWE): a [partial differential equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_differential_equation "Partial differential equation") consistent with the energy–momentum relation, and is solved for _ψ_ to predict the quantum dynamics of the particle. For space and time to be placed on equal footing, as in relativity, the orders of space and time [partial derivatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_derivative "Partial derivative") should be equal, and ideally as low as possible, so that no initial values of the derivatives need to be specified. This is important for probability interpretations, exemplified below. The lowest possible order of any differential equation is the first (zeroth order derivatives would not form a differential equation).

The [Heisenberg picture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heisenberg_picture "Heisenberg picture") is another formulation of QM, in which case the wavefunction _ψ_ is _time-independent_, and the operators _A_(_t_) contain the time dependence, governed by the equation of motion:

![Image 5: {\displaystyle {\frac {d}{dt}}A={\frac {1}{i\hbar }}[A,{\hat {H}}]+{\frac {\partial }{\partial t}}A\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4edb0eae5f7fe5e5e4ffc93e2e75a1670385fdcf)
This equation is also true in RQM, provided the Heisenberg operators are modified to be consistent with SR.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-12)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-13)

Historically, around 1926, [Schrödinger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erwin_Schr%C3%B6dinger "Erwin Schrödinger") and [Heisenberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Werner_Heisenberg "Werner Heisenberg") show that wave mechanics and [matrix mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_mechanics "Matrix mechanics") are equivalent, later furthered by Dirac using [transformation theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transformation_theory_(quantum_mechanics) "Transformation theory (quantum mechanics)").

A more modern approach to RWEs, first introduced during the time RWEs were developing for particles of any spin, is to apply [representations of the Lorentz group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Representations_of_the_Lorentz_group "Representations of the Lorentz group").

In [classical mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_mechanics "Classical mechanics") and non-relativistic QM, time is an absolute quantity all observers and particles can always agree on, "ticking away" in the background independent of space. Thus in non-relativistic QM one has for a [many particle system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Many_particle_system "Many particle system")_ψ_(**r**1, **r**2, **r**3, ..., _t_, _σ_ 1, _σ_ 2, _σ_ 3...).

In [relativistic mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_mechanics "Relativistic mechanics"), the [spatial coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_system "Coordinate system") and [coordinate time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_time "Coordinate time") are _not_ absolute; any two observers moving relative to each other can measure different locations and times of [events](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Event_(relativity) "Event (relativity)"). The position and time coordinates combine naturally into a [four-dimensional spacetime position](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Four-vector#Four-position "Four-vector")**X** = (_ct_, **r**) corresponding to events, and the energy and 3-momentum combine naturally into the [four-momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Four-momentum "Four-momentum")**P** = (_E_/_c_, **p**) of a dynamic particle, as measured in _some_[reference frame](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frame_of_reference "Frame of reference"), change according to a [Lorentz transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentz_transformation "Lorentz transformation") as one measures in a different frame boosted and/or rotated relative the original frame in consideration. The derivative operators, and hence the energy and 3-momentum operators, are also non-invariant and change under Lorentz transformations.

Under a proper [orthochronous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthochronous "Orthochronous") Lorentz transformation (**r**, _t_) → Λ(**r**, _t_) in [Minkowski space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minkowski_space "Minkowski space"), all one-particle quantum states _ψ σ_ locally transform under some [representation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Representation_theory_of_the_Lorentz_group "Representation theory of the Lorentz group")_D_ of the [Lorentz group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentz_group "Lorentz group"):[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-Weinberg-14)[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-15)

![Image 6: {\displaystyle \psi _{\sigma }(\mathbf {r} ,t)\rightarrow D(\Lambda )\psi _{\sigma }(\Lambda ^{-1}(\mathbf {r} ,t))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2943021b5f4734c6f53997919747eb7a1bdc9fcf)
where _D_(Λ) is a finite-dimensional representation, in other words a (2 _s_ + 1)×(2 _s_ + 1)[square matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_matrix "Square matrix") . Again, _ψ_ is thought of as a [column vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Column_vector "Column vector") containing components with the (2 _s_ + 1) allowed values of _σ_. The [quantum numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_number "Quantum number")_s_ and _σ_ as well as other labels, continuous or discrete, representing other quantum numbers are suppressed. One value of _σ_ may occur more than once depending on the representation.

### Non-relativistic and relativistic Hamiltonians

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Relativistic_quantum_mechanics&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Non-relativistic and relativistic Hamiltonians")]

The [classical Hamiltonian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamiltonian_mechanics "Hamiltonian mechanics") for a particle in a [potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_potential "Scalar potential") is the [kinetic energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_energy "Kinetic energy")**p**·**p**/2 _m_ plus the [potential energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potential_energy "Potential energy")_V_(**r**, _t_), with the corresponding quantum operator in the [Schrödinger picture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schr%C3%B6dinger_picture "Schrödinger picture"):

![Image 7: {\displaystyle {\hat {H}}={\frac {{\hat {\mathbf {p} }}\cdot {\hat {\mathbf {p} }}}{2m}}+V(\mathbf {r} ,t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2ddbcd1cb5f72b3fc027d3a27147c8119e5e7e91)
and substituting this into the above Schrödinger equation gives a non-relativistic QM equation for the wavefunction: the procedure is a straightforward substitution of a simple expression. By contrast this is not as easy in RQM; the energy–momentum equation is quadratic in energy _and_ momentum leading to difficulties. Naively setting:

![Image 8: {\displaystyle {\hat {H}}={\hat {E}}={\sqrt {c^{2}{\hat {\mathbf {p} }}\cdot {\hat {\mathbf {p} }}+(mc^{2})^{2}}}\quad \Rightarrow \quad i\hbar {\frac {\partial }{\partial t}}\psi ={\sqrt {c^{2}{\hat {\mathbf {p} }}\cdot {\hat {\mathbf {p} }}+(mc^{2})^{2}}}\,\psi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81ab6a62e939c2b02ccb3b281b622f2612add60e)
is not helpful for several reasons. The square root of the operators cannot be used as it stands; it would have to be expanded in a [power series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_series "Power series") before the momentum operator, raised to a power in each term, could act on _ψ_. As a result of the power series, the space and time [derivatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative_(mathematics) "Derivative (mathematics)") are _completely asymmetric_: infinite-order in space derivatives but only first order in the time derivative, which is inelegant and unwieldy. Again, there is the problem of the non-invariance of the energy operator, equated to the square root which is also not invariant. Another problem, less obvious and more severe, is that it can be shown to be [nonlocal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_nonlocality "Quantum nonlocality") and can even _violate [causality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Causality\_(physics) "Causality (physics)")_: if the particle is initially localized at a point **r**0 so that _ψ_(**r**0, _t_ = 0) is finite and zero elsewhere, then at any later time the equation predicts delocalization _ψ_(**r**, _t_) ≠ 0 everywhere, even for |**r**| >_ct_ which means the particle could arrive at a point before a pulse of light could. This would have to be remedied by the additional constraint _ψ_(|**r**| >_ct_, _t_) = 0.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-Parker_1994-16)

There is also the problem of incorporating spin in the Hamiltonian, which isn't a prediction of the non-relativistic Schrödinger theory. Particles with spin have a corresponding spin magnetic moment quantized in units of _μ B_, the [Bohr magneton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bohr_magneton "Bohr magneton"):[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-17)[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-18)

![Image 9: {\displaystyle {\hat {\boldsymbol {\mu }}}_{S}=-{\frac {g\mu _{B}}{\hbar }}{\hat {\mathbf {S} }}\,,\quad \left|{\boldsymbol {\mu }}_{S}\right|=-g\mu _{B}\sigma \,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6a6908d4a2ed3cd00431596f1c35f03db91e6077)
where _g_ is the (spin) [g-factor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G-factor_(physics) "G-factor (physics)") for the particle, and **S** the [spin operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_operator "Spin operator"), so they interact with electromagnetic fields. For a particle in an externally applied [magnetic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_field "Magnetic field")**B**, the interaction term[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-Schuam_p_181-19)

![Image 10: {\displaystyle {\hat {H}}_{B}=-\mathbf {B} \cdot {\hat {\boldsymbol {\mu }}}_{S}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7a2c2688ae6a42ac6293544eb27e36bcbd451b7c)
has to be added to the above non-relativistic Hamiltonian. On the contrary; a relativistic Hamiltonian introduces spin _automatically_ as a requirement of enforcing the relativistic energy-momentum relation.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-20)

Relativistic Hamiltonians are analogous to those of non-relativistic QM in the following respect; there are terms including [rest mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rest_mass "Rest mass") and interaction terms with externally applied fields, similar to the classical potential energy term, as well as momentum terms like the classical kinetic energy term. A key difference is that relativistic Hamiltonians contain spin operators in the form of [matrices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_(mathematics) "Matrix (mathematics)"), in which the [matrix multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_multiplication "Matrix multiplication") runs over the spin index _σ_, so in general a relativistic Hamiltonian:

![Image 11: {\displaystyle {\hat {H}}={\hat {H}}(\mathbf {r} ,t,{\hat {\mathbf {p} }},{\hat {\mathbf {S} }})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5a17195f1627b9683fa5af34141c760e3d8f36ee)
is a function of space, time, and the momentum and spin operators.

### The Klein–Gordon and Dirac equations for free particles

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Relativistic_quantum_mechanics&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: The Klein–Gordon and Dirac equations for free particles")]

Substituting the energy and momentum operators directly into the energy–momentum relation may at first sight seem appealing, to obtain the [Klein–Gordon equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Klein%E2%80%93Gordon_equation "Klein–Gordon equation"):[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-21)

![Image 12: {\displaystyle {\hat {E}}^{2}\psi =c^{2}{\hat {\mathbf {p} }}\cdot {\hat {\mathbf {p} }}\psi +(mc^{2})^{2}\psi \,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/67be09a85b3999cfe74a6c8cbcc189c6103857b9)
and was discovered by many people because of the straightforward way of obtaining it, notably by Schrödinger in 1925 before he found the non-relativistic equation named after him, and by Klein and Gordon in 1927, who included electromagnetic interactions in the equation. This _is_[relativistically invariant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentz_covariance "Lorentz covariance"), yet this equation alone isn't a sufficient foundation for RQM for at least two reasons: one is that negative-energy states are solutions,[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-Martin,_Shaw,_p_3-2)[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-22) another is the density (given below), and this equation as it stands is only applicable to spinless particles. This equation can be factored into the form:[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-Penrose_2005,_p_620%E2%80%93621-23)[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-24)

![Image 13: {\displaystyle \left({\hat {E}}-c{\boldsymbol {\alpha }}\cdot {\hat {\mathbf {p} }}-\beta mc^{2}\right)\left({\hat {E}}+c{\boldsymbol {\alpha }}\cdot {\hat {\mathbf {p} }}+\beta mc^{2}\right)\psi =0\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ed7bbb88c86416e7ea6f62d4380adc08c5461b96)
where **α** = (_α_ 1, _α_ 2, _α_ 3) and _β_ are not simply numbers or vectors, but 4 × 4 [Hermitian matrices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermitian_matrix "Hermitian matrix") that are required to [anticommute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anticommute "Anticommute") for _i_ ≠ _j_:

![Image 14: {\displaystyle \alpha _{i}\beta =-\beta \alpha _{i},\quad \alpha _{i}\alpha _{j}=-\alpha _{j}\alpha _{i}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c9d1d645cc177422a450309c5314d17d7a99067e)
and square to the [identity matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Identity_matrix "Identity matrix"):

![Image 15: {\displaystyle \alpha _{i}^{2}=\beta ^{2}=I\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/67f73218fef187b7ac5b18d23ec6cc9e6503f0c6)
so that terms with mixed second-order derivatives cancel while the second-order derivatives purely in space and time remain. The first factor:

![Image 16: {\displaystyle \left({\hat {E}}-c{\boldsymbol {\alpha }}\cdot {\hat {\mathbf {p} }}-\beta mc^{2}\right)\psi =0\quad \Leftrightarrow \quad {\hat {H}}=c{\boldsymbol {\alpha }}\cdot {\hat {\mathbf {p} }}+\beta mc^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d51abb93eff84163a87febb53f112dec092d1013)
is the Dirac equation. The other factor is also the Dirac equation, but for a particle of [negative mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Negative_mass "Negative mass").[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-Penrose_2005,_p_620%E2%80%93621-23) Each factor is relativistically invariant. The reasoning can be done the other way round: propose the Hamiltonian in the above form, as Dirac did in 1928, then pre-multiply the equation by the other factor of operators _E_ + _c_**α** · **p** + _βmc_ 2, and comparison with the KG equation determines the constraints on **α** and _β_. The positive mass equation can continue to be used without loss of continuity. The matrices multiplying _ψ_ suggest it isn't a scalar wavefunction as permitted in the KG equation, but must instead be a four-component entity. The Dirac equation still predicts negative energy solutions,[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-Martin,_Shaw,_pp._5%E2%80%936-6)[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-25) so Dirac postulated that negative energy states are always occupied, because according to the [Pauli principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pauli_exclusion_principle "Pauli exclusion principle"), [electronic transitions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronic_transition "Electronic transition") from positive to negative energy levels in [atoms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atom "Atom") would be forbidden. See [Dirac sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dirac_sea "Dirac sea") for details.

### Densities and currents

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Relativistic_quantum_mechanics&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Densities and currents")]

In non-relativistic quantum mechanics, the [square modulus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_modulus "Square modulus") of the [wavefunction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wavefunction "Wavefunction")_ψ_ gives the [probability density function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability_density_function "Probability density function")_ρ_ = |_ψ_|2. This is the [Copenhagen interpretation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Copenhagen_interpretation "Copenhagen interpretation"), circa 1927. In RQM, while _ψ_(**r**, _t_) is a wavefunction, the probability interpretation is not the same as in non-relativistic QM. Some RWEs do not predict a probability density _ρ_ or [probability current](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability_current "Probability current")**j** (really meaning _probability current density_) because they are _not_[positive-definite functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positive-definite_function "Positive-definite function") of space and time. The [Dirac equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dirac_equation "Dirac equation") does:[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-26)

![Image 17: {\displaystyle \rho =\psi ^{\dagger }\psi ,\quad \mathbf {j} =\psi ^{\dagger }\gamma ^{0}{\boldsymbol {\gamma }}\psi \quad \rightleftharpoons \quad J^{\mu }=\psi ^{\dagger }\gamma ^{0}\gamma ^{\mu }\psi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/69cf67b1476f4d352dba209aa9d0a46c0700a221)
where the dagger denotes the [Hermitian adjoint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermitian_adjoint "Hermitian adjoint") (authors usually write _ψ_ = _ψ_†_γ_ 0 for the [Dirac adjoint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dirac_adjoint "Dirac adjoint")) and _J μ_ is the [probability four-current](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability_current#Definition_(relativistic_4-current) "Probability current"), while the [Klein–Gordon equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Klein%E2%80%93Gordon_equation "Klein–Gordon equation") does not:[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-27)

![Image 18: {\displaystyle \rho ={\frac {i\hbar }{2mc^{2}}}\left(\psi ^{*}{\frac {\partial \psi }{\partial t}}-\psi {\frac {\partial \psi ^{*}}{\partial t}}\right)\,,\quad \mathbf {j} =-{\frac {i\hbar }{2m}}\left(\psi ^{*}\nabla \psi -\psi \nabla \psi ^{*}\right)\quad \rightleftharpoons \quad J^{\mu }={\frac {i\hbar }{2m}}(\psi ^{*}\partial ^{\mu }\psi -\psi \partial ^{\mu }\psi ^{*})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4f293acb5c8e94daab89bb9d8e84c925b140c261)
where ∂_μ_ is the [four-gradient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Four-gradient "Four-gradient"). Since the initial values of both _ψ_ and ∂_ψ_/∂_t_ may be freely chosen, the density can be negative.

Instead, what appears look at first sight a "probability density" and "probability current" has to be reinterpreted as [charge density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charge_density "Charge density") and [current density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Current_density "Current density") when multiplied by [electric charge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_charge "Electric charge"). Then, the wavefunction _ψ_ is not a wavefunction at all, but reinterpreted as a _field_.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-Parker_1994-16) The density and current of electric charge always satisfy a [continuity equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuity_equation "Continuity equation"):

![Image 19: {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial \rho }{\partial t}}+\nabla \cdot \mathbf {J} =0\quad \rightleftharpoons \quad \partial _{\mu }J^{\mu }=0\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0e5e447aa2db12ce82928554d08d9cb7dac959f0)
as charge is a [conserved quantity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conserved_quantity "Conserved quantity"). Probability density and current also satisfy a continuity equation because probability is conserved, however this is only possible in the absence of interactions.

Spin and electromagnetically interacting particles
--------------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Relativistic_quantum_mechanics&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Spin and electromagnetically interacting particles")]

Including interactions in RWEs is generally difficult. [Minimal coupling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minimal_coupling "Minimal coupling") is a simple way to include the electromagnetic interaction. For one charged particle of [electric charge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_charge "Electric charge")_q_ in an electromagnetic field, given by the [magnetic vector potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_vector_potential "Magnetic vector potential")**A**(**r**, _t_) defined by the magnetic field **B** = ∇ × **A**, and [electric scalar potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_scalar_potential "Electric scalar potential")_ϕ_(**r**, _t_), this is:[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-28)

![Image 20: {\displaystyle {\hat {E}}\rightarrow {\hat {E}}-q\phi \,,\quad {\hat {\mathbf {p} }}\rightarrow {\hat {\mathbf {p} }}-q\mathbf {A} \quad \rightleftharpoons \quad {\hat {P}}_{\mu }\rightarrow {\hat {P}}_{\mu }-qA_{\mu }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f74a258ffaaa4cae561ecd6455027dac3caf998f)
where _P μ_ is the [four-momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Four-momentum "Four-momentum") that has a corresponding [4-momentum operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/4-momentum_operator "4-momentum operator"), and _A μ_ the [four-potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Four-potential "Four-potential"). In the following, the non-relativistic limit refers to the limiting cases:

![Image 21: {\displaystyle E-e\phi \approx mc^{2}\,,\quad \mathbf {p} \approx m\mathbf {v} \,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/84df98200eae4835bfaa6fbd0ac22af3e0d4a859)
that is, the total energy of the particle is approximately the rest energy for small electric potentials, and the momentum is approximately the classical momentum.

In RQM, the KG equation admits the minimal coupling prescription;

![Image 22: {\displaystyle {({\hat {E}}-q\phi )}^{2}\psi =c^{2}{({\hat {\mathbf {p} }}-q\mathbf {A} )}^{2}\psi +(mc^{2})^{2}\psi \quad \rightleftharpoons \quad \left[{({\hat {P}}_{\mu }-qA_{\mu })}{({\hat {P}}^{\mu }-qA^{\mu })}-{(mc)}^{2}\right]\psi =0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c54c939aca8b9700283d653e556aded790dfc7ca)
In the case where the charge is zero, the equation reduces trivially to the free KG equation so nonzero charge is assumed below. This is a scalar equation that is invariant under the _irreducible_ one-dimensional scalar [(0,0)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Representation_theory_of_the_Lorentz_group "Representation theory of the Lorentz group") representation of the Lorentz group. This means that all of its solutions will belong to a direct sum of (0,0) representations. Solutions that do not belong to the irreducible (0,0) representation will have two or more _independent_ components. Such solutions cannot in general describe particles with nonzero spin since spin components are not independent. Other constraint will have to be imposed for that, e.g. the Dirac equation for spin⁠1/2⁠, see below. Thus if a system satisfies the KG equation _only_, it can only be interpreted as a system with zero spin.

The electromagnetic field is treated classically according to [Maxwell's equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxwell%27s_equations "Maxwell's equations") and the particle is described by a wavefunction, the solution to the KG equation. The equation is, as it stands, not always very useful, because massive spinless particles, such as the _π_-mesons, experience the much stronger strong interaction in addition to the electromagnetic interaction. It does, however, correctly describe charged spinless bosons in the absence of other interactions.

The KG equation is applicable to spinless charged [bosons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boson "Boson") in an external electromagnetic potential.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-Martin,_Shaw,_p_3-2) As such, the equation cannot be applied to the description of atoms, since the electron is a spin⁠1/2⁠ particle. In the non-relativistic limit the equation reduces to the Schrödinger equation for a spinless charged particle in an electromagnetic field:[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-Schuam_p_181-19)

![Image 23: {\displaystyle \left(i\hbar {\frac {\partial }{\partial t}}-q\phi \right)\psi ={\frac {1}{2m}}{({\hat {\mathbf {p} }}-q\mathbf {A} )}^{2}\psi \quad \Leftrightarrow \quad {\hat {H}}={\frac {1}{2m}}{({\hat {\mathbf {p} }}-q\mathbf {A} )}^{2}+q\phi .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c47da783fbb1a8935f31826832b93232dc9071e9)
Non relativistically, spin was _[phenomenologically](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phenomenological\_model "Phenomenological model")_ introduced in the [Pauli equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pauli_equation "Pauli equation") by [Pauli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Pauli "Wolfgang Pauli") in 1927 for particles in an [electromagnetic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_field "Electromagnetic field"):

![Image 24: {\displaystyle \left(i\hbar {\frac {\partial }{\partial t}}-q\phi \right)\psi =\left[{\frac {1}{2m}}{({\boldsymbol {\sigma }}\cdot (\mathbf {p} -q\mathbf {A} ))}^{2}\right]\psi \quad \Leftrightarrow \quad {\hat {H}}={\frac {1}{2m}}{({\boldsymbol {\sigma }}\cdot (\mathbf {p} -q\mathbf {A} ))}^{2}+q\phi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/73e1ec55295f23792ee2e2553fcaf99d1f791fa8)
by means of the 2 × 2 [Pauli matrices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pauli_matrices "Pauli matrices"), and _ψ_ is not just a scalar wavefunction as in the non-relativistic Schrödinger equation, but a two-component [spinor field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spinor_field "Spinor field"):

![Image 25: {\displaystyle \psi ={\begin{pmatrix}\psi _{\uparrow }\\\psi _{\downarrow }\end{pmatrix}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e214a28557ab7275ad641bc7b6bab8c6028ea2e2)
where the subscripts ↑ and ↓ refer to the "spin up" (_σ_ = +⁠1/2⁠) and "spin down" (_σ_ = −⁠1/2⁠) states.[[b]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-29)

In RQM, the Dirac equation can also incorporate minimal coupling, rewritten from above;

![Image 26: {\displaystyle \left(i\hbar {\frac {\partial }{\partial t}}-q\phi \right)\psi =\gamma ^{0}\left[c{\boldsymbol {\gamma }}\cdot {({\hat {\mathbf {p} }}-q\mathbf {A} )}-mc^{2}\right]\psi \quad \rightleftharpoons \quad \left[\gamma ^{\mu }({\hat {P}}_{\mu }-qA_{\mu })-mc^{2}\right]\psi =0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d7c29658a337382b2ac1e39f8cbf96d4d52824f4)
and was the first equation to accurately _predict_ spin, a consequence of the 4 × 4 [gamma matrices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamma_matrices "Gamma matrices")_γ_ 0 = _β_, **γ** = (_γ_ 1, _γ_ 2, _γ_ 3) = _β_**α** = (_βα_ 1, _βα_ 2, _βα_ 3). There is a 4 × 4 identity matrix pre-multiplying the energy operator (including the potential energy term), conventionally not written for simplicity and clarity (i.e. treated like the number 1). Here _ψ_ is a four-component spinor field, which is conventionally split into two two-component spinors in the form:[[c]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-30)

![Image 27: {\displaystyle \psi ={\begin{pmatrix}\psi _{+}\\\psi _{-}\end{pmatrix}}={\begin{pmatrix}\psi _{+\uparrow }\\\psi _{+\downarrow }\\\psi _{-\uparrow }\\\psi _{-\downarrow }\end{pmatrix}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/07576130283e20c50d64edde83de8632a295809b)
The 2-spinor _ψ_+ corresponds to a particle with 4-momentum (_E_, **p**) and charge _q_ and two spin states (_σ_ = ±⁠1/2⁠, as before). The other 2-spinor _ψ_− corresponds to a similar particle with the same mass and spin states, but _negative_ 4-momentum −(_E_, **p**) and _negative_ charge −_q_, that is, negative energy states, [time-reversed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/T-symmetry "T-symmetry") momentum, and [negated charge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C-symmetry "C-symmetry"). This was the first interpretation and prediction of a particle and _corresponding [antiparticle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiparticle "Antiparticle")_. See [Dirac spinor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dirac_spinor "Dirac spinor") and [bispinor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bispinor "Bispinor") for further description of these spinors. In the non-relativistic limit the Dirac equation reduces to the Pauli equation (see [Dirac equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dirac_equation#Comparison_with_the_Pauli_theory "Dirac equation") for how). When applied a one-electron atom or ion, setting **A** = **0** and _ϕ_ to the appropriate electrostatic potential, additional relativistic terms include the [spin–orbit interaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin%E2%80%93orbit_interaction "Spin–orbit interaction"), electron [gyromagnetic ratio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gyromagnetic_ratio "Gyromagnetic ratio"), and [Darwin term](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Darwin_term "Darwin term"). In ordinary QM these terms have to be put in by hand and treated using [perturbation theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perturbation_theory "Perturbation theory"). The positive energies do account accurately for the fine structure.

Within RQM, for massless particles the Dirac equation reduces to:

![Image 28: {\displaystyle \left({\frac {\hat {E}}{c}}+{\boldsymbol {\sigma }}\cdot {\hat {\mathbf {p} }}\right)\psi _{+}=0\,,\quad \left({\frac {\hat {E}}{c}}-{\boldsymbol {\sigma }}\cdot {\hat {\mathbf {p} }}\right)\psi _{-}=0\quad \rightleftharpoons \quad \sigma ^{\mu }{\hat {P}}_{\mu }\psi _{+}=0\,,\quad \sigma _{\mu }{\hat {P}}^{\mu }\psi _{-}=0\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/879cfb72b01268e1e0781251644beb050b758343)
the first of which is the [Weyl equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weyl_equation "Weyl equation"), a considerable simplification applicable for massless [neutrinos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutrino "Neutrino").[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-C.B._1994-31) This time there is a 2 × 2 [identity matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Identity_matrix "Identity matrix") pre-multiplying the energy operator conventionally not written. In RQM it is useful to take this as the zeroth Pauli matrix _σ_ 0 which couples to the energy operator (time derivative), just as the other three matrices couple to the momentum operator (spatial derivatives).

The Pauli and gamma matrices were introduced here, in theoretical physics, rather than [pure mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pure_mathematics "Pure mathematics") itself. They have applications to [quaternions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quaternion "Quaternion") and to the [SO(2)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SO(2) "SO(2)") and [SO(3)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SO(3) "SO(3)")[Lie groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_group "Lie group"), because they satisfy the important [commutator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutator "Commutator") [,] and [anticommutator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutator#Anticommutator "Commutator") [,]+ relations respectively:

![Image 29: {\displaystyle \left[\sigma _{a},\sigma _{b}\right]=2i\varepsilon _{abc}\sigma _{c}\,,\quad \left[\sigma _{a},\sigma _{b}\right]_{+}=2\delta _{ab}\sigma _{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dd680d6dfea049867d338731a9ab82437c5e5e0c)
where _ε abc_ is the [three-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional "Three-dimensional")[Levi-Civita symbol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_symbol "Levi-Civita symbol"). The gamma matrices form [bases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basis_(linear_algebra) "Basis (linear algebra)") in [Clifford algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_algebra "Clifford algebra"), and have a connection to the components of the flat spacetime [Minkowski metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minkowski_metric "Minkowski metric")_η αβ_ in the anticommutation relation:

![Image 30: {\displaystyle \left[\gamma ^{\alpha },\gamma ^{\beta }\right]_{+}=\gamma ^{\alpha }\gamma ^{\beta }+\gamma ^{\beta }\gamma ^{\alpha }=2\eta ^{\alpha \beta }\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/72b81cb71fb0be661c59d415213314bb9b480896)
(This can be extended to [curved spacetime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curved_space "Curved space") by introducing [vierbeins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartan_formalism_(physics) "Cartan formalism (physics)"), but is not the subject of special relativity).

In 1929, the [Breit equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Breit_equation "Breit equation") was found to describe two or more electromagnetically interacting massive spin⁠1/2⁠ fermions to first-order relativistic corrections; one of the first attempts to describe such a relativistic quantum [many-particle system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Many-particle_system "Many-particle system"). This is, however, still only an approximation, and the Hamiltonian includes numerous long and complicated sums.

### Helicity and chirality

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Relativistic_quantum_mechanics&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Helicity and chirality")]

The [helicity operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helicity_(particle_physics) "Helicity (particle physics)") is defined by;

![Image 31: {\displaystyle {\hat {h}}={\hat {\mathbf {S} }}\cdot {\frac {\hat {\mathbf {p} }}{|\mathbf {p} |}}={\hat {\mathbf {S} }}\cdot {\frac {c{\hat {\mathbf {p} }}}{\sqrt {E^{2}-(m_{0}c^{2})^{2}}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9981d4d8dd28e14d98a81ca045b6bc6b285eec9a)
where **p** is the momentum operator, **S** the spin operator for a particle of spin _s_, _E_ is the total energy of the particle, and _m_ 0 its rest mass. Helicity indicates the orientations of the spin and translational momentum vectors.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-32) Helicity is frame-dependent because of the 3-momentum in the definition, and is quantized due to spin quantization, which has discrete positive values for parallel alignment, and negative values for antiparallel alignment.

An automatic occurrence in the Dirac equation (and the Weyl equation) is the projection of the spin⁠1/2⁠ operator on the 3-momentum (times _c_), **σ** · _c_**p**, which is the helicity (for the spin⁠1/2⁠ case) times ![Image 32: {\displaystyle {\sqrt {E^{2}-(m_{0}c^{2})^{2}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9ae63a9599192d7cadbbd449a8eb00e4ffe817ec).

For massless particles the helicity simplifies to:

![Image 33: {\displaystyle {\hat {h}}={\hat {\mathbf {S} }}\cdot {\frac {c{\hat {\mathbf {p} }}}{E}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1d7aa1519780c04d7ffc501f118460082f96c1e5)
The Dirac equation can only describe particles of spin⁠1/2⁠. Beyond the Dirac equation, RWEs have been applied to [free particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_particle "Free particle") of various spins. In 1936, Dirac extended his equation to all fermions, three years later [Fierz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Markus_Fierz "Markus Fierz") and Pauli rederived the same equation.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-33) The [Bargmann–Wigner equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bargmann%E2%80%93Wigner_equations "Bargmann–Wigner equations") were found in 1948 using Lorentz group theory, applicable for all free particles with any spin.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-34)[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-35) Considering the factorization of the KG equation above, and more rigorously by [Lorentz group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentz_group "Lorentz group") theory, it becomes apparent to introduce spin in the form of matrices.

The wavefunctions are multicomponent [spinor fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spinor_field "Spinor field"), which can be represented as [column vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Column_vector "Column vector") of [functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)") of space and time:

![Image 34: {\displaystyle \psi (\mathbf {r} ,t)={\begin{bmatrix}\psi _{\sigma =s}(\mathbf {r} ,t)\\\psi _{\sigma =s-1}(\mathbf {r} ,t)\\\vdots \\\psi _{\sigma =-s+1}(\mathbf {r} ,t)\\\psi _{\sigma =-s}(\mathbf {r} ,t)\end{bmatrix}}\quad \rightleftharpoons \quad {\psi (\mathbf {r} ,t)}^{\dagger }={\begin{bmatrix}{\psi _{\sigma =s}(\mathbf {r} ,t)}^{\star }&{\psi _{\sigma =s-1}(\mathbf {r} ,t)}^{\star }&\cdots &{\psi _{\sigma =-s+1}(\mathbf {r} ,t)}^{\star }&{\psi _{\sigma =-s}(\mathbf {r} ,t)}^{\star }\end{bmatrix}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/053048fa751b5ea9cb6c500c6fca22608466f035)
where the expression on the right is the [Hermitian conjugate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermitian_conjugate "Hermitian conjugate"). For a _massive_ particle of spin _s_, there are 2 _s_ + 1 components for the particle, and another 2 _s_ + 1 for the corresponding [antiparticle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiparticle "Antiparticle") (there are 2 _s_ + 1 possible _σ_ values in each case), altogether forming a 2(2 _s_ + 1)-component spinor field:

![Image 35: {\displaystyle \psi (\mathbf {r} ,t)={\begin{bmatrix}\psi _{+,\,\sigma =s}(\mathbf {r} ,t)\\\psi _{+,\,\sigma =s-1}(\mathbf {r} ,t)\\\vdots \\\psi _{+,\,\sigma =-s+1}(\mathbf {r} ,t)\\\psi _{+,\,\sigma =-s}(\mathbf {r} ,t)\\\psi _{-,\,\sigma =s}(\mathbf {r} ,t)\\\psi _{-,\,\sigma =s-1}(\mathbf {r} ,t)\\\vdots \\\psi _{-,\,\sigma =-s+1}(\mathbf {r} ,t)\\\psi _{-,\,\sigma =-s}(\mathbf {r} ,t)\end{bmatrix}}\quad \rightleftharpoons \quad {\psi (\mathbf {r} ,t)}^{\dagger }{\begin{bmatrix}{\psi _{+,\,\sigma =s}(\mathbf {r} ,t)}^{\star }&{\psi _{+,\,\sigma =s-1}(\mathbf {r} ,t)}^{\star }&\cdots &{\psi _{-,\,\sigma =-s}(\mathbf {r} ,t)}^{\star }\end{bmatrix}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/42ec780358ac449c3c94c6e0fbeed29b4ee2891d)
with the + subscript indicating the particle and − subscript for the antiparticle. However, for _massless_ particles of spin _s_, there are only ever two-component spinor fields; one is for the particle in one helicity state corresponding to +_s_ and the other for the antiparticle in the opposite helicity state corresponding to −_s_:

![Image 36: {\displaystyle \psi (\mathbf {r} ,t)={\begin{pmatrix}\psi _{+}(\mathbf {r} ,t)\\\psi _{-}(\mathbf {r} ,t)\end{pmatrix}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9b9fd3095fda4d31439767abbbe99b998319c2df)
According to the relativistic energy-momentum relation, all massless particles travel at the speed of light, so particles traveling at the speed of light are also described by two-component spinors. Historically, [Élie Cartan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%89lie_Cartan "Élie Cartan") found the most general form of [spinors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spinor "Spinor") in 1913, prior to the spinors revealed in the RWEs following the year 1927.

For equations describing higher-spin particles, the inclusion of interactions is nowhere near as simple minimal coupling, they lead to incorrect predictions and self-inconsistencies.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-36) For spin greater than ⁠_ħ_/2⁠, the RWE is not fixed by the particle's mass, spin, and electric charge; the electromagnetic moments ([electric dipole moments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_dipole_moment "Electric dipole moment") and [magnetic dipole moments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_dipole_moment "Magnetic dipole moment")) allowed by the [spin quantum number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_quantum_number "Spin quantum number") are arbitrary. (Theoretically, [magnetic charge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_charge "Magnetic charge") would contribute also). For example, the spin⁠1/2⁠ case only allows a magnetic dipole, but for spin 1 particles magnetic quadrupoles and electric dipoles are also possible.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-C.B._1994-31) For more on this topic, see [multipole expansion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multipole_expansion "Multipole expansion") and (for example) Cédric Lorcé (2009).[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-37)[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-38)

The Schrödinger/Pauli velocity operator can be defined for a massive particle using the classical definition **p** = _m_**v**, and substituting quantum operators in the usual way:[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-39)

![Image 37: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathbf {v} }}={\frac {1}{m}}{\hat {\mathbf {p} }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/21ab23a18ec1f7169f95d9854efc5838577776d6)
which has eigenvalues that take _any_ value. In RQM, the Dirac theory, it is:

![Image 38: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathbf {v} }}={\frac {i}{\hbar }}\left[{\hat {H}},{\hat {\mathbf {r} }}\right]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3970b2275f8d3808e3ebb6d143b6c6ee8db3912f)
which must have eigenvalues between ±_c_. See [Foldy–Wouthuysen transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foldy%E2%80%93Wouthuysen_transformation "Foldy–Wouthuysen transformation") for more theoretical background.

Relativistic quantum Lagrangians
--------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Relativistic_quantum_mechanics&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Relativistic quantum Lagrangians")]

The Hamiltonian operators in the Schrödinger picture are one approach to forming the differential equations for _ψ_. An equivalent alternative is to determine a [Lagrangian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrangian_(field_theory) "Lagrangian (field theory)") (really meaning _[Lagrangian density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrangian\_density "Lagrangian density")_), then generate the differential equation by the [field-theoretic Euler–Lagrange equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_field_theory#Relativistic_field_theory "Classical field theory"):

![Image 39: {\displaystyle \partial _{\mu }\left({\frac {\partial {\mathcal {L}}}{\partial (\partial _{\mu }\psi )}}\right)-{\frac {\partial {\mathcal {L}}}{\partial \psi }}=0\,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6a634bb81d404a766a5506a799b2c11d2673a9a8)
For some RWEs, a Lagrangian can be found by inspection. For example, the Dirac Lagrangian is:[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-40)

![Image 40: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {L}}={\overline {\psi }}(\gamma ^{\mu }P_{\mu }-mc)\psi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4db9767c2e80dba3056116a45ad898ebf21539da)
and Klein–Gordon Lagrangian is:

![Image 41: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {L}}=-{\frac {\hbar ^{2}}{m}}\eta ^{\mu \nu }\partial _{\mu }\psi ^{*}\partial _{\nu }\psi -mc^{2}\psi ^{*}\psi \,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5a4d81c101616fc7fe8c16c6475423880fbc9ba4)
This is not possible for all RWEs; and is one reason the Lorentz group theoretic approach is important and appealing: fundamental invariance and symmetries in space and time can be used to derive RWEs using appropriate group representations. The Lagrangian approach with field interpretation of _ψ_ is the subject of QFT rather than RQM: Feynman's [path integral formulation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Path_integral_formulation "Path integral formulation") uses invariant Lagrangians rather than Hamiltonian operators, since the latter can become extremely complicated, see (for example) Weinberg (1995).[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-41)

Relativistic quantum angular momentum
-------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Relativistic_quantum_mechanics&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Relativistic quantum angular momentum")]

In non-relativistic QM, the [angular momentum operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum_operator "Angular momentum operator") is formed from the classical [pseudovector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudovector "Pseudovector") definition **L** = **r** × **p**. In RQM, the position and momentum operators are inserted directly where they appear in the orbital [relativistic angular momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_angular_momentum "Relativistic angular momentum") tensor defined from the four-dimensional position and momentum of the particle, equivalently a [bivector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bivector "Bivector") in the [exterior algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_algebra "Exterior algebra") formalism:[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-42)[[d]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-43)

![Image 42: {\displaystyle M^{\alpha \beta }=X^{\alpha }P^{\beta }-X^{\beta }P^{\alpha }=2X^{[\alpha }P^{\beta ]}\quad \rightleftharpoons \quad \mathbf {M} =\mathbf {X} \wedge \mathbf {P} \,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b2a6a30c2c2440d94cb0ff0c70265ca1ee9cd6c2)
which are six components altogether: three are the non-relativistic 3-orbital angular momenta; _M_ 12 = _L_ 3, _M_ 23 = _L_ 1, _M_ 31 = _L_ 2, and the other three _M_ 01, _M_ 02, _M_ 03 are boosts of the [centre of mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centre_of_mass "Centre of mass") of the rotating object. An additional relativistic-quantum term has to be added for particles with spin. For a particle of rest mass _m_, the _total_ angular momentum tensor is:

![Image 43: {\displaystyle J^{\alpha \beta }=2X^{[\alpha }P^{\beta ]}+{\frac {1}{m^{2}}}\varepsilon ^{\alpha \beta \gamma \delta }W_{\gamma }p_{\delta }\quad \rightleftharpoons \quad \mathbf {J} =\mathbf {X} \wedge \mathbf {P} +{\frac {1}{m^{2}}}\star (\mathbf {W} \wedge \mathbf {P} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3ee0e1cf93ff99c3fb5740d752f655ac13456f26)
where the star denotes the [Hodge dual](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hodge_dual "Hodge dual"), and

![Image 44: {\displaystyle W_{\alpha }={\frac {1}{2}}\varepsilon _{\alpha \beta \gamma \delta }M^{\beta \gamma }p^{\delta }\quad \rightleftharpoons \quad \mathbf {W} =\star (\mathbf {M} \wedge \mathbf {P} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bde6732fd5ac0a9ba060c0561a0462de11431fbd)
is the [Pauli–Lubanski pseudovector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pauli%E2%80%93Lubanski_pseudovector "Pauli–Lubanski pseudovector").[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-44) For more on relativistic spin, see (for example) Troshin & Tyurin (1994).[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-45)

### Thomas precession and spin–orbit interactions

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Relativistic_quantum_mechanics&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Thomas precession and spin–orbit interactions")]

In 1926, the [Thomas precession](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_precession "Thomas precession") is discovered: relativistic corrections to the spin of elementary particles with application in the [spin–orbit interaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin%E2%80%93orbit_interaction "Spin–orbit interaction") of atoms and rotation of macroscopic objects.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-46)[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-47) In 1939 Wigner derived the Thomas precession.

In [classical electromagnetism and special relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_electromagnetism_and_special_relativity#The_E_and_B_fields "Classical electromagnetism and special relativity"), an electron moving with a velocity **v** through an electric field **E** but not a magnetic field **B**, will in its own frame of reference experience a [Lorentz-transformed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentz_transformation "Lorentz transformation") magnetic field **B′**:

![Image 45: {\displaystyle \mathbf {B} '={\frac {\mathbf {E} \times \mathbf {v} }{c^{2}{\sqrt {1-\left(v/c\right)^{2}}}}}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/30b7a5fdf9e6072b1b7388670ba088c8f3019521)
In the non-relativistic limit _v_<<_c_:

![Image 46: {\displaystyle \mathbf {B} '={\frac {\mathbf {E} \times \mathbf {v} }{c^{2}}}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/77b3f257f94ed6320d3a53a489c7d0d223b03fd6)
so the non-relativistic spin interaction Hamiltonian becomes:[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-Kroemer-48)

![Image 47: {\displaystyle {\hat {H}}=-\mathbf {B} '\cdot {\hat {\boldsymbol {\mu }}}_{S}=-\left(\mathbf {B} +{\frac {\mathbf {E} \times \mathbf {v} }{c^{2}}}\right)\cdot {\hat {\boldsymbol {\mu }}}_{S}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b31f7bc8ce7bcd38bbffea8896cdef0ab79220f9)
where the first term is already the non-relativistic magnetic moment interaction, and the second term the relativistic correction of order (_v/c_)², but this disagrees with experimental atomic spectra by a factor of 1⁄2. It was pointed out by L.Thomas that there is a second relativistic effect: An electric field component perpendicular to the electron velocity causes an additional acceleration of the electron perpendicular to its instantaneous velocity, so the electron moves in a curved path. The electron moves in a [rotating frame of reference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotating_frame_of_reference "Rotating frame of reference"), and this additional precession of the electron is called the _Thomas precession_. It can be shown[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-49) that the net result of this effect is that the spin–orbit interaction is reduced by half, as if the magnetic field experienced by the electron has only one-half the value, and the relativistic correction in the Hamiltonian is:

![Image 48: {\displaystyle {\hat {H}}=-\mathbf {B} '\cdot {\hat {\boldsymbol {\mu }}}_{S}=-\left(\mathbf {B} +{\frac {\mathbf {E} \times \mathbf {v} }{2c^{2}}}\right)\cdot {\hat {\boldsymbol {\mu }}}_{S}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/203a000067c615e1244aac01eb78042459b8d86e)
In the case of RQM, the factor of 1⁄2 is predicted by the Dirac equation.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-Kroemer-48)

The events which led to and established RQM, and the continuation beyond into [quantum electrodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_electrodynamics "Quantum electrodynamics") (QED), are summarized below [see, for example, R. Resnick and R. Eisberg (1985),[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-50) and [P.W Atkins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Atkins "Peter Atkins") (1974)[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-51)]. More than half a century of experimental and theoretical research from the 1890s through to the 1950s in the new and mysterious quantum theory as it was up and coming revealed that a number of phenomena cannot be explained by QM alone. SR, found at the turn of the 20th century, was found to be a _necessary_ component, leading to unification: RQM. Theoretical predictions and experiments mainly focused on the newly found [atomic physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_physics "Atomic physics"), [nuclear physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_physics "Nuclear physics"), and [particle physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_physics "Particle physics"); by considering [spectroscopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopy "Spectroscopy"), [diffraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffraction "Diffraction") and [scattering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scattering "Scattering") of particles, and the electrons and nuclei within atoms and molecules. Numerous results are attributed to the effects of spin.

### Relativistic description of particles in quantum phenomena

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Relativistic_quantum_mechanics&action=edit&section=16 "Edit section: Relativistic description of particles in quantum phenomena")]

[Albert Einstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Einstein "Albert Einstein") in 1905 explained of the [photoelectric effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect "Photoelectric effect"); a particle description of light as [photons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photons "Photons"). In 1916, [Sommerfeld](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arnold_Sommerfeld "Arnold Sommerfeld") explains [fine structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fine_structure "Fine structure"); the splitting of the [spectral lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectral_line "Spectral line") of [atoms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atoms "Atoms") due to first order relativistic corrections. The [Compton effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compton_effect "Compton effect") of 1923 provided more evidence that special relativity does apply; in this case to a particle description of photon–electron scattering. [de Broglie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis_de_Broglie "Louis de Broglie") extends [wave–particle duality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wave%E2%80%93particle_duality "Wave–particle duality") to [matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matter "Matter"): the [de Broglie relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_Broglie_relations "De Broglie relations"), which are consistent with special relativity and quantum mechanics. By 1927, [Davisson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clinton_Davisson "Clinton Davisson") and [Germer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lester_Germer "Lester Germer") and separately [G. Thomson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_Paget_Thomson "George Paget Thomson") successfully diffract electrons, providing experimental evidence of wave-particle duality.

*   1897 [J. J. Thomson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J._J._Thomson "J. J. Thomson") discovers the [electron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron "Electron") and measures its [mass-to-charge ratio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass-to-charge_ratio "Mass-to-charge ratio"). Discovery of the [Zeeman effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zeeman_effect "Zeeman effect"): the splitting a [spectral line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectral_line "Spectral line") into several components in the presence of a static magnetic field.
*   1908 [Millikan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Andrews_Millikan "Robert Andrews Millikan") measures the charge on the electron and finds experimental evidence of its quantization, in the [oil drop experiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oil_drop_experiment "Oil drop experiment").
*   1911 [Alpha particle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_particle "Alpha particle") scattering in the [Geiger–Marsden experiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geiger%E2%80%93Marsden_experiment "Geiger–Marsden experiment"), led by [Rutherford](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ernest_Rutherford "Ernest Rutherford"), showed that atoms possess an internal structure: the [atomic nucleus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_nucleus "Atomic nucleus").[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-52)
*   1913 The [Stark effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stark_effect "Stark effect") is discovered: splitting of spectral lines due to a static [electric field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_field "Electric field") (compare with the Zeeman effect).
*   1922 [Stern–Gerlach experiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stern%E2%80%93Gerlach_experiment "Stern–Gerlach experiment"): experimental evidence of spin and its quantization.
*   1924 [Stoner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edmund_Clifton_Stoner "Edmund Clifton Stoner") studies splitting of [energy levels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_level "Energy level") in [magnetic fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_field "Magnetic field").
*   1932 Experimental discovery of the [neutron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron "Neutron") by [Chadwick](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Chadwick "James Chadwick"), and [positrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positron "Positron") by [Anderson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_David_Anderson "Carl David Anderson"), confirming the theoretical prediction of positrons.
*   1958 Discovery of the [Mössbauer effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M%C3%B6ssbauer_effect "Mössbauer effect"): resonant and recoil-free emission and absorption of [gamma radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamma_radiation "Gamma radiation") by atomic nuclei bound in a solid, useful for accurate measurements of [gravitational redshift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_redshift "Gravitational redshift") and [time dilation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_dilation "Time dilation"), and in the analysis of nuclear electromagnetic moments in [hyperfine interactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperfine_interaction "Hyperfine interaction").[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-53)

### Quantum non-locality and relativistic locality

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Relativistic_quantum_mechanics&action=edit&section=18 "Edit section: Quantum non-locality and relativistic locality")]

In 1935, Einstein, [Rosen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nathan_Rosen "Nathan Rosen"), [Podolsky](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boris_Podolsky "Boris Podolsky") published a paper[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-54) concerning [quantum entanglement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_entanglement "Quantum entanglement") of particles, questioning [quantum nonlocality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_nonlocality "Quantum nonlocality") and the apparent violation of causality upheld in SR: particles can appear to interact instantaneously at arbitrary distances. This was a misconception since information is not and cannot be transferred in the entangled states; rather the information transmission is in the process of measurement by two observers (one observer has to send a signal to the other, which cannot exceed _c_). QM does _not_ violate SR.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-55)[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-56) In 1959, [Bohm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Bohm "David Bohm") and [Aharonov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yakir_Aharonov "Yakir Aharonov") publish a paper[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-57) on the [Aharonov–Bohm effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aharonov%E2%80%93Bohm_effect "Aharonov–Bohm effect"), questioning the status of electromagnetic potentials in QM. The [EM field tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_field_tensor "Electromagnetic field tensor") and [EM 4-potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_four-potential "Electromagnetic four-potential") formulations are both applicable in SR, but in QM the potentials enter the Hamiltonian (see above) and influence the motion of charged particles even in regions where the fields are zero. In 1964, [Bell's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bell%27s_theorem "Bell's theorem") was published in a paper on the EPR paradox,[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-58) showing that QM cannot be derived from [local hidden-variable theories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_hidden-variable_theory "Local hidden-variable theory") if locality is to be maintained.

In 1947, the Lamb shift was discovered: a small difference in the 2 _S_ 1⁄2 and 2 _P_ 1⁄2 levels of hydrogen, due to the interaction between the electron and vacuum. [Lamb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Willis_Lamb "Willis Lamb") and [Retherford](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Retherford "Robert Retherford") experimentally measure stimulated radio-frequency transitions the 2 _S_ 1⁄2 and 2 _P_ 1⁄2 hydrogen levels by [microwave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microwave "Microwave") radiation.[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-59) An explanation of the Lamb shift is presented by [Bethe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hans_Bethe "Hans Bethe"). Papers on the effect were published in the early 1950s.[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-60)

### Development of quantum electrodynamics

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Relativistic_quantum_mechanics&action=edit&section=20 "Edit section: Development of quantum electrodynamics")]

*   1927 [Dirac](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Dirac "Paul Dirac") establishes the field of QED, also coining the term "quantum electrodynamics".[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#cite_note-61)
*   1943 [Tomonaga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sin-Itiro_Tomonaga "Sin-Itiro Tomonaga") begins work on [renormalization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Renormalization "Renormalization"), influential in QED.
*   1947 [Schwinger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Julian_Schwinger "Julian Schwinger") calculates the [anomalous magnetic moment of the electron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anomalous_magnetic_dipole_moment#Anomalous_magnetic_moment_of_the_electron "Anomalous magnetic dipole moment"). [Kusch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polykarp_Kusch "Polykarp Kusch") measures of the anomalous magnetic electron moment, confirming one of QED's great predictions.
