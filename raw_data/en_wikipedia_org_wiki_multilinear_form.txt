In [abstract algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abstract_algebra "Abstract algebra") and [multilinear algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multilinear_algebra "Multilinear algebra"), a **multilinear form** on a [vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space")![Image 1: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) over a [field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)")![Image 2: {\displaystyle K}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b76fce82a62ed5461908f0dc8f037de4e3686b0) is a [map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Map_(mathematics) "Map (mathematics)")

![Image 3: {\displaystyle f\colon V^{k}\to K}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/08d4a04ab6d8e7573e087d155de44dccd8f0695d)
that is separately ![Image 4: {\displaystyle K}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b76fce82a62ed5461908f0dc8f037de4e3686b0)-[linear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear "Linear") in each of its ![Image 5: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40) arguments.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multilinear_form#cite_note-1) More generally, one can define multilinear forms on a [module](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Module_(mathematics) "Module (mathematics)") over a [commutative ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_ring "Commutative ring"). The rest of this article, however, will only consider multilinear forms on [finite-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension_(vector_space) "Dimension (vector space)") vector spaces.

A multilinear ![Image 6: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40)-form on ![Image 7: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) over ![Image 8: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc) is called a (**covariant**) **![Image 9: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {k}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c5007edd8c173fdfa24afccd09ea3ba5fb2a12a9)-tensor**, and the vector space of such forms is usually denoted ![Image 10: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {T}}^{k}(V)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ec72f2cebf6fac3e0dc913989b7fd7e7f31b6577) or ![Image 11: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {L}}^{k}(V)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cb370ceb715716e50e43f866068cd08a0694b40e).[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multilinear_form#cite_note-2)

Given a ![Image 12: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40)-tensor ![Image 13: {\displaystyle f\in {\mathcal {T}}^{k}(V)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3935764ae3f56a9256ae48392eed298a1855b1fa) and an ![Image 14: {\displaystyle \ell }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f066e981e530bacc07efc6a10fa82deee985929e)-tensor ![Image 15: {\displaystyle g\in {\mathcal {T}}^{\ell }(V)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/50617dd8f6de3b52e154153198ee1b173c6bd7b1), a product ![Image 16: {\displaystyle f\otimes g\in {\mathcal {T}}^{k+\ell }(V)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f932f78797ace79346dd191f0f5168a79d095e5a), known as the **tensor product**, can be defined by the property

![Image 17: {\displaystyle (f\otimes g)(v_{1},\ldots ,v_{k},v_{k+1},\ldots ,v_{k+\ell })=f(v_{1},\ldots ,v_{k})g(v_{k+1},\ldots ,v_{k+\ell }),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5a5af983e87ec319e6547f189898b3b17e0afb89)
for all ![Image 18: {\displaystyle v_{1},\ldots ,v_{k+\ell }\in V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/04723de1f0e19f20df14cb53ffae1b46921928d5). The [tensor product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_product "Tensor product") of multilinear forms is not commutative; however it is bilinear and associative:

![Image 19: {\displaystyle f\otimes (ag_{1}+bg_{2})=a(f\otimes g_{1})+b(f\otimes g_{2})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/61fb7a8afda6b5de6f680886fd73c3d508235889), ![Image 20: {\displaystyle (af_{1}+bf_{2})\otimes g=a(f_{1}\otimes g)+b(f_{2}\otimes g),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eb8609e289888cb5fcea61f1416ca97f3d41f933)
and

![Image 21: {\displaystyle (f\otimes g)\otimes h=f\otimes (g\otimes h).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b1497a139f6c68b97c1beede2ad32b10d0f20c92)
If ![Image 22: {\displaystyle (v_{1},\ldots ,v_{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8ab0b34f196f25857f60aa90852f6ebba17a21ba) forms a basis for an ![Image 23: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)-dimensional vector space ![Image 24: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) and ![Image 25: {\displaystyle (\phi ^{1},\ldots ,\phi ^{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/740f9ad9a09d6975fb174a6288fe6368a5517c60) is the corresponding [dual basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_basis "Dual basis") for the [dual space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_space "Dual space")![Image 26: {\displaystyle V^{*}={\mathcal {T}}^{1}(V)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a5ddf3b5f541e084637132161d131ea202b62574), then the products ![Image 27: {\displaystyle \phi ^{i_{1}}\otimes \cdots \otimes \phi ^{i_{k}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/85e6e707f9494760ff30ad7ec0b94949f2c4fb36), with ![Image 28: {\displaystyle 1\leq i_{1},\ldots ,i_{k}\leq n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c38ecfde9e53e26e12f50f9df992a47bf89ca504) form a basis for ![Image 29: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {T}}^{k}(V)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ec72f2cebf6fac3e0dc913989b7fd7e7f31b6577). Consequently, ![Image 30: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {T}}^{k}(V)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ec72f2cebf6fac3e0dc913989b7fd7e7f31b6577) has dimension ![Image 31: {\displaystyle n^{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/53af70c07e0932d85d2fdb70c56360544c3a0b5a).

If ![Image 32: {\displaystyle k=2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0bd301789e1f25a3da4be297ff637754ebee5f5d), ![Image 33: {\displaystyle f:V\times V\to K}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/084236fa9ad0c592e124ac2235f7a7b936d2558f) is referred to as a **bilinear form**. A familiar and important example of a (symmetric) [bilinear form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bilinear_form "Bilinear form") is the [standard inner product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dot_product "Dot product") (dot product) of vectors.

### Alternating multilinear forms

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Multilinear_form&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Alternating multilinear forms")]

An important class of multilinear forms are the **alternating multilinear forms**, which have the additional property that[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multilinear_form#cite_note-:0-3)

![Image 34: {\displaystyle f(x_{\sigma (1)},\ldots ,x_{\sigma (k)})=\operatorname {sgn}(\sigma )f(x_{1},\ldots ,x_{k}),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/56f1ee6b61f6c659f419f881872f7b9a6a5f03e6)
where ![Image 35: {\displaystyle \sigma :\mathbf {N} _{k}\to \mathbf {N} _{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f7e9a2555aaa8d9c90f8ff279e753bab3f4f4cd9) is a [permutation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permutation "Permutation") and ![Image 36: {\displaystyle \operatorname {sgn}(\sigma )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/716472e58beb0c37a9bdfbf33851309a7aae45f0) denotes its [sign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sign_of_a_permutation "Sign of a permutation") (+1 if even, â€“1 if odd). As a consequence, [alternating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alternating_multilinear_map "Alternating multilinear map") multilinear forms are antisymmetric with respect to swapping of any two arguments (i.e., ![Image 37: {\displaystyle \sigma (p)=q,\sigma (q)=p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eaa16ee0ff769828df5e913d792383fdd1e0552d) and ![Image 38: {\displaystyle \sigma (i)=i,1\leq i\leq k,i\neq p,q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47c87e2573ba7ce1be29fcefa7ff0d387b4272cb)):

![Image 39: {\displaystyle f(x_{1},\ldots ,x_{p},\ldots ,x_{q},\ldots ,x_{k})=-f(x_{1},\ldots ,x_{q},\ldots ,x_{p},\ldots ,x_{k}).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/53b650e18593b173022a792a8ecece81941b2070)
With the additional hypothesis that the [characteristic of the field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Characteristic_(field) "Characteristic (field)")![Image 40: {\displaystyle K}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b76fce82a62ed5461908f0dc8f037de4e3686b0) is not 2, setting ![Image 41: {\displaystyle x_{p}=x_{q}=x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a6e7c99fb9728a78876fdf91cdfba3806c686fbf) implies as a corollary that ![Image 42: {\displaystyle f(x_{1},\ldots ,x,\ldots ,x,\ldots ,x_{k})=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35b318fa2e646e7ec781efdba6cccdf5f077ac66); that is, the form has a value of 0 whenever two of its arguments are equal. Note, however, that some authors[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multilinear_form#cite_note-4) use this last condition as the defining property of alternating forms. This definition implies the property given at the beginning of the section, but as noted above, the converse implication holds only when ![Image 43: {\displaystyle \operatorname {char} (K)\neq 2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/56504543893e7f42f54b8e30e93c1d9b5c38a4e6).

The tensor product of alternating multilinear forms is, in general, no longer alternating. However, by summing over all permutations of the tensor product, taking into account the parity of each term, the _[exterior product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior\_product "Exterior product")_ (![Image 59: {\displaystyle \wedge }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1caa4004cb216ef2930bb12fe805a76870caed94), also known as the _wedge product_) of multicovectors can be defined, so that if ![Image 60: {\displaystyle f\in {\mathcal {A}}^{k}(V)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/626685a09164d8fd64e0f7ce272b84a810b6139a) and ![Image 61: {\displaystyle g\in {\mathcal {A}}^{\ell }(V)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1d8a54862c1a25c248ee1a54d7f78d23aca5f27d), then ![Image 62: {\displaystyle f\wedge g\in {\mathcal {A}}^{k+\ell }(V)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ab85c758bb8150799f6378b88f1cb7876a7e43ab):

![Image 63: {\displaystyle (f\wedge g)(v_{1},\ldots ,v_{k+\ell })={\frac {1}{k!\ell !}}\sum _{\sigma \in S_{k+\ell }}(\operatorname {sgn}(\sigma ))f(v_{\sigma (1)},\ldots ,v_{\sigma (k)})g(v_{\sigma (k+1)},\ldots ,v_{\sigma (k+\ell )}),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/12d7a0427bf7f9b957750a5317e067131bffa8f8)
where the sum is taken over the set of all permutations over ![Image 64: {\displaystyle k+\ell }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e609658208732dd846ea24e0e791c414216e2bc6) elements, ![Image 65: {\displaystyle S_{k+\ell }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ccebef1446cc399590804ef278efdf033fc169fe). The exterior product is bilinear, associative, and graded-alternating: if ![Image 66: {\displaystyle f\in {\mathcal {A}}^{k}(V)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/626685a09164d8fd64e0f7ce272b84a810b6139a) and ![Image 67: {\displaystyle g\in {\mathcal {A}}^{\ell }(V)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1d8a54862c1a25c248ee1a54d7f78d23aca5f27d) then ![Image 68: {\displaystyle f\wedge g=(-1)^{k\ell }g\wedge f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1db6fc2483513c5ba51268093bef25ed27a87ffb).

Given a basis ![Image 69: {\displaystyle (v_{1},\ldots ,v_{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8ab0b34f196f25857f60aa90852f6ebba17a21ba) for ![Image 70: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) and dual basis ![Image 71: {\displaystyle (\phi ^{1},\ldots ,\phi ^{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/740f9ad9a09d6975fb174a6288fe6368a5517c60) for ![Image 72: {\displaystyle V^{*}={\mathcal {A}}^{1}(V)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/53df3dd871ec414098d7fabc44db48f0cc1f9cd1), the exterior products ![Image 73: {\displaystyle \phi ^{i_{1}}\wedge \cdots \wedge \phi ^{i_{k}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a4aafdde779e58ed2d454d040dc61efb38ade1de), with ![Image 74: {\displaystyle 1\leq i_{1}<\cdots <i_{k}\leq n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/57294793d34e3115b669dabf00298f4ad2576324) form a basis for ![Image 75: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {A}}^{k}(V)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3339fdf2b412098ed532345196d8e64e2f2b19dc). Hence, the dimension of ![Image 76: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {A}}^{k}(V)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3339fdf2b412098ed532345196d8e64e2f2b19dc) for _n_-dimensional ![Image 77: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) is ![Image 78: {\textstyle {\tbinom {n}{k}}={\frac {n!}{(n-k)!\,k!}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9925ea85bb73b56f323fd5b0b8a24d3cc7bb49fb).

Differential forms are mathematical objects constructed via tangent spaces and multilinear forms that behave, in many ways, like [differentials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_of_a_function "Differential of a function") in the classical sense. Though conceptually and computationally useful, differentials are founded on ill-defined notions of infinitesimal quantities developed early in the [history of calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_calculus "History of calculus"). Differential forms provide a mathematically rigorous and precise framework to modernize this long-standing idea. Differential forms are especially useful in [multivariable calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multivariable_calculus "Multivariable calculus") (analysis) and [differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry") because they possess transformation properties that allow them be integrated on curves, surfaces, and their higher-dimensional analogues ([differentiable manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differentiable_manifold "Differentiable manifold")). One far-reaching application is the modern statement of [Stokes' theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stokes%27_theorem "Stokes' theorem"), a sweeping generalization of the [fundamental theorem of calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_theorem_of_calculus "Fundamental theorem of calculus") to higher dimensions.

#### Definition of differential k-forms and construction of 1-forms

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Multilinear_form&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Definition of differential k-forms and construction of 1-forms")]

To define differential forms on open subsets ![Image 79: {\displaystyle U\subset \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c1caefb347c86337ea7cd0c354acd2294bd7d81d), we first need the notion of the **tangent space** of ![Image 80: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d)at ![Image 81: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36), usually denoted ![Image 82: {\displaystyle T_{p}\mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b1ff0cee923f9ef0490325b09a0450dc77b3e8d8) or ![Image 83: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} _{p}^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/498c9a7322133da0bb10111e65e97aaa4669579b). The vector space ![Image 84: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} _{p}^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/498c9a7322133da0bb10111e65e97aaa4669579b) can be defined most conveniently as the set of elements ![Image 85: {\displaystyle v_{p}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/537e72b1a70774ae976de89f7919dc0e0a9bb86d) (![Image 86: {\displaystyle v\in \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/37576c57a5c8ee1c052cb82a6b88aaa4b41764f4), with ![Image 87: {\displaystyle p\in \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c7d5b6202943d58e511d6616d84cd13ca0bc7547) fixed) with vector addition and [scalar multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_multiplication "Scalar multiplication") defined by ![Image 88: {\displaystyle v_{p}+w_{p}:=(v+w)_{p}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/89ba15eb715c613159a9cb6cbb3d43d6cb1c37d1) and ![Image 89: {\displaystyle a\cdot (v_{p}):=(a\cdot v)_{p}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d2d5c174c784d0a37a2a2aaaad0394bbb02baa7e), respectively. Moreover, if ![Image 90: {\displaystyle (e_{1},\ldots ,e_{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/12c4e5513493af62a4753b821ba5bc7842a5278a) is the standard basis for ![Image 91: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d), then ![Image 92: {\displaystyle ((e_{1})_{p},\ldots ,(e_{n})_{p})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1d7ca501c2677eb0f6611ccacb9e894a1329e836) is the analogous standard basis for ![Image 93: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} _{p}^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/498c9a7322133da0bb10111e65e97aaa4669579b). In other words, each tangent space ![Image 94: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} _{p}^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/498c9a7322133da0bb10111e65e97aaa4669579b) can simply be regarded as a copy of ![Image 95: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d) (a set of tangent vectors) based at the point ![Image 96: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36). The collection (disjoint union) of tangent spaces of ![Image 97: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d) at all ![Image 98: {\displaystyle p\in \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c7d5b6202943d58e511d6616d84cd13ca0bc7547) is known as the **tangent bundle** of ![Image 99: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d) and is usually denoted ![Image 100: {\textstyle T\mathbb {R} ^{n}:=\bigcup _{p\in \mathbb {R} ^{n}}\mathbb {R} _{p}^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f998330da22fec17bb3eaddf02c5b83ea5ee983f). While the definition given here provides a simple description of the tangent space of ![Image 101: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d), there are other, more sophisticated constructions that are better suited for defining the tangent spaces of [smooth manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differentiable_manifold "Differentiable manifold") in general (_see the article on [tangent spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent\_space "Tangent space") for details_).

A **differential ![Image 102: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {k}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c5007edd8c173fdfa24afccd09ea3ba5fb2a12a9)-form** on ![Image 103: {\displaystyle U\subset \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c1caefb347c86337ea7cd0c354acd2294bd7d81d) is defined as a function ![Image 104: {\displaystyle \omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/48eff443f9de7a985bb94ca3bde20813ea737be8) that assigns to every ![Image 105: {\displaystyle p\in U}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/66f04c97c84237ad610995f15df5cd7fd0034d3e) a ![Image 106: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40)-covector on the tangent space of ![Image 107: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d)at ![Image 108: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36), usually denoted ![Image 109: {\displaystyle \omega _{p}:=\omega (p)\in {\mathcal {A}}^{k}(\mathbb {R} _{p}^{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a2b852abe3021bfd5d0ecc59a7e58c07d5d3a51d). In brief, a differential ![Image 110: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40)-form is a ![Image 111: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40)-covector field. The space of ![Image 112: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40)-forms on ![Image 113: {\displaystyle U}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/458a728f53b9a0274f059cd695e067c430956025) is usually denoted ![Image 114: {\displaystyle \Omega ^{k}(U)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0f853af9f78c1fdfb132da6497dfc8fd718b746b); thus if ![Image 115: {\displaystyle \omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/48eff443f9de7a985bb94ca3bde20813ea737be8) is a differential ![Image 116: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40)-form, we write ![Image 117: {\displaystyle \omega \in \Omega ^{k}(U)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/127a0fa74d0d12d291e1beeb716bd6c1f9f1c683). By convention, a continuous function on ![Image 118: {\displaystyle U}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/458a728f53b9a0274f059cd695e067c430956025) is a differential 0-form: ![Image 119: {\displaystyle f\in C^{0}(U)=\Omega ^{0}(U)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/33112677ca68be6e91570f81c54e18268385fef6).

We first construct differential 1-forms from 0-forms and deduce some of their basic properties. To simplify the discussion below, we will only consider [smooth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smoothness "Smoothness") differential forms constructed from smooth (![Image 120: {\displaystyle C^{\infty }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/971ed05871d69309df32efdfd2020128c9cf69d8)) functions. Let ![Image 121: {\displaystyle f:\mathbb {R} ^{n}\to \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/306c097f43c91dce633d12cde024948d39e73752) be a smooth function. We define the 1-form ![Image 122: {\displaystyle df}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/53181e2067a93b6bbf150042723cb059d9d2d26f) on ![Image 123: {\displaystyle U}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/458a728f53b9a0274f059cd695e067c430956025) for ![Image 124: {\displaystyle p\in U}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/66f04c97c84237ad610995f15df5cd7fd0034d3e) and ![Image 125: {\displaystyle v_{p}\in \mathbb {R} _{p}^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e09f1fa98664b99dc928121511f6300d5b694fc9) by ![Image 126: {\displaystyle (df)_{p}(v_{p}):=Df|_{p}(v)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ea579b5fc7f106ebd34c1a7e8215bc354a012333), where ![Image 127: {\displaystyle Df|_{p}:\mathbb {R} ^{n}\to \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1cd30b1fefd1a2a67b6518e48f90413ee1716299) is the [total derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Total_derivative "Total derivative") of ![Image 128: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) at ![Image 129: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36). (Recall that the total derivative is a linear transformation.) Of particular interest are the projection maps (also known as coordinate functions) ![Image 130: {\displaystyle \pi ^{i}:\mathbb {R} ^{n}\to \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3e0ecdadbe073adc69fc375abec2aa8e95e108d2), defined by ![Image 131: {\displaystyle x\mapsto x^{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/64f2381d6eb1bf40e235adb04f41b2a869166c1b), where ![Image 132: {\displaystyle x^{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/be8cd88951c7c0e3b181f956531b0a878bbed203) is the _i_ th standard coordinate of ![Image 133: {\displaystyle x\in \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c520ee2cb6ccf8a93c89a8c58a8378796bd52e53). The 1-forms ![Image 134: {\displaystyle d\pi ^{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b36ba4d4d2b66427cfe3142140b27b31c4d192a) are known as the **basic 1-forms**; they are conventionally denoted ![Image 135: {\displaystyle dx^{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f0446df590bcf78b857119928d92ad848c426b2b). If the standard coordinates of ![Image 136: {\displaystyle v_{p}\in \mathbb {R} _{p}^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e09f1fa98664b99dc928121511f6300d5b694fc9) are ![Image 137: {\displaystyle (v^{1},\ldots ,v^{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/43f9e7b2d9e348b94c3cf4320d04addf1af271e4), then application of the definition of ![Image 138: {\displaystyle df}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/53181e2067a93b6bbf150042723cb059d9d2d26f) yields ![Image 139: {\displaystyle dx_{p}^{i}(v_{p})=v^{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7057d0381c3370a6cda9476192eb704e82b90621), so that ![Image 140: {\displaystyle dx_{p}^{i}((e_{j})_{p})=\delta _{j}^{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/adda75200a26cdd508e0ca38486ee2e14ec78b36), where ![Image 141: {\displaystyle \delta _{j}^{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c2c2996dd3d4c7152c99cf5491fd3de9f01b4f09) is the [Kronecker delta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kronecker_delta "Kronecker delta").[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multilinear_form#cite_note-7) Thus, as the dual of the standard basis for ![Image 142: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} _{p}^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/498c9a7322133da0bb10111e65e97aaa4669579b), ![Image 143: {\displaystyle (dx_{p}^{1},\ldots ,dx_{p}^{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3b18be232deb8be621283b63428dd14f77fbd7aa) forms a basis for ![Image 144: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {A}}^{1}(\mathbb {R} _{p}^{n})=(\mathbb {R} _{p}^{n})^{*}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0aeb5e7694d58da007ab1d23be078d4417a5a05d). As a consequence, if ![Image 145: {\displaystyle \omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/48eff443f9de7a985bb94ca3bde20813ea737be8) is a 1-form on ![Image 146: {\displaystyle U}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/458a728f53b9a0274f059cd695e067c430956025), then ![Image 147: {\displaystyle \omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/48eff443f9de7a985bb94ca3bde20813ea737be8) can be written as ![Image 148: {\textstyle \sum a_{i}\,dx^{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d19a18bf92bfba8b5b4e69f6396d517da7632c06) for smooth functions ![Image 149: {\displaystyle a_{i}:U\to \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e153253de00c4748d622eeb6964e098cae98e069). Furthermore, we can derive an expression for ![Image 150: {\displaystyle df}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/53181e2067a93b6bbf150042723cb059d9d2d26f) that coincides with the classical expression for a total differential:

![Image 151: {\displaystyle df=\sum _{i=1}^{n}D_{i}f\;dx^{i}={\partial f \over \partial x^{1}}\,dx^{1}+\cdots +{\partial f \over \partial x^{n}}\,dx^{n}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b9a19109223ac825044eac7a800a89ff90ebaace)
[_Comments on_ _notation:_ In this article, we follow the convention from [tensor calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_calculus "Tensor calculus") and differential geometry in which multivectors and multicovectors are written with lower and upper indices, respectively. Since differential forms are multicovector fields, upper indices are employed to index them.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multilinear_form#cite_note-:0-3) The opposite rule applies to the _components_ of multivectors and multicovectors, which instead are written with upper and lower indices, respectively. For instance, we represent the standard coordinates of vector ![Image 152: {\displaystyle v\in \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/37576c57a5c8ee1c052cb82a6b88aaa4b41764f4) as ![Image 153: {\displaystyle (v^{1},\ldots ,v^{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/43f9e7b2d9e348b94c3cf4320d04addf1af271e4), so that ![Image 154: {\textstyle v=\sum _{i=1}^{n}v^{i}e_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/89846298bfc46de70b1d12c0c4657720af97ede0) in terms of the standard basis ![Image 155: {\displaystyle (e_{1},\ldots ,e_{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/12c4e5513493af62a4753b821ba5bc7842a5278a). In addition, superscripts appearing in the _denominator_ of an expression (as in ![Image 156: {\textstyle {\frac {\partial f}{\partial x^{i}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/836c4f3afd7340922af58b4213b0ac51cde6f55d)) are treated as lower indices in this convention. When indices are applied and interpreted in this manner, the number of upper indices minus the number of lower indices in each term of an expression is conserved, both within the sum and across an equal sign, a feature that serves as a useful mnemonic device and helps pinpoint errors made during manual computation.]

#### Basic operations on differential k-forms

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Multilinear_form&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Basic operations on differential k-forms")]

The _exterior product_ (![Image 157: {\displaystyle \wedge }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1caa4004cb216ef2930bb12fe805a76870caed94)) and _exterior derivative_ (![Image 158: {\displaystyle d}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e85ff03cbe0c7341af6b982e47e9f90d235c66ab)) are two fundamental operations on differential forms. The exterior product of a ![Image 159: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40)-form and an ![Image 160: {\displaystyle \ell }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f066e981e530bacc07efc6a10fa82deee985929e)-form is a ![Image 161: {\displaystyle (k+\ell )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d0e3909ce4ca874da5b57618d00d1776d6cbb372)-form, while the exterior derivative of a ![Image 162: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40)-form is a ![Image 163: {\displaystyle (k+1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3f9f13644a6be482d7ddb19a6e0c706564773085)-form. Thus, both operations generate differential forms of higher degree from those of lower degree.

The [exterior product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_product "Exterior product")![Image 164: {\displaystyle \wedge :\Omega ^{k}(U)\times \Omega ^{\ell }(U)\to \Omega ^{k+\ell }(U)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/940610cf4189415fe8b4e440c11477e4c2aa6aa9) of differential forms is a special case of the exterior product of multicovectors in general (_see above_). As is true in general for the exterior product, the exterior product of differential forms is bilinear, associative, and is [graded-alternating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alternating_algebra "Alternating algebra").

More concretely, if ![Image 165: {\displaystyle \omega =a_{i_{1}\ldots i_{k}}\,dx^{i_{1}}\wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{i_{k}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0041440085c6f1588d1916f8db7901854e36ead8) and ![Image 166: {\displaystyle \eta =a_{j_{1}\ldots j_{\ell }}dx^{j_{1}}\wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{j_{\ell }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a2615dfeeec43c1951785f3c67eec9d707aa7f65), then

![Image 167: {\displaystyle \omega \wedge \eta =a_{i_{1}\ldots i_{k}}a_{j_{1}\ldots j_{\ell }}\,dx^{i_{1}}\wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{i_{k}}\wedge dx^{j_{1}}\wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{j_{\ell }}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6ce15c23b8bb063efb2f320712b0adda73eedc9d)
Furthermore, for any set of indices ![Image 168: {\displaystyle \{\alpha _{1}\ldots ,\alpha _{m}\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/61ff33b18cdeee9254a55d358a29fa65dc70ea6c),

![Image 169: {\displaystyle dx^{\alpha _{1}}\wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{\alpha _{p}}\wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{\alpha _{q}}\wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{\alpha _{m}}=-dx^{\alpha _{1}}\wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{\alpha _{q}}\wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{\alpha _{p}}\wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{\alpha _{m}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b33e46a48cc63bf63b7984a54f03db6825b7abf1)
If ![Image 170: {\displaystyle I=\{i_{1},\ldots ,i_{k}\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/378efa2b1cd266626c69b68959b109bdafe93d81), ![Image 171: {\displaystyle J=\{j_{1},\ldots ,j_{\ell }\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3b49873d92dca78f3a1316be25b1d42358d8b0f2), and ![Image 172: {\displaystyle I\cap J=\varnothing }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/294dc5883f545294c3132a3731b1ac94f29eff52), then the indices of ![Image 173: {\displaystyle \omega \wedge \eta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/60d8de1dff983c3e53066825841da1a3e53be0ef) can be arranged in ascending order by a (finite) sequence of such swaps. Since ![Image 174: {\displaystyle dx^{\alpha }\wedge dx^{\alpha }=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/97ca267ee5d8918de3c9147d163b2241ea8de82c), ![Image 175: {\displaystyle I\cap J\neq \varnothing }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/091af1dd143fdc913b575a7490a8e757b7002eee) implies that ![Image 176: {\displaystyle \omega \wedge \eta =0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/de7799b6ec2bd230af194bf864ebacb3a2d42fff). Finally, as a consequence of bilinearity, if ![Image 177: {\displaystyle \omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/48eff443f9de7a985bb94ca3bde20813ea737be8) and ![Image 178: {\displaystyle \eta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e4d701857cf5fbec133eebaf94deadf722537f64) are the sums of several terms, their exterior product obeys distributivity with respect to each of these terms.

The collection of the exterior products of basic 1-forms ![Image 179: {\displaystyle \{dx^{i_{1}}\wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{i_{k}}\mid 1\leq i_{1}<\cdots <i_{k}\leq n\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87ad356ac38a88b0da11ef9c8ea10629eed646d3) constitutes a basis for the space of differential _k_-forms. Thus, any ![Image 180: {\displaystyle \omega \in \Omega ^{k}(U)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/127a0fa74d0d12d291e1beeb716bd6c1f9f1c683) can be written in the form

![Image 181: {\displaystyle \omega =\sum _{i_{1}<\cdots <i_{k}}a_{i_{1}\ldots i_{k}}\,dx^{i_{1}}\wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{i_{k}},\qquad (*)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2678ceed6f7b6d453498763f42aa87a7d4c4bb65)
where ![Image 182: {\displaystyle a_{i_{1}\ldots i_{k}}:U\to \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b1c4265a13e604195003807c713c9f1f5b457e34) are smooth functions. With each set of indices ![Image 183: {\displaystyle \{i_{1},\ldots ,i_{k}\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b1f63f5ff3a877f5c6d19a9dfdba56f23c54d214) placed in ascending order, (*) is said to be the **standard presentation** of **![Image 184: {\displaystyle \omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/48eff443f9de7a985bb94ca3bde20813ea737be8)**.

In the previous section, the 1-form ![Image 185: {\displaystyle df}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/53181e2067a93b6bbf150042723cb059d9d2d26f) was defined by taking the exterior derivative of the 0-form (continuous function) ![Image 186: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61). We now extend this by defining the exterior derivative operator ![Image 187: {\displaystyle d:\Omega ^{k}(U)\to \Omega ^{k+1}(U)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c524d885268972f87215f864285ec6b32cca503f) for ![Image 188: {\displaystyle k\geq 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d30d7dcf305b7bce39d36df72fe3985b47aa9961). If the standard presentation of ![Image 189: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40)-form ![Image 190: {\displaystyle \omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/48eff443f9de7a985bb94ca3bde20813ea737be8) is given by (*), the ![Image 191: {\displaystyle (k+1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3f9f13644a6be482d7ddb19a6e0c706564773085)-form ![Image 192: {\displaystyle d\omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ad5fe5ef6e9587a089d370dc32119c60e91f1d4c) is defined by

![Image 193: {\displaystyle d\omega :=\sum _{i_{1}<\ldots <i_{k}}da_{i_{1}\ldots i_{k}}\wedge dx^{i_{1}}\wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{i_{k}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cf468688fcd838e182f7f5aa93bf7409d20ff3c8)
A property of ![Image 194: {\displaystyle d}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e85ff03cbe0c7341af6b982e47e9f90d235c66ab) that holds for all smooth forms is that the second exterior derivative of any ![Image 195: {\displaystyle \omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/48eff443f9de7a985bb94ca3bde20813ea737be8) vanishes identically: ![Image 196: {\displaystyle d^{2}\omega =d(d\omega )\equiv 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/11d907e76ac6efece8204fb323a4aa23fb0436f5). This can be established directly from the definition of ![Image 197: {\displaystyle d}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e85ff03cbe0c7341af6b982e47e9f90d235c66ab) and the [equality of mixed second-order partial derivatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry_of_second_derivatives "Symmetry of second derivatives") of ![Image 198: {\displaystyle C^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4fd6a5946b7e916352b0afc557f992328bac85e8) functions (_see the article on [closed and exact forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed\_and\_exact\_differential\_forms "Closed and exact differential forms") for details_).

#### Integration of differential forms and Stokes' theorem for chains

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Multilinear_form&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Integration of differential forms and Stokes' theorem for chains")]

To integrate a differential form over a parameterized domain, we first need to introduce the notion of the **pullback** of a differential form. Roughly speaking, when a differential form is integrated, applying the pullback transforms it in a way that correctly accounts for a change-of-coordinates.

Given a differentiable function ![Image 199: {\displaystyle f:\mathbb {R} ^{n}\to \mathbb {R} ^{m}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/aad78382c3d23bcb4051b3148f1a23b1d0ba52e3) and ![Image 200: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40)-form ![Image 201: {\displaystyle \eta \in \Omega ^{k}(\mathbb {R} ^{m})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/df755883152eb1a0a0792fbb6364d633d82ecc0f), we call ![Image 202: {\displaystyle f^{*}\eta \in \Omega ^{k}(\mathbb {R} ^{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d58b24cb45e3d80f08fccb997d51a4aa34705868) the [pullback](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pullback_(differential_geometry) "Pullback (differential geometry)") of ![Image 203: {\displaystyle \eta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e4d701857cf5fbec133eebaf94deadf722537f64) by ![Image 204: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) and define it as the ![Image 205: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40)-form such that

![Image 206: {\displaystyle (f^{*}\eta )_{p}(v_{1p},\ldots ,v_{kp}):=\eta _{f(p)}(f_{*}(v_{1p}),\ldots ,f_{*}(v_{kp})),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fdf8b7bb48f3dabb6f533262f4b11556b24c560b)
for ![Image 207: {\displaystyle v_{1p},\ldots ,v_{kp}\in \mathbb {R} _{p}^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/70e2330664a83912bb6519b6b6ea22abd4f02440), where ![Image 208: {\displaystyle f_{*}:\mathbb {R} _{p}^{n}\to \mathbb {R} _{f(p)}^{m}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fa8f7c612cc1225376e6fd19a172d807e4c89213) is the map ![Image 209: {\displaystyle v_{p}\mapsto (Df|_{p}(v))_{f(p)}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/70bff8bf07b04b70ef31d13f73f2b9e94c3c80cc).

If ![Image 210: {\displaystyle \omega =f\,dx^{1}\wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/00be3ff995aeeb13f7773bb091386dfbdf5cbea6) is an ![Image 211: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)-form on ![Image 212: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d) (i.e., ![Image 213: {\displaystyle \omega \in \Omega ^{n}(\mathbb {R} ^{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/283ad6b2c86b0522f54051a5d90b00a1efb4faa0)), we define its integral over the unit ![Image 214: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)-cell as the iterated Riemann integral of ![Image 215: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61):

![Image 216: {\displaystyle \int _{[0,1]^{n}}\omega =\int _{[0,1]^{n}}f\,dx^{1}\wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{n}:=\int _{0}^{1}\cdots \int _{0}^{1}f\,dx^{1}\cdots dx^{n}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8c58aea87cda49ec8e41d68a187d8986cfbbc8df)
Next, we consider a domain of integration parameterized by a differentiable function ![Image 217: {\displaystyle c:[0,1]^{n}\to A\subset \mathbb {R} ^{m}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1c7d011587d7fb5323adce431368c7b9c185baa6), known as an **_n_-cube**. To define the integral of ![Image 218: {\displaystyle \omega \in \Omega ^{n}(A)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/64afb1eddf41845b2b2c921e763ee2737b7107aa) over ![Image 219: {\displaystyle c}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/86a67b81c2de995bd608d5b2df50cd8cd7d92455), we "pull back" from ![Image 220: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3) to the unit _n_-cell:

![Image 221: {\displaystyle \int _{c}\omega :=\int _{[0,1]^{n}}c^{*}\omega .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4d70b5af684923666f37de10b676f455e30dcb35)
To integrate over more general domains, we define an **![Image 222: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {n}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/30bcb00be91a9d9c490e21d7ce09631eeef03365)-chain ![Image 223: {\textstyle C=\sum _{i}n_{i}c_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/064badd8af25f8c8bbfee86a59dcc40e276e9a40)** as the formal sum of ![Image 224: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)-cubes and set

![Image 225: {\displaystyle \int _{C}\omega :=\sum _{i}n_{i}\int _{c_{i}}\omega .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2309c3172a1f9e397793ace81f55923d21252929)
An appropriate definition of the ![Image 226: {\displaystyle (n-1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/df88c6333caaf6471cf277f24b802ff9931b133e)-[chain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chain_(algebraic_topology) "Chain (algebraic topology)")![Image 227: {\displaystyle \partial C}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a62fba7a766ac6a450876943bb7923057bc8509c), known as the boundary of ![Image 228: {\displaystyle C}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4fc55753007cd3c18576f7933f6f089196732029),[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multilinear_form#cite_note-8) allows us to state the celebrated **Stokes' theorem** (Stokesâ€“Cartan theorem) for chains in a subset of ![Image 229: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{m}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6a87a024931038d1858dc22e8a194e5978c3412e):

> _If ![Image 230: {\displaystyle \omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest\_v1/media/math/render/svg/48eff443f9de7a985bb94ca3bde20813ea737be8) is a_ _smooth_![Image 231: {\displaystyle (n-1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/df88c6333caaf6471cf277f24b802ff9931b133e)_-form on an open set ![Image 232: {\displaystyle A\subset \mathbb {R} ^{m}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest\_v1/media/math/render/svg/10266452edee9d7b8d7b614b8e4552794b0429ac)_ _and ![Image 233: {\displaystyle C}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest\_v1/media/math/render/svg/4fc55753007cd3c18576f7933f6f089196732029)_ _is a smooth_![Image 234: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)_-chain in ![Image 235: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest\_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3), then![Image 236: {\displaystyle \int \_{C}d\omega =\int \_{\partial C}\omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest\_v1/media/math/render/svg/21ceab34a13452ea39b3514f62cd0fae1f9800db)._

Using more sophisticated machinery (e.g., [germs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germ_(mathematics) "Germ (mathematics)") and [derivations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivation_(differential_algebra) "Derivation (differential algebra)")), the tangent space ![Image 237: {\displaystyle T_{p}M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/71b20e0304a65ead2cafb33412a383b34fe527ca) of any smooth manifold ![Image 238: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) (not necessarily embedded in ![Image 239: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{m}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6a87a024931038d1858dc22e8a194e5978c3412e)) can be defined. Analogously, a differential form ![Image 240: {\displaystyle \omega \in \Omega ^{k}(M)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cd4450f2d016c637248e8f3e283dc7de6b88e34b) on a general smooth manifold is a map ![Image 241: {\displaystyle \omega :p\in M\mapsto \omega _{p}\in {\mathcal {A}}^{k}(T_{p}M)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7202093fb339525d8b32addd5f9d1ec2e5236c07). [Stokes' theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stokes%27_theorem "Stokes' theorem") can be further generalized to arbitrary smooth manifolds-with-boundary and even certain "rough" domains (_see the article on [Stokes' theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stokes%27\_theorem "Stokes' theorem") for details_).
