[![Image 2: Scalar product spaces, inner product spaces, Hermitian product spaces.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Product_Spaces_Drawing_%281%29.png/330px-Product_Spaces_Drawing_%281%29.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Product_Spaces_Drawing_(1).png)

Scalar product spaces, over any field, have "scalar products" that are symmetrical and linear in the first argument. Hermitian product spaces are restricted to the field of complex numbers and have "Hermitian products" that are conjugate-symmetrical and linear in the first argument. Inner product spaces may be defined over any field, having "inner products" that are linear in the first argument, conjugate-symmetrical, and positive-definite. Unlike inner products, scalar products and Hermitian products need not be positive-definite.

In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), an **inner product space** (or, rarely, a **[Hausdorff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hausdorff_space "Hausdorff space") pre-Hilbert space**[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inner_product#cite_note-FOOTNOTETr%C3%A8ves2006112%E2%80%93125-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inner_product#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchaeferWolff199940%E2%80%9345-2)) is a [real vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_vector_space "Real vector space") or a [complex vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_vector_space "Complex vector space") with an [operation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operation_(mathematics) "Operation (mathematics)") called an **inner product**. The inner product of two vectors in the space is a [scalar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_(mathematics) "Scalar (mathematics)"), often denoted with [angle brackets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle_brackets "Angle brackets") such as in ![Image 3: {\displaystyle \langle a,b\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/59f9c6fc93d2e4195b610bc8a9ff366be4485387). Inner products allow formal definitions of intuitive geometric notions, such as lengths, [angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle "Angle"), and [orthogonality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonality "Orthogonality") (zero inner product) of vectors. Inner product spaces generalize [Euclidean vector spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_vector_space "Euclidean vector space"), in which the inner product is the [dot product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dot_product "Dot product") or _scalar product_ of [Cartesian coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinates "Cartesian coordinates"). Inner product spaces of infinite [dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension_(vector_space) "Dimension (vector space)") are widely used in [functional analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functional_analysis "Functional analysis"). Inner product spaces over the [field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)") of [complex numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number") are sometimes referred to as **unitary spaces**. The first usage of the concept of a vector space with an inner product is due to [Giuseppe Peano](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giuseppe_Peano "Giuseppe Peano"), in 1898.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inner_product#cite_note-3)

In this article, _F_ denotes a [field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)") that is either the [real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number")![Image 13: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0522388d36b55de7babe4bbfc49475eaf590c2bd) or the [complex numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number")![Image 14: {\displaystyle \mathbb {C} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8f4d5d3ec97eee8b915d3b14d3fb38579ee639d2) A [scalar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_(mathematics) "Scalar (mathematics)") is thus an element of _F_. A bar over an expression representing a scalar denotes the [complex conjugate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_conjugate "Complex conjugate") of this scalar. A zero vector is denoted ![Image 15: {\displaystyle \mathbf {0} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/62e8c650763635a93ddc69768c3c0c100afe985d) for distinguishing it from the scalar 0.

An _inner product_ space is a [vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space")_V_ over the field _F_ together with an _inner product_, that is, a map ![Image 16: {\displaystyle \langle \cdot ,\cdot \rangle :V\times V\to F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c7871e0b592252e518983a32886d33692d7b4614) that satisfies the following three properties for all vectors ![Image 17: {\displaystyle x,y,z\in V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/66794d289d37b67c3857e7370ecdff292a909f34) and all scalars ![Image 18: {\displaystyle a,b\in F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/40b0ed72d499490ba23dc89160fb8dac6664949b).[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inner_product#cite_note-Jain-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inner_product#cite_note-Prugovec%CC%86ki-6)

If the positive-definiteness condition is replaced by merely requiring that ![Image 19: {\displaystyle \langle x,x\rangle \geq 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8e6bce7d4d0f12edae272a1986cb3d93a305bdcc) for all ![Image 20: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4), then one obtains the definition of _positive semi-definite Hermitian form_. A positive semi-definite Hermitian form ![Image 21: {\displaystyle \langle \cdot ,\cdot \rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9a50080b735975d8001c9552ac2134b49ad534c0) is an inner product if and only if for all ![Image 22: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4), if ![Image 23: {\displaystyle \langle x,x\rangle =0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8ec60641b5cb1dcfe7d2e006530aa4c8870654ca) then ![Image 24: {\displaystyle x=\mathbf {0} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/26c74b7807eed87581b8042b641b976798bf8edd).[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inner_product#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchaeferWolff199944-8)

In the following properties, which result almost immediately from the definition of an inner product, _x_, _y_ and z are arbitrary vectors, and a and b are arbitrary scalars.

Over ![Image 25: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc), conjugate-symmetry reduces to symmetry, and sesquilinearity reduces to bilinearity. Hence an inner product on a real vector space is a _positive-definite symmetric [bilinear form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bilinear\_form "Bilinear form")_. The [binomial expansion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binomial_expansion "Binomial expansion") of a square becomes ![Image 26: {\displaystyle \langle x+y,x+y\rangle =\langle x,x\rangle +2\langle x,y\rangle +\langle y,y\rangle .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/285932a02f21f9644005947ac62ba81d1ac3f3a9)

Several notations are used for inner products, including ![Image 27: {\displaystyle \langle \cdot ,\cdot \rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9a50080b735975d8001c9552ac2134b49ad534c0), ![Image 28: {\displaystyle \left(\cdot ,\cdot \right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4361973c49cd22e26454993b5fe8de0fb10f12a5), ![Image 29: {\displaystyle \langle \cdot |\cdot \rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d36ddc87338be38721a2d721d1a6cc9ab294474a) and ![Image 30: {\displaystyle \left(\cdot |\cdot \right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/218bb58dc0d1c39bfc278ea3c6b064db2b053faf), as well as the usual dot product.

Some authors, especially in [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics") and [matrix algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_algebra "Matrix algebra"), prefer to define inner products and sesquilinear forms with linearity in the second argument rather than the first. Then the first argument becomes conjugate linear, rather than the second. [Bra-ket notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bra%E2%80%93ket_notation "Bra–ket notation") in [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics") also uses slightly different notation, i.e. ![Image 31: {\displaystyle \langle \cdot |\cdot \rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d36ddc87338be38721a2d721d1a6cc9ab294474a), where ![Image 32: {\displaystyle \langle x|y\rangle :=\left(y,x\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/36b723091bb4ba6672b91683322a04f797563d72).

### Complex coordinate space

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Inner_product_space&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Complex coordinate space")]

The general form of an inner product on ![Image 64: {\displaystyle \mathbb {C} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a53b4e76242764d1bca004168353c380fef25258) is known as the [Hermitian form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermitian_form "Hermitian form") and is given by ![Image 65: {\displaystyle \langle x,y\rangle =y^{\dagger }\mathbf {M} x={\overline {x^{\dagger }\mathbf {M} y}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0bdf7fb19ffc1f7881b0eab1a8d75762b464aa00) where ![Image 66: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) is any [Hermitian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermitian_matrix "Hermitian matrix")[positive-definite matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positive-definite_matrix "Positive-definite matrix") and ![Image 67: {\displaystyle y^{\dagger }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1354f8c5688a03cf435a56acc9f78891a905491d) is the [conjugate transpose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conjugate_transpose "Conjugate transpose") of ![Image 68: {\displaystyle y.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/83f72471aff7c6fbb27df0f971283a068efe091f) For the real case, this corresponds to the dot product of the results of directionally-different [scaling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scaling_(geometry) "Scaling (geometry)") of the two vectors, with positive [scale factors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scale_factor "Scale factor") and orthogonal directions of scaling. It is a [weighted-sum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weight_function "Weight function") version of the dot product with positive weights—up to an orthogonal transformation.

The article on [Hilbert spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_spaces "Hilbert spaces") has several examples of inner product spaces, wherein the metric induced by the inner product yields a [complete metric space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complete_metric_space "Complete metric space"). An example of an inner product space which induces an incomplete metric is the space ![Image 69: {\displaystyle C([a,b])}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d8e4f44fa2823fcdffc5fc26981c0d4fa57cade9) of continuous complex valued functions ![Image 70: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) and ![Image 71: {\displaystyle g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3556280e66fe2c0d0140df20935a6f057381d77) on the interval ![Image 72: {\displaystyle [a,b].}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3ba5cb29655f824ce80a0b6a32d9326d0e8742cd) The inner product is ![Image 73: {\displaystyle \langle f,g\rangle =\int _{a}^{b}f(t){\overline {g(t)}}\,\mathrm {d} t.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3559c0ce2344126cf72a0915602dafc6386ef041) This space is not complete; consider for example, for the interval [−1, 1] the sequence of continuous "step" functions, ![Image 74: {\displaystyle \{f_{k}\}_{k},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d855663ef5612bc74419fef776c9d80852e124a0) defined by: ![Image 75: {\displaystyle f_{k}(t)={\begin{cases}0&t\in [-1,0]\\1&t\in \left[{\tfrac {1}{k}},1\right]\\kt&t\in \left(0,{\tfrac {1}{k}}\right)\end{cases}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7e63fdb1fef334282359a93c0b232c0de8125f28)

This sequence is a [Cauchy sequence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cauchy_sequence "Cauchy sequence") for the norm induced by the preceding inner product, which does not converge to a _continuous_ function.

For real [random variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Random_variable "Random variable")![Image 76: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) and ![Image 77: {\displaystyle Y,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a3765557b7effa1a5f2f4dce9c80a25973b7009f) the [expected value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expected_value "Expected value") of their product ![Image 78: {\displaystyle \langle X,Y\rangle =\mathbb {E} [XY]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f4faa9acf0f42fa31023970f64f80526fcf4ecac) is an inner product.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inner_product#cite_note-9)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inner_product#cite_note-10)[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inner_product#cite_note-11) In this case, ![Image 79: {\displaystyle \langle X,X\rangle =0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c427982da938cd1340375129a47d39b94e90a9cb) if and only if ![Image 80: {\displaystyle \mathbb {P} [X=0]=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bbb889f8f5562ffa91331ceb66a78d4916a8e430) (that is, ![Image 81: {\displaystyle X=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d519e9e94f279ea82581dfa70a2444e896e2d860)[almost surely](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Almost_surely "Almost surely")), where ![Image 82: {\displaystyle \mathbb {P} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1053af9e662ceaf56c4455f90e0f67273422eded) denotes the [probability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability "Probability") of the event. This definition of expectation as inner product can be extended to [random vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Random_vector "Random vector") as well.

The inner product for complex square matrices of the same size is the [Frobenius inner product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frobenius_inner_product "Frobenius inner product")![Image 83: {\displaystyle \langle A,B\rangle :=\operatorname {tr} \left(AB^{\dagger }\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47a18dae3ee440abe9c2fd0210f97d4b17229eeb). Since trace and transposition are linear and the conjugation is on the second matrix, it is a sesquilinear operator. We further get Hermitian symmetry by, ![Image 84: {\displaystyle \langle A,B\rangle =\operatorname {tr} \left(AB^{\dagger }\right)={\overline {\operatorname {tr} \left(BA^{\dagger }\right)}}={\overline {\left\langle B,A\right\rangle }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9f4b554c1c3435d5ea0e11427b71bd8adab9e07c) Finally, since for ![Image 85: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3) nonzero, ![Image 86: {\displaystyle \langle A,A\rangle =\sum _{ij}\left|A_{ij}\right|^{2}>0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f3e517fcecdab0fe8518ea422decfb47d9d82626), we get that the Frobenius inner product is positive definite too, and so is an inner product.

### Vector spaces with forms

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Inner_product_space&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Vector spaces with forms")]

On an inner product space, or more generally a vector space with a [nondegenerate form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nondegenerate_form "Nondegenerate form") (hence an isomorphism ![Image 87: {\displaystyle V\to V^{*}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7e86ea24eb05dd60cbcf6eb787a5f07e3861984e)), vectors can be sent to covectors (in coordinates, via transpose), so that one can take the inner product and outer product of two vectors—not simply of a vector and a covector.

Basic results, terminology, and definitions
-------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Inner_product_space&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Basic results, terminology, and definitions")]

Every inner product space induces a [norm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norm_(mathematics) "Norm (mathematics)"), called its _canonical norm_, that is defined by ![Image 88: {\displaystyle \|x\|={\sqrt {\langle x,x\rangle }}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cf3bc02e50c147bed811cfa18ee322665cb864bc) With this norm, every inner product space becomes a [normed vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normed_vector_space "Normed vector space").

So, every general property of normed vector spaces applies to inner product spaces. In particular, one has the following properties:

[Absolute homogeneity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_homogeneity "Absolute homogeneity")![Image 89: {\displaystyle \|ax\|=|a|\,\|x\|}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7b50e6affdd645fa0292b4dc769cd51f23642bd1) for every ![Image 90: {\displaystyle x\in V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fa374e20b2db7f6b8caa71ff1865f7f84f215c9f) and ![Image 91: {\displaystyle a\in F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/130d30b75a5437ad01787d25462043ac3a9aee3c) (this results from ![Image 92: {\displaystyle \langle ax,ax\rangle =a{\overline {a}}\langle x,x\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/245b21847c20211cd8d5eda19afedd24c8f1aeaa)). [Triangle inequality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle_inequality "Triangle inequality")![Image 93: {\displaystyle \|x+y\|\leq \|x\|+\|y\|}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4359c19d7e5ed2677bd29b8d46791de01b935b5b) for ![Image 94: {\displaystyle x,y\in V.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/afae3d54ba364e742c2efa040fc447300966c7f2) These two properties show that one has indeed a norm.[Cauchy–Schwarz inequality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cauchy%E2%80%93Schwarz_inequality "Cauchy–Schwarz inequality")![Image 95: {\displaystyle |\langle x,y\rangle |\leq \|x\|\,\|y\|}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f146a809cdbeebe4de138cc45d3073b26e6e9312) for every ![Image 96: {\displaystyle x,y\in V,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b2958b4ac23c2fc37e5a332ac27193f757ba1452) with equality if and only if ![Image 97: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) and ![Image 98: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d) are [linearly dependent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linearly_independent "Linearly independent"). [Parallelogram law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram_law "Parallelogram law")![Image 99: {\displaystyle \|x+y\|^{2}+\|x-y\|^{2}=2\|x\|^{2}+2\|y\|^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b7591d0aef3015a635dae6ba304875592764c3c5) for every ![Image 100: {\displaystyle x,y\in V.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/afae3d54ba364e742c2efa040fc447300966c7f2) The parallelogram law is a necessary and sufficient condition for a norm to be defined by an inner product. [Polarization identity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polarization_identity "Polarization identity")![Image 101: {\displaystyle \|x+y\|^{2}=\|x\|^{2}+\|y\|^{2}+2\operatorname {Re} \langle x,y\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/416fb3ae3d05daeaf2bec012f6fe787bd8014705) for every ![Image 102: {\displaystyle x,y\in V.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/afae3d54ba364e742c2efa040fc447300966c7f2) The inner product can be retrieved from the norm by the polarization identity, since its imaginary part is the real part of ![Image 103: {\displaystyle \langle x,iy\rangle .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c557742cdf5903226465ea9e4d95ef3b53495ba2)[Ptolemy's inequality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ptolemy%27s_inequality "Ptolemy's inequality")![Image 104: {\displaystyle \|x-y\|\,\|z\|~+~\|y-z\|\,\|x\|~\geq ~\|x-z\|\,\|y\|}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9d380a0fe0fd877604f8eda3e7e53ccf178e5820) for every ![Image 105: {\displaystyle x,y,z\in V.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0008353bd449cdc3beba7479d1956df6e467f334) Ptolemy's inequality is a necessary and sufficient condition for a [seminorm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seminorm "Seminorm") to be the norm defined by an inner product.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inner_product#cite_note-12)[Orthogonality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonality_(mathematics) "Orthogonality (mathematics)") Two vectors ![Image y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d) are said to be _orthogonal_, often written ![Image 108: {\displaystyle x\perp y,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/023639aa862e4908285cb55cd4071e430d57caa7) if their inner product is zero, that is, if ![Image 109: {\displaystyle \langle x,y\rangle =0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8f7176152c1a5255729e4079fbdca24b8e2f30d4)

 This happens if and only if ![Image 110: {\displaystyle \|x\|\leq \|x+sy\|}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0c98bf1d9bf5270ad74505b15cea51347a6abc9b) for all scalars ![Image 111: {\displaystyle s,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e5748cdb81bf00075de8e7e6828c343687513830)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inner_product#cite_note-FOOTNOTERudin1991306%E2%80%93312-13) and if and only if the real-valued function ![Image 112: {\displaystyle f(s):=\|x+sy\|^{2}-\|x\|^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/214fc33ef1518f7a0e62fc22d771d861ac00f8d5) is non-negative. (This is a consequence of the fact that, if ![Image 113: {\displaystyle y\neq 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fe09d44abefff1fcada712301b088a303591f9e5) then the scalar ![Image 114: {\displaystyle s_{0}=-{\tfrac {\overline {\langle x,y\rangle }}{\|y\|^{2}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8090f7d93bf4cc52647d74af54aeada1498e83a5) minimizes ![Image 115: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) with value ![Image 116: {\displaystyle f\left(s_{0}\right)=-{\tfrac {|\langle x,y\rangle |^{2}}{\|y\|^{2}}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a77bd79da0b489c8443d8b27497ee7c6b91ceb62) which is always non positive).

 For a _complex_ inner product space ![Image 117: {\displaystyle H,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ef601e1519093ba6c2944b945882c119f990e704) a linear operator ![Image 118: {\displaystyle T:V\to V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f0fabb548ced097e03f45cf54dbb066d23d010f3) is identically ![Image 119: {\displaystyle 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2aae8864a3c1fec9585261791a809ddec1489950) if and only if ![Image 120: {\displaystyle x\perp Tx}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b335868b131345d8f865cb00de211d2228357919) for every ![Image 121: {\displaystyle x\in V.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2db8a2c9b98774154e833074ffbab1d3fc6c0f61)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inner_product#cite_note-FOOTNOTERudin1991306%E2%80%93312-13) This is not true in general for real inner product spaces, as it is a consequence of conjugate symmetry being distinct from symmetry for complex inner products. A counterexample in a real inner product space is ![Image 122: {\displaystyle T}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ec7200acd984a1d3a3d7dc455e262fbe54f7f6e0) a 90° rotation in ![Image 123: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e150115ab9f63023215109595b76686a1ff890fd), which maps every vector to an orthogonal vector but is not identically ![Image 124: {\displaystyle 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2aae8864a3c1fec9585261791a809ddec1489950). [Orthogonal complement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_complement "Orthogonal complement")The _orthogonal complement_ of a subset ![Image 125: {\displaystyle C\subseteq V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3674daa0563e2c8ecaaa5f0fbac07010419c4269) is the set ![Image 126: {\displaystyle C^{\bot }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e9ba6bddf4b4b174c7ce96ac011aae08f4990689) of the vectors that are orthogonal to all elements of C; that is, ![Image 127: {\displaystyle C^{\bot }:=\{\,y\in V:\langle y,c\rangle =0{\text{ for all }}c\in C\,\}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2882ec7598214b9d25588d03e99d730986cca357) This set ![Image 128: {\displaystyle C^{\bot }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e9ba6bddf4b4b174c7ce96ac011aae08f4990689) is always a closed vector subspace of ![Image 129: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) and if the [closure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closure_(topology) "Closure (topology)")![Image 130: {\displaystyle \operatorname {cl} _{V}C}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b7e16ec0acae78a283477c0ec1938bbbb828693a) of ![Image 131: {\displaystyle C}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4fc55753007cd3c18576f7933f6f089196732029) in ![Image 132: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) is a vector subspace then ![Image 133: {\displaystyle \operatorname {cl} _{V}C=\left(C^{\bot }\right)^{\bot }.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/234d78f85d081e8853ec79668967f08b44119532)[Pythagorean theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagorean_theorem "Pythagorean theorem") If ![Image 134: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) and ![Image 135: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d) are orthogonal, then ![Image 136: {\displaystyle \|x\|^{2}+\|y\|^{2}=\|x+y\|^{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/92c12007742388779a5f0f281c7fd58dee4cd1bb) This may be proved by expressing the squared norms in terms of the inner products, using additivity for expanding the right-hand side of the equation.

 The name _Pythagorean theorem_ arises from the geometric interpretation in [Euclidean geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_geometry "Euclidean geometry"). [Parseval's identity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parseval%27s_identity "Parseval's identity") An [induction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_induction "Mathematical induction") on the Pythagorean theorem yields: if ![Image 137: {\displaystyle x_{1},\ldots ,x_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/737e02a5fbf8bc31d443c91025339f9fd1de1065) are pairwise orthogonal, then ![Image 138: {\displaystyle \sum _{i=1}^{n}\|x_{i}\|^{2}=\left\|\sum _{i=1}^{n}x_{i}\right\|^{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0c827e483c60b57734c04f1ca9f2318fb042e57d)[Angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle "Angle") When ![Image 139: {\displaystyle \langle x,y\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9df1806ebe1fed1a728b18aed82c30be8b2a0acb) is a real number then the Cauchy–Schwarz inequality implies that ![Image 140: {\textstyle {\frac {\langle x,y\rangle }{\|x\|\,\|y\|}}\in [-1,1],}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/971cf06efbbe8a5278a6effdacadd021c1f693a7) and thus that ![Image 141: {\displaystyle \angle (x,y)=\arccos {\frac {\langle x,y\rangle }{\|x\|\,\|y\|}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a31c0c22827b5b760afbbadbc32886080c3ce4b3) is a real number. This allows defining the (non oriented) _angle_ of two vectors in modern definitions of [Euclidean geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_geometry "Euclidean geometry") in terms of [linear algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_algebra "Linear algebra"). This is also used in [data analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_analysis "Data analysis"), under the name "[cosine similarity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosine_similarity "Cosine similarity")", for comparing two vectors of data. Furthermore, if ![Image 142: {\displaystyle \langle x,y\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9df1806ebe1fed1a728b18aed82c30be8b2a0acb) is negative, the angle ![Image 143: {\displaystyle \angle (x,y)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3367b17ce7287ab814c34d00135234a07ae0228f) is larger than 90 degrees. This property is often used in computer graphics (e.g., in [back-face culling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back-face_culling "Back-face culling")) to analyze a direction without having to evaluate [trigonometric functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trigonometric_functions "Trigonometric functions").

### Real and complex parts of inner products

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Inner_product_space&action=edit&section=16 "Edit section: Real and complex parts of inner products")]

Suppose that ![Image 144: {\displaystyle \langle \cdot ,\cdot \rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9a50080b735975d8001c9552ac2134b49ad534c0) is an inner product on ![Image 145: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) (so it is antilinear in its second argument). The [polarization identity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polarization_identity "Polarization identity") shows that the [real part](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_part "Real part") of the inner product is ![Image 146: {\displaystyle \operatorname {Re} \langle x,y\rangle ={\frac {1}{4}}\left(\|x+y\|^{2}-\|x-y\|^{2}\right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4880b60107dbbd2e5611be9bbc8d68bae61e7899)

If ![Image 147: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) is a real vector space then ![Image 148: {\displaystyle \langle x,y\rangle =\operatorname {Re} \langle x,y\rangle ={\frac {1}{4}}\left(\|x+y\|^{2}-\|x-y\|^{2}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9b494e58d55a4b1fd626c64dce7e1eae9eb0502a) and the [imaginary part](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imaginary_part "Imaginary part") (also called the _complex part_) of ![Image 149: {\displaystyle \langle \cdot ,\cdot \rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9a50080b735975d8001c9552ac2134b49ad534c0) is always ![Image 150: {\displaystyle 0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/916e773e0593223c306a3e6852348177d1934962)

Assume for the rest of this section that ![Image 151: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) is a complex vector space. The [polarization identity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polarization_identity "Polarization identity") for complex vector spaces shows that ![Image 152: {\displaystyle {\begin{alignedat}{4}\langle x,\ y\rangle &={\frac {1}{4}}\left(\|x+y\|^{2}-\|x-y\|^{2}+i\|x+iy\|^{2}-i\|x-iy\|^{2}\right)\\&=\operatorname {Re} \langle x,y\rangle +i\operatorname {Re} \langle x,iy\rangle .\\\end{alignedat}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0f86bf15b338b6c4f651d6c6d3af44f8cf7b7dbe)

The map defined by ![Image 153: {\displaystyle \langle x\mid y\rangle =\langle y,x\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f1a0e8e02a1536b68a985dd094870ace6dbb8947) for all ![Image 154: {\displaystyle x,y\in V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/89e1c8106b3df898a0338c2ebce32902e4941fc8) satisfies the axioms of the inner product except that it is antilinear in its _first_, rather than its second, argument. The real part of both ![Image 155: {\displaystyle \langle x\mid y\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5074ba53554d9c746e5ab98e1a336e5d709b5a20) and ![Image 156: {\displaystyle \langle x,y\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9df1806ebe1fed1a728b18aed82c30be8b2a0acb) are equal to ![Image 157: {\displaystyle \operatorname {Re} \langle x,y\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/90afb230cc1b685f44db36670940ea918917b302) but the inner products differ in their complex part: ![Image 158: {\displaystyle {\begin{alignedat}{4}\langle x\mid y\rangle &={\frac {1}{4}}\left(\|x+y\|^{2}-\|x-y\|^{2}-i\|x+iy\|^{2}+i\|x-iy\|^{2}\right)\\&=\operatorname {Re} \langle x,y\rangle -i\operatorname {Re} \langle x,iy\rangle .\\\end{alignedat}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c231489714dbb4b6660191ac024fe34afa9e8b59)

The last equality is similar to the formula [expressing a linear functional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_and_imaginary_parts_of_a_linear_functional "Real and imaginary parts of a linear functional") in terms of its real part.

These formulas show that every complex inner product is completely determined by its real part. Moreover, this real part defines an inner product on ![Image 159: {\displaystyle V,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ace9595e3ce66fdec7e9d30202626accd676b11e) considered as a real vector space. There is thus a one-to-one correspondence between complex inner products on a complex vector space ![Image 160: {\displaystyle V,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ace9595e3ce66fdec7e9d30202626accd676b11e) and real inner products on ![Image 161: {\displaystyle V.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1b2661a49b86bd1a5548e527bbfb068aa9f59978)

For example, suppose that ![Image 162: {\displaystyle V=\mathbb {C} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4c72e2d1be4153418b0ef752c7aec4dbe5f094e8) for some integer ![Image 163: {\displaystyle n>0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/57ab13b00b6f8bb19aea0dcfcc78f18a2b6cbffc) When ![Image 164: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) is considered as a real vector space in the usual way (meaning that it is identified with the ![Image 165: {\displaystyle 2n-}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4657df6e16a7ff80fb893c1e3c0a53010e330837)dimensional real vector space ![Image 166: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{2n},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/244fdd1e23c4d2f60a8da8ff339e14af86a0382d) with each ![Image 167: {\displaystyle \left(a_{1}+ib_{1},\ldots ,a_{n}+ib_{n}\right)\in \mathbb {C} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c112c8fe5c9830ae2dfe135308d7f4f491a02138) identified with ![Image 168: {\displaystyle \left(a_{1},b_{1},\ldots ,a_{n},b_{n}\right)\in \mathbb {R} ^{2n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/539952abc4b278f097f8fc64b85801052c62e7f3)), then the [dot product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dot_product "Dot product")![Image 169: {\displaystyle x\,\cdot \,y=\left(x_{1},\ldots ,x_{2n}\right)\,\cdot \,\left(y_{1},\ldots ,y_{2n}\right):=x_{1}y_{1}+\cdots +x_{2n}y_{2n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/79e07b04a6ba781d1ba197d0237b69479388c9da) defines a real inner product on this space. The unique complex inner product ![Image 170: {\displaystyle \langle \,\cdot ,\cdot \,\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bd6032140d9a706feba8f8a6347597ea068a7219) on ![Image 171: {\displaystyle V=\mathbb {C} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4c72e2d1be4153418b0ef752c7aec4dbe5f094e8) induced by the dot product is the map that sends ![Image 172: {\displaystyle c=\left(c_{1},\ldots ,c_{n}\right),d=\left(d_{1},\ldots ,d_{n}\right)\in \mathbb {C} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d7250f3fdb0599ca8cd813bfba2d04e234eb6e4e) to ![Image 173: {\displaystyle \langle c,d\rangle :=c_{1}{\overline {d_{1}}}+\cdots +c_{n}{\overline {d_{n}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0697e270aa830b3aade33e6e2f28ab7632bd8ba0) (because the real part of this map ![Image 174: {\displaystyle \langle \,\cdot ,\cdot \,\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bd6032140d9a706feba8f8a6347597ea068a7219) is equal to the dot product).

#### Real vs. complex inner products

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Inner_product_space&action=edit&section=17 "Edit section: Real vs. complex inner products")]

Let ![Image 175: {\displaystyle V_{\mathbb {R} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/43e1ed20fc2bddc69536b4c4f85a4c5d81a62ecf) denote ![Image 176: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) considered as a vector space over the real numbers rather than complex numbers. The [real part](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_part "Real part") of the complex inner product ![Image 177: {\displaystyle \langle x,y\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9df1806ebe1fed1a728b18aed82c30be8b2a0acb) is the map ![Image 178: {\displaystyle \langle x,y\rangle _{\mathbb {R} }=\operatorname {Re} \langle x,y\rangle ~:~V_{\mathbb {R} }\times V_{\mathbb {R} }\to \mathbb {R} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3f6bf74f5ca9b18d42b1f5bfb50a43661c2204f6) which necessarily forms a real inner product on the real vector space ![Image 179: {\displaystyle V_{\mathbb {R} }.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b45a2de7adf0eebcb8df7271dd5ad002337f3321) Every inner product on a real vector space is a [bilinear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bilinear_map "Bilinear map") and [symmetric map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetric_map "Symmetric map").

For example, if ![Image 180: {\displaystyle V=\mathbb {C} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2f474512c3b57d03034c79cc18bb5ecf448c3f51) with inner product ![Image 181: {\displaystyle \langle x,y\rangle =x{\overline {y}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/098403440f3a4de380a1eaea1b212f73240f16b1) where ![Image 182: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) is a vector space over the field ![Image 183: {\displaystyle \mathbb {C} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c6ff6a3dc2982018ff20f1d2c927afc74a217be6) then ![Image 184: {\displaystyle V_{\mathbb {R} }=\mathbb {R} ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/31e3d58ce17b831e779ace3ec350cc3a76fdd54b) is a vector space over ![Image 185: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc) and ![Image 186: {\displaystyle \langle x,y\rangle _{\mathbb {R} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3736dae913b80683b7c360f23c37830f1ed77520) is the [dot product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dot_product "Dot product")![Image 187: {\displaystyle x\cdot y,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/699802977820dd08f3741b402f6a5d2ae097dc02) where ![Image 188: {\displaystyle x=a+ib\in V=\mathbb {C} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fc60ff91956ac3fc7f9518a949e843cb231db436) is identified with the point ![Image 189: {\displaystyle (a,b)\in V_{\mathbb {R} }=\mathbb {R} ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/afb7730b87d50f70f03e98c5bb8ac03c9bafe8e6) (and similarly for ![Image 190: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d)); thus the standard inner product ![Image 191: {\displaystyle \langle x,y\rangle =x{\overline {y}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/098403440f3a4de380a1eaea1b212f73240f16b1) on ![Image 192: {\displaystyle \mathbb {C} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f9add4085095b9b6d28d045fd9c92c2c09f549a7) is an "extension" the dot product . Also, had ![Image 193: {\displaystyle \langle x,y\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9df1806ebe1fed1a728b18aed82c30be8b2a0acb) been instead defined to be the **[symmetric map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inner_product#math_Symmetry)**![Image 194: {\displaystyle \langle x,y\rangle =xy}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5bb0d72c852448abffb7eee62c451bcd58e7776e) (rather than the usual **[conjugate symmetric map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inner_product#math_Conjugate_symmetry)**![Image 195: {\displaystyle \langle x,y\rangle =x{\overline {y}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47ffd1651587afb92a776d9bc0e9d1299fe358b8)) then its real part ![Image 196: {\displaystyle \langle x,y\rangle _{\mathbb {R} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3736dae913b80683b7c360f23c37830f1ed77520) would _not_ be the dot product; furthermore, without the complex conjugate, if ![Image 197: {\displaystyle x\in \mathbb {C} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/75e8f71807e571bdc2c1545cfe8eebfe26943789) but ![Image 198: {\displaystyle x\not \in \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/aad1ce3995cdfc0385c46342d0403b8ceaa3136e) then ![Image 199: {\displaystyle \langle x,x\rangle =xx=x^{2}\not \in [0,\infty )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9380c0cf12043a4c60abd5c57f271a5062b4114b) so the assignment ![Image 200: {\textstyle x\mapsto {\sqrt {\langle x,x\rangle }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a6654dc360a1ff5197b40719ff0da2795b0db628) would not define a norm.

The next examples show that although real and complex inner products have many properties and results in common, they are not entirely interchangeable. For instance, if ![Image 201: {\displaystyle \langle x,y\rangle =0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e39d8b3de5c9ae1f31e5a6515f1a1156c5187702) then ![Image 202: {\displaystyle \langle x,y\rangle _{\mathbb {R} }=0,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/50744b7b65cfd568184b8c66f20b83cbf1c05de6) but the next example shows that the converse is in general _not_ true. Given any ![Image 203: {\displaystyle x\in V,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0bcfd2b7752b39f2d16a9140f288e243f84c4c30) the vector ![Image 204: {\displaystyle ix}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8620946c5b679256caa25581a6fb98efa502141e) (which is the vector ![Image 205: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) rotated by 90°) belongs to ![Image 206: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) and so also belongs to ![Image 207: {\displaystyle V_{\mathbb {R} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/43e1ed20fc2bddc69536b4c4f85a4c5d81a62ecf) (although scalar multiplication of ![Image 208: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) by ![Image 209: {\displaystyle i={\sqrt {-1}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/370c8cebe9634fbfc84c29ea61680b0ad4a1ae0d) is not defined in ![Image 210: {\displaystyle V_{\mathbb {R} },}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d57c04b67cb40b5edffff9c835c3d1f1e17f73f9) the vector in ![Image 211: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) denoted by ![Image 212: {\displaystyle ix}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8620946c5b679256caa25581a6fb98efa502141e) is nevertheless still also an element of ![Image 213: {\displaystyle V_{\mathbb {R} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/43e1ed20fc2bddc69536b4c4f85a4c5d81a62ecf)). For the complex inner product, ![Image 214: {\displaystyle \langle x,ix\rangle =-i\|x\|^{2},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/59df9b1a5aa455498d693f7b31ae21addd5eac16) whereas for the real inner product the value is always ![Image 215: {\displaystyle \langle x,ix\rangle _{\mathbb {R} }=0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f4e6670b16315ba15bf0ee997f9216acde2a8b71)

If ![Image 216: {\displaystyle \langle \,\cdot ,\cdot \,\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bd6032140d9a706feba8f8a6347597ea068a7219) is a complex inner product and ![Image 217: {\displaystyle A:V\to V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2348ed6dd9b0f7cb231284ab34d02a6b60379ab8) is a continuous linear operator that satisfies ![Image 218: {\displaystyle \langle x,Ax\rangle =0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8f5c57c9614087f44141f07f308a86a9f8f136f0) for all ![Image 219: {\displaystyle x\in V,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0bcfd2b7752b39f2d16a9140f288e243f84c4c30) then ![Image 220: {\displaystyle A=0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/01dbe289a1d153a8232c394390712801aaf2613b) This statement is no longer true if ![Image 221: {\displaystyle \langle \,\cdot ,\cdot \,\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bd6032140d9a706feba8f8a6347597ea068a7219) is instead a real inner product, as this next example shows. Suppose that ![Image 222: {\displaystyle V=\mathbb {C} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2f474512c3b57d03034c79cc18bb5ecf448c3f51) has the inner product ![Image 223: {\displaystyle \langle x,y\rangle :=x{\overline {y}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1d8fa3d11722ab4699ddde7bfaeda3c5eb076304) mentioned above. Then the map ![Image 224: {\displaystyle A:V\to V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2348ed6dd9b0f7cb231284ab34d02a6b60379ab8) defined by ![Image 225: {\displaystyle Ax=ix}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9a2be348112e904b0634bc3fec32d4431110e94e) is a linear map (linear for both ![Image 226: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) and ![Image 227: {\displaystyle V_{\mathbb {R} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/43e1ed20fc2bddc69536b4c4f85a4c5d81a62ecf)) that denotes rotation by ![Image 228: {\displaystyle 90^{\circ }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c326d317eddef3ad3e6625e018a708e290a039f6) in the plane. Because ![Image 229: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) and ![Image 230: {\displaystyle Ax}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4527572c17e687922277b4ffe25b28c9411fc0a9) are perpendicular vectors and ![Image 231: {\displaystyle \langle x,Ax\rangle _{\mathbb {R} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c84d35607d2bba1ca9d815d9bc38f42c0b7d5625) is just the dot product, ![Image 232: {\displaystyle \langle x,Ax\rangle _{\mathbb {R} }=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a7afcac40a427167f955b2f7c5697cccfca78788) for all vectors ![Image 233: {\displaystyle x;}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/05a950b252371110b85a784de7babc2448d28cc9) nevertheless, this rotation map ![Image 234: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3) is certainly not identically ![Image 235: {\displaystyle 0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/916e773e0593223c306a3e6852348177d1934962) In contrast, using the complex inner product gives ![Image 236: {\displaystyle \langle x,Ax\rangle =-i\|x\|^{2},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4d202dae8bfa576bb636ded5a6a518f766c30efe) which (as expected) is not identically zero.

Orthonormal sequences
---------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Inner_product_space&action=edit&section=18 "Edit section: Orthonormal sequences")]

Let ![Image 237: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) be a finite dimensional inner product space of dimension ![Image 238: {\displaystyle n.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e59df02a9f67a5da3c220f1244c99a46cc4eb1c6) Recall that every [basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basis_(linear_algebra) "Basis (linear algebra)") of ![Image 239: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) consists of exactly ![Image 240: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) linearly independent vectors. Using the [Gram–Schmidt process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gram%E2%80%93Schmidt_process "Gram–Schmidt process") we may start with an arbitrary basis and transform it into an orthonormal basis. That is, into a basis in which all the elements are orthogonal and have unit norm. In symbols, a basis ![Image 241: {\displaystyle \{e_{1},\ldots ,e_{n}\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/014c2407d0cf40698e1a1ba829d2622919d89ede) is orthonormal if ![Image 242: {\displaystyle \langle e_{i},e_{j}\rangle =0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a263693d5bf1481495410877131a2bb5b5221985) for every ![Image 243: {\displaystyle i\neq j}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d95aeb406bb427ac96806bc00c30c91d31b858be) and ![Image 244: {\displaystyle \langle e_{i},e_{i}\rangle =\|e_{a}\|^{2}=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/39c0662b6833537b00daec7685b1c6cce471cfc7) for each index ![Image 245: {\displaystyle i.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6ffcf9ad7ad44f04fa43c5b604b4801e089981cb)

This definition of orthonormal basis generalizes to the case of infinite-dimensional inner product spaces in the following way. Let ![Image 246: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) be any inner product space. Then a collection ![Image 247: {\displaystyle E=\left\{e_{a}\right\}_{a\in A}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8472f13c8f7e47a04bd8faa166efd8a5fe29b759) is a _basis_ for ![Image 248: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) if the subspace of ![Image 249: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) generated by finite linear combinations of elements of ![Image 250: {\displaystyle E}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4232c9de2ee3eec0a9c0a19b15ab92daa6223f9b) is dense in ![Image 251: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) (in the norm induced by the inner product). Say that ![Image 252: {\displaystyle E}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4232c9de2ee3eec0a9c0a19b15ab92daa6223f9b) is an _[orthonormal basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthonormal\_basis "Orthonormal basis")_ for ![Image 253: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) if it is a basis and ![Image 254: {\displaystyle \left\langle e_{a},e_{b}\right\rangle =0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/64a834bf013d73d8b5c8f6df291d78850e661931) if ![Image 255: {\displaystyle a\neq b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3bf17ce351d08f2b7a3e54ba3aa2132f260c84f6) and ![Image 256: {\displaystyle \langle e_{a},e_{a}\rangle =\|e_{a}\|^{2}=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/30bf5414b9553cb12f6589f0f8f569d3afb3fe8e) for all ![Image 257: {\displaystyle a,b\in A.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/59ffe5b62cb35c2f183efd33cc66e9d30d68cbda)

Using an infinite-dimensional analog of the Gram-Schmidt process one may show:

**Theorem.** Any [separable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separable_space "Separable space") inner product space has an orthonormal basis.

Using the [Hausdorff maximal principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hausdorff_maximal_principle "Hausdorff maximal principle") and the fact that in a [complete inner product space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space "Hilbert space") orthogonal projection onto linear subspaces is well-defined, one may also show that

**Theorem.** Any [complete inner product space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space "Hilbert space") has an orthonormal basis.

The two previous theorems raise the question of whether all inner product spaces have an orthonormal basis. The answer, it turns out is negative. This is a non-trivial result, and is proved below. The following proof is taken from Halmos's _A Hilbert Space Problem Book_ (see the references).[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

[Parseval's identity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parseval%27s_identity "Parseval's identity") leads immediately to the following theorem:

**Theorem.** Let ![Image 258: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) be a separable inner product space and ![Image 259: {\displaystyle \left\{e_{k}\right\}_{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/277380fd3ae81a149d77925de57993736e1a81fc) an orthonormal basis of ![Image 260: {\displaystyle V.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1b2661a49b86bd1a5548e527bbfb068aa9f59978) Then the map ![Image 261: {\displaystyle x\mapsto {\bigl \{}\langle e_{k},x\rangle {\bigr \}}_{k\in \mathbb {N} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b38f38f737c73a460876f0e3b8eed48a34650364) is an isometric linear map ![Image 262: {\displaystyle V\rightarrow \ell ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8071f271637b5f4f89b7c3b51ff29cdc5bc7e3d3) with a dense image.

This theorem can be regarded as an abstract form of [Fourier series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourier_series "Fourier series"), in which an arbitrary orthonormal basis plays the role of the sequence of [trigonometric polynomials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trigonometric_polynomial "Trigonometric polynomial"). Note that the underlying index set can be taken to be any countable set (and in fact any set whatsoever, provided ![Image 263: {\displaystyle \ell ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/91f1f909abd70bd3d8fff0f7ae1ac23052387e18) is defined appropriately, as is explained in the article [Hilbert space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space "Hilbert space")). In particular, we obtain the following result in the theory of Fourier series:

**Theorem.** Let ![Image 264: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) be the inner product space ![Image 265: {\displaystyle C[-\pi ,\pi ].}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/223f9d7493adedbd3d9cf0c35f812f8236c202d1) Then the sequence (indexed on set of all integers) of continuous functions ![Image 266: {\displaystyle e_{k}(t)={\frac {e^{ikt}}{\sqrt {2\pi }}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/67231686f14dfea4f61da5fd81d1e3425020bcef) is an orthonormal basis of the space ![Image 267: {\displaystyle C[-\pi ,\pi ]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7004d3934596427928c52976c6c0457dcd4e522b) with the ![Image 268: {\displaystyle L^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ba162c66ca85776c83557af5088cc6f8584d1912) inner product. The mapping ![Image 269: {\displaystyle f\mapsto {\frac {1}{\sqrt {2\pi }}}\left\{\int _{-\pi }^{\pi }f(t)e^{-ikt}\,\mathrm {d} t\right\}_{k\in \mathbb {Z} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3047892082fa23c14a5ca6b248eb640f045c90a7) is an isometric linear map with dense image.

Orthogonality of the sequence ![Image 270: {\displaystyle \{e_{k}\}_{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/465501a55f0530a201631136abb1ef4dbce59199) follows immediately from the fact that if ![Image 271: {\displaystyle k\neq j,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a7b76832728b5272b70dc1a95c3931ac8c89fbc8) then ![Image 272: {\displaystyle \int _{-\pi }^{\pi }e^{-i(j-k)t}\,\mathrm {d} t=0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8ba695334ce8205b102c4055a7dbb4d659359dd8)

Normality of the sequence is by design, that is, the coefficients are so chosen so that the norm comes out to 1. Finally the fact that the sequence has a dense algebraic span, in the _inner product norm_, follows from the fact that the sequence has a dense algebraic span, this time in the space of continuous periodic functions on ![Image 273: {\displaystyle [-\pi ,\pi ]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cb064fd6c55820cfa660eabeeda0f6e3c4935ae6) with the uniform norm. This is the content of the [Weierstrass theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weierstrass_approximation_theorem "Weierstrass approximation theorem") on the uniform density of trigonometric polynomials.

Operators on inner product spaces
---------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Inner_product_space&action=edit&section=19 "Edit section: Operators on inner product spaces")]

Several types of [linear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear "Linear") maps ![Image 274: {\displaystyle A:V\to W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/97b4b6380b2f4700cea977536a2af33d51efb89f) between inner product spaces ![Image 275: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) and ![Image 276: {\displaystyle W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54a9c4c547f4d6111f81946cad242b18298d70b7) are of relevance:

From the point of view of inner product space theory, there is no need to distinguish between two spaces which are isometrically isomorphic. The [spectral theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectral_theorem "Spectral theorem") provides a canonical form for symmetric, unitary and more generally [normal operators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_operator "Normal operator") on finite dimensional inner product spaces. A generalization of the spectral theorem holds for continuous normal operators in Hilbert spaces.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inner_product#cite_note-14)

Any of the axioms of an inner product may be weakened, yielding generalized notions. The generalizations that are closest to inner products occur where bilinearity and conjugate symmetry are retained, but positive-definiteness is weakened.

### Degenerate inner products

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Inner_product_space&action=edit&section=21 "Edit section: Degenerate inner products")]

If ![Image 277: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) is a vector space and ![Image 278: {\displaystyle \langle \,\cdot \,,\,\cdot \,\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2027b7f5f03c87f88b943b87e1062cc837e60dd6) a semi-definite sesquilinear form, then the function: ![Image 279: {\displaystyle \|x\|={\sqrt {\langle x,x\rangle }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2a885f02102ba1179708035546be39be936979b3) makes sense and satisfies all the properties of norm except that ![Image 280: {\displaystyle \|x\|=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/787cc4ec9f7efa527a924e4104b6e6501a949f06) does not imply ![Image 281: {\displaystyle x=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/953917eaf52f2e1baad54c8c9e3d6f9bb3710cdc) (such a functional is then called a [semi-norm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semi-norm "Semi-norm")). We can produce an inner product space by considering the quotient ![Image 282: {\displaystyle W=V/\{x:\|x\|=0\}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/85743d99fceef5471b0ebc2c7ac7ff151594b9ef) The sesquilinear form ![Image 283: {\displaystyle \langle \,\cdot \,,\,\cdot \,\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2027b7f5f03c87f88b943b87e1062cc837e60dd6) factors through ![Image 284: {\displaystyle W.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/035a080f11445ba1e7745704a6031989a311a7d7)

This construction is used in numerous contexts. The [Gelfand–Naimark–Segal construction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gelfand%E2%80%93Naimark%E2%80%93Segal_construction "Gelfand–Naimark–Segal construction") is a particularly important example of the use of this technique. Another example is the representation of [semi-definite kernels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mercer%27s_theorem "Mercer's theorem") on arbitrary sets.

### Nondegenerate conjugate symmetric forms

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Inner_product_space&action=edit&section=22 "Edit section: Nondegenerate conjugate symmetric forms")]

Alternatively, one may require that the pairing be a [nondegenerate form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nondegenerate_form "Nondegenerate form"), meaning that for all non-zero ![Image 285: {\displaystyle x\neq 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35a455db7b2aab1b0e72ccbc7385e4424e2372e5) there exists some ![Image 286: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d) such that ![Image 287: {\displaystyle \langle x,y\rangle \neq 0,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/70cad7232a67fb3c44d692c1614cfe977e991705) though ![Image 288: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d) need not equal ![Image 289: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4); in other words, the induced map to the dual space ![Image 290: {\displaystyle V\to V^{*}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7e86ea24eb05dd60cbcf6eb787a5f07e3861984e) is injective. This generalization is important in [differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry"): a manifold whose tangent spaces have an inner product is a [Riemannian manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_manifold "Riemannian manifold"), while if this is related to nondegenerate conjugate symmetric form the manifold is a [pseudo-Riemannian manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudo-Riemannian_manifold "Pseudo-Riemannian manifold"). By [Sylvester's law of inertia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sylvester%27s_law_of_inertia "Sylvester's law of inertia"), just as every inner product is similar to the dot product with positive weights on a set of vectors, every nondegenerate conjugate symmetric form is similar to the dot product with _nonzero_ weights on a set of vectors, and the number of positive and negative weights are called respectively the positive index and negative index. Product of vectors in [Minkowski space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minkowski_space "Minkowski space") is an example of indefinite inner product, although, technically speaking, it is not an inner product according to the standard definition above. Minkowski space has four [dimensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension_(mathematics) "Dimension (mathematics)") and indices 3 and 1 (assignment of ["+" and "−"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sign_(mathematics) "Sign (mathematics)") to them [differs depending on conventions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sign_convention#Metric_signature "Sign convention")).

Purely algebraic statements (ones that do not use positivity) usually only rely on the nondegeneracy (the injective homomorphism ![Image 291: {\displaystyle V\to V^{*}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7e86ea24eb05dd60cbcf6eb787a5f07e3861984e)) and thus hold more generally.

The term "inner product" is opposed to [outer product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_product "Outer product") ([tensor product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_product "Tensor product")), which is a slightly more general opposite. Simply, in coordinates, the inner product is the product of a ![Image 292: {\displaystyle 1\times n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0bce5f6a6d0d32834484048c16f3b39f9c23d076)_covector_ with an ![Image 293: {\displaystyle n\times 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d24148f103e1cccb60addeeb0a64cb1c3d5622e0) vector, yielding a ![Image 294: {\displaystyle 1\times 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b4bf91a527dc01af9ef6ace81199becf1308e00) matrix (a scalar), while the outer product is the product of an ![Image 295: {\displaystyle m\times 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7f7ea91d81567531f5ef6d3b669be211ff953e6a) vector with a ![Image 296: {\displaystyle 1\times n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0bce5f6a6d0d32834484048c16f3b39f9c23d076) covector, yielding an ![Image 297: {\displaystyle m\times n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/12b23d207d23dd430b93320539abbb0bde84870d) matrix. The outer product is defined for different dimensions, while the inner product requires the same dimension. If the dimensions are the same, then the inner product is the _[trace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trace\_(linear\_algebra) "Trace (linear algebra)")_ of the outer product (trace only being properly defined for square matrices). In an informal summary: "inner is horizontal times vertical and shrinks down, outer is vertical times horizontal and expands out".

More abstractly, the outer product is the bilinear map ![Image 298: {\displaystyle W\times V^{*}\to \hom(V,W)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/230a81e4f53292e6c62c20013a939a0358bf23eb) sending a vector and a covector to a rank 1 linear transformation ([simple tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simple_tensor "Simple tensor") of type (1, 1)), while the inner product is the bilinear evaluation map ![Image 299: {\displaystyle V^{*}\times V\to F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f9e94fbfe0ac2b30ba95a9d76c33166b3b4323ba) given by evaluating a covector on a vector; the order of the domain vector spaces here reflects the covector/vector distinction.

The inner product and outer product should not be confused with the [interior product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interior_product "Interior product") and [exterior product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_product "Exterior product"), which are instead operations on [vector fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_field "Vector field") and [differential forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_form "Differential form"), or more generally on the [exterior algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_algebra "Exterior algebra").

As a further complication, in [geometric algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_algebra "Geometric algebra") the inner product and the _exterior_ (Grassmann) product are combined in the geometric product (the Clifford product in a [Clifford algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_algebra "Clifford algebra")) – the inner product sends two vectors (1-vectors) to a scalar (a 0-vector), while the exterior product sends two vectors to a bivector (2-vector) – and in this context the exterior product is usually called the _outer product_ (alternatively, _[wedge product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wedge\_product "Wedge product")_). The inner product is more correctly called a _scalar_ product in this context, as the nondegenerate quadratic form in question need not be positive definite (need not be an inner product).
