[Drag racing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drag_racing "Drag racing") is a sport in which specially-built vehicles compete to be the fastest to accelerate from a standing start.

In [mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanics "Mechanics"), **acceleration** is the [rate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rate_(mathematics) "Rate (mathematics)") of change of the [velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity "Velocity") of an object with respect to time. Acceleration is one of several components of [kinematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematics "Kinematics"), the study of [motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Motion "Motion"). Accelerations are [vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_vector "Euclidean vector") quantities (in that they have [magnitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnitude_(mathematics) "Magnitude (mathematics)") and [direction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direction_(geometry) "Direction (geometry)")).[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_acceleration#cite_note-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_acceleration#cite_note-2) The orientation of an object's acceleration is given by the orientation of the _net_[force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Force "Force") acting on that object. The magnitude of an object's acceleration, as described by [Newton's second law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_second_law "Newton's second law"),[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_acceleration#cite_note-3) is the combined effect of two causes:

*   the net balance of all external [forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Force "Force") acting onto that object — magnitude is [directly proportional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direct_proportionality "Direct proportionality") to this net resulting force;
*   that object's [mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass "Mass"), depending on the materials out of which it is made — magnitude is [inversely proportional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverse_proportionality "Inverse proportionality") to the object's mass.

The [SI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Units "International System of Units") unit for acceleration is [metre per second squared](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metre_per_second_squared "Metre per second squared") (m⋅s−2, ![Image 5: {\displaystyle \mathrm {\tfrac {m}{s^{2}}} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/98dfac31f9b284074d7828a73a97246a48a9f41d)).

For example, when a [vehicle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vehicle "Vehicle") starts from a [standstill](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/standstill "wikt:standstill") (zero velocity, in an [inertial frame of reference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inertial_frame_of_reference "Inertial frame of reference")) and travels in a straight line at increasing speeds, it is accelerating in the direction of travel. If the vehicle turns, an acceleration occurs toward the new direction and changes its motion vector. The acceleration of the vehicle in its current direction of motion is called a linear (or tangential during [circular motions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circular_motion "Circular motion")) acceleration, the [reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reaction_(physics) "Reaction (physics)") to which the passengers on board experience as a force pushing them back into their seats. When changing direction, the effecting acceleration is called radial (or centripetal during circular motions) acceleration, the reaction to which the passengers experience as a [centrifugal force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centrifugal_force "Centrifugal force"). If the speed of the vehicle decreases, this is an acceleration in the opposite direction of the velocity vector (mathematically a [negative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Negative_number "Negative number"), if the movement is unidimensional and the velocity is positive), sometimes called **deceleration**[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_acceleration#cite_note-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_acceleration#cite_note-5) or **retardation**, and passengers experience the reaction to deceleration as an [inertial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inertia "Inertia") force pushing them forward. Such negative accelerations are often achieved by [retrorocket](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Retrorocket "Retrorocket") burning in [spacecraft](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spacecraft "Spacecraft").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_acceleration#cite_note-6) Both acceleration and deceleration are treated the same, as they are both changes in velocity. Each of these accelerations (tangential, radial, deceleration) is felt by passengers until their relative (differential) velocity are neutralised in [reference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frame_of_reference "Frame of reference") to the acceleration due to change in speed.

Definition and properties
-------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Acceleration&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Definition and properties")]

[![Image 6](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Kinematics.svg/330px-Kinematics.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Kinematics.svg)

Kinematic quantities of a classical particle: mass m, position **r**, velocity **v**, acceleration **a**.

### Average acceleration

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Acceleration&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Average acceleration")]

[![Image 7](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Acceleration_as_derivative_of_velocity_along_trajectory.svg/250px-Acceleration_as_derivative_of_velocity_along_trajectory.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Acceleration_as_derivative_of_velocity_along_trajectory.svg)

Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity. At any point on a trajectory, the magnitude of the acceleration is given by the rate of change of velocity in both magnitude and direction at that point. The true acceleration at time t is found in the limit as [time interval](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_interval "Time interval")Δ _t_ → 0 of Δ**v**/Δ _t_.

An object's average acceleration over a period of [time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_in_physics "Time in physics") is its change in [velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity "Velocity"), ![Image 8: {\displaystyle \Delta \mathbf {v} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0ae2621f980eefffc0a3f3e806bfce57a5be867a), divided by the duration of the period, ![Image 9: {\displaystyle \Delta t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8c28867ecd34e2caed12cf38feadf6a81a7ee542). Mathematically, ![Image 10: {\displaystyle {\bar {\mathbf {a} }}={\frac {\Delta \mathbf {v} }{\Delta t}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/941bc4c58dbc6f6716ce1d0024ff29e2ee82a0c9)

### Instantaneous acceleration

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Acceleration&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Instantaneous acceleration")]

[![Image 11](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/1-D_kinematics.svg/250px-1-D_kinematics.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:1-D_kinematics.svg)

**From bottom to top**: 

*   an acceleration function _a_(_t_);
*   the integral of the acceleration is the velocity function _v_(_t_);
*   and the integral of the velocity is the distance function _s_(_t_).

Instantaneous acceleration, meanwhile, is the [limit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limit_of_a_function "Limit of a function") of the average acceleration over an [infinitesimal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinitesimal "Infinitesimal") interval of time. In the terms of [calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus "Calculus"), instantaneous acceleration is the [derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative "Derivative") of the velocity vector with respect to time: ![Image 12: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} =\lim _{{\Delta t}\to 0}{\frac {\Delta \mathbf {v} }{\Delta t}}={\frac {d\mathbf {v} }{dt}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9d545c82c8f7f22605abdb6b1b9d2605583e819a) As acceleration is defined as the derivative of velocity, **v**, with respect to time t and velocity is defined as the derivative of position, **x**, with respect to time, acceleration can be thought of as the [second derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_derivative "Second derivative") of **x** with respect to t: ![Image 13: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} ={\frac {d\mathbf {v} }{dt}}={\frac {d^{2}\mathbf {x} }{dt^{2}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/99cc6821e0ed27be073066484854b6ceaa22ddfc)

(Here and elsewhere, if [motion is in a straight line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectilinear_motion "Rectilinear motion"), [vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_vector "Euclidean vector") quantities can be substituted by [scalars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_(physics) "Scalar (physics)") in the equations.)

By the [fundamental theorem of calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_theorem_of_calculus "Fundamental theorem of calculus"), it can be seen that the [integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral "Integral") of the acceleration function _a_(_t_) is the velocity function _v_(_t_); that is, the area under the curve of an acceleration vs. time (a vs. t) graph corresponds to the change of velocity. [![Image 14: {\displaystyle \mathbf {\Delta v} =\int \mathbf {a} \,dt.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0f073fed8813863277c4736356f81b20bef43d72)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:MathWikibase&qid=Q11465)

Likewise, the integral of the [jerk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerk_(physics) "Jerk (physics)") function _j_(_t_), the derivative of the acceleration function, can be used to find the change of acceleration at a certain time: ![Image 15: {\displaystyle \mathbf {\Delta a} =\int \mathbf {j} \,dt.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e85ecbc1f7e6ae184700b69b478abe2abb42939c)

Acceleration has the [dimensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimensional_analysis "Dimensional analysis") of velocity (L/T) divided by time, i.e. [L](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Length "Length")[T](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time "Time")−2. The [SI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Units "International System of Units") unit of acceleration is the [metre per second squared](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metre_per_second_squared "Metre per second squared") (m s−2); or "metre per second per second", as the velocity in metres per second changes by the acceleration value, every second.

An object moving in a circular motion—such as a satellite orbiting the Earth—is accelerating due to the change of direction of motion, although its speed may be constant. In this case it is said to be undergoing _centripetal_ (directed towards the center) acceleration.

[Proper acceleration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proper_acceleration "Proper acceleration"), the acceleration of a body relative to a free-fall condition, is measured by an instrument called an [accelerometer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerometer "Accelerometer").

In [classical mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_mechanics "Classical mechanics"), for a body with constant mass, the (vector) acceleration of the body's center of mass is proportional to the net [force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Force "Force") vector (i.e. sum of all forces) acting on it ([Newton's second law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_laws_of_motion#Newton's_second_law "Newton's laws of motion")): [![Image 16: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} =m\mathbf {a} \quad \implies \quad \mathbf {a} ={\frac {\mathbf {F} }{m}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d5c0d3abfa63b485b95bf50c8938c73736982547)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:MathWikibase&qid=Q2397319) where **F** is the net force acting on the body, m is the [mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass "Mass") of the body, and **a** is the center-of-mass acceleration. As speeds approach the [speed of light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light "Speed of light"), [relativistic effects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_relativity "Special relativity") become increasingly large.

---------------------------------------

[![Image 17](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Oscillating_pendulum.gif/250px-Oscillating_pendulum.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Oscillating_pendulum.gif)

An oscillating pendulum, with velocity and acceleration marked. It experiences both tangential and centripetal acceleration.

[![Image 18](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Acceleration_components.svg/250px-Acceleration_components.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Acceleration_components.svg)

Components of acceleration for a curved motion. The tangential component **a**t is due to the change in speed of traversal, and points along the curve in the direction of the velocity vector (or in the opposite direction). The normal component (also called centripetal component for circular motion) **a**c is due to the change in direction of the velocity vector and is normal to the trajectory, pointing toward the center of curvature of the path.

The velocity of a particle moving on a curved path as a [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)") of time can be written as: ![Image 19: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} (t)=v(t){\frac {\mathbf {v} (t)}{v(t)}}=v(t)\mathbf {u} _{\mathrm {t} }(t),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0b4ed403ef705caf6d21e5f0c64cdd6b78407da8) with _v_(_t_) equal to the speed of travel along the path, and ![Image 20: {\displaystyle \mathbf {u} _{\mathrm {t} }={\frac {\mathbf {v} (t)}{v(t)}}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f24d17c9f66ed44e8a21b0f1c30f332ca59edb2e) a [unit vector tangent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry_of_curves#Tangent_vector "Differential geometry of curves") to the path pointing in the direction of motion at the chosen moment in time. Taking into account both the changing speed _v_(_t_) and the changing direction of **u**_t_, the acceleration of a particle moving on a curved path can be written using the [chain rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chain_rule "Chain rule") of differentiation[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_acceleration#cite_note-7) for the product of two functions of time as:

![Image 21: {\displaystyle {\begin{alignedat}{3}\mathbf {a} &={\frac {d\mathbf {v} }{dt}}\\&={\frac {dv}{dt}}\mathbf {u} _{\mathrm {t} }+v(t){\frac {d\mathbf {u} _{\mathrm {t} }}{dt}}\\&={\frac {dv}{dt}}\mathbf {u} _{\mathrm {t} }+{\frac {v^{2}}{r}}\mathbf {u} _{\mathrm {n} }\ ,\end{alignedat}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/49a88fa2cf023a1cac99133acfed8d7961e07c0b)

where **u**n is the unit (inward) [normal vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry_of_curves#Normal_or_curvature_vector "Differential geometry of curves") to the particle's trajectory (also called _the principal normal_), and **r** is its instantaneous [radius of curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvature#Curvature_of_plane_curves "Curvature") based upon the [osculating circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osculating_circle#Mathematical_description "Osculating circle") at time t. The components

![Image 22: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} _{\mathrm {t} }={\frac {dv}{dt}}\mathbf {u} _{\mathrm {t} }\quad {\text{and}}\quad \mathbf {a} _{\mathrm {c} }={\frac {v^{2}}{r}}\mathbf {u} _{\mathrm {n} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8cc7ccce3437ffaa274f0018f50b895faa209dd1)
are called the [tangential acceleration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangential_acceleration "Tangential acceleration") and the normal or radial acceleration (or centripetal acceleration in circular motion, see also [circular motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circular_motion "Circular motion") and [centripetal force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_force "Centripetal force")), respectively.

Geometrical analysis of three-dimensional space curves, which explains tangent, (principal) normal and binormal, is described by the [Frenet–Serret formulas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frenet%E2%80%93Serret_formulas "Frenet–Serret formulas").[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_acceleration#cite_note-Andrews-8)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_acceleration#cite_note-Chand-9)

### Uniform acceleration

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Acceleration&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Uniform acceleration")]

[![Image 23](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Strecke_und_konstante_Beschleunigung.png/250px-Strecke_und_konstante_Beschleunigung.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Strecke_und_konstante_Beschleunigung.png)

Calculation of the speed difference for a uniform acceleration

_Uniform_ or _constant_ acceleration is a type of motion in which the [velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity "Velocity") of an object changes by an equal amount in every equal time period.

A frequently cited example of uniform acceleration is that of an object in [free fall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_fall "Free fall") in a uniform gravitational field. The acceleration of a falling body in the absence of resistances to motion is dependent only on the [gravitational field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_field "Gravitational field") strength [g](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_gravity "Standard gravity") (also called _acceleration due to gravity_). Velocity vector **v**, always tangent to the path of motion.

Acceleration vector **a**, not parallel to the radial motion but offset by the angular and Coriolis accelerations, nor tangent to the path but offset by the centripetal and radial accelerations.

Kinematic vectors in plane [polar coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_coordinates "Polar coordinates"). Notice the setup is not restricted to 2d space, but may represent the [osculating plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osculating_plane "Osculating plane") plane in a point of an arbitrary curve in any higher dimension.

In uniform [circular motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circular_motion "Circular motion"), that is moving with constant _speed_ along a circular path, a particle experiences an acceleration resulting from the change of the direction of the velocity vector, while its magnitude remains constant. The derivative of the location of a point on a curve with respect to time, i.e. its velocity, turns out to be always exactly tangential to the curve, respectively orthogonal to the radius in this point. Since in uniform motion the velocity in the tangential direction does not change, the acceleration must be in radial direction, pointing to the center of the circle. This acceleration constantly changes the direction of the velocity to be tangent in the neighbouring point, thereby rotating the velocity vector along the circle.

Expressing centripetal acceleration vector in polar components, where ![Image 30: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eca0f46511c4c986c48b254073732c0bd98ae0c1) is a vector from the centre of the circle to the particle with magnitude equal to this distance, and considering the orientation of the acceleration towards the center, yields ![Image 31: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a_{c}} =-{\frac {v^{2}}{|\mathbf {r} |}}\cdot {\frac {\mathbf {r} }{|\mathbf {r} |}}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0098c4881adc467b7c2805104758c4ea471c1b6d)

As usual in rotations, the speed ![Image 32: {\displaystyle v}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e07b00e7fc0847fbd16391c778d65bc25c452597) of a particle may be expressed as an [_angular speed_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_velocity "Angular velocity") with respect to a point at the distance ![Image 33: {\displaystyle r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d1ecb613aa2984f0576f70f86650b7c2a132538) as [![Image 34: {\displaystyle \omega ={\frac {v}{r}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a9ad55b7ffead5347a91fd9c0b4008946a13ee41)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:MathWikibase&qid=Q161635)

Thus ![Image 35: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a_{c}} =-\omega ^{2}\mathbf {r} \,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e04e2c9f9a5b24de7419347a42b60da7f99af078)

This acceleration and the mass of the particle determine the necessary [centripetal force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_force "Centripetal force"), directed _toward_ the centre of the circle, as the net force acting on this particle to keep it in this uniform circular motion. The so-called '[centrifugal force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centrifugal_force "Centrifugal force")', appearing to act outward on the body, is a so-called [pseudo force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudo_force "Pseudo force") experienced in the [frame of reference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frame_of_reference "Frame of reference") of the body in circular motion, due to the body's [linear momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_momentum "Linear momentum"), a vector tangent to the circle of motion.

In a nonuniform circular motion, i.e., the speed along the curved path is changing, the acceleration has a non-zero component tangential to the curve, and is not confined to the [principal normal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principal_normal_vector "Principal normal vector"), which directs to the center of the osculating circle, that determines the radius ![Image 36: {\displaystyle r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d1ecb613aa2984f0576f70f86650b7c2a132538) for the centripetal acceleration. The tangential component is given by the angular acceleration ![Image 37: {\displaystyle \alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b79333175c8b3f0840bfb4ec41b8072c83ea88d3), i.e., the rate of change ![Image 38: {\displaystyle \alpha ={\dot {\omega }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5decfde4e2bbd6e948a608dd100f1939a3bfac30) of the angular speed ![Image 39: {\displaystyle \omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/48eff443f9de7a985bb94ca3bde20813ea737be8) times the radius ![Image 40: {\displaystyle r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d1ecb613aa2984f0576f70f86650b7c2a132538). That is, ![Image 41: {\displaystyle a_{t}=r\alpha .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/539041560190721697bf0e573f4854de5956e63e)

The sign of the tangential component of the acceleration is determined by the sign of the [angular acceleration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_acceleration "Angular acceleration") (![Image 42: {\displaystyle \alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b79333175c8b3f0840bfb4ec41b8072c83ea88d3)), and the tangent is always directed at right angles to the radius vector.

In multi-dimensional [Cartesian coordinate systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system "Cartesian coordinate system"), acceleration is broken up into components that correspond with each dimensional axis of the coordinate system. In a two-dimensional system, where there is an x-axis and a y-axis, corresponding acceleration components are defined as[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_acceleration#cite_note-12)![Image 43: {\displaystyle a_{x}=dv_{x}/dt=d^{2}x/dt^{2},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7ce6e98d2757d56c5d422228c278534876e323b5)![Image 44: {\displaystyle a_{y}=dv_{y}/dt=d^{2}y/dt^{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b2355d973a56142ddfe86d502c57d59fdf71bd7b)The two-dimensional acceleration vector is then defined as ![Image 45: {\displaystyle {\textbf {a}}=<a_{x},a_{y}>}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9fd2e70de96488f95ef8f65e8c4a6a2d3b0bf739). The magnitude of this vector is found by the [distance formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_distance "Euclidean distance") as![Image 46: {\displaystyle |a|={\sqrt {a_{x}^{2}+a_{y}^{2}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d10cd937c68307c7f4e959db3ba68a6466b3f276)In three-dimensional systems where there is an additional z-axis, the corresponding acceleration component is defined as![Image 47: {\displaystyle a_{z}=dv_{z}/dt=d^{2}z/dt^{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/beaf751a818ee39ea0bf50bf39a596399d27085c)The three-dimensional acceleration vector is defined as ![Image 48: {\displaystyle {\textbf {a}}=<a_{x},a_{y},a_{z}>}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a6f5c26e810aeeace73497545b45c5b6f7a7e4e7) with its magnitude being determined by![Image 49: {\displaystyle |a|={\sqrt {a_{x}^{2}+a_{y}^{2}+a_{z}^{2}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5a499f6b42efa9cdb347ef5b7a225d236596b193)

Relation to relativity
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Acceleration&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Relation to relativity")]

The special theory of relativity describes the behaviour of objects travelling relative to other objects at speeds approaching that of light in vacuum. [Newtonian mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newtonian_mechanics "Newtonian mechanics") is exactly revealed to be an approximation to reality, valid to great accuracy at lower speeds. As the relevant speeds increase toward the speed of light, acceleration no longer follows classical equations.

As speeds approach that of light, the acceleration produced by a given force decreases, becoming [infinitesimally](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinitesimally "Infinitesimally") small as light speed is approached; an object with mass can approach this speed [asymptotically](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asymptotically "Asymptotically"), but never reach it.

Unless the state of motion of an object is known, it is impossible to distinguish whether an observed force is due to [gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity "Gravity") or to acceleration—gravity and inertial acceleration have identical effects. [Albert Einstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Einstein "Albert Einstein") called this the [equivalence principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equivalence_principle "Equivalence principle"), and said that only observers who feel no force at all—including the force of gravity—are justified in concluding that they are not accelerating.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_acceleration#cite_note-Greene-13)
