Title: Bilinear form

URL Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bilinear_form

Published Time: 2004-01-31T18:47:27Z

Markdown Content:
In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), a **bilinear form** is a [bilinear map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bilinear_map "Bilinear map")_V_ × _V_ → _K_ on a [vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space")V (the elements of which are called _[vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector\_(mathematics) "Vector (mathematics)")_) over a [field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)")_K_ (the elements of which are called _[scalars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar\_(mathematics) "Scalar (mathematics)")_). In other words, a bilinear form is a function _B_: _V_ × _V_ → _K_ that is [linear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_map "Linear map") in each argument separately:

*   _B_(**u** + **v**, **w**) = _B_(**u**, **w**) + _B_(**v**, **w**) and _B_(_λ_**u**, **v**) = _λB_(**u**, **v**)
*   _B_(**u**, **v** + **w**) = _B_(**u**, **v**) + _B_(**u**, **w**) and _B_(**u**, _λ_**v**) = _λB_(**u**, **v**)

The [dot product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dot_product "Dot product") on ![Image 1: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d) is an example of a bilinear form which is also an [inner product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inner_product_space "Inner product space").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bilinear_form#cite_note-1) An example of a bilinear form that is not an inner product would be the [four-vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Four-vector "Four-vector") product.

The definition of a bilinear form can be extended to include [modules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Module_(mathematics) "Module (mathematics)") over a [ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ring_(mathematics) "Ring (mathematics)"), with [linear maps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_map "Linear map") replaced by [module homomorphisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Module_homomorphism "Module homomorphism").

When K is the field of [complex numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number")**C**, one is often more interested in [sesquilinear forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sesquilinear_form "Sesquilinear form"), which are similar to bilinear forms but are [conjugate linear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conjugate_linear "Conjugate linear") in one argument.

Coordinate representation
-------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bilinear_form&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Coordinate representation")]

Let _V_ be an n-[dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension_(vector_space) "Dimension (vector space)") vector space with [basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basis_(linear_algebra) "Basis (linear algebra)"){**e**1, …, **e**_n_}.

The _n_ × _n_ matrix _A_, defined by _A ij_ = _B_(**e**_i_, **e**_j_) is called the _matrix of the bilinear form_ on the basis {**e**1, …, **e**_n_}.

If the _n_ × 1 matrix _x_ represents a vector **x** with respect to this basis, and similarly, the _n_ × 1 matrix _y_ represents another vector **y**, then: ![Image 2: {\displaystyle B(\mathbf {x} ,\mathbf {y} )=\mathbf {x} ^{\textsf {T}}A\mathbf {y} =\sum _{i,j=1}^{n}x_{i}A_{ij}y_{j}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/edb35becbef36cbe72e11778f64efbb80b4197de)

A bilinear form has different matrices on different bases. However, the matrices of a bilinear form on different bases are all [congruent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congruent_matrices "Congruent matrices"). More precisely, if {**f**1, …, **f**_n_} is another basis of V, then ![Image 3: {\displaystyle \mathbf {f} _{j}=\sum _{i=1}^{n}S_{i,j}\mathbf {e} _{i},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/da89c7e27481d1ae534c03e06f8fb5a6ccb367b3) where the ![Image 4: {\displaystyle S_{i,j}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/61ae921c367b5269cd47b7ad373a50d5875d76fb) form an [invertible matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invertible_matrix "Invertible matrix")S. Then, the matrix of the bilinear form on the new basis is _S_ T _AS_.

### Non-degenerate bilinear forms

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bilinear_form&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Non-degenerate bilinear forms")]

Every bilinear form _B_ on V defines a pair of linear maps from V to its [dual space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_space "Dual space")_V_∗. Define _B_ 1, _B_ 2: _V_ → _V_∗ by

_B_ 1(**v**)(**w**) = _B_(**v**, **w**)

_B_ 2(**v**)(**w**) = _B_(**w**, **v**)

This is often denoted as

_B_ 1(**v**) = _B_(**v**, ⋅)

_B_ 2(**v**) = _B_(⋅, **v**)

where the dot ( ⋅ ) indicates the slot into which the argument for the resulting [linear functional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_functional "Linear functional") is to be placed (see [Currying](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Currying "Currying")).

For a finite-dimensional vector space V, if either of _B_ 1 or _B_ 2 is an isomorphism, then both are, and the bilinear form _B_ is said to be [nondegenerate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degenerate_form "Degenerate form"). More concretely, for a finite-dimensional vector space, non-degenerate means that every non-zero element pairs non-trivially with some other element:

![Image 5: {\displaystyle B(x,y)=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6cca9c7f7602c72de7e84b8704aeb9b5b69cc963) for all ![Image 6: {\displaystyle y\in V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/da6483ea92075167a597963b8d1cb1f398d173f9) implies that _x_ = 0 and![Image 7: {\displaystyle B(x,y)=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6cca9c7f7602c72de7e84b8704aeb9b5b69cc963) for all ![Image 8: {\displaystyle x\in V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fa374e20b2db7f6b8caa71ff1865f7f84f215c9f) implies that _y_ = 0.
The corresponding notion for a module over a commutative ring is that a bilinear form is **unimodular** if _V_ → _V_∗ is an isomorphism. Given a finitely generated module over a commutative ring, the pairing may be injective (hence "nondegenerate" in the above sense) but not unimodular. For example, over the integers, the pairing _B_(_x_, _y_) = 2 _xy_ is nondegenerate but not unimodular, as the induced map from _V_ = **Z** to _V_∗ = **Z** is multiplication by 2.

If V is finite-dimensional then one can identify V with its double dual _V_∗∗. One can then show that _B_ 2 is the [transpose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transpose_of_a_linear_map "Transpose of a linear map") of the linear map _B_ 1 (if V is infinite-dimensional then _B_ 2 is the transpose of _B_ 1 restricted to the image of V in _V_∗∗). Given _B_ one can define the _transpose_ of _B_ to be the bilinear form given by

t _B_(**v**, **w**) = _B_(**w**, **v**).

The **left radical** and **right radical** of the form _B_ are the [kernels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kernel_(algebra) "Kernel (algebra)") of _B_ 1 and _B_ 2 respectively;[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bilinear_form#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJacobson2009346-2) they are the vectors orthogonal to the whole space on the left and on the right.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bilinear_form#cite_note-FOOTNOTEZhelobenko200611-3)

If V is finite-dimensional then the [rank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rank_(linear_algebra) "Rank (linear algebra)") of _B_ 1 is equal to the rank of _B_ 2. If this number is equal to dim(_V_) then _B_ 1 and _B_ 2 are linear isomorphisms from V to _V_∗. In this case _B_ is nondegenerate. By the [rank–nullity theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rank%E2%80%93nullity_theorem "Rank–nullity theorem"), this is equivalent to the condition that the left and equivalently right radicals be trivial. For finite-dimensional spaces, this is often taken as the _definition_ of nondegeneracy:

**Definition:**_B_ is **nondegenerate** if _B_(**v**, **w**) = 0 for all **w** implies **v** = **0**.

Given any linear map _A_: _V_ → _V_∗ one can obtain a bilinear form _B_ on _V_ via

_B_(**v**, **w**) = _A_(**v**)(**w**).

This form will be nondegenerate if and only if _A_ is an isomorphism.

If V is [finite-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite-dimensional "Finite-dimensional") then, relative to some [basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basis_(linear_algebra) "Basis (linear algebra)") for V, a bilinear form is degenerate if and only if the [determinant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Determinant "Determinant") of the associated matrix is zero. Likewise, a nondegenerate form is one for which the determinant of the associated matrix is non-zero (the matrix is [non-singular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-singular_matrix "Non-singular matrix")). These statements are independent of the chosen basis. For a module over a commutative ring, a unimodular form is one for which the determinant of the associate matrix is a [unit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_(ring_theory) "Unit (ring theory)") (for example 1), hence the term; note that a form whose matrix determinant is non-zero but not a unit will be nondegenerate but not unimodular, for example _B_(_x_, _y_) = 2 _xy_ over the integers.

### Symmetric, skew-symmetric, and alternating forms

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bilinear_form&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Symmetric, skew-symmetric, and alternating forms")]

We define a bilinear form to be

*   **[symmetric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetric_bilinear_form "Symmetric bilinear form")** if _B_(**v**, **w**) = _B_(**w**, **v**) for all **v**, **w** in V;
*   **[alternating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alternating_form "Alternating form")** if _B_(**v**, **v**) = 0 for all **v** in V;
*   **skew-symmetric** or **antisymmetric** if _B_(**v**, **w**) = −_B_(**w**, **v**) for all **v**, **w** in V; Proposition Every alternating form is skew-symmetric.Proof This can be seen by expanding _B_(**v** + **w**, **v** + **w**).

If the [characteristic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Characteristic_(algebra) "Characteristic (algebra)") of K is not 2 then the converse is also true: every skew-symmetric form is alternating. However, if char(_K_) = 2 then a skew-symmetric form is the same as a symmetric form and there exist symmetric/skew-symmetric forms that are not alternating.

A bilinear form is symmetric (respectively skew-symmetric) [if and only if](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/If_and_only_if "If and only if") its coordinate matrix (relative to any basis) is [symmetric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetric_matrix "Symmetric matrix") (respectively [skew-symmetric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skew-symmetric_matrix "Skew-symmetric matrix")). A bilinear form is alternating if and only if its coordinate matrix is skew-symmetric and the diagonal entries are all zero (which follows from skew-symmetry when char(_K_) ≠ 2).

A bilinear form is symmetric if and only if the maps _B_ 1, _B_ 2: _V_ → _V_∗ are equal, and skew-symmetric if and only if they are negatives of one another. If char(_K_) ≠ 2 then one can decompose a bilinear form into a symmetric and a skew-symmetric part as follows ![Image 9: {\displaystyle B^{+}={\tfrac {1}{2}}(B+{}^{\text{t}}B)\qquad B^{-}={\tfrac {1}{2}}(B-{}^{\text{t}}B),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8910ad8cd0e93fa1c614ccfc4f4670ecb6122485) where t _B_ is the transpose of _B_ (defined above).

### Reflexive bilinear forms and orthogonal vectors

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bilinear_form&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Reflexive bilinear forms and orthogonal vectors")]

**Definition:** A bilinear form _B_: _V_ × _V_ → _K_ is called **reflexive** if _B_(**v**, **w**) = 0 implies _B_(**w**, **v**) = 0 for all **v**, **w** in _V_.

**Definition:** Let _B_: _V_ × _V_ → _K_ be a reflexive bilinear form. **v**, **w** in _V_ are **orthogonal with respect to _B_** if _B_(**v**, **w**) = 0.

A bilinear form _B_ is reflexive if and only if it is either symmetric or alternating.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bilinear_form#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGrove1997-4) In the absence of reflexivity we have to distinguish left and right orthogonality. In a reflexive space the left and right radicals agree and are termed the _kernel_ or the _radical_ of the bilinear form: the subspace of all vectors orthogonal with every other vector. A vector **v**, with matrix representation _x_, is in the radical of a bilinear form with matrix representation _A_, if and only if _Ax_ = 0 ⇔ _x_ T _A_ = 0. The radical is always a subspace of _V_. It is trivial if and only if the matrix _A_ is nonsingular, and thus if and only if the bilinear form is nondegenerate.

Suppose W is a subspace. Define the _[orthogonal complement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal\_complement "Orthogonal complement")_[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bilinear_form#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAdkinsWeintraub1992359-5)![Image 10: {\displaystyle W^{\perp }=\left\{\mathbf {v} \mid B(\mathbf {v} ,\mathbf {w} )=0{\text{ for all }}\mathbf {w} \in W\right\}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/05c6e266d0c851140b448d58773764e6a5cbfb84)

For a non-degenerate form on a finite-dimensional space, the map _V/W_ → _W_⊥ is [bijective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bijective "Bijective"), and the dimension of _W_⊥ is dim(_V_) − dim(_W_).

### Bounded and elliptic bilinear forms

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bilinear_form&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Bounded and elliptic bilinear forms")]

**Definition:** A bilinear form on a [normed vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normed_vector_space "Normed vector space")(_V_, ‖⋅‖) is **bounded**, if there is a constant _C_ such that for all **u**, **v** ∈ _V_, ![Image 11: {\displaystyle B(\mathbf {u} ,\mathbf {v} )\leq C\left\|\mathbf {u} \right\|\left\|\mathbf {v} \right\|.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a6c484d10a780aa242e2e6ed32210dab3dd1234f)

**Definition:** A bilinear form on a normed vector space (_V_, ‖⋅‖) is **elliptic**, or [coercive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coercive_function#Coercive_operators_and_forms "Coercive function"), if there is a constant _c_> 0 such that for all **u** ∈ _V_, ![Image 12: {\displaystyle B(\mathbf {u} ,\mathbf {u} )\geq c\left\|\mathbf {u} \right\|^{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9329ffe6a249d0c8410a8e288f117631a5f8ceaf)

Associated quadratic form
-------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bilinear_form&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Associated quadratic form")]

For any bilinear form _B_: _V_ × _V_ → _K_, there exists an associated [quadratic form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadratic_form "Quadratic form")_Q_: _V_ → _K_ defined by _Q_: _V_ → _K_: **v** ↦ _B_(**v**, **v**).

When char(_K_) ≠ 2, the quadratic form _Q_ is determined by the symmetric part of the bilinear form _B_ and is independent of the antisymmetric part. In this case there is a one-to-one correspondence between the symmetric part of the bilinear form and the quadratic form, and it makes sense to speak of the symmetric bilinear form associated with a quadratic form.

When char(_K_) = 2 and dim _V_> 1, this correspondence between quadratic forms and symmetric bilinear forms breaks down.

Relation to tensor products
---------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bilinear_form&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Relation to tensor products")]

By the [universal property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universal_property "Universal property") of the [tensor product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_product "Tensor product"), there is a canonical correspondence between bilinear forms on V and linear maps _V_ ⊗ _V_ → _K_. If _B_ is a bilinear form on V the corresponding linear map is given by

**v** ⊗ **w** ↦ _B_(**v**, **w**)

In the other direction, if _F_: _V_ ⊗ _V_ → _K_ is a linear map the corresponding bilinear form is given by composing _F_ with the bilinear map _V_ × _V_ → _V_ ⊗ _V_ that sends (**v**, **w**) to **v**⊗**w**.

The set of all linear maps _V_ ⊗ _V_ → _K_ is the [dual space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_space "Dual space") of _V_ ⊗ _V_, so bilinear forms may be thought of as elements of (_V_ ⊗ _V_)∗ which (when V is finite-dimensional) is canonically isomorphic to _V_∗ ⊗ _V_∗.

Likewise, symmetric bilinear forms may be thought of as elements of (Sym 2 _V_)* (dual of the second [symmetric power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetric_power "Symmetric power") of _V_) and alternating bilinear forms as elements of (Λ 2 _V_)∗ ≃ Λ 2 _V_∗ (the second [exterior power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_power "Exterior power") of _V_∗). If char(_K_) ≠ 2, (Sym 2 _V_)* ≃ Sym 2(_V_∗).

### Pairs of distinct vector spaces

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bilinear_form&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Pairs of distinct vector spaces")]

Much of the theory is available for a [bilinear mapping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bilinear_mapping "Bilinear mapping") from two vector spaces over the same base field to that field

_B_: _V_ × _W_ → _K_.

Here we still have induced linear mappings from V to _W_∗, and from W to _V_∗. It may happen that these mappings are isomorphisms; assuming finite dimensions, if one is an isomorphism, the other must be. When this occurs, _B_ is said to be a **perfect pairing**.

In finite dimensions, this is equivalent to the pairing being nondegenerate (the spaces necessarily having the same dimensions). For modules (instead of vector spaces), just as how a nondegenerate form is weaker than a unimodular form, a nondegenerate pairing is a weaker notion than a perfect pairing. A pairing can be nondegenerate without being a perfect pairing, for instance **Z** × **Z** → **Z** via (_x_, _y_) ↦ 2 _xy_ is nondegenerate, but induces multiplication by 2 on the map **Z** → **Z**∗.

Terminology varies in coverage of bilinear forms. For example, [F. Reese Harvey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/F._Reese_Harvey "F. Reese Harvey") discusses "eight types of inner product".[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bilinear_form#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHarvey199022-6) To define them he uses diagonal matrices _A ij_ having only +1 or −1 for non-zero elements. Some of the "inner products" are [symplectic forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symplectic_vector_space "Symplectic vector space") and some are [sesquilinear forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sesquilinear_form "Sesquilinear form") or [Hermitian forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sesquilinear_form#Hermitian_form "Sesquilinear form"). Rather than a general field K, the instances with real numbers **R**, complex numbers **C**, and [quaternions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quaternions "Quaternions")**H** are spelled out. The bilinear form ![Image 13: {\displaystyle \sum _{k=1}^{p}x_{k}y_{k}-\sum _{k=p+1}^{n}x_{k}y_{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e8399052e64adc3832cb5a0eba375783f49a69b8) is called the **real symmetric case** and labeled **R**(_p_, _q_), where _p_ + _q_ = _n_. Then he articulates the connection to traditional terminology:[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bilinear_form#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHarvey199023-7)

> Some of the real symmetric cases are very important. The positive definite case **R**(_n_, 0) is called _Euclidean space_, while the case of a single minus, **R**(_n_−1, 1) is called _Lorentzian space_. If _n_ = 4, then Lorentzian space is also called _[Minkowski space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minkowski\_space "Minkowski space")_ or _Minkowski spacetime_. The special case **R**(_p_, _p_) will be referred to as the _split-case_.

Given a [ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ring_(mathematics) "Ring (mathematics)")R and a right [R-module](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Module_(mathematics) "Module (mathematics)")_M_ and its [dual module](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_module "Dual module")_M_∗, a mapping _B_: _M_∗ × _M_ → _R_ is called a **bilinear form** if

_B_(_u_ + _v_, _x_) = _B_(_u_, _x_) + _B_(_v_, _x_)

_B_(_u_, _x_ + _y_) = _B_(_u_, _x_) + _B_(_u_, _y_)

_B_(_αu_, _xβ_) = _αB_(_u_, _x_)_β_

for all _u_, _v_ ∈ _M_∗, all _x_, _y_ ∈ _M_ and all _α_, _β_ ∈ _R_.

The mapping ⟨⋅,⋅⟩: _M_∗ × _M_ → _R_: (_u_, _x_) ↦ _u_(_x_) is known as the _[natural pairing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural\_pairing "Natural pairing")_, also called the _canonical bilinear form_ on _M_∗ × _M_.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bilinear_form#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBourbaki1970233-8)

A linear map _S_: _M_∗ → _M_∗: _u_ ↦ _S_(_u_) induces the bilinear form _B_: _M_∗ × _M_ → _R_: (_u_, _x_) ↦ ⟨_S_(_u_), _x_⟩, and a linear map _T_: _M_ → _M_: _x_ ↦ _T_(_x_) induces the bilinear form _B_: _M_∗ × _M_ → _R_: (_u_, _x_) ↦ ⟨_u_, _T_(_x_)⟩.

Conversely, a bilinear form _B_: _M_∗ × _M_ → _R_ induces the _R_-linear maps _S_: _M_∗ → _M_∗: _u_ ↦ (_x_ ↦ _B_(_u_, _x_)) and _T_′: _M_ → _M_∗∗: _x_ ↦ (_u_ ↦ _B_(_u_, _x_)). Here, _M_∗∗ denotes the [double dual](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double_dual "Double dual") of _M_.
