In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), a **homogeneous function** is a [function of several variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_of_several_variables "Function of several variables") such that the following holds: If each of the function's arguments is multiplied by the same [scalar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_(mathematics) "Scalar (mathematics)"), then the function's value is multiplied by some power of this scalar; the power is called the **degree of homogeneity**, or simply the _degree_. That is, if k is an integer, a function f of n variables is homogeneous of degree k if

![Image 1: {\displaystyle f(sx_{1},\ldots ,sx_{n})=s^{k}f(x_{1},\ldots ,x_{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/be45f64ccf198bc94e7251807b019ba6fae02571)
for every ![Image 2: {\displaystyle x_{1},\ldots ,x_{n},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8fb4ea72660b223c376e371c2301215a39e53a55) and ![Image 3: {\displaystyle s\neq 0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/325558648e6f31f7d1050afead1d4d289f3936d7) This is also referred to a _k th-degree_ or _k th-order_ homogeneous function.

For example, a [homogeneous polynomial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homogeneous_polynomial "Homogeneous polynomial") of degree k defines a homogeneous function of degree k.

The above definition extends to functions whose [domain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_of_a_function "Domain of a function") and [codomain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Codomain "Codomain") are [vector spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space") over a [field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)")F: a function ![Image 4: {\displaystyle f:V\to W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/574dffa1c85efaef6b6ef553ebd8ad9cf7f87fd6) between two F-vector spaces is _homogeneous_ of degree ![Image 5: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40) if

![Image 6: {\displaystyle f(s\mathbf {v} )=s^{k}f(\mathbf {v} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a7f7decc335ff003701dea43d033302da1a60af8)1

for all nonzero ![Image 7: {\displaystyle s\in F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f6fe9319682deb1cd7944bfec2a95227c49045ce) and ![Image 8: {\displaystyle v\in V.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/13375e380c5699070639c00dcc62c3d91b05c7cd) This definition is often further generalized to functions whose domain is not V, but a [cone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cone_(linear_algebra) "Cone (linear algebra)") in V, that is, a subset C of V such that ![Image 9: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} \in C}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/96396d179d3ec17538680fa7e8c5445bb48903a2) implies ![Image 10: {\displaystyle s\mathbf {v} \in C}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54d5cd254dc134b8b03fa9c9af3a5355a0ac5a4d) for every nonzero scalar s.

In the case of [functions of several real variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functions_of_several_real_variables "Functions of several real variables") and [real vector spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_vector_space "Real vector space"), a slightly more general form of homogeneity called **positive homogeneity** is often considered, by requiring only that the above identities hold for ![Image 11: {\displaystyle s>0,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c383e7faf9b1add044f091319507fccc0f8a310f) and allowing any real number k as a degree of homogeneity. Every homogeneous real function is _positively homogeneous_. The converse is not true, but is locally true in the sense that (for integer degrees) the two kinds of homogeneity cannot be distinguished by considering the behavior of a function near a given point.

A [norm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norm_(mathematics) "Norm (mathematics)") over a real vector space is an example of a positively homogeneous function that is not homogeneous. A special case is the [absolute value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_value "Absolute value") of real numbers. The quotient of two homogeneous polynomials of the same degree gives an example of a homogeneous function of degree zero. This example is fundamental in the definition of [projective schemes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_scheme "Projective scheme").

The concept of a homogeneous function was originally introduced for [functions of several real variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functions_of_several_real_variables "Functions of several real variables"). With the definition of [vector spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space") at the end of 19th century, the concept has been naturally extended to functions between vector spaces, since a [tuple](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tuple "Tuple") of variable values can be considered as a [coordinate vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_vector "Coordinate vector"). It is this more general point of view that is described in this article.

There are two commonly used definitions. The general one works for vector spaces over arbitrary [fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)"), and is restricted to degrees of homogeneity that are [integers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer").

The second one supposes to work over the field of [real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number"), or, more generally, over an [ordered field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordered_field "Ordered field"). This definition restricts to positive values the scaling factor that occurs in the definition, and is therefore called _positive homogeneity_, the qualificative _positive_ being often omitted when there is no risk of confusion. Positive homogeneity leads to considering more functions as homogeneous. For example, the [absolute value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_value "Absolute value") and all [norms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norm_(mathematics) "Norm (mathematics)") are positively homogeneous functions that are not homogeneous.

The restriction of the scaling factor to real positive values allows also considering homogeneous functions whose degree of homogeneity is any real number.

### General homogeneity

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Homogeneous_function&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: General homogeneity")]

Let V and W be two [vector spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space") over a [field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)")F. A [linear cone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_cone "Linear cone") in V is a subset C of V such that ![Image 12: {\displaystyle sx\in C}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b3fea2be1b29260df5b8ada71962e13c08a04a77) for all ![Image 13: {\displaystyle x\in C}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f7fc788379bff7289bdf694ffd68ee690e999eb7) and all nonzero ![Image 14: {\displaystyle s\in F.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/962737a32c38780ed8c98b0f73fd70e73705d984)

A _homogeneous function_ f from V to W is a [partial function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_function "Partial function") from V to W that has a linear cone C as its [domain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_of_a_function "Domain of a function"), and satisfies

![Image 15: {\displaystyle f(sx)=s^{k}f(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5edcb0c23ff2bc71d08bd1e0ad03611fce444d96)
for some [integer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer")k, every ![Image 16: {\displaystyle x\in C,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/51ee7aa46cf3ffd58b3519632983c3bb07047ab4) and every nonzero ![Image 17: {\displaystyle s\in F.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/962737a32c38780ed8c98b0f73fd70e73705d984) The integer k is called the _degree of homogeneity_, or simply the _degree_ of f.

A typical example of a homogeneous function of degree k is the function defined by a [homogeneous polynomial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homogeneous_polynomial "Homogeneous polynomial") of degree k. The [rational function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_function "Rational function") defined by the quotient of two homogeneous polynomials is a homogeneous function; its degree is the difference of the degrees of the numerator and the denominator; its _cone of definition_ is the linear cone of the points where the value of denominator is not zero.

Homogeneous functions play a fundamental role in [projective geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_geometry "Projective geometry") since any homogeneous function f from V to W defines a well-defined function between the [projectivizations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projectivization "Projectivization") of V and W. The homogeneous rational functions of degree zero (those defined by the quotient of two homogeneous polynomial of the same degree) play an essential role in the [Proj construction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proj_construction "Proj construction") of [projective schemes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_scheme "Projective scheme").

### Positive homogeneity

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Homogeneous_function&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Positive homogeneity")]

When working over the [real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number"), or more generally over an [ordered field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordered_field "Ordered field"), it is commonly convenient to consider _positive homogeneity_, the definition being exactly the same as that in the preceding section, with "nonzero s" replaced by "_s_> 0" in the definitions of a linear cone and a homogeneous function.

This change allow considering (positively) homogeneous functions with any real number as their degrees, since [exponentiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponentiation "Exponentiation") with a positive real base is well defined.

Even in the case of integer degrees, there are many useful functions that are positively homogeneous without being homogeneous. This is, in particular, the case of the [absolute value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_value "Absolute value") function and [norms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norm_(mathematics) "Norm (mathematics)"), which are all positively homogeneous of degree 1. They are not homogeneous since ![Image 18: {\displaystyle |-x|=|x|\neq -|x|}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/78385241be8016715244fa3ef6e07b0a4d604c3f) if ![Image 19: {\displaystyle x\neq 0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bdaaa6b58b67e13add7dcd2c3b0ec2c8467dc285) This remains true in the [complex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number") case, since the field of the complex numbers ![Image 20: {\displaystyle \mathbb {C} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f9add4085095b9b6d28d045fd9c92c2c09f549a7) and every complex vector space can be considered as real vector spaces.

[Euler's homogeneous function theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homogeneous_function#Euler's_theorem) is a characterization of positively homogeneous [differentiable functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differentiable_function "Differentiable function"), which may be considered as the _fundamental theorem on homogeneous functions_.

A homogeneous function is not necessarily [continuous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_function "Continuous function"), as shown by this example. This is the function ![Image 22: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) defined by ![Image 23: {\displaystyle f(x,y)=x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c6bec423b26f491e42ada1e0cface4f4f396471d) if ![Image 24: {\displaystyle xy>0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4c969e1673cf0050acb908f927dfd9d7df1fcdb2) and ![Image 25: {\displaystyle f(x,y)=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/01ede27471a9cc9a0c5eb6e1ebdc7afc8a086543) if ![Image 26: {\displaystyle xy\leq 0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/daf037112145c73828da76fb10807fd8bec585db) This function is homogeneous of degree 1, that is, ![Image 27: {\displaystyle f(sx,sy)=sf(x,y)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b84b6ec611f6fd1af3a3de4c113913b31d3207b7) for any real numbers ![Image 28: {\displaystyle s,x,y.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/06a175ca5fa82c5a4bbd04034907bde8139dfe4b) It is discontinuous at ![Image 29: {\displaystyle y=0,x\neq 0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6b2e7a857619c3e3c5855f81cfc5cd216841e88c)

The function ![Image 30: {\displaystyle f(x,y)=x^{2}+y^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/62cd27ae17a5f4e156905fdcee054a41d15fa36f) is homogeneous of degree 2: ![Image 31: {\displaystyle f(tx,ty)=(tx)^{2}+(ty)^{2}=t^{2}\left(x^{2}+y^{2}\right)=t^{2}f(x,y).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a45352268f8b8a28b4f588002b298ea742d1cf8d)

### Absolute value and norms

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Homogeneous_function&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Absolute value and norms")]

The [absolute value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_value "Absolute value") of a [real number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number") is a positively homogeneous function of degree 1, which is not homogeneous, since ![Image 32: {\displaystyle |sx|=s|x|}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3f2788944988d1d0ca34e2578da6e4b7de24a6e9) if ![Image 33: {\displaystyle s>0,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c383e7faf9b1add044f091319507fccc0f8a310f) and ![Image 34: {\displaystyle |sx|=-s|x|}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/edddc8c8861ba6f915bd35dfd3c4e9cc76a0b110) if ![Image 35: {\displaystyle s<0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/abacd4af9425cc5a1ce56d588b971b8b591f0037)

The absolute value of a [complex number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number") is a positively homogeneous function of degree ![Image 36: {\displaystyle 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/92d98b82a3778f043108d4e20960a9193df57cbf) over the real numbers (that is, when considering the complex numbers as a [vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space") over the real numbers). It is not homogeneous, over the real numbers as well as over the complex numbers.

More generally, every [norm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norm_(mathematics) "Norm (mathematics)") and [seminorm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seminorm "Seminorm") is a positively homogeneous function of degree 1 which is not a homogeneous function. As for the absolute value, if the norm or semi-norm is defined on a vector space over the complex numbers, this vector space has to be considered as vector space over the real number for applying the definition of a positively homogeneous function.

Any [linear map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_map "Linear map")![Image 37: {\displaystyle f:V\to W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/574dffa1c85efaef6b6ef553ebd8ad9cf7f87fd6) between [vector spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space") over a [field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)")F is homogeneous of degree 1, by the definition of linearity: ![Image 38: {\displaystyle f(\alpha \mathbf {v} )=\alpha f(\mathbf {v} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bd6016686092a9e78baecb0c45010bc8319d3981) for all ![Image 39: {\displaystyle \alpha \in {F}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f2a68f8490f6858570a31bb92ef7e085c7d47221) and ![Image 40: {\displaystyle v\in V.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/13375e380c5699070639c00dcc62c3d91b05c7cd)

Similarly, any [multilinear function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multilinear_map "Multilinear map")![Image 41: {\displaystyle f:V_{1}\times V_{2}\times \cdots V_{n}\to W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1e44bcde84f75912fbc831240bf2668b28fdb92d) is homogeneous of degree ![Image 42: {\displaystyle n,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/397bfafc701afdf14c2743278a097f6f2957eabb) by the definition of multilinearity: ![Image 43: {\displaystyle f\left(\alpha \mathbf {v} _{1},\ldots ,\alpha \mathbf {v} _{n}\right)=\alpha ^{n}f(\mathbf {v} _{1},\ldots ,\mathbf {v} _{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0ea1fe9b39c34dbc8f9778ec4c294068f839e39d) for all ![Image 44: {\displaystyle \alpha \in {F}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f2a68f8490f6858570a31bb92ef7e085c7d47221) and ![Image 45: {\displaystyle v_{1}\in V_{1},v_{2}\in V_{2},\ldots ,v_{n}\in V_{n}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a59fc5a282e81cc5628345f9f8e77cd8e27031c5)

### Homogeneous polynomials

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Homogeneous_function&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Homogeneous polynomials")]

[Monomials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monomials "Monomials") in ![Image 46: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) variables define homogeneous functions ![Image 47: {\displaystyle f:\mathbb {F} ^{n}\to \mathbb {F} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dfd279cb6957fb90c8707264d9489bcae47b051f) For example, ![Image 48: {\displaystyle f(x,y,z)=x^{5}y^{2}z^{3}\,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a54f2d7d31414ef3577a8091486d8618ab35fb5c) is homogeneous of degree 10 since ![Image 49: {\displaystyle f(\alpha x,\alpha y,\alpha z)=(\alpha x)^{5}(\alpha y)^{2}(\alpha z)^{3}=\alpha ^{10}x^{5}y^{2}z^{3}=\alpha ^{10}f(x,y,z).\,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1e98a467d5fbcd6037515c9ecc44b5f9adbdbf86) The degree is the sum of the exponents on the variables; in this example, ![Image 50: {\displaystyle 10=5+2+3.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/07c5dd5a38b8348bf391bfbeaceee48a340764d3)

A [homogeneous polynomial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homogeneous_polynomial "Homogeneous polynomial") is a [polynomial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial "Polynomial") made up of a sum of monomials of the same degree. For example, ![Image 51: {\displaystyle x^{5}+2x^{3}y^{2}+9xy^{4}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/51db9a7b25751430fe980a07982859492343788a) is a homogeneous polynomial of degree 5. Homogeneous polynomials also define homogeneous functions.

Given a homogeneous polynomial of degree ![Image 52: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40) with real coefficients that takes only positive values, one gets a positively homogeneous function of degree ![Image 53: {\displaystyle k/d}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3bd2bd4bd56b1127b5bb6be79c7f860d220807c0) by raising it to the power ![Image 54: {\displaystyle 1/d.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a0430f1369c1e3525c6153655b81e021e3016a12) So for example, the following function is positively homogeneous of degree 1 but not homogeneous: ![Image 55: {\displaystyle \left(x^{2}+y^{2}+z^{2}\right)^{\frac {1}{2}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b14bcdb04e9e42b23184880a72c743a09ea48e56)

For every set of weights ![Image 56: {\displaystyle w_{1},\dots ,w_{n},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bedb4fa0e84c757f8b27d9983579359fe5ee6bc4) the following functions are positively homogeneous of degree 1, but not homogeneous:

[Rational functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_function "Rational function") formed as the ratio of two _homogeneous_ polynomials are homogeneous functions in their [domain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_of_a_function "Domain of a function"), that is, off of the [linear cone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_cone "Linear cone") formed by the [zeros](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero_of_a_function "Zero of a function") of the denominator. Thus, if ![Image 57: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) is homogeneous of degree ![Image 58: {\displaystyle m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a07d98bb302f3856cbabc47b2b9016692e3f7bc) and ![Image 59: {\displaystyle g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3556280e66fe2c0d0140df20935a6f057381d77) is homogeneous of degree ![Image 60: {\displaystyle n,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/397bfafc701afdf14c2743278a097f6f2957eabb) then ![Image 61: {\displaystyle f/g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b5c6b7962d3532e248f07cd42b1bdc9e007b137d) is homogeneous of degree ![Image 62: {\displaystyle m-n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/767c4b0c3cbd063f836169c2db77f5ffd833d136) away from the zeros of ![Image 63: {\displaystyle g.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/23a3f421f58ef3bc6f9ec70e883e1496ff871e9f)

The homogeneous [real functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_functions "Real functions") of a single variable have the form ![Image 64: {\displaystyle x\mapsto cx^{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/20736c4f706539a4f7b04aea2de70a8c6d23a64b) for some constant c. So, the [affine function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_function "Affine function")![Image 65: {\displaystyle x\mapsto x+5,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f62f4cb0340da19a633c419f645954bc32e893f0) the [natural logarithm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_logarithm "Natural logarithm")![Image 66: {\displaystyle x\mapsto \ln(x),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ec867398b849a5f5932391fd79268127853a79da) and the [exponential function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponential_function "Exponential function")![Image 67: {\displaystyle x\mapsto e^{x}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1ee414f477283eac0138240ce73ee26f0f8dc63b) are not homogeneous.

Roughly speaking, **Euler's homogeneous function theorem** asserts that the positively homogeneous functions of a given degree are exactly the solution of a specific [partial differential equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_differential_equation "Partial differential equation"). More precisely:

**Euler's homogeneous function theorem**—If f is a [(partial) function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_function "Partial function") of n real variables that is positively homogeneous of degree k, and [continuously differentiable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuously_differentiable "Continuously differentiable") in some open subset of ![Image 68: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d7035fcb9fe3ebecc6bc9f372f82d0352202c8bf) then it satisfies in this open set the [partial differential equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_differential_equation "Partial differential equation")![Image 69: {\displaystyle k\,f(x_{1},\ldots ,x_{n})=\sum _{i=1}^{n}x_{i}{\frac {\partial f}{\partial x_{i}}}(x_{1},\ldots ,x_{n}).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a113f1d95fda4dc699a3e3dcbcaa517a7ed100ac)

Conversely, every maximal continuously differentiable solution of this partial differentiable equation is a positively homogeneous function of degree k, defined on a positive cone (here, _maximal_ means that the solution cannot be prolongated to a function with a larger domain).

As a consequence, if ![Image 70: {\displaystyle f:\mathbb {R} ^{n}\to \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/306c097f43c91dce633d12cde024948d39e73752) is continuously differentiable and homogeneous of degree ![Image 71: {\displaystyle k,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/44e185ab9c990830d5055fa3ae698a4225ce67e0) its first-order [partial derivatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_derivative "Partial derivative")![Image 72: {\displaystyle \partial f/\partial x_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2c4abc6fa2414772b9c4e03b6c6e5d0516eaf4f3) are homogeneous of degree ![Image 73: {\displaystyle k-1.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c51f394a0e0100cf4d267b3d9858960b408cfc85) This results from Euler's theorem by differentiating the partial differential equation with respect to one variable.

In the case of a function of a single real variable (![Image 74: {\displaystyle n=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d9ec7e1edc2e6d98f5aec2a39ae5f1c99d1e1425)), the theorem implies that a continuously differentiable and positively homogeneous function of degree k has the form ![Image 75: {\displaystyle f(x)=c_{+}x^{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a0f1162367c48a4810cff0cd2372639ddeb8b0d3) for ![Image 76: {\displaystyle x>0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/80d24be5f0eb4a9173da6038badc8659546021d0) and ![Image 77: {\displaystyle f(x)=c_{-}x^{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dc40642031bbea31189f952e705e71d93be337b2) for ![Image 78: {\displaystyle x<0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eae2426ff09e1cd53f1658b8949e8dbd0fd8ad04) The constants ![Image 79: {\displaystyle c_{+}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/caf71677baf1b02ee987eb0c30315d372bf3a39e) and ![Image 80: {\displaystyle c_{-}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e5988f680a5934fa016df56463f969cc1c3840c9) are not necessarily the same, as it is the case for the [absolute value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_value "Absolute value").

### Homogeneity under a monoid action

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Homogeneous_function&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: Homogeneity under a monoid action")]

The definitions given above are all specialized cases of the following more general notion of homogeneity in which ![Image 86: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) can be any set (rather than a vector space) and the real numbers can be replaced by the more general notion of a [monoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monoid "Monoid").

All of the above definitions can be generalized by replacing the condition ![Image 175: {\displaystyle f(rx)=rf(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3c337ed0392e732cfa839a9a6a66698fef797c3) with ![Image 176: {\displaystyle f(rx)=|r|f(x),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/303998649db9f97426378375821436bcf0b2c966) in which case that definition is prefixed with the word "_absolute_" or "_absolutely_." For example,

1.   _Absolute homogeneity_:[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homogeneous_function#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKubrusly2011200-3)![Image 177: {\displaystyle f(sx)=|s|f(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/18c01313e134714e53fe514ca6fb57fd83785a5f) for all ![Image 178: {\displaystyle x\in X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3e580967f68f36743e894aa7944f032dda6ea01d) and all scalars ![Image 179: {\displaystyle s\in \mathbb {F} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/468bb1a7982adbe20df702b75491406a87b9afba)

If ![Image 180: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40) is a fixed real number then the above definitions can be further generalized by replacing the condition ![Image 181: {\displaystyle f(rx)=rf(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3c337ed0392e732cfa839a9a6a66698fef797c3) with ![Image 182: {\displaystyle f(rx)=r^{k}f(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/aa5658d54f960a7879e3aaae78cbec4e333e4995) (and similarly, by replacing ![Image 183: {\displaystyle f(rx)=|r|f(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf26cf71d4a5b52843813c101ea57629798f13ec) with ![Image 184: {\displaystyle f(rx)=|r|^{k}f(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2d987f4cedd5cff5707e8615999239d28b2ad11c) for conditions using the absolute value, etc.), in which case the homogeneity is said to be "_of degree ![Image 185: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest\_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40)_" (where in particular, all of the above definitions are "_of degree ![Image 186: {\displaystyle 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest\_v1/media/math/render/svg/92d98b82a3778f043108d4e20960a9193df57cbf)_").
