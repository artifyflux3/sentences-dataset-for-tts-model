From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), a **topological space** is, roughly speaking, a [geometrical space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry") in which [closeness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closeness_(mathematics) "Closeness (mathematics)") is defined but cannot necessarily be measured by a numeric [distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distance_(mathematics) "Distance (mathematics)"). More specifically, a topological space is a [set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_(mathematics) "Set (mathematics)") whose elements are called [points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_(geometry) "Point (geometry)"), along with an additional structure called a topology, which can be defined as a set of [neighbourhoods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neighbourhood_(mathematics) "Neighbourhood (mathematics)") for each point that satisfy some [axioms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiom#Non-logical_axioms "Axiom") formalizing the concept of closeness. There are several equivalent definitions of a topology, the most commonly used of which is the definition through [open sets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_set "Open set"), which is easier than the others to manipulate.

A topological space is the most general type of a [mathematical space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_(mathematics) "Space (mathematics)") that allows for the definition of [limits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limit_(mathematics) "Limit (mathematics)"), [continuity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_function_(topology) "Continuous function (topology)"), and [connectedness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connected_space "Connected space").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_space#cite_note-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_space#cite_note-2) Common types of topological spaces include [Euclidean spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space"), [metric spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space "Metric space") and [manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manifold "Manifold").

Although very general, the concept of topological spaces is fundamental, and used in virtually every branch of modern mathematics. The study of topological spaces in their own right is called [general topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_topology "General topology") (or point-set topology).

Around 1735, [Leonhard Euler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leonhard_Euler "Leonhard Euler") discovered the [formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planar_graph#Euler's_formula "Planar graph")![Image 1: {\displaystyle V-E+F=2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/759601e482258ff7a359a7db381abf60372c5b06) relating the number of vertices (V), edges (E) and faces (F) of a [convex polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_polytope "Convex polytope"), and hence of a [planar graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planar_graph "Planar graph"). The study and generalization of this formula, specifically by [Cauchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Augustin-Louis_Cauchy "Augustin-Louis Cauchy") (1789–1857) and [L'Huilier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simon_Antoine_Jean_L%27Huilier "Simon Antoine Jean L'Huilier") (1750–1840), [boosted the study](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler%27s_Gem "Euler's Gem") of topology. In 1827, [Carl Friedrich Gauss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Friedrich_Gauss "Carl Friedrich Gauss") published _General investigations of curved surfaces_, which in section 3 defines the curved surface in a similar manner to the modern topological understanding: "A curved surface is said to possess continuous curvature at one of its points A, if the direction of all the straight lines drawn from A to points of the surface at an infinitesimal distance from A are deflected infinitesimally from one and the same plane passing through A."[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_space#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGauss1827-3)[_[non-primary source needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:No\_original\_research#Primary,\_secondary\_and\_tertiary\_sources "Wikipedia:No original research")_]

Yet, "until [Riemann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bernhard_Riemann "Bernhard Riemann")'s work in the early 1850s, surfaces were always dealt with from a local point of view (as parametric surfaces) and topological issues were never considered".[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_space#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGallierXu2013-4) "[Möbius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/August_Ferdinand_M%C3%B6bius "August Ferdinand Möbius") and [Jordan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camille_Jordan "Camille Jordan") seem to be the first to realize that the main problem about the topology of (compact) surfaces is to find invariants (preferably numerical) to decide the equivalence of surfaces, that is, to decide whether two surfaces are [homeomorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homeomorphism "Homeomorphism") or not."[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_space#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGallierXu2013-4)

The subject is clearly defined by [Felix Klein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Felix_Klein "Felix Klein") in his "[Erlangen Program](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erlangen_Program "Erlangen Program")" (1872): the geometry invariants of arbitrary continuous transformation, a kind of geometry. The term "topology" was introduced by [Johann Benedict Listing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johann_Benedict_Listing "Johann Benedict Listing") in 1847, although he had used the term in correspondence some years earlier instead of previously used "Analysis situs". The foundation of this science, for a space of any dimension, was created by [Henri Poincaré](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Poincar%C3%A9 "Henri Poincaré"). His first article on this topic appeared in 1894.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_space#cite_note-5) In the 1930s, [James Waddell Alexander II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Waddell_Alexander_II "James Waddell Alexander II") and [Hassler Whitney](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hassler_Whitney "Hassler Whitney") first expressed the idea that a surface is a topological space that is [locally like a Euclidean plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_manifold "Topological manifold").

Topological spaces were first defined by [Felix Hausdorff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Felix_Hausdorff "Felix Hausdorff") in 1914 in his seminal "Principles of Set Theory". [Metric spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_spaces "Metric spaces") had been defined earlier in 1906 by [Maurice Fréchet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maurice_Fr%C3%A9chet "Maurice Fréchet"), though it was Hausdorff who popularised the term "metric space" ([German](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_language "German language"): _metrischer Raum_).[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_space#cite_note-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_space#cite_note-7)[_[better source needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTRS "Wikipedia:NOTRS")_]

The utility of the concept of a _topology_ is shown by the fact that there are several equivalent definitions of this [mathematical structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_structure "Mathematical structure"). Thus one chooses the [axiomatization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiomatization "Axiomatization") suited for the application. The most commonly used is that in terms of _[open sets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open\_set "Open set")_, but perhaps more intuitive is that in terms of _[neighbourhoods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neighbourhood\_(mathematics) "Neighbourhood (mathematics)")_ and so this is given first.

### Definition via neighbourhoods

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Topological_space&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Definition via neighbourhoods")]

This axiomatization is due to [Felix Hausdorff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Felix_Hausdorff "Felix Hausdorff"). Let ![Image 2: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) be a (possibly empty) set. The elements of ![Image 3: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) are usually called _points_, though they can be any mathematical object. Let ![Image 4: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {N}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b7551c7bed2cd2ee83e10536d157c94a5f8f72fd) be a [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)") assigning to each ![Image 5: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) (point) in ![Image 6: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) a non-empty collection ![Image 7: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {N}}(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fa099930dd7af7c3339be12e975001ffb45e6241) of subsets of ![Image 8: {\displaystyle X.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5ba76c5a460c4a0bb1639a193bc1830f0a773e03) The elements of ![Image 9: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {N}}(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fa099930dd7af7c3339be12e975001ffb45e6241) will be called _neighbourhoods_ of ![Image 10: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) with respect to ![Image 11: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {N}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b7551c7bed2cd2ee83e10536d157c94a5f8f72fd) (or, simply, _neighbourhoods of ![Image 12: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest\_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4)_). The function ![Image 13: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {N}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b7551c7bed2cd2ee83e10536d157c94a5f8f72fd) is called a [neighbourhood topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neighbourhood_(topology) "Neighbourhood (topology)") if the [axioms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiom "Axiom") below[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_space#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrown2006section_2.1-8) are satisfied; and then ![Image 14: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) with ![Image 15: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {N}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b7551c7bed2cd2ee83e10536d157c94a5f8f72fd) is called a **topological space**.

1.   If ![Image 16: {\displaystyle N}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5e3890c981ae85503089652feb48b191b57aae3) is a neighbourhood of ![Image 17: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) (i.e., ![Image 18: {\displaystyle N\in {\mathcal {N}}(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/847449020bc5ca58340e29a209d34a3ec535f137)), then ![Image 19: {\displaystyle x\in N.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/75e7d98beda3b876ca3149c9947a6993a2c162da) In other words, each point of the set ![Image 20: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) belongs to every one of its neighbourhoods with respect to ![Image 21: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {N}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b7551c7bed2cd2ee83e10536d157c94a5f8f72fd).
2.   If ![Image 22: {\displaystyle N}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5e3890c981ae85503089652feb48b191b57aae3) is a subset of ![Image 23: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) and includes a neighbourhood of ![Image 24: {\displaystyle x,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/feff4d40084c7351bf57b11ba2427f6331f5bdbe) then ![Image 25: {\displaystyle N}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5e3890c981ae85503089652feb48b191b57aae3) is a neighbourhood of ![Image 26: {\displaystyle x.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d07e9f568a88785ae48006ac3c4b951020f1699a) I.e., every [superset](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superset "Superset") of a neighbourhood of a point ![Image 27: {\displaystyle x\in X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3e580967f68f36743e894aa7944f032dda6ea01d) is again a neighbourhood of ![Image 28: {\displaystyle x.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d07e9f568a88785ae48006ac3c4b951020f1699a)
3.   The [intersection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intersection_(set_theory) "Intersection (set theory)") of two neighbourhoods of ![Image 29: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) is a neighbourhood of ![Image 30: {\displaystyle x.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d07e9f568a88785ae48006ac3c4b951020f1699a)
4.   Any neighbourhood ![Image 31: {\displaystyle N}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5e3890c981ae85503089652feb48b191b57aae3) of ![Image 32: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) includes a neighbourhood ![Image 33: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) of ![Image 34: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) such that ![Image 35: {\displaystyle N}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5e3890c981ae85503089652feb48b191b57aae3) is a neighbourhood of each point of ![Image 36: {\displaystyle M.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b865c33e30eb83000cd6387517c66dbbf3c3df9e)

The first three axioms for neighbourhoods have a clear meaning. The fourth axiom has a very important use in the structure of the theory, that of linking together the neighbourhoods of different points of ![Image 37: {\displaystyle X.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5ba76c5a460c4a0bb1639a193bc1830f0a773e03)

A standard example of such a system of neighbourhoods is for the real line ![Image 38: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0522388d36b55de7babe4bbfc49475eaf590c2bd) where a subset ![Image 39: {\displaystyle N}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5e3890c981ae85503089652feb48b191b57aae3) of ![Image 40: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc) is defined to be a _neighbourhood_ of a real number ![Image 41: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) if it includes an open interval containing ![Image 42: {\displaystyle x.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d07e9f568a88785ae48006ac3c4b951020f1699a)

Given such a structure, a subset ![Image 43: {\displaystyle U}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/458a728f53b9a0274f059cd695e067c430956025) of ![Image 44: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) is defined to be **open** if ![Image 45: {\displaystyle U}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/458a728f53b9a0274f059cd695e067c430956025) is a neighbourhood of all points in ![Image 46: {\displaystyle U.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7a305ef479ab152035f334467a2c314baa23eb36) The open sets then satisfy the axioms given below in the next definition of a topological space. Conversely, when given the open sets of a topological space, the neighbourhoods satisfying the above axioms can be recovered by defining ![Image 47: {\displaystyle N}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5e3890c981ae85503089652feb48b191b57aae3) to be a neighbourhood of ![Image 48: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) if ![Image 49: {\displaystyle N}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5e3890c981ae85503089652feb48b191b57aae3) includes an open set ![Image 50: {\displaystyle U}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/458a728f53b9a0274f059cd695e067c430956025) such that ![Image 51: {\displaystyle x\in U.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2494bed786064aac20efd5ea0fcaa3f787dd6f14)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_space#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrown2006section_2.2-9)

### Definition via open sets

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Topological_space&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Definition via open sets")]

A _topology_ on a [set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_(mathematics) "Set (mathematics)")X may be defined as a collection ![Image 52: {\displaystyle \tau }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38a7dcde9730ef0853809fefc18d88771f95206c) of [subsets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subset "Subset") of X, called **open sets** and satisfying the following axioms:[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_space#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArmstrong1983definition_2.1-10)

1.   The [empty set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empty_set "Empty set") and ![Image 53: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) itself belong to ![Image 54: {\displaystyle \tau .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/871bb01391136d3551c8ea59059e106be2a403cd)
2.   Any arbitrary (finite or infinite) [union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Union_(set_theory) "Union (set theory)") of members of ![Image 55: {\displaystyle \tau }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38a7dcde9730ef0853809fefc18d88771f95206c) belongs to ![Image 56: {\displaystyle \tau .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/871bb01391136d3551c8ea59059e106be2a403cd)
3.   The intersection of any finite number of members of ![Image 57: {\displaystyle \tau }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38a7dcde9730ef0853809fefc18d88771f95206c) belongs to ![Image 58: {\displaystyle \tau .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/871bb01391136d3551c8ea59059e106be2a403cd)

As this definition of a topology is the most commonly used, the set ![Image 59: {\displaystyle \tau }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38a7dcde9730ef0853809fefc18d88771f95206c) of the open sets is commonly called a **topology** on ![Image 60: {\displaystyle X.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5ba76c5a460c4a0bb1639a193bc1830f0a773e03)

A subset ![Image 61: {\displaystyle C\subseteq X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a98beab5a3637ffa2321ef617c02a3b5b26ffd3a) is said to be _closed_ in ![Image 62: {\displaystyle (X,\tau )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dede4b8004c6222d11e9b1a9802dc0496ad7e1fc) if its [complement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complement_(set_theory) "Complement (set theory)")![Image 63: {\displaystyle X\setminus C}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/aca1f69dbb7c551cdbb95ea1ef9f7e489828a248) is an open set. Note that from this definition, it follows that the empty set and ![Image 64: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) are simultaneously open _and_ closed – that is, the two sets are complements of one another, while each of them is, itself, open. In general, any subset of ![Image 65: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) with this property is said to be _[clopen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clopen\_set "Clopen set")._

### Definition via closed sets

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Topological_space&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Definition via closed sets")]

Using [de Morgan's laws](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_Morgan%27s_laws "De Morgan's laws"), the above axioms defining open sets become axioms defining **[closed sets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_set "Closed set")**:

1.   The empty set and ![Image 95: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) are closed.
2.   The intersection of any collection of closed sets is also closed.
3.   The union of any finite number of closed sets is also closed.

Using these axioms, another way to define a topological space is as a set ![Image 96: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) together with a collection ![Image 97: {\displaystyle \tau }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38a7dcde9730ef0853809fefc18d88771f95206c) of closed subsets of ![Image 98: {\displaystyle X.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5ba76c5a460c4a0bb1639a193bc1830f0a773e03) Thus the sets in the topology ![Image 99: {\displaystyle \tau }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38a7dcde9730ef0853809fefc18d88771f95206c) are the closed sets, and their complements in ![Image 100: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) are the open sets.

There are many other equivalent ways to define a topological space: in other words the concepts of neighbourhood, or that of open or closed sets can be reconstructed from other starting points and satisfy the correct axioms.

Another way to define a topological space is by using the [Kuratowski closure axioms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kuratowski_closure_axioms "Kuratowski closure axioms"), which define the closed sets as the [fixed points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fixed_point_(mathematics) "Fixed point (mathematics)") of an [operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operator_(mathematics) "Operator (mathematics)") on the [power set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_set "Power set") of ![Image 101: {\displaystyle X.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5ba76c5a460c4a0bb1639a193bc1830f0a773e03)

A [net](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Net_(mathematics) "Net (mathematics)") is a generalisation of the concept of [sequence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sequence "Sequence"). A topology is completely determined if for every net in ![Image 102: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) the set of its [accumulation points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology_glossary "Topology glossary") is specified.

Comparison of topologies
------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Topological_space&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Comparison of topologies")]

Many topologies can be defined on a set to form a topological space. When every open set of a topology ![Image 103: {\displaystyle \tau _{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ef3e70085bbfec0d2ed0f898618bbb48db4ab7b1) is also open for a topology ![Image 104: {\displaystyle \tau _{2},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/51f4d4ffc2ef9bcc4ad8d9f10ba3b9d780998e86) one says that ![Image 105: {\displaystyle \tau _{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/236a07f6109f6e8c7dd3d9229ab240a983045cd8) is _finer_ than ![Image 106: {\displaystyle \tau _{1},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6dee97de5b363ac108aefce706eb89b5053df633) and ![Image 107: {\displaystyle \tau _{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ef3e70085bbfec0d2ed0f898618bbb48db4ab7b1) is _coarser_ than ![Image 108: {\displaystyle \tau _{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5bcee57d9e421b3cda35d0a37a5f849a471a0a2d) A proof that relies only on the existence of certain open sets will also hold for any finer topology, and similarly a proof that relies only on certain sets not being open applies to any coarser topology. The terms _larger_ and _smaller_ are sometimes used in place of finer and coarser, respectively. The terms _stronger_ and _weaker_ are also used in the literature, but with little agreement on the meaning, so one should always be sure of an author's convention when reading.

Continuous functions
--------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Topological_space&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Continuous functions")]

A [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)")![Image 117: {\displaystyle f:X\to Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/abd1e080abef4bbdab67b43819c6431e7561361c) between topological spaces is called **[continuous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuity_(topology) "Continuity (topology)")** if for every ![Image 118: {\displaystyle x\in X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3e580967f68f36743e894aa7944f032dda6ea01d) and every neighbourhood ![Image 119: {\displaystyle N}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5e3890c981ae85503089652feb48b191b57aae3) of ![Image 120: {\displaystyle f(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/202945cce41ecebb6f643f31d119c514bec7a074) there is a neighbourhood ![Image 121: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) of ![Image 122: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) such that ![Image 123: {\displaystyle f(M)\subseteq N.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/908553a4e6ee33399be648df5d6877b3b628ae90) This relates easily to the usual definition in analysis. Equivalently, ![Image 124: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) is continuous if the [inverse image](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverse_image "Inverse image") of every open set is open.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_space#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArmstrong1983theorem_2.6-11) This is an attempt to capture the intuition that there are no "jumps" or "separations" in the function. A [homeomorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homeomorphism "Homeomorphism") is a [bijection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bijection "Bijection") that is continuous and whose [inverse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverse_function "Inverse function") is also continuous. Two spaces are called _homeomorphic_ if there exists a homeomorphism between them. From the standpoint of topology, homeomorphic spaces are essentially identical.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_space#cite_note-12)

In [category theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category_theory "Category theory"), one of the fundamental [categories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category_(mathematics) "Category (mathematics)") is **Top**, which denotes the [category of topological spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category_of_topological_spaces "Category of topological spaces") whose [objects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Object_(category_theory) "Object (category theory)") are topological spaces and whose [morphisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morphism "Morphism") are continuous functions. The attempt to classify the objects of this category ([up to](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Up_to "Up to")[homeomorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homeomorphism "Homeomorphism")) by [invariants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invariant_(mathematics) "Invariant (mathematics)") has motivated areas of research, such as [homotopy theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homotopy "Homotopy"), [homology theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homology_(mathematics) "Homology (mathematics)"), and [K-theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K-theory "K-theory").[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

Examples of topological spaces
------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Topological_space&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Examples of topological spaces")]

A given set may have many different topologies. If a set is given a different topology, it is viewed as a different topological space. Any set can be given the [discrete topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discrete_space "Discrete space") in which every subset is open. The only convergent sequences or nets in this topology are those that are eventually constant. Also, any set can be given the [trivial topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trivial_topology "Trivial topology") (also called the indiscrete topology), in which only the empty set and the whole space are open. Every sequence and net in this topology converges to every point of the space. This example shows that in general topological spaces, limits of sequences need not be unique. However, often topological spaces must be [Hausdorff spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hausdorff_space "Hausdorff space") where limit points are unique.

There exist numerous topologies on any given [finite set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_set "Finite set"). Such spaces are called [finite topological spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_topological_space "Finite topological space"). Finite spaces are sometimes used to provide examples or counterexamples to conjectures about topological spaces in general.

Any set can be given the [cofinite topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cofinite_topology "Cofinite topology") in which the open sets are the empty set and the sets whose complement is finite. This is the smallest [T 1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/T1_space "T1 space") topology on any infinite set.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_space#cite_note-13)

Any set can be given the [cocountable topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cocountable_topology "Cocountable topology"), in which a set is defined as open if it is either empty or its complement is countable. When the set is uncountable, this topology serves as a counterexample in many situations.

The real line can also be given the [lower limit topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lower_limit_topology "Lower limit topology"). Here, the basic open sets are the half open intervals ![Image 125: {\displaystyle [a,b).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/01bbf239acf3745ea7d49bb7f313d6947ea2764a) This topology on ![Image 126: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc) is strictly finer than the Euclidean topology defined above; a sequence converges to a point in this topology if and only if it converges from above in the Euclidean topology. This example shows that a set may have many distinct topologies defined on it.

If ![Image 127: {\displaystyle \gamma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a223c880b0ce3da8f64ee33c4f0010beee400b1a) is an [ordinal number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordinal_number "Ordinal number"), then the set ![Image 128: {\displaystyle \gamma =[0,\gamma )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cbf2f2096ddb6f4ff29ebef9f1f9254460aa4a57) may be endowed with the [order topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Order_topology "Order topology") generated by the intervals ![Image 129: {\displaystyle (\alpha ,\beta ),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/93ff2482d0666d264b52cc38c72d90b1dd6f32f8)![Image 130: {\displaystyle [0,\beta ),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c1903c8cc786ad08510154fed5e41cbd47cc8a6f) and ![Image 131: {\displaystyle (\alpha ,\gamma )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eedc5f5c479c598820797b4aba7de8c5a84aea8f) where ![Image 132: {\displaystyle \alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b79333175c8b3f0840bfb4ec41b8072c83ea88d3) and ![Image 133: {\displaystyle \beta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7ed48a5e36207156fb792fa79d29925d2f7901e8) are elements of ![Image 134: {\displaystyle \gamma .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f423d4c0d1a3f651562797e2198c75a3f65e09fe)

Every [manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manifold "Manifold") has a [natural topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_topology "Natural topology") since it is locally Euclidean. Similarly, every [simplex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simplex "Simplex") and every [simplicial complex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simplicial_complex "Simplicial complex") inherits a natural topology from .

The [Sierpiński space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sierpi%C5%84ski_space "Sierpiński space") is the simplest non-discrete topological space. It has important relations to the [theory of computation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theory_of_computation "Theory of computation") and semantics.

### Topology from other topologies

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Topological_space&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Topology from other topologies")]

Every subset of a topological space can be given the [subspace topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subspace_topology "Subspace topology") in which the open sets are the intersections of the open sets of the larger space with the subset. For any [indexed family](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indexed_family "Indexed family") of topological spaces, the product can be given the [product topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Product_topology "Product topology"), which is generated by the inverse images of open sets of the factors under the [projection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projection_(mathematics) "Projection (mathematics)") mappings. For example, in finite products, a basis for the product topology consists of all products of open sets. For infinite products, there is the additional requirement that in a basic open set, all but finitely many of its projections are the entire space. This construction is a special case of an [initial topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Initial_topology "Initial topology").

A [quotient space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quotient_space_(topology) "Quotient space (topology)") is defined as follows: if ![Image 135: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) is a topological space and ![Image 136: {\displaystyle Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/961d67d6b454b4df2301ac571808a3538b3a6d3f) is a set, and if ![Image 137: {\displaystyle f:X\to Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/abd1e080abef4bbdab67b43819c6431e7561361c) is a [surjective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surjection "Surjection")[function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)"), then the quotient topology on ![Image 138: {\displaystyle Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/961d67d6b454b4df2301ac571808a3538b3a6d3f) is the collection of subsets of ![Image 139: {\displaystyle Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/961d67d6b454b4df2301ac571808a3538b3a6d3f) that have open [inverse images](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverse_image "Inverse image") under ![Image 140: {\displaystyle f.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ecb3ed2e17fa8f336dcc0fd4b3eddbfb02a50ef3) In other words, the quotient topology is the finest topology on ![Image 141: {\displaystyle Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/961d67d6b454b4df2301ac571808a3538b3a6d3f) for which ![Image 142: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) is continuous. A common example of a quotient topology is when an [equivalence relation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equivalence_relation "Equivalence relation") is defined on the topological space ![Image 143: {\displaystyle X.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5ba76c5a460c4a0bb1639a193bc1830f0a773e03) The map ![Image 144: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) is then the natural projection onto the set of [equivalence classes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equivalence_class "Equivalence class"). This construction is a special case of a [final topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Final_topology "Final topology").

Metric spaces embody a [metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_(mathematics) "Metric (mathematics)"), a precise notion of distance between points.

Every [metric space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space "Metric space") can be given a metric topology, in which the basic open sets are open balls defined by the metric. This is the standard topology on any [normed vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normed_vector_space "Normed vector space"). On a finite-dimensional [vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space") this topology is the same for all norms.

There are many ways of defining a topology on ![Image 145: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0522388d36b55de7babe4bbfc49475eaf590c2bd) the set of [real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number"). The standard topology on ![Image 146: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc) is generated by the [open intervals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interval_(mathematics)#Definitions "Interval (mathematics)"). The set of all open intervals forms a [base](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Base_(topology) "Base (topology)") or basis for the topology, meaning that every open set is a union of some collection of sets from the base. In particular, this means that a set is open if there exists an open interval of non zero radius about every point in the set. More generally, the [Euclidean spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space")![Image 147: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d) can be given a topology. In the **usual topology** on ![Image 148: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d) the basic open sets are the open [balls](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ball_(mathematics) "Ball (mathematics)"). Similarly, ![Image 149: {\displaystyle \mathbb {C} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c6ff6a3dc2982018ff20f1d2c927afc74a217be6) the set of [complex numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number"), and ![Image 150: {\displaystyle \mathbb {C} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a53b4e76242764d1bca004168353c380fef25258) have a standard topology in which the basic open sets are open balls.

### Topology from algebraic structure

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Topological_space&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Topology from algebraic structure")]

For any [algebraic objects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_structure "Algebraic structure") we can introduce the discrete topology, under which the algebraic operations are continuous functions. For any such structure that is not finite, we often have a natural topology compatible with the algebraic operations, in the sense that the algebraic operations are still continuous. This leads to concepts such as [topological groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_group "Topological group"), [topological rings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_ring "Topological ring"), [topological fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_field "Topological field") and [topological vector spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_vector_space "Topological vector space") over the latter. [Local fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_field "Local field") are topological fields important in [number theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_theory "Number theory").

The [Zariski topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zariski_topology "Zariski topology") is defined algebraically on the [spectrum of a ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectrum_of_a_ring "Spectrum of a ring") or an [algebraic variety](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_variety "Algebraic variety"). On ![Image 151: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d) or ![Image 152: {\displaystyle \mathbb {C} ^{n},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0370e52054a4ddcb753d34ec7eeff4115b90359f) the closed sets of the Zariski topology are the [solution sets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solution_set "Solution set") of systems of [polynomial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial "Polynomial") equations.

Classification of topological spaces
------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Topological_space&action=edit&section=16 "Edit section: Classification of topological spaces")]

Topological spaces can be broadly classified, [up to](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Up_to "Up to") homeomorphism, by their [topological properties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_properties "Topological properties"). A topological property is a property of spaces that is invariant under homeomorphisms. To prove that two spaces are not homeomorphic it is sufficient to find a topological property not shared by them. Examples of such properties include [connectedness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connectedness_(topology) "Connectedness (topology)"), [compactness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compactness_(topology) "Compactness (topology)"), and various [separation axioms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separation_axiom "Separation axiom"). For algebraic invariants see [algebraic topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_topology "Algebraic topology").

*   [Complete Heyting algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complete_Heyting_algebra "Complete Heyting algebra") – The system of all open sets of a given topological space ordered by inclusion is a complete Heyting algebra.
*   [Compact space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compact_space "Compact space")– Type of mathematical space
*   [Convergence space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convergence_space "Convergence space")– Generalization of the notion of convergence that is found in general topology
*   [Exterior space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_space "Exterior space")
*   [Hausdorff space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hausdorff_space "Hausdorff space")– Type of topological space
*   [Hilbert space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space "Hilbert space")– Type of vector space in math
*   [Hemicontinuity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hemicontinuity "Hemicontinuity")– Semicontinuity for set-valued functions
*   [Linear subspace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_subspace "Linear subspace")– In mathematics, vector subspace
*   [Pointless topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pointless_topology "Pointless topology")
*   [Quasitopological space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasitopological_space "Quasitopological space")– Function in topology
*   [Relatively compact subspace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relatively_compact_subspace "Relatively compact subspace")– Subset of a topological space whose closure is compact
*   [Space (mathematics)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_(mathematics) "Space (mathematics)")– Mathematical set with some added structure
