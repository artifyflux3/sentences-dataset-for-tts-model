The **newton-second** (also **newton second**; symbol: **N⋅s** or **N s**)[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton_second#cite_note-1) is the unit of [impulse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Impulse_(physics) "Impulse (physics)") in the [International System of Units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Units "International System of Units") (SI). It is [dimensionally equivalent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimensional_analysis "Dimensional analysis") to the [momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum "Momentum") unit **kilogram-metre per second** (**kg⋅m/s**). One newton-second corresponds to a one-[newton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton_(unit) "Newton (unit)") force applied for one [second](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second "Second").

F→⋅t=Δ m v→{\displaystyle {\vec {F}}\cdot t=\Delta m{\vec {v}}}![Image 8: {\displaystyle {\vec {F}}\cdot t=\Delta m{\vec {v}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2ba691187c1e7df0064ae2566a789795877f631b)
It can be used to identify the resultant velocity of a mass if a force accelerates the mass for a specific time interval.

----------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Newton-second&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Definition")]

*   m{\displaystyle m}![Image 11: {\displaystyle m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a07d98bb302f3856cbabc47b2b9016692e3f7bc) is the [mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass "Mass") in [kilograms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kilogram "Kilogram") (kg)

| 1300 | 10 | 13 000 | A four-door car weighing 1 300 kg (2 900 lb) crashing at 36 km/h (22 mph). |
| 2000 | 10 | 20 000 | A mid-size SUV weighing 2 000 kg (4 400 lb) crashing at 36 km/h (22 mph). |
| 6 | 1 | 6 | The total impulse of a [class C](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Model_rocket_motor_classification "Model rocket motor classification") model rocket engine, which can be found in amateur fireworks. |
| 10 | 2 | 20 | The total impulse of a [class D](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Model_rocket_motor_classification "Model rocket motor classification") model rocket engine, which also can be found in amateur fireworks. |
| 132 500 | 8050 | 1.07×10 9 | [Space Shuttle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_Shuttle "Space Shuttle") launched from Earth to orbit[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton_second#cite_note-2) |

| 0.004 | 945 | 3.8 | A 4-gram (0.14 oz) rifle bullet (e.g. [5.56×45mm NATO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/5.56%C3%9745mm_NATO "5.56×45mm NATO")) fired at 945 m/s (3 100 ft/s). |
| 0.05 | 860 | 43 | A 50-gram (1.8 oz) [anti-material](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anti-material "Anti-material") bullet (e.g. [.50 BMG](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/.50_BMG ".50 BMG")) fired at 860 m/s (2 800 ft/s). |

1.   **[^](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton_second#cite_ref-2 "Jump up")**Space Shuttle weight here includes the heaviest possible [payload](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satellite "Satellite") (27500 kg), empty [external tank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_Shuttle_external_tank "Space Shuttle external tank") (30000 kg), and the [shuttle itself](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_Shuttle_orbiter "Space Shuttle orbiter") (75000 kg) all in a low Earth orbit (8.05 km/s). As the Space Shuttle uses [staging](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multistage_rocket "Multistage rocket"), not all launched components reach all the way to orbit (e.g. the [boosters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Solid_Rocket_Booster "Space Shuttle Solid Rocket Booster")). The total impulse gained by all stages together during the launch is 5.7×10 9 Ns.
