A **hydrogen atom** is an [atom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atom "Atom") of the [chemical element](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_element "Chemical element")[hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen"). The [electrically](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_charge "Electric charge") neutral hydrogen atom contains a single positively charged [proton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton "Proton") in the nucleus, and a single negatively charged [electron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron "Electron") bound to the nucleus by the [Coulomb force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coulomb_force "Coulomb force"). **Atomic hydrogen** constitutes [about 75%](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abundance_of_the_chemical_elements "Abundance of the chemical elements") of the [baryonic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baryon "Baryon") mass of the universe.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_atom#cite_note-1)

In everyday life on Earth, isolated hydrogen atoms (called "atomic hydrogen") are extremely rare. Instead, a hydrogen atom tends to combine with other atoms in compounds, or with another hydrogen atom to form ordinary ([diatomic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diatomic "Diatomic")) hydrogen gas, H 2. "Atomic hydrogen" and "hydrogen atom" in ordinary English use have overlapping, yet distinct, meanings. For example, a water molecule contains two hydrogen atoms, but does not contain atomic hydrogen (which would refer to isolated hydrogen atoms).

[Atomic spectroscopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_spectroscopy "Atomic spectroscopy") shows that there is a discrete infinite set of states in which a hydrogen (or any) atom can exist, contrary to the predictions of [classical physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_physics "Classical physics"). Attempts to develop a theoretical understanding of the states of the hydrogen atom have been important to the [history of quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_quantum_mechanics "History of quantum mechanics"), since all other atoms can be roughly understood by knowing in detail about this simplest atomic structure.

The most [abundant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abundance_of_the_chemical_elements "Abundance of the chemical elements")[isotope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotope "Isotope"), protium (1 H), or light hydrogen, contains no [neutrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron "Neutron") and is simply a [proton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton "Proton") and an [electron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron "Electron"). Protium is [stable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton_decay "Proton decay") and makes up 99.985% of naturally occurring hydrogen atoms.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_atom#cite_note-Housecroft-2)

[Deuterium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deuterium "Deuterium") (2 H) contains one neutron and one proton in its nucleus. Deuterium is stable, makes up 0.0156% of naturally occurring hydrogen,[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_atom#cite_note-Housecroft-2) and is used in industrial processes like [nuclear reactors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_reactor "Nuclear reactor") and [Nuclear Magnetic Resonance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deuterium_NMR "Deuterium NMR").

[Tritium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tritium "Tritium") (3 H) contains two neutrons and one proton in its nucleus and is not stable, decaying with a [half-life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Half-life "Half-life") of 12.32 years. Because of its short half-life, tritium does not exist in nature except in trace amounts.

Heavier isotopes of hydrogen are only created artificially in [particle accelerators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_accelerators "Particle accelerators") and have half-lives on the order of 10−22 seconds. They are unbound [resonances](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resonance_(particle_physics) "Resonance (particle physics)") located beyond the [neutron drip line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron_drip_line "Neutron drip line"); this results in prompt [emission of a neutron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron_emission "Neutron emission").

The formulas below are valid for all three isotopes of hydrogen, but slightly different values of the [Rydberg constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rydberg_constant "Rydberg constant") (correction formula given below) must be used for each hydrogen isotope.

Lone neutral hydrogen atoms are rare under normal conditions. However, neutral hydrogen is common when it is [covalently bound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covalent_bond "Covalent bond") to another atom, and hydrogen atoms can also exist in [cationic and anionic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ion#Anions_and_cations "Ion") forms.

If a neutral hydrogen atom loses its electron, it becomes a cation. The resulting ion, which consists solely of a proton for the usual isotope, is written as "H+" and sometimes called _[hydron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydron\_(chemistry) "Hydron (chemistry)")_. Free protons are common in the [interstellar medium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_medium "Interstellar medium"), and [solar wind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_wind "Solar wind"). In the context of [aqueous solutions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aqueous_solution "Aqueous solution") of classical [Brønsted–Lowry acids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Br%C3%B8nsted%E2%80%93Lowry_acid "Brønsted–Lowry acid"), such as [hydrochloric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid "Hydrochloric acid"), it is actually [hydronium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydronium "Hydronium"), [H](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen")3[O](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen "Oxygen")+, that is meant. Instead of a literal ionized single hydrogen atom being formed, the acid transfers the hydrogen to H 2 O, forming H 3 O+.

If instead a hydrogen atom gains a second electron, it becomes an anion. The [hydrogen anion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_anion "Hydrogen anion") is written as "H–" and called _[hydride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydride "Hydride")_.

Theoretical analysis
--------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen_atom&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Theoretical analysis")]

The hydrogen atom has special significance in [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics") and [quantum field theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_field_theory "Quantum field theory") as a simple [two-body problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two-body_problem "Two-body problem") physical system which has yielded many simple [analytical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed-form_expression "Closed-form expression") solutions in closed-form.

### Failed classical description

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen_atom&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Failed classical description")]

[Experiments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rutherford_model "Rutherford model") by [Ernest Rutherford](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ernest_Rutherford "Ernest Rutherford") in 1909 showed the structure of the atom to be a dense, positive nucleus with a tenuous negative charge cloud around it. This immediately raised questions about how such a system could be stable. [Classical electromagnetism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_electromagnetism "Classical electromagnetism") had shown that any accelerating charge radiates energy, as shown by the [Larmor formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Larmor_formula "Larmor formula"). If the electron is assumed to orbit in a perfect circle and radiates energy continuously, the electron would rapidly spiral into the nucleus with a fall time of:[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_atom#cite_note-3)![Image 3: {\displaystyle t_{\text{fall}}\approx {\frac {a_{0}^{3}}{4r_{0}^{2}c}}\approx 1.6\times 10^{-11}{\text{ s}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bfc39bf4877b5f17c0b77320afa2429a058304a9) where ![Image 4: {\displaystyle a_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/693ad9f934775838bd72406b41ada4a59785d7ba) is the [Bohr radius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bohr_radius "Bohr radius") and ![Image 5: {\displaystyle r_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fb12fcfddb65e3d1e6a044215f6e833f0cd4337b) is the [classical electron radius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_electron_radius "Classical electron radius"). If this were true, all atoms would instantly collapse. However, atoms seem to be stable. Furthermore, the spiral inward would release a smear of electromagnetic frequencies as the orbit got smaller. Instead, atoms were observed to emit only discrete frequencies of radiation. The resolution would lie in the development of [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics").

### Bohr–Sommerfeld Model

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen_atom&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Bohr–Sommerfeld Model")]

In 1913, [Niels Bohr](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Niels_Bohr "Niels Bohr") obtained the energy levels and spectral frequencies of the hydrogen atom after making a number of simple assumptions in order to correct the failed classical model. The assumptions included:

1.   Electrons can only be in certain, discrete circular orbits or _stationary states_, thereby having a discrete set of possible radii and energies.
2.   Electrons do not emit radiation while in one of these stationary states.
3.   An electron can gain or lose energy by jumping from one discrete orbit to another.

Bohr supposed that the electron's angular momentum is quantized with possible values: ![Image 6: {\displaystyle L=n\hbar }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6ac7d2abfd338c95f2ba503eb0be903cc74a3a0b) where ![Image 7: {\displaystyle n=1,2,3,\ldots }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a508e267228dcaa193fe3a7df64c94b0f31e143e) and ![Image 8: {\displaystyle \hbar }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/de68de3a92517953436c93b5a76461d49160cc41) is [Planck constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant "Planck constant") over ![Image 9: {\displaystyle 2\pi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/73efd1f6493490b058097060a572606d2c550a06). He also supposed that the [centripetal force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_force "Centripetal force") which keeps the electron in its orbit is provided by the [Coulomb force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coulomb%27s_law "Coulomb's law"), and that energy is conserved. Bohr derived the energy of each orbit of the hydrogen atom to be:[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_atom#cite_note-4)![Image 10: {\displaystyle E_{n}=-{\frac {m_{\text{e}}e^{4}}{2(4\pi \varepsilon _{0})^{2}\hbar ^{2}}}{\frac {1}{n^{2}}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b6b362ea13832ad518fca2b5bc96ef8f792cf36a) where ![Image 11: {\displaystyle m_{\text{e}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c45a5eb082ea4dafd3cb43ca39e033989e4a52eb) is the [electron mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_mass "Electron mass"), ![Image 12: {\displaystyle e}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cd253103f0876afc68ebead27a5aa9867d927467) is the [electron charge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_charge "Electron charge"), ![Image 13: {\displaystyle \varepsilon _{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/acb0a8377db20e42274444cb181d51b5532b5844) is the [vacuum permittivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vacuum_permittivity "Vacuum permittivity"), and ![Image 14: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) is the [quantum number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_number "Quantum number") (now known as the [principal quantum number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principal_quantum_number "Principal quantum number")). Bohr's predictions matched experiments measuring the [hydrogen spectral series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_spectral_series "Hydrogen spectral series") to the first order, giving more confidence to a theory that used quantized values.

For ![Image 15: {\displaystyle n=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d9ec7e1edc2e6d98f5aec2a39ae5f1c99d1e1425), the value[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_atom#cite_note-codata-5)![Image 16: {\displaystyle {\frac {m_{\text{e}}e^{4}}{2(4\pi \varepsilon _{0})^{2}\hbar ^{2}}}={\frac {m_{\text{e}}e^{4}}{8h^{2}\varepsilon _{0}^{2}}}=1\,{\text{Ry}}=13.605\;693\;122\;994(26)\,{\text{eV}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/16c8c2b35d968e07a3bfbd791658a8d28ceec607) is called the Rydberg unit of energy. It is related to the [Rydberg constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rydberg_constant "Rydberg constant")![Image 17: {\displaystyle R_{\infty }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/639146705058e9d335aed47b55a9f802bc290b5f) of [atomic physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_physics "Atomic physics") by ![Image 18: {\displaystyle 1\,{\text{Ry}}\equiv hcR_{\infty }.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fbe829950be4a402aa0fc45373f6afb72402a5b5)

The exact value of the Rydberg constant assumes that the nucleus is infinitely massive with respect to the electron. For hydrogen-1, hydrogen-2 ([deuterium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deuterium "Deuterium")), and hydrogen-3 ([tritium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tritium "Tritium")) which have finite mass, the constant must be slightly modified to use the [reduced mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reduced_mass "Reduced mass") of the system, rather than simply the mass of the electron. This includes the kinetic energy of the nucleus in the problem, because the total (electron plus nuclear) kinetic energy is equivalent to the kinetic energy of the reduced mass moving with a velocity equal to the electron velocity relative to the nucleus. However, since the nucleus is much heavier than the electron, the electron mass and reduced mass are nearly the same. The Rydberg constant _R M_ for a hydrogen atom (one electron), _R_ is given by ![Image 19: {\displaystyle R_{M}={\frac {R_{\infty }}{1+m_{\text{e}}/M}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a27af3fd3f302f7de1b9886efadf2e14cdd4a850) where ![Image 20: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) is the mass of the atomic nucleus. For hydrogen-1, the quantity ![Image 21: {\displaystyle m_{\text{e}}/M,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8f91659a787398a1787cf0bbb09f77264d537deb) is about 1/1836 (i.e. the electron-to-proton mass ratio). For deuterium and tritium, the ratios are about 1/3670 and 1/5497 respectively. These figures, when added to 1 in the denominator, represent very small corrections in the value of _R_, and thus only small corrections to all energy levels in corresponding hydrogen isotopes.

There were still problems with Bohr's model:

1.   it failed to predict other spectral details such as [fine structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fine_structure "Fine structure") and [hyperfine structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperfine_structure "Hyperfine structure")
2.   it could only predict energy levels with any accuracy for single–electron atoms (hydrogen-like atoms)
3.   the predicted values were only correct to ![Image 22: {\displaystyle \alpha ^{2}\approx 10^{-5}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/165ade58185294a8c12c63e4689bb6302ec05c60), where ![Image 23: {\displaystyle \alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b79333175c8b3f0840bfb4ec41b8072c83ea88d3) is the [fine-structure constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fine-structure_constant "Fine-structure constant").

Most of these shortcomings were resolved by [Arnold Sommerfeld's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arnold_Sommerfeld "Arnold Sommerfeld") modification of the Bohr model. Sommerfeld introduced two additional degrees of freedom, allowing an electron to move on an elliptical orbit characterized by its [eccentricity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity "Orbital eccentricity") and [declination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Declination "Declination") with respect to a chosen axis. This introduced two additional quantum numbers, which correspond to the orbital [angular momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum "Angular momentum") and its projection on the chosen axis. Thus the correct multiplicity of states (except for the factor 2 accounting for the yet unknown electron spin) was found. Further, by applying [special relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_relativity "Special relativity") to the elliptic orbits, Sommerfeld succeeded in deriving the correct expression for the fine structure of hydrogen spectra (which happens to be exactly the same as in the most elaborate Dirac theory). However, some observed phenomena, such as the anomalous [Zeeman effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zeeman_effect "Zeeman effect"), remained unexplained. These issues were resolved with the full development of quantum mechanics and the [Dirac equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dirac_equation "Dirac equation"). It is often alleged that the [Schrödinger equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schr%C3%B6dinger_equation "Schrödinger equation") is superior to the Bohr–Sommerfeld theory in describing hydrogen atom. This is not the case, as most of the results of both approaches coincide or are very close (a remarkable exception is the problem of hydrogen atom in crossed electric and magnetic fields, which cannot be self-consistently solved in the framework of the Bohr–Sommerfeld theory), and in both theories the main shortcomings result from the absence of the electron spin. It was the complete failure of the Bohr–Sommerfeld theory to explain many-electron systems (such as helium atom or hydrogen molecule) which demonstrated its inadequacy in describing quantum phenomena.

### Schrödinger equation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen_atom&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Schrödinger equation")]

The Schrödinger equation is the standard quantum-mechanics model; it allows one to calculate the stationary states and also the time evolution of quantum systems. Exact analytical answers are available for the nonrelativistic hydrogen atom. Before we go to present a formal account, here we give an elementary overview.

Given that the hydrogen atom contains a nucleus and an electron, quantum mechanics allows one to predict the probability of finding the electron at any given radial distance ![Image 24: {\displaystyle r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d1ecb613aa2984f0576f70f86650b7c2a132538). It is given by the square of a mathematical function known as the "[wavefunction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wave_function "Wave function")", which is a solution of the Schrödinger equation. The lowest energy equilibrium state of the hydrogen atom is known as the ground state. The ground state wave function is known as the ![Image 25: {\displaystyle 1\mathrm {s} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e1a34c7aa58ac9a2be11ff9ec86b0bf1b89d38b4) wavefunction. It is written as: ![Image 26: {\displaystyle \psi _{1\mathrm {s} }(r)={\frac {1}{{\sqrt {\pi }}a_{0}^{3/2}}}\mathrm {e} ^{-r/a_{0}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a416c8f5516144046ea69b00085cd92a108a740f)

Here, ![Image 27: {\displaystyle a_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/693ad9f934775838bd72406b41ada4a59785d7ba) is the numerical value of the Bohr radius. The probability density of finding the electron at a distance ![Image 28: {\displaystyle r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d1ecb613aa2984f0576f70f86650b7c2a132538) in any radial direction is the squared value of the wavefunction: ![Image 29: {\displaystyle |\psi _{1\mathrm {s} }(r)|^{2}={\frac {1}{\pi a_{0}^{3}}}\mathrm {e} ^{-2r/a_{0}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ec6cdc34c7d9aa4804ce3430e97a2334d0d94b20)

The ![Image 30: {\displaystyle 1\mathrm {s} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e1a34c7aa58ac9a2be11ff9ec86b0bf1b89d38b4) wavefunction is spherically symmetric, and the surface area of a shell at distance ![Image 31: {\displaystyle r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d1ecb613aa2984f0576f70f86650b7c2a132538) is ![Image 32: {\displaystyle 4\pi r^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b81fcce302776a01dc66fc186a1ce0a616b4d772), so the total probability ![Image 33: {\displaystyle P(r)\,dr}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6763a796c47a24dcdf4c205a66931b5e51adca3c) of the electron being in a shell at a distance ![Image 34: {\displaystyle r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d1ecb613aa2984f0576f70f86650b7c2a132538) and thickness ![Image 35: {\displaystyle dr}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dd2fef376e2849acfec1506df7edb9becf6cb136) is ![Image 36: {\displaystyle P(r)\,\mathrm {d} r=4\pi r^{2}|\psi _{1\mathrm {s} }(r)|^{2}\,\mathrm {d} r.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7ac07b9bbe3cbf77d8ffc73f4868b84cdd53f352)

It turns out that this is a maximum at ![Image 37: {\displaystyle r=a_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8e02946c820b910442410e30c0541d000973edc3). That is, the Bohr picture of an electron orbiting the nucleus at radius ![Image 38: {\displaystyle a_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/693ad9f934775838bd72406b41ada4a59785d7ba) corresponds to the most probable radius. Actually, there is a finite probability that the electron may be found at any place ![Image 39: {\displaystyle r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d1ecb613aa2984f0576f70f86650b7c2a132538), with the probability indicated by the square of the wavefunction. Since the probability of finding the electron _somewhere_ in the whole volume is unity, the integral of ![Image 40: {\displaystyle P(r)\,\mathrm {d} r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d8e71cf556155ee4d8e87ea2d62ef2061c64f381) is unity. Then we say that the wavefunction is properly normalized.

As discussed below, the ground state ![Image 41: {\displaystyle 1\mathrm {s} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e1a34c7aa58ac9a2be11ff9ec86b0bf1b89d38b4) is also indicated by the [quantum numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_number#Electron_in_an_atom "Quantum number")![Image 42: {\displaystyle (n=1,\ell =0,m=0)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cfee8d23be5e5722ac31558baaa85d7c765c78ce). The second lowest energy states, just above the ground state, are given by the quantum numbers ![Image 43: {\displaystyle (2,0,0)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a55ae623f6199e05185701003adc933e0243db81), ![Image 44: {\displaystyle (2,1,0)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/01092bd3d90f0765c2ccf9498fdfd19e700ab4c4), and ![Image 45: {\displaystyle (2,1,\pm 1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4feede153741b1780fd6a7fe175348a9170d473c). The solutions to the Schrödinger equation for hydrogen are [analytical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytical_expression "Analytical expression"), giving a simple expression for the hydrogen [energy levels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_levels "Energy levels") and thus the frequencies of the hydrogen [spectral lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectral_line "Spectral line") and fully reproduced the Bohr model and went beyond it. It also yields two other quantum numbers and the shape of the electron's wave function ("orbital") for the various possible quantum-mechanical states, thus explaining the [anisotropic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anisotropic "Anisotropic") character of atomic bonds.

The Schrödinger equation also applies to more complicated atoms and [molecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecule "Molecule"). When there is more than one electron or nucleus the solution is not analytical and either computer calculations are necessary or simplifying assumptions must be made.

Since the Schrödinger equation is only valid for non-relativistic quantum mechanics, the solutions it yields for the hydrogen atom are not entirely correct. The [Dirac equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dirac_equation "Dirac equation") of relativistic quantum theory improves these solutions (see below).

#### Results of Schrödinger equation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen_atom&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Results of Schrödinger equation")]

The solution of the Schrödinger equation (wave equation) for the hydrogen atom uses the fact that the [Coulomb potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coulomb%27s_law "Coulomb's law") produced by the nucleus is [isotropic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotropic "Isotropic") (it is radially symmetric in space and only depends on the distance to the nucleus). Although the resulting [energy eigenfunctions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_eigenfunctions "Energy eigenfunctions") (the _orbitals_) are not necessarily isotropic themselves, their dependence on the [angular coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_coordinate_system "Spherical coordinate system") follows completely generally from this isotropy of the underlying potential: the [eigenstates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eigenstates "Eigenstates") of the [Hamiltonian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamiltonian_(quantum_mechanics) "Hamiltonian (quantum mechanics)") (that is, the energy eigenstates) can be chosen as simultaneous eigenstates of the [angular momentum operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum_operator "Angular momentum operator"). This corresponds to the fact that angular momentum is conserved in the [orbital motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_motion_(quantum) "Orbital motion (quantum)") of the electron around the nucleus. Therefore, the energy eigenstates may be classified by two angular momentum [quantum numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_number "Quantum number"), ![Image 79: {\displaystyle \ell }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f066e981e530bacc07efc6a10fa82deee985929e) and ![Image 80: {\displaystyle m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a07d98bb302f3856cbabc47b2b9016692e3f7bc) (both are integers). The angular momentum quantum number ![Image 81: {\displaystyle \ell =0,1,2,\ldots }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/539209570b62d2bb78a149a06221b804b9ff2593) determines the magnitude of the angular momentum. The magnetic quantum number ![Image 82: {\displaystyle m=-\ell ,\ldots ,+\ell }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38d6e3cf59462bd2ff326fe33d8891833d3e7e3d) determines the projection of the angular momentum on the (arbitrarily chosen) ![Image 83: {\displaystyle z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf368e72c009decd9b6686ee84a375632e11de98)-axis.

In addition to mathematical expressions for total angular momentum and angular momentum projection of wavefunctions, an expression for the radial dependence of the wave functions must be found. It is only here that the details of the ![Image 84: {\displaystyle 1/r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2ab96580d23ec5eff6bb0e666531eccb7a8035d6) Coulomb potential enter (leading to [Laguerre polynomials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laguerre_polynomials "Laguerre polynomials") in ![Image 85: {\displaystyle r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d1ecb613aa2984f0576f70f86650b7c2a132538)). This leads to a third quantum number, the principal quantum number ![Image 86: {\displaystyle n=1,2,3,\ldots }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a508e267228dcaa193fe3a7df64c94b0f31e143e). The principal quantum number in hydrogen is related to the atom's total energy.

Note that the maximum value of the angular momentum quantum number is limited by the principal quantum number: it can run only up to ![Image 87: {\displaystyle n-1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fbd0b0f32b28f51962943ee9ede4fb34198a2521), i.e., ![Image 88: {\displaystyle \ell =0,1,\ldots ,n-1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f95a34ba8483e8f750c8d2289f77103c3f56affe).

Due to angular momentum conservation, states of the same ![Image 89: {\displaystyle \ell }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f066e981e530bacc07efc6a10fa82deee985929e) but different ![Image 90: {\displaystyle m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a07d98bb302f3856cbabc47b2b9016692e3f7bc) have the same energy (this holds for all problems with [rotational symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_symmetry "Rotational symmetry")). In addition, for the hydrogen atom, states of the same ![Image 91: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) but different ![Image 92: {\displaystyle \ell }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f066e981e530bacc07efc6a10fa82deee985929e) are also [degenerate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degenerate_energy_levels "Degenerate energy levels") (i.e., they have the same energy). However, this is a specific property of hydrogen and is no longer true for more complicated atoms which have an (effective) potential differing from the form ![Image 93: {\displaystyle 1/r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2ab96580d23ec5eff6bb0e666531eccb7a8035d6) (due to the presence of the inner electrons shielding the nucleus potential).

Taking into account the [spin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_(physics) "Spin (physics)") of the electron adds a last quantum number, the projection of the electron's spin angular momentum along the ![Image 94: {\displaystyle z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf368e72c009decd9b6686ee84a375632e11de98)-axis, which can take on two values. Therefore, any [eigenstate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eigenstate "Eigenstate") of the electron in the hydrogen atom is described fully by four quantum numbers. According to the usual rules of quantum mechanics, the actual state of the electron may be any [superposition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_superposition "Quantum superposition") of these states. This explains also why the choice of ![Image 95: {\displaystyle z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf368e72c009decd9b6686ee84a375632e11de98)-axis for the directional [quantization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantization_(physics) "Quantization (physics)") of the angular momentum vector is immaterial: an orbital of given ![Image 96: {\displaystyle \ell }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f066e981e530bacc07efc6a10fa82deee985929e) and ![Image 97: {\displaystyle m'}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b2ea8347f7588b19652c2098395f059d76b12b60) obtained for another preferred axis ![Image 98: {\displaystyle z'}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7c017ef8616d0c836e4b2a88c40931333c38dd19) can always be represented as a suitable superposition of the various states of different ![Image 99: {\displaystyle m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a07d98bb302f3856cbabc47b2b9016692e3f7bc) (but same ![Image 100: {\displaystyle \ell }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f066e981e530bacc07efc6a10fa82deee985929e)) that have been obtained for ![Image 101: {\displaystyle z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf368e72c009decd9b6686ee84a375632e11de98).

#### Mathematical summary of eigenstates of hydrogen atom

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen_atom&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Mathematical summary of eigenstates of hydrogen atom")]

In 1928, [Paul Dirac](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Dirac "Paul Dirac") found [an equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dirac_equation "Dirac equation") that was fully compatible with [special relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_relativity "Special relativity"), and (as a consequence) made the wave function a 4-component "[Dirac spinor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dirac_spinor "Dirac spinor")" including "up" and "down" spin components, with both positive and "negative" energy (or matter and antimatter). The solution to this equation gave the following results, more accurate than the Schrödinger solution.

The energy levels of hydrogen, including [fine structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fine_structure "Fine structure") (excluding [Lamb shift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lamb_shift "Lamb shift") and [hyperfine structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperfine_structure "Hyperfine structure")), are given by the [Sommerfeld fine-structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fine-structure_constant "Fine-structure constant") expression:[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_atom#cite_note-Sommerfeld-13)![Image 102: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}E_{j\,n}={}&-\mu c^{2}\left[1-\left(1+\left[{\frac {\alpha }{n-j-{\frac {1}{2}}+{\sqrt {\left(j+{\frac {1}{2}}\right)^{2}-\alpha ^{2}}}}}\right]^{2}\right)^{-1/2}\right]\\\approx {}&-{\frac {\mu c^{2}\alpha ^{2}}{2n^{2}}}\left[1+{\frac {\alpha ^{2}}{n^{2}}}\left({\frac {n}{j+{\frac {1}{2}}}}-{\frac {3}{4}}\right)\right],\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9e54f0064eafaeab9e7d8b3e5e41e667a3138a7b) where ![Image 103: {\displaystyle \alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b79333175c8b3f0840bfb4ec41b8072c83ea88d3) is the [fine-structure constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fine-structure_constant "Fine-structure constant") and ![Image 104: {\displaystyle j}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2f461e54f5c093e92a55547b9764291390f0b5d0) is the [total angular momentum quantum number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Total_angular_momentum_quantum_number "Total angular momentum quantum number"), which is equal to ![Image 105: {\displaystyle \left|\ell \pm {\tfrac {1}{2}}\right|}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ab90fe683d19906d827d285f4251566726c8d3d1), depending on the orientation of the electron spin relative to the orbital angular momentum.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_atom#cite_note-14) This formula represents a small correction to the energy obtained by Bohr and Schrödinger as given above. The factor in square brackets in the last expression is nearly one; the extra term arises from relativistic effects (for details, see [#Features going beyond the Schrödinger solution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_atom#Features_going_beyond_the_Schr%C3%B6dinger_solution)). It is worth noting that this expression was first obtained by [A. Sommerfeld](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arnold_Sommerfeld "Arnold Sommerfeld") in 1916 based on the relativistic version of the [old Bohr theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Old_quantum_theory "Old quantum theory"). Sommerfeld has however used different notation for the quantum numbers.

### Visualizing the hydrogen electron orbitals

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen_atom&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Visualizing the hydrogen electron orbitals")]

[![Image 106](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/cf/HAtomOrbitals.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:HAtomOrbitals.png)

Probability densities through the _xz_-plane for the electron at different quantum numbers (_ℓ_, across top; _n_, down side; _m_ = 0)

The image to the right shows the first few hydrogen atom orbitals (energy eigenfunctions). These are cross-sections of the [probability density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability_amplitude "Probability amplitude") that are color-coded (black represents zero density and white represents the highest density). The angular momentum (orbital) quantum number _ℓ_ is denoted in each column, using the usual spectroscopic letter code (_s_ means _ℓ_=0, _p_ means _ℓ_=1, _d_ means _ℓ_=2). The main (principal) quantum number _n_ (= 1, 2, 3, ...) is marked to the right of each row. For all pictures the magnetic quantum number _m_ has been set to 0, and the cross-sectional plane is the _xz_-plane (_z_ is the vertical axis). The probability density in three-dimensional space is obtained by rotating the one shown here around the _z_-axis.

The "[ground state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ground_state "Ground state")", i.e. the state of lowest energy, in which the electron is usually found, is the first one, the 1 _s_ state ([principal quantum level](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principal_quantum_level "Principal quantum level")_n_ = 1, _ℓ_ = 0).

Black lines occur in each but the first orbital: these are the nodes of the wavefunction, i.e. where the probability density is zero. (More precisely, the nodes are [spherical harmonics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_harmonics "Spherical harmonics") that appear as a result of solving the [Schrödinger equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schr%C3%B6dinger_equation "Schrödinger equation") in spherical coordinates.)

The [quantum numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_number "Quantum number") determine the layout of these nodes. There are:[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

*   ![Image 108: {\displaystyle \ell }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f066e981e530bacc07efc6a10fa82deee985929e) of which are angular nodes:

#### Features going beyond the Schrödinger solution

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen_atom&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Features going beyond the Schrödinger solution")]

There are several important effects that are neglected by the Schrödinger equation and which are responsible for certain small but measurable deviations of the real spectral lines from the predicted ones:

*   Although the mean speed of the electron in hydrogen is only 1/137th of the [speed of light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light "Speed of light"), many modern experiments are sufficiently precise that a complete theoretical explanation requires a fully relativistic treatment of the problem. A relativistic treatment results in a momentum increase of about 1 part in 37,000 for the electron. Since the electron's wavelength is determined by its momentum, orbitals containing higher speed electrons show contraction due to smaller wavelengths.
*   Even when there is no external [magnetic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_field "Magnetic field"), in the [inertial frame](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inertial_frame "Inertial frame") of the moving electron, the electromagnetic field of the nucleus has a magnetic component. The spin of the electron has an associated [magnetic moment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_moment "Magnetic moment") which interacts with this magnetic field. This effect is also explained by special relativity, and it leads to the so-called _[spin–orbit coupling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin%E2%80%93orbit\_interaction "Spin–orbit interaction")_, i.e., an interaction between the [electron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron "Electron")'s [orbital motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_motion_(quantum) "Orbital motion (quantum)") around the nucleus, and its [spin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_(physics) "Spin (physics)").

Both of these features (and more) are incorporated in the relativistic [Dirac equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dirac_equation "Dirac equation"), with predictions that come still closer to experiment. Again the Dirac equation may be solved analytically in the special case of a two-body system, such as the hydrogen atom. The resulting solution quantum states now must be classified by the [total angular momentum number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Total_angular_momentum_quantum_number "Total angular momentum quantum number")_j_ (arising through the coupling between [electron spin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_spin "Electron spin") and [orbital angular momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum_operator "Angular momentum operator")). States of the same _j_ and the same _n_ are still degenerate. Thus, direct analytical solution of [Dirac equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dirac_equation "Dirac equation") predicts 2S(⁠1/2⁠) and 2P(⁠1/2⁠) levels of hydrogen to have exactly the same energy, which is in a contradiction with observations ([Lamb–Retherford experiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lamb_shift "Lamb shift")).

*   There are always [vacuum fluctuations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_fluctuation "Quantum fluctuation") of the [electromagnetic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_field "Electromagnetic field"), according to quantum mechanics. Due to such fluctuations degeneracy between states of the same _j_ but different _l_ is lifted, giving them slightly different energies. This has been demonstrated in the famous [Lamb–Retherford experiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lamb_shift "Lamb shift") and was the starting point for the development of the theory of [quantum electrodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_electrodynamics "Quantum electrodynamics") (which is able to deal with these vacuum fluctuations and employs the famous [Feynman diagrams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feynman_diagram "Feynman diagram") for approximations using [perturbation theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perturbation_theory_(quantum_mechanics) "Perturbation theory (quantum mechanics)")). This effect is now called [Lamb shift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lamb_shift "Lamb shift").

For these developments, it was essential that the solution of the Dirac equation for the hydrogen atom could be worked out exactly, such that any experimentally observed deviation had to be taken seriously as a signal of failure of the theory.

Alternatives to the Schrödinger theory
--------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen_atom&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Alternatives to the Schrödinger theory")]

In the language of [Heisenberg's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Werner_Heisenberg "Werner Heisenberg")[matrix mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_mechanics "Matrix mechanics"), the hydrogen atom was first solved by [Wolfgang Pauli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Pauli "Wolfgang Pauli")[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_atom#cite_note-pauli_1926-15) using a [rotational symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_symmetry "Rotational symmetry") in four dimensions [O(4)-symmetry] generated by the [angular momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum "Angular momentum") and the [Laplace–Runge–Lenz vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laplace%E2%80%93Runge%E2%80%93Lenz_vector "Laplace–Runge–Lenz vector"). By extending the symmetry group O(4) to the [dynamical group](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Dynamical_group&action=edit&redlink=1 "Dynamical group (page does not exist)") O(4,2), the entire spectrum and all transitions were embedded in a single irreducible group representation.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_atom#cite_note-16)

In 1979 the (non-relativistic) hydrogen atom was solved for the first time within [Feynman's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/R.P._Feynman "R.P. Feynman")[path integral formulation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Path_integral_formulation "Path integral formulation") of [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics") by Duru and Kleinert.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_atom#cite_note-17)[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_atom#cite_note-18) This work greatly extended the range of applicability of [Feynman's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/R.P._Feynman "R.P. Feynman") method.

Further alternative models are [Bohm mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_Broglie%E2%80%93Bohm_theory "De Broglie–Bohm theory") and the [complex Hamilton–Jacobi formulation of quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_potential#Quantum_Hamilton%E2%80%93Jacobi_equation "Quantum potential").
