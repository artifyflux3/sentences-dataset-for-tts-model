Title: History

URL Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History

Published Time: 2001-11-18T00:42:42Z

Markdown Content:
**History** is the systematic study of the [past](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Past "Past"), focusing primarily on the [human past](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_history "Human history"). As an [academic discipline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Academic_discipline "Academic discipline"), it analyses and interprets [evidence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evidence "Evidence") to construct [narratives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Narrative "Narrative") about what happened and explain why it happened. Some theorists categorize history as a [social science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_science "Social science"), while others see it as part of the [humanities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Humanities "Humanities") or consider it a hybrid discipline. Similar debates surround the purpose of history—for example, whether its main aim is theoretical, to uncover the [truth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Truth "Truth"), or practical, to learn lessons from the past. In a more general sense, the term _history_ refers not to an academic field but to the [past](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Past "Past") itself, times in the past, or to individual texts about the past.

[Historical research](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Historical_research "Historical research") relies on [primary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primary_source "Primary source") and [secondary sources](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Secondary_sources "Secondary sources") to reconstruct past events and validate interpretations. [Source criticism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Source_criticism "Source criticism") is used to evaluate these sources, assessing their authenticity, content, and reliability. Historians strive to integrate the perspectives of several sources to develop a coherent narrative. Different schools of thought, such as [positivism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positivism "Positivism"), the [_Annales_ school](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Annales_school "Annales school"), [Marxism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marxism "Marxism"), and [postmodernism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism "Postmodernism"), have distinct [methodological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methodological "Methodological") approaches.

History is a broad discipline encompassing many branches. Some focus on specific [time periods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Periodization "Periodization"), such as [ancient history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_history "Ancient history"), while others concentrate on particular [geographic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geographic "Geographic") regions, such as the [history of Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_Africa "History of Africa"). Thematic categorizations include [political history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_history "Political history"), [military history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Military_history "Military history"), [social history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_history "Social history"), and [economic history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economic_history "Economic history"). Branches associated with specific research methods and sources include [quantitative history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantitative_history "Quantitative history"), [comparative history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comparative_history "Comparative history"), and [oral history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oral_history "Oral history").

History emerged as a field of inquiry in [antiquity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_history "Ancient history") to replace myth-infused narratives, with influential early traditions originating in [Greece](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greece "Ancient Greece"), [China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_China "Ancient China"), and later in the [Islamic world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamic_world "Islamic world"). Historical writing evolved throughout the ages and became increasingly professional, particularly during the 19th century, when a rigorous methodology and various academic institutions were established. History is related to many fields, including [historiography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Historiography "Historiography"), [philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophy "Philosophy"), [education](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Education "Education"), and [politics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics "Politics").

Definition
----------

As an [academic discipline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Academic_discipline "Academic discipline"), history is the study of the past with the main focus on the human past.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-1) It conceptualizes and describes what happened by collecting and analysing [evidence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evidence "Evidence") to construct [narratives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Narrative "Narrative"). These narratives cover not only how events developed over time but also why they happened and in which contexts, providing an explanation of relevant background conditions and [causal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Causality "Causality") mechanisms. History further examines the meaning of historical events and the underlying [human motives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Motivation "Motivation") driving them.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-2)

In a slightly different sense, _history_ refers to the past events themselves. Under this interpretation, history is what happened rather than the academic field studying what happened. When used as a [countable noun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Countable_noun "Countable noun"), _a history_ is a representation of the past in the form of a history text. History texts are cultural products involving active [interpretation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interpretation_(philosophy) "Interpretation (philosophy)") and reconstruction. The narratives presented in them can change as historians discover new evidence or reinterpret already-known sources. The past itself, by contrast, is static and unchangeable.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-3) Some historians focus on the interpretative and explanatory aspects to distinguish histories from [chronicles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chronicle "Chronicle"), arguing that chronicles only catalogue events in chronological order, whereas histories aim at a comprehensive understanding of their causes, contexts, and consequences.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-4)[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-6)

History has been primarily concerned with written documents. It focused on [recorded history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recorded_history "Recorded history") since the [invention of writing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invention_of_writing "Invention of writing"), leaving [prehistory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prehistory "Prehistory")[[b]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-8) to other fields, such as [archaeology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archaeology "Archaeology").[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-9) Its scope broadened in the 20th century as historians became interested in the human past before the invention of writing.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-10)[[c]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-12)

Historians debate whether history is a [social science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_science "Social science") or forms part of the [humanities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Humanities "Humanities"). Like social scientists, historians formulate [hypotheses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypotheses "Hypotheses"), gather objective evidence, and present arguments based on this evidence. At the same time, history aligns closely with the humanities because of its reliance on subjective aspects associated with interpretation, [storytelling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Storytelling "Storytelling"), human experience, and [cultural heritage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cultural_heritage "Cultural heritage").[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-13) Some historians strongly support one or the other classification while others characterize history as a hybrid discipline that does not belong to one category at the exclusion of the other.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-14) History contrasts with _[pseudohistory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudohistory "Pseudohistory")_, a label used to describe practices that deviate from historiographical standards by relying on disputed historical evidence, selectively ignoring genuine evidence, or using other means to distort the historical record. Often motivated by specific [ideological agendas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ideology "Ideology"), pseudohistorical practices mimic [historical methodology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Historical_method "Historical method") to promote biased, misleading narratives that lack rigorous analysis and scholarly consensus.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-auto1-15)

### Purpose

Various suggestions about the purpose or value of history have been made. Some historians propose that its primary function is the pure discovery of [truth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Truth "Truth") about the past. This view emphasizes that the disinterested pursuit of truth is an [end in itself](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/End_in_itself "End in itself"), while external purposes, associated with ideology or [politics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics "Politics"), threaten to undermine the accuracy of historical research by distorting the past. In this role, history also challenges traditional [myths](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Myth "Myth") lacking factual support.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-16)[[d]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-18)

A different perspective suggests that the main value of history lies in the lessons it teaches for the present. This view is based on the idea that an understanding of the past can guide [decision-making](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decision-making "Decision-making"), for example, to avoid repeating previous mistakes.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-19) A related perspective focuses on a general understanding of the [human condition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_condition "Human condition"), making people aware of the diversity of [human behaviour](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_behavior "Human behavior") across different contexts—similar to what one can learn by visiting foreign countries.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-20) History can also foster [social cohesion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_cohesion "Social cohesion") by providing people with a [collective identity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collective_identity "Collective identity") through a shared past, helping to preserve and cultivate cultural heritage and values across generations.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-21) For some scholars, including [Whig historians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whig_historians "Whig historians") and the Marxist scholar [E. H. Carr](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/E._H._Carr "E. H. Carr"), history is a key to understanding the present[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-22) and, in Carr's case, shaping the future.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-Evans2002p2-23)

History has sometimes been used for political or ideological purposes, for instance, to justify the status quo by emphasising the respectability of certain traditions or to promote change by highlighting past injustices.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-24) In extreme forms, evidence is intentionally ignored or misinterpreted to construct misleading narratives, which can result in pseudohistory or [historical denialism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Historical_denialism "Historical denialism").[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-auto1-15)[[e]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-26) Influential examples are [Holocaust denial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holocaust_denial "Holocaust denial"), [Armenian genocide denial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Armenian_genocide_denial "Armenian genocide denial"), [Nanjing Massacre denial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nanjing_Massacre_denial "Nanjing Massacre denial"), and [Holodomor denial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holodomor_denial "Holodomor denial").[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-27)

### Etymology

[![Image 1: Photo of a damaged text written in black ink](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/POxy_v0017_n2099_a_01_hires_-_cropped.jpg/250px-POxy_v0017_n2099_a_01_hires_-_cropped.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:POxy_v0017_n2099_a_01_hires_-_cropped.jpg)

Fragment of the _[Histories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Histories\_(Herodotus) "Histories (Herodotus)")_ by [Herodotus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herodotus "Herodotus"), an Ancient Greek historical text[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-28)

The word _history_ comes from the [Ancient Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek "Ancient Greek") term [ἵστωρ](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E1%BC%B5%CF%83%CF%84%CF%89%CF%81 "wikt:ἵστωρ") (_histōr_), meaning 'learned, wise man'. It gave rise to the Ancient Greek word [ἱστορία](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E1%BC%B1%CF%83%CF%84%CE%BF%CF%81%CE%AF%CE%B1 "wikt:ἱστορία") (_historiā_), which had a wide meaning associated with inquiry in general and giving testimony. The term was later adopted into [Classical Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_Latin "Classical Latin") as _historia_. In [Hellenistic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hellenistic_period "Hellenistic period") and [Roman times](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Empire "Roman Empire"), the meaning of the term shifted, placing more emphasis on narrative aspects and the art of presentation rather than focusing on investigation and testimony.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-29)

The word entered [Middle English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_English "Middle English") in the 14th century via the [Old French](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Old_French "Old French") term _histoire_.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-30) At this time, it meant 'story, tale', encompassing both factual and fictional narratives. In the 15th century, its meaning shifted to cover the branch of knowledge studying the past in addition to narratives about the past.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-31) In the 18th and 19th centuries, the word _history_ became more closely associated with factual accounts and evidence-based inquiry, coinciding with the professionalization of historical inquiry, a meaning still dominant in contemporary usage.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-32) The dual meaning, referring to both mere stories and factual accounts of the past, is present in the terms for history in many other European languages. They include the French _histoire_, the Italian _storia_, and the German _Geschichte_.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-33)

Methods
-------

The historical method is a [set of techniques](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methodology "Methodology") historians use to research and interpret the past, covering the processes of collecting, evaluating, and synthesizing evidence.[[f]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-35) It seeks to ensure scholarly rigour, accuracy, and reliability in how historical [evidence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evidence "Evidence") is chosen, analysed, and interpreted.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-36) Historical research often starts with a [research question](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Research_question "Research question") to define the scope of the inquiry. Some research questions focus on a simple description of what happened. Others aim to explain why a particular event occurred, refute an existing theory, or confirm a new hypothesis.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-37)

### Sources and source criticism

To answer research questions, historians rely on various types of evidence to reconstruct the past and support their conclusions. Historical evidence is usually divided into [primary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primary_source "Primary source") and [secondary sources](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Secondary_sources "Secondary sources").[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-38) A primary source is a source that originated during the period that is studied. Primary sources can take various forms, such as official documents, letters, diaries, eyewitness accounts, photographs, and audio or video recordings. They also include historical remains examined in [archaeology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archaeology "Archaeology"), [geology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geology "Geology"), and the [medical sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medical_sciences "Medical sciences"), such as artefacts and [fossils](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fossil "Fossil") unearthed from [excavations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Excavations "Excavations"). Primary sources offer the most direct evidence of historical events.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-39)

[![Image 2: Photo of archive storage area; on the left, the hand cranks to operate shelving units; on the right, the shelves of one unit containing storage boxes](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Collections_Archive%2C_St._Petersburg_Museum_of_History%2C_St._Petersburg%2C_FL.jpg/250px-Collections_Archive%2C_St._Petersburg_Museum_of_History%2C_St._Petersburg%2C_FL.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Collections_Archive,_St._Petersburg_Museum_of_History,_St._Petersburg,_FL.jpg)

Archives preserve large quantities of original sources for researchers to access.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-40)

A secondary source is a source that analyses or interprets information found in other sources.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-41) Whether a document is a primary or a secondary source depends not only on the document itself but also on the purpose for which it is used. For example, if a historian writes a text about slavery based on an analysis of historical documents, then the text is a secondary source on slavery and a primary source on the historian's opinion.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-42)[[g]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-44) Consistency with available sources is one of the main standards of historical works. For instance, the discovery of new sources may lead historians to revise or dismiss previously accepted narratives.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-45) To find and access primary and secondary sources, historians consult [archives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archive "Archive"), [libraries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Libraries "Libraries"), and [museums](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Museum "Museum"). Archives play a central role by preserving countless original sources and making them available to researchers in a systematic and accessible manner. Thanks to technological advances, historians increasingly rely on online resources, which offer vast digital [databases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Database "Database") with methods to search and access specific documents.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-46)

[Source criticism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Source_criticism "Source criticism") is the process of analysing and evaluating the information a source provides.[[h]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-48) Typically, this process begins with external criticism, which evaluates the authenticity of a source. It addresses the questions of when and where the source was created and seeks to identify the author, understand their reasons for producing the source, and determine if it has undergone some type of modification since its creation. Additionally, the process involves distinguishing between original works, copies, and deceptive forgeries.[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-49)

Internal criticism evaluates the content of a source, typically beginning with the clarification of the meaning within the source. This involves disambiguating individual terms that could be misunderstood but may also require a general translation if the source is written in an unfamiliar language.[[i]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-51) Once the information content of a source is understood, internal criticism is specifically interested in determining accuracy. Critics ask whether the information is reliable or misrepresents the topic and further question whether the source is comprehensive or omits important details. One way to make these assessments is to evaluate whether the author was able, in principle, to provide a faithful presentation of the studied event. Other approaches include the assessment of the influences of the author's intentions and prejudices, and cross-referencing information with other credible sources. Being aware of the inadequacies of a source helps historians decide whether and which aspects of it to trust, and how to use it to construct a narrative.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-52)

### Synthesis and schools of thought

The selection, analysis, and criticism of sources result in the validation of a large collection of mostly isolated statements about the past. As a next step, sometimes termed _historical synthesis_, historians examine how the individual pieces of evidence fit together to form part of a larger story.[[j]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-54) Constructing this broader perspective is crucial for a comprehensive understanding of the topic as a whole. It is a creative aspect[[k]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-56) of historical writing that reconstructs, interprets, and explains what happened by showing how different events are connected.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-57) In this way, historians address not only which events occurred but also why they occurred and what consequences they had.[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-58) While there are no universally accepted techniques for this synthesis, historians rely on various interpretative tools and approaches in this process.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-59)

[![Image 3: Drawing of a seated man in formal dark clothes](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Auguste_Comte.jpg/250px-Auguste_Comte.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Auguste_Comte.jpg)

[Auguste Comte](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Auguste_Comte "Auguste Comte") articulated [positivism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positivism "Positivism"), advocating a science-based approach to history.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-60)

One tool to provide an accessible overview of complex developments is the use of [periodization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Periodization "Periodization"), which divides a timeframe into different periods, each organized around central themes or developments that shaped the period. For example, the [three-age system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-age_system "Three-age system") is traditionally used to divide early human history into [Stone Age](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stone_Age "Stone Age"), [Bronze Age](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bronze_Age "Bronze Age"), and [Iron Age](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron_Age "Iron Age") based on the predominant materials and technologies during these periods.[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-61) Another methodological tool is the examination of silences, gaps or omissions in the historical record of events that occurred but did not leave significant evidential traces. Silences can happen when contemporaries find information too obvious to document but may also occur if there are specific reasons to withhold or destroy information.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-auto-62)[[l]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-65) Conversely, when large [datasets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dataset "Dataset") are available, [quantitative approaches](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantitative_history "Quantitative history") can be used. For instance, economic and social historians commonly employ [statistical analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistical_analysis "Statistical analysis") to identify patterns and trends associated with large groups.[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-66)

Different schools of thought often come with their own methodological implications for how to write history.[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-67)[Positivists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positivism "Positivism") emphasize the scientific nature of historical inquiry, focusing on [empirical evidence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empirical_evidence "Empirical evidence") to discover [objective truths](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Objectivity_(science) "Objectivity (science)").[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-68) In contrast, [postmodernists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism "Postmodernism") reject [grand narratives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grand_narratives "Grand narratives") that claim to offer a single, objective truth. Instead, they highlight the [subjective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subjectivity_and_objectivity_(philosophy) "Subjectivity and objectivity (philosophy)") nature of historical interpretation, which leads to a multiplicity of divergent perspectives.[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-69)[Marxists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marxism "Marxism") interpret historical developments as expressions of economic forces and [class struggles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Class_struggle "Class struggle").[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-70) The [_Annales_ school](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Annales_school "Annales school") highlights long-term social and economic trends while relying on quantitative and interdisciplinary methods.[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-71)[Feminist historians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feminist_history "Feminist history") study the role of [gender](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gender "Gender") in history, with a particular interest in analysing the experiences of women to challenge [patriarchal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patriarchy "Patriarchy") perspectives.[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-72)

Areas of study
--------------

History is a wide field of inquiry encompassing many branches. Some branches focus on a specific time period, while others concentrate on a particular geographic region or a distinct theme. Specializations of different types can usually be combined; for example, a work on economic history in ancient Egypt merges temporal, regional, and thematic perspectives. For topics with a broad scope, the amount of primary sources is often too extensive for an individual historian to review, forcing them to either narrow the scope of their topic or also rely on secondary sources to arrive at a wide overview.[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-73)

### By period

Chronological division is a common approach to organizing the vast expanse of history into more manageable segments. Different [periods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Periodization "Periodization") are often defined based on dominant themes that characterize a specific time frame and significant events that initiated these developments or brought them to an end. Depending on the selected context and level of detail, a period may be as short as a decade or longer than several centuries.[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-74) A traditionally influential approach divides [human history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_history "Human history") into [prehistory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prehistory "Prehistory"), [ancient history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_history "Ancient history"), [post-classical history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Post-classical_history "Post-classical history"), [early modern history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Early_modern_history "Early modern history"), and [modern history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modern_history "Modern history").[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-75)[[m]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-77) Depending on the region and theme, the time frames covered by these periods can vary and historians may use entirely different periodizations.[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-78) For example, traditional periodizations of [Chinese history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_history "Chinese history") follow the [main dynasties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynasties_of_China "Dynasties of China"),[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-79) and the division into [pre-Columbian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pre-Columbian_era "Pre-Columbian era"), [colonial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_colonization_of_the_Americas "European colonization of the Americas"), and post-colonial periods plays a central role in the [history of the Americas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_the_Americas "History of the Americas").[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-80)

[![Image 4: Photo of the skeleton of a female hominin in a standing position, displayed in a museum](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Lucy_Mexico.jpg/250px-Lucy_Mexico.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Lucy_Mexico.jpg)

Historians draw on evidence from various fields to examine prehistory, including fossils like [Lucy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lucy_(Australopithecus) "Lucy (Australopithecus)").[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-81)

The study of prehistory includes the examination of the evolution of [human-like species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hominini "Hominini") several million years ago, leading to the emergence of [anatomically modern humans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homo_sapiens "Homo sapiens") about 200,000 years ago.[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-82) Subsequently, [humans migrated out of Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recent_African_origin_of_modern_humans "Recent African origin of modern humans") to populate most of the earth. Towards the end of prehistory, technological advances in the form of new and improved tools led many groups to give up their established [nomadic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nomadic "Nomadic") lifestyle, based on [hunting and gathering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hunting_and_gathering "Hunting and gathering"), in favour of a [sedentary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sedentary "Sedentary") lifestyle supported by [early forms of agriculture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neolithic_Revolution "Neolithic Revolution").[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-83) The absence of written documents from this period presents researchers with unique challenges. It results in an interdisciplinary approach relying on other forms of evidence from fields such as [archaeology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archaeology "Archaeology"), [anthropology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anthropology "Anthropology"), [palaeontology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palaeontology "Palaeontology"), and [geology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geology "Geology").[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-84)

Historians studying the ancient period examine the [emergence of the first major civilizations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cradles_of_civilization "Cradles of civilization") in regions such as [Mesopotamia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mesopotamia "Mesopotamia"), Egypt, the [Indus Valley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indus_Valley_Civilisation "Indus Valley Civilisation"), China, and Peru, beginning approximately 3500 BCE in some regions. The new social, economic, and political complexities necessitated the [development of writing systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invention_of_writing "Invention of writing"). Thanks to advancements in agriculture, surplus food allowed these civilizations to support larger populations, leading to [urbanization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urbanization "Urbanization"), the establishment of trade networks, and the emergence of regional empires. In the later part of the ancient period, sometimes termed the _classical period_, societies in China, India, Persia, and the Mediterranean expanded further, reaching new cultural, scientific, and political heights. Meanwhile, influential religious systems and philosophical ideas were first formulated, such as [Hinduism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hinduism "Hinduism"), [Buddhism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buddhism "Buddhism"), [Confucianism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confucianism "Confucianism"), [Judaism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Judaism "Judaism"), and [Greek philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_philosophy "Greek philosophy").[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-85)

In the study of post-classical or medieval history, which began around 500 CE, historians note the growing influence of major religions. [Missionary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Missionary "Missionary") religions, like Buddhism, [Christianity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christianity "Christianity"), and [Islam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islam "Islam"), spread rapidly and established themselves as [world religions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_religions "World religions"), marking a cultural shift as they gradually replaced other belief systems. Meanwhile, inter-regional trade networks flourished, leading to increased technological and cultural exchange. Conquering many territories in Asia and Europe, the [Mongol Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mongol_Empire "Mongol Empire") became a dominant force during the 13th and 14th centuries.[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-86)

Historians focused on early modern history, which started roughly in 1500 CE, commonly highlight how European states rose to global power. As [gunpowder empires](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gunpowder_empires "Gunpowder empires"), they explored and [colonized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colonialism "Colonialism") large parts of the world. As a result, the Americas were integrated into the global network, triggering a vast [biological exchange](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Columbian_exchange "Columbian exchange") of plants, animals, people, and diseases.[[n]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-88) The [Scientific Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_Revolution "Scientific Revolution") prompted major discoveries and accelerated technological progress. It was accompanied by other intellectual developments, such as [humanism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Humanism "Humanism") and the [Enlightenment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment "Age of Enlightenment"), which ushered in [secularization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Secularization "Secularization").[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-89)

[![Image 5: Oil painting of worker in an iron rolling mill](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Adolph_Menzel_-_Eisenwalzwerk_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/250px-Adolph_Menzel_-_Eisenwalzwerk_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Adolph_Menzel_-_Eisenwalzwerk_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg)

The Industrial Revolution had a profound impact on economic and social life, marking the transition from [agrarian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agrarian_society "Agrarian society") to [industrial societies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Industrial_societies "Industrial societies").[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-90)

In the study of modern history, which began at the end of the 18th century, historians are interested in how the [Industrial Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Industrial_Revolution "Industrial Revolution") transformed economies by introducing more efficient modes of production. Western powers [established vast colonial empires](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Age_of_Imperialism "Age of Imperialism"), gaining superiority through industrialized military technology. The increased international exchange of goods, ideas, and people marked the beginning of [globalization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Globalization "Globalization"). Various social revolutions challenged [autocratic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autocratic "Autocratic") and colonial regimes, paving the way for [democracies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democracies "Democracies"). Many developments in fields like science, technology, economy, living standards, and human population accelerated at unprecedented rates. This happened despite the widespread destruction caused by two [world wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_wars "World wars"), which rebalanced international power relations by undermining European dominance.[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-91)

### By geographic location

Areas of historical study can also be categorized by the geographic locations they examine.[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-92)[Geography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography "Geography") plays a central role in history through its influence on [food production](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Food_production "Food production"), [natural resources](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_resources "Natural resources"), economic activities, political boundaries, and cultural interactions.[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-93)[[o]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-95) Some historical works limit their scope to small regions, such as a village or a settlement. Others focus on broad territories that encompass entire continents, like the histories of Africa, Asia, Europe, the Americas, and Oceania.[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-96)

[![Image 6: Photo of the Pyramids of Giza, with the three main pyramids at the center against a blue sky in the background](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Pyramids_of_the_Giza_Necropolis.jpg/250px-Pyramids_of_the_Giza_Necropolis.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Pyramids_of_the_Giza_Necropolis.jpg)

The [Pyramids of Giza](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyramids_of_Giza "Pyramids of Giza") showcase the lasting heritage of the ancient Egyptian civilization.[[82]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-97)

The [history of Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_Africa "History of Africa") begins with the examination of the evolution of anatomically modern humans.[[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-98) Ancient historians describe how the invention of writing and the establishment of civilization happened in [ancient Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Egypt "Ancient Egypt") in the 4th millennium BCE.[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-99) Over the next millennia, other notable civilizations and kingdoms formed in [Nubia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nubia "Nubia"), [Axum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Aksum "Kingdom of Aksum"), [Carthage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Carthage "Ancient Carthage"), [Ghana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ghana_Empire "Ghana Empire"), [Mali](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mali_Empire "Mali Empire"), and [Songhay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Songhai_Empire "Songhai Empire").[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-100) Islam began spreading across North Africa in the 7th century CE and became the dominant faith in many empires. Meanwhile, trade along the [trans-Saharan route](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trans-Saharan_route "Trans-Saharan route") intensified.[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-101) Beginning in the 15th century, millions of Africans were enslaved and forcibly transported to the Americas as part of the [Atlantic slave trade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atlantic_slave_trade "Atlantic slave trade").[[87]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-102) Most of the continent [was colonized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scramble_for_Africa "Scramble for Africa") by European powers in the late 19th and early 20th centuries.[[88]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-103) Amid rising [nationalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/African_nationalism "African nationalism"), African states gradually [gained independence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decolonisation_of_Africa "Decolonisation of Africa") in the aftermath of [World War II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II "World War II"), a period that saw economic progress, rapid population growth, and struggles for political stability.[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-104)

Historians studying the [history of Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_Asia "History of Asia") note the arrival of anatomically modern humans around 100,000 years ago.[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-105) They explore Asia's role as one of the cradles of civilization, with the emergence of some of the first ancient civilizations in Mesopotamia, the Indus Valley, and China beginning in the 4th and 3rd millennia BCE.[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-106) In the following millennia, civilisations on the Asian continent gave birth to all major world religions and several influential philosophical traditions, such as Hinduism, Buddhism, Confucianism, [Taoism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taoism "Taoism"), Christianity, and Islam.[[92]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-107) Other developments were the establishment of the [Silk Road](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silk_Road "Silk Road"), which facilitated trade and cultural exchange across [Eurasia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eurasia "Eurasia"), and the formation of powerful empires, such as the Mongol Empire.[[93]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-auto3-108) European influence grew over the following centuries, ushering in the modern era. It culminated in the 19th and early 20th centuries when many parts of Asia came under direct colonial control [until the end of World War II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decolonisation_of_Asia "Decolonisation of Asia").[[94]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-109) The post-independence period was characterized by modernization, economic growth, and a steep increase in population.[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-110)

[![Image 7: A map of Ancient Greece, showing both sides of the Aegean Sea](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Greecemap-en.svg/250px-Greecemap-en.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Greecemap-en.svg)

Due to its influence on Western culture and philosophy, [Ancient Greece](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greece "Ancient Greece") is an important area of study for historians of Europe.[[96]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-Roberts_1997_Importance-111)

In the study of the [history of Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_Europe "History of Europe"), historians describe the arrival of the first anatomically modern humans about 45,000 years ago.[[97]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-112) They explore how in the first millennium BCE the [Ancient Greeks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greeks "Ancient Greeks") contributed key elements to the [culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_culture "Western culture"), [philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_philosophy "Western philosophy"), and politics associated with the [Western world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_world "Western world"),[[96]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-Roberts_1997_Importance-111) and how their cultural heritage influenced the [Roman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Empire "Roman Empire") and [Byzantine Empires](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantine_Empire "Byzantine Empire").[[98]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-113) The [medieval period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval_period "Medieval period") began with the fall of the [Western Roman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_Roman_Empire "Western Roman Empire") in the 5th century CE and was marked by the [spread of Christianity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spread_of_Christianity "Spread of Christianity").[[99]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-114) Starting in the 15th century, European exploration and colonization interconnected the globe, while cultural, intellectual, and scientific developments transformed Western societies.[[100]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-115) From the late 18th to the early 20th centuries, European global dominance was further solidified by the Industrial Revolution and the establishment of large overseas colonies.[[101]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-116) It came to an end because of the devastating effects of two world wars.[[102]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-117) In the following [Cold War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cold_War "Cold War") era, the continent was divided into a [Western](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_bloc "Western bloc") and an [Eastern bloc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastern_bloc "Eastern bloc"). They pursued political and economic integration in the aftermath of the Cold War.[[103]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-118)

Historians examining the history of the Americas document the arrival of the first humans around 20,000 to 15,000 years ago.[[104]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-119) The Americas were home to some of the earliest civilizations, like the [Norte Chico civilization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norte_Chico_civilization "Norte Chico civilization") in South America and the [Maya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maya_civilization "Maya civilization") and [Olmec civilizations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Olmec_civilization "Olmec civilization") in Central America.[[105]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-120) Over the next millennia, major empires arose beside them, such as the [Teotihuacan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teotihuacan "Teotihuacan"), [Aztec](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aztec "Aztec"), and [Inca empires](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inca_empire "Inca empire").[[106]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-121) Following the arrival of the Europeans from the late 15th century onwards, the spread of newly introduced diseases drastically reduced the local population. Together with colonization, it led to the collapse of major empires as demographic and cultural landscapes were reshaped.[[107]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-122)[Independence movements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decolonization_of_the_Americas "Decolonization of the Americas") in the 18th and 19th centuries led to the formation of new nations across the Americas.[[108]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-123) In the 20th century, the United States emerged as a dominant global power and a key player in the Cold War.[[109]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-124)

In the study of the [history of Oceania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_Oceania "History of Oceania"), historians note the arrival of humans about 60,000 to 50,000 years ago.[[110]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-125) They explore the establishment of [diverse regional societies and cultures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_Oceania "Indigenous peoples of Oceania"), first in Australia and Papua New Guinea and later also on other [Pacific Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pacific_Islands "Pacific Islands").[[111]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-126) The arrival of the Europeans in the 16th century prompted significant transformations, and by the end of the 19th century, most of the region had come under Western control.[[112]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-127) Oceania became involved in various conflicts during the world wars and experienced [decolonization in the post-war period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decolonisation_of_Oceania "Decolonisation of Oceania").[[113]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-128)

### By theme

Historians often limit their inquiry to a specific theme.[[114]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-129) Some propose a general subdivision into three major themes: [political history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_history "Political history"), [economic history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economic_history "Economic history"), and [social history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_history "Social history"). However, the boundaries between these branches are vague and their relation to other thematic branches, such as [intellectual history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intellectual_history "Intellectual history"), is not always clear.[[115]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-130)

Political history studies the organization of [power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_(social_and_political) "Power (social and political)") in society, examining how power structures arise, develop, and interact. Throughout most of recorded history, [states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_(polity) "State (polity)") or state-like structures have been central to this field of study. It explores how a state was [organized internally](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_system "Political system"), like [factions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_faction "Political faction"), [parties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_party "Political party"), leaders, and other political institutions. It also examines which [policies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Policies "Policies") were implemented and how the state interacted with other states.[[116]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-131) Political history has been studied since antiquity by historians such as Herodotus and Thucydides, making it one of the oldest branches of history, while other major subfields have only become established branches in the past century.[[117]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-132)

[![Image 8: Photo of a reconstructed medieval trebuchet on a stone platform against a cloudy sky in the background](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Tr%C3%A9buchet_Castelnaud.jpg/250px-Tr%C3%A9buchet_Castelnaud.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Tr%C3%A9buchet_Castelnaud.jpg)

Military history studies armed conflicts, including advancements in military technology, like [trebuchets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trebuchet "Trebuchet").[[118]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-133)

[Diplomatic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diplomatic_history "Diplomatic history") and [military history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Military_history "Military history") are associated with political history. Diplomatic history examines [international relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_relations "International relations") between states. It covers [foreign policy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foreign_policy "Foreign policy") topics such as negotiations, strategic considerations, [treaties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaties "Treaties"), and conflicts between nations as well as the role of [international organizations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_organizations "International organizations") in these processes.[[119]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-134) Military history studies the impact and development of [armed conflicts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Armed_conflict "Armed conflict") in human history. This includes the examination of specific events, like the analysis of a particular battle and the discussion of the different causes of a war. It also involves more general considerations about the evolution of warfare, including advancements in [military technology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Military_technology "Military technology"), [strategies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Military_strategy "Military strategy"), [tactics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Military_tactics "Military tactics"), [logistics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Military_logistics "Military logistics"), and institutions.[[120]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-135)

Economic history examines how [commodities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commodities "Commodities") are produced, exchanged, and consumed. It covers economic aspects such as the use of land, [labour](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Work_(human_activity) "Work (human activity)"), and [capital](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capital_(economics) "Capital (economics)"), the [supply and demand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supply_and_demand "Supply and demand") of goods, the costs and [means of production](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Means_of_production "Means of production"), and the [distribution of income](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distribution_of_income "Distribution of income") and [wealth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distribution_of_wealth "Distribution of wealth"). Economic historians typically focus on general trends in the form of impersonal forces, such as [inflation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inflation "Inflation"), rather than the actions and decisions of individuals. If enough data is available, they rely on quantitative methods, like statistical analysis. For periods before the modern era, available data is often limited, forcing economic historians to rely on scarce sources and extrapolate information from them.[[121]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-136)

Social history is a broad field investigating social phenomena, but its precise definition is disputed. Some theorists understand it as the study of everyday life outside the domains of politics and economics, including cultural practices, family structures, community interactions, and education. A closely related approach focuses on experience rather than activities, examining how members of particular social groups, like [social classes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_class "Social class"), [races](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Race_(human_categorization) "Race (human categorization)"), [genders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gender "Gender"), or [age groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Age_group "Age group"), experienced their world. Other definitions see social history as the study of social problems, like poverty, disease, and crime, or take a broader perspective by examining how whole [societies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Societies "Societies") developed.[[122]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-137) Closely related fields include [cultural history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cultural_history "Cultural history"), [gender history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gender_history "Gender history"), and [religious history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Religious_history "Religious history").[[123]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-138)

Intellectual history is the history of ideas and studies how concepts, philosophies, and [ideologies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ideologies "Ideologies") have evolved. It is particularly interested in academic fields but not limited to them, including the study of the beliefs and prejudices of ordinary people. In addition to studying intellectual movements themselves, it also examines the cultural and social contexts that shaped them and their influence on other historical developments.[[124]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-139) As closely related fields, the [history of philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_philosophy "History of philosophy") investigates the development of philosophical thought[[125]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-140) while the [history of science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_science "History of science") studies the evolution of scientific theories and practices, such as the scientific contributions of [Charles Darwin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Darwin "Charles Darwin") and [Albert Einstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Einstein "Albert Einstein").[[126]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-141)[Art history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Art_history "Art history"), another connected discipline, examines historical [works of art](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Works_of_art "Works of art") and the development of artistic activities, [styles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artistic_style "Artistic style"), and [movements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artistic_movement "Artistic movement"). It includes a discussion of the cultural, social, and political contexts of art production.[[127]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-142)

[Environmental history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Environmental_history "Environmental history") studies the relation between humans and their environment. It seeks to understand how humans and the rest of nature have affected each other in the course of history.[[128]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-143) Other thematic branches include [constitutional history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitutional_history "Constitutional history"), [legal history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legal_history "Legal history"), [urban history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urban_history "Urban history"), [business history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Business_history "Business history"), [history of technology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_technology "History of technology"), [medical history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medical_history "Medical history"), [history of education](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_education "History of education"), and [people's history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_history "People's history").[[129]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-144)

### Others

Some branches of history are characterized by the [methods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methodology "Methodology") they employ, such as [quantitative history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantitative_history "Quantitative history") and [digital history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digital_history "Digital history"), which rely on [quantitative methods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantitative_research "Quantitative research") and [digital media](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digital_media "Digital media").[[130]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-145)[Comparative history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comparative_history "Comparative history") compares historical phenomena from distinct times, regions, or cultures to examine their similarities and differences.[[131]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-146) Unlike most other branches, [oral history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oral_history "Oral history") relies on oral reports rather than written documents, encompassing eyewitness accounts, [hearsay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hearsay "Hearsay"), and communal [legends](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legend "Legend"). It reflects the personal experiences, interpretations, and [memories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Memory "Memory") of common people, showcasing how people subjectively remember the past.[[132]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-147)[Counterfactual history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Counterfactual_history "Counterfactual history") uses [counterfactual thinking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Counterfactual_thinking "Counterfactual thinking") to examine alternative courses of history, exploring what could have happened under different circumstances.[[133]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-148) Certain branches of history are distinguished by their theoretical outlook, such as [Marxist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marxist_history "Marxist history") and [feminist history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feminist_history "Feminist history").[[134]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-149)

[![Image 9: An old double-hemisphere map of the world](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Map_of_the_World_%281820%29.jpg/250px-Map_of_the_World_%281820%29.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Map_of_the_World_(1820).jpg)

[World history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_history "World history") examines history on a global level, incorporating the whole of [human history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_history "Human history").[[135]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-auto5-150)

Some distinctions focus on the scope of the studied topic. [Big History](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_History "Big History") is the branch with the broadest scope, covering everything from the [Big Bang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang "Big Bang") to the present, incorporating elements of [cosmology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmology "Cosmology"), geology, [biology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology "Biology"), and anthropology.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-auto4-11)[World history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_history_(field) "World history (field)") is another branch with a wide topic. It examines human history as a whole, starting with the evolution of human-like species.[[135]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-auto5-150) The terms _[macrohistory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macrohistory "Macrohistory")_, _mesohistory_, and _[microhistory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microhistory "Microhistory")_ refer to different scales of analysis, ranging from large-scale patterns that affect the whole globe to detailed studies of [local contexts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_history "Local history"), small communities, [family histories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Family_history "Family history"), particular individuals, or specific events.[[136]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-151) Closely related to microhistory is the genre of [historical biography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Historical_biography "Historical biography"), which recounts an individual's life in its historical context and the legacy it left.[[137]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-152)

[Public history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_history "Public history") involves activities that present history to the [general public](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_public "General public"). It usually happens outside the traditional academic settings in contexts like [museums](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Museum "Museum"), [historical sites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Historical_site "Historical site"), [heritage tourism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heritage_tourism "Heritage tourism"), and popular media.[[138]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-153)

Evolution of the discipline
---------------------------

Before the invention of writing, the preservation and transmission of historical knowledge were limited to oral traditions.[[139]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-154) Early forms of historical writing mixed facts with mythological elements, such as the _[Epic of Gilgamesh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epic\_of\_Gilgamesh "Epic of Gilgamesh")_ from [ancient Mesopotamia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Mesopotamia "Ancient Mesopotamia") and the _[Odyssey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Odyssey "Odyssey")_, an [ancient Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greece "Ancient Greece") text attributed to [Homer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homer "Homer").[[140]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-155) Published in the 5th century BCE, the _[Histories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Histories\_(Herodotus) "Histories (Herodotus)")_ by [Herodotus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herodotus "Herodotus")[[p]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-157) was one of the foundational texts of the Western historical tradition, putting more emphasis on rational and evidence-based inquiry than the stories of Homer and other poets.[[142]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-158)[Thucydides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thucydides "Thucydides") followed and further refined Herodotus's approach but focused more on particular political and military developments in contrast to the wide scope and [ethnographic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethnographic "Ethnographic") elements of Herodotus's work.[[143]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-159)[Roman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Rome "Ancient Rome") historiography was heavily influenced by Greek traditions. It often included not only historical facts but also [moral judgments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethics "Ethics") of [historical figures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Historical_figure "Historical figure").[[q]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-161) Early Roman historians used an [annalistic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Annalistic "Annalistic") style, arranging past events by year with little commentary, while later ones preferred a more narrative and analytical approach.[[145]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-162)

[![Image 10: Photo of historical manuscript written in black ink](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/%E3%80%8E%E5%8F%B2%E8%A8%98%E3%80%8F%E5%94%90%E5%86%99%E6%9C%AC.jpg/250px-%E3%80%8E%E5%8F%B2%E8%A8%98%E3%80%8F%E5%94%90%E5%86%99%E6%9C%AC.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:%E3%80%8E%E5%8F%B2%E8%A8%98%E3%80%8F%E5%94%90%E5%86%99%E6%9C%AC.jpg)

[Sima Qian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sima_Qian "Sima Qian")'s _[Shiji](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shiji "Shiji")_ was one of the earliest comprehensive accounts of the [history of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_China "History of China") and the wider world known to the Chinese.[[146]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-163)

Another complex [tradition of historical writing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_historiography "Chinese historiography") emerged in [ancient China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_China "Ancient China"), with early precursors starting in the late 2nd millennium BCE. It considered annals the highest form of historical writing and emphasized verification through sources. This tradition was associated with [Confucian philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confucian_philosophy "Confucian philosophy") and closely tied to the government in the form of the [ruling dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynasties_of_China "Dynasties of China"). Chinese historians established a coherent and systematic method for recording historical events earlier than other traditions.[[147]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-164) Of particular influence was the work of [Sima Qian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sima_Qian "Sima Qian"), whose meticulous research method and inclusion of alternative viewpoints shaped subsequent historiographical standards.[[148]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-165) In [ancient India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_India "Ancient India"), historical narratives were closely associated with religion. They often mixed factual accounts with supernatural elements, as seen in works like the _[Mahabharata](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mahabharata "Mahabharata")_.[[149]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-166)

In Europe during the [medieval period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval_period "Medieval period"), history was primarily documented by the [clergy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clergy "Clergy") in the form of chronicles. [Christian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christianity "Christianity") historians drew from [Greco-Roman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greco-Roman "Greco-Roman") and [Jewish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jewish "Jewish") traditions and reinterpreted the past from a religious perspective as a narrative highlighting God's divine plan.[[150]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-167) Influential contributions shaping this tradition were made by the historians [Eusebius of Caesarea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eusebius_of_Caesarea "Eusebius of Caesarea") and [Bede](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bede "Bede") and by the theologian [Augustine of Hippo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Augustine_of_Hippo "Augustine of Hippo").[[151]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-168) In the Islamic world, historical writing was similarly influenced by religion, interpreting the past from a [Muslim](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muslim "Muslim") perspective. It placed great importance on the [chain of transmission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isnad "Isnad") to preserve the authority of historical accounts.[[152]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-169)[Al-Tabari](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Tabari "Al-Tabari") wrote a comprehensive history, spanning from the creation of the world to his present day. [Ibn Khaldun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ibn_Khaldun "Ibn Khaldun") reflected on philosophical issues underlying the practice of historians, such as universal patterns shaping historical changes and the limits of historical truth.[[153]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-170)

With the emergence of the [Tang Dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tang_Dynasty "Tang Dynasty") (618–907 CE) in China, historical writing became increasingly institutionalized as a bureau for the writing of history was established in 629 CE. The bureau oversaw the establishment of [Veritable Records](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Veritable_Records "Veritable Records"), a comprehensive compilation serving as the basis of the standard national history. Tang dynasty historians emphasized the difference between actual events that occurred in the past and the way these events are documented in historical texts.[[154]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-171) Historical writing in the [Song dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Song_dynasty "Song dynasty") (960–1279 CE) happened in a variety of historical genres, including encyclopedias, biographies, and historical novels, while history became a standard subject in the [Chinese educational system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imperial_examination "Imperial examination").[[155]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-172) Influenced by the Chinese model, a [tradition of historical writing emerged in Japan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Historiography_of_Japan "Historiography of Japan") in the 8th century CE. Like in China, historical writing was closely related to the [imperial household](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imperial_House_of_Japan "Imperial House of Japan"), but Japanese historians placed less importance on critical source evaluation than their Chinese counterparts.[[156]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-173)

During the [Renaissance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Renaissance "Renaissance") and the [early modern period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Early_modern_period "Early modern period") (approximately 1500 to 1800), the different historical traditions came increasingly into contact with each other.[[157]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-174) Starting in 14th-century Europe, the Renaissance led to a shift away from medieval religious outlooks towards a renewed interest in the earlier classical tradition of Greece and Rome. [Renaissance humanists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Renaissance_humanism "Renaissance humanism") used sophisticated text criticism to scrutinize earlier religious historical works, which contributed to the [secularization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Secularization "Secularization") of historical writing. During the 15th to 17th centuries, historians placed greater emphasis on the [didactic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Didactic "Didactic") role of history, using it to promote the established order or argue for a return to an idealised vision of the past. As the invention of the [printing press](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Printing_press "Printing press") made written documents more accessible and affordable, interest in history expanded outside the [clergy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clergy "Clergy") and [nobility](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nobility "Nobility"). At the same time, [empiricist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empiricist "Empiricist") thought associated with the [Scientific Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_Revolution "Scientific Revolution") questioned the possibility of arriving at universal historical truths.[[158]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-175) During the [Age of Enlightenment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment "Age of Enlightenment") in the 18th century, historical writing was influenced by [rationalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rationalism "Rationalism") and [scepticism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scepticism "Scepticism"). Aiming to challenge traditional authority and dogma through reason and empirical methods, historians tried to uncover deeper patterns and meaning in the past, while the scope of historical inquiry expanded with an increased focus on societal and economic topics as well as [comparisons between different cultures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comparative_history "Comparative history").[[159]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-176)

In China during the [Ming dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ming_dynasty "Ming dynasty") (1368–1644), public interest in historical writings and their availability also increased. In addition to the continuation of the Veritable Records by official governmental historians, non-official works by private scholars flourished. These scholars tended to use a more creative style and sometimes challenged orthodox accounts.[[160]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-177) In the Islamic world, new traditions of historical writings emerged in the [Safavid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Safavid_Empire "Safavid Empire"), [Mughal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mughal_Empire "Mughal Empire"), and [Ottoman empires](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ottoman_Empire "Ottoman Empire").[[161]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-178) Meanwhile, in the [Americas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Americas "Americas"), European explorers recorded and interpreted indigenous narratives, which had been passed down through oral and [pictographic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pictographic "Pictographic") practices. These views sometimes contested traditional European perspectives.[[162]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-179)

[![Image 11: Painting of an older man with white hair dressed in a formal black garment with an ornate badge on his chest](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Jebens%2C_Adolf_-_Leopold_von_Ranke_%28detail%29_-_1875.jpg/250px-Jebens%2C_Adolf_-_Leopold_von_Ranke_%28detail%29_-_1875.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Jebens,_Adolf_-_Leopold_von_Ranke_(detail)_-_1875.jpg)

[Leopold von Ranke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leopold_von_Ranke "Leopold von Ranke") revolutionized the standards of historical scholarship by introducing a thorough evaluation of primary sources.[[163]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-180)

Historical writing was transformed in the 19th century as it became more [professional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Professionalization "Professionalization") and science-oriented. Following the work of [Leopold von Ranke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leopold_von_Ranke "Leopold von Ranke"), a systematic method of source criticism was widely accepted while [academic institutions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Academic_institution "Academic institution") dedicated to history were established in the form of university departments, professional associations, and journals.[[164]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-181) In tune with this scientific outlook, [Auguste Comte](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Auguste_Comte "Auguste Comte") formulated the school of [positivism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positivism "Positivism") and aimed to discover general laws of history, similar to the [laws of nature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_law "Scientific law") studied by physicists.[[165]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-182) Building on the philosophy of [Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georg_Wilhelm_Friedrich_Hegel "Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel"), [Karl Marx](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karl_Marx "Karl Marx") proposed one such general law in his theory of [historical materialism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Historical_materialism "Historical materialism"), arguing that economic forces and [class struggle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Class_struggle "Class struggle") are the fundamental drivers of historical change.[[166]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-183) Another influential development was the spread of European historiographical methods, which became the dominant approach to the academic study of the past worldwide.[[167]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-184)

In the 20th century, traditional historical assumptions and practices were challenged while the scope of historical research broadened.[[168]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-185) The [_Annales_ school](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Annales_school "Annales school") used insights from [sociology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sociology "Sociology"), [psychology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Psychology "Psychology"), and [economics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economics "Economics") to study long-term developments.[[169]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-186)[Authoritarian regimes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Authoritarian_regime "Authoritarian regime"), like [Nazi Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nazi_Germany "Nazi Germany"), the [Soviet Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Union "Soviet Union"), and China, manipulated historical narratives for [ideological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ideological "Ideological") purposes.[[170]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-187) Various historians covered unconventional perspectives, focusing on the experiences of [marginalized groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marginalized_groups "Marginalized groups") through approaches such as [history from below](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_from_below "History from below"), [microhistory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microhistory "Microhistory"), [oral history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oral_history "Oral history"), and [feminist history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feminist_history "Feminist history").[[171]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-188)[Postcolonialism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postcolonialism "Postcolonialism") aimed to undermine the hegemony of the Western approach and [postmodernism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism "Postmodernism") rejected the claim to a single universal truth in history.[[172]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-189)[Intellectual historians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intellectual_history "Intellectual history") examined the historical development of ideas.[[173]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-190) In the second half of the century, renewed attempts to write [histories of the world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_history_(field) "World history (field)") as a whole gained momentum, while technological advances fostered the growth of [quantitative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantitative_history "Quantitative history") and [digital history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digital_history "Digital history").[[174]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-191)

### Historiography

[![Image 12: Bust of a man with a turban and a beard](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Bust_of_Ibn_Khaldun_%28Casbah_of_Bejaia%2C_Algeria%29.jpg/250px-Bust_of_Ibn_Khaldun_%28Casbah_of_Bejaia%2C_Algeria%29.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Bust_of_Ibn_Khaldun_(Casbah_of_Bejaia,_Algeria).jpg)

[Ibn Khaldun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ibn_Khaldun "Ibn Khaldun") was an influential figure in Islamic historiography.[[175]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-192)

Historiography is the study of the methods and development of historical research. Historiographers examine what historians do, resulting in a [metatheory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metatheory "Metatheory") in the form of a history of history. Some theorists use the term _historiography_ in a different sense to refer to written accounts of the past.[[176]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-193)

A central topic in historiography as a metatheory focuses on the standards of [evidence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evidence "Evidence") and reasoning in historical inquiry. Historiographers examine and codify how historians use sources to construct narratives about the past, including the analysis of the interpretative assumptions from which they proceed. Closely related issues include the [style](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Writing_style "Writing style") and [rhetorical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhetorical "Rhetorical") presentation of works of history.[[177]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-194)

By comparing the works of different historians, historiographers identify schools of thought based on shared research methods, assumptions, and styles.[[178]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-195) For example, they examine the characteristics of the [_Annales_ school](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Annales_school "Annales school"), like its use of quantitative data from various disciplines and its interest in economic and social developments taking place over extended periods.[[179]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-196) Comparisons also extend to whole eras from ancient to modern times. This way, historiography traces the development of history as an academic discipline, highlighting how the dominant methods, themes, and research goals have changed over time.[[180]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-197)

### Philosophy of history

The philosophy of history[[r]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-199) investigates the theoretical foundations of history. It is interested both in the past itself as a series of interconnected events and in the academic field studying this process. Insights and approaches from various branches of philosophy are relevant to this endeavour, such as [metaphysics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metaphysics "Metaphysics"), [epistemology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epistemology "Epistemology"), [hermeneutics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermeneutics "Hermeneutics"), and [ethics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethics "Ethics").[[182]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-200)

In examining history as a process, philosophers explore the basic entities that make up historical phenomena. Some approaches rely primarily on the beliefs and actions of individual humans, while others include [collective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collective "Collective") and other general entities, such as [civilizations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civilization "Civilization"), [institutions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Institution "Institution"), [ideologies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ideologies "Ideologies"), and social forces.[[183]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-201) A related topic concerns the nature of [causal mechanisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Causality "Causality") connecting historic events with their causes and consequences.[[184]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-202) One view holds that there are general laws of history that determine the course of events, similar to the [laws of nature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_law "Scientific law") studied in the [natural sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_sciences "Natural sciences"). According to another perspective, causal relations between historic events are unique and shaped by contingent factors.[[185]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-203) Historically, some philosophers have suggested that the general direction of the course of history follows large patterns. According to one proposal, history is cyclic, meaning that on a sufficiently large scale, individual events or general trends [repeat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Historic_recurrence "Historic recurrence"). Another such theory asserts that history is a linear, [teleological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teleological "Teleological") process moving towards a predetermined goal.[[186]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-204)[[s]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-206)

The topics of philosophy of history and historiography overlap as both are interested in the standards of [historical reasoning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Historical_thinking "Historical thinking"). Historiographers typically focus more on describing specific methods and developments encountered in the study of history. Philosophers of history, by contrast, tend to explore more general patterns, including evaluative questions about which methods and assumptions are correct.[[188]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-207) Historical reasoning is sometimes used in philosophy and other disciplines as a method to explain phenomena. This approach, known as [historicism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Historicism "Historicism"), argues that understanding something requires knowledge of its unique history or how it evolved. For instance, historicism about [truth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Truth "Truth") states that truth depends on historical circumstances, meaning that there are no transhistorical truths. Historicism contrasts with approaches that seek a timeless and universal understanding of their subject matter.[[189]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-208)

#### Historical objectivity

Diverse debates in the philosophy of history focus on the possibility of an [objective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Objectivity_(science) "Objectivity (science)") account of history. Various theorists argue that this ideal is not achievable, pointing to the [subjective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subjectivity_and_objectivity_(philosophy) "Subjectivity and objectivity (philosophy)") nature of [interpretation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interpretation_(philosophy) "Interpretation (philosophy)"), the narrative aspect of history, and the influence of personal values and biases on the perspective and actions of both historic individuals and historians. According to one view, some particular facts are objective, for example, facts about when a drought occurred or which army was defeated. However, this view does not ensure general objectivity since historians have to interpret and synthesize facts to arrive at an overall narrative describing large trends and developments.[[190]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-209) As a result, some historians, such as [G. M. Trevelyan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G._M._Trevelyan "G. M. Trevelyan") and [Keith Jenkins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Keith_Jenkins "Keith Jenkins"), assert that all history is biased, arguing that historical narratives are never free of subjective presuppositions and value judgments.[[191]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-210)

Some outlooks associated with realism, [empiricism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empiricism "Empiricism"), and reconstructionism,[[192]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-211) conceptualise history as the search for truth or knowledge, which they see as recoverable through rigorous evaluation and careful interpretation of evidence.[[193]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-auto6-212)[[t]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-214) Other scholars critique this view, emphasising the subjective and partial nature of historical knowledge.[[u]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-218)[Perspectivists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perspectivism "Perspectivism") claim that historical perspectives are inherently subjective, as they require selecting particular sources and inquiries, and ascertaining what information can be regarded as historical fact. They argue that statements can only be objective within or relative to one of several competing historical perspectives.[[198]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-219) A stronger [scepticist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skepticism "Skepticism") or [relativist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativism "Relativism") outlook states that no historical knowledge can be proven objective.[[199]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-220)[[v]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-222) This emphasis on subjectivities has been extended by [postmodernist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism "Postmodernism") theories that suggest that it is impossible to know the past objectively, adding that meaning is created through human-made texts, the language of which "constitute our world as we perceive it".[[201]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-223)[[w]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-225) Neo-realists have responded to this trend by reemphasising the centrality of empiricist methodologies to historical analysis. They acknowledge the influence of subjective evaluations but contend that historical truth is reachable nonetheless.[[203]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-DonnellyNorton104-106-226)[[x]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-227)

### Education

[![Image 13: Black-and-white photo of a highschool classroom of girls looking to the front while their teacher stands behind them and explains](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Girls_at_Albany_Senior_School_in_Enfield_during_a_lesson_on_American_History%2C_1943._D13781.jpg/250px-Girls_at_Albany_Senior_School_in_Enfield_during_a_lesson_on_American_History%2C_1943._D13781.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Girls_at_Albany_Senior_School_in_Enfield_during_a_lesson_on_American_History,_1943._D13781.jpg)

History is a standard school subject in most countries.[[204]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-Metzger_2018_2-228)

History is part of the school [curriculum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curriculum "Curriculum") in most countries.[[204]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-Metzger_2018_2-228) Early history education aims to make students interested in the past and familiarize them with fundamental concepts of historical thought. By fostering a basic historical awareness, it seeks to instil a [sense of identity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Personal_identity "Personal identity") by helping them understand their cultural roots.[[205]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-229) It often takes a narrative form by presenting children with simple stories, which may focus on historic individuals or the origins of local holidays, festivals, and food.[[206]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-230) More advanced history education encountered in [secondary school](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Secondary_school "Secondary school") covers a broader spectrum of topics, ranging from ancient to modern history, at both local and global levels. It further aims to acquaint students with historical research methodologies, including the abilities to interpret and critically evaluate historical claims.[[207]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-231)

History teachers employ a variety of [teaching methods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teaching_methods "Teaching methods"). They include narrative presentations of historical developments, questions to engage students and prompt [critical thinking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Critical_thinking "Critical thinking"), and discussions on historical topics. Students work with historical sources directly to learn how to analyse and interpret evidence, both individually and in group activities. They engage in historical writing to develop the skills of articulating their thoughts clearly and persuasively. [Assessment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Educational_assessment "Educational assessment") through oral or written tests aims to ensure that learning goals are reached.[[208]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-232) Traditional methodologies in history education often present numerous facts, like dates of significant events and names of historical figures, which students are expected to memorize. Some modern approaches, by contrast, seek to foster a more active engagement and a deeper interdisciplinary understanding of general patterns, focusing not only on what happened but also on why it happened and its lasting historical significance.[[209]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-233)

History education in [state schools](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_school "State school") serves a variety of purposes. A key skill is historical literacy, the ability to comprehend, critically analyse, and respond to historical claims. By making students aware of significant developments in the past, they can become familiar with various contexts of human life, helping them understand the present and its diverse cultures. At the same time, history education can foster a sense of [cultural identity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cultural_identity "Cultural identity") by connecting students with their heritage, traditions, and practices, for example, by introducing them to iconic elements ranging from national landmarks and monuments to historical figures and traditional festivities.[[210]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-234) Knowledge of a shared past and cultural heritage can contribute to the formation of a [national identity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_identity "National identity") and prepares students for active [citizenship](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Citizenship "Citizenship"). This political aspect of history education may spark disputes about which topics school [textbooks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Textbook "Textbook") should cover. In various regions, it has resulted in so-called _history wars_ over the curriculum.[[211]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-235) It can lead to a biased treatment of controversial topics in an attempt to present their national heritage in a favourable light.[[212]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-236)[[y]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-238)

[![Image 14: Photo of a museum gallery showcasing a collection of historic artefacts](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Tokyo_National_Museum_Toyokan_P1145505.jpg/250px-Tokyo_National_Museum_Toyokan_P1145505.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Tokyo_National_Museum_Toyokan_P1145505.jpg)

[Informal education](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Informal_education "Informal education") provided by exhibitions of historic artefacts in museums is part of [public history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_history "Public history").[[214]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-auto2-239)

In addition to the [formal education](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formal_education "Formal education") provided in public schools, history is also taught in [informal settings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Informal_education "Informal education") outside the classroom. [Public history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_history "Public history") takes place in locations like [museums](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Museum "Museum") and memorial sites, where selected artefacts are often used to tell specific stories.[[214]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-auto2-239) It includes [popular history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Popular_history "Popular history"), which aims to make the past accessible and appealing to a wide audience of non-specialists in media such as books, television programmes, and online content.[[215]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-240) Informal history education also happens in [oral traditions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oral_tradition "Oral tradition") as narratives about the past are transmitted across generations.[[216]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-241)

### Other fields

History employs an interdisciplinary [methodology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methodology "Methodology"), drawing on findings from fields such as [archaeology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archaeology "Archaeology"), [geology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geology "Geology"), [genetics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genetics "Genetics"), [anthropology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anthropology "Anthropology"), and [linguistics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linguistics "Linguistics").[[217]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-242)[[z]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-244) Archaeologists study human-made historical artefacts and other forms of [material culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Material_culture "Material culture"). Their findings provide crucial insights into past human activities and cultural developments.[[219]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-245) The interpretation of archaeological evidence presents challenges that differ from standard historical work with written documents. At the same time, it offers new possibilities by presenting information that was not recorded, allowing historians to access the past of non-literate societies and marginalized groups within literate societies by studying the remains of their material culture. Before the advent of modern archaeology in the 19th century, [antiquarianism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiquarianism "Antiquarianism") laid the groundwork for this discipline and played a vital role in preserving historical artefacts.[[220]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-246)

Geology and other [earth sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth_sciences "Earth sciences") help historians understand the environmental contexts and physical processes that affected past societies, including [climate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate "Climate") conditions, landscapes, and natural events.[[221]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-247) Genetics provides key information about the evolutionary origins of humans as a species, [human migration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_migration "Human migration"), [ancestry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancestry "Ancestry"), and [demographic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Demographic "Demographic") changes.[[222]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-248) Anthropologists investigate [human culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_culture "Human culture") and behaviour, such as [social structures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_structure "Social structure"), [belief systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belief_system "Belief system"), and ritual practices. This knowledge offers contexts for the interpretation of historical events.[[223]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-249)[Historical linguistics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Historical_linguistics "Historical linguistics") studies the development of languages over time, which can be crucial for the interpretation of ancient documents and can also provide information about migration patterns and cultural exchanges.[[224]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-250) Historians further rely on evidence from various other fields belonging to the [physical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_sciences "Physical sciences"), [biological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biological_sciences "Biological sciences"), and [social sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_sciences "Social sciences") as well as the [humanities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Humanities "Humanities").[[225]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-251)

In virtue of its relation to [ideology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ideology "Ideology") and national identity, history is closely connected to [politics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics "Politics") and historical theories can directly impact political decisions. For example, [irredentist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irredentist "Irredentist") attempts by one state to annex territory of another state often rely on historical theories claiming that the disputed territory belonged to the first state in the past.[[226]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-252) History also plays a central role in so-called _historical religions_, which base some of their core doctrines on historical events. For instance, [Christianity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christianity "Christianity") is often categorized as a historical religion because it is centred around historical events surrounding [Jesus Christ](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jesus_Christ "Jesus Christ").[[227]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-253) History is relevant to many fields through the study of their past, including the [history of science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_science "History of science"), [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_mathematics "History of mathematics"), [philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_philosophy "History of philosophy"), and [art](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_art "History of art").[[228]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History#cite_note-254)
