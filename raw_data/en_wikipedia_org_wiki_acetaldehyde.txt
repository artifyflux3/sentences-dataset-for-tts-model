**Acetaldehyde** (IUPAC systematic name **ethanal**) is an [organic chemical compound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_compound "Organic compound") with the [formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_formula "Chemical formula")CH 3 CH=O, sometimes abbreviated as [Me](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methyl "Methyl")CH=O. It is a colorless liquid or gas, boiling near room temperature. It is one of the most important [aldehydes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aldehyde "Aldehyde"), occurring widely in nature and being produced on a large scale in industry. Acetaldehyde occurs naturally in coffee, bread, and ripe fruit,[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-:2-11) and is produced by plants. It is also produced by the partial oxidation of [ethanol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethanol "Ethanol") by the liver enzyme [alcohol dehydrogenase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alcohol_dehydrogenase "Alcohol dehydrogenase") and is a contributing cause of [hangover](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hangover "Hangover") after [alcohol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alcohol_(drug) "Alcohol (drug)") consumption.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-hsf-12) Pathways of exposure include air, water, land, or groundwater, as well as drink and smoke.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-chem-13) Consumption of [disulfiram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disulfiram "Disulfiram") inhibits [acetaldehyde dehydrogenase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde_dehydrogenase "Acetaldehyde dehydrogenase"), the enzyme responsible for the metabolism of acetaldehyde, thereby causing it to build up in the body.

[The International Agency for Research on Cancer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Agency_for_Research_on_Cancer "International Agency for Research on Cancer") (IARC) has listed acetaldehyde as a Group 1 [carcinogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carcinogen "Carcinogen").[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-14) Acetaldehyde is "one of the most frequently found air toxins with cancer risk greater than one in a million".[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-:0-15)

Acetaldehyde was first observed by the Swedish pharmacist/chemist [Carl Wilhelm Scheele](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Wilhelm_Scheele "Carl Wilhelm Scheele") (1774);[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-16) it was then investigated by the French chemists [Antoine François, comte de Fourcroy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antoine_Fran%C3%A7ois,_comte_de_Fourcroy "Antoine François, comte de Fourcroy") and [Louis Nicolas Vauquelin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis_Nicolas_Vauquelin "Louis Nicolas Vauquelin") (1800),[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-17) and the German chemists [Johann Wolfgang Döbereiner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johann_Wolfgang_D%C3%B6bereiner "Johann Wolfgang Döbereiner") (1821, 1822, 1832)[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-18) and [Justus von Liebig](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Justus_von_Liebig "Justus von Liebig") (1835).[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-19)[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-20)

In 1835, Liebig named it "aldehyde",[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-21) and in the middle of the century the name was altered to "acetaldehyde".[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-22)

In 2013, global production was about 438,000 tonnes.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-23) Before 1962, [ethanol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethanol "Ethanol") and [acetylene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene "Acetylene") were the major sources of acetaldehyde. Since then, ethylene is the dominant [feedstock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raw_material "Raw material").[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-Ull-24)

The main method of production is the oxidation of [ethene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethene "Ethene") by the [Wacker process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wacker_process "Wacker process"), which involves oxidation of ethene using a [homogeneous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homogeneous_catalysis "Homogeneous catalysis") palladium/copper catalyst system:

2 CH 2=CH 2 + O 2 → 2 CH 3 CH=O
In the 1970s, the world capacity of the Wacker-Hoechst direct oxidation process exceeded two million tonnes annually.

Smaller quantities can be prepared by the partial [oxidation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxidation "Oxidation") of ethanol in an exothermic reaction. This process typically is conducted over a silver catalyst at about 500–650°C (950–1,200°F).[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-Ull-24)

2 CH 3 CH 2 OH + O 2 → 2 CH 3 CH=O + 2 H 2 O
This method is one of the oldest routes for the industrial preparation of acetaldehyde.

#### Hydration of acetylene

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Acetaldehyde&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Hydration of acetylene")]

Prior to the [Wacker process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wacker_process "Wacker process") and the availability of cheap ethylene, acetaldehyde was produced by the [hydration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydration_reaction "Hydration reaction") of [acetylene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene "Acetylene").[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-Dmitry2007-25) This reaction is catalyzed by [mercury(II)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mercury_(element) "Mercury (element)") salts:

C 2 H 2 + Hg 2+ + H 2 O → CH 3 CH=O + Hg
The mechanism involves the intermediacy of [vinyl alcohol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vinyl_alcohol "Vinyl alcohol"), which [tautomerizes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tautomer "Tautomer") to acetaldehyde. The reaction is conducted at 90–95°C (194–203°F), and the acetaldehyde formed is separated from water and mercury and cooled to 25–30°C (77–86°F). In the [wet oxidation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wet_oxidation "Wet oxidation") process, [iron(III) sulfate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron(III)_sulfate "Iron(III) sulfate") is used to reoxidize the mercury back to the mercury(II) salt. The resulting [iron(II) sulfate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron(II)_sulfate "Iron(II) sulfate") is oxidized in a separate reactor with [nitric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitric_acid "Nitric acid").[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-Ull-24)

The enzyme [Acetylene hydratase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene_hydratase "Acetylene hydratase") discovered in the strictly anaerobic bacterium _[Pelobacter acetylenicus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pelobacter\_acetylenicus "Pelobacter acetylenicus")_ can catalyze an analogous reaction without involving any compounds of mercury.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-26) However, it has thus far not been brought to any large-scale or commercial use.

#### Dehydrogenation of ethanol

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Acetaldehyde&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Dehydrogenation of ethanol")]

Traditionally, acetaldehyde was produced by the partial [dehydrogenation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dehydrogenation "Dehydrogenation") of ethanol:

CH 3 CH 2 OH → CH 3 CH=O + H 2
In this endothermic process, ethanol vapor is passed at 260–290°C over a copper-based catalyst. The process was once attractive because of the value of the hydrogen coproduct,[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-Ull-24) but in modern times is not economically viable.

#### Hydroformylation of methanol

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Acetaldehyde&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Hydroformylation of methanol")]

The [hydroformylation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroformylation "Hydroformylation") of [methanol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol "Methanol") with catalysts like cobalt, nickel, or iron salts also produces acetaldehyde, although this process is of no industrial importance. Similarly noncompetitive, acetaldehyde arises from [synthesis gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synthesis_gas "Synthesis gas") with modest selectivity.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-Ull-24)

### Tautomerization to vinyl alcohol

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Acetaldehyde&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Tautomerization to vinyl alcohol")]

[![Image 22](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Ethanal_Ethenol_Tautomerie.svg/330px-Ethanal_Ethenol_Tautomerie.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Ethanal_Ethenol_Tautomerie.svg)

Tautomeric equilibrium between acetaldehyde and vinyl alcohol.

Like many other [carbonyl compounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonyl_compound "Carbonyl compound"), acetaldehyde [tautomerizes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tautomer "Tautomer") to give an [enol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enol "Enol") ([vinyl alcohol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vinyl_alcohol "Vinyl alcohol"); IUPAC name: ethenol):

CH 3 CH=O ⇌ CH 2=CHOH ∆_H_ 298,g = +42.7 kJ/mol
The [equilibrium constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equilibrium_constant "Equilibrium constant") is 6×10−7 at room temperature, thus that the relative amount of the enol form in a sample of acetaldehyde is very small.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-27) At room temperature, acetaldehyde (CH 3 CH=O) is more stable than [vinyl alcohol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vinyl_alcohol "Vinyl alcohol") (CH 2=CHOH) by 42.7 kJ/mol:[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-28) Overall the keto-enol tautomerization occurs slowly but is catalyzed by acids.

Photo-induced keto-enol tautomerization is viable under [atmospheric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere "Atmosphere") or stratospheric conditions. This photo-tautomerization is relevant to the Earth's atmosphere, because vinyl alcohol is thought to be a precursor to [carboxylic acids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carboxylic_acid "Carboxylic acid") in the atmosphere.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-29)[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-30)

### Addition and condensation reactions

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Acetaldehyde&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Addition and condensation reactions")]

Acetaldehyde is a common electrophile in [organic synthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_synthesis "Organic synthesis").[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-31) In [addition reactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Addition_reaction "Addition reaction") acetaldehyde is [prochiral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prochiral "Prochiral"). It is used primarily as a source of the "CH 3 C+H(OH)" [synthon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synthon "Synthon") in [aldol reactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aldol_reaction "Aldol reaction") and related [condensation reactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensation_reaction "Condensation reaction").[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-Behrens2004-32) Grignard reagents and [organolithium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organolithium_reagent "Organolithium reagent") compounds react with MeCHO to give [hydroxyethyl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroxyethyl "Hydroxyethyl") derivatives.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-Walter1955-33) In one of the more spectacular addition reactions, [formaldehyde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde "Formaldehyde") in the presence of [calcium hydroxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calcium_hydroxide "Calcium hydroxide") adds to MeCHO to give [pentaerythritol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentaerythritol "Pentaerythritol"), C(CH 2 OH)4 and [formate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formate "Formate").[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-Schurink1941-34)

In a [Strecker reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strecker_amino_acid_synthesis "Strecker amino acid synthesis"), acetaldehyde condenses with [cyanide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyanide "Cyanide") and [ammonia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia "Ammonia") to give, after [hydrolysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrolysis "Hydrolysis"), the [amino acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amino_acid "Amino acid")[alanine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alanine "Alanine").[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-Kendall1941-35) Acetaldehyde can condense with [amines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amine "Amine") to yield [imines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imine "Imine"); for example, with [cyclohexylamine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclohexylamine "Cyclohexylamine") to give _N_-[ethylidenecyclohexylamine](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ethylidene&action=edit&redlink=1 "Ethylidene (page does not exist)"). These imines can be used to direct subsequent reactions like an aldol condensation.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-36)

It is also a building block in the synthesis of [heterocyclic compounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heterocyclic_compounds "Heterocyclic compounds"). In one example, it converts, upon treatment with [ammonia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia "Ammonia"), to [5-ethyl-2-methylpyridine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/5-Ethyl-2-methylpyridine "5-Ethyl-2-methylpyridine") ("aldehyde-collidine").[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-Frank1963-37)

[![Image 23](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Paraldehyde_structure.svg/250px-Paraldehyde_structure.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Paraldehyde_structure.svg)

[![Image 24](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Metaldehyde.svg/250px-Metaldehyde.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Metaldehyde.svg)

Three molecules of acetaldehyde condense to form "[paraldehyde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paraldehyde "Paraldehyde")", a cyclic trimer containing C-O single bonds. Similarly condensation of four molecules of acetaldehyde give the cyclic molecule [metaldehyde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metaldehyde "Metaldehyde"). Paraldehyde can be produced in good yields, using a sulfuric acid catalyst. Metaldehyde is only obtained in a few percent yield and with cooling, often using HBr rather than H 2 SO 4 as the catalyst. At −40°C (−40°F) in the presence of acid catalysts, polyacetaldehyde is produced.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-Ull-24) There are two [stereomers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stereomer "Stereomer") of paraldehyde and four of metaldehyde.

The German chemist Valentin Hermann Weidenbusch (1821–1893) synthesized paraldehyde in 1848 by treating acetaldehyde with acid (either sulfuric or nitric acid) and cooling to 0°C (32°F). He found it quite remarkable that when paraldehyde was _heated_ with a trace of the same acid, the reaction went the other way, recreating acetaldehyde.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-38)

Although [vinyl alcohol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vinyl_alcohol "Vinyl alcohol") is a polymeric form of acetaldehyde ([§Tautomerization to vinyl alcohol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#Tautomerization_to_vinyl_alcohol)), [polyvinyl alcohol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyvinyl_alcohol "Polyvinyl alcohol") cannot be produced from acetaldehyde.

[![Image 25](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Acetal_formation_2.png/500px-Acetal_formation_2.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Acetal_formation_2.png)

Conversion of acetaldehyde to [1,1-diethoxyethane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1,1-diethoxyethane "1,1-diethoxyethane"), R 1=CH 3 R 2=CH 3 CH 2

Acetaldehyde forms a stable [acetal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetal "Acetal") upon reaction with [ethanol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethanol "Ethanol") under conditions that favor dehydration. The product, CH 3 CH(OCH 2 CH 3)2, is formally named [1,1-diethoxyethane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1,1-diethoxyethane "1,1-diethoxyethane") but is commonly referred to as "acetal".[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-Adkins1941-39) This can cause confusion as "acetal" is more commonly used to describe compounds with the [functional groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functional_group "Functional group") RCH(OR')2 or RR'C(OR'')2 rather than referring to this specific compound — in fact, 1,1-diethoxyethane is also described as the diethyl acetal of acetaldehyde.

### Precursor to vinylphosphonic acid

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Acetaldehyde&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Precursor to vinylphosphonic acid")]

Acetaldehyde is a precursor to [vinylphosphonic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vinylphosphonic_acid "Vinylphosphonic acid"), which is used to make adhesives and [ion conductive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ion_conductivity "Ion conductivity") membranes. The synthesis sequence begins with a reaction with [phosphorus trichloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphorus_trichloride "Phosphorus trichloride"):[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-Lavinia-40)

1.   PCl 3 + CH 3 CHO → CH 3 CH(O−)PCl+3
2.   CH 3 CH(O−)PCl+3 + 2 CH 3 CO 2 H → CH 3 CH(Cl)PO(OH)2 + 2 CH 3 COCl
3.   CH 3 CH(Cl)PO(OH)2 → CH 2=CHPO(OH)2 + HCl

In the [liver](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liver "Liver"), the [enzyme](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enzyme "Enzyme"), [alcohol dehydrogenase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alcohol_dehydrogenase "Alcohol dehydrogenase") oxidizes [ethanol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethanol "Ethanol") into acetaldehyde, which is then further oxidized into harmless [acetic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid "Acetic acid") by [acetaldehyde dehydrogenase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde_dehydrogenase "Acetaldehyde dehydrogenase"). These two oxidation reactions are coupled with the reduction of NAD+ to [NADH](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NADH "NADH").[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-Hipolito,_L._2007-41) In the brain, the enzyme [catalase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catalase "Catalase") is primarily responsible for oxidizing ethanol to acetaldehyde, and [alcohol dehydrogenase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alcohol_dehydrogenase "Alcohol dehydrogenase") plays a minor role.[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-Hipolito,_L._2007-41) The last steps of alcoholic [fermentation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermentation_(food) "Fermentation (food)") in bacteria, plants, and [yeast](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yeast "Yeast") involve the conversion of [pyruvate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyruvate "Pyruvate") into acetaldehyde and [carbon dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_dioxide "Carbon dioxide") by the enzyme [pyruvate decarboxylase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyruvate_decarboxylase "Pyruvate decarboxylase"), followed by the conversion of acetaldehyde into ethanol. The latter reaction is again catalyzed by an alcohol dehydrogenase, now operating in the opposite direction.

Many [East Asian people](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_Asian_people "East Asian people") have an [ALDH2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ALDH2 "ALDH2") mutation which makes them significantly less efficient at oxidizing acetaldehyde. On consuming alcohol, their bodies tend to accumulate excessive amounts of acetaldehyde, causing the so-called [alcohol flush reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alcohol_flush_reaction "Alcohol flush reaction").[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-Kim2014-42) They develop a characteristic flush on the face and body, along with "nausea, headache and general physical discomfort".[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-43) Ingestion of the drug [disulfiram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disulfiram "Disulfiram"), which inhibits ALDH2, leads to a similar reaction (see: [§Aggravating factors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#Aggravating_factors) below).[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-disul-new-44)

Traditionally, acetaldehyde was mainly used as a precursor to acetic acid. This application has declined because acetic acid is produced more efficiently from methanol by the [Monsanto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monsanto_process "Monsanto process") and [Cativa processes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cativa_process "Cativa process"). Acetaldehyde is an important precursor to [pyridine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyridine "Pyridine") derivatives, [pentaerythritol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentaerythritol "Pentaerythritol"), and [crotonaldehyde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crotonaldehyde "Crotonaldehyde"). Urea and acetaldehyde combine to give a useful [resin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resin "Resin"). [Acetic anhydride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_anhydride "Acetic anhydride") reacts with acetaldehyde to give [ethylidene diacetate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylidene_diacetate "Ethylidene diacetate"), a precursor to [vinyl acetate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vinyl_acetate "Vinyl acetate"), which is used to produce [polyvinyl acetate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyvinyl_acetate "Polyvinyl acetate").[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-Ull-24)

The global market for acetaldehyde is declining. Demand has been impacted by changes in the production of plasticizer alcohols, which has shifted because [_n_-butyraldehyde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Butyraldehyde "Butyraldehyde") is less often produced from acetaldehyde, instead being generated by hydroformylation of [propylene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propylene "Propylene"). Likewise, [acetic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid "Acetic acid"), once produced from acetaldehyde, is made predominantly by the lower-cost methanol carbonylation process.[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-45) The impact on demand has led to increase in prices and thus slowdown in the market.

[China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China "China") is the largest consumer of acetaldehyde in the world, accounting for almost half of global consumption in 2012. Major use has been the production of acetic acid. Other uses such as [pyridines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyridine "Pyridine") and pentaerythritol are expected to grow faster than acetic acid, but the volumes are not large enough to offset the decline in acetic acid. As a consequence, overall acetaldehyde consumption in [China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China "China") may grow slightly at 1.6% per year through 2018. Western Europe is the second-largest consumer of acetaldehyde worldwide, accounting for 20% of world consumption in 2012. As with China, the Western European acetaldehyde market is expected to increase only very slightly at 1% per year during 2012–2018. However, [Japan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japan "Japan") could emerge as a potential consumer for acetaldehyde in the next five years due to newfound use in commercial production of [butadiene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Butadiene "Butadiene"). The supply of butadiene has been volatile in Japan and the rest of Asia. This should provide the much needed boost to the flat market, as of 2013.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-46)

The [threshold limit value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Threshold_limit_value "Threshold limit value") is 25ppm (STEL/ceiling value) and the MAK (Maximum Workplace Concentration) is 50 ppm. At 50 ppm acetaldehyde, no irritation or local tissue damage in the [nasal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nasal_cavity "Nasal cavity")[mucosa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mucous_membrane "Mucous membrane") is observed. When taken up by the organism, acetaldehyde is metabolized rapidly in the liver to acetic acid. Only a small proportion is exhaled unchanged. After [intravenous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intravenous_therapy "Intravenous therapy") injection, the half-life in the blood is approximately 90 seconds.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-Ull-24)

Many serious cases of acute intoxication have been recorded.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-Ull-24) Acetaldehyde naturally breaks down in the human body.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-chem-13)[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-47)

Acetaldehyde is an irritant of the skin, eyes, mucous membranes, throat, and respiratory tract. This occurs at concentrations as low as 1000 ppm. Symptoms of exposure to this compound include [nausea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nausea "Nausea"), [vomiting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vomiting "Vomiting"), and [headache](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Headache "Headache"). These symptoms may not happen immediately. The [perception](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perception "Perception") threshold for acetaldehyde in air is in the range between 0.07 and 0.25 ppm.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-Ull-24) At such concentrations, the [fruity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fruit "Fruit")[odor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Odor "Odor") of acetaldehyde is apparent. [Conjunctival](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conjunctiva "Conjunctiva") irritations have been observed after a 15-minute exposure to concentrations of 25 and 50 ppm, but transient conjunctivitis and irritation of the [respiratory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Respiratory_system "Respiratory system") tract have been reported after exposure to 200 ppm acetaldehyde for 15 minutes.

Acetaldehyde is [carcinogenic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carcinogenic "Carcinogenic") in humans.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-Chemical_Summary_For_Acetaldehyde-48)[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-49) In 1988 the [International Agency for Research on Cancer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Agency_for_Research_on_Cancer "International Agency for Research on Cancer") stated, "There is _sufficient_ evidence for the carcinogenicity of acetaldehyde (the major metabolite of ethanol) in [experimental animals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Experimental_animal "Experimental animal")."[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-International1988-50) In October 2009 the [International Agency for Research on Cancer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Agency_for_Research_on_Cancer "International Agency for Research on Cancer") updated the classification of acetaldehyde stating that acetaldehyde included in and generated [endogenously](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endogenous "Endogenous") from [alcoholic beverages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alcoholic_beverage "Alcoholic beverage") is a Group I human carcinogen.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-51) In addition, acetaldehyde is damaging to [DNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNA "DNA")[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-52) and causes abnormal muscle development as it binds to proteins.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-Nicholas2004-53)

Acetaldehyde induces DNA interstrand crosslinks, a form of DNA damage. These can be repaired by either of two replication-coupled DNA repair pathways.[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-Hodskinson2020-54) The first is referred to as the FA pathway, because it employs gene products defective in [Fanconi's anemia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fanconi%27s_anemia "Fanconi's anemia") patients. This repair pathway results in increased mutation frequency and altered mutational spectrum.[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-Hodskinson2020-54) The second repair pathway requires replication fork convergence, breakage of the acetaldehyde crosslink, translesion synthesis by a Y-family DNA polymerase and homologous recombination.[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-Hodskinson2020-54)

### Aggravating factors

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Acetaldehyde&action=edit&section=22 "Edit section: Aggravating factors")]

#### Alzheimer's disease

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Acetaldehyde&action=edit&section=23 "Edit section: Alzheimer's disease")]

People with a genetic deficiency for the enzyme responsible for the conversion of acetaldehyde into [acetic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid "Acetic acid") may have a greater risk of [Alzheimer's disease](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alzheimer%27s_disease "Alzheimer's disease"). "These results indicate that the [ALDH2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ALDH2 "ALDH2") deficiency is a risk factor for LOAD [late-onset Alzheimer's disease] ..."[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-ohta-55)

A study of 818 heavy drinkers found that those exposed to more acetaldehyde than normal through a genetic variant of the gene encoding for [ADH1C](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ADH1C "ADH1C"), ADH1C*1, are at greater risk of developing cancers of the [upper gastrointestinal tract](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Upper_gastrointestinal_tract "Upper gastrointestinal tract") and liver.[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-56)

The drug [disulfiram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disulfiram "Disulfiram") (Antabuse) inhibits acetaldehyde dehydrogenase, an enzyme that oxidizes the compound into acetic acid. Metabolism of ethanol forms acetaldehyde before acetaldehyde dehydrogenase forms acetic acid, but with the enzyme inhibited, acetaldehyde accumulates. If one consumes ethanol while taking disulfiram, the hangover effect of ethanol is felt more rapidly and intensely ([disulfiram-alcohol reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disulfiram-alcohol_reaction "Disulfiram-alcohol reaction")). As such, disulfiram is sometimes used as a deterrent for alcoholics wishing to stay sober.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-disul-new-44)

### Sources of exposure

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Acetaldehyde&action=edit&section=26 "Edit section: Sources of exposure")]

Acetaldehyde is a potential contaminant in workplace, indoors, and ambient environments. Moreover, the majority of humans spend more than 90% of their time in indoor environments, increasing any exposure and the risk to human health.[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-57)

In a study in [France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/France "France"), the mean indoor concentration of acetaldehydes measured in 16 homes was approximately seven times higher than the outside acetaldehyde concentration. The [living room](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Living_room "Living room") had a mean of 18.1±17.5 μg m−3 and the [bedroom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bedroom "Bedroom") was 18.2±16.9 μg m−3, whereas the outdoor air had a mean concentration of 2.3±2.6 μg m−3.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

It has been concluded that [volatile organic compounds (VOC)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volatile_organic_compound "Volatile organic compound") such as benzene, formaldehyde, acetaldehyde, toluene, and [xylenes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xylene "Xylene") have to be considered priority [pollutants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pollutant "Pollutant") with respect to their health effects. It has been pointed that in renovated or completely new buildings, the VOCs concentration levels are often several orders of magnitude higher. The main sources of acetaldehydes in homes include building materials, laminate, PVC flooring, varnished wood flooring, and varnished cork/pine flooring (found in the varnish, not the wood). It is also found in plastics, oil-based and water-based paints, in composite wood ceilings, particle-board, plywood, treated pine wood, and laminated chipboard furniture.[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-dafni-58)

The use of acetaldehyde is widespread in different industries, and it may be released into waste water or the air during production, use, transportation and storage. Sources of acetaldehyde include fuel combustion emissions from stationary internal combustion engines and power plants that burn fossil fuels, wood, or trash, oil and gas extraction, refineries, cement kilns, lumber and wood mills and paper mills.[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-59) Acetaldehyde is also present in automobile and [diesel exhaust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diesel_exhaust "Diesel exhaust").[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-60) As a result, acetaldehyde is "one of the most frequently found air toxics with cancer risk greater than one in a million".[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-:0-15)

Natural tobacco [polysaccharides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polysaccharides "Polysaccharides"), including [cellulose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cellulose "Cellulose"), have been shown to be the primary precursors making acetaldehyde a significant constituent of [tobacco smoke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tobacco_smoke "Tobacco smoke").[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-61)[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-TalhoutSchulz2011-62) It has been demonstrated to have a [synergistic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synergy "Synergy") effect with [nicotine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nicotine "Nicotine") in [rodent studies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Animal_testing_on_rodents "Animal testing on rodents") of [addiction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Addiction "Addiction").[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-63)[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-64) Acetaldehyde is also the most abundant carcinogen in tobacco smoke; it is dissolved into the [saliva](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saliva "Saliva") while smoking.

Acetaldehyde has been found in [cannabis smoke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cannabis_smoking "Cannabis smoking"). This finding emerged through the use of new chemical techniques that demonstrated the acetaldehyde present was causing DNA damage in laboratory settings.[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-65)

#### Alcohol consumption

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Acetaldehyde&action=edit&section=31 "Edit section: Alcohol consumption")]

Many [microbes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microbes "Microbes") produce acetaldehyde from ethanol, but they have a lower capacity to eliminate the acetaldehyde, which can lead to the accumulation of acetaldehyde in saliva, stomach acid, and intestinal contents. [Fermented](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermentation "Fermentation") food and many alcoholic beverages can also contain significant amounts of acetaldehyde. Acetaldehyde, derived from mucosal or microbial oxidation of ethanol, tobacco smoke, and diet, appears to act as a cumulative carcinogen in the upper digestive tract of humans.[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-SALASPURO_M_2011-66) According to European Commission's Scientific Committee on Consumer Safety's (SCCS) "Opinion on Acetaldehyde" (2012) the [cosmetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmetics "Cosmetics") products special risk limit is 5 mg/L and acetaldehyde should not be used in [mouth-washing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mouthwash "Mouthwash") products.[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-67)

Acetaldehyde can be produced by the [photo-oxidation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photo-oxidation_of_polymers "Photo-oxidation of polymers") of [polyethylene terephthalate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyethylene_terephthalate "Polyethylene terephthalate") (PET), via a [Type II Norrish reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norrish_reaction "Norrish reaction").[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-Wiles&DayIII-68)

[![Image 27](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Poly%28ethylene_terephthalate%29_-_Type_II_Norrish_to_acetaldehyde.png/960px-Poly%28ethylene_terephthalate%29_-_Type_II_Norrish_to_acetaldehyde.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Poly(ethylene_terephthalate)_-_Type_II_Norrish_to_acetaldehyde.png)

Although the levels produced by this process are minute acetaldehyde has an exceedingly low taste/[odor threshold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Odor_threshold "Odor threshold") of around 20–40 ppb and can cause an off-taste in bottled water.[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-69) The level at which an average consumer could detect acetaldehyde is still considerably lower than any toxicity.[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-70)

The yeast _[Candida albicans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Candida\_albicans "Candida albicans")_ in patients with potentially carcinogenic oral diseases has been shown to produce acetaldehyde in quantities sufficient to cause problems.[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde#cite_note-71)
