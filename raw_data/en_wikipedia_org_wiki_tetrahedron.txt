Markdown Content:
In [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry"), a **tetrahedron** (pl.: **tetrahedra** or **tetrahedrons**), also known as a **triangular pyramid**, is a [polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyhedron "Polyhedron") composed of four triangular [faces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Face_(geometry) "Face (geometry)"), six straight [edges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edge_(geometry) "Edge (geometry)"), and four [vertices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_(geometry) "Vertex (geometry)"). The tetrahedron is the simplest of all the ordinary [convex polyhedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_polytope "Convex polytope").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron#cite_note-MW-1)

The tetrahedron is the [three-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional "Three-dimensional") case of the more general concept of a [Euclidean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_geometry "Euclidean geometry")[simplex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simplex "Simplex"), and may thus also be called a **3-simplex**.

The tetrahedron is one kind of [pyramid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyramid_(geometry) "Pyramid (geometry)"), which is a polyhedron with a flat [polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon "Polygon") base and triangular faces connecting the base to a common point. In the case of a tetrahedron, the base is a [triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle "Triangle") (any of the four faces can be considered the base), so a tetrahedron is also known as a "triangular pyramid".

Like all [convex polyhedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_polyhedra "Convex polyhedra"), a tetrahedron can be folded from a single sheet of paper. It has two such [nets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Net_(polyhedron) "Net (polyhedron)").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron#cite_note-MW-1)

For any tetrahedron there exists a sphere (called the [circumsphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumsphere "Circumsphere")) on which all four vertices lie, and another sphere (the [insphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Insphere "Insphere")) [tangent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent "Tangent") to the tetrahedron's faces.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron#cite_note-2)

Regular tetrahedron
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tetrahedron&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Regular tetrahedron")]

[![Image 1](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Tetrahedron.svg/250px-Tetrahedron.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Tetrahedron.svg)

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Tetrahedron_flat.svg/179px-Tetrahedron_flat.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Tetrahedron_flat.svg)

A regular tetrahedron and its net

A [regular tetrahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_tetrahedron "Regular tetrahedron") is a tetrahedron in which all four faces are [equilateral triangles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equilateral_triangle "Equilateral triangle"). In other words, all of its faces are the same size and shape (congruent) and all edges are the same length. The regular tetrahedron is the simplest [deltahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deltahedron "Deltahedron"), a polyhedron all of whose faces are equilateral triangles; there are seven other convex deltahedra.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTECundy1952-3)

Irregular tetrahedra
--------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tetrahedron&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Irregular tetrahedra")]

If all three pairs of opposite edges of a tetrahedron are [perpendicular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular "Perpendicular"), then it is called an _[orthocentric tetrahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthocentric\_tetrahedron "Orthocentric tetrahedron")_. When only one pair of opposite edges are perpendicular, it is called a _**semi-orthocentric tetrahedron**_. In a _[trirectangular tetrahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trirectangular\_tetrahedron "Trirectangular tetrahedron")_ the three face angles at _one_ vertex are [right angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_angle "Right angle"), as at the corner of a cube.

An **isodynamic tetrahedron** is one in which the [cevians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cevian "Cevian") that join the vertices to the [incenters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Incircle_and_excircles_of_a_triangle "Incircle and excircles of a triangle") of the opposite faces are [concurrent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concurrent_lines "Concurrent lines").

An **isogonic tetrahedron** has concurrent cevians that join the vertices to the points of contact of the opposite faces with the [inscribed sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inscribed_sphere "Inscribed sphere") of the tetrahedron.

[![Image 3](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Oblate_tetrahedrille_cell.png/250px-Oblate_tetrahedrille_cell.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Oblate_tetrahedrille_cell.png)

A space-filling tetrahedral disphenoid inside a cube. Two edges have [dihedral angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihedral_angle "Dihedral angle") of 90°, and four edges have dihedral angles of 60°.

A [disphenoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disphenoid "Disphenoid") is a tetrahedron with four congruent triangles as faces; the triangles necessarily have all angles acute. The regular tetrahedron is a special case of a disphenoid. Other names for the same shape include bisphenoid, isosceles tetrahedron and equifacial tetrahedron.

[![Image 4](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Triangulated_cube.svg/250px-Triangulated_cube.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Triangulated_cube.svg)

A cube dissected into six characteristic orthoschemes.

A **3-orthoscheme** is a tetrahedron where all four faces are [right triangles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#By_internal_angles "Triangle"). A 3-orthoscheme is not a disphenoid, because its opposite edges are not of equal length. It is not possible to construct a [disphenoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disphenoid#Special_cases_and_generalizations "Disphenoid") with right triangle or obtuse triangle faces.

An [orthoscheme](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schl%C3%A4fli_orthoscheme "Schläfli orthoscheme") is an irregular [simplex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simplex "Simplex") that is the [convex hull](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_hull "Convex hull") of a [tree](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tree_(graph_theory) "Tree (graph theory)") in which all edges are mutually perpendicular. In a 3-dimensional orthoscheme, the tree consists of three perpendicular edges connecting all four vertices in a linear path that makes two right-angled turns. The 3-orthoscheme is a tetrahedron having two right angles at each of two vertices, so another name for it is _**birectangular tetrahedron**_. It is also called a _**quadrirectangular**_ tetrahedron because it contains four right angles.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron#cite_note-4)

Coxeter also calls quadrirectangular tetrahedra "characteristic tetrahedra", because of their integral relationship to the regular polytopes and their symmetry groups.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTECoxeter197371%E2%80%9372%C2%A74.7_Characteristic_tetrahedra-5) For example, the special case of a 3-orthoscheme with equal-length perpendicular edges is [characteristic of the cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyhedron#Ancient "Polyhedron"), which means that the cube can be subdivided into instances of this orthoscheme. If its three perpendicular edges are of unit length, its remaining edges are two of length √2 and one of length √3, so all its edges are edges or diagonals of the cube. The cube ![Image 5](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/bd/CDel_node_1.png)![Image 6](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/8c/CDel_4.png)![Image 7](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/5e/CDel_node.png)![Image 8](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c3/CDel_3.png)![Image 9](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/5e/CDel_node.png) can be dissected into six such 3-orthoschemes ![Image 10](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/5e/CDel_node.png)![Image 11](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/8c/CDel_4.png)![Image 12](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/5e/CDel_node.png)![Image 13](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c3/CDel_3.png)![Image 14](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/5e/CDel_node.png) four different ways, with all six surrounding the same √3 cube diagonal. The cube can also be dissected into 48 _smaller_ instances of this same characteristic 3-orthoscheme (just one way, by all of its symmetry planes at once). The characteristic tetrahedron of the cube is an example of a [Heronian tetrahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heronian_tetrahedron "Heronian tetrahedron").

Every regular polytope, including the regular tetrahedron, has its [characteristic orthoscheme](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthoscheme#Characteristic_simplex_of_the_general_regular_polytope "Orthoscheme"). There is a 3-orthoscheme, which is the "characteristic tetrahedron of the regular tetrahedron". ### Space-filling tetrahedra

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tetrahedron&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Space-filling tetrahedra")]

A **space-filling tetrahedron** packs with directly congruent or enantiomorphous ([mirror image](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mirror_image "Mirror image")) copies of itself to tile space.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTECoxeter197333%E2%80%9334%C2%A73.1_Congruent_transformations-8) The cube can be dissected into six 3-orthoschemes, three left-handed and three right-handed (one of each at each cube face), and cubes can fill space, so the characteristic 3-orthoscheme of the cube is a space-filling tetrahedron in this sense. (The characteristic orthoscheme of the cube is one of the [Hill tetrahedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hill_tetrahedra "Hill tetrahedra"), a family of space-filling tetrahedra. All space-filling tetrahedra are [scissors-congruent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scissors-congruent "Scissors-congruent") to a cube.)

A disphenoid can be a space-filling tetrahedron in the directly congruent sense, as in the [disphenoid tetrahedral honeycomb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disphenoid_tetrahedral_honeycomb "Disphenoid tetrahedral honeycomb"). Regular tetrahedra, however, cannot fill space by themselves (moreover, it is not scissors-congruent to any other polyhedra which can fill the space, see [Hilbert's third problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert%27s_third_problem "Hilbert's third problem")). The [tetrahedral-octahedral honeycomb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedral-octahedral_honeycomb "Tetrahedral-octahedral honeycomb") fills space with alternating regular tetrahedron cells and regular [octahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octahedron "Octahedron") cells in a ratio of 2:1.

### Fundamental domains

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tetrahedron&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Fundamental domains")]

[![Image 63](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Coxeter-Dynkin_3-space_groups.png/250px-Coxeter-Dynkin_3-space_groups.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Coxeter-Dynkin_3-space_groups.png)

For Euclidean 3-space, there are 3 simple and related Goursat tetrahedra. They can be seen as points on and within a cube.

An irregular tetrahedron which is the [fundamental domain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_domain "Fundamental domain")[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTECoxeter197363%C2%A74.3_Rotation_groups_in_two_dimensions;_notion_of_a_''fundamental_region''-9) of a [symmetry group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coxeter_group "Coxeter group") is an example of a [Goursat tetrahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goursat_tetrahedron "Goursat tetrahedron"). The Goursat tetrahedra generate all the regular polyhedra (and many other uniform polyhedra) by mirror reflections, a process referred to as [Wythoff's kaleidoscopic construction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wythoff_construction "Wythoff construction").

For polyhedra, Wythoff's construction arranges three mirrors at angles to each other, as in a [kaleidoscope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaleidoscope "Kaleidoscope"). Unlike a cylindrical kaleidoscope, Wythoff's mirrors are located at three faces of a Goursat tetrahedron such that all three mirrors intersect at a single point. (The [Coxeter-Dynkin diagram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coxeter-Dynkin_diagram "Coxeter-Dynkin diagram") of the generated polyhedron contains three _nodes_ representing the three mirrors. The dihedral angle between each pair of mirrors is encoded in the diagram, as well as the location of a single _generating point_ which is multiplied by mirror reflections into the vertices of the polyhedron.)

Among the Goursat tetrahedra which generate 3-dimensional [honeycombs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Honeycomb_(geometry) "Honeycomb (geometry)") we can recognize an orthoscheme (the characteristic tetrahedron of the cube), a double orthoscheme (the characteristic tetrahedron of the cube face-bonded to its mirror image), and the space-filling disphenoid illustrated [above](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron#Disphenoid).[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTECoxeter197371%E2%80%9372%C2%A74.7_Characteristic_tetrahedra-5) The disphenoid is the double orthoscheme face-bonded to its mirror image (a quadruple orthoscheme). Thus all three of these Goursat tetrahedra, and all the polyhedra they generate by reflections, can be [dissected into characteristic tetrahedra of the cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dissection_into_orthoschemes "Dissection into orthoschemes").

Subdivision and similarity classes
----------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tetrahedron&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Subdivision and similarity classes")]

Tetrahedra subdivision is a process used in computational geometry and 3D modeling to divide a tetrahedron into several smaller tetrahedra. This process enhances the complexity and detail of tetrahedral meshes, which is particularly beneficial in numerical simulations, finite element analysis, and computer graphics. One of the commonly used subdivision methods is the **Longest Edge Bisection (LEB)**, which identifies the longest edge of the tetrahedron and bisects it at its midpoint, generating two new, smaller tetrahedra. When this process is repeated multiple times, bisecting all the tetrahedra generated in each previous iteration, the process is called iterative LEB.

A **similarity class** is the set of tetrahedra with the same geometric shape, regardless of their specific position, orientation, and scale. So, any two tetrahedra belonging to the same similarity class may be transformed to each other by an affine transformation. The outcome of having a limited number of similarity classes in iterative subdivision methods is significant for computational modeling and simulation. It reduces the variability in the shapes and sizes of generated tetrahedra, preventing the formation of highly irregular elements that could compromise simulation results.

The iterative LEB of the regular tetrahedron has been shown to produce only 8 similarity classes. Furthermore, in the case of nearly equilateral tetrahedra where their two longest edges are not connected to each other, and the ratio between their longest and their shortest edge is less than or equal to ![Image 64: {\displaystyle {\sqrt {3/2}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9cf43edf8c5bb223b47f1e791713abfe88063cad), the iterated LEB produces no more than 37 similarity classes.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron#cite_note-10)

In general, a tetrahedron is a [three-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_space "Three-dimensional space") object with four faces, six edges, and four vertices. It can be considered as [pyramid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyramid_(geometry) "Pyramid (geometry)") whenever one of its faces can be considered as the [base](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Base_(geometry) "Base (geometry)"). Its [1-skeleton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_of_a_polytope "Graph of a polytope") can be generally seen as a [graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_(discrete_mathematics) "Graph (discrete mathematics)") by [Steinitz's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steinitz%27s_theorem "Steinitz's theorem"), known as **tetrahedral graph**, one of the [Platonic graphs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platonic_graph "Platonic graph"). It is [complete graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complete_graph "Complete graph")![Image 65: {\displaystyle K_{4}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fe633da926900748cc19ee1ffec1853834a6c061) because every pair of its vertices has a unique edge. In a plane, this graph can be regarded as a triangle in which three vertices connect to its fourth vertex in the center, known as the [universal vertex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universal_vertex "Universal vertex"); hence, the tetrahedral graph is a [wheel graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wheel_graph "Wheel graph").[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPisanskiServatius2013[httpsbooksgooglecombooksid3vnEcMCx0HkCpgPA21_21]-11)

The tetrahedron is one of the polyhedra that does not have [space diagonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_diagonal "Space diagonal"); the other polyhedra with such a property are [Császár polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cs%C3%A1sz%C3%A1r_polyhedron "Császár polyhedron") and [Schonhardt polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schonhardt_polyhedron "Schonhardt polyhedron").[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBagemihl1948-12) It is also known as [3-simplex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simplex "Simplex"), the generalization of a triangle in multi-dimension. It is [self-dual](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-dual "Self-dual"), meaning its [dual polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_polyhedron "Dual polyhedron") is a tetrahedron itself.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWohlleben2019[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidrEpjDwAAQBAJpgPA485_485]-13) Many other properties of tetrahedra are explicitly described in the following sections.

### Analogous to a triangle

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tetrahedron&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Analogous to a triangle")]

The tetrahedron has many properties analogous to those of a triangle, including an insphere, circumsphere, medial tetrahedron, and exspheres. It has respective centers such as incenter, circumcenter, excenters, [Spieker center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spieker_circle "Spieker circle") and points such as a centroid. However, there is generally no orthocenter in the sense of intersecting altitudes.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron#cite_note-19)

[Gaspard Monge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaspard_Monge "Gaspard Monge") found a center that exists in every tetrahedron, now known as the **Monge point**: the point where the six midplanes of a tetrahedron intersect. A midplane is defined as a plane that is orthogonal to an edge joining any two vertices that also contains the centroid of an opposite edge formed by joining the other two vertices.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrabbs2003-20) An orthogonal line dropped from the Monge point to any face meets that face at the midpoint of the line segment between that face's orthocenter and the foot of the altitude dropped from the opposite vertex. If the tetrahedron's altitudes do intersect, then the Monge point and the orthocenter coincide to give the class of [orthocentric tetrahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthocentric_tetrahedron "Orthocentric tetrahedron").

A line segment joining a vertex of a tetrahedron with the [centroid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centroid "Centroid") of the opposite face is called a _median_ and a line segment joining the midpoints of two opposite edges is called a _bimedian_ of the tetrahedron. Hence, a tetrahedron has four medians and three bimedians. These seven line segments are all [concurrent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concurrent_lines "Concurrent lines") at a point called the _centroid_ of the tetrahedron.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTELeungSuen199453%E2%80%9354-21) In addition, [the four medians are divided in a 3:1 ratio by the centroid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commandino%27s_theorem "Commandino's theorem").[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlsinaNelsen2015[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidFEl2CgAAQBAJpgPA97_97]-22) The centroid of a tetrahedron is the midpoint between its Monge point and circumcenter. These points define the _Euler line_ of the tetrahedron that is analogous to the [Euler line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler_line "Euler line") of a triangle.

The [nine-point circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nine-point_circle "Nine-point circle") of the general triangle has an analogue in the circumsphere of a tetrahedron's medial tetrahedron. It is the **twelve-point sphere** and besides the centroids of the four faces of the reference tetrahedron, it passes through four substitute _Euler points_, one third of the way from the Monge point toward each of the four vertices. Finally, it passes through the four base points of orthogonal lines dropped from each Euler point to the face not containing the vertex that generated the Euler point.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron#cite_note-23)

The center ![Image 131: {\displaystyle T}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ec7200acd984a1d3a3d7dc455e262fbe54f7f6e0) of the twelve-point sphere also lies on the Euler line. Unlike its triangular counterpart, this center lies one-third of the way from the Monge point ![Image 132: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) towards the circumcenter. Additionally, an orthogonal line through ![Image 133: {\displaystyle T}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ec7200acd984a1d3a3d7dc455e262fbe54f7f6e0) to a chosen face is coplanar with two other orthogonal lines to the same face. The first is an orthogonal line passing through the corresponding Euler point to the chosen face. The second is an orthogonal line passing through the centroid of the chosen face. This orthogonal line through the twelve-point center lies midway between the Euler point orthogonal line and the centroidal orthogonal line. Furthermore, for any face, the twelve-point center lies at the midpoint of the corresponding Euler point and the orthocenter for that face.

The radius of the twelve-point sphere is one-third of the circumradius of the reference tetrahedron.

There is a relation among the angles made by the faces of a general tetrahedron given by[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron#cite_note-24)

The [geometric median](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_median "Geometric median") of the vertex position coordinates of a tetrahedron and its isogonic center are associated, under circumstances analogous to those observed for a triangle. [Lorenz Lindelöf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorenz_Lindel%C3%B6f "Lorenz Lindelöf") found that, corresponding to any given tetrahedron is a point now known as an isogonic center, ![Image 138: {\displaystyle O}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9d70e1d0d87e2ef1092ea1ffe2923d9933ff18fc), at which the solid angles subtended by the faces are equal, having a common value of ![Image 139: {\displaystyle \pi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9be4ba0bb8df3af72e90a0535fabcc17431e540a)[sr](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steradian "Steradian"), and at which the angles subtended by opposite edges are equal.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron#cite_note-25) A solid angle of ![Image 140: {\displaystyle \pi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9be4ba0bb8df3af72e90a0535fabcc17431e540a) sr is one quarter of that subtended by all of space. When all the solid angles at the vertices of a tetrahedron are smaller than ![Image 141: {\displaystyle \pi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9be4ba0bb8df3af72e90a0535fabcc17431e540a) sr, ![Image 142: {\displaystyle O}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9d70e1d0d87e2ef1092ea1ffe2923d9933ff18fc) lies inside the tetrahedron. Because the sum of distances from ![Image 143: {\displaystyle O}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9d70e1d0d87e2ef1092ea1ffe2923d9933ff18fc) to the vertices is a minimum, ![Image 144: {\displaystyle O}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9d70e1d0d87e2ef1092ea1ffe2923d9933ff18fc) coincides with the [geometric median](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_median "Geometric median")![Image 145: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) of the vertices. If the solid angle at one of the vertices ![Image 146: {\displaystyle v}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e07b00e7fc0847fbd16391c778d65bc25c452597) measures exactly ![Image 147: {\displaystyle \pi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9be4ba0bb8df3af72e90a0535fabcc17431e540a) sr, then ![Image 148: {\displaystyle O}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9d70e1d0d87e2ef1092ea1ffe2923d9933ff18fc) and ![Image 149: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) coincide with ![Image 150: {\displaystyle v}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e07b00e7fc0847fbd16391c778d65bc25c452597). However, if a tetrahedron has a vertex ![Image 151: {\displaystyle v}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e07b00e7fc0847fbd16391c778d65bc25c452597) with solid angle greater than ![Image 152: {\displaystyle \pi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9be4ba0bb8df3af72e90a0535fabcc17431e540a) sr, ![Image 153: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) still corresponds to ![Image 154: {\displaystyle v}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e07b00e7fc0847fbd16391c778d65bc25c452597), but ![Image 155: {\displaystyle O}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9d70e1d0d87e2ef1092ea1ffe2923d9933ff18fc) lies outside the tetrahedron.

### Trigonometry and the space of all shapes

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tetrahedron&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Trigonometry and the space of all shapes")]

[![Image 156](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Tetra.png/250px-Tetra.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Tetra.png)

Illustration of the law of sines for tetrahedra

A corollary of the usual [law of sines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law_of_sines "Law of sines") is that in a tetrahedron with vertices ![Image 157: {\displaystyle O}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9d70e1d0d87e2ef1092ea1ffe2923d9933ff18fc), ![Image 158: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3), ![Image 159: {\displaystyle B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47136aad860d145f75f3eed3022df827cee94d7a), ![Image 160: {\displaystyle C}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4fc55753007cd3c18576f7933f6f089196732029), is given by ![Image 161: {\displaystyle \sin \angle OAB\cdot \sin \angle OBC\cdot \sin \angle OCA=\sin \angle OAC\cdot \sin \angle OCB\cdot \sin \angle OBA.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bb7dfd9cc3fe5bbaeec0855adbd608fd20f81da9) One may view the two sides of this identity as corresponding to clockwise and counterclockwise orientations of the surface. Putting any of the four vertices in the role of ![Image 162: {\displaystyle O}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9d70e1d0d87e2ef1092ea1ffe2923d9933ff18fc) yields four such identities, but at most three of them are independent. If the "clockwise" sides of three of them are multiplied and the product is inferred to be equal to the product of the "counterclockwise" sides of the same three identities. Hence, common factors are cancelled from both sides, and the result is the fourth identity.

Three angles are the angles of some triangle if and only if their sum is 180° (π radians). What condition on 12 angles is necessary and sufficient for them to be the 12 angles of some tetrahedron? The sum of the angles of any side of the tetrahedron must be 180°. Since there are four such triangles, there are four such constraints on sums of angles, and the number of [degrees of freedom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degrees_of_freedom_(statistics) "Degrees of freedom (statistics)") is thereby reduced from 12 to 8. The four relations given by this sine law further reduce the number of degrees of freedom, from 8 down to not 4 but 5, since the fourth constraint is not independent of the first three. Thus, the space of all shapes of tetrahedra is 5-dimensional.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron#cite_note-26)

Denote the circumradius of a tetrahedron as _R_. Let _a_, _b_, _c_ be the lengths of the three edges that meet at a vertex, and _A_, _B_, _C_ the length of the opposite edges. Let _V_ be the volume of the tetrahedron. Then[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron#cite_note-31)[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron#cite_note-32)

The circumcenter of a tetrahedron can be found as intersection of three bisector planes. A bisector plane is defined as the plane centered on, and orthogonal to an edge of the tetrahedron. With this definition, the circumcenter _C_ of a tetrahedron with vertices _x_ 0,_x_ 1,_x_ 2,_x_ 3 can be formulated as matrix-vector product:[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron#cite_note-33)

In contrast to the centroid, the circumcenter may not always lay on the inside of a tetrahedron. Analogously to an obtuse triangle, the circumcenter is outside of the object for an obtuse tetrahedron.

There exist tetrahedra having integer-valued edge lengths, face areas and volume. These are called [Heronian tetrahedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heronian_tetrahedron "Heronian tetrahedron"). One example has one edge of 896, the opposite edge of 990 and the other four edges of 1073; two faces are [isosceles triangles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isosceles_triangle "Isosceles triangle") with areas of 436 800 and the other two are isosceles with areas of 47 120, while the volume is 124 185 600.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron#cite_note-34)

A tetrahedron can have integer volume and consecutive integers as edges, an example being the one with edges 6, 7, 8, 9, 10, and 11 and volume 48.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron#cite_note-Sierpinski-35)

Several applications of tetrahedra in related fields with engineering are the following:

*   In [numerical analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Numerical_analysis "Numerical analysis"), complicated three-dimensional shapes are commonly broken down into, or [approximated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Approximate "Approximate") by, a [polygonal mesh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon_mesh "Polygon mesh") of irregular [tetrahedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedra "Tetrahedra") in the process of setting up the equations for [finite element analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_element_analysis "Finite element analysis") especially in the [numerical solution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Numerical_solution "Numerical solution") of [partial differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_differential_equations "Partial differential equations").[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTERuas2016[httpbooksgooglecombooksidIp4tCwAAQBAJpgPA245_245%E2%80%93249]6.2.2_Tetrahedron-based_Methods-38) These methods have wide applications in practical applications in [computational fluid dynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computational_fluid_dynamics "Computational fluid dynamics"),[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchneider_et_al.2022-39)[aerodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aerodynamics "Aerodynamics"), [electromagnetic fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_field "Electromagnetic field"), [civil engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civil_engineering "Civil engineering"), [chemical engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_engineering "Chemical engineering"), [naval architecture and engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naval_architecture "Naval architecture"), and related fields.
*   The tetrahedron is [inherently rigid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rigidity_(mathematics) "Rigidity (mathematics)").[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWester1997[httpbooksgooglecombooksidFkM0945nFV8CpgPA306_306]-40) For this reason, it is often used to stiffen frame structures such as [spaceframes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spaceframe "Spaceframe").
*   The tetrahedron is used in [caltrops](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caltrop "Caltrop") to provide an [area denial weapon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area_denial_weapon "Area denial weapon"). This is due to their nature of having a sharp corner that always points upwards.[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBachrach2023[httpbooksgooglecombooksidVdmzEAAAQBAJpgPA42_42]-41) Large steel tetrahedra have been used as [anti-tank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anti-tank "Anti-tank") measures,[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTEZaloga2013[httpbooksgooglecombooksidMWP3AQAAQBAJpgPT208_208]-42) or concrete tetrahedra as [tetrapods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrapod_(structure) "Tetrapod (structure)") to break down waves at coastlines.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTEParkDodaranHanShahmirzadi2014-43)
*   At some [airfields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Airfield "Airfield"), a large frame in the shape of a tetrahedron with two sides covered with a thin material is mounted on a rotating pivot and always points into the wind. It is built big enough to be seen from the air and is sometimes illuminated. Its purpose is to serve as a reference to pilots, indicating wind direction.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron#cite_note-44)

The tetrahedron can be described as [tetrahedral molecular geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedral_molecular_geometry "Tetrahedral molecular geometry") in [stereochemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stereochemistry "Stereochemistry").[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPetrucciHarwoodHerring2002[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidEZEoAAAAYAAJpgPA414_413%E2%80%93414]-45) This chemical structure can be found in [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water "Water")H

2 O and [methane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane "Methane") (CH

4).[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBachrach2023[httpbooksgooglecombooksidVdmzEAAAQBAJpgPA43_43]-46) The tetrahedron shape is seen in nature in [covalently bonded](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covalent_bond "Covalent bond") molecules. All [sp 3-hybridized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_hybridisation "Orbital hybridisation") atoms are surrounded by atoms (or [lone electron pairs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lone_pair "Lone pair")) at the four corners of a tetrahedron. For this reason, one of the leading journals in organic chemistry is called _[Tetrahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron\_(journal) "Tetrahedron (journal)")_. For a perfect tetrahedron, the [central angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_angle "Central angle") between any two vertices is arccos(−⁠1/3⁠), or approximately 109.47°.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrittin1945-47)

Quaternary [phase diagrams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phase_diagram "Phase diagram") of mixtures of chemical substances are represented graphically as tetrahedra. However, quaternary phase diagrams in [communication engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communication_engineering "Communication engineering") are represented graphically on a two-dimensional plane.

The vertices of a regular tetrahedron applies as the minimum solution known for ![Image 211: {\displaystyle n=4}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d928ec15aeef83aade867992ee473933adb6139d) in both [Thomson problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomson_problem "Thomson problem") (concerning the minimum-energy configuration of ![Image 212: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) charged particles on a sphere) and for the [Tammes problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tammes_problem "Tammes problem") (of constructing a [spherical code](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_code "Spherical code") maximizing the smallest distance among the points).[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWhyte1952-48)

If six equal [resistors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resistor "Resistor") are [soldered](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solder "Solder") together to form a tetrahedron, then the resistance measured between any two vertices is half that of one resistor.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron#cite_note-49) Since [silicon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon "Silicon") is the most common [semiconductor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semiconductor "Semiconductor") used in [solid-state electronics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid-state_electronics "Solid-state electronics"), and silicon has a [valence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Valence_(chemistry) "Valence (chemistry)") of four, the tetrahedral shape of the four chemical bonds in silicon is a strong influence on how [crystals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystal "Crystal") of silicon form and what shapes they assume.

Tetrahedra are used in color space conversion algorithms specifically for cases in which the luminance axis diagonally segments the color space (e.g. RGB, CMY).[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron#cite_note-50)

The [tetrahedral hypothesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedral_hypothesis "Tetrahedral hypothesis"), originally published by [William Lowthian Green](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Lowthian_Green "William Lowthian Green"), explains the formation of the Earth with the shape of a tetrahedron.[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron#cite_note-51) It was popular through the early 20th century.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron#cite_note-52)[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron#cite_note-53)

Regular tetrahedra cannot [tessellate space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Honeycomb_(geometry) "Honeycomb (geometry)") by themselves, although this result seems likely enough that [Aristotle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotle "Aristotle") claimed it was possible. However, two regular tetrahedra can be combined with an octahedron, giving a [rhombohedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhombohedron "Rhombohedron") that can tile space as the [tetrahedral-octahedral honeycomb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedral-octahedral_honeycomb "Tetrahedral-octahedral honeycomb"). On the other hand, several irregular tetrahedra are known, of which copies can tile space, for instance, the [characteristic orthoscheme of the cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron#Orthoschemes) and the [disphenoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron#Disphenoid) of the [disphenoid tetrahedral honeycomb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disphenoid_tetrahedral_honeycomb "Disphenoid tetrahedral honeycomb"). The complete list remains an open problem.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTESenechal1981-54)

If one relaxes the requirement that the tetrahedra be all the same shape, one can tile space using only tetrahedra in many different ways. For example, one can divide an octahedron into four identical tetrahedra and combine them again with two regular ones. (As a side-note: these two kinds of tetrahedron have the same volume.)

The tetrahedron is unique among the [uniform polyhedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uniform_polyhedron "Uniform polyhedron") in possessing no parallel faces.
