The red edges of this [tetragonal disphenoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetragonal_disphenoid "Tetragonal disphenoid") represent a regular zig-zag skew quadrilateral.

In [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry"), a **skew polygon** is a closed [polygonal chain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygonal_chain "Polygonal chain") in [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space"). It is a [figure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Figure_(geometry) "Figure (geometry)") similar to a [polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon "Polygon") except its [vertices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_(geometry) "Vertex (geometry)") are not all [coplanar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coplanarity "Coplanarity").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skew_polygon#cite_note-FOOTNOTECoxeter1973%C2%A71.1_Regular_polygons-1) While a polygon is ordinarily defined as a [plane figure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_figure "Plane figure"), the [edges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edge_(geometry) "Edge (geometry)") and vertices of a skew polygon form a [space curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_curve "Space curve"). Skew polygons must have at least four vertices. The _interior_[surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_(geometry) "Surface (geometry)") and corresponding area measure of such a polygon is not uniquely defined.

[Skew infinite polygons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skew_infinite_polygon "Skew infinite polygon") (apeirogons) have vertices which are not all colinear.

A **zig-zag skew polygon** or **[antiprismatic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiprism "Antiprism") polygon**[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skew_polygon#cite_note-rcp-2) has vertices which alternate on two parallel planes, and thus must be even-sided.

**Regular skew polygons** in 3 dimensions (and regular skew apeirogons in two dimensions) are always zig-zag.

Skew polygons in three dimensions
---------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Skew_polygon&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Skew polygons in three dimensions")]

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Dodecagonal_antiprism.png/250px-Dodecagonal_antiprism.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Dodecagonal_antiprism.png)

A uniform _n_-gonal [antiprism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiprism "Antiprism") has a 2 _n_-sided regular skew polygon defined along its side edges.

A **regular skew polygon** is a faithful symmetric realization of a polygon in dimension greater than 2. In 3 dimensions a regular skew polygon has vertices alternating between two parallel planes.

A regular skew n-gon can be given a [Schläfli symbol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schl%C3%A4fli_symbol "Schläfli symbol"){p}#{} as a _blend_ of a [regular polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polygon "Regular polygon")p and an orthogonal [line segment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_segment "Line segment") { }.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skew_polygon#cite_note-3) The symmetry operation between sequential vertices is [glide reflection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glide_reflection "Glide reflection").

Examples are shown on the uniform square and pentagon antiprisms. The [star antiprisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_antiprism "Star antiprism") also generate regular skew polygons with different connection order of the top and bottom polygons. The filled top and bottom polygons are drawn for structural clarity, and are not part of the skew polygons.

[Petrie polygons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petrie_polygon "Petrie polygon") are regular skew polygons defined within regular polyhedra and polytopes. For example, the five [Platonic solids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platonic_solid "Platonic solid") have 4-, 6-, and 10-sided regular skew polygons, as seen in these [orthogonal projections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_projection "Orthogonal projection") with red edges around their respective [projective envelopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_envelope "Projective envelope"). The tetrahedron and the octahedron include all the vertices in their respective zig-zag skew polygons, and can be seen as a digonal antiprism and a triangular antiprism respectively.

Regular skew polygon as vertex figure of regular skew polyhedron
----------------------------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Skew_polygon&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Regular skew polygon as vertex figure of regular skew polyhedron")]

A [regular skew polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_skew_polyhedron "Regular skew polyhedron") has regular polygon faces, and a regular skew polygon [vertex figure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_figures "Vertex figures").

Three infinite regular skew polyhedra are [space-filling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_skew_apeirohedron "Regular skew apeirohedron") in 3-space; others [exist in 4-space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_skew_polyhedron#Finite_regular_skew_polyhedra_of_4-space "Regular skew polyhedron"), some within the [uniform 4-polytopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uniform_4-polytope "Uniform 4-polytope").

Regular skew polygons in four dimensions
----------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Skew_polygon&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Regular skew polygons in four dimensions")]

In 4 dimensions, a regular skew polygon can have vertices on a [Clifford torus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_torus "Clifford torus") and related by a [Clifford displacement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_displacement "Clifford displacement"). Unlike zig-zag skew polygons, skew polygons on double rotations can include an odd-number of sides.

The [Petrie polygons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petrie_polygon "Petrie polygon") of the [regular 4-polytopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_4-polytope "Regular 4-polytope") define regular zig-zag skew polygons. The [Coxeter number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coxeter_number "Coxeter number") for each [coxeter group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coxeter_group "Coxeter group") symmetry expresses how many sides a Petrie polygon has. This is 5 sides for a [5-cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/5-cell "5-cell"), 8 sides for a [tesseract](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tesseract "Tesseract") and [16-cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/16-cell "16-cell"), 12 sides for a [24-cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/24-cell "24-cell"), and 30 sides for a [120-cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/120-cell "120-cell") and [600-cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/600-cell "600-cell").

When orthogonally projected onto the [Coxeter plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coxeter_plane "Coxeter plane"), these regular skew polygons appear as regular polygon envelopes in the plane.
