A cylindrical coordinate system with origin O, polar axis A, and longitudinal axis L. The dot is the point with radial distance _ρ_ = 4, angular coordinate _φ_ = 130°, and height _z_ = 4. The reference plane contains the purple section.

A **cylindrical coordinate system** is a [three-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional "Three-dimensional")[coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_system "Coordinate system") that specifies point positions around a main axis (a chosen [directed line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Directed_line "Directed line")) and an auxiliary axis (a reference [ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ray_(geometry) "Ray (geometry)")). The three cylindrical [coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinates "Coordinates") are: the point [perpendicular distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular_distance "Perpendicular distance")_ρ_ from the main axis; the point [signed distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Signed_distance "Signed distance")_z_ along the main axis from a chosen [origin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Origin_(mathematics) "Origin (mathematics)"); and the [plane angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_angle "Plane angle")_φ_ of the [point projection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_projection_on_a_plane "Vector projection on a plane") on a reference plane (passing through the origin and perpendicular to the main axis)

The main axis is variously called the _cylindrical_ or _longitudinal_ axis. The auxiliary axis is called the _polar axis_, which lies in the reference plane, starting at the origin, and pointing in the reference direction. Other directions perpendicular to the longitudinal axis are called _radial lines_.

The distance from the axis may be called the _radial distance_ or _radius_, while the angular coordinate is sometimes referred to as the _angular position_ or as the _azimuth_. The radius and the azimuth are together called the _polar coordinates_, as they correspond to a two-dimensional [polar coordinate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_coordinates "Polar coordinates") system in the plane through the point, parallel to the reference plane. The third coordinate may be called the _height_ or _altitude_ (if the reference plane is considered horizontal), _longitudinal position_,[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylindrical_coordinates#cite_note-1) or _axial position_.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylindrical_coordinates#cite_note-2)

Cylindrical coordinates are useful in connection with objects and phenomena that have some rotational [symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry "Symmetry") about the longitudinal axis, such as water flow in a straight pipe with round cross-section, heat distribution in a metal [cylinder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylinder_(geometry) "Cylinder (geometry)"), [electromagnetic fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_fields "Electromagnetic fields") produced by an [electric current](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_current "Electric current") in a long, straight wire, [accretion disks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accretion_disk "Accretion disk") in astronomy, and so on.

They are sometimes called _cylindrical polar coordinates_[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylindrical_coordinates#cite_note-3) or _polar cylindrical coordinates_,[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylindrical_coordinates#cite_note-4) and are sometimes used to specify the position of stars in a galaxy (_galactocentric cylindrical polar coordinates_).[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylindrical_coordinates#cite_note-5)

The three coordinates ([ρ](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rho_(letter) "Rho (letter)"), [φ](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phi "Phi"), z) of a point P are defined as:

*   The _radial distance_ ρ is the [Euclidean distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_distance "Euclidean distance") from the z-axis to the point P.
*   The _azimuth_ φ is the angle between the reference direction on the chosen plane and the line from the origin to the projection of P on the plane.
*   The _axial coordinate_ or _height_ z is the signed distance from the chosen plane to the point P.

### Unique cylindrical coordinates

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cylindrical_coordinate_system&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Unique cylindrical coordinates")]

As in polar coordinates, the same point with cylindrical coordinates (_ρ_, _φ_, _z_) has infinitely many equivalent coordinates, namely (_ρ_, _φ_ ± _n_×360°, _z_) and (−_ρ_, _φ_ ± (2 _n_ + 1)×180°, _z_), where n is any integer. Moreover, if the radius ρ is zero, the azimuth is arbitrary.

In situations where someone wants a unique set of coordinates for each point, one may restrict the radius to be [non-negative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-negative "Non-negative") (_ρ_ ≥ 0) and the azimuth φ to lie in a specific [interval](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interval_(mathematics) "Interval (mathematics)") spanning 360°, such as [−180°,+180°] or [0,360°].

The notation for cylindrical coordinates is not uniform. The [ISO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization "International Organization for Standardization") standard [31-11](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO_31-11 "ISO 31-11") recommends (_ρ_, _φ_, _z_), where ρ is the radial coordinate, φ the azimuth, and z the height. However, the radius is also often denoted r or s, the azimuth by θ or t, and the third coordinate by h or (if the cylindrical axis is considered horizontal) x, or any context-specific letter.

In concrete situations, and in many mathematical illustrations, a positive angular coordinate is measured [counterclockwise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clockwise "Clockwise") as seen from any point with positive height.

Coordinate system conversions
-----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cylindrical_coordinate_system&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Coordinate system conversions")]

The cylindrical coordinate system is one of many three-dimensional coordinate systems. The following formulae may be used to convert between them.

### Cartesian coordinates

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cylindrical_coordinate_system&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Cartesian coordinates")]

For the conversion between cylindrical and Cartesian coordinates, it is convenient to assume that the reference plane of the former is the Cartesian xy-plane (with equation _z_ = 0), and the cylindrical axis is the Cartesian z-axis. Then the z-coordinate is the same in both systems, and the correspondence between cylindrical (_ρ_, _φ_, _z_) and Cartesian (_x_, _y_, _z_) are the same as for polar coordinates, namely ![Image 4: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}x&=\rho \cos \varphi \\y&=\rho \sin \varphi \\z&=z\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a945cdbc60f54b90dd2fb4a081681778c9cc6a42) in one direction, and ![Image 5: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\rho &={\sqrt {x^{2}+y^{2}}}\\\varphi &={\begin{cases}{\text{indeterminate}}&{\text{if }}x=0{\text{ and }}y=0\\\arcsin \left({\frac {y}{\rho }}\right)&{\text{if }}x\geq 0\\-\arcsin \left({\frac {y}{\rho }}\right)+\pi &{\mbox{if }}x<0{\text{ and }}y\geq 0\\-\arcsin \left({\frac {y}{\rho }}\right)-\pi &{\mbox{if }}x<0{\text{ and }}y<0\end{cases}}\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bcdf211de4c15f5ac6225a95793c5d83247fb75d) in the other. The [arcsine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arcsine "Arcsine") function is the inverse of the [sine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sine "Sine") function, and is assumed to return an angle in the range [−⁠π/2⁠, +⁠π/2⁠] = [−90°, +90°]. These formulas yield an azimuth φ in the range [−180°, +180°].

By using the [arctangent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arctangent "Arctangent") function that returns also an angle in the range [−⁠π/2⁠, +⁠π/2⁠] = [−90°, +90°], one may also compute ![Image 6: {\displaystyle \varphi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/33ee699558d09cf9d653f6351f9fda0b2f4aaa3e) without computing ![Image 7: {\displaystyle \rho }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1f7d439671d1289b6a816e6af7a304be40608d64) first ![Image 8: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\varphi &={\begin{cases}{\text{indeterminate}}&{\text{if }}x=0{\text{ and }}y=0\\{\frac {\pi }{2}}{\frac {y}{|y|}}&{\text{if }}x=0{\text{ and }}y\neq 0\\\arctan \left({\frac {y}{x}}\right)&{\mbox{if }}x>0\\\arctan \left({\frac {y}{x}}\right)+\pi &{\mbox{if }}x<0{\text{ and }}y\geq 0\\\arctan \left({\frac {y}{x}}\right)-\pi &{\mbox{if }}x<0{\text{ and }}y<0\end{cases}}\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/58f12f85430607558bd888c81aa5f3094d12f27a) For other formulas, see the article [Polar coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_coordinate_system "Polar coordinate system").

Many modern programming languages provide a function that will compute the correct azimuth φ, in the range (−π, π), given _x_ and _y_, without the need to perform a case analysis as above. For example, this function is called by [atan2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atan2 "Atan2")(_y_, _x_) in the [C](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C_(programming_language) "C (programming language)") programming language, and (atan _y_ _x_) in [Common Lisp](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Common_Lisp "Common Lisp").

Line and volume elements
------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cylindrical_coordinate_system&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Line and volume elements")]

In many problems involving cylindrical polar coordinates, it is useful to know the line and volume elements; these are used in integration to solve problems involving paths and volumes.

The [line element](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_element "Line element") is ![Image 9: {\displaystyle \mathrm {d} {\boldsymbol {r}}=\mathrm {d} \rho \,{\boldsymbol {\hat {\rho }}}+\rho \,\mathrm {d} \varphi \,{\boldsymbol {\hat {\varphi }}}+\mathrm {d} z\,{\boldsymbol {\hat {z}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ccf946d80259dc676db8a8f3fa08a9c64daa9ecb)

The [volume element](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume_element "Volume element") is ![Image 10: {\displaystyle \mathrm {d} V=\rho \,\mathrm {d} \rho \,\mathrm {d} \varphi \,\mathrm {d} z.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e307466397fe3deda1676d16c77d0a95b02fcbdf)

The [surface element](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_(infinitesimal) "Differential (infinitesimal)") in a surface of constant radius ρ (a vertical cylinder) is ![Image 11: {\displaystyle \mathrm {d} S_{\rho }=\rho \,\mathrm {d} \varphi \,\mathrm {d} z.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f468b4b4b5ab0001499b550fd6323155a36ace8a)

The surface element in a surface of constant azimuth φ (a vertical half-plane) is ![Image 12: {\displaystyle \mathrm {d} S_{\varphi }=\mathrm {d} \rho \,\mathrm {d} z.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/66090f43e102d0630f4691b7a683a2475ae44709)

The surface element in a surface of constant height z (a horizontal plane) is ![Image 13: {\displaystyle \mathrm {d} S_{z}=\rho \,\mathrm {d} \rho \,\mathrm {d} \varphi .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6b28320b9bd3f573fa80276a482ee009b0405813)

The [del](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Del "Del") operator in this system leads to the following expressions for [gradient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gradient "Gradient"), [divergence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divergence "Divergence"), [curl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curl_(mathematics) "Curl (mathematics)") and [Laplacian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laplacian "Laplacian"): ![Image 14: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\nabla f&={\frac {\partial f}{\partial \rho }}{\boldsymbol {\hat {\rho }}}+{\frac {1}{\rho }}{\frac {\partial f}{\partial \varphi }}{\boldsymbol {\hat {\varphi }}}+{\frac {\partial f}{\partial z}}{\boldsymbol {\hat {z}}}\\[8px]\nabla \cdot {\boldsymbol {A}}&={\frac {1}{\rho }}{\frac {\partial }{\partial \rho }}\left(\rho A_{\rho }\right)+{\frac {1}{\rho }}{\frac {\partial A_{\varphi }}{\partial \varphi }}+{\frac {\partial A_{z}}{\partial z}}\\[8px]\nabla \times {\boldsymbol {A}}&=\left({\frac {1}{\rho }}{\frac {\partial A_{z}}{\partial \varphi }}-{\frac {\partial A_{\varphi }}{\partial z}}\right){\boldsymbol {\hat {\rho }}}+\left({\frac {\partial A_{\rho }}{\partial z}}-{\frac {\partial A_{z}}{\partial \rho }}\right){\boldsymbol {\hat {\varphi }}}+{\frac {1}{\rho }}\left({\frac {\partial }{\partial \rho }}\left(\rho A_{\varphi }\right)-{\frac {\partial A_{\rho }}{\partial \varphi }}\right){\boldsymbol {\hat {z}}}\\[8px]\nabla ^{2}f&={\frac {1}{\rho }}{\frac {\partial }{\partial \rho }}\left(\rho {\frac {\partial f}{\partial \rho }}\right)+{\frac {1}{\rho ^{2}}}{\frac {\partial ^{2}f}{\partial \varphi ^{2}}}+{\frac {\partial ^{2}f}{\partial z^{2}}}\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f83defb6183acc79213d062ed3734d4c6f691bbf)

---------------------
