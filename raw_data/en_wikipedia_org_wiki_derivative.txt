The derivative at different points of a differentiable function. In this case, the derivative is equal to ![Image 3: {\displaystyle \sin \left(x^{2}\right)+2x^{2}\cos \left(x^{2}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e1bc29b1b9b8b9fb68b455948b053d2ee14e8f01).

In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), the **derivative** is a fundamental tool that quantifies the sensitivity to change of a [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)")'s output with respect to its input. The derivative of a function of a single variable at a chosen input value, when it exists, is the [slope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slope "Slope") of the [tangent line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent "Tangent") to the [graph of the function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_of_a_function "Graph of a function") at that point. The tangent line is the best [linear approximation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_approximation "Linear approximation") of the function near that input value. For this reason, the derivative is often described as the **instantaneous rate of change**, the ratio of the instantaneous change in the dependent variable to that of the independent variable.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative#cite_note-FOOTNOTEApostol1967160Stewart2002129%E2%80%93130Strang_et_al.2023[httpsopenstaxorgbookscalculus-volume-1pages3-1-defining-the-derivative_224]-1) The process of finding a derivative is called **differentiation**.

There are multiple different [notations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_notation "Mathematical notation") for differentiation. _[Leibniz notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leibniz\_notation "Leibniz notation")_, named after [Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gottfried_Wilhelm_Leibniz "Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz"), is represented as the ratio of two [differentials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_(mathematics) "Differential (mathematics)"), whereas _prime notation_ is written by adding a [prime mark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_mark "Prime mark"). Higher order notations represent repeated differentiation, and they are usually denoted in Leibniz notation by adding superscripts to the differentials, and in prime notation by adding additional prime marks. The [higher order derivatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Higher_order_derivative "Higher order derivative") can be applied in physics; for example, while the first derivative of the position of a moving object with respect to [time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time "Time") is the object's [velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity "Velocity"), how the position changes as time advances, the second derivative is the object's [acceleration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acceleration "Acceleration"), how the velocity changes as time advances.

Derivatives can be generalized to [functions of several real variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_of_several_real_variables "Function of several real variables"). In this case, the derivative is reinterpreted as a [linear transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_transformation "Linear transformation") whose graph is (after an appropriate translation) the best linear approximation to the graph of the original function. The [Jacobian matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacobian_matrix "Jacobian matrix") is the [matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_(mathematics) "Matrix (mathematics)") that represents this linear transformation with respect to the basis given by the choice of independent and dependent variables. It can be calculated in terms of the [partial derivatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_derivative "Partial derivative") with respect to the independent variables. For a [real-valued function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real-valued_function "Real-valued function") of several variables, the Jacobian matrix reduces to the [gradient vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gradient_vector "Gradient vector").

The ratio in the definition of the derivative is the slope of the line through two points on the graph of the function ⁠![Image 54: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61)⁠, specifically the points ![Image 55: {\displaystyle (a,f(a))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2250e602b41f737373c715600ba8f2d2cad1759d) and ![Image 56: {\displaystyle (a+h,f(a+h))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cd6ff3f4080ee8a9c8f7179581a91d464bc82a3f). In other words, the derivative is the slope of the tangent.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThompson199834,_104Stewart2002128-7)

### Using infinitesimals

One way to think of the derivative ![Image 60: {\textstyle {\frac {df}{dx}}(a)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6566b20cc877cc08c9d859a2c8b40eb97104f028) is as the ratio of an [infinitesimal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinitesimal "Infinitesimal") change in the output of the function ![Image 61: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) to an infinitesimal change in its input.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThompson199884%E2%80%9385-8) In order to make this intuition rigorous, a system of rules for manipulating infinitesimal quantities is required.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKeisler2012902%E2%80%93904-9) The system of [hyperreal numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperreal_number "Hyperreal number") is a way of treating [infinite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinity "Infinity") and infinitesimal quantities. The hyperreals are an [extension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_extension "Field extension") of the [real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number") that contain numbers greater than anything of the form ![Image 62: {\displaystyle 1+1+\cdots +1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fe420bc40161e1c9af002a820411964f7841c085) for any finite number of terms. Such numbers are infinite, and their [reciprocals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplicative_inverse "Multiplicative inverse") are infinitesimals. The application of hyperreal numbers to the foundations of calculus is called [nonstandard analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nonstandard_analysis "Nonstandard analysis"). This provides a way to define the basic concepts of calculus such as the derivative and integral in terms of infinitesimals, thereby giving a precise meaning to the ![Image 63: {\displaystyle d}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e85ff03cbe0c7341af6b982e47e9f90d235c66ab) in the Leibniz notation. Thus, the derivative of ![Image 64: {\displaystyle f(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/202945cce41ecebb6f643f31d119c514bec7a074) becomes ![Image 65: {\displaystyle f'(x)=\operatorname {st} \left({\frac {f(x+dx)-f(x)}{dx}}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7e6873165261012d02916403cf27b0ec818c0bc9) for an arbitrary infinitesimal ⁠![Image 66: {\displaystyle dx}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/845c817e348381a13f3fad5184169ce0e021c685)⁠, where ![Image 67: {\displaystyle \operatorname {st} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/41b5dcf91dd3c0064f20fcf8d2ecbd483ac605a1) denotes the [standard part function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_part_function "Standard part function"), which "rounds off" each finite hyperreal to the nearest real.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKeisler201245HenleKleinberg200366-10) Taking the squaring function ![Image 68: {\displaystyle f(x)=x^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/84ddac4ae10b1aa4a11741c79771a583419fb1fb) as an example again, ![Image 69: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}f'(x)&=\operatorname {st} \left({\frac {x^{2}+2x\cdot dx+(dx)^{2}-x^{2}}{dx}}\right)\\&=\operatorname {st} \left({\frac {2x\cdot dx+(dx)^{2}}{dx}}\right)\\&=\operatorname {st} \left({\frac {2x\cdot dx}{dx}}+{\frac {(dx)^{2}}{dx}}\right)\\&=\operatorname {st} \left(2x+dx\right)\\&=2x.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bfb98f27a96b968bf3d03921e0c541aae48c7eb5)

Continuity and differentiability
--------------------------------

[![Image 70](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Right-continuous.svg/206px-Right-continuous.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Right-continuous.svg)

This function does not have a derivative at the marked point, as the function is not continuous there (specifically, it has a [jump discontinuity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jump_discontinuity "Jump discontinuity")).

[![Image 71](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Absolute_value.svg/330px-Absolute_value.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Absolute_value.svg)

The absolute value function is continuous but fails to be differentiable at _x_ = 0 since the tangent slopes do not approach the same value from the left as they do from the right.

If ![Image 72: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) is [differentiable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differentiable "Differentiable") at ⁠![Image 73: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc)⁠, then ![Image 74: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) must also be [continuous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_function "Continuous function") at ![Image 75: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc).[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGonick2012156Thomas_et_al.2014114Strang_et_al.2023[httpsopenstaxorgbookscalculus-volume-1pages3-2-the-derivative-as-a-function_237]-11) As an example, choose a point ![Image 76: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc) and let ![Image 77: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) be the [step function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Step_function "Step function") that returns the value 1 for all ![Image 78: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) less than ⁠![Image 79: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc)⁠, and returns a different value 10 for all ![Image 80: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) greater than or equal to ![Image 81: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc). The function ![Image 82: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) cannot have a derivative at ![Image 83: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc). If ![Image 84: {\displaystyle h}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b26be3e694314bc90c3215047e4a2010c6ee184a) is negative, then ![Image 85: {\displaystyle a+h}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7e3cc36bc70730d4be0d4d6f64b5284a9d11d44c) is on the low part of the step, so the secant line from ![Image 86: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc) to ![Image 87: {\displaystyle a+h}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7e3cc36bc70730d4be0d4d6f64b5284a9d11d44c) is very steep; as ![Image 88: {\displaystyle h}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b26be3e694314bc90c3215047e4a2010c6ee184a) tends to zero, the slope tends to infinity. If ![Image 89: {\displaystyle h}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b26be3e694314bc90c3215047e4a2010c6ee184a) is positive, then ![Image 90: {\displaystyle a+h}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7e3cc36bc70730d4be0d4d6f64b5284a9d11d44c) is on the high part of the step, so the secant line from ![Image 91: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc) to ![Image 92: {\displaystyle a+h}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7e3cc36bc70730d4be0d4d6f64b5284a9d11d44c) has slope zero. Consequently, the secant lines do not approach any single slope, so the limit of the difference quotient does not exist. However, even if a function is continuous at a point, it may not be differentiable there. For example, the [absolute value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_value "Absolute value") function given by ![Image 93: {\displaystyle f(x)=|x|}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/094ddc9667a7f47e36687c1d2a94d35c0c7d5c67) is continuous at ⁠![Image 94: {\displaystyle x=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/953917eaf52f2e1baad54c8c9e3d6f9bb3710cdc)⁠, but it is not differentiable there. If ![Image 95: {\displaystyle h}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b26be3e694314bc90c3215047e4a2010c6ee184a) is positive, then the slope of the secant line from 0 to ![Image 96: {\displaystyle h}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b26be3e694314bc90c3215047e4a2010c6ee184a) is one; if ![Image 97: {\displaystyle h}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b26be3e694314bc90c3215047e4a2010c6ee184a) is negative, then the slope of the secant line from ![Image 98: {\displaystyle 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2aae8864a3c1fec9585261791a809ddec1489950) to ![Image 99: {\displaystyle h}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b26be3e694314bc90c3215047e4a2010c6ee184a) is ⁠![Image 100: {\displaystyle -1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/704fb0427140d054dd267925495e78164fee9aac)⁠.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGonick2012149Thomas_et_al.2014113Strang_et_al.2023[httpsopenstaxorgbookscalculus-volume-1pages3-2-the-derivative-as-a-function_237]-12) This can be seen graphically as a "kink" or a "cusp" in the graph at ![Image 101: {\displaystyle x=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/953917eaf52f2e1baad54c8c9e3d6f9bb3710cdc). Even a function with a smooth graph is not differentiable at a point where its [tangent is vertical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertical_tangent "Vertical tangent"): For instance, the function given by ![Image 102: {\displaystyle f(x)=x^{1/3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3d7d422b68444a7b74773a6121d45b7b5abf0abf) is not differentiable at ![Image 103: {\displaystyle x=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/953917eaf52f2e1baad54c8c9e3d6f9bb3710cdc). In summary, a function that has a derivative is continuous, but there are continuous functions that do not have a derivative.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGonick2012156Thomas_et_al.2014114Strang_et_al.2023[httpsopenstaxorgbookscalculus-volume-1pages3-2-the-derivative-as-a-function_237%E2%80%93238]-13)

Most functions that occur in practice have derivatives at all points or [almost every](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Almost_everywhere "Almost everywhere") point. Early in the [history of calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_calculus "History of calculus"), many mathematicians assumed that a continuous function was differentiable at most points.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJa%C5%A1ek1922Jarn%C3%ADk1922Rychl%C3%ADk1923-14) Under mild conditions (for example, if the function is a [monotone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monotone_function "Monotone function") or a [Lipschitz function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lipschitz_function "Lipschitz function")), this is true. However, in 1872, Weierstrass found the first example of a function that is continuous everywhere but differentiable nowhere. This example is now known as the [Weierstrass function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weierstrass_function "Weierstrass function").[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDavid2018-15) In 1931, [Stefan Banach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stefan_Banach "Stefan Banach") proved that the set of functions that have a derivative at some point is a [meager set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meager_set "Meager set") in the space of all continuous functions. Informally, this means that hardly any random continuous functions have a derivative at even one point.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative#cite_note-16)

Notation
--------

One common way of writing the derivative of a function is [Leibniz notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leibniz_notation "Leibniz notation"), introduced by [Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gottfried_Leibniz "Gottfried Leibniz") in 1675, which denotes a derivative as the quotient of two [differentials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_(mathematics) "Differential (mathematics)"), such as ![Image 104: {\displaystyle dy}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5c5eda9ec854eb0076d43c147eb8956637a1003f) and ⁠![Image 105: {\displaystyle dx}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/845c817e348381a13f3fad5184169ce0e021c685)⁠.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative#cite_note-FOOTNOTEApostol1967172Cajori2007204-17) It is still commonly used when the equation ![Image 106: {\displaystyle y=f(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2311a6a75c54b0ea085a381ba472c31d59321514) is viewed as a functional relationship between [dependent and independent variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dependent_and_independent_variables "Dependent and independent variables"). The first derivative is denoted by ⁠![Image 107: {\displaystyle \textstyle {\frac {dy}{dx}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4f361449076c4395a36b5d2524edfd3892ec49dc)⁠, read as "the derivative of ![Image 108: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d) with respect to ⁠![Image 109: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4)⁠".[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMooreSiegel2013110-18) This derivative can alternately be treated as the application of a [differential operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_operator "Differential operator") to a function, ![Image 110: {\textstyle {\frac {dy}{dx}}={\frac {d}{dx}}f(x).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47f2726d414984b6bfa432b80ca58e979a48ae7f) Higher derivatives are expressed using the notation ![Image 111: {\textstyle {\frac {d^{n}y}{dx^{n}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b13e55b8e7cd22a755110a09075300b5075fbb50) for the ![Image 112: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)-th derivative of ![Image 113: {\displaystyle y=f(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2311a6a75c54b0ea085a381ba472c31d59321514). These are abbreviations for multiple applications of the derivative operator; for example, ![Image 114: {\textstyle {\frac {d^{2}y}{dx^{2}}}={\frac {d}{dx}}{\Bigl (}{\frac {d}{dx}}f(x){\Bigr )}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/14ea46e2861392b96396046ae6f051220267f714)[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVarbergPurcellRigdon2007125%E2%80%93126-19) Unlike some alternatives, Leibniz notation involves explicit specification of the variable for differentiation, in the denominator, which removes ambiguity when working with multiple interrelated quantities. The derivative of a [composed function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_composition "Function composition") can be expressed using the [chain rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chain_rule "Chain rule"): if ![Image 115: {\displaystyle u=g(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54c52880c0cf28150ff774cb45cddcd2a027674f) and ![Image 116: {\displaystyle y=f(g(x))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1f65d809b2f57d05fbc48971b56283e667f6c770) then ![Image 117: {\textstyle {\frac {dy}{dx}}={\frac {dy}{du}}\cdot {\frac {du}{dx}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/527c4e92baf0ddd9e259092adfc5c95c3a4639e7)[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative#cite_note-20)

This notation, due to [Joseph-Louis Lagrange](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph-Louis_Lagrange "Joseph-Louis Lagrange"), is now known as _prime notation_.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchwartzman1994[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidPsH2DwAAQBAJpgPA171_171]Cajori19236%E2%80%937,_10%E2%80%9312,_21%E2%80%9324-21) The first derivative is written as ⁠![Image 119: {\displaystyle f'(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d0cd7d7c75340e779d82658e19d1720ce84ab127)⁠, read as "⁠![Image 120: y'}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3a535de94a2183d7130731eab8a83531d7c35c6b)⁠, read as "⁠![Image 123: {\displaystyle prime".[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMooreSiegel2013110Goodman196378%E2%80%9379-22) Similarly, the second and the third derivatives can be written as ![Image 124: {\displaystyle f''}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fbbdf186092f4353b7630fa8dda903e493cbbdc8) and ⁠![Image 125: {\displaystyle some authors use Roman numerals in [superscript](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subscript_and_superscript "Subscript and superscript"), whereas others place the number in parentheses, such as ![Image The latter notation generalizes to yield the notation ![Image 128: {\displaystyle f^{(n)}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9dfb1963ccde0e87eb3838f51dc19041e2ff3816) for the In [Newton's notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_notation "Newton's notation") or the _dot notation,_ a dot is placed over a symbol to represent a time derivative. If ![Image 131: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d) is a function of ⁠![Image 132: {\displaystyle t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/65658b7b223af9e1acc877d848888ecdb4466560)⁠, then the first and second derivatives can be written as ![Image 133: {\displaystyle {\dot {y}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ea068ce646833369cccf19795f23613159b5f89f) and ⁠![Image 134: {\displaystyle {\ddot {y}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/61a2fbf9e038c6f150fe23bfd2a6681c1f30120e)⁠, respectively. This notation is used exclusively for derivatives with respect to time or [arc length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arc_length "Arc length"). It is typically used in [differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_equation "Differential equation") in [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics") and [differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry").[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEvans199963Kreyszig19911-25) However, the dot notation becomes unmanageable for high-order derivatives (of order 4 or more) and cannot deal with multiple independent variables.

Another notation is _D-notation_, which represents the differential operator by the symbol ⁠![Image 135: {\displaystyle D}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f34a0c600395e5d4345287e21fb26efd386990e6)⁠.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVarbergPurcellRigdon2007125%E2%80%93126-19) The first derivative is written ![Image 136: {\displaystyle Df(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e9a890517132ee5973bf3fd826c31e1355adf036) and higher derivatives are written with a superscript, so the ![Image 137: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)-th derivative is ⁠![Image 138: {\displaystyle D^{n}f(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5c34d1f69c24e60baf94634068e69b170211f867)⁠. This notation is sometimes called _Euler notation_, although it seems that [Leonhard Euler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leonhard_Euler "Leonhard Euler") did not use it, and the notation was introduced by [Louis François Antoine Arbogast](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis_Fran%C3%A7ois_Antoine_Arbogast "Louis François Antoine Arbogast").[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative#cite_note-FOOTNOTECajori1923-26) To indicate a partial derivative, the variable differentiated by is indicated with a subscript, for example given the function ⁠![Image 139: {\displaystyle u=f(x,y)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81e13ad9067c45634116c3f2cc35af2dec62e820)⁠, its partial derivative with respect to ![Image 140: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) can be written ![Image 141: {\displaystyle D_{x}u}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/acf38b6f054495c95aababbec7a2a86f42608c8f) or ⁠![Image 142: {\displaystyle D_{x}f(x,y)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e29cff53d8a819582f60aa42046cdb9eea275304)⁠. Higher partial derivatives can be indicated by superscripts or multiple subscripts, e.g. ![Image 143: {\textstyle D_{xy}f(x,y)={\frac {\partial }{\partial y}}{\Bigl (}{\frac {\partial }{\partial x}}f(x,y){\Bigr )}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7607574a6dcaeb74bd35f64fbae78c652a9d08cb) and ⁠![Image 144: {\displaystyle \textstyle D_{x}^{2}f(x,y)={\frac {\partial }{\partial x}}{\Bigl (}{\frac {\partial }{\partial x}}f(x,y){\Bigr )}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5529a456c71c32a4d1878322f9b06676c66e0e7c)⁠.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative#cite_note-FOOTNOTEApostol1967172VarbergPurcellRigdon2007125%E2%80%93126-27)

Rules of computation
--------------------

In principle, the derivative of a function can be computed from the definition by considering the difference quotient and computing its limit. Once the derivatives of a few simple functions are known, the derivatives of other functions are more easily computed using _rules_ for obtaining derivatives of more complicated functions from simpler ones. This process of finding a derivative is known as **differentiation**.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative#cite_note-FOOTNOTEApostol1967160-28)

### Rules for basic functions

The following are the rules for the derivatives of the most common basic functions. Here, ![Image 145: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc) is a real number, and ![Image 146: {\displaystyle e}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cd253103f0876afc68ebead27a5aa9867d927467) is [the base of the natural logarithm, approximately 2.71828](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/E_(mathematical_constant) "E (mathematical constant)").[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative#cite_note-29)

### Rules for combined functions

Given that the ![Image 147: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) and ![Image 148: {\displaystyle g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3556280e66fe2c0d0140df20935a6f057381d77) are the functions. The following are some of the most basic rules for deducing the derivative of functions from derivatives of basic functions.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative#cite_note-30)

### Computation example

The derivative of the function given by ![Image 149: {\displaystyle f(x)=x^{4}+\sin \left(x^{2}\right)-\ln(x)e^{x}+7}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cb0674e453c727ebe6a253133e369263531772d1) is ![Image 150: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}f'(x)&=4x^{(4-1)}+{\frac {d\left(x^{2}\right)}{dx}}\cos \left(x^{2}\right)-{\frac {d\left(\ln {x}\right)}{dx}}e^{x}-\ln(x){\frac {d\left(e^{x}\right)}{dx}}+0\\&=4x^{3}+2x\cos \left(x^{2}\right)-{\frac {1}{x}}e^{x}-\ln(x)e^{x}.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9b2fe68d50ae0d718cd766c35ceb64407013a166) Here the second term was computed using the [chain rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chain_rule "Chain rule") and the third term using the [product rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Product_rule "Product rule"). The known derivatives of the elementary functions ![Image 151: {\displaystyle x^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cf0bf28fd28f45d07e1ceb909ce333c18c558c93), ![Image 152: {\displaystyle x^{4}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5613a29a5077db2494368feec90c0fb58cc2f703), ![Image 153: {\displaystyle \sin(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3a990a5545cac26c1c6821dca95d898bc80fe3a8), ![Image 154: {\displaystyle \ln(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0df055b8e294310e6785701c1c67105e109191d8), and ![Image 155: {\displaystyle \exp(x)=e^{x}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/13cc0e0007b0b0baf6553e5cd4ea883f030cc03b), as well as the constant ![Image 156: {\displaystyle 7}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ee716ec61382a6b795092c0edd859d12e64cbba8), were also used.

Antidifferentiation
-------------------

An _[antiderivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiderivative "Antiderivative")_ of a function ![Image 157: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) is a function whose derivative is ![Image 158: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61). Antiderivatives are not unique: if ![Image 159: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3) is an antiderivative of ![Image 160: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61), then so is ![Image 161: {\displaystyle A+c}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6b0a2f369077b4b24cefd506b4011afcba7c7c3f), where ![Image 162: {\displaystyle c}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/86a67b81c2de995bd608d5b2df50cd8cd7d92455) is any constant, because the derivative of a constant is zero.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStrang_et_al.2023[httpsopenstaxorgbookscalculus-volume-1pages4-10-antiderivatives_485%E2%80%93486]-31) The [fundamental theorem of calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_theorem_of_calculus "Fundamental theorem of calculus") shows that finding an antiderivative of a function gives a way to compute the areas of shapes bounded by that function. More precisely, the [integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral "Integral") of a function over a [closed interval](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interval_(mathematics) "Interval (mathematics)") is equal to the difference between the values of an antiderivative evaluated at the endpoints of that interval.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStrang_et_al.2023[httpsopenstaxorgbookscalculus-volume-1pages5-3-the-fundamental-theorem-of-calculus_552%E2%80%93559]-32)

Higher-order derivatives
------------------------

_Higher order derivatives_ are the result of differentiating a function repeatedly. Given that ![Image 163: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) is a differentiable function, the derivative of ![Image 164: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) is the first derivative, denoted as ⁠![Image 165: {\displaystyle f'}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/258eaada38956fb69b8cb1a2eef46bcb97d3126b)⁠. The derivative of ![Image 166: {\displaystyle f'}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/258eaada38956fb69b8cb1a2eef46bcb97d3126b) is the [second derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_derivative "Second derivative"), denoted as ⁠![Image 167: {\displaystyle f''}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fbbdf186092f4353b7630fa8dda903e493cbbdc8)⁠, and the derivative of ![Image 168: {\displaystyle f''}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fbbdf186092f4353b7630fa8dda903e493cbbdc8) is the [third derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Third_derivative "Third derivative"), denoted as ⁠![Image 169: {\displaystyle f'''}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b05804fd3402a8dc714b756d2f1e42c6653641a0)⁠. By continuing this process, if it exists, the ⁠![Image 170: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)⁠th derivative is the derivative of the ⁠![Image 171: {\displaystyle (n-1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/df88c6333caaf6471cf277f24b802ff9931b133e)⁠th derivative or the _derivative of order ⁠![Image 172: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest\_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)⁠_. As has been [discussed above](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative#Notation), the generalization of derivative of a function ![Image 173: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) may be denoted as ⁠![Image 174: {\displaystyle f^{(n)}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9dfb1963ccde0e87eb3838f51dc19041e2ff3816)⁠.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative#cite_note-FOOTNOTEApostol1967160VarbergPurcellRigdon2007125%E2%80%93126-33) A function that has ![Image 175: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40) successive derivatives is called _![Image 176: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest\_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40) times differentiable_. If the ![Image 177: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40)-th derivative is continuous, then the function is said to be of [differentiability class](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differentiability_class "Differentiability class")⁠![Image 178: {\displaystyle C^{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/167fdb0cfb5644c4623b5842e1a9141acd83b534)⁠.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWarner19835-34) A function that has infinitely many derivatives is called _infinitely differentiable_ or _[smooth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smoothness "Smoothness")_.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDebnathShah2015[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidqPuWBQAAQBAJpgPA40_40]-35) Any [polynomial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial "Polynomial") function is infinitely differentiable; taking derivatives repeatedly will eventually result in a [constant function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constant_function "Constant function"), and all subsequent derivatives of that function are zero.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative#cite_note-FOOTNOTECarothers2000[httpsbooksgooglecombooksid4VFDVy1NFiACpgPA176_176]-36)

One [application of higher-order derivatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_calculus#Applications_of_derivatives "Differential calculus") is in [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics"). Suppose that a function represents the position of an object at the time. The first derivative of that function is the [velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity "Velocity") of an object with respect to time, the second derivative of the function is the [acceleration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acceleration "Acceleration") of an object with respect to time,[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative#cite_note-FOOTNOTEApostol1967160-28) and the third derivative is the [jerk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerk_(physics) "Jerk (physics)").[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStewart2002193-37)

### Vector-valued functions

A [vector-valued function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector-valued_function "Vector-valued function")![Image 179: {\displaystyle \mathbf {y} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bb25a040b592282dc2a254c3117e792c3c81161f) of a real variable sends real numbers to vectors in some [vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space")![Image 180: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d). A vector-valued function can be split up into its coordinate functions ![Image 181: {\displaystyle y_{1}(t),y_{2}(t),\dots ,y_{n}(t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dd2017a48392d43dc469db87a7c7bf7d2ca8799b), meaning that ![Image 182: {\displaystyle \mathbf {y} =(y_{1}(t),y_{2}(t),\dots ,y_{n}(t))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8d203be75183e74845c6cf23c26e8dfc0a4ab96b). This includes, for example, [parametric curves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parametric_curve "Parametric curve") in ![Image 183: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e150115ab9f63023215109595b76686a1ff890fd) or ![Image 184: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5). The coordinate functions are real-valued functions, so the above definition of derivative applies to them. The derivative of ![Image 185: {\displaystyle \mathbf {y} (t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1a85cc905ba3c42b0f18262a7073b5738df7337a) is defined to be the [vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_(geometric) "Vector (geometric)"), called the [tangent vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry_of_curves "Differential geometry of curves"), whose coordinates are the derivatives of the coordinate functions. That is,[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStewart2002893-38)![Image 186: {\displaystyle \mathbf {y} '(t)=\lim _{h\to 0}{\frac {\mathbf {y} (t+h)-\mathbf {y} (t)}{h}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/712c8b9e518061e39498c82a35cac2ac37afa7ba) if the limit exists. The subtraction in the numerator is the subtraction of vectors, not scalars. If the derivative of ![Image 187: {\displaystyle \mathbf {y} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bb25a040b592282dc2a254c3117e792c3c81161f) exists for every value of ⁠![Image 188: {\displaystyle t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/65658b7b223af9e1acc877d848888ecdb4466560)⁠, then ![Image 189: {\displaystyle \mathbf {y} '}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c5ce91809395fda8acc214bf6c56c0d6995d5af1) is another vector-valued function.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStewart2002893-38)

### Directional derivatives

If ![Image 218: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) is a real-valued function on ⁠![Image 219: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d)⁠, then the partial derivatives of ![Image 220: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) measure its variation in the direction of the coordinate axes. For example, if ![Image 221: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) is a function of ![Image 222: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) and ⁠![Image 223: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d)⁠, then its partial derivatives measure the variation in ![Image 224: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) in the ![Image 225: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) and ![Image 226: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d) direction. However, they do not directly measure the variation of ![Image 227: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) in any other direction, such as along the diagonal line ⁠![Image 228: {\displaystyle y=x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d0abe2e7da593fb7b41d44e87a97fefdd8998b77)⁠. These are measured using directional derivatives. Given a vector ⁠![Image 229: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} =(v_{1},\ldots ,v_{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8326c1433623213f7b20658b61e3b3221890f902)⁠, then the [directional derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Directional_derivative "Directional derivative") of ![Image 230: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) in the direction of ![Image 231: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35c1866e359fbfd2e0f606c725ba5cc37a5195d6) at the point ![Image 232: {\displaystyle \mathbf {x} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/32adf004df5eb0a8c7fd8c0b6b7405183c5a5ef2) is:[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVarbergPurcellRigdon2007642-44)![Image 233: {\displaystyle D_{\mathbf {v} }{f}(\mathbf {x} )=\lim _{h\rightarrow 0}{\frac {f(\mathbf {x} +h\mathbf {v} )-f(\mathbf {x} )}{h}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/73d04a1a95dfff9f09c9a1b64d51b406ad006193)

If all the partial derivatives of ![Image 234: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) exist and are continuous at ⁠![Image 235: {\displaystyle \mathbf {x} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/32adf004df5eb0a8c7fd8c0b6b7405183c5a5ef2)⁠, then they determine the directional derivative of ![Image 236: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) in the direction ![Image 237: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35c1866e359fbfd2e0f606c725ba5cc37a5195d6) by the formula:[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuzman2003[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidaI_qBwAAQBAJpgPA35_35]-45)![Image 238: {\displaystyle D_{\mathbf {v} }{f}(\mathbf {x} )=\sum _{j=1}^{n}v_{j}{\frac {\partial f}{\partial x_{j}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/24490ea7f8d3e8381468df437d50175ae8b766bb)

### Total derivative and Jacobian matrix

When ![Image 239: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) is a function from an open subset of ![Image 240: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d) to ⁠![Image 241: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{m}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6a87a024931038d1858dc22e8a194e5978c3412e)⁠, then the directional derivative of ![Image 242: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) in a chosen direction is the best linear approximation to ![Image 243: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) at that point and in that direction. However, when ⁠![Image 244: {\displaystyle n>1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ee74e1cc07e7041edf0fcbd4481f5cd32ad17b64)⁠, no single directional derivative can give a complete picture of the behavior of ![Image 245: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61). The total derivative gives a complete picture by considering all directions at once. That is, for any vector ![Image 246: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35c1866e359fbfd2e0f606c725ba5cc37a5195d6) starting at ⁠![Image 247: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1a957216653a9ee0d0133dcefd13fb75e36b8b9d)⁠, the linear approximation formula holds:[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDavvaz2023[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidofzKEAAAQBAJpgPA266_266]-46)![Image 248: {\displaystyle f(\mathbf {a} +\mathbf {v} )\approx f(\mathbf {a} )+f'(\mathbf {a} )\mathbf {v} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b7bd709cae081a1858252d84ca312450297a697a) Similarly with the single-variable derivative, ![Image 249: {\displaystyle f'(\mathbf {a} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/338762cf208554747565397af18800354d1cf10b) is chosen so that the error in this approximation is as small as possible. The total derivative of ![Image 250: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) at ![Image 251: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1a957216653a9ee0d0133dcefd13fb75e36b8b9d) is the unique linear transformation ![Image 252: {\displaystyle f'(\mathbf {a} )\colon \mathbb {R} ^{n}\to \mathbb {R} ^{m}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47a202526123f9c20a952cc77c54114b731cdec7) such that[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDavvaz2023[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidofzKEAAAQBAJpgPA266_266]-46)![Image 253: {\displaystyle \lim _{\mathbf {h} \to 0}{\frac {\lVert f(\mathbf {a} +\mathbf {h} )-(f(\mathbf {a} )+f'(\mathbf {a} )\mathbf {h} )\rVert }{\lVert \mathbf {h} \rVert }}=0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a592ab79ec7e0e0e0ecd257cae25587e0ea14eb9) Here ![Image 254: {\displaystyle \mathbf {h} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1fce1c8de3a01ae39379db83781850619c4c0987) is a vector in ⁠![Image 255: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d)⁠, so the norm in the denominator is the standard length on ![Image 256: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d). However, ![Image 257: {\displaystyle f'(\mathbf {a} )\mathbf {h} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4e83d6cbcb8354e47f710d63068e03445caa1b7b) is a vector in ⁠![Image 258: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{m}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6a87a024931038d1858dc22e8a194e5978c3412e)⁠, and the norm in the numerator is the standard length on ![Image 259: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{m}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6a87a024931038d1858dc22e8a194e5978c3412e).[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDavvaz2023[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidofzKEAAAQBAJpgPA266_266]-46) If ![Image 260: {\displaystyle v}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e07b00e7fc0847fbd16391c778d65bc25c452597) is a vector starting at ⁠![Image 261: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc)⁠, then ![Image 262: {\displaystyle f'(\mathbf {a} )\mathbf {v} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e74442d4ba737f4962b030c524fb1083f001f6ba) is called the [pushforward](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pushforward_(differential) "Pushforward (differential)") of ![Image 263: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35c1866e359fbfd2e0f606c725ba5cc37a5195d6) by ![Image 264: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61).[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative#cite_note-FOOTNOTELee201372-47)

If the total derivative exists at ⁠![Image 265: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1a957216653a9ee0d0133dcefd13fb75e36b8b9d)⁠, then all the partial derivatives and directional derivatives of ![Image 266: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) exist at ⁠![Image 267: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1a957216653a9ee0d0133dcefd13fb75e36b8b9d)⁠, and for all ⁠![Image 268: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35c1866e359fbfd2e0f606c725ba5cc37a5195d6)⁠, ![Image 269: {\displaystyle f'(\mathbf {a} )\mathbf {v} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e74442d4ba737f4962b030c524fb1083f001f6ba) is the directional derivative of ![Image 270: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) in the direction ⁠![Image 271: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35c1866e359fbfd2e0f606c725ba5cc37a5195d6)⁠. If ![Image 272: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) is written using coordinate functions, so that ⁠![Image 273: {\displaystyle f=(f_{1},f_{2},\dots ,f_{m})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/651e4ae2a9cbebab30ed5c662713bfe12b82d319)⁠, then the total derivative can be expressed using the partial derivatives as a [matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_(mathematics) "Matrix (mathematics)"). This matrix is called the [Jacobian matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacobian_matrix "Jacobian matrix") of ![Image 274: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) at ![Image 275: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1a957216653a9ee0d0133dcefd13fb75e36b8b9d):[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDavvaz2023[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidofzKEAAAQBAJpgPA267_267]-48)![Image 276: {\displaystyle f'(\mathbf {a} )=\operatorname {Jac} _{\mathbf {a} }=\left({\frac {\partial f_{i}}{\partial x_{j}}}\right)_{ij}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7d11d99f370bc7c090bf255c835e0fb4e5ea72d4)

Generalizations
---------------

The concept of a derivative can be extended to many other settings. The common thread is that the derivative of a function at a point serves as a [linear approximation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_approximation "Linear approximation") of the function at that point.

*   An important generalization of the derivative concerns [complex functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_function "Complex function") of [complex variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number"), such as functions from (a domain in) the complex numbers ![Image 277: {\displaystyle \mathbb {C} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f9add4085095b9b6d28d045fd9c92c2c09f549a7) to ⁠![Image 278: {\displaystyle \mathbb {C} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f9add4085095b9b6d28d045fd9c92c2c09f549a7)⁠. The notion of the derivative of such a function is obtained by replacing real variables with complex variables in the definition.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative#cite_note-FOOTNOTERoussos2014303-49) If ![Image 279: {\displaystyle \mathbb {C} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f9add4085095b9b6d28d045fd9c92c2c09f549a7) is identified with ![Image 280: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e150115ab9f63023215109595b76686a1ff890fd) by writing a complex number ![Image 281: {\displaystyle z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf368e72c009decd9b6686ee84a375632e11de98) as ⁠![Image 282: {\displaystyle x+iy}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ceb1c6ce62a20dbfe9cb3d82dca889577b469703)⁠ then a differentiable function from ![Image 283: {\displaystyle \mathbb {C} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f9add4085095b9b6d28d045fd9c92c2c09f549a7) to ![Image 284: {\displaystyle \mathbb {C} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f9add4085095b9b6d28d045fd9c92c2c09f549a7) is certainly differentiable as a function from ![Image 285: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e150115ab9f63023215109595b76686a1ff890fd) to ![Image 286: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e150115ab9f63023215109595b76686a1ff890fd) (in the sense that its partial derivatives all exist), but the converse is not true in general: the complex derivative only exists if the real derivative is _complex linear_ and this imposes relations between the partial derivatives called the [Cauchy–Riemann equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cauchy%E2%80%93Riemann_equations "Cauchy–Riemann equations") – see [holomorphic functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holomorphic_function "Holomorphic function").[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGbur2011261%E2%80%93264-50)
*   Another generalization concerns functions between [differentiable or smooth manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smooth_manifold "Smooth manifold"). Intuitively speaking such a manifold ![Image 287: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) is a space that can be approximated near each point ![Image 288: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) by a vector space called its [tangent space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_space "Tangent space"): the prototypical example is a [smooth surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smooth_surface "Smooth surface") in ⁠![Image 289: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5)⁠. The derivative (or differential) of a (differentiable) map ![Image 290: {\displaystyle f:M\to N}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1dbd50e2de9728ee14a7c232441137f588b109f6) between manifolds, at a point ![Image 291: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) in ⁠![Image 292: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd)⁠, is then a [linear map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_map "Linear map") from the tangent space of ![Image 293: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) at ![Image 294: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) to the tangent space of ![Image 295: {\displaystyle N}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5e3890c981ae85503089652feb48b191b57aae3) at ⁠![Image 296: {\displaystyle f(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/202945cce41ecebb6f643f31d119c514bec7a074)⁠. The derivative function becomes a map between the [tangent bundles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_bundle "Tangent bundle") of ![Image 297: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) and ⁠![Image 298: {\displaystyle N}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5e3890c981ae85503089652feb48b191b57aae3)⁠. This definition is used in [differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry").[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGrayAbbenaSalamon2006[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidowEj9TMYo7ICpgPA826_826]-51)
*   Differentiation can also be defined for maps between [vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space"), such as [Banach space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Banach_space "Banach space"), in which those generalizations are the [Gateaux derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gateaux_derivative "Gateaux derivative") and the [Fréchet derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fr%C3%A9chet_derivative "Fréchet derivative").[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative#cite_note-52)
*   One deficiency of the classical derivative is that very many functions are not differentiable. Nevertheless, there is a way of extending the notion of the derivative so that all [continuous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_function "Continuous function") functions and many other functions can be differentiated using a concept known as the [weak derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weak_derivative "Weak derivative"). The idea is to embed the continuous functions in a larger space called the space of [distributions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distribution_(mathematics) "Distribution (mathematics)") and only require that a function is differentiable "on average".[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFunaro1992[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidCX4SXf3mdeUCpgPA84_84%E2%80%9385]-53)
*   Properties of the derivative have inspired the introduction and study of many similar objects in algebra and topology; an example is [differential algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_algebra "Differential algebra"). Here, it consists of the derivation of some topics in abstract algebra, such as [rings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ring_(mathematics) "Ring (mathematics)"), [ideals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ideal_(ring_theory) "Ideal (ring theory)"), [field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)"), and so on.[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKolchin1973[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidyDCfhIjka-8CpgPA58_58],_[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidyDCfhIjka-8CpgPA126_126]-54)
*   The discrete equivalent of differentiation is [finite differences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_difference "Finite difference"). The study of differential calculus is unified with the calculus of finite differences in [time scale calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_scale_calculus "Time scale calculus").[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGeorgiev2018[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidOJJVDwAAQBAJpgPA8_8]-55)
*   The [arithmetic derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic_derivative "Arithmetic derivative") involves the function that is defined for the [integers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer") by the [prime factorization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_factorization "Prime factorization"). This is an analogy with the product rule.[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarbeau1961-56)

Others define ![Image 302: {\displaystyle dx}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/845c817e348381a13f3fad5184169ce0e021c685) as an independent variable, and define ![Image 303: {\displaystyle du}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d33341cb51d52b43431e85e57ffceadeb44b04a2) by ⁠![Image 304: {\displaystyle \textstyle du=dxf'(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b8fdb07375bf666ad32172f0e2bd0181fe830f0)⁠. It is also interpreted as the [exterior derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_derivative "Exterior derivative") of a function ⁠![Image 306: {\displaystyle u}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3e6bb763d22c20916ed4f0bb6bd49d7470cffd8)⁠.
