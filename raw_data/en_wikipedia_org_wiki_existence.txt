Title: Existence

URL Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence

Published Time: 2001-04-04T17:53:26Z

Markdown Content:
[![Image 1: Existential quantifier](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Existential_quantifier.svg/250px-Existential_quantifier.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Existential_quantifier.svg)

The [existential quantifier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existential_quantifier "Existential quantifier") ∃ is often used in [logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logic "Logic") to express existence.

**Existence** is the state of having **being** or [reality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reality "Reality") in contrast to **nonexistence** and nonbeing. Existence is often contrasted with [essence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Essence "Essence"): the essence of an [entity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entity "Entity") is its essential features or qualities, which can be understood even if one does not know whether the entity exists.

[Ontology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ontology "Ontology") is the philosophical discipline studying the nature and types of existence. Singular existence is the existence of individual entities while general existence refers to the existence of [concepts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concepts "Concepts") or [universals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universals "Universals"). Entities present in space and time have [concrete](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abstract_and_concrete "Abstract and concrete") existence in contrast to abstract entities, like numbers and sets. Other distinctions are between [possible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subjunctive_possibility "Subjunctive possibility"), [contingent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contingency_(philosophy) "Contingency (philosophy)"), and [necessary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metaphysical_necessity "Metaphysical necessity") existence and between [physical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matter "Matter") and [mental](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mind "Mind") existence. The common view is that an entity either exists or not with nothing in between, but some philosophers say that there are degrees of existence, meaning that some entities exist to a higher degree than others.

The orthodox position in ontology is that existence is a second-order [property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Property_(philosophy) "Property (philosophy)"), or a property of properties. For example, to say that lions exist means that the property of being a lion is possessed by an entity. A different view sees existence as a first-order property, or a property of [individuals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particular "Particular"), meaning existence is similar to other properties of individuals, like color and shape. [Alexius Meinong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexius_Meinong "Alexius Meinong") and his followers accept this idea and say that not all individuals have this property; they state that there are some individuals, such as [Santa Claus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Santa_Claus "Santa Claus"), that do not exist. Universalists reject this view; they see existence as a universal property of every individual.

The concept of existence has been discussed throughout the [history of philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_philosophy "History of philosophy") and already played a role in [ancient philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_philosophy "Ancient philosophy"), including [Presocratic philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Presocratic_philosophy "Presocratic philosophy") in [Ancient Greece](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greece "Ancient Greece"), [Hindu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hindu_philosophy "Hindu philosophy") and [Buddhist philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buddhist_philosophy "Buddhist philosophy") in [Ancient India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_India "Ancient India"), and [Daoist philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Daoist_philosophy "Daoist philosophy") in [ancient China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_China "Ancient China"). It is relevant to fields such as [logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logic "Logic"), [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), [epistemology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epistemology "Epistemology"), [philosophy of mind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophy_of_mind "Philosophy of mind"), [philosophy of language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophy_of_language "Philosophy of language"), and [existentialism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existentialism "Existentialism").

Existence is the state of being real or participating in [reality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reality "Reality").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-1) Existence sets real entities apart from imaginary ones,[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-auto4-2) and can refer both to individual entities or to the totality of reality.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-3) The word "existence" entered the English language in the late 14th century from [old French](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Old_French "Old French") and has its roots in the [medieval Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval_Latin "Medieval Latin") term _[ex(s)istere](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/en:exsisto#Latin "wiktionary:en:exsisto")_, which means "to stand forth", "to appear", and "to arise".[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-4) Existence is studied by the subdiscipline of [metaphysics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metaphysics "Metaphysics") known as [ontology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ontology "Ontology").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-5)[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-8)

The terms "being", "reality", and "actuality" are often used as synonyms of "existence",[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-9) but the exact definition of _existence_ and its connection to these terms is disputed.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-10) According to metaphysician [Alexius Meinong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexius_Meinong "Alexius Meinong") (1853–1920), all entities have being but not all entities have existence. He argues merely possible objects like [Santa Claus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Santa_Claus "Santa Claus") have being but lack existence.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-11) Ontologist Takashi Yagisawa (20th century–present) contrasts existence with reality; he sees "reality" as the more-fundamental term because it equally characterizes all entities and defines existence as a relative term that connects an entity to the world it inhabits.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-12) According to philosopher [Gottlob Frege](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gottlob_Frege "Gottlob Frege") (1848–1925), actuality is narrower than existence because only actual entities can produce and undergo changes, in contrast to non-actual existing entities like [numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Numbers "Numbers") and [sets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_(mathematics) "Set (mathematics)").[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-13) According to some philosophers, like [Edmund Husserl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edmund_Husserl "Edmund Husserl") (1859–1938), existence is an elementary concept, meaning it cannot be defined in other terms without involving circularity. This would imply characterizing existence or talking about its nature in a non-trivial manner may be difficult or impossible.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-14)

Disputes about the nature of existence are reflected in the distinction between thin and [thick concepts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thick_concept "Thick concept") of existence. Thin concepts of existence understand existence as a logical property that every existing thing shares; they do not include any substantial content about the metaphysical implications of having existence. According to one view, existence is the same as the logical property of [self-identity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law_of_identity "Law of identity"). This view articulates a thin concept of existence because it merely states what exists is identical to itself without discussing any substantial characteristics of the nature of existence.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-15) Thick concepts of existence encompass a metaphysical analysis of what it means that something exists and what essential features existence implies. According to one proposal, to exist is to be present in space and time, and to have effects on other things. This definition is controversial because it implies abstract objects such as numbers do not exist. Philosopher [George Berkeley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_Berkeley "George Berkeley") (1685–1753) gave a different thick concept of existence; he stated: "to be is to be perceived", meaning all existence is mental.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-16)

Existence contrasts with nonexistence, a lack of reality. Whether objects can be divided into existent and [nonexistent objects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nonexistent_objects "Nonexistent objects") is a subject of controversy. This distinction is sometimes used to explain how it is possible to think of fictional objects like dragons and unicorns but the concept of nonexistent objects is not generally accepted; some philosophers say the concept is contradictory.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-17) Closely related contrasting terms are [nothingness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nothingness "Nothingness") and nonbeing.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-18) Existence is commonly associated with mind-independent reality[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-19) but this position is not universally accepted because there could also be forms of mind-dependent existence, such as the existence of an idea inside a person's mind. According to some [idealists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Idealists "Idealists"), this may apply to all of reality.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-20)

Another contrast is made between _existence_ and _[essence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Essence "Essence")_. Essence refers to the intrinsic nature or defining qualities of an entity. The essence of something determines what kind of entity it is and how it differs from other kinds of entities. Essence corresponds to what an entity is, while existence corresponds to the fact that it is. For instance, it is possible to understand what an [object](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Object_(philosophy) "Object (philosophy)") is and grasp its nature even if one does not know whether this object exists.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-21) According to some philosophers, there is a difference between entities and the fundamental characteristics that make them the entities they are.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-22)[Martin Heidegger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Martin_Heidegger "Martin Heidegger") (1889–1976) introduced this concept; he calls it the [ontological difference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ontological_difference "Ontological difference") and contrasts individual beings with being. According to his response to the question of being, being is not an entity but the background context that makes all individual entities intelligible.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-23)[[b]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-25)

Types of existing entities
--------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Existence&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Types of existing entities")]

Many discussions of the [types of existing entities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theory_of_categories "Theory of categories") revolve around the definitions of different types, the existence or nonexistence of entities of a specific type, the way entities of different types are related to each other, and whether some types are more fundamental than others.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-26) Examples are the existence or nonexistence of [souls](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soul "Soul"); whether there are abstract, fictional, and universal entities; and the existence or nonexistence of [possible worlds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Possible_world "Possible world") and objects besides the actual world.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-27) These discussions cover the topics of the basic stuff or constituents underlying all reality and the most general features of entities.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-28)

### Singular and general

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Existence&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Singular and general")]

There is a distinction between singular existence and general existence. Singular existence is the existence of individual entities. For example, the sentence "[Angela Merkel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angela_Merkel "Angela Merkel") exists" expresses the existence of one particular person. General existence pertains to general concepts, properties, or [universals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universals "Universals").[[c]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-30) For instance, the sentence "politicians exist" states the general term "politician" has instances without referring to a particular politician.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-31)

Singular and general existence are closely related to each other, and some philosophers have tried to explain one as a special case of the other. For example, according to Frege, general existence is more basic than singular existence. One argument in favor of this position is that singular existence can be expressed in terms of general existence. For instance, the sentence "Angela Merkel exists" can be expressed as "entities that are identical to Angela Merkel exist", where the expression "being identical to Angela Merkel" is understood as a general term. Philosopher [Willard Van Orman Quine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Willard_Van_Orman_Quine "Willard Van Orman Quine") (1908–2000) defends a different position by giving primacy to singular existence and arguing that general existence can be expressed in terms of singular existence.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-32)

A related question is whether there can be general existence without singular existence. According to philosopher Henry S. Leonard (1905–1967), a property only has general existence if there is at least one actual object that instantiates[[d]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-34) it. Philosopher [Nicholas Rescher](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nicholas_Rescher "Nicholas Rescher") (1928–2024), by contrast, states that properties can exist if they have no actual instances, like the property of "being a unicorn".[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-35) This question has a long philosophical tradition in relation to the existence of universals. According to [Platonists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platonists "Platonists"), universals have general existence as [Platonic forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platonic_forms "Platonic forms") independently of the particulars[[e]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-37) that exemplify them. According to this view, the universal of redness exists independently of the existence or nonexistence of red objects.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-38)[Aristotelianism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotelianism "Aristotelianism") also accepts the existence of universals but says their existence depends on particulars that instantiate them and that they are unable to exist by themselves. According to this view, a universal that is not present in the space and time does not exist.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-39) According to [nominalists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nominalists "Nominalists"), only particulars have existence and universals do not exist.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-40)

### Concrete and abstract

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Existence&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Concrete and abstract")]

There is an influential distinction in ontology between [concrete and abstract objects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abstract_and_concrete "Abstract and concrete"). Many concrete objects, like rocks, plants, and other people, are encountered in everyday life. They exist in space and time. They [have effects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Causality "Causality") on each other, like when a rock falls on a plant and damages it, or a plant grows through rock and breaks it. Abstract objects, like numbers, sets, and types, have no location in space and time, and lack causal powers.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-41) The distinction between concrete objects and abstract objects is sometimes treated as the most-general division of being.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-42)

The existence of concrete objects is widely agreed upon but opinions about abstract objects are divided. [Realists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophical_realism "Philosophical realism") such as [Plato](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plato "Plato") accept the idea that abstract objects have independent existence.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-43) Some realists say abstract objects have the same mode of existence as concrete objects; according to others, they exist in a different way.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-44)[Anti-realists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anti-realism "Anti-realism") state that abstract objects do not exist, a view that is often combined with the idea that existence requires a location in space and time or the ability to causally interact.[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-45)

### Possible, contingent, and necessary

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Existence&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Possible, contingent, and necessary")]

A further distinction is between merely possible, [contingent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contingency_(philosophy) "Contingency (philosophy)"), and [necessary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metaphysical_necessity "Metaphysical necessity") existence.[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-46) An entity has necessary existence if it must exist or could not fail to exist. This means that it is not possible to newly create or destroy necessary entities. Entities that exist but could fail to exist are contingent; merely possible entities do not exist but could exist.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-47)

[![Image 2: Lithograph of Avicenna](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Avicenna_lithograph_-_cropped.png/250px-Avicenna_lithograph_-_cropped.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Avicenna "Avicenna")

[![Image 3: Painting of Thomas Aquinas](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Thomas_Aquinas_by_Carlo_Crivelli.png/250px-Thomas_Aquinas_by_Carlo_Crivelli.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Aquinas "Thomas Aquinas")

Most entities encountered in ordinary experience, like telephones, sticks, and flowers, have contingent existence.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-48) The contingent existence of telephones is reflected in the fact that they exist in the present but did not exist in the past, meaning that it is not necessary that they exist. It is an open question whether any entities have necessary existence.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-49) According to some [nominalists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nominalism "Nominalism"), all concrete objects have contingent existence while all abstract objects have necessary existence.[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-50)

According to some theorists, one or several necessary beings are required as the explanatory foundation of the cosmos. For instance, the philosophers [Avicenna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Avicenna "Avicenna") (980–1037) and [Thomas Aquinas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Aquinas "Thomas Aquinas") (1225–1274) say that [God](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/God_in_Christianity "God in Christianity") has necessary existence.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-51) A few philosophers, such as [Baruch Spinoza](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baruch_Spinoza "Baruch Spinoza") (1632–1677), see [God and the world as the same thing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pantheism "Pantheism"), and say that all entities have necessary existence to provide a unified and rational explanation of everything.[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-52)

There are many academic debates about the existence of merely possible objects. According to [actualism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Actualism "Actualism"), only actual entities have being; this includes both contingent and necessary entities but excludes merely possible entities.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-53)[Possibilists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Possibilism_(philosophy) "Possibilism (philosophy)") reject this view and state there are also merely possible objects besides actual objects.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-54) For example, metaphysician [David Lewis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Lewis_(philosopher) "David Lewis (philosopher)") (1941–2001) states that possible objects exist in the same way as actual objects so as to provide a robust explanation of why statements about what is possible and necessary are true. According to him, possible objects exist in possible worlds while actual objects exist in the actual world. Lewis says the only difference between possible worlds and the actual world is the location of the speaker; the term "actual" refers to the world of the speaker, similar to the way the terms "here" and "now" refer to the spatial and temporal location of the speaker.[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-55)

The problem of contingent and necessary existence is closely related to the ontological question of [why there is anything at all](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Why_there_is_anything_at_all "Why there is anything at all") or why is there something rather than [nothing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nothing "Nothing"). According to one view, the existence of something is a contingent fact, meaning the world could have been totally empty. This is not possible if there are necessary entities, which could not have failed to exist. In this case, global nothingness is impossible because the world needs to contain at least all necessary entities.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-56)

### Physical and mental

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Existence&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Physical and mental")]

Entities that exist on a physical level include objects encountered in everyday life, like stones, trees, and human bodies, as well as entities discussed in [modern physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modern_physics "Modern physics"), like electrons and protons.[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-57)[[f]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-59) Physical entities can be observed and measured; they possess mass and a location in space and time.[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-60) Mental entities like perceptions, experiences of pleasure and pain as well as beliefs, desires, and emotions belong to the realm of the mind; they are primarily associated with conscious experiences but also include unconscious states like unconscious beliefs, desires, and memories.[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-61)

The [mind–body problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mind%E2%80%93body_problem "Mind–body problem") concerns the ontological status of and relation between physical and mental entities and is a frequent topic in [metaphysics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metaphysics "Metaphysics") and [philosophy of mind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophy_of_mind "Philosophy of mind").[[g]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-63) According to [materialists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Materialists "Materialists"), only physical entities exist on the most-fundamental level. Materialists usually explain mental entities in terms of physical processes; for example, as brain states or as patterns of neural activation.[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-64) Idealism,[[h]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-66) a minority view in contemporary philosophy,[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-67) rejects matter as ultimate and views the mind as the most basic reality.[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-68)[Dualists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dualists "Dualists") like [René Descartes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Descartes "René Descartes") (1596–1650) believe both physical and mental entities exist on the most-fundamental level. They state they are connected to one another in several ways but that one cannot be reduced to the other.[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-69)

Fictional entities are entities that exist as inventions inside works of [fiction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiction "Fiction").[[i]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-71) For example, [Sherlock Holmes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sherlock_Holmes "Sherlock Holmes") is a fictional character in [Arthur Conan Doyle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arthur_Conan_Doyle "Arthur Conan Doyle")'s book _[A Study in Scarlet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A\_Study\_in\_Scarlet "A Study in Scarlet")_ and [flying carpets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flying_carpets "Flying carpets") are fictional objects in the folktales _[One Thousand and One Nights](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One\_Thousand\_and\_One\_Nights "One Thousand and One Nights")_.[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-72) According to anti-realism, fictional entities do not form part of reality in any substantive sense. Possibilists, by contrast, see fictional entities as a subclass of possible objects; creationists say that they are artifacts that depend for their existence on the authors who first conceived them.[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-73)

[Intentional inexistence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intentionality#The_problem_of_intentional_inexistence "Intentionality") is a similar phenomenon concerned with the existence of objects within mental states. This happens when a person perceives or thinks about an object. In some cases, the [intentional object](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intentional_object "Intentional object") corresponds to a real object outside the mental state, like when accurately perceiving a tree in the garden. In other cases, the intentional object does not have a real counterpart, like when thinking about [Bigfoot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bigfoot "Bigfoot"). The [problem of intentional inexistence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Problem_of_intentional_inexistence "Problem of intentional inexistence") is the challenge of explaining how one can think about entities that do not exist since this seems to have the paradoxical implication that the thinker stands in a [relation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relation_(philosophy) "Relation (philosophy)") to a nonexisting object.[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-74)

Modes and degrees of existence
------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Existence&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Modes and degrees of existence")]

Closely related to the problem of different types of entities is the question of whether their modes of existence also vary. This is the case according to ontological pluralism, which states entities belonging to different types differ in both their essential features and in the ways they exist.[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-75) This position is sometimes found in theology; it states God is radically different from his creation and emphasizes his uniqueness by saying the difference affects both God's features and God's mode of existence.[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-76)

Another form of ontological pluralism distinguishes the existence of material objects from the existence of [space-time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space-time "Space-time"). According to this view, material objects have relative existence because they exist in space-time; the existence of space-time itself is not relative in this sense because it just exists without existing within another space-time.[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-77)

The topic of degrees of existence is closely related to the problem of modes of existence. This topic is based on the idea that some entities exist to a higher degree or have more being than other entities, similar to the way some properties, such as heat and mass, have degrees. According to philosopher Plato (428/427–348/347 BCE), for example, unchangeable [Platonic forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platonic_form "Platonic form") have a higher degree of existence than physical objects.[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-78)

The view that there are different types of entities is common in metaphysics but the idea that they differ from each other in their modes or degrees of existence is often rejected, implying that a thing either exists or does not exist without in-between alternatives.[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-79) Metaphysician [Peter van Inwagen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_van_Inwagen "Peter van Inwagen") (1942–present) uses the idea that there is an intimate relationship between existence and quantification to argue against different modes of existence. [Quantification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantification_(science) "Quantification (science)") is related to the counting of objects; according to Inwagen, if there were different modes of entities, people would need different types of numbers to count them. Because the same numbers can be used to count different types of entities, he concludes all entities have the same mode of existence.[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-80)

Theories of the nature of existence
-----------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Existence&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Theories of the nature of existence")]

[![Image 4: Mosaic depicting Pegasus](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Bellerophon_riding_Pegasus_and_killing_the_Chimera%2C_Roman_mosaic%2C_the_Rolin_Museum_in_Autun%2C_France%2C_2nd_to_3rd_century_AD.jpg/250px-Bellerophon_riding_Pegasus_and_killing_the_Chimera%2C_Roman_mosaic%2C_the_Rolin_Museum_in_Autun%2C_France%2C_2nd_to_3rd_century_AD.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Bellerophon_riding_Pegasus_and_killing_the_Chimera,_Roman_mosaic,_the_Rolin_Museum_in_Autun,_France,_2nd_to_3rd_century_AD.jpg)

One of the topics covered by theories of the nature of existence concerns the ontological status of fictional objects like [Pegasus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pegasus "Pegasus").[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-auto3-81)

Theories of the nature of existence aim to explain what it means for something to exist. A central dispute in the academic discourse about the nature of existence is whether existence is a [property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Property_(philosophy) "Property (philosophy)") of individuals.[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-auto2-82) An individual is a unique entity, like [Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socrates "Socrates") or a particular apple. A property is something that is attributed to an entity, like "being human" or "being red", and usually expresses a quality or feature of that entity.[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-83) The two main theories of existence are first-order and second-order theories. First-order theories understand existence as a property of individuals while second-order theories say existence is a second-order property; that is, a property of properties.[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-84)

A central challenge for theories of the nature of existence is an understanding of the possibility of coherently denying the existence of something, like the statement: "Santa Claus does not exist". One difficulty is explaining how the name "Santa Claus" can be meaningful even though there is no Santa Claus.[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-85)

### Second-order theories

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Existence&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Second-order theories")]

Second-order theories understand existence as a second-order property rather than a first-order property. They are often seen as the orthodox position in ontology.[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-86) For instance, the [Empire State Building](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empire_State_Building "Empire State Building") is an individual object and "being 443.2 meters (1,454 ft) tall" is a first-order property of it. "Being instantiated" is a property of "being 443.2 meters tall" and therefore a second-order property. According to second-order theories, to talk about existence is to talk about which properties have instances.[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-auto-87) For example, this view says that the sentence "God exists" means "Godhood is instantiated" rather than "God has the property of existing".[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-auto4-2)

A key reason against characterizing existence as a property of individuals is that existence differs from regular properties. Regular properties, such as _being a building_ and _being 443.2 meters tall_, express what an object is like but do not directly describe whether or not that building exists.[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-88) According to this view, existence is more fundamental than regular properties because an object cannot have any properties if it does not exist.[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-89)

According to second-order theorists, [quantifiers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantifier_(logic) "Quantifier (logic)") rather than [predicates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Predication_(philosophy) "Predication (philosophy)") express existence.[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-90) Predicates are expressions that apply to and classify objects, usually by attributing features to them, such as "is a butterfly" and "is happy".[[82]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-91) Quantifiers are terms that talk about the quantity of objects that have certain properties. [Existential quantifiers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existential_quantifier "Existential quantifier") express that there is at least one object, like the expressions "some" and "there exists", as in "some cows eat grass" and "there exists an even prime number".[[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-92) In this regard, existence is closely related to counting because to assert that something exists is to assert that the corresponding concept has one or more instances.[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-auto-87)

Second-order views imply a sentence like "[egg-laying mammals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egg-laying_mammals "Egg-laying mammals") exist" is misleading because the word "exist" is used as a predicate in them. These views say the true logical form is better expressed in reformulations like "there exist entities that are egg-laying mammals". This way, "existence" has the role of a quantifier and "egg-laying mammals" is the predicate. Quantifier constructions can also be used to express negative existential statements; for instance, the sentence "talking tigers do not exist" can be expressed as "it is not the case that there exist talking tigers".[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-93)

[![Image 5: Photo of Bertrand Russell](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Russell1907-2.jpg/250px-Russell1907-2.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Russell1907-2.jpg)

[Bertrand Russell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bertrand_Russell "Bertrand Russell") proposed his [theory of descriptions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theory_of_descriptions "Theory of descriptions") to dissolve paradoxes surrounding negative existential statements.

Many ontologists accept that second-order theories provide a correct analysis of many types of existential sentences. It is, however, controversial whether it is correct for all cases.[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-94) Some problems relate to assumptions associated with everyday language about sentences like "[Ronald McDonald](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ronald_McDonald "Ronald McDonald") does not exist". This type of statement is called _negative singular existential_ and the expression _Ronald McDonald_ is a [singular term](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Singular_term "Singular term") that seems to refer to an individual. It is not clear how the expression can refer to an individual if, as the sentence asserts, this individual does not exist. According to a solution philosopher [Bertrand Russell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bertrand_Russell "Bertrand Russell") (1872–1970) proposed, singular terms do not refer to individuals but are [descriptions of individuals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theory_of_descriptions "Theory of descriptions"). This theory states negative singular existentials deny an object matching the descriptions exists without referring to a nonexistent individual. Following this approach, the sentence "Ronald McDonald does not exist" expresses the idea: "it is not the case there is a unique happy hamburger clown".[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-95)

### First-order theories

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Existence&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: First-order theories")]

According to first-order theories, existence is a property of individuals. These theories are less-widely accepted than second-order theories but also have some influential proponents. There are two types of first-order theories: Meinongianism and universalism.[[87]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-96)

Meinongianism, which describes existence as a property of some but not all entities, was first formulated by [Alexius Meinong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexius_Meinong "Alexius Meinong"). Its main assertion is that there are some entities that do not exist, meaning [objecthood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Objecthood "Objecthood") is independent of existence. Proposed examples of nonexistent objects are merely possible objects such as flying pigs, as well as fictional and mythical objects like Sherlock Holmes and Zeus. According to this view, these objects are real and have being, even though they do not exist.[[88]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-97) Meinong states there is an object for any combination of properties. For example, there is an object that only has the single property of "being a singer" with no other properties. This means neither the attribute of "wearing a dress" nor the absence of it applies to this object. Meinong also includes impossible objects like round squares in this classification.[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-98)

[![Image 6: Photo of Alexius Meinong](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Alexius_Meinong_1900.jpg/250px-Alexius_Meinong_1900.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Alexius_Meinong_1900.jpg)

According to [Alexius Meinong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexius_Meinong "Alexius Meinong"), there are some entities that do not exist.

According to Meinongians, sentences describing Sherlock Holmes and Zeus refer to nonexisting objects. They are true or false depending on whether these objects have the properties ascribed to them.[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-99) For instance, the sentence "Pegasus has wings" is true because having wings is a property of Pegasus, even though Pegasus lacks the property of existing.[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-auto1-100)

One key motivation of Meinongianism is to explain how negative singular existentials like "Ronald McDonald does not exist" can be true. Meinongians accept the idea that singular terms like "Ronald McDonald" refer to individuals. For them, a negative singular existential is true if the individual it refers to does not exist.[[92]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-101)

Meinongianism has important implications for understandings of quantification. According to an influential view defended by [Willard Van Orman Quine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Willard_Van_Orman_Quine "Willard Van Orman Quine"), the domain of quantification is restricted to existing objects. This view implies quantifiers carry [ontological commitments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ontological_commitments "Ontological commitments") about what exists and what does not exist. Meinongianism differs from this view by saying the widest domain of quantification includes both existing and nonexisting objects.[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-auto3-81)

Some aspects of Meinongianism are controversial and have received substantial criticism. According to one objection, one cannot distinguish between being an object and being an existing object.[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-auto1-100) A closely related criticism states objects cannot have properties if they do not exist.[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-auto1-100) A further objection is that Meinongianism leads to an "overpopulated universe" because there is an object corresponding to any combination of properties.[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-auto3-81) A more specific criticism rejects the idea that there are incomplete and impossible objects.[[93]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-102)

Universalists agree with Meinongians that existence is a property of individuals but deny there are nonexistent entities. Instead, universalists state existence is a universal property; all entities have it, meaning everything exists. One approach is to say existence is the same as self-identity. According to the [law of identity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law_of_identity "Law of identity"), every object is identical to itself or has the property of self-identity. This can be expressed in [predicate logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Predicate_logic "Predicate logic") as ![Image 7: {\displaystyle \forall x(x=x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/85edab7d337e612666d6ac199171fe807f7da20a).[[94]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-auto5-103)

An influential argument in favor of universalism is that the denial of the existence of something is contradictory. This conclusion follows from the premises that one can only deny the existence of something by referring to that entity and that one can only refer to entities that exist.[[94]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-auto5-103)

Universalists have proposed different ways of interpreting negative singular existentials. According to one view, names of fictional entities like "Ronald McDonald" refer to [abstract objects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abstract_object "Abstract object"), which exist even though they do not exist in space and time. This means, when understood in a strict sense, all negative singular existentials are false, including the assertion that "Ronald McDonald does not exist". Universalists can interpret such sentences slightly differently in relation to the context. In everyday life, for example, people use sentences like "Ronald McDonald does not exist" to express the idea that Ronald McDonald does not exist as a concrete object, which is true.[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-104) Another approach is to understand negative singular existentials as neither true nor false but [meaningless](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meaning_(philosophy) "Meaning (philosophy)") because their singular terms do not refer to anything.[[96]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-105)

[Western philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_philosophy "Western philosophy") originated with the [Presocratic philosophers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Presocratic_philosophy "Presocratic philosophy"), who aimed to replace earlier [mythological accounts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_mythology "Greek mythology") of the universe by providing rational explanations based on foundational principles of all existence. Some, like [Thales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thales "Thales") (c. 624–545 BCE) and [Heraclitus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heraclitus "Heraclitus") (c. 540–480 BCE), suggested concrete principles like water and fire are the root of existence. [Anaximander](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anaximander "Anaximander") (c. 610–545 BCE) opposed this position; he believed the source must lie in an abstract principle that is beyond the world of human perception.[[97]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-106)

[![Image 8: Painting of Plato and Aristotle](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/%22The_School_of_Athens%22_by_Raffaello_Sanzio_da_Urbino_%28cropped%29.jpg/250px-%22The_School_of_Athens%22_by_Raffaello_Sanzio_da_Urbino_%28cropped%29.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:%22The_School_of_Athens%22_by_Raffaello_Sanzio_da_Urbino_(cropped).jpg)

[Plato](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plato "Plato") and his student [Aristotle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotle "Aristotle") disagreed on whether form and matter depend on one another for their existence.

[Plato](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plato "Plato") (428/427–348/347 BCE) argued that different types of entities have different degrees of existence and that shadows and images exist in a weaker sense than regular material objects. He said unchangeable [Platonic forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platonic_forms "Platonic forms") have the highest type of existence, and saw material objects as imperfect and impermanent copies of Platonic forms.[[98]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-107)

Philosopher [Aristotle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotle "Aristotle") (384–322 BCE) accepted Plato's idea that forms are different from matter, but he challenged the idea that forms have a higher type of existence. Instead, he believed forms cannot exist without matter.[[99]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-108) He stated: "being is said in many ways" and explored how different types of entities have different modes of existence. For example, he distinguished between [substances](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ousia "Ousia") and their [accidents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accident_(philosophy) "Accident (philosophy)"), and between [potentiality and actuality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potentiality_and_actuality "Potentiality and actuality").[[100]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-109)[[j]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-111)

[Neoplatonists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neoplatonists "Neoplatonists") like [Plotinus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plotinus "Plotinus") (204–270 CE) suggested reality has a hierarchical structure. They believed a transcendent entity, called "the One" or "the Good", is responsible for all existence. From it emerges the intellect, which in turn gives rise to the soul and the material world.[[102]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-112)

[![Image 9: Painting of Anselm of Canterbury](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Anselm_of_Canterbury2.png/250px-Anselm_of_Canterbury2.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Anselm_of_Canterbury2.png)

[Anselm of Canterbury](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anselm_of_Canterbury "Anselm of Canterbury") is known for his formulation of the ontological argument aiming to prove the existence of God.

In [medieval philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval_philosophy "Medieval philosophy"), [Anselm of Canterbury](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anselm_of_Canterbury "Anselm of Canterbury") (1033–1109 CE) formulated the influential [ontological argument](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ontological_argument "Ontological argument"), which aims to deduce the existence of God from the concept of God. Anselm defined God as the greatest conceivable being. He reasoned that an entity that did not exist outside his mind would not be the greatest conceivable being, leading him to the conclusion God exists.[[103]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-113)

[Thomas Aquinas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Aquinas "Thomas Aquinas") (1224–1274 CE) distinguished between the essence of a thing and its existence. According to him, the essence of a thing constitutes its fundamental nature. He argued it is possible to understand what an object is and grasp its essence, even if one does not know whether the object exists. He concluded from this observation that existence is not part of the qualities of an object and should be understood as a separate property.[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-auto2-82) Aquinas also considered the problem of [creation from nothing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Creation_from_nothing "Creation from nothing") and said only God has the power to truly bring new entities into existence. These ideas later inspired metaphysician [Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gottfried_Wilhelm_Leibniz "Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz")'s (1646–1716) theory of creation; Leibniz said to create is to confer actual existence to possible objects.[[104]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-114)

The philosophers [David Hume](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Hume "David Hume") (1711–1776) and [Immanuel Kant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immanuel_Kant "Immanuel Kant") (1724–1804) rejected the idea that existence is a property. According to Hume, objects are [bundles of qualities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bundle_theory "Bundle theory"). He said existence is not a property because there is no impression of existence besides the bundled qualities.[[105]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-115) Kant came to a similar conclusion in his criticism of the ontological argument; according to him, this proof fails because one cannot deduce from the definition of a concept whether entities described by this concept exist. Kant said existence does not add anything to the concept of the object; it only indicates this concept is exemplified.[[106]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-116) According to philosopher [Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georg_Wilhelm_Friedrich_Hegel "Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel") (1770–1831), there is no pure being or pure nothing, only becoming.[[107]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-117)

[![Image 10: Photo of Franz Brentano](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Franz_Brentano_in_Vienna%2C_1875.png/250px-Franz_Brentano_in_Vienna%2C_1875.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Franz_Brentano_in_Vienna,_1875.png)

[Franz Brentano](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Franz_Brentano "Franz Brentano") defended the idea that all judgments are existential judgments.

Philosopher and psychologist [Franz Brentano](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Franz_Brentano "Franz Brentano") (1838–1917) agreed with Kant's criticism and his position that existence is not a real predicate. Brentano used this idea to develop his theory of judgments, which states all judgments are existential judgments; they either affirm or deny the existence of something. He stated judgments like "some zebras are striped" have the logical form "there is a striped zebra" while judgments like "all zebras are striped" have the logical form "there is not a non-striped zebra".[[108]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-118)

[Gottlob Frege](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gottlob_Frege "Gottlob Frege") (1848–1925) and [Bertrand Russell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bertrand_Russell "Bertrand Russell") (1872–1970) aimed to refine the idea of what it means that existence is not a regular property. They distinguished between regular first-order properties of individuals and second-order properties of other properties. According to their view, existence is the second-order property of "being instantiated".[[109]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-119) Russell further developed the idea that general sentences like "lions exist" are at their most fundamental form about individuals by stating that there is an individual that is a lion.[[110]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-120)

Willard Van Orman Quine (1908–2000) followed Frege and Russell in accepting existence as a second-order property. He drew a close link between existence and the role of quantification in formal logic.[[111]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-121) He applied this idea to scientific theories and stated a scientific theory is committed to the existence of an entity if the theory quantifies over this entity. For example, if a theory in biology asserts that "there are populations with genetic diversity", this theory has an ontological commitment to the existence of populations with genetic diversity.[[112]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-122) Alexius Meinong (1853–1920) was an influential critic of second-order theories and developed the alternative view that existence is a property of individuals and that not all individuals have this property.[[113]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-123)

[![Image 11: Painting of Adi Shankara](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Raja_Ravi_Varma_-_Sankaracharya_-_cropped.png/250px-Raja_Ravi_Varma_-_Sankaracharya_-_cropped.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Raja_Ravi_Varma_-_Sankaracharya_-_cropped.png)

[Adi Shankara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adi_Shankara "Adi Shankara") taught that only the divine exists on the most fundamental level.

Many schools of thought in Eastern philosophy discuss the problem of existence and its implications. For instance, the ancient [Hindu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hindu_philosophy "Hindu philosophy") school of [Samkhya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samkhya "Samkhya") articulated a metaphysical dualism according to which the two types of existence are pure consciousness (_[Purusha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Purusha "Purusha")_) and matter (_[Prakriti](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prakriti "Prakriti")_). Samkhya explains the manifestation of the universe as the interaction between these two principles.[[114]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-124) The [Vedic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vedic "Vedic") philosopher [Adi Shankara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adi_Shankara "Adi Shankara") (c. 700–750 CE) developed a different approach in his school of [Advaita Vedanta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Advaita_Vedanta "Advaita Vedanta"). Shankara defended a metaphysical monism by defining the divine (_[Brahman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brahman "Brahman")_) as the ultimate reality and the only existent. According to this view, the impression that there is a universe consisting of many distinct entities is an illusion (_[Maya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maya\_(religion) "Maya (religion)")_).[[115]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-125) The essential features of ultimate reality are described as _[Sat Chit Ananda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sat\_Chit\_Ananda "Sat Chit Ananda")_—meaning existence, consciousness, and bliss.[[116]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-126)

A central doctrine in [Buddhist philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buddhist_philosophy "Buddhist philosophy") is called the "[three marks of existence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three_marks_of_existence "Three marks of existence")", which are _[aniccā](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anicca "Anicca")_ (impermanence), _[anattā](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anatt%C4%81 "Anattā")_ (absence of a permanent self), and _[dukkha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dukkha "Dukkha")_ (suffering). _Aniccā_ is the doctrine that all of existence is subject to change, meaning everything changes at some point and nothing lasts forever. _Anattā_ expresses a similar state in relation to persons by stating that people do not have a permanent identity or a separate self. Ignorance about _aniccā_ and _anattā_ is seen as the main cause of _dukkha_ by leading people to form attachments that cause suffering.[[117]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-127)

[![Image 12: Bust of Laozi](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Head_of_Laozi_marble_Tang_Dynasty_%28618-906_CE%29_Shaanxi_Province_China.jpg/250px-Head_of_Laozi_marble_Tang_Dynasty_%28618-906_CE%29_Shaanxi_Province_China.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Head_of_Laozi_marble_Tang_Dynasty_(618-906_CE)_Shaanxi_Province_China.jpg)

[Laozi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laozi "Laozi") saw [dao](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dao "Dao") as a fundamental principle that constitutes the root of all existence.

A central idea in many schools of [Chinese philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_philosophy "Chinese philosophy"), like [Laozi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laozi "Laozi")'s (6th century BCE) [Daoism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Daoism "Daoism"), is that a fundamental principle known as _[dao](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dao "Dao")_ is the source of all existence. The term is often translated as "the way" and is understood as a cosmic force that governs the natural order of the world. Chinese metaphysicians debated whether _dao_ is a form of being or whether, as the source of being, it belongs to non-being.[[118]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-128)

The concept of existence played a central role in [Arabic-Persian philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamic_philosophy "Islamic philosophy"). The Islamic philosophers [Avicenna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Avicenna "Avicenna") (980–1037 CE) and [Al-Ghazali](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Ghazali "Al-Ghazali") (1058–1111 CE) discussed the relationship between existence and essence, and said the essence of an entity is prior to its existence. The additional step of instantiating the essence is required for the entity to come into existence. Philosopher [Mulla Sadra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mulla_Sadra "Mulla Sadra") (1571–1636 CE) rejected this priority of essence over existence, and said essence is only a concept that is used by the mind to grasp existence. Existence, by contrast, encompasses the whole of reality, according to his view.[[119]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-129)

[Indigenous American philosophies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indigenous_American_philosophy "Indigenous American philosophy") tend to emphasize the interconnectedness of all existence and the importance of maintaining balance and harmony with nature. This is often combined with an animist outlook that ascribes a spiritual essence to some or all entities, including plants, rocks, and places.[[120]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-130)

The interest in the relational aspect of existence is also found in [African philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/African_philosophy "African philosophy"), which explores how all entities are causally linked to form an ordered world. African philosophy also examines the idea of an underlying and all-pervading life force responsible for animating entities and their influence on each other.[[121]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-131)

In various disciplines
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Existence&action=edit&section=18 "Edit section: In various disciplines")]

Formal logic studies [deductively valid arguments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deductive_validity "Deductive validity").[[122]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-132) In [first-order logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First-order_logic "First-order logic"), which is the most-commonly used system of formal logic, existence is expressed using the [existential quantifier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existential_quantifier "Existential quantifier") (![Image 13: {\displaystyle \exists }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/77ed842b6b90b2fdd825320cf8e5265fa937b583)). For example, the formula ![Image 14: {\displaystyle \exists x{\text{Horse}}(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c7be4112f83da1be39b64751b598c22b08c7065d) can be used to state horses exist. The variable _x_ ranges over all elements in the [domain of quantification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_of_discourse "Domain of discourse") and the existential quantifier expresses that at least one element in this domain is a horse. In first-order logic, all singular terms like names refer to objects in the domain and imply the object exists. Because of this, one can deduce ![Image 15: {\displaystyle \exists x{\text{Honest}}(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/11935d85db817e7aacd4836e84b5add385cb20ea) (someone is honest) from ![Image 16: {\displaystyle {\text{Honest}}(Bill)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/76888a1c217536ed3ebd6544bad04db20010a04b) (Bill is honest).[[123]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-133) If only one object matching the description exists, the [unique existential quantifier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uniqueness_quantification "Uniqueness quantification")![Image 17: {\displaystyle \exists !}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3aab884c076b7332eab3860c2f32086012df840b) can be used.[[124]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-134)

Many logical systems that are based on first-order logic also follow this idea. [Free logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_logic "Free logic") is an exception because it allows the presence of empty names that do not refer to an object in the domain.[[125]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-135) With this modification, it is possible to apply [logical reasoning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logical_reasoning "Logical reasoning") to fictional objects instead of limiting it to regular objects.[[126]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-136) In free logic one can express that Pegasus is a flying horse using the formula ![Image 18: {\displaystyle {\text{Flyinghorse}}(Pegasus)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1cd75e11a9fb2a7593c982312821e52d8e0c99ac). As a consequence of this modification, one cannot infer from this type of statement that something exists. This means the inference from ![Image 19: {\displaystyle {\text{Flyinghorse}}(Pegasus)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1cd75e11a9fb2a7593c982312821e52d8e0c99ac) to ![Image 20: {\displaystyle \exists x{\text{Flyinghorse}}(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1b82181a318e83198edce46b6618ed4167036a25) is invalid in free logic, even though it is valid in first-order logic. Free logic uses an additional existence predicate (![Image 21: {\displaystyle E!}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0019d83f380ffc8a76e6711e68801cd9cfb2f81e)) to say a singular term refers to an existing object. For example, the formula ![Image 22: {\displaystyle E!(Homer)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c0008591d044a6709e0bb6f281c1aeede303263b) can be used to say [Homer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homer "Homer") exists while the formula ![Image 23: {\displaystyle \lnot E!(Pegasus)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/67e69256aa87ed51416b726dfe62bde06567d1b4) states Pegasus does not exist.[[127]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-137)

The disciplines of [epistemology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epistemology "Epistemology"), [philosophy of mind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophy_of_mind "Philosophy of mind"), and [philosophy of language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophy_of_language "Philosophy of language") deal with [mental](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mental_representation "Mental representation") and linguistic representations in their attempt to understand the nature of knowledge, the mind, and language. This brings with it the problem of reference or how representations can refer to existing objects. Examples of such representations are beliefs, thoughts, perceptions, words, and sentences. For instance, in the sentence "Barack Obama is a Democrat", the name "Barack Obama" refers to a particular individual. The problem of reference also affects the epistemology of perception. In particular, this concerns the problem of whether perceptual impressions establish a direct contact with reality.[[128]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-138)

Closely related to the problem of reference is the relationship between true representations and existence. According to [truthmaker theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Truthmaker_theory "Truthmaker theory"), true representations require a truthmaker, i.e., an entity whose existence is responsible for the representation being true. For example, the sentence "kangaroos live in Australia" is true because there are kangaroos in Australia; the existence of these kangaroos is the truthmaker of the sentence. Truthmaker theory states there is a close relationship between truth and existence; there exists a truthmaker for every true representation.[[129]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-139)

Many of the individual sciences are concerned with the existence of particular types of entities and the laws governing them, such as physical things in physics and living entities in biology.[[130]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-140) The [natural sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_science "Natural science") employ a great variety of concepts to classify entities; these are known as [natural kinds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_kinds "Natural kinds"), and include categories like protons, gold, and elephants. According to [scientific realists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_realism "Scientific realism"), these entities have mind-independent being; [scientific anti-realists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_anti-realism "Scientific anti-realism") say the existence of these entities and categories is based on human perceptions, theories, and social constructs.[[131]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-141)

A similar problem concerns the existence of social kinds, which are basic concepts used in the [social sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_sciences "Social sciences"), such as race, gender, disability, money, and nation state.[[132]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-142) Social kinds are often understood as social constructions that, while useful for describing the complexities of human social life, do not form part of objective reality on the most fundamental level.[[133]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-143) According to the controversial [Sapir–Whorf hypothesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sapir%E2%80%93Whorf_hypothesis "Sapir–Whorf hypothesis"), the social institution of language influences or fully determines how people perceive and understand the world.[[134]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-144)

[Existentialism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existentialism "Existentialism") is a school of thought that explores the nature of human existence. Among its key ideas is that [existence precedes essence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence_precedes_essence "Existence precedes essence"). This means that existence is more basic than essence. As a result, the nature and purpose of human beings are not pre-existing but develop in the process of living. According to this view, humans are thrown into a world that lacks pre-existing intrinsic meaning. They must determine for themselves their purpose and what [meaning their life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meaning_of_life "Meaning of life") should have. Existentialists use this idea to explore the role of freedom and responsibility in actively shaping one's life.[[135]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-145)[Feminist existentialists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feminist_existentialism "Feminist existentialism") investigate the effects of [gender](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gender "Gender") on human existence, for example, on the experience of freedom.[[136]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-146) Influential existentialists include [Søren Kierkegaard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%C3%B8ren_Kierkegaard "Søren Kierkegaard") (1813–1855), [Friedrich Nietzsche](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Nietzsche "Friedrich Nietzsche") (1844–1900), [Jean-Paul Sartre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean-Paul_Sartre "Jean-Paul Sartre") (1905–1980), and [Simone de Beauvoir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simone_de_Beauvoir "Simone de Beauvoir") (1908–1986).[[137]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-147) Existentialism has influenced reflections on the role of human existence in [sociology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sociology "Sociology"). Existentialist sociology examines the ways humans experience the social world and [construct reality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_constructionism "Social constructionism").[[138]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-148) Existence theory is a relatively recent approach that focuses on the temporal aspect of existence in society. It explores how the existential milestones to which people aspire influence their lives.[[139]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-149)

Mathematicians are often interested in the existence of certain [mathematical objects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_object "Mathematical object").[[140]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-150) For example, number theorists ask how many [prime numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_number "Prime number") exist within a certain interval.[[141]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-151) The statement that at least one mathematical object matching a certain description exists is called an [existence theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence_theorem "Existence theorem").[[142]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-152) Metaphysicians of mathematics investigate whether mathematical objects exist not only in relation to mathematical [axioms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiom "Axiom") but also as part of the fundamental structure of reality. This position is affirmed by [Platonists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platonists "Platonists"), while [nominalists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nominalists "Nominalists") believe mathematical objects lack a more-substantial form of existence, for instance, because they are merely useful fictions.[[143]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-153)

Many debates in theology revolve around the existence of the divine, and arguments have been presented for and against God's existence. [Cosmological arguments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmological_argument "Cosmological argument") state that God must exist as the first cause to explain facts about the existence and aspects of the universe.[[144]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-154) According to [teleological arguments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teleological_argument "Teleological argument"), the only way to explain the order and complexity of the universe and human life is by reference to God as the [intelligent designer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intelligent_design "Intelligent design").[[145]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-155) An influential argument against the existence of God relies on [the problem of evil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_problem_of_evil "The problem of evil") since it is not clear how evil could exist if there was an all-powerful, all-knowing, and benevolent God.[[146]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-156) Another argument points to a lack of concrete evidence for God's existence.[[147]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence#cite_note-157)
