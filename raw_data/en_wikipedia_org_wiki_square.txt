In [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry"), a **square** is a [regular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polygon "Regular polygon")[quadrilateral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral "Quadrilateral"). It has four straight sides of equal length and four equal [angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle "Angle"). Squares are special cases of [rectangles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle "Rectangle"), which have four equal angles, and of [rhombuses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhombus "Rhombus"), which have four equal sides. As with all rectangles, a square's angles are [right angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_angle "Right angle") (90 [degrees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_(angle) "Degree (angle)"), or [π](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi "Pi")/2 [radians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radian "Radian")), making adjacent sides [perpendicular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular "Perpendicular"). The [area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area "Area") of a square is the side length multiplied by itself, and so in [algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra "Algebra"), multiplying a number by itself is called [squaring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_(algebra) "Square (algebra)").

Equal squares can tile the plane edge-to-edge in the [square tiling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_tiling "Square tiling"). Square tilings are ubiquitous in [tiled](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tile "Tile") floors and walls, [graph paper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_paper "Graph paper"), image [pixels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pixel "Pixel"), and [game boards](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Game_board "Game board"). Square shapes are also often seen in building [floor plans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Floor_plan "Floor plan"), [origami paper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Origami_paper "Origami paper"), food servings, in [graphic design](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graphic_design "Graphic design") and [heraldry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heraldry "Heraldry"), and in instant photos and fine art.

The formula for the area of a square forms the basis of the calculation of area and motivates the search for methods for [squaring the circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Squaring_the_circle "Squaring the circle") by [compass and straightedge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compass_and_straightedge "Compass and straightedge"), now known to be impossible. Squares can be inscribed in any smooth or convex curve such as a circle or triangle, but it remains unsolved [whether a square can be inscribed in every simple closed curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inscribed_square_problem "Inscribed square problem"). Several problems of [squaring the square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Squaring_the_square "Squaring the square") involve subdividing squares into unequal squares. Mathematicians have also studied packing squares as tightly as possible into other shapes.

Squares can be constructed by [straightedge and compass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Straightedge_and_compass "Straightedge and compass"), through their [Cartesian coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinates "Cartesian coordinates"), or by repeated multiplication by ![Image 2: {\displaystyle i}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/add78d8608ad86e54951b8c8bd6c8d8416533d20) in the [complex plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_plane "Complex plane"). They form the [metric balls](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ball_(mathematics) "Ball (mathematics)") for [taxicab geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taxicab_geometry "Taxicab geometry") and [Chebyshev distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chebyshev_distance "Chebyshev distance"), two forms of non-Euclidean geometry. Although [spherical geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_geometry "Spherical geometry") and [hyperbolic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_geometry "Hyperbolic geometry") both lack [polygons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon "Polygon") with four equal sides and right angles, they have square-like regular polygons with four sides and other angles, or with right angles and different numbers of sides.

Definitions and characterizations
---------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Square&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Definitions and characterizations")]

[![Image 3](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/The_square_among_a_family_of_rectangles_or_rhombuses.png/330px-The_square_among_a_family_of_rectangles_or_rhombuses.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:The_square_among_a_family_of_rectangles_or_rhombuses.png)

Among rectangles (top row), the square is the shape with equal sides (blue, middle). Among rhombuses (bottom row), the square is the shape with right angles (blue, middle).

Squares can be defined or characterized in many equivalent ways. If a [polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon "Polygon") in the [Euclidean plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_plane "Euclidean plane") satisfies any one of the following criteria, it satisfies all of them:

Squares are the only [regular polygons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polygon "Regular polygon") whose [internal angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internal_and_external_angle "Internal and external angle"), [central angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_angle#Central_angle_of_a_regular_polygon "Central angle"), and [external angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internal_and_external_angle "Internal and external angle") are all equal (they are all right angles).[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-rich-4)

A square is a special case of a [rhombus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhombus "Rhombus") (equal sides, opposite equal angles), a [kite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kite_(geometry) "Kite (geometry)") (two pairs of adjacent equal sides), a [trapezoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid "Trapezoid") (one pair of opposite sides parallel), a [parallelogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram "Parallelogram") (all opposite sides parallel), a [quadrilateral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral "Quadrilateral") or tetragon (four-sided polygon), and a [rectangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle "Rectangle") (opposite sides equal, right-angles),[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-zalgri-1) and therefore has all the properties of all these shapes, namely:

*   All four internal angles of a square are equal (each being 90°, a right angle).[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-rich-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-godsid-5)
*   The central angle of a square is equal to 90°.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-rich-4)
*   The external angle of a square is equal to 90°.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-rich-4)
*   The diagonals of a square are equal and [bisect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bisection "Bisection") each other, meeting at 90°.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-godsid-5)
*   The diagonals of a square bisect its internal angles, forming [adjacent angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle#adjacent "Angle") of 45°.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-6)
*   All four sides of a square are equal.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGodfreySiddons1919135-7)
*   Opposite sides of a square are [parallel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_(geometry) "Parallel (geometry)").[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchorlingClarkCarter1935101-8)

All squares are [similar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Similarity_(geometry) "Similarity (geometry)") to each other, meaning they have the same shape.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-9) One parameter (typically the length of a side or diagonal)[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-10) suffices to specify a square's size. Squares of the same size are [congruent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congruence_(geometry) "Congruence (geometry)").[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-11)

The area of a square is the product of the lengths of its sides.

A square whose four sides have length ![Image 6: {\displaystyle \ell }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f066e981e530bacc07efc6a10fa82deee985929e) has [perimeter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perimeter "Perimeter")[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-FOOTNOTERich1963[httpsarchiveorgdetailsinernetdli2015131938pagen147_131]-12)![Image 7: {\displaystyle P=4\ell }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d009f88863d47239430002ef235d8b3d8e603f57) and [diagonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diagonal "Diagonal") length ![Image 8: {\displaystyle d={\sqrt {2}}\ell }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7f40925a7920f169927c5c417f05ca550cce3dea).[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-FOOTNOTERich1963[httpsarchiveorgdetailsinernetdli2015131938pagen135_120]-13) The [square root of 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_root_of_2 "Square root of 2"), appearing in this formula, is [irrational](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irrational_number "Irrational number"), meaning that it cannot be written exactly as a [fraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fraction "Fraction"). It is approximately equal to 1.414,[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-14) and its approximate value was already known in [Babylonian mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Babylonian_mathematics "Babylonian mathematics").[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-15) A square's [area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area "Area") is[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-FOOTNOTERich1963[httpsarchiveorgdetailsinernetdli2015131938pagen135_120]-13)![Image 9: {\displaystyle A=\ell ^{2}={\tfrac {1}{2}}d^{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e9b2898b5ce48dff04a24144f997c5e0d9fe0c0e) This formula for the area of a square as the second power of its side length led to the use of the term _[squaring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square\_(algebra) "Square (algebra)")_ to mean raising any number to the second power.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-16) Reversing this relation, the side length of a square of a given area is the [square root](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_root "Square root") of the area. Squaring an [integer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer"), or taking the area of a square with integer sides, results in a [square number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_number "Square number"); these are [figurate numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Figurate_number "Figurate number") representing the numbers of points that can be arranged into a square grid.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-FOOTNOTEConwayGuy199630%E2%80%9333,_38%E2%80%9340-17)

Since four squared equals sixteen, a four by four square has an area equal to its perimeter. That is, it is an [equable shape](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equable_shape "Equable shape"). The only other equable integer rectangle is a three by six rectangle.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-18)

Because it is a [regular polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polygon "Regular polygon"), a square is the quadrilateral of least perimeter enclosing a given area. Dually, a square is the quadrilateral containing the largest area within a given perimeter.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-19) Indeed, if _A_ and _P_ are the area and perimeter enclosed by a quadrilateral, then the following [isoperimetric inequality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isoperimetric_inequality "Isoperimetric inequality") holds: ![Image 10: {\displaystyle 16A\leq P^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cd38306aee3d076f8ac44eaa889716b73d242b28) with equality if and only if the quadrilateral is a square.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-20)[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlsinaNelsen2020187Theorem_9.2.2-21)

The square is the most symmetrical of the quadrilaterals.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-berger-22) Eight [rigid transformations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rigid_transformation "Rigid transformation") of the plane take the square to itself:[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-miller-23)

The axes of reflection symmetry and centers of rotation symmetry of a square (top), rectangle and rhombus (center), [isosceles trapezoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isosceles_trapezoid "Isosceles trapezoid"), kite, and parallelogram (bottom)

For an axis-parallel square centered at the [origin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Origin_(mathematics) "Origin (mathematics)"), each symmetry acts by a combination of negating and swapping the [Cartesian coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate "Cartesian coordinate") of points.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-ers-24) The symmetries permute the eight isosceles triangles between the half-edges and the square's center (which stays in place); any of these triangles can be taken as the [fundamental region](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_region "Fundamental region") of the transformations.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-25) Each two vertices, each two edges, and each two half-edges are mapped one to the other by at least one symmetry (exactly one for half-edges).[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-berger-22) All [regular polygons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polygon "Regular polygon") also have these properties,[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-26) which are expressed by saying that symmetries of a square and, more generally, a regular polygon act [transitively](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transitive_action "Transitive action") on vertices and edges, and [simply transitively](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simply_transitively "Simply transitively") on half-edges.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-27)

Combining any two of these transformations by performing one after the other continues to take the square to itself, and therefore produces another symmetry. Repeated rotation produces another rotation with the summed rotation angle. Two reflections with the same axis return to the identity transformation, while two reflections with different axes rotate the square. A rotation followed by a reflection, or vice versa, produces a different reflection. This [composition operation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_composition "Function composition") gives the eight symmetries of a square the mathematical structure of a [group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_(mathematics) "Group (mathematics)"), called the _group of the square_ or the _[dihedral group of order eight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihedral\_group\_of\_order\_8 "Dihedral group of order 8")_.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-miller-23) Other quadrilaterals, like the rectangle and rhombus, have only a [subgroup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subgroup "Subgroup") of these symmetries.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-28)[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-29)

[![Image 20](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Perspective-3point.svg/250px-Perspective-3point.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Perspective-3point.svg)

Three-point perspective of a cube, showing perspective transformations of its six square faces into six different quadrilaterals

The shape of a square, but not its size, is preserved by [similarities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Similarity_(geometry) "Similarity (geometry)") of the plane.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-30) Other kinds of transformations of the plane can take squares to other kinds of quadrilateral. An [affine transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_transformation "Affine transformation") can take a square to any parallelogram, or vice versa;[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-31) a [projective transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_transformation "Projective transformation") can take a square to any convex [quadrilateral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral "Quadrilateral"), or vice versa.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-32) This implies that, when [viewed in perspective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perspective_(graphical) "Perspective (graphical)"), a square can look like any convex quadrilateral, or vice versa.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-33) A [Möbius transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M%C3%B6bius_transformation "Möbius transformation") can take the vertices of a square (but not its edges) to the vertices of a [harmonic quadrilateral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harmonic_quadrilateral "Harmonic quadrilateral").[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-34)

The [wallpaper groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wallpaper_group "Wallpaper group") are symmetry groups of two-dimensional repeating patterns. For many of these groups the basic unit of repetition (the unit cell of its [period lattice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Period_lattice "Period lattice")) can be a square, and for three of these groups, p4, p4m, and p4g, it must be a square.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-35)

### Inscribed and circumscribed circles

The [inscribed circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inscribed_circle "Inscribed circle") of a square is the largest circle that can fit inside that square. Squares are so well-established as the shape of [tiles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tiles "Tiles") that the [Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin_language "Latin language") word [tessera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tessera "Tessera"), for a small tile as used in [mosaics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mosaic "Mosaic"), comes from an ancient Greek word for the number four, referring to the four corners of a square tile.[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-40)[Graph paper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_paper "Graph paper"), preprinted with a [square tiling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_tiling "Square tiling"), is widely used for [data visualization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_visualization "Data visualization") using [Cartesian coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinates "Cartesian coordinates").[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-41) The [pixels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pixel "Pixel") of [bitmap images](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitmap_image "Bitmap image"), as recorded by [image scanners](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Image_scanner "Image scanner") and [digital cameras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digital_camera "Digital camera") or displayed on [electronic visual displays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronic_visual_display "Electronic visual display"), conventionally lie at the intersections of a square grid, and are often considered as small squares, arranged in a square tiling.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-42)[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-43) Standard techniques for [image compression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Image_compression "Image compression") and [video compression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Video_compression "Video compression"), including the [JPEG](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/JPEG "JPEG") format, are based on the subdivision of images into larger square blocks of pixels.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-44) The [quadtree](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadtree "Quadtree") data structure used in data compression and [computational geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computational_geometry "Computational geometry") is based on the [recursive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recursive "Recursive") subdivision of squares into smaller squares.[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-45)

Architectural structures from both ancient and modern cultures have featured a square floor plan, base, or footprint. Ancient examples include the [Egyptian pyramids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egyptian_pyramids "Egyptian pyramids"),[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-46)[Mesoamerican pyramids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mesoamerican_pyramids "Mesoamerican pyramids") such as those at [Teotihuacan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teotihuacan "Teotihuacan"),[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-47) the [Chogha Zanbil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chogha_Zanbil "Chogha Zanbil") ziggurat in Iran,[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-48) the four-fold design of Persian walled gardens, said to model the four rivers of Paradise, and later structures inspired by their design such as the [Taj Mahal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taj_Mahal "Taj Mahal") in India,[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-49) the square bases of Buddhist [stupas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stupa "Stupa"),[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-50) and East Asian [pagodas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pagoda "Pagoda"), buildings that symbolically face to the four points of the compass and reach to the heavens.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-51) Norman [keeps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Keep "Keep") such as the [Tower of London](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tower_of_London "Tower of London") often take the form of a low square tower.[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-52) In modern architecture, a majority of [skyscrapers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skyscraper "Skyscraper") feature a square plan for pragmatic rather than aesthetic or symbolic reasons.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-53)

A [unit square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_square "Unit square") is a square of side length one. Often it is represented in [Cartesian coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate "Cartesian coordinate") as the square enclosing the points ![Image 60: {\displaystyle (x,y)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/41cf50e4a314ca8e2c30964baa8d26e5be7a9386) that have ![Image 61: {\displaystyle 0\leq x\leq 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/30810e06ad49f3a837bd2193d4392eda1f74e7ab) and ![Image 62: {\displaystyle 0\leq y\leq 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/877a791a23880968c5e285359bbf496b626a4e20). Its vertices are the four points that have 0 or 1 in each of their coordinates.[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-84)

### Compass and straightedge

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Square&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Compass and straightedge")]

The construction of a square with a given side, using a [compass and straightedge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compass_and_straightedge_constructions "Compass and straightedge constructions"), is given in [Euclid's _Elements_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid%27s_Elements "Euclid's Elements") I.46.[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-89) The existence of this construction means that squares are [constructible polygons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constructible_polygon "Constructible polygon"). A regular ![Image 96: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)-gon is constructible exactly when the odd [prime factors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_factor "Prime factor") of ![Image 97: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) are distinct [Fermat primes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermat_prime "Fermat prime"),[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-90) and in the case of a square ![Image 98: {\displaystyle n=4}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d928ec15aeef83aade867992ee473933adb6139d) has no odd prime factors so this condition is [vacuously true](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vacuously_true "Vacuously true").[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-91)

_Elements_ IV.6–7 also give constructions for a square inscribed in a circle and circumscribed about a circle, respectively.[[92]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-92)

higher dimensions.[[96]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-FOOTNOTECoxeter1948121%E2%80%93122-96) The cube and hypercubes can be given vertex coordinates that are all ![Image 105: {\displaystyle \pm 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0bfeaa85da53ad1947d8000926cfea33827ef1e0), giving an axis-parallel square in two dimensions, while the octahedron and cross-polytopes have one coordinate ![Image 106: {\displaystyle \pm 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0bfeaa85da53ad1947d8000926cfea33827ef1e0) and the rest zero, giving a The [Sierpiński carpet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sierpi%C5%84ski_carpet "Sierpiński carpet") is a square [fractal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal "Fractal"), with square holes.[[98]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-98)[Space-filling curves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space-filling_curve "Space-filling curve") including the [Hilbert curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_curve "Hilbert curve"), [Peano curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peano_curve "Peano curve"), and [Sierpiński curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sierpi%C5%84ski_curve "Sierpiński curve") cover a square as the continuous image of a line segment.[[99]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-99) The [Z-order curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Z-order_curve "Z-order curve") is analogous but not continuous.[[100]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-100) Other mathematical functions associated with squares include [Arnold's cat map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arnold%27s_cat_map "Arnold's cat map") and the [baker's map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baker%27s_map "Baker's map"), which generate chaotic [dynamical systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynamical_system "Dynamical system") on a square,[[101]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-101) and the [lemniscate elliptic functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lemniscate_elliptic_functions "Lemniscate elliptic functions"), complex functions periodic on a square grid.[[102]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-102)

[![Image 107](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Calabi_triangle.svg/250px-Calabi_triangle.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Calabi_triangle.svg)

The [Calabi triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calabi_triangle "Calabi triangle") and the three placements of its largest square.[[103]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-FOOTNOTEConwayGuy1996[httpsbooksgooglecombooksid0--3rcO7dMYCpgPA206_206]-103) The placement on the long side of the triangle is inscribed; the other two are not.

A square is [inscribed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inscribed_figure "Inscribed figure") in a curve when all four vertices of the square lie on the curve. The unsolved [inscribed square problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inscribed_square_problem "Inscribed square problem") asks whether every [simple closed curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simple_closed_curve "Simple closed curve") has an inscribed square. It is true for every [smooth curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smooth_curve "Smooth curve"),[[104]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-104) and for any closed [convex curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_curve "Convex curve"). The only other regular polygon that can always be inscribed in every closed convex curve is the [equilateral triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equilateral_triangle "Equilateral triangle"), as there exists a convex curve on which no other regular polygon can be inscribed.[[105]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-105)

For an [inscribed square in a triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inscribed_square_in_a_triangle "Inscribed square in a triangle"), at least one side of the square lies on a side of the triangle. Every [acute triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acute_triangle "Acute triangle") has three inscribed squares, one for each of its three sides. A [right triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_triangle "Right triangle") has two inscribed squares, one touching its right angle and the other lying on its hypotenuse. An [obtuse triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_triangle "Obtuse triangle") has only one inscribed square, on its longest side. A square inscribed in a triangle can cover at most half the triangle's area.[[106]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-gardner-106)

### Area and quadrature

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Square&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Area and quadrature")]

[![Image 108](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Pythagorean.svg/250px-Pythagorean.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Pythagorean.svg)

The [Pythagorean theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagorean_theorem "Pythagorean theorem"): the two smaller squares on the sides of a right triangle have equal total area to the larger square on the hypotenuse.

[![Image 109](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Squaring_the_Circle_J.svg/150px-Squaring_the_Circle_J.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Squaring_the_Circle_J.svg)

A circle and square with the same area

Since ancient times, many units for surface [area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area "Area") have been defined from squares, typically with a standard unit of [length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Length "Length") as its side, for example a [square meter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_meter "Square meter") or [square inch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_inch "Square inch").[[107]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-107)

In [ancient Greek deductive geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_geometry "Euclidean geometry"), the area of a planar shape was measured and compared by constructing a square with the same area by using only a finite number of steps with [compass and straightedge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compass_and_straightedge "Compass and straightedge"), a process called _[quadrature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrature\_(geometry) "Quadrature (geometry)")_ or _squaring_. [Euclid's _Elements_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid%27s_Elements "Euclid's Elements") shows how to do this for rectangles, parallelograms, triangles, and then more generally for [simple polygons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simple_polygon "Simple polygon") by breaking them into triangular pieces.[[108]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-108) Some shapes with curved sides could also be squared, such as the [lune of Hippocrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lune_of_Hippocrates "Lune of Hippocrates")[[109]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-109) and the [parabola](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrature_of_the_Parabola "Quadrature of the Parabola").[[110]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-110)

This use of a square as the defining shape for area measurement also occurs in the Greek formulation of the [Pythagorean theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagorean_theorem "Pythagorean theorem"): squares constructed on the two sides of a [right triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_triangle "Right triangle") have equal total area to a square constructed on the [hypotenuse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypotenuse "Hypotenuse").[[111]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-111) Stated in this form, the theorem would be equally valid for other shapes on the sides of the triangle, such as equilateral triangles or semicircles,[[112]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-112) but the Greeks used squares. In modern mathematics, this formulation of the theorem using areas of squares has been replaced by an algebraic formulation involving [squaring numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_(algebra) "Square (algebra)"): the lengths of the sides and hypotenuse of the right triangle obey the equation ![Image 110: {\displaystyle a^{2}+b^{2}=c^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7ef0a5a4b8ab98870ae5d6d7c7b4dfe3fb6612e2).[[113]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-113)

Because of this focus on quadrature as a measure of area, the Greeks and later mathematicians sought unsuccessfully to [square the circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Squaring_the_circle "Squaring the circle"), constructing a square with the same area as a given circle, again using finitely many steps with a compass and straightedge. In 1882, the task was proven to be impossible as a consequence of the [Lindemann–Weierstrass theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lindemann%E2%80%93Weierstrass_theorem "Lindemann–Weierstrass theorem"). This theorem proves that [pi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi "Pi") (π) is a [transcendental number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transcendental_number "Transcendental number") rather than an [algebraic irrational number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_number "Algebraic number"); that is, it is not the [root](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Root_of_a_function "Root of a function") of any [polynomial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial "Polynomial") with [rational](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_number "Rational number") coefficients. A construction for squaring the circle could be translated into a polynomial formula for π, which does not exist.[[114]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-114) In philosophy, the concept of a "square circle" has been used as an example of an [oxymoron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxymoron "Oxymoron") since [Aristotle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotle "Aristotle"), sparking attempts to find contexts such as [taxicab geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taxicab_geometry "Taxicab geometry") ([below](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#Other_geometries)) in which this phrase is meaningful.[[115]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-115)

[![Image 111](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Tiling_Regular_4-4_Square.svg/250px-Tiling_Regular_4-4_Square.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Tiling_Regular_4-4_Square.svg)

[![Image 112](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Academ_Periodic_tiling_by_squares_of_two_different_sizes.svg/250px-Academ_Periodic_tiling_by_squares_of_two_different_sizes.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Academ_Periodic_tiling_by_squares_of_two_different_sizes.svg)

The [square tiling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_tiling "Square tiling"), familiar from flooring and game boards, is one of three [regular tilings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tiling_by_regular_polygons "Tiling by regular polygons") of the plane. The other two use the [equilateral triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equilateral_triangle "Equilateral triangle") and the [regular hexagon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_hexagon "Regular hexagon").[[116]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-116) The vertices of a square tiling form a [square lattice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_lattice "Square lattice").[[117]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGr%C3%BCnbaumShephard198729-117) Squares of more than one size can also tile the plane,[[118]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGr%C3%BCnbaumShephard198776%E2%80%9378-118)[[119]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-119) for instance in the [Pythagorean tiling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagorean_tiling "Pythagorean tiling"), named for its connection to proofs of the [Pythagorean theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagorean_theorem "Pythagorean theorem").[[120]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-120)

[![Image 113](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Packing_11_unit_squares_in_a_square_with_side_length_3.87708359....svg/250px-Packing_11_unit_squares_in_a_square_with_side_length_3.87708359....svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Packing_11_unit_squares_in_a_square_with_side_length_3.87708359....svg)

The smallest known square that can contain 11 unit squares has side length approximately 3.877084.[[121]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-friedman-121)

[Square packing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_packing "Square packing") problems seek the smallest square or circle into which a given number of [unit squares](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_square "Unit square") can fit. A chessboard optimally packs a square number of unit squares into a larger square, but beyond a few special cases such as this, the optimal solutions to these problems remain unsolved;[[121]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-friedman-121)[[122]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-122)[[123]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-123) the same is true for [circle packing in a square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle_packing_in_a_square "Circle packing in a square").[[124]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-124) Packing squares into other shapes can have high [computational complexity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computational_complexity "Computational complexity"): testing whether a given number of unit squares can fit into an [orthogonally convex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_convexity "Orthogonal convexity")[rectilinear polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectilinear_polygon "Rectilinear polygon") with [half-integer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Half-integer "Half-integer") vertex coordinates is [NP-complete](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NP-complete "NP-complete").[[125]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-125)

[Squaring the square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Squaring_the_square "Squaring the square") involves subdividing a given square into smaller squares, all having integer side lengths. A subdivision with distinct smaller squares is called a perfect squared square.[[126]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-126) Another variant of squaring the square called "Mrs. Perkins's quilt" allows repetitions, but uses as few smaller squares as possible in order to make the [greatest common divisor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greatest_common_divisor "Greatest common divisor") of the side lengths be 1.[[127]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-127) The entire plane can be tiled by squares, with exactly one square of each integer side length.[[128]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-128)

[![Image 115](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Petrie-1.gif/143px-Petrie-1.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Petrie-1.gif)

[![Image 116](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Teabag.jpg/250px-Teabag.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Teabag.jpg)

Numerical simulation of an inflated square pillow

In higher dimensions, other surfaces than the plane can be tiled by equal squares, meeting edge-to-edge. One of these surfaces is the [Clifford torus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_torus "Clifford torus"), the four-dimensional [Cartesian product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_product "Cartesian product") of two congruent circles; it has the same intrinsic geometry as a single square with each pair of opposite edges glued together.[[129]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-129) Another square-tiled surface, a [regular skew apeirohedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_skew_apeirohedron "Regular skew apeirohedron") in three dimensions, has six squares meeting at each vertex.[[130]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-130) The [paper bag problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paper_bag_problem "Paper bag problem") seeks the maximum volume that can be enclosed by a surface tiled with two squares glued edge to edge; its exact answer is unknown.[[131]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-131) Gluing two squares in a different pattern, with the vertex of each square attached to the midpoint of an edge of the other square (or alternatively subdividing these two squares into eight squares glued edge-to-edge) produces a pincushion shape called a [biscornu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biscornu "Biscornu").[[132]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-132)

[![Image 117](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Two_square_counting_puzzles.svg/330px-Two_square_counting_puzzles.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Two_square_counting_puzzles.svg)

Two square-counting puzzles: There are 14 squares in a 3 × 3 grid of squares (top), but as a 4 × 4 grid of points it has six more off-axis squares (bottom) for a total of 20.

A common [mathematical puzzle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_puzzle "Mathematical puzzle") involves counting the squares of all sizes in a square grid of ![Image 118: {\displaystyle n\times n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/59d2b4cb72e304526cf5b5887147729ea259da78) squares. For instance, a square grid of nine squares has 14 squares: the nine squares that form the grid, four more ![Image 119: {\displaystyle 2\times 2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f8a0e3400ffb97d67c00267ed50cddfe824cbe80) squares, and one ![Image 120: {\displaystyle 3\times 3}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ddc0d4d6106875f8006be1d898512ca5843bad8e) square. The answer to the puzzle is ![Image 121: {\displaystyle n(n+1)(2n+1)/6}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/801bfabe9a78e34cb154636452e814c12f637689), a [square pyramidal number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_pyramidal_number "Square pyramidal number").[[133]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-133) For ![Image 122: {\displaystyle n=1,2,3,\dots }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b207567215497887a3250644b8876c23dc3ebf10) these numbers are:[[134]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-134)

1, 5, 14, 30, 55, 91, 140, 204, 285, ...

A variant of the same puzzle asks for the number of squares formed by a grid of ![Image 123: {\displaystyle n\times n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/59d2b4cb72e304526cf5b5887147729ea259da78) points, allowing squares that are not axis-parallel. For instance, a grid of nine points has five axis-parallel squares as described above, but it also contains one more diagonal square for a total of six.[[135]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-135) In this case, the answer is given by the _4-dimensional pyramidal numbers_![Image 124: {\displaystyle n^{2}(n^{2}-1)/12}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7f56d5254b4d9474b9427ff9bd3f7c5aa402e68e). For ![Image 125: {\displaystyle n=1,2,3,\dots }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b207567215497887a3250644b8876c23dc3ebf10) these numbers are:[[136]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-136)

0, 1, 6, 20, 50, 105, 196, 336, 540, ...

Partitions of a square into three similar rectangles

Another counting problem involving squares asks for the number of different shapes of rectangle that can be used when [dividing a square into similar rectangles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dividing_a_square_into_similar_rectangles "Dividing a square into similar rectangles"). A square can be divided into two similar rectangles only in one way, by bisecting it, but when dividing a square into three similar rectangles there are three possible [aspect ratios](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aspect_ratio "Aspect ratio") of the rectangles,[[137]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-137) 3:1, 3:2, and the square of the [plastic ratio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plastic_ratio "Plastic ratio"), approximately 1.755:1.[[138]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-138) The number of proportions that are possible when dividing into ![Image 127: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) rectangles is known for small values of ![Image 128: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b), but not as a general formula. For ![Image 129: {\displaystyle n=1,2,3,\dots }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b207567215497887a3250644b8876c23dc3ebf10) these numbers are:[[139]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-139)

1, 1, 3, 11, 51, 245, 1372, ...

[![Image 133](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/H2_tiling_246-1.png/250px-H2_tiling_246-1.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:H2_tiling_246-1.png)

In the familiar Euclidean geometry, space is flat, and every convex quadrilateral has internal angles summing to 360°, so a square (a regular quadrilateral) has four equal sides and four right angles (each 90°). By contrast, in [spherical geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_geometry "Spherical geometry") and [hyperbolic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_geometry "Hyperbolic geometry"), space is curved and the internal angles of a convex quadrilateral never sum to 360°, so quadrilaterals with four right angles do not exist. Both of these geometries have regular quadrilaterals, with four equal sides and four equal angles, often called squares,[[140]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-maraner-140) but some authors avoid that name because they lack right angles. These geometries also have regular polygons with right angles, but with numbers of sides different from four.[[141]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-singer-141)

In spherical geometry, space has uniform positive curvature, and every convex quadrilateral (a [polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_polygon "Spherical polygon") with four [great-circle arc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great-circle_arc "Great-circle arc") edges) has angles whose sum exceeds 360° by an amount called the [angular excess](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_excess "Angular excess"), proportional to its surface area. Small spherical squares are approximately Euclidean, and larger squares' angles increase with area.[[140]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-maraner-140) One special case is the face of a [spherical cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_cube "Spherical cube") with four 120° angles, covering one sixth of the sphere's surface.[[142]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-142) Another is a [hemisphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hemisphere_(geometry) "Hemisphere (geometry)"), the face of a spherical square [dihedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihedron "Dihedron"), with four [straight angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Straight_angle "Straight angle"); the [Peirce quincuncial projection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peirce_quincuncial_projection "Peirce quincuncial projection") for [world maps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_map "World map")[conformally maps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conformal_mapping "Conformal mapping") two such faces to Euclidean squares.[[143]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-143) An [octant of a sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octant_of_a_sphere "Octant of a sphere") is a regular [spherical triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_triangle "Spherical triangle"), with three equal sides and three right angles; eight of them tile the sphere, with four meeting at each vertex, to form a [spherical octahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_octahedron "Spherical octahedron").[[144]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-144) A [spherical lune](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_lune "Spherical lune") is a regular [digon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digon "Digon"), with two semicircular sides and two equal angles at [antipodal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antipodal_points "Antipodal points") vertices; a right-angled lune covers one quarter of the sphere, one face of a four-lune [hosohedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hosohedron "Hosohedron").[[145]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-145)

In [hyperbolic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_geometry "Hyperbolic geometry"), space has uniform negative curvature, and every convex quadrilateral has angles whose sum falls short of 360° by an amount called the [angular defect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_defect "Angular defect"), proportional to its surface area. Small hyperbolic squares are approximately Euclidean, and larger squares' angles decrease with increasing area. Special cases include the squares with angles of 360°/_n_ for every value of n larger than 4, each of which can tile the [hyperbolic plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_plane "Hyperbolic plane").[[141]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-singer-141) In the infinite limit, an [ideal square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ideal_point#Polygons_with_ideal_vertices "Ideal point") has four sides of infinite length and four vertices at [ideal points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ideal_point "Ideal point") outside the hyperbolic plane, with 0° internal angles;[[146]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-146) an ideal square, like every ideal quadrilateral, has finite area proportional to its angular defect of 360°.[[147]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-147) It is also possible to make a regular hyperbolic polygon with right angles at every vertex and any number of sides greater than four; such polygons can [uniformly tile the hyperbolic plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uniform_tilings_in_hyperbolic_plane "Uniform tilings in hyperbolic plane"), [dual](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_polyhedron "Dual polyhedron") to the tiling with n squares about each vertex.[[141]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-singer-141)

[![Image 134](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Metric_circles.png/250px-Metric_circles.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Metric_circles.png)

Metric circles using Chebyshev, Euclidean, and taxicab distance functions

The Euclidean plane can be defined in terms of the [real coordinate plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_coordinate_plane "Real coordinate plane") by adoption of the [Euclidean distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_distance "Euclidean distance") function, according to which the distance between any two points ![Image 135: {\displaystyle (x_{1},y_{1})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7fc74086e56542bd28b46a84faaee3cebdd4a899) and ![Image 136: {\displaystyle (x_{2},y_{2})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d52d44e16a796acee486af49af05f678566d181a) is ![Image 137: {\displaystyle \textstyle {\sqrt {(x_{1}-x_{2})^{2}+(y_{1}-y_{2})^{2}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/85e1c903e6b0dc79193724d921b8a5a68554c69b). Other metric geometries are formed when a different [distance function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space "Metric space") is adopted instead, and in some of these geometries shapes that would be Euclidean squares become the "[circles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ball_(mathematics)#In_normed_vector_spaces "Ball (mathematics)")" (set of points of equal distance from a center point). Squares tilted at 45° to the coordinate axes are the circles in [taxicab geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taxicab_geometry "Taxicab geometry"), based on the ![Image 138: {\displaystyle L_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0e79dc1b001f8b923df475ed14de023cbc456013) distance ![Image 139: {\displaystyle |x_{1}-x_{2}|+|y_{1}-y_{2}|}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c7fbb2f18e5ce70da5f0afbf20ecedf0b461f466). The points with taxicab distance ![Image 140: {\displaystyle d}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e85ff03cbe0c7341af6b982e47e9f90d235c66ab) from any given point form a diagonal square, centered at the given point, with diagonal length ![Image 141: {\displaystyle 2d}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d8106478cb4da6af49992eeb3a3b8690d27797ad). In the same way, axis-parallel squares are the circles for the ![Image 142: {\displaystyle L_{\infty }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a5ecf05fae003abbd91bc8c749d5a8a807d6efd8) or [Chebyshev distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chebyshev_distance "Chebyshev distance"), ![Image 143: {\displaystyle \max(|x_{1}-x_{2}|,|y_{1}-y_{2}|)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ff16a809baefbe732a2c5dd3f0a92e49e298fe62). In this metric, the points with distance ![Image 144: {\displaystyle d}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e85ff03cbe0c7341af6b982e47e9f90d235c66ab) from some point form an axis-parallel square, centered at the given point, with side length ![Image 145: {\displaystyle 2d}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d8106478cb4da6af49992eeb3a3b8690d27797ad).[[148]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-148)[[149]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-149)[[150]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#cite_note-150)

*   [Finsler–Hadwiger theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finsler%E2%80%93Hadwiger_theorem "Finsler–Hadwiger theorem") on a square derived from two squares sharing a vertex
