[![Image 1](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Night_Sky_from_Hawai%E2%80%98i_and_Chile_%28iotw2225c%29.jpg/500px-Night_Sky_from_Hawai%E2%80%98i_and_Chile_%28iotw2225c%29.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Night_Sky_from_Hawai%E2%80%98i_and_Chile_(iotw2225c).jpg)

The interplanetary dust cloud illuminated and visible as [zodiacal light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zodiacal_light "Zodiacal light"), with its parts the _false dawn_,[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_dust_cloud#cite_note-1)_[gegenschein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gegenschein "Gegenschein")_ and the rest of its band, which is visually crossed by the [Milky Way](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milky_Way "Milky Way"), in this composite image of the night sky above the northern and southern hemisphere

The **interplanetary dust cloud**, or **zodiacal cloud** (as the source of the [zodiacal light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zodiacal_light "Zodiacal light")), consists of [cosmic dust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_dust "Cosmic dust") (small [particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dust "Dust") floating in [outer space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_space "Outer space")) that pervades the space between [planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planet "Planet") within [planetary systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_system "Planetary system"), such as the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_dust_cloud#cite_note-EA-20190312-2) This system of particles has been studied for many years in order to understand its nature, origin, and relationship to larger bodies. There are several methods to obtain [space dust measurement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_dust_measurement "Space dust measurement").

In the Solar System, interplanetary dust particles have a role in [scattering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_scattering_by_particles "Light scattering by particles")[sunlight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sunlight "Sunlight") and in emitting [thermal radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermal_radiation "Thermal radiation"), which is the most prominent feature of the [night sky](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Night_sky "Night sky")'s radiation, with wavelengths ranging 5–50 [μm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Micrometre "Micrometre").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_dust_cloud#cite_note-3) The [particle sizes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_size "Particle size") of grains characterizing the [infrared](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infrared "Infrared") emission near [Earth's orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_orbit "Earth's orbit") typically range 10–100 μm.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_dust_cloud#cite_note-4) Microscopic impact craters on lunar rocks returned by the [Apollo Program](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apollo_Program "Apollo Program")[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_dust_cloud#cite_note-5) revealed the size distribution of [cosmic dust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_dust "Cosmic dust") particles bombarding the lunar surface. The [’’Grün’’](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eberhard_Gr%C3%BCn "Eberhard Grün") distribution of interplanetary dust at 1 AU,[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_dust_cloud#cite_note-GrunEA1985-6) describes the flux of [cosmic dust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_dust "Cosmic dust") from nm to mm sizes at 1 AU.

The total mass of the interplanetary dust cloud is approximately 3.5×10 16 kg, or the mass of an [asteroid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asteroid "Asteroid") of radius 15 km (with density of about 2.5 g/cm 3).[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_dust_cloud#cite_note-7) Straddling the [zodiac](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zodiac "Zodiac") along the [ecliptic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecliptic "Ecliptic"), this dust cloud is visible as the zodiacal light in a moonless and naturally dark sky and is best seen sunward during astronomical [twilight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Twilight "Twilight").

The _[Pioneer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pioneer\_10 "Pioneer 10")_ spacecraft observations in the 1970s linked the [zodiacal light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zodiacal_light "Zodiacal light") with the interplanetary dust cloud in the Solar System.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_dust_cloud#cite_note-8) Also, the [VBSDC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Venetia_Burney_Student_Dust_Counter "Venetia Burney Student Dust Counter") instrument on the _[New Horizons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New\_Horizons "New Horizons")_ probe was designed to detect impacts of the dust from the zodiacal cloud in the Solar System.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_dust_cloud#cite_note-boulder.swri.edu-9)

[Artist's concept](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artist%27s_impression "Artist's impression") of a view from an [exoplanet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exoplanet "Exoplanet"), with light from an [extrasolar interplanetary dust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exozodiacal_dust "Exozodiacal dust") cloud

The sources of interplanetary dust particles (IDPs) include at least: asteroid collisions, [cometary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comet "Comet") activity and collisions in the inner Solar System, [Kuiper belt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kuiper_belt "Kuiper belt") collisions, and [interstellar medium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_medium "Interstellar medium") grains (Backman, D., 1997). The origins of the zodiacal cloud have long been subject to one of the most heated controversies in the field of astronomy.

It was believed that IDPs had originated from comets or asteroids whose particles had dispersed throughout the extent of the cloud. However, further observations have suggested that Mars [dust storms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dust_storms_on_Mars "Dust storms on Mars") may be responsible for the zodiacal cloud's formation.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_dust_cloud#cite_note-10)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_dust_cloud#cite_note-EA-20190312-2)

Life cycle of a particle
------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Interplanetary_dust_cloud&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Life cycle of a particle")]

The main physical processes "affecting" (destruction or expulsion mechanisms) interplanetary dust particles are: expulsion by [radiation pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiation_pressure "Radiation pressure"), inward [Poynting-Robertson (PR) radiation drag](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poynting-Robertson_drag "Poynting-Robertson drag"), [solar wind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_wind "Solar wind") pressure (with significant electromagnetic effects), [sublimation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sublimation_(chemistry) "Sublimation (chemistry)"), mutual collisions, and the dynamical effects of planets (Backman, D., 1997).

The lifetimes of these dust particles are very short compared to the lifetime of the Solar System. If one finds grains around a star that is older than about 10,000,000 years, then the grains must have been from recently released fragments of larger objects, i.e. they cannot be leftover grains from the [protoplanetary disk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protoplanetary_disk "Protoplanetary disk") (Backman, private communication).[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] Therefore, the grains would be "later-generation" dust. The zodiacal dust in the Solar System is 99.9% later-generation dust and 0.1% intruding [interstellar medium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_medium "Interstellar medium") dust. All primordial grains from the Solar System's formation were removed long ago.

Particles which are affected primarily by radiation pressure are known as "beta meteoroids". They are generally less than 1.4 × 10−12 g and are pushed outward from the Sun into interstellar space.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_dust_cloud#cite_note-11)

[![Image 3](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/HL_Tau_protoplanetary_disk.jpg/250px-HL_Tau_protoplanetary_disk.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:HL_Tau_protoplanetary_disk.jpg)

An image of a [protoplanetary disk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protoplanetary_disk "Protoplanetary disk"), comparable to images of simulations of the Solar System's interplanetary dust cloud, which has been suggested to be imaged from beyond it in the [Outer Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_Solar_System "Outer Solar System").[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_dust_cloud#cite_note-n092-12)

The interplanetary dust cloud has a complex structure (Reach, W., 1997). Apart from a background density, this includes:

*   At least 8 [dust trails](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dust_trail "Dust trail")—their source is thought to be [short-period comets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Short-period_comet "Short-period comet").
*   A number of dust bands, the sources of which are thought to be [asteroid families](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asteroid_families "Asteroid families") in the [main asteroid belt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Main_asteroid_belt "Main asteroid belt"). The three strongest bands arise from the [Themis family](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Themis_family "Themis family"), the [Koronis family](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Koronis_family "Koronis family"), and the [Eos family](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eos_family "Eos family"). Other source families include the [Maria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maria_family "Maria family"), [Eunomia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eunomia_family "Eunomia family"), and possibly the [Vesta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vesta_family "Vesta family") and/or [Hygiea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hygiea_family "Hygiea family") families (Reach et al. 1996).
*   At least 2 resonant dust rings are known (for example, the Earth-resonant dust ring, although every planet in the Solar System is thought to have a resonant ring with a "wake") (Jackson and Zook, 1988, 1992) (Dermott, S.F. et al., 1994, 1997)

[![Image 4](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Venus_dust_ring.png/250px-Venus_dust_ring.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Venus_dust_ring.png)

First ever panorama image of the dust ring of Venus's orbital space, imaged by [Parker Solar Probe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parker_Solar_Probe "Parker Solar Probe").

Interplanetary dust has been found to form rings of dust in the orbital space of Mercury and Venus.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_dust_cloud#cite_note-Garner_2019-13) Venus's orbital dust ring is suspected to originate either from yet undetected Venus trailing asteroids,[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_dust_cloud#cite_note-Garner_2019-13) interplanetary dust migrating in waves from orbital space to orbital space, or from the remains of the Solar System's [circumstellar disc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumstellar_disc "Circumstellar disc"), out of which its [proto-planetary disc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proto-planetary_disc "Proto-planetary disc") and then itself, the Solar [planetary system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_system "Planetary system"), formed.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_dust_cloud#cite_note-Rehm_2021-14)

Dust collection on Earth
------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Interplanetary_dust_cloud&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Dust collection on Earth")]

In 1951, [Fred Whipple](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fred_Whipple "Fred Whipple") predicted that micrometeorites smaller than 100 micrometers in diameter might be decelerated on impact with the Earth's upper atmosphere without melting.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_dust_cloud#cite_note-Whipple-1950-15) The modern era of laboratory study of these particles began with the stratospheric collection flights of [Donald E. Brownlee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Donald_E._Brownlee "Donald E. Brownlee") and collaborators in the 1970s using balloons and then [U-2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lockheed_U-2 "Lockheed U-2") aircraft.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_dust_cloud#cite_note-Brownlee-1977-16)

Although some of the particles found were similar to the material in present-day meteorite collections, the [nanoporous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nanoporous "Nanoporous") nature and unequilibrated cosmic-average composition of other particles suggested that they began as fine-grained aggregates of nonvolatile building blocks and cometary ice.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_dust_cloud#cite_note-17)[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_dust_cloud#cite_note-Walker-1986-18) The interplanetary nature of these particles was later verified by [noble gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noble_gas "Noble gas")[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_dust_cloud#cite_note-Hudson-1981-19) and [solar flare](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_flare "Solar flare") track[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_dust_cloud#cite_note-Bradley-1984-20) observations.

In that context a program for atmospheric collection and curation of these particles was developed at [Johnson Space Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johnson_Space_Center "Johnson Space Center") in Texas.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_dust_cloud#cite_note-Cosmic-Dust-Sample-Collection-21) This stratospheric micrometeorite collection, along with [presolar grains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Presolar_grains "Presolar grains") from meteorites, are unique sources of [extraterrestrial material](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extraterrestrial_material "Extraterrestrial material") (not to mention being small astronomical objects in their own right) available for study in laboratories today.

Spacecraft that have carried dust detectors include _[Helios](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helios\_Dust\_Instrumentation "Helios Dust Instrumentation")_, _[Pioneer 10](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pioneer\_10 "Pioneer 10")_, _[Pioneer 11](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pioneer\_11 "Pioneer 11")_, _[Ulysses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ulysses\_(spacecraft) "Ulysses (spacecraft)")_ (heliocentric orbit out to the distance of Jupiter), _[Galileo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galileo\_(spacecraft) "Galileo (spacecraft)")_ (Jupiter Orbiter), _[Cassini](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens "Cassini–Huygens")_ (Saturn orbiter), and _[New Horizons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New\_Horizons "New Horizons")_ (see [Venetia Burney Student Dust Counter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Venetia_Burney_Student_Dust_Counter "Venetia Burney Student Dust Counter")).

The Solar interplanetary dust cloud obscures the [extragalactic background light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extragalactic_background_light "Extragalactic background light"), making observations of it from the [Inner Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inner_Solar_System "Inner Solar System") very limited.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_dust_cloud#cite_note-n092-12)

Major Review Collections
------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Interplanetary_dust_cloud&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Major Review Collections")]

Collections of review articles on various aspects of interplanetary dust and related fields appeared in the following books:

In 1978 [Tony McDonnell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tony_McDonnell_(space_scientist) "Tony McDonnell (space scientist)") edited the book **Cosmic Dust**[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_dust_cloud#cite_note-22) which contained chapters[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_dust_cloud#cite_note-23) on comets along with zodiacal light as indicator of interplanetary dust, meteors, interstellar dust, microparticle studies by sampling techniques, and microparticle studies by space instrumentation. Attention is also given to lunar and planetary impact erosion, aspects of particle dynamics, and acceleration techniques and high-velocity impact processes employed for the laboratory simulation of effects produced by micrometeoroids.

2001 [Eberhard Grün](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eberhard_Gr%C3%BCn "Eberhard Grün"), Bo Gustafson, Stan Dermott, and Hugo Fechtig published the book **Interplanetary Dust**.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_dust_cloud#cite_note-24) Topics covered[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_dust_cloud#cite_note-25) are: historical perspectives; cometary dust; near-Earth environment; meteoroids and meteors; properties of interplanetary dust, information from collected samples; in situ measurements of cosmic dust; numerical modeling of the Zodiacal Cloud structure; synthesis of observations; instrumentation; physical processes; optical properties of interplanetary dust; orbital evolution of interplanetary dust; circumplanetary dust, observations and simple physics; interstellar dust and circumstellar dust disks.

2019 Rafael Rodrigo, Jürgen Blum, Hsiang-Wen Hsu, Detlef V. Koschny, [Anny-Chantal Levasseur-Regourd](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anny-Chantal_Levasseur-Regourd "Anny-Chantal Levasseur-Regourd"), Jesús Martín-Pintado, Veerle J. Sterken, and Andrew Westphal collected reviews in the book **Cosmic Dust from the Laboratory to the Stars**.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_dust_cloud#cite_note-26) Included are discussions[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_dust_cloud#cite_note-27) of dust in various environments: from planetary atmospheres and airless bodies over interplanetary dust, meteoroids, comet dust and emissions from active moons to interstellar dust and protoplanetary disks. Diverse research techniques and results, including in-situ measurement, remote observation, laboratory experiments and modelling, and analysis of returned samples are discussed.
