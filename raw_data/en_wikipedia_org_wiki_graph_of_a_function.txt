In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), the **graph of a function**f{\displaystyle f}![Image 39: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) is the set of [ordered pairs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordered_pair "Ordered pair")(x,y){\displaystyle (x,y)}![Image 40: {\displaystyle (x,y)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/41cf50e4a314ca8e2c30964baa8d26e5be7a9386), where f(x)=y.{\displaystyle f(x)=y.}![Image 41: {\displaystyle f(x)=y.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/de09d5f1e42dc957864c2d2321712e2e168c1298) In the common case where x{\displaystyle x}![Image 42: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) and f(x){\displaystyle f(x)}![Image 43: {\displaystyle f(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/202945cce41ecebb6f643f31d119c514bec7a074) are [real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number"), these pairs are [Cartesian coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinates "Cartesian coordinates") of points in a [plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_(geometry) "Plane (geometry)") and often form a [curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_curve "Plane curve"). The graphical representation of the graph of a [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)") is also known as a _[plot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plot\_(graphics) "Plot (graphics)")_.

In the case of [functions of two variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bivariate_function "Bivariate function") – that is, functions whose [domain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_of_a_function "Domain of a function") consists of pairs (x,y){\displaystyle (x,y)}![Image 44: {\displaystyle (x,y)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/41cf50e4a314ca8e2c30964baa8d26e5be7a9386) –, the graph usually refers to the set of [ordered triples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordered_triple "Ordered triple")(x,y,z){\displaystyle (x,y,z)}![Image 45: {\displaystyle (x,y,z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/22a8c93372e8f8b6e24d523bd5545aed3430baf4) where f(x,y)=z{\displaystyle f(x,y)=z}![Image 46: {\displaystyle f(x,y)=z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c63ff9d90eb71a4addd66cafa9d882431d0df385). This is a subset of [three-dimensional space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_space "Three-dimensional space"); for a continuous [real-valued function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real-valued_function "Real-valued function") of two real variables, its graph forms a [surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_(mathematics) "Surface (mathematics)"), which can be visualized as a _[surface plot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface\_plot\_(graphics) "Surface plot (graphics)")_.

In [science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Science "Science"), [engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineering "Engineering"), [technology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Technology "Technology"), [finance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finance "Finance"), and other areas, graphs are tools used for many purposes. In the simplest case one variable is plotted as a function of another, typically using [rectangular axes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangular_coordinate_system "Rectangular coordinate system"); see _[Plot (graphics)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plot\_(graphics) "Plot (graphics)")_ for details.

A graph of a function is a special case of a [relation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relation_(mathematics) "Relation (mathematics)"). In the modern [foundations of mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foundations_of_mathematics "Foundations of mathematics"), and, typically, in [set theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_theory "Set theory"), a function is actually equal to its graph.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_of_a_function#cite_note-Pinter2014-1) However, it is often useful to see functions as [mappings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Map_(mathematics) "Map (mathematics)"),[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_of_a_function#cite_note-2) which consist not only of the relation between input and output, but also which set is the domain, and which set is the [codomain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Codomain "Codomain"). For example, to say that a function is onto ([surjective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surjective_function "Surjective function")) or not the codomain should be taken into account. The graph of a function on its own does not determine the codomain. It is common[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_of_a_function#cite_note-3) to use both terms _function_ and _graph of a function_ since even if considered the same object, they indicate viewing it from a different perspective.

Definition
----------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Graph_of_a_function&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Definition")]

Given a [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)")f:X→Y{\displaystyle f:X\to Y}![Image 49: {\displaystyle f:X\to Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/abd1e080abef4bbdab67b43819c6431e7561361c) from a set X (the [domain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_of_a_function "Domain of a function")) to a set Y (the [codomain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Codomain "Codomain")), the graph of the function is the set[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_of_a_function#cite_note-4)G(f)={(x,f(x)):x∈X},{\displaystyle G(f)=\{(x,f(x)):x\in X\},}![Image 50: {\displaystyle G(f)=\{(x,f(x)):x\in X\},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4fa5ed0e661ffd83cef8f2d9f9b4c075f77c1c7) which is a subset of the [Cartesian product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_product "Cartesian product")X×Y{\displaystyle X\times Y}![Image 51: {\displaystyle X\times Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1613c1ff4b6fbfb6c80a8da83e90ad28f0ab3483). In the definition of a function in terms of [set theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_theory "Set theory"), it is common to identify a function with its graph, although, formally, a function is formed by the triple consisting of its domain, its codomain and its graph.

From the graph, the domain {1,2,3}{\displaystyle \{1,2,3\}}![Image 58: {\displaystyle \{1,2,3\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/959905040c4110bae682eae9db986227c5506dcd) is recovered as the set of first component of each pair in the graph {1,2,3}={x:∃y,such that(x,y)∈G(f)}{\displaystyle \{1,2,3\}=\{x:\ \exists y,{\text{ such that }}(x,y)\in G(f)\}}![Image 59: {\displaystyle \{1,2,3\}=\{x:\ \exists y,{\text{ such that }}(x,y)\in G(f)\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cef9dfca6f162d6b7fd41ecff6a9fa9923da93ad).
