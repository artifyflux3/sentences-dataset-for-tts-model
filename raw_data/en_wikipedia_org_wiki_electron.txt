The **electron** (**e−**, or **β−** in nuclear reactions) is a [subatomic particle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subatomic_particle "Subatomic particle") with a negative one [elementary electric charge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementary_charge "Elementary charge"). It is a fundamental particle that comprises the ordinary matter that makes up the universe, along with [up](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Up_quark "Up quark") and [down quarks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Down_quark "Down quark").

Electrons are extremely lightweight particles. In [atoms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atom "Atom"), an electron's [matter wave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matter_wave "Matter wave") forms an [atomic orbital](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_orbital "Atomic orbital") around a positively charged [atomic nucleus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_nucleus "Atomic nucleus"). The configuration and energy levels of an atom's electrons determine the atom's [chemical properties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_properties "Chemical properties"). Electrons are bound to the nucleus to different degrees. The outermost or [valence electrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Valence_electron "Valence electron") are the least tightly bound and are responsible for the formation of [chemical bonds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_bond "Chemical bond") between atoms to create [molecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecule "Molecule") and [crystals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystal "Crystal"). These valence electrons also facilitate all types of [chemical reactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_reaction "Chemical reaction") by being transferred or [shared](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covalent_bond "Covalent bond") between atoms. The inner electron shells make up the [atomic core](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_core "Atomic core").

Electrons play a vital role in numerous [physical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics") phenomena due to their charge and mobile nature. In [metals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metal "Metal"), the outermost electrons are [delocalised](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Delocalised "Delocalised") and able to move freely, accounting for the high [electrical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrical_conductivity "Electrical conductivity") and [thermal conductivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermal_conduction "Thermal conduction") of metals. In [semiconductors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semiconductor "Semiconductor"), the number of mobile [charge carriers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charge_carrier "Charge carrier") (electrons and [holes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_hole "Electron hole")) can be finely tuned by [doping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doping_(semiconductor) "Doping (semiconductor)"), temperature, voltage and radiation - the basis of all modern [electronics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronics "Electronics").

Electrons can be stripped entirely from their atoms to exist as [free particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_particle "Free particle"). As [particle beams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_beam "Particle beam") in a [vacuum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vacuum "Vacuum"), free electrons can be [accelerated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_acceleration "Particle acceleration"), [focused](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrostatic_lens "Electrostatic lens") and used for applications like [cathode ray tubes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cathode_ray_tube "Cathode ray tube"), [electron microscopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_microscope "Electron microscope"), [electron beam welding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_beam_welding "Electron beam welding"), [lithography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_beam_lithography "Electron beam lithography") and [particle accelerators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_accelerator "Particle accelerator") that generate [synchrotron radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synchrotron_radiation "Synchrotron radiation"). Their charge and [wave-particle duality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wave%E2%80%93particle_duality "Wave–particle duality") make electrons indispensable in the modern technological world.

Electrons belong to the first [generation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generation_(particle_physics) "Generation (particle physics)") of the [lepton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lepton "Lepton") particle family,[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-curtis74-15) elementary particles that do not feel the strong nuclear force, and only interact through the weak and electromagnetic forces. Electrons are generally thought to be [elementary particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementary_particle "Elementary particle") because they have no known components or substructure.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-prl50-1) An electron's [mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invariant_mass "Invariant mass") is approximately [⁠1/1836⁠](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton-to-electron_mass_ratio "Proton-to-electron mass ratio") that of a [proton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton "Proton").[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-nist_codata_mu-16)[Quantum mechanical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics") properties of the electron include an intrinsic [angular momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum "Angular momentum") ([spin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_(physics) "Spin (physics)")) of half the [reduced Planck constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reduced_Planck_constant "Reduced Planck constant"), i.e. ⁠_ħ_/2⁠. Being [fermions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermion "Fermion"), no two electrons can occupy the same [quantum state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_state "Quantum state"), according to the [Pauli exclusion principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pauli_exclusion_principle "Pauli exclusion principle").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-curtis74-15) Like all elementary particles, electrons exhibit properties of [both particles and waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wave%E2%80%93particle_duality "Wave–particle duality"). For example, electrons can collide like particles with other particles and can also be [diffracted](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_diffraction "Electron diffraction") like light waves. The [wave properties of electrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#Quantum_properties) are easier to observe with experiments than those of other particles like [neutrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron "Neutron") and protons because electrons have a lower mass and hence a longer [de Broglie wavelength](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_Broglie_wavelength "De Broglie wavelength") for a given energy.

Electrons play an essential role in numerous [physical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics") phenomena, such as [electricity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electricity "Electricity"), [magnetism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetism "Magnetism"), [chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemistry "Chemistry"), and [thermal conductivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermal_conductivity "Thermal conductivity"); they also participate in [gravitational](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_wave "Gravitational wave"), [electromagnetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentz_force "Lorentz force"), and [weak interactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weak_interaction "Weak interaction").[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-anastopoulos1-17) Since an electron has charge, it has a surrounding [electric field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_field "Electric field"); if that electron is moving relative to an observer, the observer will observe it to generate a [magnetic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_field "Magnetic field"). Electromagnetic fields produced from other sources will affect the motion of an electron according to the [Lorentz force law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentz_force_law "Lorentz force law"). Electrons radiate or absorb energy in the form of [photons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon "Photon") when they are accelerated.

Laboratory instruments are capable of trapping individual electrons as well as [electron plasma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasma_(physics) "Plasma (physics)") by the use of electromagnetic fields. Special [telescopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Telescope "Telescope") can detect electron plasma in outer space. Electrons are involved in many applications, such as [tribology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tribology "Tribology") or frictional charging, electrolysis, electrochemistry, battery technologies, [electronics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronics "Electronics"), [welding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_beam_welding "Electron beam welding"), [cathode-ray tubes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cathode-ray_tube "Cathode-ray tube"), photoelectricity, photovoltaic solar panels, [electron microscopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_microscope "Electron microscope"), [radiation therapy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiation_therapy "Radiation therapy"), [lasers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free-electron_laser "Free-electron laser"), [gaseous ionization detectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaseous_ionization_detectors "Gaseous ionization detectors"), and [particle accelerators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_accelerator "Particle accelerator").

Interactions involving electrons with other subatomic particles are of interest in fields such as [chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemistry "Chemistry") and [nuclear physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_physics "Nuclear physics"). [Atoms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atoms "Atoms") are composed of positive [protons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton "Proton") within [atomic nuclei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_nucleus "Atomic nucleus") and the negative electrons without, held together by [Coulomb force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coulomb%27s_law "Coulomb's law") interaction. Ionization state (differences in the proportions of negative electrons versus positive nuclei) or sharing of the electrons between two or more atoms are the main causes of [chemical bonding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_bond "Chemical bond").[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-Pauling-18)

Electrons participate in [nuclear reactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_reaction "Nuclear reaction"), such as [nucleosynthesis in stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis "Stellar nucleosynthesis"), where they are known as [beta particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beta_particle "Beta particle"). Electrons can be created through [beta decay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beta_decay "Beta decay") of [radioactive isotopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radionuclide "Radionuclide") and in high-energy collisions, for instance, when [cosmic rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_ray "Cosmic ray") enter the atmosphere. The [antiparticle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiparticle "Antiparticle") of the electron is called the [positron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positron "Positron"); it is identical to the electron, except that it carries electrical [charge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charge_(physics) "Charge (physics)") of the opposite sign. When an [electron collides with a positron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron%E2%80%93positron_annihilation "Electron–positron annihilation"), both particles can be [annihilated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Annihilation "Annihilation"), producing [gamma ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamma_ray "Gamma ray")[photons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon "Photon").

In 1838, British natural philosopher [Richard Laming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Laming "Richard Laming") first hypothesized the concept of an indivisible quantity of electric charge to explain the [chemical properties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_property "Chemical property") of atoms.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-arabatzis-4) Irish physicist [George Johnstone Stoney](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_Johnstone_Stoney "George Johnstone Stoney") named this charge "electron" in 1891, and [J. J. Thomson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J._J._Thomson "J. J. Thomson") and his team of British physicists identified it as a particle in 1897 during the [cathode-ray tube experiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cathode_ray "Cathode ray").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-thomson-6)

### Discovery of effect of electric force

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Electron&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Discovery of effect of electric force")]

The [ancient Greeks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greece#Science_and_technology "Ancient Greece") noticed that [amber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amber "Amber") attracted small objects when rubbed with fur. Along with [lightning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lightning "Lightning"), this phenomenon is one of humanity's earliest recorded experiences with [electricity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electricity#History "Electricity").[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-DictOrigins-19) In his 1600 treatise _[De Magnete](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De\_Magnete "De Magnete")_, the English scientist [William Gilbert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Gilbert_(astronomer) "William Gilbert (astronomer)") coined the [Neo-Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neo-Latin "Neo-Latin") term _electrica_, to refer to those substances with property similar to that of amber which attract small objects after being rubbed.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-Benjamin-20) Both _electric_ and _electricity_ are derived from the Latin _\_ēlectrum\__ (also the root of the [alloy of the same name](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrum "Electrum")), which came from the [Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek "Ancient Greek") word for amber, ἤλεκτρον (_\_ēlektron\__).

### Discovery of two kinds of charges

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Electron&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Discovery of two kinds of charges")]

In the early 1700s, French chemist [Charles François du Fay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Fran%C3%A7ois_de_Cisternay_du_Fay "Charles François de Cisternay du Fay") found that if a charged gold-leaf is repulsed by glass rubbed with silk, then the same charged gold-leaf is attracted by amber rubbed with wool. From this and other results of similar types of experiments, du Fay concluded that electricity consists of two [electrical fluids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aether_theories "Aether theories"), _vitreous_ fluid from glass rubbed with silk and _resinous_ fluid from amber rubbed with wool. These two fluids can neutralize each other when combined.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-Benjamin-20)[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-21) American scientist [Ebenezer Kinnersley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ebenezer_Kinnersley "Ebenezer Kinnersley") later also independently reached the same conclusion.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-Cajori1917-22): 118  A decade later [Benjamin Franklin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benjamin_Franklin "Benjamin Franklin") proposed that electricity was not from different types of electrical fluid, but a single electrical fluid showing an excess (+) or deficit (−). He gave them the modern [charge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_charge "Electric charge") nomenclature of positive and negative respectively.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-23) Franklin thought of the charge carrier as being positive, but he did not correctly identify which situation was a surplus of the charge carrier, and which situation was a deficit.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-24)

Between 1838 and 1851, British natural philosopher [Richard Laming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Laming "Richard Laming") developed the idea that an atom is composed of a core of matter surrounded by subatomic particles that had unit [electric charges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_charge "Electric charge").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-farrar-3) Beginning in 1846, German physicist [Wilhelm Eduard Weber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilhelm_Eduard_Weber "Wilhelm Eduard Weber") theorized that electricity was composed of positively and negatively charged fluids, and their interaction was governed by the [inverse square law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverse-square_law "Inverse-square law"). After studying the phenomenon of [electrolysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrolysis "Electrolysis") in 1874, Irish physicist [George Johnstone Stoney](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_Johnstone_Stoney "George Johnstone Stoney") suggested that there existed a "single definite quantity of electricity", the charge of a [monovalent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Valence_(chemistry) "Valence (chemistry)")[ion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ion "Ion"). He was able to estimate the value of this elementary charge _e_ by means of [Faraday's laws of electrolysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Faraday%27s_laws_of_electrolysis "Faraday's laws of electrolysis").[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-25) However, Stoney believed these charges were permanently attached to atoms and could not be removed. In 1881, German physicist [Hermann von Helmholtz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_von_Helmholtz "Hermann von Helmholtz") argued that both positive and negative charges were divided into elementary parts, each of which "behaves like atoms of electricity".[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-arabatzis-4)

Stoney initially coined the term _electrolion_ in 1881. Ten years later, he switched to _electron_ to describe these elementary charges, writing in 1894: "...an estimate was made of the actual amount of this most remarkable fundamental unit of electricity, for which I have since ventured to suggest the name _electron_". A 1906 proposal to change to _electrion_ failed because [Hendrik Lorentz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hendrik_Lorentz "Hendrik Lorentz") preferred to keep _electron_.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-26)[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-GJStoney-27) The word _electron_ is a combination of the words _electr ic_ and _i on_.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-28) The suffix [-_on_](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-on "wikt:-on") which is now used to designate other subatomic particles, such as a proton or neutron, is in turn derived from electron.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-29)[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-30)

### Discovery of free electrons outside matter

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Electron&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Discovery of free electrons outside matter")]

[![Image 2: A round glass vacuum tube with a glowing circular beam inside](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Cyclotron_motion_wider_view.jpg/250px-Cyclotron_motion_wider_view.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Cyclotron_motion_wider_view.jpg)

A beam of electrons deflected by a magnetic field into a circle[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-31)

While studying electrical conductivity in [rarefied](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rarefied "Rarefied") gases in 1859, the German physicist [Julius Plücker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Julius_Pl%C3%BCcker "Julius Plücker") observed the radiation emitted from the cathode caused phosphorescent light to appear on the tube wall near the cathode; and the region of the phosphorescent light could be moved by application of a magnetic field.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-32) In 1869, Plücker's student [Johann Wilhelm Hittorf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johann_Wilhelm_Hittorf "Johann Wilhelm Hittorf") found that a solid body placed in between the cathode and the phosphorescence would cast a shadow upon the phosphorescent region of the tube. Hittorf inferred that there are straight rays emitted from the cathode and that the phosphorescence was caused by the rays striking the tube walls. Furthermore, he also discovered that these rays are deflected by magnets just like lines of current.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-33)

In 1876, the German physicist [Eugen Goldstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eugen_Goldstein "Eugen Goldstein") showed that the rays were emitted perpendicular to the cathode surface, which distinguished between the rays that were emitted from the cathode and the incandescent light. Goldstein dubbed the rays [cathode rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cathode_ray "Cathode ray").[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-leicester-34)[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-Whittaker-35): 393  Decades of experimental and theoretical research involving cathode rays were important in [J. J. Thomson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J._J._Thomson "J. J. Thomson")'s eventual discovery of electrons.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-arabatzis-4) Goldstein also experimented with double cathodes and hypothesized that one ray may repulse another, although he didn't believe that any particles might be involved.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-:0-36)

During the 1870s, the English chemist and physicist Sir [William Crookes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Crookes "William Crookes") developed the first cathode-ray tube to have a [high vacuum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vacuum "Vacuum") inside.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-dekosky-37) He then showed in 1874 that the cathode rays can turn a small paddle wheel when placed in their path. Therefore, he concluded that the rays carried momentum. Furthermore, by applying a magnetic field, he was able to deflect the rays, thereby demonstrating that the beam behaved as though it were negatively charged.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-leicester-34) In 1879, he proposed that these properties could be explained by regarding cathode rays as composed of negatively charged gaseous [molecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecule "Molecule") in a fourth [state of matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_of_matter "State of matter"), in which the mean free path of the particles is so long that collisions may be ignored.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-Whittaker-35): 394–395

In 1883, not yet well-known German physicist [Heinrich Hertz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heinrich_Hertz "Heinrich Hertz") tried to prove that cathode rays are electrically neutral and got what he interpreted as a confident absence of deflection in electrostatic, as opposed to magnetic, field. However, as [J. J. Thomson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J._J._Thomson "J. J. Thomson") explained in 1897, Hertz placed the deflecting electrodes in a highly-conductive area of the tube, resulting in a strong screening effect close to their surface.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-:0-36)

The German-born British physicist [Arthur Schuster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arthur_Schuster "Arthur Schuster") expanded upon Crookes's experiments by placing metal plates parallel to the cathode rays and applying an [electric potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_potential "Electric potential") between the plates.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-schu1890-38) The field deflected the rays toward the positively charged plate, providing further evidence that the rays carried negative charge. By measuring the amount of deflection for a given [electric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_field "Electric field") and [magnetic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_field "Magnetic field"), in 1890 Schuster was able to estimate the [charge-to-mass ratio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass-to-charge_ratio "Mass-to-charge ratio")[[c]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-39) of the ray components. However, this produced a value that was more than a thousand times greater than what was expected, so little credence was given to his calculations at the time.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-leicester-34) This is because it was assumed that the charge carriers were much heavier [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen") or [nitrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen "Nitrogen") atoms.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-schu1890-38) Schuster's estimates would subsequently turn out to be largely correct.

In 1892 [Hendrik Lorentz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hendrik_Lorentz "Hendrik Lorentz") suggested that the mass of these particles (electrons) could be a consequence of their electric charge.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-40)

[![Image 3](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/J.J_Thomson.jpg/250px-J.J_Thomson.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:J.J_Thomson.jpg)

[J. J. Thomson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J._J._Thomson "J. J. Thomson")

While studying naturally [fluorescing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluorescence "Fluorescence") minerals in 1896, the French physicist [Henri Becquerel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Becquerel "Henri Becquerel") discovered that they emitted radiation without any exposure to an external energy source. These [radioactive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radioactive_decay "Radioactive decay") materials became the subject of much interest by scientists, including the New Zealand physicist [Ernest Rutherford](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ernest_Rutherford "Ernest Rutherford") who discovered they emitted particles. He designated these particles [alpha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_particle "Alpha particle") and [beta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beta_particle "Beta particle"), on the basis of their ability to penetrate matter.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-41) In 1900, Becquerel showed that the beta rays emitted by [radium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radium "Radium") could be deflected by an electric field, and that their mass-to-charge ratio was the same as for cathode rays.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-42) This evidence strengthened the view that electrons existed as components of atoms.[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-BaW9091-43)[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-44)

In 1897, the British physicist [J. J. Thomson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J._J._Thomson "J. J. Thomson"), with his colleagues [John S. Townsend](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Sealy_Townsend "John Sealy Townsend") and [H. A. Wilson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harold_A._Wilson_(physicist) "Harold A. Wilson (physicist)"), performed experiments indicating that cathode rays really were unique particles, rather than waves, atoms or molecules as was believed earlier.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-thomson-6) By 1899 he showed that their charge-to-mass ratio, _e_/_m_, was independent of cathode material. He further showed that the negatively charged particles produced by radioactive materials, by heated materials and by illuminated materials were universal.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-thomson-6)[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-45) Thomson measured _m_/_e_ for cathode ray "corpuscles", and made good estimates of the charge _e_, leading to value for the mass _m_, finding a value 1400 times less massive than the least massive ion known: hydrogen.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-Whittaker-35): 364 [[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-thomson-6) In the same year [Emil Wiechert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emil_Wiechert "Emil Wiechert") and [Walter Kaufmann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Walter_Kaufmann_(physicist) "Walter Kaufmann (physicist)") also calculated the _e_/_m_ ratio but did not take the step of interpreting their results as showing a new particle, while J. J. Thomson would subsequently in 1899 give estimates for the electron charge and mass as well: _e_≈6.8×10−10[esu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statcoulomb "Statcoulomb") and _m_≈3×10−26 g.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-46)[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-47)

[![Image 4](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Millikan.jpg/250px-Millikan.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Millikan.jpg)

[Robert Millikan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Andrews_Millikan "Robert Andrews Millikan")

The name "electron" was adopted for these particles by the scientific community, mainly due to the advocation by [G. F. FitzGerald](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_Francis_FitzGerald "George Francis FitzGerald"), [J. Larmor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Larmor "Joseph Larmor"), and [H. A. Lorentz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hendrik_Lorentz "Hendrik Lorentz").[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-OHara1975-48): 273  The term was originally coined by [George Johnstone Stoney](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_Johnstone_Stoney "George Johnstone Stoney") in 1891 as a tentative name for the basic unit of electrical charge (which had then yet to be discovered).[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-49)[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-GJStoney-27)

The electron's charge was more carefully measured by the American physicists [Robert Millikan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Andrews_Millikan "Robert Andrews Millikan") and [Harvey Fletcher](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harvey_Fletcher "Harvey Fletcher") in their [oil-drop experiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oil_drop_experiment "Oil drop experiment") of 1909, the results of which were published in 1911. This experiment used an electric field to prevent a charged droplet of oil from falling as a result of gravity. This device could measure the electric charge from as few as 1–150 ions with an error margin of less than 0.3%. Comparable experiments had been done earlier by Thomson's team,[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-thomson-6) using clouds of charged water droplets generated by electrolysis, and in 1911 by [Abram Ioffe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abram_Ioffe "Abram Ioffe"), who independently obtained the same result as Millikan using charged microparticles of metals, then published his results in 1913.[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-50) However, oil drops were more stable than water drops because of their slower evaporation rate, and thus more suited to precise experimentation over longer periods of time.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-51)

Around the beginning of the twentieth century, it was found that under certain conditions a fast-moving charged particle caused a condensation of [supersaturated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supersaturation "Supersaturation") water vapor along its path. In 1911, [Charles Wilson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Thomson_Rees_Wilson "Charles Thomson Rees Wilson") used this principle to devise his [cloud chamber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cloud_chamber "Cloud chamber") so he could photograph the tracks of charged particles, such as fast-moving electrons.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-52)

[![Image 5: Three concentric circles about a nucleus, with an electron moving from the second to the first circle and releasing a photon](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Bohr_atom_model_%28mul%29.svg/250px-Bohr_atom_model_%28mul%29.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Bohr_atom_model_(mul).svg)

The [Bohr model of the atom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bohr_model "Bohr model"), showing states of an electron with energy [quantized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_number "Quantum number") by the number _n_. An electron dropping to a lower orbit emits a photon equal to the energy difference between the orbits

By 1914, experiments by physicists [Ernest Rutherford](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ernest_Rutherford "Ernest Rutherford"), [Henry Moseley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry_Moseley "Henry Moseley"), [James Franck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Franck "James Franck") and [Gustav Hertz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gustav_Ludwig_Hertz "Gustav Ludwig Hertz") had largely established the structure of an atom as a dense [nucleus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_nucleus "Atomic nucleus") of positive charge surrounded by lower-mass electrons.[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-smirnov-53) In 1913, Danish physicist [Niels Bohr](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Niels_Bohr "Niels Bohr") postulated that electrons resided in quantized energy states, with their energies determined by the angular momentum of the electron's orbit about the nucleus. The electrons could move between those states, or orbits, by the emission or absorption of photons of specific frequencies. By means of these quantized orbits, he accurately explained the [spectral lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectral_line "Spectral line") of the hydrogen atom.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-54) However, Bohr's model failed to account for the relative intensities of the spectral lines and it was unsuccessful in explaining the spectra of more complex atoms.[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-smirnov-53)

Chemical bonds between atoms were explained by [Gilbert Newton Lewis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gilbert_N._Lewis "Gilbert N. Lewis"), who in 1916 proposed that a [covalent bond](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covalent_bond "Covalent bond") between two atoms is maintained by a pair of electrons shared between them.[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-55) Later, in 1927, [Walter Heitler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Walter_Heitler "Walter Heitler") and [Fritz London](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fritz_London "Fritz London") gave the full explanation of the electron-pair formation and chemical bonding in terms of [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics").[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-Arabatzis-56) In 1919, the American chemist [Irving Langmuir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irving_Langmuir "Irving Langmuir") elaborated on the Lewis's static model of the atom and suggested that all electrons were distributed in successive "concentric (nearly) spherical shells, all of equal thickness".[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-57) In turn, he divided the shells into a number of cells each of which contained one pair of electrons. With this model Langmuir was able to qualitatively explain the [chemical properties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_property "Chemical property") of all elements in the periodic table,[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-Arabatzis-56) which were known to largely repeat themselves according to the [periodic law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Periodic_table "Periodic table").[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-58)

In 1924, Austrian physicist [Wolfgang Pauli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Pauli "Wolfgang Pauli") observed that the shell-like structure of the atom could be explained by a set of four parameters that defined every quantum energy state, as long as each state was occupied by no more than a single electron. This prohibition against more than one electron occupying the same quantum energy state became known as the [Pauli exclusion principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pauli_exclusion_principle "Pauli exclusion principle").[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-59) The physical mechanism to explain the fourth parameter, which had two distinct possible values, was provided by the Dutch physicists [Samuel Goudsmit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samuel_Goudsmit "Samuel Goudsmit") and [George Uhlenbeck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_Uhlenbeck "George Uhlenbeck"). In 1925, they suggested that an electron, in addition to the angular momentum of its orbit, possesses an intrinsic angular momentum and [magnetic dipole moment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_moment "Magnetic moment").[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-smirnov-53)[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-60) This is analogous to the rotation of the Earth on its axis as it orbits the Sun. The intrinsic angular momentum became known as [spin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_(physics) "Spin (physics)"), and explained the previously mysterious splitting of spectral lines observed with a high-resolution [spectrograph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectrometer "Spectrometer"); this phenomenon is known as [fine structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fine_structure "Fine structure") splitting.[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-61)

Further information: [§Quantum properties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#Quantum_properties)

In his 1924 dissertation _\_Recherches sur la théorie des quanta\__ (Research on Quantum Theory), French physicist [Louis de Broglie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis_de_Broglie "Louis de Broglie") hypothesized that all matter can be represented as a [de Broglie wave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matter_wave "Matter wave") in the manner of [light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light "Light").[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-de_broglie-62) That is, under the appropriate conditions, electrons and other matter would show properties of either particles or waves. The [corpuscular properties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corpuscular_theory_of_light "Corpuscular theory of light") of a particle are demonstrated when it is shown to have a localized position in space along its trajectory at any given moment.[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-63) The wave-like nature of light is displayed, for example, when a beam of light is passed through parallel slits thereby creating [interference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interference_(wave_propagation) "Interference (wave propagation)") patterns. In 1927, [George Paget Thomson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_Paget_Thomson "George Paget Thomson") and Alexander Reid discovered the interference effect was produced when a beam of electrons was passed through thin celluloid foils and later metal films, and by American physicists [Clinton Davisson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clinton_Davisson "Clinton Davisson") and [Lester Germer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lester_Germer "Lester Germer") by the reflection of electrons from a crystal of [nickel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nickel "Nickel").[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-64) Alexander Reid, who was Thomson's graduate student, performed the first experiments but he died soon after in a motorcycle accident[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-65) and is rarely mentioned.

[![Image 6: A spherically symmetric blue cloud that decreases in intensity from the center outward](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Orbital_s1.png/250px-Orbital_s1.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Orbital_s1.png)

In quantum mechanics, the behavior of an electron in an atom is described by an [orbital](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_orbital "Atomic orbital"), which is a probability distribution rather than an orbit. In the figure, the shading indicates the relative probability to "find" the electron, having the energy corresponding to the given [quantum numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_number "Quantum number"), at that point.

De Broglie's prediction of a wave nature for electrons led [Erwin Schrödinger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erwin_Schr%C3%B6dinger "Erwin Schrödinger") to postulate a wave equation for electrons moving under the influence of the nucleus in the atom. In 1926, this equation, the [Schrödinger equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schr%C3%B6dinger_equation "Schrödinger equation"), successfully described how electron waves propagated.[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-66) Rather than yielding a solution that determined the location of an electron over time, this wave equation also could be used to predict the probability of finding an electron near a position, especially a position near where the electron was bound in space, for which the electron wave equations did not change in time. This approach led to a second formulation of [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics") (the first by Heisenberg in 1925), and solutions of Schrödinger's equation, like Heisenberg's, provided derivations of the energy states of an electron in a hydrogen atom that were equivalent to those that had been derived first by Bohr in 1913, and that were known to reproduce the hydrogen spectrum.[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-67) Once spin and the interaction between multiple electrons were describable, quantum mechanics made it possible to predict the configuration of electrons in atoms with atomic numbers greater than hydrogen.[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-68)

In 1928, building on Wolfgang Pauli's work, [Paul Dirac](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Dirac "Paul Dirac") produced a model of the electron– the [Dirac equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dirac_equation "Dirac equation"), consistent with [relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principle_of_relativity "Principle of relativity") theory, by applying relativistic and symmetry considerations to the [hamiltonian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamiltonian_(quantum_mechanics) "Hamiltonian (quantum mechanics)") formulation of the quantum mechanics of the electromagnetic field.[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-69) In order to resolve some problems within his relativistic equation, Dirac developed in 1930 a model of the vacuum as an infinite sea of particles with negative energy, later dubbed the [Dirac sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dirac_sea "Dirac sea"). This led him to predict the existence of a positron, the [antimatter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antimatter "Antimatter") counterpart of the electron.[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-70) This particle was discovered in 1932 by [Carl Anderson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_David_Anderson "Carl David Anderson"), who proposed calling standard electrons _negatrons_ and using _electron_ as a generic term to describe both the positively and negatively charged variants.[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-71)

In 1947, [Willis Lamb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Willis_Lamb "Willis Lamb"), working in collaboration with graduate student [Robert Retherford](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Retherford "Robert Retherford"), found that certain quantum states of the hydrogen atom, which should have the same energy, were shifted in relation to each other; the difference came to be called the [Lamb shift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lamb_shift "Lamb shift"). About the same time, [Polykarp Kusch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polykarp_Kusch "Polykarp Kusch"), working with [Henry M. Foley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry_M._Foley "Henry M. Foley"), discovered the magnetic moment of the electron is slightly larger than predicted by Dirac's theory. This small difference was later called [anomalous magnetic dipole moment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anomalous_magnetic_dipole_moment "Anomalous magnetic dipole moment") of the electron. This difference was later explained by the theory of [quantum electrodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_electrodynamics "Quantum electrodynamics"), developed by [Sin-Itiro Tomonaga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sin-Itiro_Tomonaga "Sin-Itiro Tomonaga"), [Julian Schwinger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Julian_Schwinger "Julian Schwinger") and [Richard Feynman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Feynman "Richard Feynman") in the late 1940s.[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-72)

### Particle accelerators

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Electron&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Particle accelerators")]

With the development of the [particle accelerator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_accelerator "Particle accelerator") during the first half of the twentieth century, physicists began to delve deeper into the properties of [subatomic particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subatomic_particle "Subatomic particle").[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-73) The first successful attempt to accelerate electrons using [electromagnetic induction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_induction "Electromagnetic induction") was made in 1942 by [Donald Kerst](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Donald_William_Kerst "Donald William Kerst"). His initial [betatron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Betatron "Betatron") reached energies of 2.3 MeV, while subsequent betatrons achieved 300 MeV. In 1947, [synchrotron radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synchrotron_radiation "Synchrotron radiation") was discovered with a 70 MeV electron synchrotron at [General Electric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Electric "General Electric"). This radiation was caused by the acceleration of electrons through a magnetic field as they moved near the speed of light.[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-74)

With a beam energy of 1.5 GeV, the first high-energy particle [collider](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collider "Collider") was [ADONE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ADONE "ADONE"), which began operations in 1968.[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-75) This device accelerated electrons and positrons in opposite directions, effectively doubling the energy of their collision when compared to striking a static target with an electron.[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-76) The [Large Electron–Positron Collider](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Large_Electron%E2%80%93Positron_Collider "Large Electron–Positron Collider") (LEP) at [CERN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CERN "CERN"), which was operational from 1989 to 2000, achieved collision energies of 209 GeV and made important measurements for the [Standard Model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model "Standard Model") of particle physics.[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-77)[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-78)

### Confinement of individual electrons

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Electron&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Confinement of individual electrons")]

Individual electrons can now be easily confined in ultra small (_L_ = 20 nm, _W_ = 20 nm) CMOS transistors operated at cryogenic temperature over a range of about 4[K](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvin "Kelvin") (−269°C) to 15[K](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvin "Kelvin") (−258°C).[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-79) The electron wavefunction spreads in a semiconductor lattice and negligibly interacts with the valence band electrons, so it can be treated in the single particle formalism, by replacing its mass with the [effective-mass tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Effective_mass_(solid-state_physics) "Effective mass (solid-state physics)").

[![Image 7: A table with four rows and four columns, with each cell containing a particle identifier](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Standard_Model_of_Elementary_Particles.svg/330px-Standard_Model_of_Elementary_Particles.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Standard_Model_of_Elementary_Particles.svg)

Standard Model of elementary particles. The electron (symbol e) is on the left.

In the [Standard Model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model "Standard Model") of particle physics, electrons belong to the group of subatomic particles called [leptons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lepton "Lepton"), which are believed to be fundamental or [elementary particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementary_particle "Elementary particle"). Electrons have the lowest mass of any charged lepton (or electrically charged particle of any type) and belong to the first [generation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generation_(particle_physics) "Generation (particle physics)") of fundamental particles.[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-80) The second and third generation contain charged leptons, the [muon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muon "Muon") and the [tau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tau_(particle) "Tau (particle)"), which are identical to the electron in charge, [spin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_(physics) "Spin (physics)") and [interactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_interaction "Fundamental interaction"), but are more massive. Leptons differ from the other basic constituent of matter, the [quarks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quark "Quark"), by their lack of [strong interaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strong_interaction "Strong interaction"). All members of the lepton group are fermions because they all have half-odd integer spin; the electron has spin ⁠1/2⁠.[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-raith-81)

### Fundamental properties

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Electron&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Fundamental properties")]

The [invariant mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invariant_mass "Invariant mass") of an electron is approximately 9.109×10−31 kg,[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-physconst-me-7) or 5.486×10−4 Da. Due to [mass–energy equivalence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass%E2%80%93energy_equivalence "Mass–energy equivalence"), this corresponds to a rest energy of 8.19×10−14 J (0.511 MeV). The ratio between the mass of a [proton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton "Proton") and that of an electron is about 1836.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-nist_codata_mu-16)[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-Zombeck2007-82) Astronomical measurements show that the [proton-to-electron mass ratio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton-to-electron_mass_ratio "Proton-to-electron mass ratio") has held the same value, as is predicted by the Standard Model, for at least half the [age of the universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Age_of_the_universe "Age of the universe").[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-83)

Electrons have an [electric charge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_charge "Electric charge") of −1.602 176 634×10−19[C](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coulomb "Coulomb"),[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-CODATA-84) which is used as a standard unit of charge for subatomic particles, and is also called the [elementary charge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementary_charge "Elementary charge"). Within the limits of experimental accuracy, the electron charge is identical to the charge of a proton, but with the opposite sign.[[82]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-85) The electron is commonly symbolized by e−

, and the positron is symbolized by e+

.[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-raith-81)[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-CODATA-84)

The electron has an intrinsic [angular momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum "Angular momentum") or spin of ⁠_ħ_/2⁠.[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-CODATA-84) This property is usually stated by referring to the electron as a [spin-1/2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin-1/2 "Spin-1/2") particle.[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-raith-81) For such particles the spin magnitude is ⁠_ħ_/2⁠,[[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-Gupta2001-86) while the result of the measurement of a [projection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projection_(mathematics) "Projection (mathematics)") of the spin on any axis can only be ±⁠_ħ_/2⁠. In addition to spin, the electron has an intrinsic [magnetic moment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_magnetic_moment "Electron magnetic moment") along its spin axis.[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-CODATA-84) It is approximately equal to one [Bohr magneton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bohr_magneton "Bohr magneton"),[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-Hanneke-87)[[d]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-88) which is a physical constant that is equal to 9.274 010 0657(29)×10−24 J⋅T−1.[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-physconst-muB-89) The orientation of the spin with respect to the momentum of the electron defines the property of elementary particles known as [helicity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helicity_(particle_physics) "Helicity (particle physics)").[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-anastopoulos-90)

The electron has no known [substructure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Preon "Preon").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-prl50-1)[[87]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-91) Nevertheless, in [condensed matter physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics "Condensed matter physics"), [spin–charge separation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin%E2%80%93charge_separation "Spin–charge separation") can occur in some materials. In such cases, electrons 'split' into three independent particles, the [spinon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spinon "Spinon"), the [orbiton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbiton "Orbiton") and the [holon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holon_(physics) "Holon (physics)") (or chargon). The electron can always be theoretically considered as a bound state of the three, with the spinon carrying the spin of the electron, the orbiton carrying the orbital degree of freedom and the chargon carrying the charge, but in certain conditions they can behave as independent [quasiparticles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasiparticles "Quasiparticles").[[88]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-bbc-92)[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-93)[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-gov-94)

The issue of the radius of the electron is a challenging problem of modern theoretical physics. The admission of the hypothesis of a finite radius of the electron is incompatible to the premises of the theory of relativity. On the other hand, a point-like electron (zero radius) generates serious mathematical difficulties due to the [self-energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-energy "Self-energy") of the electron tending to infinity.[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-95) Observation of a single electron in a [Penning trap](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Penning_trap "Penning trap") suggests the upper limit of the particle's radius to be 10−22 meters.[[92]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-96) The upper bound of the electron radius of 10−18 meters[[93]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-97) can be derived using the [uncertainty relation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uncertainty_relation "Uncertainty relation") in energy. There _is_ also a physical constant called the "[classical electron radius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_electron_radius "Classical electron radius")", with the much larger value of 2.8179×10−15 m, greater than the radius of the proton. However, the terminology comes from a simplistic calculation that ignores the effects of [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics"); in reality, the so-called classical electron radius has little to do with the true fundamental structure of the electron.[[94]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-98)[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-HakenWolfBrewer2005-99)[[e]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-100)

There are [elementary particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementary_particle "Elementary particle") that spontaneously [decay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_decay "Particle decay") into less massive particles. An example is the [muon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muon "Muon"), with a [mean lifetime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponential_decay#Mean_lifetime "Exponential decay") of 2.2×10−6 seconds, which decays into an electron, a muon [neutrino](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutrino "Neutrino") and an electron [antineutrino](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutrino#Antineutrinos "Neutrino"). The electron, on the other hand, is thought to be stable on theoretical grounds: the electron is the least massive particle with non-zero electric charge, so its decay would violate [charge conservation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charge_conservation "Charge conservation").[[96]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-101) The experimental lower bound for the electron's mean lifetime is 6.6×10 28 years, at a 90% [confidence level](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confidence_interval "Confidence interval").[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-bx2015-11)[[97]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-102)[[98]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-103)

As with all particles, electrons can act as waves. This is called the [wave–particle duality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wave%E2%80%93particle_duality "Wave–particle duality") and can be demonstrated using the [double-slit experiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double-slit_experiment "Double-slit experiment").

The wave-like nature of the electron allows it to pass through two parallel slits simultaneously, rather than just one slit as would be the case for a classical particle. In quantum mechanics, the wave-like property of one particle can be described mathematically as a [complex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number")-valued function, the [wave function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wave_function "Wave function"), commonly denoted by the [Greek letter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_alphabet "Greek alphabet")[psi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Psi_(Greek) "Psi (Greek)") (_ψ_). When the [absolute value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_value#Complex_numbers "Absolute value") of this function is [squared](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_(algebra) "Square (algebra)"), it gives the probability that a particle will be observed near a location—a [probability density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability_density_function "Probability density function").[[99]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-munowitz-104): 162–218

[![Image 8: A three dimensional projection of a two dimensional plot. There are symmetric hills along one axis and symmetric valleys along the other, roughly giving a saddle-shape](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Asymmetricwave2.png/250px-Asymmetricwave2.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Asymmetricwave2.png)

Example of an antisymmetric wave function for a quantum state of [two identical fermions in a one-dimensional box](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_in_a_box "Particle in a box"), with each horizontal axis corresponding to the position of one particle. If the particles swap position, the wave function inverts its sign.

Electrons are [identical particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Identical_particles "Identical particles") because they cannot be distinguished from each other by their intrinsic physical properties. In quantum mechanics, this means that a pair of interacting electrons must be able to swap positions without an observable change to the state of the system. The wave function of fermions, including electrons, is antisymmetric, meaning that it changes sign when two electrons are swapped; that is, _ψ_(_r_ 1, _r_ 2) = −_ψ_(_r_ 2, _r_ 1), where the variables _r_ 1 and _r_ 2 correspond to the first and second electrons, respectively. Since the absolute value is not changed by a sign swap, this corresponds to equal probabilities. [Bosons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boson "Boson"), such as the photon, have symmetric wave functions instead.[[99]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-munowitz-104): 162–218

In the case of antisymmetry, solutions of the wave equation for interacting electrons result in a [zero probability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero_probability "Zero probability") that each pair will occupy the same location or state. This is responsible for the [Pauli exclusion principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pauli_exclusion_principle "Pauli exclusion principle"), which precludes any two electrons from occupying the same quantum state. This principle explains many of the properties of electrons. For example, it causes groups of bound electrons to occupy different [orbitals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_orbital "Atomic orbital") in an atom, rather than all overlapping each other in the same orbit.[[99]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-munowitz-104): 162–218

In a simplified picture, which often tends to give the wrong idea but may serve to illustrate some aspects, every photon spends some time as a combination of a virtual electron plus its antiparticle, the virtual positron, which rapidly [annihilate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Annihilation "Annihilation") each other shortly thereafter.[[100]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-105) The combination of the energy variation needed to create these particles, and the time during which they exist, fall under the threshold of detectability expressed by the [Heisenberg uncertainty relation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uncertainty_principle "Uncertainty principle"), Δ _E_·Δ _t_≥_ħ_. In effect, the energy needed to create these virtual particles, Δ _E_, can be "borrowed" from the [vacuum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vacuum_state "Vacuum state") for a period of time, Δ _t_, so that their product is no more than the [reduced Planck constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reduced_Planck_constant "Reduced Planck constant"), _ħ_ ≈ 6.6×10−16 eV·s. Thus, for a virtual electron, Δ _t_ is at most 1.3×10−21 s.[[101]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-taylor-106)

[![Image 9: A sphere with a minus sign at lower left symbolizes the electron, while pairs of spheres with plus and minus signs show the virtual particles](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Virtual_pairs_near_electron.png/250px-Virtual_pairs_near_electron.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Virtual_pairs_near_electron.png)

A schematic depiction of virtual electron–positron pairs appearing at random near an electron (at lower left)

While an electron–positron virtual pair is in existence, the [Coulomb force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coulomb%27s_law "Coulomb's law") from the ambient [electric field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_field "Electric field") surrounding an electron causes a created positron to be attracted to the original electron, while a created electron experiences a repulsion. This causes what is called [vacuum polarization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vacuum_polarization "Vacuum polarization"). In effect, the vacuum behaves like a medium having a [dielectric permittivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relative_permittivity "Relative permittivity") more than [unity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1 "1"). Thus the effective charge of an electron is actually smaller than its true value, and the charge decreases with increasing distance from the electron.[[102]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-genz-107)[[103]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-108) This polarization was confirmed experimentally in 1997 using the Japanese [TRISTAN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/KEKB_(accelerator) "KEKB (accelerator)") particle accelerator.[[104]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-109) Virtual particles cause a comparable [shielding effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shielding_effect "Shielding effect") for the mass of the electron.[[105]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-110)

The interaction with virtual particles also explains the small (about 0.1%) deviation of the intrinsic magnetic moment of the electron from the Bohr magneton (the [anomalous magnetic moment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anomalous_magnetic_dipole_moment "Anomalous magnetic dipole moment")).[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-Hanneke-87)[[106]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-111) The extraordinarily precise agreement of this predicted difference with the experimentally determined value is viewed as one of the great achievements of [quantum electrodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_electrodynamics "Quantum electrodynamics").[[107]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-112)

The apparent paradox in [classical physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_physics "Classical physics") of a point particle electron having intrinsic angular momentum and magnetic moment can be explained by the formation of [virtual photons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virtual_particle "Virtual particle") in the electric field generated by the electron. These photons can heuristically be thought of as causing the electron to shift about in a jittery fashion (known as [zitterbewegung](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zitterbewegung "Zitterbewegung")), which results in a net circular motion with [precession](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Precession "Precession").[[108]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-113) This motion produces both the spin and the magnetic moment of the electron.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-curtis74-15) In atoms, this creation of virtual photons explains the [Lamb shift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lamb_shift "Lamb shift") observed in [spectral lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectral_line "Spectral line").[[102]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-genz-107) The Compton Wavelength shows that near elementary particles such as the electron, the uncertainty of the energy allows for the creation of virtual particles near the electron. This wavelength explains the "static" of virtual particles around elementary particles at a close distance.

An electron generates an electric field that exerts an attractive force on a particle with a positive charge, such as the proton, and a repulsive force on a particle with a negative charge. The strength of this force in nonrelativistic approximation is determined by [Coulomb's inverse square law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coulomb%27s_law "Coulomb's law").[[109]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-Griffiths1998-114): 58–61  When an electron is in motion, it generates a [magnetic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_field "Magnetic field").[[99]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-munowitz-104): 140  The [Ampère–Maxwell law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amp%C3%A8re%27s_circuital_law "Ampère's circuital law") relates the magnetic field to the mass motion of electrons (the [current](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_current "Electric current")) with respect to an observer. This property of induction supplies the magnetic field that drives an [electric motor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_motor "Electric motor").[[110]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-115) The electromagnetic field of an arbitrary moving charged particle is expressed by the [Liénard–Wiechert potentials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Li%C3%A9nard%E2%80%93Wiechert_potential "Liénard–Wiechert potential"), which are valid even when the particle's speed is close to that of light ([relativistic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_relativity "Special relativity")).[[109]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-Griffiths1998-114): 429–434

[![Image 10: A graph with arcs showing the motion of charged particles](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Lorentz_force.svg/250px-Lorentz_force.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Lorentz_force.svg)

A particle with charge _q_ (at left) is moving with velocity _v_ through a magnetic field _B_ that is oriented toward the viewer. For an electron, _q_ is negative, so it follows a curved trajectory toward the top.

When an electron is moving through a magnetic field, it is subject to the [Lorentz force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentz_force "Lorentz force") that acts perpendicularly to the plane defined by the magnetic field and the electron velocity. This [centripetal force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_force "Centripetal force") causes the electron to follow a [helical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helix "Helix") trajectory through the field at a radius called the [gyroradius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gyroradius "Gyroradius"). The acceleration from this curving motion induces the electron to radiate energy in the form of synchrotron radiation.[[111]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-116)[[f]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-117)[[99]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-munowitz-104): 160  The energy emission in turn causes a recoil of the electron, known as the [Abraham–Lorentz–Dirac Force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abraham%E2%80%93Lorentz_force#Abraham%E2%80%93Lorentz%E2%80%93Dirac_Force "Abraham–Lorentz force"), which creates a friction that slows the electron. This force is caused by a [back-reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back-reaction "Back-reaction") of the electron's own field upon itself.[[112]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-118)

[![Image 11: A curve shows the motion of the electron, a red dot shows the nucleus, and a wiggly line the emitted photon](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Bremsstrahlung.svg/250px-Bremsstrahlung.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Bremsstrahlung.svg)

Here, [Bremsstrahlung](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bremsstrahlung "Bremsstrahlung") is produced by an electron _e_ deflected by the electric field of an atomic nucleus. The energy change _E_ 2−_E_ 1 determines the frequency _f_ of the emitted photon.

Photons mediate electromagnetic interactions between particles in [quantum electrodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_electrodynamics "Quantum electrodynamics"). An isolated electron at a constant velocity cannot emit or absorb a real photon; doing so would violate [conservation of energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservation_of_energy "Conservation of energy") and [momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum "Momentum"). Instead, virtual photons can transfer momentum between two charged particles. This exchange of virtual photons, for example, generates the Coulomb force.[[113]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-119) Energy emission can occur when a moving electron is deflected by a charged particle, such as a proton. The deceleration of the electron results in the emission of [Bremsstrahlung](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bremsstrahlung "Bremsstrahlung") radiation.[[114]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-120)

An inelastic collision between a photon (light) and a solitary (free) electron is called [Compton scattering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compton_scattering "Compton scattering"). This collision results in a transfer of momentum and energy between the particles, which modifies the wavelength of the photon by an amount called the [Compton shift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compton_scattering "Compton scattering").[[g]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-121) The maximum magnitude of this wavelength shift is _h_/_m_ e _c_, which is known as the [Compton wavelength](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compton_wavelength "Compton wavelength").[[115]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-122) For an electron, it has a value of 2.43×10−12 m.[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-CODATA-84) When the wavelength of the light is long (for instance, the wavelength of the [visible light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light "Light") is 0.4–0.7 μm) the wavelength shift becomes negligible. Such interaction between the light and free electrons is called [Thomson scattering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomson_scattering "Thomson scattering") or linear Thomson scattering.[[116]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-Chen1998-123)

The relative strength of the electromagnetic interaction between two charged particles, such as an electron and a proton, is given by the [fine-structure constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fine-structure_constant "Fine-structure constant"). This value is a dimensionless quantity formed by the ratio of two energies: the electrostatic energy of attraction (or repulsion) at a separation of one Compton wavelength, and the rest energy of the charge. It is given by _α_≈0.007 297 353,[[117]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-physconst-alpha-124) which is approximately equal to ⁠1/137⁠.

When electrons and positrons collide, they [annihilate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron%E2%80%93positron_annihilation "Electron–positron annihilation") each other, giving rise to two or more gamma ray photons. If the electron and positron have negligible momentum, a [positronium atom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positronium "Positronium") can form before annihilation results in two or three gamma ray photons whose energies total 1.022 MeV.[[118]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-125)[[119]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-126) On the other hand, a high-energy photon can transform into an electron and a positron by a process called [pair production](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pair_production "Pair production"), but only in the presence of a nearby charged particle, such as a nucleus.[[120]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-127)[[121]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-128)

In the theory of [electroweak interaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electroweak_interaction "Electroweak interaction"), the [left-handed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chirality_(physics) "Chirality (physics)") component of electron's wavefunction forms a [weak isospin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weak_isospin "Weak isospin") doublet with the [electron neutrino](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutrino "Neutrino"). This means that during [weak interactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weak_interaction "Weak interaction"), electron neutrinos behave like electrons. Either member of this doublet can undergo a [charged current](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charged_current "Charged current") interaction by emitting or absorbing a [W](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/W_boson "W boson") and be converted into the other member. Charge is conserved during this reaction because the W boson also carries a charge, canceling out any net change during the transmutation. Charged current interactions are responsible for the phenomenon of [beta decay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beta_decay "Beta decay") in a [radioactive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radioactive_decay "Radioactive decay") atom. Both the electron and electron neutrino can undergo a [neutral current](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutral_current "Neutral current") interaction via a [Z 0](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Z_boson "Z boson") exchange, and this is responsible for neutrino–electron [elastic scattering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elastic_scattering "Elastic scattering").[[122]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-quigg-129)

### Atoms and molecules

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Electron&action=edit&section=17 "Edit section: Atoms and molecules")]

Main article: [Atom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atom "Atom")

[![Image 12: A table of five rows and five columns, with each cell portraying a color-coded probability density](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Hydrogen_Density_Plots.png/330px-Hydrogen_Density_Plots.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Hydrogen_Density_Plots.png)

Probability densities for the first few hydrogen atom orbitals, seen in cross-section. The energy level of a bound electron determines the orbital it occupies, and the color reflects the probability of finding the electron at a given position.

An electron can be _bound_ to the nucleus of an atom by the attractive Coulomb force. A system of one or more electrons bound to a nucleus is called an atom. If the number of electrons is different from the nucleus's electrical charge, such an atom is called an [ion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ion "Ion"). The wave-like behavior of a bound electron is described by a function called an [atomic orbital](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_orbital "Atomic orbital"). Each orbital has its own set of quantum numbers such as energy, angular momentum, and projection of angular momentum. Only a discrete set of these orbitals exist around the nucleus. According to the Pauli exclusion principle, each orbital can be occupied by up to two electrons, which must differ in their [spin quantum number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_quantum_number "Spin quantum number").

Electrons can transfer between different orbitals by the emission or absorption of photons with an energy that matches the difference in potential between the orbitals.[[123]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-Tipler2003-130): 159–160  Other methods of orbital transfer include collisions with particles, such as electrons, and the [Auger effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Auger_effect "Auger effect").[[124]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-131) To escape the atom, the energy of the electron must be increased above its [binding energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ionization_energy "Ionization energy") to the atom. This occurs, for example, with the [photoelectric effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect "Photoelectric effect"), where an incident photon exceeding the atom's [ionization energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ionization_energy "Ionization energy") is absorbed by the electron.[[123]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-Tipler2003-130): 127–132

The orbital angular momentum of electrons is [quantized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum_operator#Quantization "Angular momentum operator"). Because the electron is charged, it produces an orbital magnetic moment that is proportional to the angular momentum. The net magnetic moment of an atom is equal to the vector sum of orbital and spin magnetic moments of all electrons and the nucleus. The magnetic moment of the nucleus is negligible compared with that of the electrons. The magnetic moments of the electrons that occupy the same orbital, called paired electrons, cancel each other out.[[125]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-132)

The [chemical bond](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_bond "Chemical bond") between atoms occurs as a result of electromagnetic interactions, as described by the laws of quantum mechanics.[[126]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-133) The strongest bonds are formed by the [sharing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covalent_bond "Covalent bond") or [transfer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_transfer "Electron transfer") of electrons between atoms, allowing the formation of [molecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecule "Molecule").[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-Pauling-18) Within a molecule, electrons move under the influence of several nuclei, and occupy [molecular orbitals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_orbital "Molecular orbital"); much as they can occupy atomic orbitals in isolated atoms.[[127]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-134) A fundamental factor in these molecular structures is the existence of [electron pairs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_pair "Electron pair"). These are electrons with opposed spins, allowing them to occupy the same molecular orbital without violating the Pauli exclusion principle (much like in atoms). Different molecular orbitals have different spatial distribution of the electron density. For instance, electrons can be found with the maximal probability in a relatively small volume between the nuclei when in bonded pairs (the pairs that actually bind atoms together). In contrast, electrons are distributed in a large volume around nuclei in non-bonded pairs.[[128]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-135)

[![Image 13: Four bolts of lightning strike the ground](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Lightning_over_Oradea_Romania_cropped.jpg/250px-Lightning_over_Oradea_Romania_cropped.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Lightning_over_Oradea_Romania_cropped.jpg)

A [lightning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lightning "Lightning") discharge consists primarily of a flow of electrons.[[129]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-136) The electric potential needed for lightning can be generated by a triboelectric effect.[[130]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-137)[[131]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-138)

An object has a net electric charge if the total negative charge provided by electrons does not equal the positive charge from the nuclei. When there is an excess of electrons, the object is said to be negatively charged. When there is a lack of electrons, the object is said to be positively charged. When the number of electrons and the number of protons are equal, their charges cancel each other and the object is said to be electrically neutral. A macroscopic body can develop an electric charge through rubbing, by the [triboelectric effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triboelectric_effect "Triboelectric effect").[[132]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-139)

Independent electrons moving in vacuum are termed _free_ electrons. Electrons in metals also behave as if they were free. In reality the particles that are commonly termed electrons in metals and other solids are quasi-electrons—[quasiparticles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasiparticle "Quasiparticle"), which have the same electrical charge, spin, and magnetic moment as real electrons but might have a different mass.[[133]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-Liang-fu_Lou-140) When free electrons – both in vacuum and metals – move, they produce a [net flow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flow_network "Flow network") of charge called an [electric current](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_current "Electric current"), which generates a magnetic field. Likewise a current can be created by a changing magnetic field. These interactions are described mathematically by [Maxwell's equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxwell%27s_equations "Maxwell's equations").[[134]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-141)

At a given temperature, each material has an [electrical conductivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrical_resistivity_and_conductivity "Electrical resistivity and conductivity") that determines the value of electric current when an [electric potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_potential "Electric potential") is applied. Examples of good conductors include metals such as copper and gold, whereas glass and [Teflon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polytetrafluoroethylene "Polytetrafluoroethylene") are poor conductors. In any [dielectric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dielectric "Dielectric") material, the electrons remain bound to their respective atoms and the material behaves as an [insulator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Insulator_(electricity) "Insulator (electricity)"). Most [semiconductors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semiconductor "Semiconductor") have a variable level of conductivity that lies between the extremes of conduction and insulation.[[135]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-142) On the other hand, [metals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metallic_bond "Metallic bond") have an [electronic band structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronic_band_structure "Electronic band structure") containing partially filled electronic bands. The presence of such bands allows electrons in metals to behave as if they were free or [delocalized electrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Delocalized_electron "Delocalized electron"). These electrons are not associated with specific atoms, so when an electric field is applied, they are free to move like a gas (called [Fermi gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermi_gas "Fermi gas"))[[136]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-ziman-143) through the material much like free electrons.

Because of collisions between electrons and atoms, the [drift velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drift_velocity "Drift velocity") of electrons in a conductor is on the order of millimeters per second. However, the speed at which a change of current at one point in the material causes changes in currents in other parts of the material, the [velocity of propagation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wave_propagation_speed "Wave propagation speed"), is typically about 75% of light speed.[[137]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-144) This occurs because electrical signals propagate as a wave, with the velocity dependent on the [dielectric constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relative_permittivity "Relative permittivity") of the material.[[138]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-145)

Metals make relatively good conductors of heat, primarily because the delocalized electrons are free to transport thermal energy between atoms. However, unlike electrical conductivity, the thermal conductivity of a metal is nearly independent of temperature. This is expressed mathematically by the [Wiedemann–Franz law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wiedemann%E2%80%93Franz_law "Wiedemann–Franz law"),[[136]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-ziman-143) which states that the ratio of [thermal conductivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermal_conductivity "Thermal conductivity") to the electrical conductivity is proportional to the temperature. The thermal disorder in the metallic lattice increases the electrical [resistivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrical_resistivity_and_conductivity "Electrical resistivity and conductivity") of the material, producing a temperature dependence for electric current.[[139]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-durrant-146)

When cooled below a point called the [critical temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Critical_point_(thermodynamics) "Critical point (thermodynamics)"), materials can undergo a phase transition in which they lose all resistivity to electric current, in a process known as [superconductivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superconductivity "Superconductivity"). In [BCS theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BCS_theory "BCS theory"), pairs of electrons called [Cooper pairs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cooper_pair "Cooper pair") have their motion coupled to nearby matter via lattice vibrations called [phonons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phonon "Phonon"), thereby avoiding the collisions with atoms that normally create electrical resistance.[[140]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-147) (Cooper pairs have a radius of roughly 100 nm, so they can overlap each other.)[[141]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-148) However, the mechanism by which [higher temperature superconductors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unconventional_superconductor "Unconventional superconductor") operate remains uncertain.

Electrons inside conducting solids, which are quasi-particles themselves, when tightly confined at temperatures close to [absolute zero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_zero "Absolute zero"), behave as though they had split into three other [quasiparticles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasiparticle "Quasiparticle"): [spinons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spinon "Spinon"), [orbitons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbiton "Orbiton") and [holons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holon_(physics) "Holon (physics)").[[142]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-149)[[143]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-150) The former carries spin and magnetic moment, the next carries its orbital location while the latter electrical charge.

According to [Einstein's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Einstein "Albert Einstein") theory of [special relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_relativity "Special relativity"), as an electron's speed approaches the [speed of light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light "Speed of light"), from an observer's point of view its [relativistic mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass_in_special_relativity "Mass in special relativity") increases, thereby making it more and more difficult to accelerate it from within the observer's frame of reference. The speed of an electron can approach, but never reach, the speed of light in vacuum, _c_. However, when relativistic electrons—that is, electrons moving at a speed close to _c_—are injected into a dielectric medium such as water, where the local speed of light is significantly less than _c_, the electrons temporarily travel faster than light in the medium. As they interact with the medium, they generate a faint light called [Cherenkov radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cherenkov_radiation "Cherenkov radiation").[[144]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-151)

[![Image 14: The plot starts at zero and curves sharply upward toward the right](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Lorentz_factor.svg/250px-Lorentz_factor.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Lorentz_factor.svg)

Lorentz factor as a function of velocity. It starts at value 1 and goes to infinity as _v_ approaches _c_.

The effects of special relativity are based on a quantity known as the [Lorentz factor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentz_factor "Lorentz factor"), defined as ![Image 15: {\displaystyle \textstyle \gamma =1/{\sqrt {1-{v^{2}}/{c^{2}}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a81d755db6cbfd78312d2c1f8436aa5b97011ed3), where _v_ is the speed of the particle. The kinetic energy _K_ e of an electron moving with velocity _v_ is:

![Image 16: {\displaystyle \displaystyle K_{\mathrm {e} }=(\gamma -1)m_{\mathrm {e} }c^{2},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e42637fd9232874fb72329a18d1ce7fdfc5addb8)
where _m_ e is the mass of electron. For example, the [Stanford linear accelerator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SLAC_National_Accelerator_Laboratory "SLAC National Accelerator Laboratory") can [accelerate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acceleration "Acceleration") an electron to roughly 51 GeV.[[145]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-152) Since an electron behaves as a wave, at a given velocity it has a characteristic [de Broglie wavelength](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matter_wave "Matter wave"). This is given by _λ_ e=_h_/_p_ where _h_ is the [Planck constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant "Planck constant") and _p_ is the momentum.[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-de_broglie-62) For the 51 GeV electron above, the wavelength is about 2.4×10−17 m, small enough to explore structures well below the size of an atomic nucleus.[[146]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-153)

[![Image 17: A photon approaches the nucleus from the left, with the resulting electron and positron moving off to the right](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Pair_production.png/250px-Pair_production.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Pair_production.png)

[Pair production](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pair_production "Pair production") of an electron and positron, caused by the close approach of a photon with an atomic nucleus. The lightning symbol represents an exchange of a virtual photon, thus an electric force acts. The angle between the particles is very small.[[147]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-154)

The [Big Bang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang "Big Bang") theory is the most widely accepted scientific theory to explain the early stages in the evolution of the Universe.[[148]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-155) For the first millisecond of the Big Bang, the temperatures were over 10 billion[kelvins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvin "Kelvin") and photons had mean energies over a million [electronvolts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronvolt "Electronvolt"). These photons were sufficiently energetic that they could react with each other to form pairs of electrons and positrons. Likewise, positron–electron pairs annihilated each other and emitted energetic photons:

[γ](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon "Photon") + γ ↔ [e+](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positron "Positron") + e−

An equilibrium between electrons, positrons and photons was maintained during this phase of the evolution of the Universe. After 15 seconds had passed, however, the temperature of the universe dropped below the threshold where electron-positron formation could occur. Most of the surviving electrons and positrons annihilated each other, releasing gamma radiation that briefly reheated the universe.[[149]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-156)

For reasons that remain uncertain, during the annihilation process there was an excess in the number of particles over antiparticles. Hence, about one electron for every billion electron–positron pairs survived. This excess matched the excess of protons over antiprotons, in a condition known as [baryon asymmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baryon_asymmetry "Baryon asymmetry"), resulting in a net charge of zero for the universe.[[150]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-157)[[151]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-158) The surviving protons and neutrons began to participate in reactions with each other—in the process known as [nucleosynthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleosynthesis "Nucleosynthesis"), forming isotopes of hydrogen and [helium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium "Helium"), with trace amounts of [lithium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lithium "Lithium"). This process peaked after about five minutes.[[152]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-159) Any leftover neutrons underwent negative [beta decay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beta_decay "Beta decay") with a half-life of about a thousand seconds, releasing a proton and electron in the process,

[n](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron "Neutron") → [p](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton "Proton") + e−

 + [ν e](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_antineutrino "Electron antineutrino")
For about the next 300 000–400 000 years, the excess electrons remained too energetic to bind with [atomic nuclei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_nucleus "Atomic nucleus").[[153]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-160) What followed is a period known as [recombination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chronology_of_the_universe#Recombination,_photon_decoupling,_and_the_cosmic_microwave_background_(CMB) "Chronology of the universe"), when neutral atoms were formed and the expanding universe became transparent to radiation.[[154]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-science5789-161)

Roughly one million years after the big bang, the first generation of [stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star "Star") began to form.[[154]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-science5789-161) Within a star, [stellar nucleosynthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis "Stellar nucleosynthesis") results in the production of positrons from the fusion of atomic nuclei. These antimatter particles immediately annihilate with electrons, releasing gamma rays. The net result is a steady reduction in the number of electrons, and a matching increase in the number of neutrons. However, the process of [stellar evolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_evolution "Stellar evolution") can result in the synthesis of radioactive isotopes. Selected isotopes can subsequently undergo negative beta decay, emitting an electron and antineutrino from the nucleus.[[155]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-162) An example is the [cobalt-60](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cobalt-60 "Cobalt-60") (60 Co) isotope, which decays to form [nickel-60](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotopes_of_nickel "Isotopes of nickel") (60

Ni).[[156]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-163)

[![Image 18: A branching tree representing the particle production](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/AirShower.svg/250px-AirShower.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:AirShower.svg)

An extended air shower generated by an energetic cosmic ray striking the Earth's atmosphere

At the end of its lifetime, a star with more than about 20 [solar masses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_mass "Solar mass") can undergo [gravitational collapse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_collapse "Gravitational collapse") to form a [black hole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_hole "Black hole").[[157]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-164) According to [classical physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_physics "Classical physics"), these massive stellar objects exert a [gravitational attraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitation "Gravitation") that is strong enough to prevent anything, even [electromagnetic radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation "Electromagnetic radiation"), from escaping past the [Schwarzschild radius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schwarzschild_radius "Schwarzschild radius"). However, quantum mechanical effects are believed to potentially allow the emission of [Hawking radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hawking_radiation "Hawking radiation") at this distance. Electrons (and positrons) are thought to be created at the [event horizon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Event_horizon "Event horizon") of these [stellar remnants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compact_star "Compact star").

When a pair of virtual particles (such as an electron and positron) is created in the vicinity of the event horizon, random spatial positioning might result in one of them to appear on the exterior; this process is called [quantum tunnelling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_tunnelling "Quantum tunnelling"). The [gravitational potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_potential "Gravitational potential") of the black hole can then supply the energy that transforms this virtual particle into a real particle, allowing it to radiate away into space.[[158]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-165) In exchange, the other member of the pair is given negative energy, which results in a net loss of mass–energy by the black hole. The rate of Hawking radiation increases with decreasing mass, eventually causing the black hole to evaporate away until, finally, it explodes.[[159]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-166)

[Cosmic rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_ray "Cosmic ray") are particles traveling through space with high energies. Energy events as high as 3.0×10 20 eV have been recorded.[[160]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-167) When these particles collide with nucleons in the [Earth's atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth "Atmosphere of Earth"), a shower of particles is generated, including [pions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pion "Pion").[[161]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-168) More than half of the cosmic radiation observed from the Earth's surface consists of [muons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muon "Muon"). The particle called a muon is a lepton produced in the upper atmosphere by the decay of a pion.

[π−](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pion "Pion") → [μ−](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muon "Muon") + [ν μ](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muon_antineutrino "Muon antineutrino")
A muon, in turn, can decay to form an electron or positron.[[162]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-169)

μ−

 → e−

 + [ν e](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_antineutrino "Electron antineutrino") + [ν μ](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muon_neutrino "Muon neutrino")[![Image 19: A swirling green glow in the night sky above snow-covered ground](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Aurore_australe_-_Aurora_australis.jpg/250px-Aurore_australe_-_Aurora_australis.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Aurore_australe_-_Aurora_australis.jpg)

[Aurorae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aurora_(astronomy) "Aurora (astronomy)") are mostly caused by energetic electrons precipitating into the [atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere "Atmosphere")[[163]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-170)

Remote observation of electrons requires detection of their radiated energy. For example, in high-energy environments such as the [corona](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_corona "Stellar corona") of a star, free electrons form a [plasma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasma_(physics) "Plasma (physics)") that radiates energy due to [Bremsstrahlung](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bremsstrahlung "Bremsstrahlung") radiation. Electron gas can undergo [plasma oscillation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasma_oscillation "Plasma oscillation"), which is waves caused by synchronized variations in electron density, and these produce energy emissions that can be detected by using [radio telescopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radio_telescope "Radio telescope").[[164]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-171)

The [frequency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frequency "Frequency") of a [photon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon "Photon") is proportional to its energy. As a bound electron transitions between different energy levels of an atom, it absorbs or emits photons at characteristic frequencies. For instance, when atoms are irradiated by a source with a broad spectrum, distinct [dark lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectral_line "Spectral line") appear in the spectrum of transmitted radiation in places where the corresponding frequency is absorbed by the atom's electrons. Each element or molecule displays a characteristic set of spectral lines, such as the [hydrogen spectral series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_spectral_series "Hydrogen spectral series"). When detected, [spectroscopic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopy "Spectroscopy") measurements of the strength and width of these lines allow the composition and physical properties of a substance to be determined.[[165]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-172)[[166]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-173)

In laboratory conditions, the interactions of individual electrons can be observed by means of [particle detectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_detector "Particle detector"), which allow measurement of specific properties such as energy, spin and charge.[[167]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-grupen-174) The development of the [Paul trap](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrupole_ion_trap "Quadrupole ion trap") and [Penning trap](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Penning_trap "Penning trap") allows charged particles to be contained within a small region for long durations. This enables precise measurements of the particle properties. For example, in one instance a Penning trap was used to contain a single electron for a period of 10 months.[[168]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-nobel1989-175) The magnetic moment of the electron was measured to a precision of eleven digits, which, in 1980, was a greater accuracy than for any other physical constant.[[169]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-176)

The first video images of an electron's energy distribution were captured by a team at [Lund University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lund_University "Lund University") in Sweden, February 2008. The scientists used extremely short flashes of light, called [attosecond](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Attosecond "Attosecond") pulses, which allowed an electron's motion to be observed for the first time.[[170]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-177)[[171]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-Mauritsson-178)

The distribution of the electrons in solid materials can be visualized by [angle-resolved photoemission spectroscopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle-resolved_photoemission_spectroscopy "Angle-resolved photoemission spectroscopy") (ARPES). This technique employs the photoelectric effect to measure the [reciprocal space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reciprocal_lattice "Reciprocal lattice")—a mathematical representation of periodic structures that is used to infer the original structure. ARPES can be used to determine the direction, speed and scattering of electrons within the material.[[172]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-179)

Plasma applications
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Electron&action=edit&section=22 "Edit section: Plasma applications")]

[![Image 20: A violet beam from above produces a blue glow about a Space shuttle model](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Nasa_Shuttle_Test_Using_Electron_Beam_full.jpg/250px-Nasa_Shuttle_Test_Using_Electron_Beam_full.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Nasa_Shuttle_Test_Using_Electron_Beam_full.jpg)

During a [NASA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NASA "NASA")[wind tunnel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wind_tunnel "Wind tunnel") test, a model of the [Space Shuttle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_Shuttle "Space Shuttle") is targeted by a beam of electrons, simulating the effect of [ionizing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ion "Ion") gases during [re-entry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmospheric_entry "Atmospheric entry").[[173]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-180)

[Electron beams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cathode_ray "Cathode ray") are used in [welding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_beam_welding "Electron beam welding").[[174]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-181) They allow energy densities up to 10 7 W·cm−2 across a narrow focus diameter of 0.1–1.3 mm and usually require no filler material. This welding technique must be performed in a vacuum to prevent the electrons from interacting with the gas before reaching their target, and it can be used to join conductive materials that would otherwise be considered unsuitable for welding.[[175]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-182)[[176]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-183)

[Electron-beam lithography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron-beam_lithography "Electron-beam lithography") (EBL) is a method of etching semiconductors at resolutions smaller than a [micrometer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Micrometre "Micrometre").[[177]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-184) This technique is limited by high costs, slow performance, the need to operate the beam in the vacuum and the tendency of the electrons to scatter in solids. The last problem limits the resolution to about 10 nm. For this reason, EBL is primarily used for the production of small numbers of specialized [integrated circuits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integrated_circuit "Integrated circuit").[[178]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-185)

[Electron beam processing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_beam_processing "Electron beam processing") is used to irradiate materials in order to change their physical properties or [sterilize](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sterilization_(microbiology) "Sterilization (microbiology)") medical and food products.[[179]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-186) Electron beams fluidise or quasi-melt glasses without significant increase of temperature on intensive irradiation: e.g. intensive electron radiation causes a many orders of magnitude decrease of viscosity and stepwise decrease of its activation energy.[[180]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-187)

[Linear particle accelerators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_particle_accelerator "Linear particle accelerator") generate electron beams for treatment of superficial tumors in [radiation therapy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiation_therapy "Radiation therapy"). [Electron therapy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_therapy "Electron therapy") can treat such skin lesions as [basal-cell carcinomas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basal-cell_carcinoma "Basal-cell carcinoma") because an electron beam only penetrates to a limited depth before being absorbed, typically up to 5 cm for electron energies in the range 5–20 MeV. An electron beam can be used to supplement the treatment of areas that have been irradiated by [X-rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray "X-ray").[[181]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-188)[[182]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-189)

[Particle accelerators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_accelerator "Particle accelerator") use electric fields to propel electrons and their antiparticles to high energies. These particles emit synchrotron radiation as they pass through magnetic fields. The dependency of the intensity of this radiation upon spin polarizes the electron beam – a process known as the [Sokolov–Ternov effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sokolov%E2%80%93Ternov_effect "Sokolov–Ternov effect").[[h]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-190) Polarized electron beams can be useful for various experiments. [Synchrotron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synchrotron "Synchrotron") radiation can also [cool](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiation_damping "Radiation damping") the electron beams to reduce the momentum spread of the particles. Electron and positron beams are collided upon the particles' accelerating to the required energies; [particle detectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_detector "Particle detector") observe the resulting energy emissions, which [particle physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_physics "Particle physics") studies.[[183]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-191)

[Low-energy electron diffraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Low-energy_electron_diffraction "Low-energy electron diffraction") (LEED) is a method of bombarding a crystalline material with a [collimated beam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collimated_light "Collimated light") of electrons and then observing the resulting diffraction patterns to determine the structure of the material. The required energy of the electrons is typically in the range 20–200 eV.[[184]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-192) The [reflection high-energy electron diffraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflection_high-energy_electron_diffraction "Reflection high-energy electron diffraction") (RHEED) technique uses the reflection of a beam of electrons fired at various low angles to characterize the surface of crystalline materials. The beam energy is typically in the range 8–20 keV and the angle of incidence is 1–4°.[[185]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-193)[[186]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-194)

The [electron microscope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_microscope "Electron microscope") directs a focused beam of electrons at a specimen. Some electrons change their properties, such as movement direction, angle, and relative phase and energy as the beam interacts with the material. Microscopists can record these changes in the electron beam to produce atomically resolved images of the material.[[187]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-195) In blue light, conventional [optical microscopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optical_microscope "Optical microscope") have a diffraction-limited resolution of about 200 nm.[[188]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-196) By comparison, electron microscopes are limited by the [de Broglie wavelength](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matter_wave "Matter wave") of the electron. This wavelength, for example, is equal to 0.0037 nm for electrons accelerated across a 100,000-[volt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volt "Volt") potential.[[189]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-197) The [Transmission Electron Aberration-Corrected Microscope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transmission_Electron_Aberration-Corrected_Microscope "Transmission Electron Aberration-Corrected Microscope") is capable of sub-0.05 nm resolution, which is more than enough to resolve individual atoms.[[190]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-198) This capability makes the electron microscope a useful laboratory instrument for high resolution imaging. However, electron microscopes are expensive instruments that are costly to maintain.

Two main types of electron microscopes exist: [transmission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transmission_electron_microscopy "Transmission electron microscopy") and [scanning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scanning_electron_microscope "Scanning electron microscope"). Transmission electron microscopes function like [overhead projectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Overhead_projector "Overhead projector"), with a beam of electrons passing through a slice of material then being projected by lenses on a [photographic slide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reversal_film "Reversal film") or a [charge-coupled device](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charge-coupled_device "Charge-coupled device"). Scanning electron microscopes [rasteri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raster_scan "Raster scan") a finely focused electron beam, as in a TV set, across the studied sample to produce the image. Magnifications range from 100× to 1,000,000× or higher for both microscope types. The [scanning tunneling microscope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scanning_tunneling_microscope "Scanning tunneling microscope") uses quantum tunneling of electrons from a sharp metal tip into the studied material and can produce atomically resolved images of its surface.[[191]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-bozzola_1999-199)[[192]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-200)[[193]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-201)

In the [free-electron laser](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free-electron_laser "Free-electron laser") (FEL), a [relativistic electron beam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_electron_beam "Relativistic electron beam") passes through a pair of [undulators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Undulator "Undulator") that contain arrays of [dipole magnets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dipole_magnet "Dipole magnet") whose fields point in alternating directions. The electrons emit synchrotron radiation that [coherently](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coherence_(physics) "Coherence (physics)") interacts with the same electrons to strongly amplify the radiation field at the [resonance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resonance "Resonance") frequency. FEL can emit a coherent high-[brilliance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiance "Radiance") electromagnetic radiation with a wide range of frequencies, from [microwaves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microwave "Microwave") to soft X-rays. These devices are used in manufacturing, communication, and in medical applications, such as soft tissue surgery.[[194]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-202)

Electrons are important in [cathode-ray tubes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cathode-ray_tube "Cathode-ray tube"), which have been extensively used as display devices in laboratory instruments, [computer monitors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_monitor "Computer monitor") and [television sets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Television_set "Television set").[[195]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-203) In a [photomultiplier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photomultiplier "Photomultiplier") tube, every photon striking the [photocathode](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photocathode "Photocathode") initiates an avalanche of electrons that produces a detectable current pulse.[[196]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-204)[Vacuum tubes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vacuum_tube "Vacuum tube") use the flow of electrons to manipulate electrical signals, and they played a critical role in the development of electronics technology. However, they have been largely supplanted by [solid-state devices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid-state_electronics "Solid-state electronics") such as the [transistor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transistor "Transistor").[[197]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_note-205)

5.   **[^](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron#cite_ref-100)**The classical electron radius is derived as follows. This energy is assumed to equal the electron's [rest energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invariant_mass#Rest_energy "Invariant mass"), defined by [special relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_relativity "Special relativity") (_E_=_mc_ 2).

 From [electrostatics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrostatics "Electrostatics") theory, the [potential energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potential_energy "Potential energy") of a sphere with radius _r_ and charge _e_ is given by: ![Image 22: {\displaystyle E_{\mathrm {p} }={\frac {e^{2}}{8\pi \varepsilon _{0}r}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/238db178ce107ab71d2e0476ce4e45d4d5b36556) where _ε_ 0 is the [vacuum permittivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vacuum_permittivity "Vacuum permittivity"). For an electron with rest mass _m_ 0, the rest energy is equal to: ![Image 23: {\displaystyle \textstyle E_{\mathrm {p} }=m_{0}c^{2},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/aa5cf5958808515b7ffc4151ff2c752cd232a63a) where _c_ is the speed of light in vacuum. Setting them equal and solving for _r_ gives the classical electron radius.
