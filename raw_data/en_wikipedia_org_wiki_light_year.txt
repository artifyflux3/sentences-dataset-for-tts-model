A **light-year**, alternatively spelled **light year** (**ly** or **lyr**[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_year#cite_note-3)), is a [unit of length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_of_length "Unit of length") used to express [astronomical distances](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_distance "Astronomical distance") and is equal to exactly 9 460 730 472 580.8 km, which is approximately 9.46 trillion km or 5.88 trillion mi. As defined by the [International Astronomical Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union "International Astronomical Union") (IAU), a light-year is the distance that [light travels in vacuum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light "Speed of light") in one [Julian year](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Julian_year_(astronomy) "Julian year (astronomy)") (365.25 days).[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_year#cite_note-IAUgen-2) Despite its inclusion of the word "year", the term should not be misinterpreted as a [unit of time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_of_time "Unit of time").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_year#cite_note-es18-4)

The _light-year_ is most often used when expressing distances to stars and other distances on a [galactic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy "Galaxy") scale, especially in [non-specialist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_understanding_of_science "Public understanding of science") contexts and [popular science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Popular_science "Popular science") publications.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_year#cite_note-es18-4) The unit most commonly used in professional [astronomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy "Astronomy") is the [parsec](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parsec "Parsec") (symbol: pc, about 3.26 light-years).[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_year#cite_note-IAUgen-2)

As defined by the [International Astronomical Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union "International Astronomical Union") (IAU), the light-year is the product of the [Julian year](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Julian_year_(astronomy) "Julian year (astronomy)")[[note 1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_year#cite_note-6) (365.25 days, as opposed to the 365.2425-day [Gregorian year](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gregorian_calendar "Gregorian calendar") or the 365.24219-day [Tropical year](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tropical_year "Tropical year") that both approximate) and the [speed of light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light "Speed of light") (299 792 458 m/s).[[note 2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_year#cite_note-7) Both of these values are included in the [IAU (1976) System of Astronomical Constants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_constant "Astronomical constant"), used since 1984.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_year#cite_note-8) From this, the following conversions can be derived:

The abbreviation used by the IAU for light-year is "ly",[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_year#cite_note-IAUgen-2) International standards like [ISO 80000:2006](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO_80000 "ISO 80000") (now superseded) have used "l.y."[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_year#cite_note-9)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_year#cite_note-10) and localized abbreviations are frequent, such as "al" in French, Spanish, and Italian (from _année-lumière_, _año luz_ and _anno luce_, respectively), "Lj" in German (from _Lichtjahr_), etc.

Before 1984, the [tropical year](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tropical_year "Tropical year") (not the Julian year) and a measured (not defined) speed of light were included in the IAU (1964) System of Astronomical Constants, used from 1968 to 1983.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_year#cite_note-Seidelmann-11) The product of [Simon Newcomb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simon_Newcomb "Simon Newcomb")'s [J1900.0](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J1900.0 "J1900.0") mean tropical year of 31 556 925.9747[ephemeris seconds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ephemeris_second "Ephemeris second") and a speed of light of 299 792.5 km/s produced a light-year of 9.460 530×10 15 m (rounded to the seven [significant digits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Significant_digits "Significant digits") in the speed of light) found in several modern sources[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_year#cite_note-12)[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_year#cite_note-13)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_year#cite_note-14) was probably derived from an old source such as [C. W. Allen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clabon_Allen "Clabon Allen")'s 1973 _Astrophysical Quantities_ reference work,[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_year#cite_note-15) which was updated in 2000, including the IAU (1976) value cited above (truncated to 10 significant digits).[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_year#cite_note-16)

Other high-precision values are not derived from a [coherent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coherent_units "Coherent units") IAU system. A value of 9.460 536 207×10 15 m found in some modern sources[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_year#cite_note-17)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_year#cite_note-18) is the product of a mean Gregorian year (365.2425 days or 31 556 952 s) and the defined speed of light (299 792 458 m/s). Another value, 9.460 528 405×10 15 m,[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_year#cite_note-19) is the product of the J1900.0 mean tropical year and the defined speed of light.

Abbreviations used for light-years and multiples of light-years are:

*   "ly" for one light-year[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_year#cite_note-IAUgen-2)
*   "kly" [[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_year#cite_note-Comins-20) or "klyr"[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_year#cite_note-21) for a kilolight-year (1,000 light-years)
*   "Mly" for a megalight-year (1,000,000 light-years)[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_year#cite_note-Hassani-22)
*   "Gly"[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_year#cite_note-Deza-23) or "Glyr"[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_year#cite_note-24) for a gigalight-year (1 000 000 000 light-years)

The light-year unit appeared a few years after the first successful measurement of the distance to a star other than the Sun, by [Friedrich Bessel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Bessel "Friedrich Bessel") in 1838. The star was [61 Cygni](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/61_Cygni "61 Cygni"), and he used a 160-millimetre (6.2 in) [heliometre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heliometer "Heliometer") designed by [Joseph von Fraunhofer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_von_Fraunhofer "Joseph von Fraunhofer"). The largest unit for expressing distances across space at that time was the [astronomical unit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_unit "Astronomical unit"), equal to the radius of the Earth's orbit at 150 million kilometres (93 million miles). In those terms, trigonometric calculations based on 61 Cygni's [parallax](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallax "Parallax") of 0.314 arcseconds, showed the distance to the star to be 660 000 astronomical units (9.9×10 13 km; 6.1×10 13 mi). Bessel added that light takes 10.3 years to traverse this distance.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_year#cite_note-25) He recognized that his readers would enjoy the mental picture of the approximate transit time for light, but he refrained from using the light-year as a unit. He may have resisted expressing distances in light-years because it would reduce the accuracy of his parallax data due to multiplying with the uncertain parameter of the speed of light.

The speed of light was not yet precisely known in 1838; the estimate of its value changed in 1849 ([Fizeau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hippolyte_Fizeau "Hippolyte Fizeau")) and 1862 ([Foucault](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L%C3%A9on_Foucault "Léon Foucault")). It was not yet considered to be a fundamental constant of nature, and the propagation of light through the [aether](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luminiferous_aether "Luminiferous aether") or space was still enigmatic.

The light-year unit appeared in 1851 in a German popular astronomical article by [Otto Ule](https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Otto_Eduard_Vincenz_Ule "de:Otto Eduard Vincenz Ule").[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_year#cite_note-26) Ule explained the oddity of a distance unit name ending in "year" by comparing it to a walking hour (_Wegstunde_).

A contemporary German popular astronomical book also noticed that light-year is an odd name.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_year#cite_note-27) In 1868 an English journal labelled the light-year as a unit used by the Germans.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_year#cite_note-28)[Eddington](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arthur_Eddington "Arthur Eddington") called the light-year an inconvenient and irrelevant unit, which had sometimes crept from popular use into technical investigations.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_year#cite_note-29)

Although modern astronomers often prefer to use the [parsec](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parsec "Parsec"), light-years are also popularly used to gauge the expanses of interstellar and intergalactic space.

Distances expressed in light-years include those between [stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star "Star") in the same general area, such as those belonging to the same [spiral arm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spiral_arm "Spiral arm") or [globular cluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Globular_cluster "Globular cluster"). Galaxies themselves span from a few thousand to a few hundred thousand light-years in diameter, and are separated from neighbouring galaxies and [galaxy clusters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_groups_and_clusters "Galaxy groups and clusters") by millions of light-years. Distances to objects such as [quasars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasar "Quasar") and the [Sloan Great Wall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sloan_Great_Wall "Sloan Great Wall") run up into the billions of light-years.

Distances between objects within a [star system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system "Star system") tend to be small fractions of a light-year, and are usually expressed in [astronomical units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_unit "Astronomical unit"). However, smaller units of length can similarly be formed usefully by multiplying units of time by the speed of light. For example, the [light-second](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light-second "Light-second"), useful in astronomy, telecommunications and relativistic physics, is exactly 299 792 458 metres or ⁠1/31 557 600⁠ of a light-year. Units such as the light-minute, light-hour and light-day are sometimes used in [popular science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Popular_science "Popular science") publications. The light-month, roughly one-twelfth of a light-year, is also used occasionally for approximate measures.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_year#cite_note-39)[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_year#cite_note-40) The [Hayden Planetarium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hayden_Planetarium "Hayden Planetarium") specifies the light month more precisely as 30 days of light travel time.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_year#cite_note-41)

Light travels approximately one foot in a [nanosecond](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nanosecond "Nanosecond"); the term "light-foot" is sometimes used as an informal measure of time.[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_year#cite_note-42)
