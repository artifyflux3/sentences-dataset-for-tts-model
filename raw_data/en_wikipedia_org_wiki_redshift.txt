In [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics"), a **redshift** is an increase in the [wavelength](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wavelength "Wavelength"), or equivalently, a decrease in the [frequency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frequency "Frequency") and [photon energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon_energy "Photon energy"), of [electromagnetic radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation "Electromagnetic radiation") (such as [light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light "Light")). The opposite change, a decrease in wavelength and increase in frequency and energy, is known as a **[blueshift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#Blueshift)**. The terms derive from the colours [red](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red "Red") and [blue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blue "Blue") which form the extremes of the [visible light spectrum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visible_spectrum "Visible spectrum").

Three forms of redshift occur in [astronomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy "Astronomy") and [cosmology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmology "Cosmology"): [Doppler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doppler_effect "Doppler effect") redshifts due to the relative motions of radiation sources, [gravitational redshift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_redshift "Gravitational redshift") as radiation escapes from gravitational potentials, and cosmological redshifts caused by the [universe expanding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expansion_of_the_universe "Expansion of the universe"). In astronomy, the value of a redshift is often denoted by the letter _z_, corresponding to the fractional change in wavelength (positive for redshifts, negative for blueshifts), and by the wavelength ratio 1 + _z_ (which is greater than 1 for redshifts and less than 1 for blueshifts). Automated astronomical redshift surveys are an important tool for learning about the large scale structure of the universe.

Examples of strong redshifting are a [gamma ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamma_ray "Gamma ray") perceived as an [X-ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray "X-ray"), or initially visible light perceived as [radio waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radio_wave "Radio wave"). The initial heat from the [Big Bang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang "Big Bang") has redshifted far down to become the [cosmic microwave background](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background "Cosmic microwave background"). Subtler redshifts are seen in the [spectroscopic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_spectroscopy "Astronomical spectroscopy") observations of [astronomical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical "Astronomical") objects, and are used in terrestrial technologies such as [Doppler radar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doppler_radar "Doppler radar") and [radar guns](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radar_gun "Radar gun"). [Gravitational waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_wave "Gravitational wave"), which also travel at [the speed of light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light "Speed of light"), are subject to the same redshift phenomena.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-1)

Other physical processes exist that can lead to a shift in the frequency of electromagnetic radiation, including [scattering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scattering "Scattering") and [optical effects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_optics "Physical optics"); however, the resulting changes are distinguishable from (astronomical) redshift and are not generally referred to as such (see section on [physical optics and radiative transfer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#Effects_from_physical_optics_or_radiative_transfer)).

The resulting spectrum can be compared to the spectrum from hot gases expected in stars, such as [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen"), in a laboratory on Earth. [Doppler effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doppler_effect "Doppler effect") blueshifts (_z_< 0) are associated with objects approaching (moving closer to) the observer with the light shifting to greater [energies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy "Energy"). Conversely, Doppler effect redshifts (_z_> 0) are associated with objects receding (moving away) from the observer with the light shifting to lower energies. Likewise, gravitational blueshifts are associated with light emitted from a source residing within a weaker [gravitational field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_field "Gravitational field") as observed from within a stronger gravitational field, while gravitational redshifting implies the opposite conditions.

The history of the subject began in the 19th century, with the development of classical [wave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wave "Wave") mechanics and the exploration of phenomena which are associated with the [Doppler effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doppler_effect "Doppler effect"). The effect is named after the [Austrian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Austria "Austria") mathematician [Christian Doppler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christian_Doppler "Christian Doppler"), who offered the first known physical explanation for the phenomenon in 1842.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-Becker-2011-5): 107  In 1845, the hypothesis was tested and confirmed for [sound waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sound_wave "Sound wave") by the [Dutch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Netherlands "Netherlands") scientist [Christophorus Buys Ballot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C._H._D._Buys_Ballot "C. H. D. Buys Ballot").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-6) Doppler correctly predicted that the phenomenon would apply to all waves and, in particular, suggested that the varying [colors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Color "Color") of [stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star "Star") could be attributed to their motion with respect to the Earth.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-7)

Unaware of Doppler's work, French physicist [Hippolyte Fizeau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hippolyte_Fizeau "Hippolyte Fizeau") suggested in 1848 that a shift in [spectral lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectral_line "Spectral line") from stars might be used to measure their motion relative to Earth.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-Becker-2011-5): 109  In 1850, [François-Napoléon-Marie Moigno](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois-Napol%C3%A9on-Marie_Moigno "François-Napoléon-Marie Moigno") analyzed both Doppler's and Fizeau's ideas in a publication read by both [James Clerk Maxwell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Clerk_Maxwell "James Clerk Maxwell") and [William Huggins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Huggins "William Huggins"), who initially stuck to the idea that the color of stars related to their chemistry, however by 1868, Huggins was the first to determine the velocity of a star moving away from the Earth by the analysis of spectral shifts.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-Huggins-8)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-Becker-2011-5): 111

In 1871, optical redshift was confirmed when the phenomenon was observed in [Fraunhofer lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fraunhofer_lines "Fraunhofer lines"), using solar rotation, about 0.1 Å in the red.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-Nolte-9) In 1887, [Hermann Carl Vogel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Carl_Vogel "Hermann Carl Vogel") and [Julius Scheiner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Julius_Scheiner "Julius Scheiner") discovered the "annual Doppler effect", the yearly change in the Doppler shift of stars located near the ecliptic, due to the orbital velocity of the Earth.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-10) In 1901, [Aristarkh Belopolsky](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristarkh_Belopolsky "Aristarkh Belopolsky") verified optical redshift in the laboratory using a system of rotating mirrors.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-11)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-Nolte-9)

Beginning with observations in 1912, [Vesto Slipher](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vesto_Slipher "Vesto Slipher") discovered that the [Andromeda Galaxy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andromeda_Galaxy "Andromeda Galaxy") had a blue shift, indicating that it was moving towards the Earth.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-SmithInKragh-2019-12) Slipher first reported his measurement in the inaugural volume of the _[Lowell Observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lowell\_Observatory "Lowell Observatory") Bulletin_.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-13) Three years later, he wrote a review in the journal _[Popular Astronomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Popular\_Astronomy\_(US\_magazine) "Popular Astronomy (US magazine)")_.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-Slipher-14) In it he stated that "the early discovery that the great Andromeda spiral had the quite exceptional velocity of –300 km[/s] showed the means then available, capable of investigating not only the spectra of the spirals but their velocities as well."[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-Slipher-14) Slipher reported the velocities for 15 spiral nebulae spread across the entire [celestial sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celestial_sphere "Celestial sphere"), all but three having observable "positive" (that is recessional) velocities.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-SmithInKragh-2019-12)

Until 1923 the nature of the nebulae was unclear. By that year [Edwin Hubble](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edwin_Hubble "Edwin Hubble") had established that these were [galaxies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxies "Galaxies") and worked out a procedure to measure distance based on the period-luminosity relation of variable [Cepheids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cepheids "Cepheids") stars. This made it possible to test a prediction by [Willem de Sitter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Willem_de_Sitter "Willem de Sitter") in 1917 that redshift would be correlated with distance. In 1929 Hubble combined his distance estimates with redshift data from Slipher's reports and measurements by [Milton Humason](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milton_Humason "Milton Humason") to report an approximate relationship between the redshift and [distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distance "Distance"), a result now called [Hubble's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble%27s_law "Hubble's law").[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-SmithInKragh-2019-12): 64 [[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-15)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-16)

Theories relating to the redshift-distance relation also evolved during the 1920s. The solution to the equations of general relativity described by de Sitter contained no matter, but in 1922 [Alexander Friedmann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_Friedmann "Alexander Friedmann") derived dynamic solutions, now called the [Friedmann equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedmann_equations "Friedmann equations"), based on frictionless fluid models.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-17) Independently [Georges Lemaître](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georges_Lema%C3%AEtre "Georges Lemaître") derived similar equations in 1927 and his analysis became widely known around the time of Hubble's key publication.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-SmithInKragh-2019-12): 77

By early 1930 the combination of the redshift measurements and theoretical models established a major breakthrough in the new science of cosmology: the universe had a history and its expansion could be investigated with physical models backed up with observational astronomy.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-SmithInKragh-2019-12): 99

[Arthur Eddington](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arthur_Eddington "Arthur Eddington") used the term "red shift" as early as 1923, which is the oldest example of the term reported by the _[Oxford English Dictionary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxford\_English\_Dictionary "Oxford English Dictionary")_.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-18)[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-19) Willem de Sitter used the single-word version _redshift_ in 1934.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-20)

In the 1960s the discovery of [quasars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasars "Quasars"), which appear as very blue point sources and thus were initially thought to be unusual stars, lead to the idea that they were as bright as they were because they were closer than their redshift data indicated. A flurry of theoretical and observational work concluded that these objects were very powerful but distant astronomical objects.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-SmithInKragh-2019-12): 261

Redshifts are differences between two wavelength measurements and wavelengths are a property of both the photons and the measuring equipment. Thus redshifts characterize differences between two measurement locations. These differences are commonly organized in three groups, attributed to relative motion between the source and the observer, to the expansion of the universe, and to gravity.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-21) The following sections explain these groups.

Redshift and blueshift

If a source of the light is moving away from an observer, then redshift (_z_> 0) occurs; if the source moves towards the observer, then [blueshift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blueshift "Blueshift") (_z_< 0) occurs. This is true for all electromagnetic waves and is explained by the [Doppler effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doppler_effect "Doppler effect"). Consequently, this type of redshift is called the _Doppler redshift_. If the source moves away from the observer with [velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity "Velocity")_v_, which is much less than the speed of light (_v_ ≪ _c_), the redshift is given by

![Image 10: {\displaystyle z\approx {\frac {v}{c}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ebb477fa2d573b6c2c0629fe2d63adf8d871488d) (since ![Image 11: {\displaystyle \gamma \approx 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8fcbd0174b5d1b88845a6b75ee34893bda890f25))
where _c_ is the [speed of light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light "Speed of light"). In the classical Doppler effect, the frequency of the source is not modified, but the recessional motion causes the illusion of a lower frequency.

A more complete treatment of the Doppler redshift requires considering relativistic effects associated with motion of sources close to the speed of light. A complete derivation of the effect can be found in the article on the [relativistic Doppler effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_Doppler_effect "Relativistic Doppler effect"). In brief, objects moving close to the speed of light will experience deviations from the above formula due to the [time dilation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_dilation "Time dilation") of [special relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_relativity "Special relativity") which can be corrected for by introducing the [Lorentz factor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentz_factor "Lorentz factor")_γ_ into the classical Doppler formula as follows (for motion solely in the line of sight):

This phenomenon was first observed in a 1938 experiment performed by [Herbert E. Ives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herbert_E._Ives "Herbert E. Ives") and G. R. Even when the source is moving towards the observer, if there is a transverse component to the motion then there is some speed at which the dilation just cancels the expected blueshift and at higher speed the approaching source will be redshifted.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-25)

The observations of increasing redshifts from more and more distant galaxies can be modeled assuming a [homogeneous and isotropic universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmological_principle "Cosmological principle") combined with [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity"). This cosmological redshift can be written as a function of _a_, the time-dependent cosmic [scale factor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scale_factor_(cosmology) "Scale factor (cosmology)"):[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-26): 72

![Image 16: {\displaystyle 1+z={\frac {a_{\mathrm {now} }}{a_{\mathrm {then} }}}={\frac {a_{0}}{a(t)}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1a04ab79e8658c0c7cb8e30d746a4ca64cd14b6f)
The scale factor is [monotonically increasing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monotonic_function "Monotonic function") as time passes. Thus _z_ is positive, close to zero for local stars, and increasing for distant galaxies that appear redshifted.

with _ρ_ crit the critical density demarcating a universe that eventually crunches from one that simply expands. This density is about three hydrogen atoms per cubic meter of space.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-Weinberg-27) At large redshifts, _1 + z_> Ω 0−1, one finds:

![Image 18: {\displaystyle t(z)\approx {\frac {2}{3H_{0}{\Omega _{0}}^{1/2}}}z^{-3/2}\ ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b35f0563369c2ee1555ff73db0fdff6d4f4670b6)
where _H_ 0 is the present-day [Hubble constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble_constant "Hubble constant"), and _z_ is the redshift.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-Bergstr%C3%B6m-28)[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-Longair-29)

The **cosmological redshift** is commonly attributed to stretching of the wavelengths of photons due to the stretching of space. This interpretation can be misleading. As required by [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity"), the cosmological expansion of space has no effect on local physics. There is no term related to expansion in [Maxwell's equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxwell%27s_equations "Maxwell's equations") that govern light propagation. The cosmological redshift can be interpreted as an accumulation of infinitesimal Doppler shifts along the trajectory of the light.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-Hogg-30)

There are several websites for calculating various times and distances from redshift, as the precise calculations require numerical integrals for most values of the parameters.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-UCLA-2018-31)[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-ICRAR-2022-32)

#### Distinguishing between cosmological and local effects

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Redshift&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Distinguishing between cosmological and local effects")]

The redshift of a galaxy includes both a component related to [recessional velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recessional_velocity "Recessional velocity") from expansion of the universe, and a component related to the [peculiar motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peculiar_motion "Peculiar motion") of the galaxy with respect to its local universe.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-33) The redshift due to expansion of the universe depends upon the recessional velocity in a fashion determined by the cosmological model chosen to describe the expansion of the universe, which is very different from how Doppler redshift depends upon local velocity.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-Harrison2-34) Describing the cosmological expansion origin of redshift, cosmologist [Edward Robert Harrison](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edward_Robert_Harrison "Edward Robert Harrison") said, "Light leaves a galaxy, which is stationary in its local region of space, and is eventually received by observers who are stationary in their own local region of space. Between the galaxy and the observer, light travels through vast regions of expanding space. As a result, all wavelengths of the light are stretched by the expansion of space. It is as simple as that..."[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-35)[Steven Weinberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steven_Weinberg "Steven Weinberg") clarified, "The increase of wavelength from emission to absorption of light does not depend on the rate of change of _a_(_t_) [the [scale factor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scale_factor_(cosmology) "Scale factor (cosmology)")] at the times of emission or absorption, but on the increase of _a_(_t_) in the whole period from emission to absorption."[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-Weinberg_Cosmology-36)

If the universe were contracting instead of expanding, we would see distant galaxies blueshifted by an amount proportional to their distance instead of redshifted.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-37)

### Gravitational redshift

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Redshift&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Gravitational redshift")]

In the theory of [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity"), there is time dilation within a gravitational well. Light emitted within the well will appear to have fewer cycles per second when measured outside of the well, due to differences in the two clocks.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-38): 284  This is known as the [gravitational redshift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_redshift "Gravitational redshift") or _Einstein Shift_.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-39) The theoretical derivation of this effect follows from the [Schwarzschild solution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schwarzschild_solution "Schwarzschild solution") of the [Einstein equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_field_equations "Einstein field equations") which yields the following formula for redshift associated with a photon traveling in the [gravitational field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_field "Gravitational field") of an [uncharged](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_charge "Electric charge"), [nonrotating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation "Rotation"), [spherically symmetric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_symmetry "Spherical symmetry") mass:

![Image 19: {\displaystyle 1+z={\frac {1}{\sqrt {1-{\frac {2GM}{rc^{2}}}}}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/32281ba5d64b16f8f2379257d6c1182af6c7481e)
where

*   _G_ is the [gravitational constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_constant "Gravitational constant"),
*   _M_ is the [mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass "Mass") of the object creating the gravitational field,
*   _r_ is the radial coordinate of the source (which is analogous to the classical distance from the center of the object, but is actually a [Schwarzschild coordinate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schwarzschild_coordinates "Schwarzschild coordinates")), and
*   _c_ is the [speed of light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light "Speed of light").

This gravitational redshift result can be derived from the assumptions of [special relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_relativity "Special relativity") and the [equivalence principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equivalence_principle "Equivalence principle"); the full theory of general relativity is not required.[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-40)

The effect is very small but measurable on Earth using the [Mössbauer effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M%C3%B6ssbauer_effect "Mössbauer effect") and was first observed in the [Pound–Rebka experiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pound%E2%80%93Rebka_experiment "Pound–Rebka experiment").[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-41) However, it is significant near a [black hole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_hole "Black hole"), and as an object approaches the [event horizon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Event_horizon "Event horizon") the red shift becomes infinite. It is also the dominant cause of large angular-scale temperature fluctuations in the [cosmic microwave background](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background "Cosmic microwave background") radiation (see [Sachs–Wolfe effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sachs%E2%80%93Wolfe_effect "Sachs–Wolfe effect")).[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-42)

Observations in astronomy
-------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Redshift&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Observations in astronomy")]

[![Image 35](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Look-back_time_by_redshift.png/250px-Look-back_time_by_redshift.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Look-back_time_by_redshift.png)

The [lookback time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lookback_time "Lookback time") of extragalactic observations by their redshift up to z=20.[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-Pilipenko-45) There are websites for calculating many such physical measures from redshift.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-UCLA-2018-31)[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-ICRAR-2022-32)

The redshift observed in astronomy can be measured because the [emission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emission_spectrum "Emission spectrum") and [absorption](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absorption_spectroscopy "Absorption spectroscopy") spectra for [atoms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atom "Atom") are distinctive and well known, calibrated from [spectroscopic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic "Spectroscopic") experiments in [laboratories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laboratories "Laboratories") on Earth. When the redshifts of various absorption and emission lines from a single astronomical object are measured, _z_ is found to be remarkably constant. Although distant objects may be slightly blurred and lines broadened, it is by no more than can be explained by [thermal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases "Kinetic theory of gases") or mechanical [motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Motion "Motion") of the source. For these reasons and others, the consensus among astronomers is that the redshifts they observe are due to some combination of the three established forms of Doppler-like redshifts. Alternative hypotheses and explanations for redshift such as [tired light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tired_light "Tired light") are not generally considered plausible.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-reboul-46)

Spectroscopy, as a measurement, is considerably more difficult than simple [photometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photometry_(astronomy) "Photometry (astronomy)"), which measures the [brightness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brightness "Brightness") of astronomical objects through certain [filters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optical_filter "Optical filter").[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-47) When photometric data is all that is available (for example, the [Hubble Deep Field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble_Deep_Field "Hubble Deep Field") and the [Hubble Ultra Deep Field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble_Ultra_Deep_Field "Hubble Ultra Deep Field")), astronomers rely on a technique for measuring [photometric redshifts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photometric_redshift "Photometric redshift").[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-48) Due to the broad wavelength ranges in photometric filters and the necessary assumptions about the nature of the spectrum at the light-source, [errors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observational_error "Observational error") for these sorts of measurements can range up to δ _z_ = 0.5, and are much less reliable than spectroscopic determinations.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-49)

However, photometry does at least allow a qualitative characterization of a redshift. For example, if a Sun-like spectrum had a redshift of _z_ = 1, it would be brightest in the [infrared](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infrared "Infrared") (1000nm) rather than at the blue-green (500nm) color associated with the peak of its [blackbody](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_body "Black body") spectrum, and the light intensity will be reduced in the filter by a factor of four, (1 + _z_)2. Both the photon count rate and the photon energy are redshifted. (See [K correction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K_correction "K correction") for more details on the photometric consequences of redshift.)[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-50)

Determining the redshift of an object with spectroscopy requires the wavelength of the emitted light in the rest frame of the source. Astronomical applications rely on distinct spectral lines. Redshifts cannot be calculated by looking at unidentified features whose rest-frame frequency is unknown, or with a spectrum that is featureless or [white noise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_noise "White noise") (random fluctuations in a spectrum). Thus [gamma-ray bursts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamma-ray_burst "Gamma-ray burst") themselves cannot be used for reliable redshift measurements, but optical afterglow associated with the burst can be analyzed for redshifts.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-51)

In nearby objects (within our [Milky Way](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milky_Way "Milky Way") galaxy) observed redshifts are almost always related to the [line-of-sight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line-of-sight_propagation "Line-of-sight propagation") velocities associated with the objects being observed. Observations of such redshifts and blueshifts enable astronomers to measure [velocities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity "Velocity") and parametrize the [masses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass "Mass") of the [orbiting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbit "Orbit")[stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star "Star") in [spectroscopic binaries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_binaries "Spectroscopic binaries"). Similarly, small redshifts and blueshifts detected in the spectroscopic measurements of individual stars are one way astronomers have been able to [diagnose and measure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methods_of_detecting_exoplanets#Radial_velocity "Methods of detecting exoplanets") the presence and characteristics of [planetary systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exoplanet "Exoplanet") around other stars and have even made very [detailed differential measurements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rossiter%E2%80%93McLaughlin_effect "Rossiter–McLaughlin effect") of redshifts during [planetary transits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methods_of_detecting_exoplanets "Methods of detecting exoplanets") to determine precise orbital parameters. Some approaches are able to track the redshift variations in multiple objects at once.[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-52)

Finely detailed measurements of redshifts are used in [helioseismology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helioseismology "Helioseismology") to determine the precise movements of the [photosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photosphere "Photosphere") of the [Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun "Sun").[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-53) Redshifts have also been used to make the first measurements of the [rotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation "Rotation") rates of [planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planet "Planet"),[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-54) velocities of [interstellar clouds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_cloud "Interstellar cloud"),[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-55) the [rotation of galaxies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_rotation_curve "Galaxy rotation curve"),[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-basicastronomy-43) and the [dynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynamics_(mechanics) "Dynamics (mechanics)") of [accretion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accretion_disk "Accretion disk") onto [neutron stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron_star "Neutron star") and [black holes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_hole "Black hole") which exhibit both Doppler and gravitational redshifts.[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-56) The [temperatures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperature "Temperature") of various emitting and absorbing objects can be obtained by measuring [Doppler broadening](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doppler_broadening "Doppler broadening")—effectively redshifts and blueshifts over a single emission or absorption line.[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-57) By measuring the broadening and shifts of the 21-centimeter [hydrogen line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_line "Hydrogen line") in different directions, astronomers have been able to measure the [recessional velocities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recessional_velocity "Recessional velocity") of [interstellar gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_gas "Interstellar gas"), which in turn reveals the [rotation curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation_curve "Rotation curve") of our Milky Way.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-basicastronomy-43) Similar measurements have been performed on other galaxies, such as [Andromeda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andromeda_Galaxy "Andromeda Galaxy").[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-basicastronomy-43)

The most distant objects exhibit larger redshifts corresponding to the [Hubble flow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble_flow "Hubble flow") of the [universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe "Universe"). The largest-observed redshift, corresponding to the greatest distance and furthest back in time, is that of the [cosmic microwave background](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background "Cosmic microwave background") radiation; the [numerical value of its redshift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble%27s_law#Redshift_velocity "Hubble's law") is about _z_ = 1089 (_z_ = 0 corresponds to present time), and it shows the state of the universe about 13.8 billion years ago,[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-58) and 379,000 years after the initial moments of the [Big Bang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang "Big Bang").[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-59)

The luminous point-like cores of [quasars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasar "Quasar") were the first "high-redshift" (_z_> 0.1) objects discovered before the improvement of telescopes allowed for the discovery of other high-redshift galaxies.[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-Kellermann-60)

For galaxies more distant than the [Local Group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_Group "Local Group") and the nearby [Virgo Cluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgo_Cluster "Virgo Cluster"), but within a thousand mega[parsecs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parsec "Parsec") or so, the redshift is approximately proportional to the galaxy's distance. This correlation was first observed by [Edwin Hubble](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edwin_Hubble "Edwin Hubble") and has come to be known as [Hubble's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble%27s_law "Hubble's law"). [Vesto Slipher](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vesto_Slipher "Vesto Slipher") was the first to discover galactic redshifts, in about 1912, while Hubble correlated Slipher's measurements with distances he [measured by other means](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_distance_ladder "Cosmic distance ladder") to formulate his law.[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPeebles199378%E2%80%9379-61) Because it is usually not known how [luminous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luminosity "Luminosity") objects are, measuring the redshift is easier than more direct distance measurements, so redshift is sometimes in practice converted to a crude distance measurement using Hubble's law.[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-62)

[Gravitational](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitation "Gravitation") interactions of galaxies with each other and clusters cause a significant [scatter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variance "Variance") in the normal plot of the Hubble diagram. The [peculiar velocities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peculiar_velocity "Peculiar velocity") associated with galaxies superimpose a rough trace of the [mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass "Mass") of [virialized objects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virial_theorem "Virial theorem") in the universe. This effect leads to such phenomena as nearby galaxies (such as the [Andromeda Galaxy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andromeda_Galaxy "Andromeda Galaxy")) exhibiting blueshifts as we fall towards a common [barycenter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barycenter "Barycenter"), and redshift maps of clusters showing a [fingers of god](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fingers_of_god "Fingers of god") effect due to the scatter of peculiar velocities in a roughly spherical distribution.[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPeebles199334-63) These "redshift-space distortions" can be used as a cosmological probe in their own right, providing information on how structure formed in the Universe,[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-64) and how gravity behaves on large scales.[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-65)

The Hubble law's linear relationship between distance and redshift assumes that the rate of expansion of the universe is constant. However, when the universe was much younger, the expansion rate, and thus the Hubble "constant", was larger than it is today. For more distant galaxies, then, whose light has been travelling to us for much longer times, the approximation of constant expansion rate fails, and the Hubble law becomes a non-linear integral relationship and dependent on the history of the expansion rate since the emission of the light from the galaxy in question. Observations of the redshift-distance relationship can be used, then, to determine the expansion history of the universe and thus the matter and energy content.[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-66)

While it was long believed that the expansion rate has been continuously decreasing since the Big Bang, observations beginning in 1988 of the redshift-distance relationship using [Type Ia supernovae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Type_Ia_supernova "Type Ia supernova") have suggested that in comparatively recent times the expansion rate of the universe has [begun to accelerate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe "Accelerating expansion of the universe").[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-67)

[![Image 36](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Comoving_distance_and_lookback_time_%28Planck_2018%29.png/500px-Comoving_distance_and_lookback_time_%28Planck_2018%29.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Comoving_distance_and_lookback_time_(Planck_2018).png)

[Comoving distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comoving_and_proper_distances "Comoving and proper distances") and [lookback time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lookback_time "Lookback time") for the Planck 2018 cosmology parameters, from redshift 0 to 15, with distance (blue solid line) on the left axis, and time (orange dashed line) on the right. Note that the time that has passed (in giga years) from a given redshift until now is not the same as the distance (in giga light years) light would have traveled from that redshift, due to the expansion of the universe over the intervening period.

The most reliable redshifts are from [spectroscopic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic "Spectroscopic") data,[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-68) and the highest-confirmed spectroscopic redshift of a galaxy is that of [JADES-GS-z14-0](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/JADES-GS-z14-0 "JADES-GS-z14-0") with a redshift of _z_ = 14.32, corresponding to 290 million years after the Big Bang.[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-69) The previous record was held by [GN-z11](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GN-z11 "GN-z11"),[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-70) with a redshift of _z_ = 11.1, corresponding to 400 million years after the Big Bang.

Slightly less reliable are [Lyman-break](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lyman-break_galaxy "Lyman-break galaxy") redshifts, the highest of which is the lensed galaxy A1689-zD1 at a redshift _z_ = 7.5[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-71)[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-72) and the next highest being _z_ = 7.0.[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-73) The most distant-observed [gamma-ray burst](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamma-ray_burst "Gamma-ray burst") with a spectroscopic redshift measurement was [GRB 090423](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GRB_090423 "GRB 090423"), which had a redshift of _z_ = 8.2.[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-74) The most distant-known quasar, [ULAS J1342+0928](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ULAS_J1342%2B0928 "ULAS J1342+0928"), is at _z_ = 7.54.[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-75)[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-Nature-2018-01-76) The highest-known redshift radio galaxy (TGSS1530) is at a redshift _z_ = 5.72[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-77) and the highest-known redshift molecular material is the detection of emission from the CO molecule from the quasar SDSS J1148+5251 at _z_ = 6.42.[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-78)

_Extremely red objects_ (EROs) are [astronomical sources](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radio_astronomy#Astronomical_sources "Radio astronomy") of radiation that radiate energy in the red and near infrared part of the electromagnetic spectrum. These may be starburst galaxies that have a high redshift accompanied by reddening from intervening dust, or they could be highly redshifted elliptical galaxies with an older (and therefore redder) stellar population.[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-79) Objects that are even redder than EROs are termed _hyper extremely red objects_ (HEROs).[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-80)

The [cosmic microwave background](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background "Cosmic microwave background") has a redshift of z = 1089, corresponding to an age of approximately 379,000 years after the Big Bang and a [proper distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comoving_and_proper_distances "Comoving and proper distances") of more than 46 billion light-years.[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-ly93-81) This redshift corresponds to a shift in average temperature from 3000K down to 3K.[[82]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-82) The yet-to-be-observed first light from the oldest [Population III stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Population_III_stars "Population III stars"), not long after atoms first formed and the CMB ceased to be absorbed almost completely, may have redshifts in the range of 20 <_z_< 100.[[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-83) Other high-redshift events predicted by physics but not presently observable are the [cosmic neutrino background](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_neutrino_background "Cosmic neutrino background") from about two seconds after the Big Bang (and a redshift in excess of _z_> 10 10)[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-84) and the cosmic [gravitational wave background](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_wave_background "Gravitational wave background") emitted directly from [inflation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inflation_(cosmology) "Inflation (cosmology)") at a redshift in excess of _z_> 10 25.[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-85)

In June 2015, astronomers reported evidence for [Population III stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_population#Population_III_stars "Stellar population") in the [Cosmos Redshift 7](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmos_Redshift_7 "Cosmos Redshift 7")[galaxy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy "Galaxy") at _z_ = 6.60. Such stars are likely to have existed in the very early universe (i.e., at high redshift), and may have started the production of [chemical elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_element "Chemical element") heavier than [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen") that are needed for the later formation of [planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planet "Planet") and [life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life "Life") as we know it.[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-AJ-20150604-86)[[87]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-NYT-20150617-87)

[![Image 37](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/2dfgrs.png/250px-2dfgrs.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:2dfgrs.png)

Rendering of the 2dFGRS data

With advent of automated [telescopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Telescope "Telescope") and improvements in [spectroscopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_spectroscopy "Astronomical spectroscopy"), a number of collaborations have been made to map the universe in redshift space. By combining redshift with angular position data, a redshift survey maps the 3D distribution of matter within a field of the sky. These observations are used to measure properties of the [large-scale structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe "Observable universe") of the universe. The [Great Wall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CfA2_Great_Wall "CfA2 Great Wall"), a vast [supercluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supercluster "Supercluster") of galaxies over 500 million [light-years](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light-year "Light-year") wide, provides a dramatic example of a large-scale structure that redshift surveys can detect.[[88]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-88)

The first redshift survey was the [CfA Redshift Survey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CfA_Redshift_Survey "CfA Redshift Survey"), started in 1977 with the initial data collection completed in 1982.[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-89) More recently, the [2dF Galaxy Redshift Survey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2dF_Galaxy_Redshift_Survey "2dF Galaxy Redshift Survey") determined the large-scale structure of one section of the universe, measuring redshifts for over 220,000 galaxies; data collection was completed in 2002, and the final [data set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_set "Data set") was released 30 June 2003.[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-90) The [Sloan Digital Sky Survey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sloan_Digital_Sky_Survey "Sloan Digital Sky Survey") (SDSS) began collecting data in 1998[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-91) and published its eighteenth data release in 2023.[[92]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-92) SSDS has measured redshifts for galaxies as high as 0.8, and has recorded over 100,000 [quasars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasar "Quasar") at _z_ = 3 and beyond.[[93]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-93) The [DEEP2 Redshift Survey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DEEP2_Redshift_Survey "DEEP2 Redshift Survey") used the [Keck telescopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Keck_telescopes "Keck telescopes") with the "DEIMOS" [spectrograph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectrograph "Spectrograph"); a follow-up to the pilot program DEEP1, DEEP2 was designed to measure faint galaxies with redshifts 0.7 and above, and it recorded redshifts of over 38,000 objects by its conclusion in 2013.[[94]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-94)[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-95)

Effects from physical optics or radiative transfer
--------------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Redshift&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Effects from physical optics or radiative transfer")]

The interactions and phenomena summarized in the subjects of [radiative transfer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiative_transfer "Radiative transfer") and [physical optics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_optics "Physical optics") can result in shifts in the wavelength and frequency of electromagnetic radiation. In such cases, the shifts correspond to a physical energy transfer to matter or other photons rather than being by a transformation between reference frames. Such shifts can be from such physical phenomena as [coherence effects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolf_effect "Wolf effect") or the [scattering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scattering "Scattering") of [electromagnetic radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation "Electromagnetic radiation") whether from [charged](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_charge "Electric charge")[elementary particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementary_particle "Elementary particle"), from [particulates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particulates "Particulates"), or from fluctuations of the [index of refraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Index_of_refraction "Index of refraction") in a [dielectric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dielectric "Dielectric") medium as occurs in the radio phenomenon of [radio whistlers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whistler_(radio) "Whistler (radio)").[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-basicastronomy-43) While such phenomena are sometimes referred to as "redshifts" and "blueshifts", in astrophysics light-matter interactions that result in energy shifts in the radiation field are generally referred to as "reddening" rather than "redshifting" which, as a term, is normally reserved for the [effects discussed above](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#Redshift_formulae).[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-basicastronomy-43)

In many circumstances scattering causes radiation to redden because [entropy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy "Entropy") results in the predominance of many low-[energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy "Energy") photons over few high-energy ones (while [conserving total energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservation_of_energy "Conservation of energy")).[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-basicastronomy-43) Except possibly under carefully controlled conditions, scattering does not produce the same relative change in wavelength across the whole spectrum; that is, any calculated _z_ is generally a [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)") of wavelength. Furthermore, scattering from [random](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Randomness "Randomness")[media](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matter "Matter") generally occurs at many [angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle "Angle"), and _z_ is a function of the scattering angle. If multiple scattering occurs, or the scattering particles have relative motion, then there is generally distortion of [spectral lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectral_line "Spectral line") as well.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-basicastronomy-43)

In [interstellar astronomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_medium "Interstellar medium"), [visible spectra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visible_spectrum "Visible spectrum") can appear redder due to scattering processes in a phenomenon referred to as [interstellar reddening](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_reddening "Interstellar reddening")[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-basicastronomy-43)—similarly [Rayleigh scattering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rayleigh_scattering "Rayleigh scattering") causes the [atmospheric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth "Atmosphere of Earth") reddening of the Sun seen in the sunrise or sunset and causes the rest of the sky to have a blue color. This phenomenon is distinct from red _shift_ ing because the [spectroscopic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic "Spectroscopic") lines are not shifted to other wavelengths in reddened objects and there is an additional [dimming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction_(astronomy) "Extinction (astronomy)") and distortion associated with the phenomenon due to photons being scattered in and out of the [line of sight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line-of-sight_propagation "Line-of-sight propagation").[[96]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-96)

The opposite of a redshift is a **blueshift**. A blueshift is any decrease in [wavelength](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wavelength "Wavelength") (increase in [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy "Energy")), with a corresponding increase in frequency, of an [electromagnetic wave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_wave "Electromagnetic wave"). In [visible light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light "Light"), this shifts a color towards the blue end of the spectrum.

[![Image 38](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Redshift_blueshift.svg/250px-Redshift_blueshift.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Redshift_blueshift.svg)

Doppler redshift and blueshift

[Doppler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doppler_effect "Doppler effect") blueshift is caused by movement of a source towards the observer. The term applies to any decrease in wavelength and increase in frequency caused by relative motion, even outside the [visible spectrum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visible_spectrum "Visible spectrum"). Only objects moving at near-[relativistic speeds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_speed "Relativistic speed") toward the observer are noticeably bluer to the [naked eye](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naked_eye "Naked eye"), but the wavelength of any reflected or emitted photon or other particle is shortened in the direction of travel.[[97]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-97)

Doppler blueshift is used in [astronomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy "Astronomy") to determine relative motion:

*   The [Andromeda Galaxy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andromeda_Galaxy "Andromeda Galaxy") is moving toward our own [Milky Way](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milky_Way "Milky Way")[galaxy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy "Galaxy") within the [Local Group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_Group "Local Group"); thus, when observed from Earth, its light is undergoing a blueshift.[[98]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-98)
*   Components of a [binary star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_star "Binary star") system will be blueshifted when moving towards Earth
*   When observing spiral galaxies, the side spinning toward us will have a slight blueshift _relative to_ the side spinning away from us (see [Tully–Fisher relation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tully%E2%80%93Fisher_relation "Tully–Fisher relation")).
*   [Blazars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blazar "Blazar") are known to propel [relativistic jets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_jet "Relativistic jet") toward us, emitting [synchrotron radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synchrotron_radiation "Synchrotron radiation") and [bremsstrahlung](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bremsstrahlung "Bremsstrahlung") that appears blueshifted.[[99]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-99)
*   Nearby stars such as [Barnard's Star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barnard%27s_Star "Barnard's Star") are moving toward us, resulting in a very small blueshift.
*   Doppler blueshift of distant objects with a high _z_ can be subtracted from the much larger [cosmological redshift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble%27s_law "Hubble's law") to determine relative motion in the [expanding universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_expansion_of_space "Metric expansion of space").[[100]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-Aoki2005-100)

### Gravitational blueshift

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Redshift&action=edit&section=17 "Edit section: Gravitational blueshift")]

[![Image 39](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Gravitional_well.jpg/250px-Gravitional_well.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Gravitional_well.jpg)

[Matter waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matter_waves "Matter waves") (protons, electrons, photons, etc.) falling into a [gravity well](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity_well "Gravity well") become more energetic and undergo observer-independent blueshifting.

Unlike the _relative_ Doppler blueshift, caused by movement of a source towards the observer and thus dependent on the received angle of the photon, gravitational blueshift is _absolute_ and does not depend on the received angle of the photon:

> Photons climbing out of a gravitating object become less energetic. This loss of energy is known as a "redshifting", as photons in the visible spectrum would appear more red. Similarly, photons falling into a gravitational field become more energetic and exhibit a blueshifting. ... Note that the magnitude of the redshifting (blueshifting) effect is not a function of the emitted angle or the received angle of the photon—it depends only on how far radially the photon had to climb out of (fall into) the potential well.[[101]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-R.N_1-101)[[102]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-R.N_2-102)

It is a natural consequence of [conservation of energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservation_of_energy "Conservation of energy") and [mass–energy equivalence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass%E2%80%93energy_equivalence "Mass–energy equivalence"), and was confirmed experimentally in 1959 with the [Pound–Rebka experiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pound%E2%80%93Rebka_experiment "Pound–Rebka experiment"). Gravitational blueshift contributes to [cosmic microwave background](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background "Cosmic microwave background") (CMB) anisotropy via the [Sachs–Wolfe effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sachs%E2%80%93Wolfe_effect "Sachs–Wolfe effect"): when a gravitational well evolves while a photon is passing, the amount of blueshift on approach will differ from the amount of [gravitational redshift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_redshift "Gravitational redshift") as it leaves the region.[[103]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-Bonometto2002-103)

There are faraway [active galaxies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Active_galaxies "Active galaxies") that show a blueshift in their [[O III]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doubly_ionized_oxygen "Doubly ionized oxygen") emission [lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emission_spectrum "Emission spectrum"). One of the largest blueshifts is found in the narrow-line [quasar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasar "Quasar"), [PG 1543+489](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PG_1543%2B489 "PG 1543+489"), which has a relative velocity of −1150 km/s.[[100]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-Aoki2005-100) These types of galaxies are called "blue outliers".[[100]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-Aoki2005-100)

### Cosmological blueshift

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Redshift&action=edit&section=19 "Edit section: Cosmological blueshift")]

In a hypothetical universe undergoing a runaway [Big Crunch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Crunch "Big Crunch") contraction, a cosmological blueshift would be observed, with galaxies further away being increasingly blueshifted—the exact opposite of the actually observed [cosmological redshift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmological_redshift "Cosmological redshift") in the present [expanding universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expanding_universe "Expanding universe").[[104]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#cite_note-104)
