In [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry"), a **Platonic solid** is a [convex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_polytope "Convex polytope"), [regular polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polyhedron "Regular polyhedron") in [three-dimensional Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_space "Three-dimensional space"). Being a regular polyhedron means that the [faces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Face_(geometry) "Face (geometry)") are [congruent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congruence_(geometry) "Congruence (geometry)") (identical in shape and size) [regular polygons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polygon "Regular polygon") (all [angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle "Angle") congruent and all [edges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edge_(geometry) "Edge (geometry)") congruent), and the same number of faces meet at each [vertex.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_(geometry) "Vertex (geometry)") There are only five such polyhedra: a [tetrahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron "Tetrahedron") (four faces), a [cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube "Cube") (six faces), an [octahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octahedron "Octahedron") (eight faces), a [dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dodecahedron "Dodecahedron") (twelve faces), and an [icosahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Icosahedron "Icosahedron") (twenty faces).

[Geometers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometer "Geometer") have studied the Platonic solids for thousands of years.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platonic_solid#cite_note-1) They are named for the ancient Greek philosopher [Plato](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plato "Plato"), who hypothesized in one of his dialogues, the _[Timaeus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timaeus\_(dialogue) "Timaeus (dialogue)")_, that the [classical elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_element "Classical element") were made of these regular solids.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platonic_solid#cite_note-The_Stanford_Encyclopedia_of_Philosophy-2)

The Platonic solids have been known since antiquity. It has been suggested that certain [carved stone balls](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carved_stone_balls "Carved stone balls") created by the [late Neolithic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Late_Neolithic "Late Neolithic") people of [Scotland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scotland "Scotland") represent these shapes; however, these balls have rounded knobs rather than being polyhedral, the numbers of knobs frequently differed from the numbers of vertices of the Platonic solids, there is no ball whose knobs match the 20 vertices of the dodecahedron, and the arrangement of the knobs was not always symmetrical.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platonic_solid#cite_note-FOOTNOTELloyd2012-3)

The [ancient Greeks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greeks "Ancient Greeks") studied the Platonic solids extensively. Some sources (such as [Proclus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proclus "Proclus")) credit [Pythagoras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras "Pythagoras") with their discovery. Other evidence suggests that he may have only been familiar with the tetrahedron, cube, and dodecahedron and that the discovery of the octahedron and icosahedron belong to [Theaetetus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theaetetus_(mathematician) "Theaetetus (mathematician)"), a contemporary of Plato. In any case, Theaetetus gave a mathematical description of all five and may have been responsible for the first known proof that no other convex regular polyhedra exist.

The Platonic solids are prominent in the philosophy of [Plato](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plato "Plato"), their namesake. Plato wrote about them in the dialogue [_Timaeus_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timaeus_(dialogue) "Timaeus (dialogue)")c. 360 B.C. in which he associated each of the four [classical elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_element "Classical element") ([earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth_(classical_element) "Earth (classical element)"), [air](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Air_(classical_element) "Air (classical element)"), [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water_(classical_element) "Water (classical element)"), and [fire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fire_(classical_element) "Fire (classical element)")) with a regular solid. Earth was associated with the cube, air with the octahedron, water with the icosahedron, and fire with the tetrahedron. Of the fifth Platonic solid, the dodecahedron, Plato obscurely remarked, "...the god used [it] for arranging the constellations on the whole heaven". [Aristotle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotle "Aristotle") added a fifth element, [aither](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aether_(classical_element) "Aether (classical element)") (aether in Latin, "ether" in English) and postulated that the heavens were made of this element, but he had no interest in matching it with Plato's fifth solid.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platonic_solid#cite_note-4)

[Euclid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid "Euclid") completely mathematically described the Platonic solids in the [_Elements_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid%27s_Elements "Euclid's Elements"), the last book (Book XIII) of which is devoted to their properties. Propositions 13–17 in Book XIII describe the construction of the tetrahedron, octahedron, cube, icosahedron, and dodecahedron in that order. For each solid Euclid finds the ratio of the diameter of the circumscribed sphere to the edge length. In Proposition 18 he argues that there are no further convex regular polyhedra. [Andreas Speiser](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andreas_Speiser "Andreas Speiser") has advocated the view that the construction of the five regular solids is the chief goal of the deductive system canonized in the _Elements_.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platonic_solid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeyl195274-5) Much of the information in Book XIII is probably derived from the work of Theaetetus.

[![Image 7](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Mysterium_Cosmographicum_solar_system_model.jpg/250px-Mysterium_Cosmographicum_solar_system_model.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Mysterium_Cosmographicum_solar_system_model.jpg)

[Kepler's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johannes_Kepler "Johannes Kepler") Platonic solid model of the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System") from _[Mysterium Cosmographicum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mysterium\_Cosmographicum "Mysterium Cosmographicum")_ (1596)

In the 16th century, the German [astronomer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomer "Astronomer")[Johannes Kepler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johannes_Kepler "Johannes Kepler") attempted to relate the five extraterrestrial [planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planet "Planet") known at that time to the five Platonic solids. In _[Mysterium Cosmographicum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mysterium\_Cosmographicum "Mysterium Cosmographicum")_, published in 1596, Kepler proposed a model of the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System") in which the five solids were set inside one another and separated by a series of inscribed and circumscribed spheres. Kepler proposed that the distance relationships between the six planets known at that time could be understood in terms of the five Platonic solids enclosed within a sphere that represented the orbit of [Saturn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saturn "Saturn"). The six spheres each corresponded to one of the planets ([Mercury](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mercury_(planet) "Mercury (planet)"), [Venus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Venus "Venus"), [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth "Earth"), [Mars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mars "Mars"), [Jupiter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jupiter "Jupiter"), and Saturn). The solids were ordered with the innermost being the octahedron, followed by the icosahedron, dodecahedron, tetrahedron, and finally the cube, thereby dictating the structure of the solar system and the distance relationships between the planets by the Platonic solids. In the end, Kepler's original idea had to be abandoned, but out of his research came his [three laws of orbital dynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler%27s_laws_of_planetary_motion "Kepler's laws of planetary motion"), the first of which was that [the orbits of planets are ellipses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler%27s_laws_of_planetary_motion#First_law "Kepler's laws of planetary motion") rather than circles, changing the course of physics and astronomy.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platonic_solid#cite_note-6) He also discovered the [Kepler solids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler%E2%80%93Poinsot_polyhedron "Kepler–Poinsot polyhedron"), which are two _nonconvex_ regular polyhedra.

The coordinates for the tetrahedron, dodecahedron, and icosahedron are given in two positions such that each can be deduced from the other: in the case of the tetrahedron, by changing all coordinates of sign ([central symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_symmetry "Central symmetry")), or, in the other cases, by exchanging two coordinates ([reflection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflection_(geometry) "Reflection (geometry)") with respect to any of the three diagonal planes).

These coordinates reveal certain relationships between the Platonic solids: the vertices of the tetrahedron represent half of those of the cube, as {4,3} or ![Image 10](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/bd/CDel_node_1.png)![Image 11](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/8c/CDel_4.png)![Image 12](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/5e/CDel_node.png)![Image 13](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c3/CDel_3.png)![Image 14](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/5e/CDel_node.png), one of two sets of 4 vertices in dual positions, as h{4,3} or ![Image 15](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/28/CDel_node_h.png)![Image 16](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/8c/CDel_4.png)![Image 17](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/5e/CDel_node.png)![Image 18](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c3/CDel_3.png)![Image 19](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/5e/CDel_node.png). Both tetrahedral positions make the compound [stellated octahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellated_octahedron "Stellated octahedron").

The coordinates of the icosahedron are related to two alternated sets of coordinates of a nonuniform [truncated octahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Truncated_octahedron "Truncated octahedron"), t{3,4} or ![Image 20](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/bd/CDel_node_1.png)![Image 21](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c3/CDel_3.png)![Image 22](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/bd/CDel_node_1.png)![Image 23](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/8c/CDel_4.png)![Image 24](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/5e/CDel_node.png), also called a _[snub octahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Icosahedron#Pyritohedral\_symmetry "Icosahedron")_, as s{3,4} or ![Image 25](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/28/CDel_node_h.png)![Image 26](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c3/CDel_3.png)![Image 27](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/28/CDel_node_h.png)![Image 28](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/8c/CDel_4.png)![Image 29](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/5e/CDel_node.png), and seen in the [compound of two icosahedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compound_of_two_icosahedra "Compound of two icosahedra").

Eight of the vertices of the dodecahedron are shared with the cube. Completing all orientations leads to the [compound of five cubes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compound_of_five_cubes "Compound of five cubes").

Combinatorial properties
------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Platonic_solid&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Combinatorial properties")]

A convex polyhedron is a Platonic solid if and only if all three of the following requirements are met.

*   All of its faces are [congruent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congruence_(geometry) "Congruence (geometry)") convex [regular polygons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polygon "Regular polygon").
*   None of its faces intersect except at their edges.
*   The same number of faces meet at each of its [vertices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_(geometry) "Vertex (geometry)").

Each Platonic solid can therefore be assigned a pair {_p_,_q_} of integers, where _p_ is the number of edges (or, equivalently, vertices) of each face, and _q_ is the number of faces (or, equivalently, edges) that meet at each vertex. This pair {_p_,_q_}, called the [Schläfli symbol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schl%C3%A4fli_symbol "Schläfli symbol"), gives a [combinatorial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Combinatorics "Combinatorics") description of the polyhedron. The Schläfli symbols of the five Platonic solids are given in the table below.

This can be proved in many ways. Together these three relationships completely determine _V_, _E_, and _F_:

![Image 37: {\displaystyle V={\frac {4p}{4-(p-2)(q-2)}},\quad E={\frac {2pq}{4-(p-2)(q-2)}},\quad F={\frac {4q}{4-(p-2)(q-2)}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6df862153fa52059bba868e54445e16b88365379)

Swapping _p_ and _q_ interchanges _F_ and _V_ while leaving _E_ unchanged. For a geometric interpretation of this property, see [§Dual polyhedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platonic_solid#Dual_polyhedra).

The elements of a polyhedron can be expressed in a [configuration matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Configuration_(polytope)#Higher_dimensions "Configuration (polytope)"). The rows and columns correspond to vertices, edges, and faces. The diagonal numbers say how many of each element occur in the whole polyhedron. The nondiagonal numbers say how many of the column's element occur in or at the row's element. Dual pairs of polyhedra have their configuration matrices rotated 180 degrees from each other.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platonic_solid#cite_note-7)

Defect 0°
A vertex needs at least 3 faces, and an [angle defect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle_defect "Angle defect"). 

A 0° angle defect will fill the Euclidean plane with a regular tiling. 

By [Descartes' theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_defect#Descartes'_theorem "Angular defect"), the number of vertices is 720°/_defect_.

The following geometric argument is very similar to the one given by [Euclid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid "Euclid") in the [_Elements_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid%27s_Elements "Euclid's Elements"):

1.   Each vertex of the solid must be a vertex for at least three faces.
2.   At each vertex of the solid, the total, among the adjacent faces, of the angles between their respective adjacent sides must be strictly less than 360°. The amount less than 360° is called an [angle defect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle_defect "Angle defect").
3.   Regular polygons of [six](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hexagon "Hexagon") or more sides have only angles of 120° or more, so the common face must be the triangle, square, or pentagon. For these different shapes of faces the following holds: [Triangular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle "Triangle") faces Each vertex of a regular triangle is 60°, so a shape may have three, four, or five triangles meeting at a vertex; these are the tetrahedron, octahedron, and icosahedron respectively.[Square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_(geometry) "Square (geometry)") faces Each vertex of a square is 90°, so there is only one arrangement possible with three faces at a vertex, the cube.[Pentagonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentagon "Pentagon") faces Each vertex is 108°; again, only one arrangement of three faces at a vertex is possible, the dodecahedron. Altogether this makes five possible Platonic solids.

A purely [topological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology "Topology") proof can be made using only combinatorial information about the solids. The key is [Euler's observation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler_characteristic "Euler characteristic") that _V_−_E_+_F_=2, and the fact that _pF_=2 _E_=_qV_, where _p_ stands for the number of edges of each face and _q_ for the number of edges meeting at each vertex. Combining these equations one obtains the equation

![Image 47: {\displaystyle {\frac {2E}{q}}-E+{\frac {2E}{p}}=2.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/652ab2fe681efa7e0d4695cfb1c9c8d05cadc3b2)

Simple algebraic manipulation then gives

![Image 48: {\displaystyle {1 \over q}+{1 \over p}={1 \over 2}+{1 \over E}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/665b926626ed0870f467322365e64ab9d7e595af)

Since _E_ is strictly positive we must have

![Image 49: {\displaystyle {\frac {1}{q}}+{\frac {1}{p}}>{\frac {1}{2}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/179b11d04806df402acfd4325a351a4f76c15b5f)

Using the fact that _p_ and _q_ must both be at least 3, one can easily see that there are only five possibilities for {_p_,_q_}:

{3,3}, {4,3}, {3,4}, {5,3}, {3,5}.

Geometric properties
--------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Platonic_solid&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Geometric properties")]

There are a number of [angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle "Angle") associated with each Platonic solid. The [dihedral angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihedral_angle "Dihedral angle") is the interior angle between any two face planes. The dihedral angle, _θ_, of the solid {_p_,_q_} is given by the formula

![Image 50: {\displaystyle \sin(\theta /2)={\frac {\cos(\pi /q)}{\sin(\pi /p)}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fa715463181c3b39c945efa0f83c20fb2ddb108b)

This is sometimes more conveniently expressed in terms of the [tangent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_(trigonometric_function) "Tangent (trigonometric function)") by

![Image 51: {\displaystyle \tan(\theta /2)={\frac {\cos(\pi /q)}{\sin(\pi /h)}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4964e27784e94a3cd8182c9f919a6cde96a182b7)

The quantity _h_ (called the [Coxeter number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coxeter_number "Coxeter number")) is 4, 6, 6, 10, and 10 for the tetrahedron, cube, octahedron, dodecahedron, and icosahedron respectively.

The [angular deficiency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_deficiency "Angular deficiency") at the vertex of a polyhedron is the difference between the sum of the face-angles at that vertex and 2 π. The defect, _δ_, at any vertex of the Platonic solids {_p_,_q_} is

![Image 52: {\displaystyle \delta =2\pi -q\pi \left(1-{2 \over p}\right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/75527e34a157514fb9d4ae4a17ce6c14f1179dd2)

By a theorem of Descartes, this is equal to 4 π divided by the number of vertices (i.e. the total defect at all vertices is 4 π).

The three-dimensional analog of a plane angle is a [solid angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_angle "Solid angle"). The solid angle, _Ω_, at the vertex of a Platonic solid is given in terms of the dihedral angle by

![Image 53: {\displaystyle \Omega =q\theta -(q-2)\pi .\,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/194edbe85a1ee98d0b5f20524f0329db6acbcc74)

This follows from the [spherical excess](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_excess "Spherical excess") formula for a [spherical polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_polygon "Spherical polygon") and the fact that the [vertex figure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_figure "Vertex figure") of the polyhedron {_p_,_q_} is a regular _q_-gon.

The solid angle of a face subtended from the center of a platonic solid is equal to the solid angle of a full sphere (4 π steradians) divided by the number of faces. This is equal to the angular deficiency of its dual.

### Radii, area, and volume

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Platonic_solid&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Radii, area, and volume")]

Another virtue of regularity is that the Platonic solids all possess three concentric spheres:

*   the [circumscribed sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumscribed_sphere "Circumscribed sphere") that passes through all the vertices,
*   the [midsphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Midsphere "Midsphere") that is tangent to each edge at the midpoint of the edge, and
*   the [inscribed sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inscribed_sphere "Inscribed sphere") that is tangent to each face at the center of the face.

The [radii](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radius "Radius") of these spheres are called the _circumradius_, the _midradius_, and the _inradius_. These are the distances from the center of the polyhedron to the vertices, edge midpoints, and face centers respectively. The circumradius _R_ and the inradius _r_ of the solid {_p_,_q_} with edge length _a_ are given by

![Image 74: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}R&={\frac {a}{2}}\tan \left({\frac {\pi }{q}}\right)\tan \left({\frac {\theta }{2}}\right)\\[3pt]r&={\frac {a}{2}}\cot \left({\frac {\pi }{p}}\right)\tan \left({\frac {\theta }{2}}\right)\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cd89ad09dadad2c68f092a9317b285619b2b983a)

where _θ_ is the dihedral angle. The midradius _ρ_ is given by

![Image 75: {\displaystyle \rho ={\frac {a}{2}}\cos \left({\frac {\pi }{p}}\right)\,{\csc }{\biggl (}{\frac {\pi }{h}}{\biggr )}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/872160c2337d5b2bbfc36f5d6ce5b54a1a3a4887)

where _h_ is the quantity used above in the definition of the dihedral angle (_h_ = 4, 6, 6, 10, or 10). The ratio of the circumradius to the inradius is symmetric in _p_ and _q_:

Among the Platonic solids, either the dodecahedron or the icosahedron may be seen as the best approximation to the sphere. The icosahedron has the largest number of faces and the largest dihedral angle, it hugs its inscribed sphere the most tightly, and its surface area to volume ratio is closest to that of a sphere of the same size (i.e. either the same surface area or the same volume). The dodecahedron, on the other hand, has the smallest angular defect, the largest vertex solid angle, and it fills out its circumscribed sphere the most.

Every polyhedron has a [dual (or "polar") polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_polyhedron "Dual polyhedron")**with faces and vertices interchanged**. The dual of every Platonic solid is another Platonic solid, so that we can arrange the five solids into dual pairs.

*   The tetrahedron is [self-dual](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-dual_polyhedron "Self-dual polyhedron") (i.e. its dual is another tetrahedron).
*   The cube and the octahedron form a dual pair.
*   The dodecahedron and the icosahedron form a dual pair.

If a polyhedron has Schläfli symbol {_p_,_q_}, then its dual has the symbol {_q_,_p_}. Indeed, every combinatorial property of one Platonic solid can be interpreted as another combinatorial property of the dual.

One can construct the dual polyhedron by taking the vertices of the dual to be the centers of the faces of the original figure. Connecting the centers of adjacent faces in the original forms the edges of the dual and thereby interchanges the number of faces and vertices while maintaining the number of edges.

More generally, one can dualize a Platonic solid with respect to a sphere of radius _d_ concentric with the solid. The radii (_R_,_ρ_,_r_) of a solid and those of its dual (_R_*,_ρ_*,_r_*) are related by

![Image 117: {\displaystyle d^{2}=R^{\ast }r=r^{\ast }R=\rho ^{\ast }\rho .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3882378475f6c01ac91731d0bf82c0c8ac197441)

Dualizing with respect to the midsphere (_d_=_ρ_) is often convenient because the midsphere has the same relationship to both polyhedra. Taking _d_ 2=_Rr_ yields a dual solid with the same circumradius and inradius (i.e. _R_*=_R_ and _r_*=_r_).

In mathematics, the concept of [symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry "Symmetry") is studied with the notion of a [mathematical group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_(mathematics) "Group (mathematics)"). Every polyhedron has an associated [symmetry group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry_group "Symmetry group"), which is the set of all transformations ([Euclidean isometries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_isometry "Euclidean isometry")) which leave the polyhedron invariant. The [order](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Order_(group_theory) "Order (group theory)") of the symmetry group is the number of symmetries of the polyhedron. One often distinguishes between the _full symmetry group_, which includes [reflections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflection_(mathematics) "Reflection (mathematics)"), and the _proper symmetry group_, which includes only [rotations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation_(mathematics) "Rotation (mathematics)").

The symmetry groups of the Platonic solids are a special class of [three-dimensional point groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_groups_in_three_dimensions "Point groups in three dimensions") known as [polyhedral groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyhedral_group "Polyhedral group"). The high degree of symmetry of the Platonic solids can be interpreted in a number of ways. Most importantly, the vertices of each solid are all equivalent under the [action](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_action_(mathematics) "Group action (mathematics)") of the symmetry group, as are the edges and faces. One says the action of the symmetry group is [transitive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transitive_action "Transitive action") on the vertices, edges, and faces. In fact, this is another way of defining regularity of a polyhedron: a polyhedron is _regular_ if and only if it is [vertex-uniform](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex-uniform "Vertex-uniform"), [edge-uniform](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edge-uniform "Edge-uniform"), and [face-uniform](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Face-uniform "Face-uniform").

There are only three symmetry groups associated with the Platonic solids rather than five, since the symmetry group of any polyhedron coincides with that of its dual. This is easily seen by examining the construction of the dual polyhedron. Any symmetry of the original must be a symmetry of the dual and vice versa. The three polyhedral groups are:

*   the [tetrahedral group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedral_group "Tetrahedral group")_T_,
*   the [octahedral group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octahedral_group "Octahedral group")_O_ (which is also the symmetry group of the cube), and
*   the [icosahedral group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Icosahedral_group "Icosahedral group")_I_ (which is also the symmetry group of the dodecahedron).

The orders of the proper (rotation) groups are 12, 24, and 60 respectively – precisely twice the number of edges in the respective polyhedra. The orders of the full symmetry groups are twice as much again (24, 48, and 120). See (Coxeter 1973) for a derivation of these facts. All Platonic solids except the tetrahedron are _centrally symmetric,_ meaning they are preserved under [reflection through the origin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflection_through_the_origin "Reflection through the origin").

In nature and technology
------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Platonic_solid&action=edit&section=16 "Edit section: In nature and technology")]

[![Image 121](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/02/Circogoniaicosahedra_ekw.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Circogoniaicosahedra_ekw.jpg)

_Circogonia icosahedra_, a species of [radiolaria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiolaria "Radiolaria"), shaped like a [regular icosahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron "Regular icosahedron").

The tetrahedron, cube, and octahedron all occur naturally in [crystal structures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystal_structure "Crystal structure"). These by no means exhaust the numbers of possible forms of crystals. However, neither the regular icosahedron nor the regular dodecahedron are amongst them. One of the forms, called the [pyritohedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyritohedron "Pyritohedron") (named for the group of [minerals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrite "Pyrite") of which it is typical) has twelve pentagonal faces, arranged in the same pattern as the faces of the regular dodecahedron. The faces of the pyritohedron are, however, not regular, so the pyritohedron is also not regular. [Allotropes of boron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allotropes_of_boron "Allotropes of boron") and many [boron compounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystal_structure_of_boron-rich_metal_borides "Crystal structure of boron-rich metal borides"), such as [boron carbide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boron_carbide "Boron carbide"), include discrete B 12 icosahedra within their crystal structures. [Carborane acids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carborane_acid "Carborane acid") also have molecular structures approximating regular icosahedra.

In the early 20th century, [Ernst Haeckel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ernst_Haeckel "Ernst Haeckel") described a number of species of [Radiolaria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiolaria "Radiolaria"), some of whose skeletons are shaped like various regular polyhedra. Examples include _Circoporus octahedrus_, _Circogonia icosahedra_, _Lithocubus geometricus_ and _Circorrhegma dodecahedra_. The shapes of these creatures should be obvious from their names.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platonic_solid#cite_note-12)

Many [viruses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virus "Virus"), such as the [herpes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herpes "Herpes")[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platonic_solid#cite_note-13) virus, have the shape of a regular icosahedron. Viral structures are built of repeated identical [protein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protein "Protein") subunits and the icosahedron is the easiest shape to assemble using these subunits. A regular polyhedron is used because it can be built from a single basic unit protein used over and over again; this saves space in the viral [genome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genome "Genome").

In [meteorology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteorology "Meteorology") and [climatology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climatology "Climatology"), global numerical models of atmospheric flow are of increasing interest which employ [geodesic grids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic_grid "Geodesic grid") that are based on an icosahedron (refined by [triangulation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangulation "Triangulation")) instead of the more commonly used [longitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Longitude "Longitude")/[latitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latitude "Latitude") grid. This has the advantage of evenly distributed spatial resolution without [singularities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_singularity "Mathematical singularity") (i.e. the poles) at the expense of somewhat greater numerical difficulty.

Geometry of [space frames](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_frame "Space frame") is often based on platonic solids. In the MERO system, Platonic solids are used for naming convention of various space frame configurations. For example, ⁠1/2⁠O+T refers to a configuration made of one half of octahedron and a tetrahedron.

Several [Platonic hydrocarbons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platonic_hydrocarbons "Platonic hydrocarbons") have been synthesised, including [cubane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cubane "Cubane") and [dodecahedrane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dodecahedrane "Dodecahedrane").

There exist four regular polyhedra that are not convex, called [Kepler–Poinsot polyhedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler%E2%80%93Poinsot_polyhedron "Kepler–Poinsot polyhedron"). These all have [icosahedral symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Icosahedral_symmetry "Icosahedral symmetry") and may be obtained as [stellations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellation "Stellation") of the dodecahedron and the icosahedron.

The next most regular convex polyhedra after the Platonic solids are the [cuboctahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cuboctahedron "Cuboctahedron"), which is a [rectification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectification_(geometry) "Rectification (geometry)") of the cube and the octahedron, and the [icosidodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Icosidodecahedron "Icosidodecahedron"), which is a rectification of the dodecahedron and the icosahedron (the rectification of the self-dual tetrahedron is a regular octahedron). These are both _quasi-regular_, meaning that they are vertex- and edge-uniform and have regular faces, but the faces are not all congruent (coming in two different classes). They form two of the thirteen [Archimedean solids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archimedean_solid "Archimedean solid"), which are the convex [uniform polyhedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uniform_polyhedron "Uniform polyhedron") with polyhedral symmetry. Their duals, the [rhombic dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhombic_dodecahedron "Rhombic dodecahedron") and [rhombic triacontahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhombic_triacontahedron "Rhombic triacontahedron"), are edge- and face-transitive, but their faces are not regular and their vertices come in two types each; they are two of the thirteen [Catalan solids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catalan_solid "Catalan solid").

The uniform polyhedra form a much broader class of polyhedra. These figures are vertex-uniform and have one or more types of [regular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polygon "Regular polygon") or [star polygons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_polygon "Star polygon") for faces. These include all the polyhedra mentioned above together with an infinite set of [prisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prism_(geometry) "Prism (geometry)"), an infinite set of [antiprisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiprism "Antiprism"), and 53 other non-convex forms.

The [Johnson solids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johnson_solid "Johnson solid") are convex polyhedra which have regular faces but are not uniform. Among them are five of the eight convex [deltahedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deltahedron "Deltahedron"), which have identical, regular faces (all equilateral triangles) but are not uniform. (The other three convex deltahedra are the Platonic tetrahedron, octahedron, and icosahedron.)

The three [regular tessellations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_tilings_by_convex_regular_polygons#Regular_tilings "Euclidean tilings by convex regular polygons") of the plane are closely related to the Platonic solids. Indeed, one can view the Platonic solids as regular tessellations of the [sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere "Sphere"). This is done by projecting each solid onto a concentric sphere. The faces project onto regular [spherical polygons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_polygon "Spherical polygon") which exactly cover the sphere. Spherical tilings provide two infinite additional sets of regular tilings, the [hosohedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hosohedra "Hosohedra"), {2,_n_} with 2 vertices at the poles, and [lune](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lune_(mathematics) "Lune (mathematics)") faces, and the dual [dihedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihedra "Dihedra"), {_n_,2} with 2 hemispherical faces and regularly spaced vertices on the equator. Such tesselations would be degenerate in true 3D space as polyhedra.

Every regular tessellation of the sphere is characterized by a pair of integers {_p_,_q_} with ⁠1/_p_⁠+⁠1/_q_⁠>⁠1/2⁠. Likewise, a regular tessellation of the plane is characterized by the condition ⁠1/_p_⁠+⁠1/_q_⁠=⁠1/2⁠. There are three possibilities:

In a similar manner, one can consider regular tessellations of the [hyperbolic plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_geometry "Hyperbolic geometry"). These are characterized by the condition ⁠1/_p_⁠+⁠1/_q_⁠<⁠1/2⁠. There is an infinite family of such tessellations.

In more than three dimensions, polyhedra generalize to [polytopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polytope "Polytope"), with higher-dimensional convex [regular polytopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polytope "Regular polytope") being the equivalents of the three-dimensional Platonic solids.

In the mid-19th century the Swiss mathematician [Ludwig Schläfli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ludwig_Schl%C3%A4fli "Ludwig Schläfli") discovered the four-dimensional analogues of the Platonic solids, called [convex regular 4-polytopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_regular_4-polytope "Convex regular 4-polytope"). There are exactly six of these figures; five are analogous to the Platonic solids: [5-cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/5-cell "5-cell") as {3,3,3}, [16-cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/16-cell "16-cell") as {3,3,4}, [600-cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/600-cell "600-cell") as {3,3,5}, [tesseract](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tesseract "Tesseract") as {4,3,3}, and [120-cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/120-cell "120-cell") as {5,3,3}, and a sixth one, the self-dual [24-cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/24-cell "24-cell"), {3,4,3}.

In all dimensions higher than four, there are only three convex regular polytopes: the [simplex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simplex "Simplex") as {3,3,...,3}, the [hypercube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypercube "Hypercube") as {4,3,...,3}, and the [cross-polytope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross-polytope "Cross-polytope") as {3,3,...,4}.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platonic_solid#cite_note-FOOTNOTECoxeter1973136-18) In three dimensions, these coincide with the tetrahedron as {3,3}, the cube as {4,3}, and the octahedron as {3,4}.
