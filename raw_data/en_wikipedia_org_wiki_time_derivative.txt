From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

A **time derivative** is a [derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative "Derivative") of a function with respect to [time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time "Time"), usually interpreted as the rate of change of the value of the function.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_derivative#cite_note-1) The variable denoting time is usually written as ![Image 1: {\displaystyle t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/65658b7b223af9e1acc877d848888ecdb4466560).

A variety of notations are used to denote the time derivative. In addition to the normal ([Leibniz's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leibniz%27s_notation "Leibniz's notation")) notation,

![Image 2: {\displaystyle {\frac {dx}{dt}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5cd8f4577337ea1e3aa08b83b86f156fc04aff2c)
A very common short-hand notation used, especially in physics, is the 'over-dot'. I.E.

![Image 3: {\displaystyle {\dot {x}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a82c85f33714da82ab42d6b69eae07ab7e5e234b)
(This is called [Newton's notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_notation "Newton's notation"))

Higher time derivatives are also used: the [second derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_derivative "Second derivative") with respect to time is written as

![Image 4: {\displaystyle {\frac {d^{2}x}{dt^{2}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ea37779ec9019a8946c99c4c49767f90a7442399)
with the corresponding shorthand of ![Image 5: {\displaystyle {\ddot {x}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/06e0e705ddda28c6cd06cdc6e18be9abf88bb395).

As a generalization, the time derivative of a vector, say:

![Image 6: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} =\left[v_{1},\ v_{2},\ v_{3},\ldots \right]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68dfa5814b9db087a469264531cd6a72a34305a5)
is defined as the vector whose components are the derivatives of the components of the original vector. That is,

![Image 7: {\displaystyle {\frac {d\mathbf {v} }{dt}}=\left[{\frac {dv_{1}}{dt}},{\frac {dv_{2}}{dt}},{\frac {dv_{3}}{dt}},\ldots \right].}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/920d22bf6c5750867769364cf644a2fbe7245905)
Time derivatives are a key concept in [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics"). For example, for a changing [position](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Position_(vector) "Position (vector)")![Image 8: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4), its time derivative ![Image 9: {\displaystyle {\dot {x}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a82c85f33714da82ab42d6b69eae07ab7e5e234b) is its [velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity "Velocity"), and its second derivative with respect to time, ![Image 10: {\displaystyle {\ddot {x}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/06e0e705ddda28c6cd06cdc6e18be9abf88bb395), is its [acceleration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acceleration "Acceleration"). Even higher derivatives are sometimes also used: the third derivative of position with respect to time is known as the [jerk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerk_(physics) "Jerk (physics)"). See [motion graphs and derivatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Motion_graphs_and_derivatives "Motion graphs and derivatives").

A large number of fundamental equations in physics involve first or second time derivatives of quantities. Many other fundamental quantities in science are time derivatives of one another:

*   [force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Force "Force") is the time derivative of [momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum "Momentum")
*   [power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_(physics) "Power (physics)") is the time derivative of [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy "Energy")
*   [electric current](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_current "Electric current") is the time derivative of [electric charge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_charge "Electric charge")

and so on.

A common occurrence in physics is the time derivative of a [vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_(geometric) "Vector (geometric)"), such as velocity or displacement. In dealing with such a derivative, both magnitude and orientation may depend upon time.

### Example: circular motion

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Time_derivative&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Example: circular motion")]

[![Image 11](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Polar_rectangular.svg/330px-Polar_rectangular.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Polar_rectangular.svg)

Relation between Cartesian coordinates (_x_,_y_) and [polar coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_coordinates "Polar coordinates") (_r_,_θ_).

For example, consider a particle moving in a circular path. Its position is given by the displacement vector ![Image 12: {\displaystyle r=x{\hat {\imath }}+y{\hat {\jmath }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/95eb819fc3b57015f436010b9b483c9bec39dd8b), related to the angle, _θ_, and radial distance, _r_, as defined in the figure:

![Image 13: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}x&=r\cos(\theta )\\y&=r\sin(\theta )\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eaa2d4aa076d8ce94947e24604a12fbd47035292)
For this example, we assume that _θ_ = _t_. Hence, the displacement (position) at any time _t_ is given by

![Image 14: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} (t)=r\cos(t){\hat {\imath }}+r\sin(t){\hat {\jmath }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ebcc8dc6e13ff48932c63e4de72f9e9c0a3c5752)
This form shows the motion described by **r**(_t_) is in a circle of radius _r_ because the _magnitude_ of **r**(_t_) is given by

![Image 15: {\displaystyle |\mathbf {r} (t)|={\sqrt {\mathbf {r} (t)\cdot \mathbf {r} (t)}}={\sqrt {x(t)^{2}+y(t)^{2}}}=r\,{\sqrt {\cos ^{2}(t)+\sin ^{2}(t)}}=r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/53536686718734797966e31566ebc4bffe1402e4)
using the [trigonometric identity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trigonometric_identity "Trigonometric identity")sin 2(_t_) + cos 2(_t_) = 1 and where ![Image 16: {\displaystyle \cdot }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ba2c023bad1bd39ed49080f729cbf26bc448c9ba) is the usual Euclidean dot product.

With this form for the displacement, the velocity now is found. The time derivative of the displacement vector is the velocity vector. In general, the derivative of a vector is a vector made up of components each of which is the derivative of the corresponding component of the original vector. Thus, in this case, the velocity vector is:

![Image 17: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\mathbf {v} (t)={\frac {d\,\mathbf {r} (t)}{dt}}&=r\left[{\frac {d\,\cos(t)}{dt}},{\frac {d\,\sin(t)}{dt}}\right]\\&=r\ [-\sin(t),\ \cos(t)]\\&=[-y(t),x(t)].\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2430c683f9ec15ff667b1689397d12fb54568fca)
Thus the velocity of the particle is nonzero even though the magnitude of the position (that is, the radius of the path) is constant. The velocity is directed perpendicular to the displacement, as can be established using the [dot product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dot_product "Dot product"):

![Image 18: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} \cdot \mathbf {r} =[-y,x]\cdot [x,y]=-yx+xy=0\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fb7ed48b27b1ee26f840146fcc93fc1e6f08186e)
Acceleration is then the time-derivative of velocity:

![Image 19: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} (t)={\frac {d\,\mathbf {v} (t)}{dt}}=[-x(t),-y(t)]=-\mathbf {r} (t)\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d393d537ee16a061158073b64746fcc022b4a1b6)
The acceleration is directed inward, toward the axis of rotation. It points opposite to the position vector and perpendicular to the velocity vector. This inward-directed acceleration is called [centripetal acceleration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_force "Centripetal force").

In differential geometry
------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Time_derivative&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: In differential geometry")]

In [differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry"), quantities are often expressed with respect to the local [covariant basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvilinear_coordinates#Covariant_and_contravariant_bases "Curvilinear coordinates"), ![Image 20: {\displaystyle \mathbf {e} _{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ba77c5a75ef9e230d1a36183785477a2eb3c5c1e), where _i_ ranges over the number of dimensions. The components of a vector ![Image 21: {\displaystyle \mathbf {U} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b2141bec2344e3dc5241ff50b0fd366755e00223) expressed this way transform as a contravariant [tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_field "Tensor field"), as shown in the expression ![Image 22: {\displaystyle \mathbf {U} =U^{i}\mathbf {e} _{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fc53ab01268219d68222c38ec849911e047d1a7e), invoking [Einstein summation convention](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_summation_convention "Einstein summation convention"). If we want to calculate the time derivatives of these components along a trajectory, so that we have ![Image 23: {\displaystyle \mathbf {U} (t)=U^{i}(t)\mathbf {e} _{i}(t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f6ec7dd0cd19d5159c6b180b44d02202a15004c7), we can define a new operator, the invariant derivative ![Image 24: {\displaystyle \delta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c5321cfa797202b3e1f8620663ff43c4660ea03a), which will continue to return contravariant tensors:[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_derivative#cite_note-2)

![Image 25: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}{\frac {\delta U^{i}}{\delta t}}={\frac {dU^{i}}{dt}}+V^{j}\Gamma _{jk}^{i}U^{k}\\\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/325bdc4bbced0407c6ee2477d36496ba18741074)
where ![Image 26: {\displaystyle V^{j}={\frac {dx^{j}}{dt}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/26afe1cc2789de3107530c71328893cb821eadf7) (with ![Image 27: {\displaystyle x^{j}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/720ef99822043e29664d4eccb8f866c897fcab09) being the _j_ th coordinate) captures the components of the velocity in the local covariant basis, and ![Image 28: {\displaystyle \Gamma _{jk}^{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4738f0f3db1071df585ecf33486ea86614e2b885) are the [Christoffel symbols](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christoffel_symbols "Christoffel symbols") for the coordinate system. Note that explicit dependence on _t_ has been repressed in the notation. We can then write:

![Image 29: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}{\frac {d\mathbf {U} }{dt}}={\frac {\delta U^{i}}{\delta t}}\mathbf {e} _{i}\\\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8ed78b3b9a06108dea318ec96cf250204be7703e)
as well as:

![Image 30: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}{\frac {d^{2}\mathbf {U} }{dt^{2}}}={\frac {\delta ^{2}U^{i}}{\delta t^{2}}}\mathbf {e} _{i}\\\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9b949cce7a650f19604b8fd38dfb10636890b9c4)
In terms of the [covariant derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covariant_derivative "Covariant derivative"), ![Image 31: {\displaystyle \nabla _{j}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a9e252d1dd7ab8513a5f83b0d03380d8ee63d64d), we have:

![Image 32: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}{\frac {\delta U^{i}}{\delta t}}=V^{j}\nabla _{j}U^{i}\\\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0f19a250f263237685182e2dcd2d7087ffe6a734)
In [economics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economics "Economics"), many theoretical models of the evolution of various economic variables are constructed in [continuous time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_time "Continuous time") and therefore employ time derivatives.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_derivative#cite_note-3): ch. 1-3  One situation involves a [stock variable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stocks_and_flows "Stocks and flows") and its time derivative, a [flow variable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stocks_and_flows "Stocks and flows"). Examples include:

*   The flow of net [fixed investment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fixed_investment "Fixed investment") is the time derivative of the [capital stock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capital_stock "Capital stock").
*   The flow of [inventory investment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inventory_investment "Inventory investment") is the time derivative of the stock of [inventories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inventories "Inventories").
*   The growth rate of the [money supply](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Money_supply "Money supply") is the time derivative of the money supply divided by the money supply itself.

Sometimes the time derivative of a flow variable can appear in a model:

*   The growth rate of [output](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Output_(economics) "Output (economics)") is the time derivative of the flow of output divided by output itself.
*   The growth rate of the [labor force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Labor_force "Labor force") is the time derivative of the labor force divided by the labor force itself.

And sometimes there appears a time derivative of a variable which, unlike the examples above, is not measured in units of currency:

*   The time derivative of a key [interest rate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interest_rate "Interest rate") can appear.
*   The [inflation rate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inflation_rate "Inflation rate") is the growth rate of the [price level](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Price_level "Price level")—that is, the time derivative of the price level divided by the price level itself.
