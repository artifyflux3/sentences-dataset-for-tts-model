Title: Bose–Einstein statistics

URL Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bose%E2%80%93Einstein_distribution

Published Time: 2003-02-26T23:39:50Z

Markdown Content:
In [quantum statistics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_statistics "Quantum statistics"), **Bose–Einstein statistics** (**B–E statistics**) describes one of two possible ways in which a collection of non-interacting [identical particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Identical_particles "Identical particles") may occupy a set of available discrete [energy states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_level "Energy level") at [thermodynamic equilibrium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamic_equilibrium "Thermodynamic equilibrium"). The aggregation of particles in the same state, which is a characteristic of particles obeying Bose–Einstein statistics, accounts for the cohesive streaming of [laser light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laser#Quantum_vs._classical_emission_processes "Laser") and the frictionless creeping of [superfluid helium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superfluid_helium-4 "Superfluid helium-4"). The theory of this behaviour was developed (1924–25) by [Satyendra Nath Bose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satyendra_Nath_Bose "Satyendra Nath Bose"), who recognized that a collection of identical and indistinguishable particles could be distributed in this way. The idea was later adopted and extended by [Albert Einstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Einstein "Albert Einstein") in collaboration with Bose.

Bose–Einstein statistics apply only to particles that do not follow the [Pauli exclusion principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pauli_exclusion_principle "Pauli exclusion principle") restrictions. Particles that follow Bose-Einstein statistics are called [bosons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bosons "Bosons"), which have integer values of [spin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_(physics) "Spin (physics)"). In contrast, particles that follow [Fermi-Dirac statistics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermi%E2%80%93Dirac_statistics "Fermi–Dirac statistics") are called [fermions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermion "Fermion") and have [half-integer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Half-integer "Half-integer") spins.

Bose–Einstein distribution
--------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bose%E2%80%93Einstein_statistics&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Bose–Einstein distribution")]

At low temperatures, bosons behave differently from [fermions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermion "Fermion") (which obey the [Fermi–Dirac statistics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermi%E2%80%93Dirac_statistics "Fermi–Dirac statistics")) in a way that an unlimited number of them can "condense" into the same energy state. This apparently unusual property also gives rise to the special state of matter – the [Bose–Einstein condensate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bose%E2%80%93Einstein_condensate "Bose–Einstein condensate"). Fermi–Dirac and Bose–Einstein statistics apply when [quantum effects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum "Quantum") are important and the particles are "[indistinguishable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Identical_particles "Identical particles")". Quantum effects appear if the concentration of particles satisfies ![Image 7: {\displaystyle {\frac {N}{V}}\geq n_{\text{q}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dc1c51370d6811200e01289a4514fd27584ca352) where N is the number of particles, V is the volume, and _n_ q is the [quantum concentration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_concentration "Quantum concentration"), for which the interparticle distance is equal to the [thermal de Broglie wavelength](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermal_de_Broglie_wavelength "Thermal de Broglie wavelength"), so that the [wavefunctions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wavefunction "Wavefunction") of the particles are barely overlapping.

Fermi–Dirac statistics applies to fermions (particles that obey the [Pauli exclusion principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pauli_exclusion_principle "Pauli exclusion principle")), and Bose–Einstein statistics applies to [bosons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bosons "Bosons"). As the quantum concentration depends on temperature, most systems at high temperatures obey the classical (Maxwell–Boltzmann) limit, unless they also have a very high density, as for a [white dwarf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_dwarf "White dwarf"). Both Fermi–Dirac and Bose–Einstein become [Maxwell–Boltzmann statistics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxwell%E2%80%93Boltzmann_statistics "Maxwell–Boltzmann statistics") at high temperature or at low concentration.

Bose–Einstein statistics was introduced for [photons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon "Photon") in 1924 by [Bose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satyendra_Nath_Bose "Satyendra Nath Bose") and generalized to atoms by [Einstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Einstein "Albert Einstein") in 1924–25.

In 1900, [Max Planck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Max_Planck "Max Planck") derived the [Planck law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_law "Planck law") to explain [blackbody radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blackbody_radiation "Blackbody radiation"). For this purpose, he introduced the concept of [quanta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum "Quantum") of energy.

[Władysław Natanson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/W%C5%82adys%C5%82aw_Natanson "Władysław Natanson") in 1911 concluded that Planck's law requires indistinguishability of "units of energy", although he did not frame this in terms of Einstein's light quanta.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bose%E2%80%93Einstein_distribution#cite_note-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bose%E2%80%93Einstein_distribution#cite_note-3)

While presenting a lecture at the [University of Dhaka](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Dhaka "University of Dhaka") (in what was then [British India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_India "British India") and is now [Bangladesh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bangladesh "Bangladesh")) on the theory of radiation and the [ultraviolet catastrophe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultraviolet_catastrophe "Ultraviolet catastrophe"), [Satyendra Nath Bose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satyendra_Nath_Bose "Satyendra Nath Bose") intended to show his students that the contemporary theory was inadequate, because it predicted results not in accordance with experimental results. During this lecture, Bose committed an error in applying the theory, which unexpectedly gave a prediction that agreed with the experiment. The error was a simple mistake – similar to arguing that flipping two fair coins will produce two heads one-third of the time – that would appear obviously wrong to anyone with a basic understanding of statistics (remarkably, this error resembled the famous blunder by [Jean le Rond d'Alembert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_le_Rond_d%27Alembert "Jean le Rond d'Alembert") known from his _Croix ou Pile_ article[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bose%E2%80%93Einstein_distribution#cite_note-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bose%E2%80%93Einstein_distribution#cite_note-5)). However, the results it predicted agreed with experiment, and Bose realized it might not be a mistake after all. For the first time, he took the position that the Maxwell–Boltzmann distribution would not be true for all microscopic particles at all scales. Thus, he studied the probability of finding particles in various states in phase space, where each state is a little patch having phase volume of _h_ 3, and the position and momentum of the particles are not kept particularly separate but are considered as one variable.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

Bose adapted this lecture into a short article called "Planck's law and the hypothesis of light quanta"[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bose%E2%80%93Einstein_distribution#cite_note-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bose%E2%80%93Einstein_distribution#cite_note-7) and submitted it to the _[Philosophical Magazine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophical\_Magazine "Philosophical Magazine")_. However, the referee's report was negative, and the paper was rejected. Undaunted, he sent the manuscript to Albert Einstein requesting publication in the _[Zeitschrift für Physik](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zeitschrift\_f%C3%BCr\_Physik "Zeitschrift für Physik")_. Einstein immediately agreed, personally translated the article from English into German (Bose had earlier translated Einstein's article on the general theory of relativity from German to English), and saw to it that it was published. Bose's theory achieved respect when Einstein sent his own paper in support of Bose's to _Zeitschrift für Physik_, asking that they be published together. The paper came out in 1924.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bose%E2%80%93Einstein_distribution#cite_note-8)

The reason Bose produced accurate results was that since photons are indistinguishable from each other, one cannot treat any two photons having equal quantum numbers (e.g., polarization and momentum vector) as being two distinct identifiable photons. Bose originally had a factor of 2 for the possible spin states, but Einstein changed it to polarization.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bose%E2%80%93Einstein_distribution#cite_note-9) By analogy, if in an alternate universe coins were to behave like photons and other bosons, the probability of producing two heads would indeed be one-third, and so is the probability of getting a head and a tail which equals one-half for the conventional (classical, distinguishable) coins. Bose's "error" leads to what is now called Bose–Einstein statistics.

Bose and Einstein extended the idea to atoms and this led to the prediction of the existence of phenomena which became known as Bose–Einstein condensate, a dense collection of bosons (which are particles with integer spin, named after Bose), which was demonstrated to exist by experiment in 1995.

### Derivation from the grand canonical ensemble

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bose%E2%80%93Einstein_statistics&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Derivation from the grand canonical ensemble")]

The Bose–Einstein distribution, which applies only to a quantum system of non-interacting bosons, is naturally derived from the [grand canonical ensemble](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grand_canonical_ensemble "Grand canonical ensemble") without any approximations.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bose%E2%80%93Einstein_distribution#cite_note-sriva7-10) In this ensemble, the system is able to exchange energy and exchange particles with a reservoir (temperature _T_ and chemical potential _μ_ fixed by the reservoir).

Due to the non-interacting quality, each available single-particle level (with energy level _ϵ_) forms a separate thermodynamic system in contact with the reservoir. That is, the number of particles within the overall system _that occupy a given single particle state_ form a sub-ensemble that is also grand canonical ensemble; hence, it may be analysed through the construction of a [grand partition function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grand_partition_function "Grand partition function").

Every single-particle state is of a fixed energy, ![Image 56: {\displaystyle \varepsilon }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a30c89172e5b88edbd45d3e2772c7f5e562e5173). As the sub-ensemble associated with a single-particle state varies by the number of particles only, it is clear that the total energy of the sub-ensemble is also directly proportional to the number of particles in the single-particle state; where ![Image 57: {\displaystyle N}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5e3890c981ae85503089652feb48b191b57aae3) is the number of particles, the total energy of the sub-ensemble will then be ![Image 58: {\displaystyle N\varepsilon }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf6214f4b5ff6f5a30c8fb14bafdb6cb6cc11d4d). Beginning with the standard expression for a grand partition function and replacing ![Image 59: {\displaystyle E}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4232c9de2ee3eec0a9c0a19b15ab92daa6223f9b) with ![Image 60: {\displaystyle N\varepsilon }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf6214f4b5ff6f5a30c8fb14bafdb6cb6cc11d4d), the grand partition function takes the form ![Image 61: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {Z}}=\sum _{N}\exp((N\mu -N\varepsilon )/k_{\text{B}}T)=\sum _{N}\exp(N(\mu -\varepsilon )/k_{\text{B}}T)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e1d7f1c2149dde96dbaedace48033087ba813)

This formula applies to fermionic systems as well as bosonic systems. Fermi–Dirac statistics arises when considering the effect of the [Pauli exclusion principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pauli_exclusion_principle "Pauli exclusion principle"): whilst the number of fermions occupying the same single-particle state can only be either 1 or 0, the number of bosons occupying a single particle state may be any integer. Thus, the grand partition function for bosons can be considered a [geometric series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_series "Geometric series") and may be evaluated as such: ![Image 62: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}{\mathcal {Z}}&=\sum _{N=0}^{\infty }\exp(N(\mu -\varepsilon )/k_{\text{B}}T)=\sum _{N=0}^{\infty }[\exp((\mu -\varepsilon )/k_{\text{B}}T)]^{N}\\&={\frac {1}{1-\exp((\mu -\varepsilon )/k_{\text{B}}T)}}.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f9c6c070b2d4a1ffb8cfa6f4f96b06c3736c9bf7)

Note that the geometric series is convergent only if ![Image 63: {\displaystyle e^{(\mu -\varepsilon )/k_{\text{B}}T}<1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b52ebd1ef6b09c6934a62e45dabf027b582bebd9), including the case where ![Image 64: {\displaystyle \varepsilon =0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0cbe653d369abd71fc0be98efbf59e9edc1bdfb2). This implies that the chemical potential for the Bose gas must be negative, i.e., ![Image 65: {\displaystyle \mu <0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fa905b901e1874757c6f7bb0d1b243022920a965), whereas the Fermi gas is allowed to take both positive and negative values for the chemical potential.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bose%E2%80%93Einstein_distribution#cite_note-11)

The average particle number for that single-particle substate is given by ![Image 66: {\displaystyle \langle N\rangle =k_{\text{B}}T{\frac {1}{\mathcal {Z}}}\left({\frac {\partial {\mathcal {Z}}}{\partial \mu }}\right)_{V,T}={\frac {1}{\exp((\varepsilon -\mu )/k_{\text{B}}T)-1}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/986a6f2cf8289738635a1a99310da11812e27181) This result applies for each single-particle level and thus forms the Bose–Einstein distribution for the entire state of the system.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bose%E2%80%93Einstein_distribution#cite_note-sriva6-12)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bose%E2%80%93Einstein_distribution#cite_note-13)

The [variance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variance "Variance") in particle number, ![Image 67: {\textstyle \sigma _{N}^{2}=\langle N^{2}\rangle -\langle N\rangle ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d566569b5154dde6bc3befd6ec0de90f44d5cb45), is: ![Image 68: {\displaystyle \sigma _{N}^{2}=k_{\text{B}}T\left({\frac {d\langle N\rangle }{d\mu }}\right)_{V,T}={\frac {\exp((\varepsilon -\mu )/k_{\text{B}}T)}{(\exp((\varepsilon -\mu )/k_{\text{B}}T)-1)^{2}}}=\langle N\rangle (1+\langle N\rangle ).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d5d74b0623d1d706e48db96729ffe380c0f272b1)

As a result, for highly occupied states the [standard deviation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_deviation "Standard deviation") of the particle number of an energy level is very large, slightly larger than the particle number itself: ![Image 69: {\displaystyle \sigma _{N}\approx \langle N\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4763ba44babe77e4632fe2eae9a12acd8e2f1157). This large uncertainty is due to the fact that the [probability distribution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability_distribution "Probability distribution") for the number of bosons in a given energy level is a [geometric distribution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_distribution "Geometric distribution"); somewhat counterintuitively, the most probable value for _N_ is always 0. (In contrast, [classical particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxwell%E2%80%93Boltzmann_statistics "Maxwell–Boltzmann statistics") have instead a [Poisson distribution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poisson_distribution "Poisson distribution") in particle number for a given state, with a much smaller uncertainty of ![Image 70: {\textstyle \sigma _{N,{\rm {classical}}}={\sqrt {\langle N\rangle }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d95ab470a4b3df8d32d081c0e92bc1f542039f9e), and with the most-probable _N_ value being near ![Image 71: {\displaystyle \langle N\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/542fff6bf1b6670c3dfc774b9395ea132af7c7b9).)

### Derivation in the canonical approach

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bose%E2%80%93Einstein_statistics&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Derivation in the canonical approach")]

It is also possible to derive approximate Bose–Einstein statistics in the [canonical ensemble](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canonical_ensemble "Canonical ensemble"). These derivations are lengthy and only yield the above results in the asymptotic limit of a large number of particles. The reason is that the total number of bosons is fixed in the canonical ensemble. The Bose–Einstein distribution in this case can be derived as in most texts by maximization, but the mathematically best derivation is by the [Darwin–Fowler method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Darwin%E2%80%93Fowler_method "Darwin–Fowler method") of mean values as emphasized by Dingle.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bose%E2%80%93Einstein_distribution#cite_note-14) See also Müller-Kirsten.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bose%E2%80%93Einstein_distribution#cite_note-:0-15) The fluctuations of the ground state in the condensed region are however markedly different in the canonical and grand-canonical ensembles.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bose%E2%80%93Einstein_distribution#cite_note-ZiffKacUhlenbeck77-16)

Interdisciplinary applications
------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bose%E2%80%93Einstein_statistics&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Interdisciplinary applications")]

Viewed as a pure [probability distribution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability_distribution "Probability distribution"), the Bose–Einstein distribution has found application in other fields:

*   In recent years, Bose–Einstein statistics has also been used as a method for term weighting in [information retrieval](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information_retrieval "Information retrieval"). The method is one of a collection of DFR ("Divergence From Randomness") models,[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bose%E2%80%93Einstein_distribution#cite_note-bia1-18) the basic notion being that Bose–Einstein statistics may be a useful indicator in cases where a particular term and a particular document have a significant relationship that would not have occurred purely by chance. Source code for implementing this model is available from the [Terrier project](http://ir.dcs.gla.ac.uk/terrier/doc/dfr_description.html) at the University of Glasgow.
*   The evolution of many complex systems, including the [World Wide Web](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Wide_Web "World Wide Web"), business, and citation networks, is encoded in the dynamic web describing the interactions between the system's constituents. Despite their irreversible and nonequilibrium nature these networks follow Bose statistics and can undergo Bose–Einstein condensation. Addressing the dynamical properties of these nonequilibrium systems within the framework of equilibrium quantum gases predicts that the "first-mover-advantage", "fit-get-rich" (FGR) and "winner-takes-all" phenomena observed in competitive systems are thermodynamically distinct phases of the underlying evolving networks.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bose%E2%80%93Einstein_distribution#cite_note-bia2-19)
