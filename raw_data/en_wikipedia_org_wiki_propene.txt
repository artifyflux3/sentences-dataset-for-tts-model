Chemical compound

**Propylene**, also known as **propene**, is an [unsaturated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unsaturated_compound "Unsaturated compound")[organic compound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_compound "Organic compound") with the [chemical formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_formula "Chemical formula")CH 3 CH=CH 2. It has one [double bond](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double_bond "Double bond"), and is the second simplest member of the [alkene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkene "Alkene") class of [hydrocarbons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrocarbon "Hydrocarbon"). It is a colorless gas with a faint petroleum-like odor.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propene#cite_note-4)

Propylene is a product of combustion from forest fires, cigarette smoke, and motor vehicle and aircraft exhaust.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propene#cite_note-MDPI-5) It was discovered in 1850 by [A. W. von Hoffmann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/August_Wilhelm_von_Hofmann "August Wilhelm von Hofmann")'s student Captain (later Major General[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propene#cite_note-6)) [John Williams Reynolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Williams_Reynolds "John Williams Reynolds") as the only gaseous product of [thermal decomposition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermal_decomposition "Thermal decomposition") of [amyl alcohol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amyl_alcohol "Amyl alcohol") to react with [chlorine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chlorine "Chlorine") and [bromine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bromine "Bromine").[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propene#cite_note-7)

Production
----------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Propylene&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Production")]

### Steam cracking

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Propylene&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Steam cracking")]

Main article: [Steam cracking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steam_cracking "Steam cracking")

The dominant technology for producing propylene is [steam cracking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steam_cracking "Steam cracking"), using [propane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propane "Propane") as the [feedstock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feedstock "Feedstock"). Cracking propane yields a mixture of [ethylene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene "Ethylene"), propylene, [methane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane "Methane"), [hydrogen gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_gas "Hydrogen gas"), and other related compounds. The yield of propylene is about 15%. The other principal feedstock is [naphtha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naphtha "Naphtha"), especially in the [Middle East](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_East "Middle East") and Asia.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propene#cite_note-8) Propylene can be separated by [fractional distillation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractional_distillation "Fractional distillation") from the hydrocarbon mixtures obtained from cracking and other refining processes; refinery-grade propene is about 50 to 70%.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propene#cite_note-Dow-9) In the United States, [shale gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shale_gas "Shale gas") is a major source of propane.

### Olefin conversion technology

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Propylene&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Olefin conversion technology")]

In the Phillips triolefin or [olefin conversion technology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Olefin_conversion_technology "Olefin conversion technology"), propylene is interconverted with [ethylene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene "Ethylene") and [2-butenes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2-butene "2-butene"). [Rhenium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhenium "Rhenium") and [molybdenum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molybdenum "Molybdenum") catalysts are used:[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propene#cite_note-MG-10)

CH 2=CH 2+CH 3 CH=CHCH 3→Re, Mo catalyst 2 CH 2=CHCH 3{\displaystyle {\ce {CH2=CH2{}+CH3CH=CHCH3->[][{\text{Re, Mo}} \atop {\text{catalyst}}]2CH2=CHCH3}}}![Image 45: {\displaystyle {\mathrm {CH} {\vphantom {A}}_{\smash[{t}]{2}}{=}\mathrm {CH} {\vphantom {A}}_{\smash[{t}]{2}}{}+{}\mathrm {CH} {\vphantom {A}}_{\smash[{t}]{3}}\mathrm {CH} {=}\mathrm {CHCH} {\vphantom {A}}_{\smash[{t}]{3}}{}\mathrel {\xrightarrow[{{\text{Re, Mo}}~ \atop {\text{catalyst}}}]{}} {}2\,\mathrm {CH} {\vphantom {A}}_{\smash[{t}]{2}}{=}\mathrm {CHCH} {\vphantom {A}}_{\smash[{t}]{3}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/acdd4b00da58144bb78872e0663521fe689b07b1)
The technology is founded on an [olefin metathesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Olefin_metathesis "Olefin metathesis") reaction discovered at [Phillips Petroleum Company](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phillips_Petroleum_Company "Phillips Petroleum Company").[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propene#cite_note-11)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propene#cite_note-KO-12) Propylene yields of about 90 wt% are achieved.

Main article: [Syngas to gasoline plus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syngas_to_gasoline_plus "Syngas to gasoline plus")

Related is the [Methanol-to-Olefins/Methanol-to-Propene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas_to_liquids#Methanol_to_gasoline_(MTG)_and_methanol_to_olefins "Gas to liquids") process. It converts [synthesis gas (syngas)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas_to_liquids#Syngas_to_gasoline_plus_process_(STG+) "Gas to liquids") to [methanol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol "Methanol"), and then [converts the methanol to ethylene and/or propene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syngas_to_gasoline_plus "Syngas to gasoline plus"). The process produces water as a by-product. [Synthesis gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synthesis_gas "Synthesis gas") is produced from the reformation of natural gas or by the steam-induced reformation of petroleum products such as naphtha, or by [gasification of coal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coal_gasification "Coal gasification") or natural gas.

### Fluid catalytic cracking

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Propylene&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Fluid catalytic cracking")]

High severity [fluid catalytic cracking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluid_catalytic_cracking "Fluid catalytic cracking") (FCC) uses traditional FCC technology under severe conditions (higher catalyst-to-oil ratios, higher steam injection rates, higher temperatures, etc.) in order to maximize the amount of propene and other light products. A high severity FCC unit is usually fed with gas oils (paraffins) and residues, and produces about 20–25% (by mass) of propene on feedstock together with greater volumes of motor gasoline and distillate byproducts. These high temperature processes are expensive and have a high carbon footprint. For these reasons, alternative routes to propylene continue to attract attention.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propene#cite_note-13)

### Other commercialized methods

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Propylene&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Other commercialized methods")]

On-purpose propylene production technologies were developed throughout the twentieth century. Of these, propane dehydrogenation technologies such as the CATOFIN and OLEFLEX processes have become common, although they still make up a minority of the market, with most of the olefin being sourced from the above mentioned cracking technologies. Platinum, chromia, and vanadium catalysts are common in propane dehydrogenation processes.

### Market

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Propylene&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Market")]

Propene production has remained static at around 35 million [tonnes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tonnes "Tonnes") (Europe and North America only) from 2000 to 2008, but it has been increasing in East Asia, most notably Singapore and China.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propene#cite_note-realityck-14) Total world production of propene is currently about half that of ethylene.

### Research

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Propylene&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Research")]

The use of engineered [enzymes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enzyme "Enzyme") has been explored but has not been commercialized.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propene#cite_note-15)

There is ongoing research into the use of oxygen carrier catalysts for the oxidative dehydrogenation of propane. This poses several advantages, as this reaction mechanism can occur at lower temperatures than conventional dehydrogenation, and may not be equilibrium-limited because oxygen is used to combust the hydrogen by-product.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propene#cite_note-Wu_2020-16)

Uses
----

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Propylene&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Uses")]

Propylene is the second most important starting product in the [petrochemical industry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petrochemical_industry "Petrochemical industry") after [ethylene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene "Ethylene"). It is the raw material for a wide variety of products. [Polypropylene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polypropylene "Polypropylene") manufacturers consume nearly two thirds of global production.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propene#cite_note-Ceresana-17) Polypropylene end uses include films, fibers, containers, packaging, and caps and closures. Propene is also used for the production of chemicals such as [propylene oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propylene_oxide "Propylene oxide"), [acrylonitrile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acrylonitrile "Acrylonitrile"), [cumene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cumene "Cumene"), [butyraldehyde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Butyraldehyde "Butyraldehyde"), and [acrylic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acrylic_acid "Acrylic acid"). In the year 2013 about 85 million tonnes of propylene were processed worldwide.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propene#cite_note-Ceresana-17)

Propylene and [benzene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene "Benzene") are converted to [acetone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone "Acetone") and [phenol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phenol "Phenol") via the [cumene process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cumene_process "Cumene process").

[![Image 46: Overview of the cumene process](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Cumene-process-overview-2D-skeletal.png/500px-Cumene-process-overview-2D-skeletal.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Cumene-process-overview-2D-skeletal.png "Overview of the cumene process")

Overview of the cumene process

Propylene is also used to produce [isopropyl alcohol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isopropyl_alcohol "Isopropyl alcohol") (propan-2-ol), [acrylonitrile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acrylonitrile "Acrylonitrile"), [propylene oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propylene_oxide "Propylene oxide"), and [epichlorohydrin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epichlorohydrin "Epichlorohydrin").[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propene#cite_note-18) The industrial production of [acrylic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acrylic_acid "Acrylic acid") involves the catalytic partial oxidation of propylene.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propene#cite_note-19) Propylene is an intermediate in the oxidation to acrylic acid.

In industry and workshops, propylene is used as an alternative fuel to acetylene in [Oxy-fuel welding and cutting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxy-fuel_welding_and_cutting "Oxy-fuel welding and cutting"), brazing and heating of metal for the purpose of bending. It has become a standard in [BernzOmatic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BernzOmatic "BernzOmatic") products and others in MAPP substitutes,[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propene#cite_note-20) now that true [MAPP gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MAPP_gas "MAPP gas") is no longer available.

Reactions
---------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Propylene&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Reactions")]

Propylene resembles other alkenes in that it undergoes [electrophilic addition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrophilic_addition "Electrophilic addition") reactions relatively easily at room temperature. The relative weakness of its double bond explains its tendency to react with substances that can achieve this transformation. Alkene reactions include:

*   [Polymerization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polymerization "Polymerization") and [oligomerization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oligomerization "Oligomerization")
*   [Oxidation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxidation "Oxidation")
*   [Halogenation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halogenation "Halogenation")
*   [Hydrohalogenation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrohalogenation "Hydrohalogenation")
*   [Alkylation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkylation "Alkylation")
*   [Hydration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydration_reaction "Hydration reaction")
*   [Hydroformylation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroformylation "Hydroformylation")

### Complexes of transition metals

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Propylene&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Complexes of transition metals")]

Foundational to hydroformylation, alkene metathesis, and polymerization are [metal-propylene complexes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metal-alkene_complex "Metal-alkene complex"), which are intermediates in these processes. Propylene is [prochiral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prochiral "Prochiral"), meaning that binding of a reagent (such as a metal electrophile) to the C=C group yields one of two [enantiomers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enantiomer "Enantiomer").

### Polymerization

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Propylene&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Polymerization")]

See also: [Polypropylene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polypropylene "Polypropylene")

The majority of propylene is used to form polypropylene, a very important commodity [thermoplastic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermoplastic "Thermoplastic"), through [chain-growth polymerization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chain-growth_polymerization "Chain-growth polymerization").[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propene#cite_note-Ceresana-17) In the presence of a suitable catalyst (typically a [Ziegler–Natta catalyst](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ziegler%E2%80%93Natta_catalyst "Ziegler–Natta catalyst")), propylene will polymerize. There are multiple ways to achieve this, such as using high pressures to suspending the catalyst in a solution of liquid propylene, or running gaseous propylene through a [fluidized bed reactor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluidized_bed_reactor "Fluidized bed reactor").[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propene#cite_note-:0-21)

[![Image 47](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Polypropyl%C3%A8ne.png/593px-Polypropyl%C3%A8ne.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Polypropyl%C3%A8ne.png)

### Oligomerizationn

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Propylene&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Oligomerizationn")]

In the presence of [catalysts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catalyst "Catalyst"), propylene will form various short [oligomers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oligomer "Oligomer"). It can [dimerizes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimerize "Dimerize") to give [2,3-dimethyl-1-butene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2,3-Dimethyl-1-butene "2,3-Dimethyl-1-butene") and/or [2,3-dimethyl-2-butene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2,3-Dimethyl-2-butene "2,3-Dimethyl-2-butene").[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propene#cite_note-cat-22) or trimerise to form [tripropylene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tripropylene "Tripropylene").

Environmental safety
--------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Propylene&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Environmental safety")]

Propene is a product of combustion from forest fires, cigarette smoke, and motor vehicle and aircraft exhaust.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propene#cite_note-MDPI-5) It is an impurity in some heating gases. Observed concentrations have been in the range of 0.1–4.8 parts per billion ([ppb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parts_per_billion "Parts per billion")) in rural air, 4–10.5 ppb in urban air, and 7–260 ppb in industrial air samples.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propene#cite_note-Dow-9)

In the United States and some European countries a [threshold limit value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Threshold_limit_value "Threshold limit value") of 500 parts per million ([ppm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parts_per_million "Parts per million")) was established for occupational (8-hour [time-weighted average](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permissible_exposure_limit "Permissible exposure limit")) exposure. It is considered a [volatile organic compound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volatile_organic_compound "Volatile organic compound") (VOC) and emissions are regulated by many governments, but it is not listed by the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) as a [hazardous air pollutant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hazardous_air_pollutant "Hazardous air pollutant") under the [Clean Air Act](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clean_Air_Act_(United_States) "Clean Air Act (United States)"). With a relatively short half-life, it is not expected to bioaccumulate.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propene#cite_note-Dow-9)

Propene has low acute toxicity from inhalation and is not considered to be carcinogenic. Chronic toxicity studies in mice did not yield significant evidence suggesting adverse effects. Humans briefly exposed to 4,000 ppm did not experience any noticeable effects.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propene#cite_note-23) Propene is dangerous from its potential to displace oxygen as an [asphyxiant gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asphyxiant_gas "Asphyxiant gas"), and from its high flammability/explosion risk.

**Bio-propylene** is the [bio-based](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drop-in_bioplastic "Drop-in bioplastic") propylene.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propene#cite_note-24)[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propene#cite_note-25) It has been examined, motivated by diverse interests such a [carbon footprint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_footprint "Carbon footprint"). Production from [glucose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glucose "Glucose") has been considered.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propene#cite_note-26) More advanced ways of addressing such issues focus on electrification alternatives to [steam cracking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steam_cracking "Steam cracking").

Storage and handling
--------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Propylene&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Storage and handling")]

Propene is flammable. Propene is usually stored as liquid under pressure, although it is also possible to store it safely as gas at ambient temperature in approved containers.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propene#cite_note-27)

Occurrence in nature
--------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Propylene&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: Occurrence in nature")]

Propene is detected in the [interstellar medium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_medium "Interstellar medium") through microwave spectroscopy.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propene#cite_note-28) On September 30, 2013, [NASA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NASA "NASA") announced the detection of small amounts of naturally occurring propene in the atmosphere of [Titan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Titan_(moon) "Titan (moon)") using infrared spectroscopy.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propene#cite_note-29)[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propene#cite_note-30)[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propene#cite_note-31) The detection was made by a team led by [NASA GSFC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goddard_Space_Flight_Center "Goddard Space Flight Center") scientist [Conor Nixon](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Conor_Nixon&action=edit&redlink=1 "Conor Nixon (page does not exist)") using data from the [CIRS](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Composite_Infrared_Spectrometer_(CIRS)&action=edit&redlink=1 "Composite Infrared Spectrometer (CIRS) (page does not exist)") instrument [[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propene#cite_note-32)[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propene#cite_note-33) on the Cassini orbiter spacecraft, part of the [Cassini-Huygens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cassini-Huygens "Cassini-Huygens") mission. Its confirmation solved a 32-year old mystery by filling a predicted gap in Titan's detected [hydrocarbons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrocarbon "Hydrocarbon"), adding the C 3 H 6 species (propene) to the already-detected C 3 H 4 ([propyne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propyne "Propyne")) and C 3 H 8 ([propane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propane "Propane")).[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propene#cite_note-34)
