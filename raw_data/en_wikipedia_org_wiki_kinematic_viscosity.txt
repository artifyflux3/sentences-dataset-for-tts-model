**Viscosity** is a measure of a [fluid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluid "Fluid")'s rate-dependent [resistance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drag_(physics) "Drag (physics)") to a change in shape or to movement of its neighboring portions relative to one another.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-Britanica-1) For liquids, it corresponds to the informal concept of _thickness_; for example, [syrup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syrup "Syrup") has a higher viscosity than [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water "Water").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-2) Viscosity is defined scientifically as a force multiplied by a time divided by an area. Thus its [SI units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SI_unit "SI unit") are newton-seconds per metre squared, or pascal-seconds.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-Britanica-1)

Viscosity quantifies the internal [frictional force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friction "Friction") between adjacent layers of fluid that are in relative motion.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-Britanica-1) For instance, when a viscous fluid is forced through a tube, it flows more quickly near the tube's center line than near its walls.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-3) Experiments show that some [stress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stress_(physics) "Stress (physics)") (such as a [pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pressure "Pressure") difference between the two ends of the tube) is needed to sustain the flow. This is because a force is required to overcome the friction between the layers of the fluid which are in [relative motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relative_motion "Relative motion"). For a tube with a constant rate of flow, the strength of the compensating force is proportional to the fluid's viscosity.

In general, viscosity depends on a fluid's state, such as its temperature, pressure, and rate of deformation. However, the dependence on some of these properties is negligible in certain cases. For example, the viscosity of a [Newtonian fluid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newtonian_fluid "Newtonian fluid") does not vary significantly with the rate of deformation.

Zero viscosity (no resistance to [shear stress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shear_stress "Shear stress")) is observed only at [very low temperatures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cryogenics "Cryogenics") in [superfluids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superfluidity "Superfluidity"); otherwise, the [second law of thermodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_law_of_thermodynamics "Second law of thermodynamics") requires all fluids to have positive viscosity.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBalescu1975428%E2%80%93429-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTELandauLifshitz1987-5) A fluid that has zero viscosity (non-viscous) is called _ideal_ or _inviscid_.

For [non-Newtonian fluids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-Newtonian_fluid "Non-Newtonian fluid")' viscosity, there are [pseudoplastic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudoplastic "Pseudoplastic"), [plastic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plastic_flow "Plastic flow"), and [dilatant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dilatant "Dilatant") flows that are time-independent, and there are [thixotropic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thixotropic "Thixotropic") and [rheopectic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rheopectic "Rheopectic") flows that are time-dependent.

The word "viscosity" is derived from the [Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin "Latin")_\_viscum\__ ("[mistletoe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mistletoe "Mistletoe")"). _\_Viscum\__ also referred to a viscous [glue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glue "Glue") derived from mistletoe berries.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-etym_visc-6)

Illustration of a planar [Couette flow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Couette_flow "Couette flow"). Since the shearing flow is opposed by friction between adjacent layers of fluid (which are in relative motion), a force is required to sustain the motion of the upper plate. The relative strength of this force is a measure of the fluid's viscosity.

[![Image 4](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Laminar_shear_flow.svg/250px-Laminar_shear_flow.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Laminar_shear_flow.svg)

In a general parallel flow, the shear stress is proportional to the gradient of the velocity.

In [materials science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Materials_science "Materials science") and [engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineering "Engineering"), there is often interest in understanding the forces or [stresses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stress_(mechanics) "Stress (mechanics)") involved in the [deformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deformation_(mechanics) "Deformation (mechanics)") of a material. For instance, if the material were a simple spring, the answer would be given by [Hooke's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hooke%27s_law "Hooke's law"), which says that the force experienced by a spring is proportional to the distance displaced from equilibrium. Stresses which can be attributed to the deformation of a material from some rest state are called [elastic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elasticity_(physics) "Elasticity (physics)") stresses. In other materials, stresses are present which can be attributed to the [deformation rate over time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strain_rate "Strain rate"). These are called viscous stresses. For instance, in a fluid such as water the stresses which arise from shearing the fluid do not depend on the _distance_ the fluid has been sheared; rather, they depend on how _quickly_ the shearing occurs.

Viscosity is the material property which relates the viscous stresses in a material to the rate of change of a deformation (the strain rate). Although it applies to general flows, it is easy to visualize and define in a simple shearing flow, such as a planar [Couette flow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Couette_flow "Couette flow").

In the Couette flow, a fluid is trapped between two infinitely large plates, one fixed and one in parallel motion at constant speed ![Image 5: {\displaystyle u}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3e6bb763d22c20916ed4f0bb6bd49d7470cffd8) (see illustration to the right). If the speed of the top plate is low enough (to avoid turbulence), then in steady state the fluid particles move parallel to it, and their speed varies from ![Image 6: {\displaystyle 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2aae8864a3c1fec9585261791a809ddec1489950) at the bottom to ![Image 7: {\displaystyle u}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3e6bb763d22c20916ed4f0bb6bd49d7470cffd8) at the top.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMewisWagner201219-7) Each layer of fluid moves faster than the one just below it, and friction between them gives rise to a [force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Force_(physics) "Force (physics)") resisting their relative motion. In particular, the fluid applies on the top plate a force in the direction opposite to its motion, and an equal but opposite force on the bottom plate. An external force is therefore required in order to keep the top plate moving at constant speed.

In many fluids, the flow velocity is observed to vary linearly from zero at the bottom to ![Image 8: {\displaystyle u}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3e6bb763d22c20916ed4f0bb6bd49d7470cffd8) at the top. Moreover, the magnitude of the force, ![Image 9: {\displaystyle F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/545fd099af8541605f7ee55f08225526be88ce57), acting on the top plate is found to be proportional to the speed ![Image 10: {\displaystyle u}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3e6bb763d22c20916ed4f0bb6bd49d7470cffd8) and the area ![Image 11: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3) of each plate, and inversely proportional to their separation ![Image 12: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d):

![Image 13: {\displaystyle F=\mu A{\frac {u}{y}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/413ef0745d061f367dd3cb768a833a47c5e5bae4)
The proportionality factor is the _dynamic viscosity_ of the fluid, often simply referred to as the _viscosity_. It is denoted by the [Greek letter mu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mu_(letter) "Mu (letter)") (μ). The dynamic viscosity has the [dimensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimensional_analysis "Dimensional analysis")![Image 14: {\displaystyle \mathrm {(mass/length)/time} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1643916c6922137cf5d421fa9036a112e3752fc5), therefore resulting in the [SI units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Units "International System of Units") and the [derived units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SI_derived_unit "SI derived unit"):

![Image 15: {\displaystyle [\mu ]={\frac {\rm {kg}}{\rm {m{\cdot }s}}}={\frac {\rm {N}}{\rm {m^{2}}}}{\cdot }{\rm {s}}={\rm {Pa{\cdot }s}}=}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a0505c4de127e4d762cc7174f9e205606cbef004)[pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pressure "Pressure") multiplied by [time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time "Time")![Image 16: {\displaystyle =}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/505a4ceef454c69dffd23792c84b90f488543743) energy per unit volume multiplied by time.
The aforementioned ratio ![Image 17: {\displaystyle u/y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/43abd4cf5e9cc7266564a98c97d9437dd2d6d89e) is called the _rate of shear deformation_ or _[shear velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shear\_velocity "Shear velocity")_, and is the [derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative "Derivative") of the fluid speed in the direction [parallel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_(geometry) "Parallel (geometry)") to the normal vector of the plates (see illustrations to the right). If the velocity does not vary linearly with ![Image 18: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d), then the appropriate generalization is:

![Image 19: {\displaystyle \tau =\mu {\frac {\partial u}{\partial y}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/36db791abdbffb5b7822b4d2ecda9706c70f4777)
where ![Image 20: {\displaystyle \tau =F/A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/22f373a47fc8548cf14fe51a206b2056c520c0a6), and ![Image 21: {\displaystyle \partial u/\partial y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b90ebe5203a48f9eb132e7136331dd1155c1e4d7) is the local shear velocity. This expression is referred to as [Newton's law of viscosity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_law_of_viscosity "Newton's law of viscosity"). In shearing flows with planar symmetry, it is what _defines_![Image 22: {\displaystyle \mu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9fd47b2a39f7a7856952afec1f1db72c67af6161). It is a special case of the general definition of viscosity (see below), which can be expressed in coordinate-free form.

Use of the [Greek letter mu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mu_(letter) "Mu (letter)") (![Image 23: {\displaystyle \mu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9fd47b2a39f7a7856952afec1f1db72c67af6161)) for the dynamic viscosity (sometimes also called the _absolute viscosity_) is common among [mechanical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanical_engineering "Mechanical engineering") and [chemical engineers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_engineer "Chemical engineer"), as well as mathematicians and physicists.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStreeterWylieBedford1998-8)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHolman2002-9)[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIncroperaDeWittBergmanLavine2007-10) However, the [Greek letter eta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eta_(letter) "Eta (letter)") (![Image 24: {\displaystyle \eta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e4d701857cf5fbec133eebaf94deadf722537f64)) is also used by chemists, physicists, and the [IUPAC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IUPAC "IUPAC").[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTENi%C4%8DJir%C3%A1tKo%C5%A1ataJenkins1997-11) The viscosity ![Image 25: {\displaystyle \mu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9fd47b2a39f7a7856952afec1f1db72c67af6161) is sometimes also called the _shear viscosity_. However, at least one author discourages the use of this terminology, noting that ![Image 26: {\displaystyle \mu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9fd47b2a39f7a7856952afec1f1db72c67af6161) can appear in non-shearing flows in addition to shearing flows.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBirdStewartLightfoot200719-12)

### Kinematic viscosity

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Viscosity&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Kinematic viscosity")]

In fluid dynamics, it is sometimes more appropriate to work in terms of _kinematic viscosity_ (sometimes also called the _momentum diffusivity_), defined as the ratio of the dynamic viscosity (μ) over the [density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Density "Density") of the fluid (ρ). It is usually denoted by the [Greek letter nu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nu_(letter) "Nu (letter)") (ν):

![Image 27: {\displaystyle \nu ={\frac {\mu }{\rho }},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9360cb86665dfec20c9b49252d65e6f2c210a3d1)
and has the [dimensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimensional_analysis "Dimensional analysis")![Image 28: {\displaystyle \mathrm {(length)^{2}/time} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/84464328f5df854f423ac3e64d08472e06160c45), therefore resulting in the [SI units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Units "International System of Units") and the [derived units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SI_derived_unit "SI derived unit"):

![Image 29: {\displaystyle [\nu ]=\mathrm {\frac {m^{2}}{s}} =\mathrm {{\frac {N{\cdot }m}{kg}}{\cdot }s} =\mathrm {{\frac {J}{kg}}{\cdot }s} =}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/abefedddb99ec0896354cfcfcdffd26d00903265)[specific energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Specific_energy "Specific energy") multiplied by [time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time "Time")![Image 30: {\displaystyle =}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/505a4ceef454c69dffd23792c84b90f488543743) energy per unit mass multiplied by time.
In very general terms, the viscous stresses in a fluid are defined as those resulting from the relative velocity of different fluid particles. As such, the viscous stresses must depend on spatial gradients of the flow velocity. If the velocity gradients are small, then to a first approximation the viscous stresses depend only on the first derivatives of the velocity.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTELandauLifshitz198744%E2%80%9345-13) (For Newtonian fluids, this is also a linear dependence.) In Cartesian coordinates, the general relationship can then be written as

![Image 31: {\displaystyle \tau _{ij}=\sum _{k}\sum _{\ell }\mu _{ijk\ell }{\frac {\partial v_{k}}{\partial r_{\ell }}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/225b6f2a22322d9c692d855546851a6250ff33df)
where ![Image 32: {\displaystyle \mu _{ijk\ell }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3a3252a506287883a348729441bcd21cafe3010f) is a viscosity tensor that maps the [velocity gradient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity_gradient "Velocity gradient") tensor ![Image 33: {\displaystyle \partial v_{k}/\partial r_{\ell }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d0ec861059d90d942d8d26b2ebf79f4ef7f0df23) onto the viscous stress tensor ![Image 34: {\displaystyle \tau _{ij}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/97d0190e7517254a655e8033badbc01c8590381c).[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBirdStewartLightfoot200718-14) Since the indices in this expression can vary from 1 to 3, there are 81 "viscosity coefficients" ![Image 35: {\displaystyle \mu _{ijkl}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0ccc7586bc977f4d32cd07ebe15e44c6bd85a983) in total. However, assuming that the viscosity rank-2 tensor is [isotropic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotropic "Isotropic") reduces these 81 coefficients to three independent parameters ![Image 36: {\displaystyle \alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b79333175c8b3f0840bfb4ec41b8072c83ea88d3), ![Image 37: {\displaystyle \beta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7ed48a5e36207156fb792fa79d29925d2f7901e8), ![Image 38: {\displaystyle \gamma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a223c880b0ce3da8f64ee33c4f0010beee400b1a):

![Image 39: {\displaystyle \mu _{ijk\ell }=\alpha \delta _{ij}\delta _{k\ell }+\beta \delta _{ik}\delta _{j\ell }+\gamma \delta _{i\ell }\delta _{jk},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4f572060e79931c80f6ca66f4a2a04d49ef4c784)
and furthermore, it is assumed that no viscous forces may arise when the fluid is undergoing simple rigid-body rotation, thus ![Image 40: {\displaystyle \beta =\gamma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b82201fad2d4f0a3b374681cf6963f7b85cd3687), leaving only two independent parameters.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTELandauLifshitz198744%E2%80%9345-13) The most usual decomposition is in terms of the standard (scalar) viscosity ![Image 41: {\displaystyle \mu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9fd47b2a39f7a7856952afec1f1db72c67af6161) and the [bulk viscosity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bulk_viscosity "Bulk viscosity")![Image 42: {\displaystyle \kappa }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54ddec2e922c5caea4e47d04feef86e782dc8e6d) such that ![Image 43: {\displaystyle \alpha =\kappa -{\tfrac {2}{3}}\mu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e2c110353da78f2f1aa9c1bbedcffd9cdf5a6acf) and ![Image 44: {\displaystyle \beta =\gamma =\mu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/27e8190aa7e1ca1496ba6f5b54f1303e0f536144). In vector notation this appears as:

![Image 45: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {\tau }}=\mu \left[\nabla \mathbf {v} +(\nabla \mathbf {v} )^{\mathrm {T} }\right]-\left({\frac {2}{3}}\mu -\kappa \right)(\nabla \cdot \mathbf {v} )\mathbf {\delta } ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c881ede5c0e043dbe36b7b5a30b4c6bf92204e5a)
where ![Image 46: {\displaystyle \mathbf {\delta } }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/32cabc0eaa140779f6e02929b1d7e7e3537e88b8) is the unit tensor.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBirdStewartLightfoot200719-12)[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTELandauLifshitz198745-15) This equation can be thought of as a generalized form of Newton's law of viscosity.

The bulk viscosity (also called volume viscosity) expresses a type of internal friction that resists the shearless compression or expansion of a fluid. Knowledge of ![Image 47: {\displaystyle \kappa }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54ddec2e922c5caea4e47d04feef86e782dc8e6d) is frequently not necessary in fluid dynamics problems. For example, an incompressible fluid satisfies ![Image 48: {\displaystyle \nabla \cdot \mathbf {v} =0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/760025de0202db29f86a1c2c4bef18d54ccc0f64) and so the term containing ![Image 49: {\displaystyle \kappa }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54ddec2e922c5caea4e47d04feef86e782dc8e6d) drops out. Moreover, ![Image 50: {\displaystyle \kappa }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54ddec2e922c5caea4e47d04feef86e782dc8e6d) is often assumed to be negligible for gases since it is ![Image 51: {\displaystyle 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2aae8864a3c1fec9585261791a809ddec1489950) in a [monatomic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monatomic "Monatomic")[ideal gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ideal_gas "Ideal gas").[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBirdStewartLightfoot200719-12) One situation in which ![Image 52: {\displaystyle \kappa }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54ddec2e922c5caea4e47d04feef86e782dc8e6d) can be important is the calculation of energy loss in [sound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sound "Sound") and [shock waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shock_wave "Shock wave"), described by [Stokes' law of sound attenuation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stokes%27_law_(sound_attenuation) "Stokes' law (sound attenuation)"), since these phenomena involve rapid expansions and compressions.

The defining equations for viscosity are not fundamental laws of nature, so their usefulness, as well as methods for measuring or calculating the viscosity, must be established using separate means. A potential issue is that viscosity depends, in principle, on the full microscopic state of the fluid, which encompasses the positions and momenta of every particle in the system.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBalescu1975-16) Such highly detailed information is typically not available in realistic systems. However, under certain conditions most of this information can be shown to be negligible. In particular, for Newtonian fluids near equilibrium and far from boundaries (bulk state), the viscosity depends only space- and time-dependent macroscopic fields (such as temperature and density) defining local equilibrium.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBalescu1975-16)[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChapmanCowling1970-17)

Nevertheless, viscosity may still carry a non-negligible dependence on several system properties, such as temperature, pressure, and the amplitude and frequency of any external forcing. Therefore, precision measurements of viscosity are only defined with respect to a specific fluid state.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMillat1996-18) To standardize comparisons among experiments and theoretical models, viscosity data is sometimes extrapolated to ideal limiting cases, such as the _zero shear_ limit, or (for gases) the _zero density_ limit.

Transport theory provides an alternative interpretation of viscosity in terms of momentum transport: viscosity is the material property which characterizes momentum transport within a fluid, just as [thermal conductivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermal_conductivity "Thermal conductivity") characterizes [heat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat "Heat") transport, and (mass) [diffusivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass_diffusivity "Mass diffusivity") characterizes mass transport.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBirdStewartLightfoot2007-19) This perspective is implicit in Newton's law of viscosity, ![Image 53: {\displaystyle \tau =\mu (\partial u/\partial y)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/477b9bd2cefa37576eb367b39b8cab4365f4f525), because the shear stress ![Image 54: {\displaystyle \tau }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38a7dcde9730ef0853809fefc18d88771f95206c) has units equivalent to a momentum [flux](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flux "Flux"), i.e., momentum per unit time per unit area. Thus, ![Image 55: {\displaystyle \tau }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38a7dcde9730ef0853809fefc18d88771f95206c) can be interpreted as specifying the flow of momentum in the ![Image 56: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d) direction from one fluid layer to the next. Per Newton's law of viscosity, this momentum flow occurs across a velocity gradient, and the magnitude of the corresponding momentum flux is determined by the viscosity.

The analogy with heat and mass transfer can be made explicit. Just as heat flows from high temperature to low temperature and mass flows from high density to low density, momentum flows from high velocity to low velocity. These behaviors are all described by compact expressions, called [constitutive relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitutive_relations "Constitutive relations"), whose one-dimensional forms are given here:

![Image 57: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\mathbf {J} &=-D{\frac {\partial \rho }{\partial x}}&&{\text{(Fick's law of diffusion)}}\\[5pt]\mathbf {q} &=-k_{t}{\frac {\partial T}{\partial x}}&&{\text{(Fourier's law of heat conduction)}}\\[5pt]\tau &=\mu {\frac {\partial u}{\partial y}}&&{\text{(Newton's law of viscosity)}}\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6380b89b0d24d9c9deb9ef04f333430b073c45cc)
where ![Image 58: {\displaystyle \rho }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1f7d439671d1289b6a816e6af7a304be40608d64) is the density, ![Image 59: {\displaystyle \mathbf {J} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7686846b1a6b756cb514954000004ab5e7b2a5ba) and ![Image 60: {\displaystyle \mathbf {q} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7be005a326b7ac3fe4c24bca391369f44c4c2876) are the mass and heat fluxes, and ![Image 61: {\displaystyle D}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f34a0c600395e5d4345287e21fb26efd386990e6) and ![Image 62: {\displaystyle k_{t}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/352386366dcb1dcc184d82669513975561f2d2ae) are the mass diffusivity and thermal conductivity.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchroeder1999-20) The fact that mass, momentum, and energy (heat) transport are among the most relevant processes in continuum mechanics is not a coincidence: these are among the few physical quantities that are conserved at the microscopic level in interparticle collisions. Thus, rather than being dictated by the fast and complex microscopic interaction timescale, their dynamics occurs on macroscopic timescales, as described by the various equations of transport theory and hydrodynamics.

Newtonian and non-Newtonian fluids
----------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Viscosity&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Newtonian and non-Newtonian fluids")]

[![Image 63](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Viscous_regimes_chart.png/330px-Viscous_regimes_chart.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Viscous_regimes_chart.png)

Viscosity, the slope of each line, varies among materials.

Newton's law of viscosity is not a fundamental law of nature, but rather a [constitutive equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitutive_equation "Constitutive equation") (like [Hooke's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hooke%27s_law "Hooke's law"), [Fick's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fick%27s_law "Fick's law"), and [Ohm's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ohm%27s_law "Ohm's law")) which serves to define the viscosity ![Image 64: {\displaystyle \mu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9fd47b2a39f7a7856952afec1f1db72c67af6161). Its form is motivated by experiments which show that for a wide range of fluids, ![Image 65: {\displaystyle \mu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9fd47b2a39f7a7856952afec1f1db72c67af6161) is independent of strain rate. Such fluids are called [Newtonian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newtonian_fluid "Newtonian fluid"). [Gases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas "Gas"), [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water "Water"), and many common liquids can be considered Newtonian in ordinary conditions and contexts. However, there are many [non-Newtonian fluids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-Newtonian_fluid "Non-Newtonian fluid") that significantly deviate from this behavior. For example:

*   [Shear-thickening](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shear_thickening "Shear thickening") (dilatant) liquids, whose viscosity increases with the rate of shear strain.
*   [Shear-thinning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shear_thinning "Shear thinning") liquids, whose viscosity decreases with the rate of shear strain.
*   [Thixotropic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thixotropic "Thixotropic") liquids, that become less viscous over time when shaken, agitated, or otherwise stressed.
*   [Rheopectic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rheopectic "Rheopectic") liquids, that become more viscous over time when shaken, agitated, or otherwise stressed.
*   [Bingham plastics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bingham_plastic "Bingham plastic") that behave as a solid at low stresses but flow as a viscous fluid at high stresses.

[Trouton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frederick_Thomas_Trouton "Frederick Thomas Trouton")'s ratio is the ratio of [extensional viscosity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extensional_viscosity "Extensional viscosity") to [shear viscosity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shear_viscosity "Shear viscosity"). For a Newtonian fluid, the Trouton ratio is 3.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTER%C3%B3%C5%BCa%C5%84skaR%C3%B3%C5%BCa%C5%84skiOchowiakMitkowski201447%E2%80%9355-21)[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTETrouton1906426%E2%80%93440-22) Shear-thinning liquids are very commonly, but misleadingly, described as thixotropic.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMewisWagner2012228%E2%80%93230-23)

Viscosity may also depend on the fluid's physical state (temperature and pressure) and other, _external_, factors. For gases and other [compressible fluids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compressible_fluid "Compressible fluid"), it depends on temperature and varies very slowly with pressure. The viscosity of some fluids may depend on other factors. A [magnetorheological fluid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetorheological_fluid "Magnetorheological fluid"), for example, becomes thicker when subjected to a [magnetic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_field "Magnetic field"), possibly to the point of behaving like a solid.

The viscous forces that arise during fluid flow are distinct from the [elastic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elasticity_(physics) "Elasticity (physics)") forces that occur in a solid in response to shear, compression, or extension stresses. While in the latter the stress is proportional to the _amount_ of shear deformation, in a fluid it is proportional to the _rate_ of deformation over time. For this reason, [James Clerk Maxwell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Clerk_Maxwell "James Clerk Maxwell") used the term _fugitive elasticity_ for fluid viscosity.

However, many liquids (including water) will briefly react like elastic solids when subjected to sudden stress. Conversely, many "solids" (even [granite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Granite "Granite")) will flow like liquids, albeit very slowly, even under arbitrarily small stress.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKumagaiSasajimaIto1978157%E2%80%93161-24) Such materials are best described as [viscoelastic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viscoelasticity "Viscoelasticity")—that is, possessing both elasticity (reaction to deformation) and viscosity (reaction to rate of deformation).

Viscoelastic solids may exhibit both shear viscosity and bulk viscosity. The [extensional viscosity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extensional_viscosity "Extensional viscosity") is a [linear combination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_combination "Linear combination") of the shear and bulk viscosities that describes the reaction of a solid elastic material to elongation. It is widely used for characterizing polymers.

In [geology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geology "Geology"), earth materials that exhibit viscous deformation at least three [orders of magnitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Order_of_magnitude "Order of magnitude") greater than their elastic deformation are sometimes called [rheids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rheid "Rheid").[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchererPardenekSwiatek198814-25)

"Rhe" redirects here. For other uses, see [RHE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RHE_(disambiguation) "RHE (disambiguation)").

Viscosity is measured with various types of [viscometers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viscometer "Viscometer") and [rheometers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rheometer "Rheometer"). Close temperature control of the fluid is essential to obtain accurate measurements, particularly in materials like lubricants, whose viscosity can double with a change of only 5°C. A rheometer is used for fluids that cannot be defined by a single value of viscosity and therefore require more parameters to be set and measured than is the case for a viscometer.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHannan2007-26)

For some fluids, the viscosity is constant over a wide range of shear rates ([Newtonian fluids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newtonian_fluids "Newtonian fluids")). The fluids without a constant viscosity ([non-Newtonian fluids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-Newtonian_fluid "Non-Newtonian fluid")) cannot be described by a single number. Non-Newtonian fluids exhibit a variety of different correlations between shear stress and shear rate.

One of the most common instruments for measuring kinematic viscosity is the glass capillary viscometer.

In [coating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coating "Coating") industries, viscosity may be measured with a cup in which the [efflux time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Efflux_time "Efflux time") is measured. There are several sorts of cup—such as the [Zahn cup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zahn_cup "Zahn cup") and the [Ford viscosity cup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ford_viscosity_cup "Ford viscosity cup")—with the usage of each type varying mainly according to the industry.

Also used in coatings, a _Stormer viscometer_ employs load-based rotation to determine viscosity. The viscosity is reported in Krebs units (KU), which are unique to Stormer viscometers.

Vibrating viscometers can also be used to measure viscosity. Resonant, or vibrational viscometers work by creating shear waves within the liquid. In this method, the sensor is submerged in the fluid and is made to resonate at a specific frequency. As the surface of the sensor shears through the liquid, energy is lost due to its viscosity. This dissipated energy is then measured and converted into a viscosity reading. A higher viscosity causes a greater loss of energy.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

_Extensional viscosity_ can be measured with various [rheometers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rheometer "Rheometer") that apply [extensional stress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extensional_stress "Extensional stress").

[Volume viscosity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume_viscosity "Volume viscosity") can be measured with an [acoustic rheometer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acoustic_rheometer "Acoustic rheometer").

[Apparent viscosity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apparent_viscosity "Apparent viscosity") is a calculation derived from tests performed on [drilling fluid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drilling_fluid "Drilling fluid") used in oil or gas well development. These calculations and tests help engineers develop and maintain the properties of the drilling fluid to the specifications required.

Nanoviscosity (viscosity sensed by nanoprobes) can be measured by [fluorescence correlation spectroscopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluorescence_correlation_spectroscopy "Fluorescence correlation spectroscopy").[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKwapiszewskaSzczepa%C5%84skiKalwarczykMichalska2020-27)

The [SI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Units "International System of Units") unit of dynamic viscosity is the [newton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton_(unit) "Newton (unit)")-second per metre squared (N·s/m 2), also frequently expressed in the equivalent forms [pascal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pascal_(unit) "Pascal (unit)")-[second](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second "Second") (Pa·s), [kilogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kilogram "Kilogram") per meter per second (kg·m−1·s−1) and [poiseuille](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poiseuille_(unit) "Poiseuille (unit)") (Pl). The [CGS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CGS_system "CGS system") unit is the [poise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poise_(unit) "Poise (unit)") (P, or g·cm−1·s−1 = 0.1 Pa·s),[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcNaughtWilkinson1997poise-28) named after [Jean Léonard Marie Poiseuille](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_L%C3%A9onard_Marie_Poiseuille "Jean Léonard Marie Poiseuille"). It is commonly expressed, particularly in [ASTM](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ASTM "ASTM") standards, as _centipoise_ (cP). The centipoise is convenient because the viscosity of water at 20°C is about 1 cP, and one centipoise is equal to the SI millipascal second (mPa·s).

The SI unit of kinematic viscosity is metre squared per second (m 2/s), whereas the CGS unit for kinematic viscosity is the **stokes** (St, or cm 2·s−1 = 0.0001 m 2·s−1), named after Sir [George Gabriel Stokes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_Gabriel_Stokes "George Gabriel Stokes").[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGyllenbok2018213-29) In U.S. usage, _stoke_ is sometimes used as the singular form. The [submultiple](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Submultiple "Submultiple")_centistokes_ (cSt) is often used instead, 1 cSt= 1 mm 2·s−1= 10−6 m 2·s−1. 1 cSt is 1 cP divided by 1000 kg/m^3, close to the density of water. The kinematic viscosity of water at 20°C is about 1 cSt.

The most frequently used systems of [US customary, or Imperial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imperial_and_US_customary_measurement_systems "Imperial and US customary measurement systems"), units are the [British Gravitational](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foot-pound-second_system "Foot-pound-second system") (BG) and [English Engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English_Engineering_units "English Engineering units") (EE). In the BG system, dynamic viscosity has units of [_pound_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pound_(force) "Pound (force)")-seconds per square [foot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foot "Foot") (lb·s/ft 2), and in the EE system it has units of [_pound-force_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pound_(force) "Pound (force)")-seconds per square foot (lbf·s/ft 2). The pound and pound-force are equivalent; the two systems differ only in how force and mass are defined. In the BG system the pound is a basic unit from which the unit of mass (the [slug](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slug_(unit) "Slug (unit)")) is defined by [Newton's second law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_second_law "Newton's second law"), whereas in the EE system the units of force and mass (the pound-force and [pound-mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pound_(mass) "Pound (mass)") respectively) are defined independently through the second law using the [proportionality constant _g c_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gc_(engineering) "Gc (engineering)").

Kinematic viscosity has units of square feet per second (ft 2/s) in both the BG and EE systems.

Nonstandard units include the [reyn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reyn "Reyn") (lbf·s/in 2), a British unit of dynamic viscosity.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-30) In the automotive industry the [viscosity index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viscosity_index "Viscosity index") is used to describe the change of viscosity with temperature.

The [reciprocal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplicative_inverse "Multiplicative inverse") of viscosity is _fluidity_, usually symbolized by ![Image 66: {\displaystyle \phi =1/\mu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6c07421ec9c5c44bc833e20f6dd55b54f18a8d45) or ![Image 67: {\displaystyle F=1/\mu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2ca1bd8b8dfd6d80de780c2d3b005aa70a507128), depending on the convention used, measured in _reciprocal poise_ (P−1, or [cm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centimetre "Centimetre")·[s](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second "Second")·[g](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gram "Gram")−1), sometimes called the _rhe_. Fluidity is seldom used in [engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineering "Engineering") practice.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

At one time the petroleum industry relied on measuring kinematic viscosity by means of the [Saybolt viscometer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saybolt_viscometer "Saybolt viscometer"), and expressing kinematic viscosity in units of [Saybolt universal seconds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saybolt_universal_second "Saybolt universal second") (SUS).[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-ASTM_D2161-31) Other abbreviations such as SSU (_Saybolt seconds universal_) or SUV (_Saybolt universal viscosity_) are sometimes used. Kinematic viscosity in centistokes can be converted from SUS according to the arithmetic and the reference table provided in [ASTM](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ASTM "ASTM") D 2161.

Momentum transport in gases is mediated by discrete molecular collisions, and in liquids by attractive forces that bind molecules close together.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBirdStewartLightfoot2007-19) Because of this, the dynamic viscosities of liquids are typically much larger than those of gases. In addition, viscosity tends to increase with temperature in gases and decrease with temperature in liquids.

Above the liquid-gas [critical point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Critical_point_(thermodynamics) "Critical point (thermodynamics)"), the liquid and gas phases are replaced by a single [supercritical phase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supercritical_fluid "Supercritical fluid"). In this regime, the mechanisms of momentum transport interpolate between liquid-like and gas-like behavior. For example, along a supercritical [isobar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isobaric_process "Isobaric process") (constant-pressure surface), the kinematic viscosity decreases at low temperature and increases at high temperature, with a minimum in between.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTETrachenkoBrazhkin2020-32)[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTETrachenkoBrazhkin2021-33) A rough estimate for the value at the minimum is

where ![Image 69: {\displaystyle \hbar }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/de68de3a92517953436c93b5a76461d49160cc41) is the [Planck constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant "Planck constant"), ![Image 70: {\displaystyle m_{\text{e}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c45a5eb082ea4dafd3cb43ca39e033989e4a52eb) is the [electron mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_mass "Electron mass"), and ![Image 71: {\displaystyle m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a07d98bb302f3856cbabc47b2b9016692e3f7bc) is the molecular mass.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTETrachenkoBrazhkin2021-33)

In general, however, the viscosity of a system depends in detail on how the molecules constituting the system interact, and there are no simple but correct formulas for it. The simplest exact expressions are the [Green–Kubo relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green%E2%80%93Kubo_relations "Green–Kubo relations") for the linear shear viscosity or the _transient time correlation function_ expressions derived by Evans and Morriss in 1988.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEvansMorriss1988-34) Although these expressions are each exact, calculating the viscosity of a dense fluid using these relations currently requires the use of [molecular dynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_dynamics "Molecular dynamics") computer simulations. Somewhat more progress can be made for a dilute gas, as elementary assumptions about how gas molecules move and interact lead to a basic understanding of the molecular origins of viscosity. More sophisticated treatments can be constructed by systematically coarse-graining the [equations of motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equations_of_motion "Equations of motion") of the gas molecules. An example of such a treatment is [Chapman–Enskog theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chapman%E2%80%93Enskog_theory "Chapman–Enskog theory"), which derives expressions for the viscosity of a dilute gas from the [Boltzmann equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boltzmann_equation "Boltzmann equation").[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChapmanCowling1970-17)

In this case ![Image 86: {\displaystyle \lambda }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b43d0ea3c9c025af1be9128e62a18fa74bedda2a) is independent of temperature, so ![Image 87: {\displaystyle \mu \propto T^{1/2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf4eec5fd44de3461bf9ab6c72a545de66dcd351). For more complicated molecular models, however, ![Image 88: {\displaystyle \lambda }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b43d0ea3c9c025af1be9128e62a18fa74bedda2a) depends on temperature in a non-trivial way, and simple kinetic arguments as used here are inadequate. More fundamentally, the notion of a mean free path becomes imprecise for particles that interact over a finite range, which limits the usefulness of the concept for describing real-world gases.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChapmanCowling1970103-36)

#### Chapman–Enskog theory

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Viscosity&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Chapman–Enskog theory")]

A technique developed by [Sydney Chapman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sydney_Chapman_(mathematician) "Sydney Chapman (mathematician)") and [David Enskog](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Enskog "David Enskog") in the early 1900s allows a more refined calculation of ![Image 89: {\displaystyle \mu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9fd47b2a39f7a7856952afec1f1db72c67af6161).[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChapmanCowling1970-17) It is based on the [Boltzmann equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boltzmann_equation "Boltzmann equation"), which provides a statistical description of a dilute gas in terms of intermolecular interactions.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTECercignani1975-37) The technique allows accurate calculation of ![Image 90: {\displaystyle \mu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9fd47b2a39f7a7856952afec1f1db72c67af6161) for molecular models that are more realistic than rigid elastic spheres, such as those incorporating intermolecular attractions. Doing so is necessary to reproduce the correct temperature dependence of ![Image 91: {\displaystyle \mu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9fd47b2a39f7a7856952afec1f1db72c67af6161), which experiments show increases more rapidly than the ![Image 92: {\displaystyle T^{1/2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/20a2f8e9d493c53592f6eae070aeb0f5ca398a30) trend predicted for rigid elastic spheres.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBirdStewartLightfoot2007-19) Indeed, the Chapman–Enskog analysis shows that the predicted temperature dependence can be tuned by varying the parameters in various molecular models. A simple example is the Sutherland model,[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-38) which describes rigid elastic spheres with _weak_ mutual attraction. In such a case, the attractive force can be treated [perturbatively](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perturbation_theory "Perturbation theory"), which leads to a simple expression for ![Image 93: {\displaystyle \mu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9fd47b2a39f7a7856952afec1f1db72c67af6161):

![Image 94: {\displaystyle \mu ={\frac {5}{16\sigma ^{2}}}\left({\frac {k_{\text{B}}mT}{\pi }}\right)^{\!\!1/2}\ \left(1+{\frac {S}{T}}\right)^{\!\!-1},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a4d21b449e12fc6a2557849fa76c8506620bb82e)
where ![Image 95: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2) is independent of temperature, being determined only by the parameters of the intermolecular attraction. To connect with experiment, it is convenient to rewrite as

![Image 96: {\displaystyle \mu =\mu _{0}\left({\frac {T}{T_{0}}}\right)^{\!\!3/2}\ {\frac {T_{0}+S}{T+S}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/aea6949185e0ed3d8ef8137c064f2e8ab77af3cb)
where ![Image 97: {\displaystyle \mu _{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fe2fd9b8decb38a3cd158e7b6c0c6e2d987fefcc) is the viscosity at temperature ![Image 98: {\displaystyle T_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/55b9e7d7b96196b5a6a26f4349caa3ac82fd67e3). This expression is usually named Sutherland's formula.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTESutherland1893507%E2%80%93531-39) If ![Image 99: {\displaystyle \mu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9fd47b2a39f7a7856952afec1f1db72c67af6161) is known from experiments at ![Image 100: {\displaystyle T=T_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ed837f1758b02ae3912d00544954a950d76be7ca) and at least one other temperature, then ![Image 101: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2) can be calculated. Expressions for ![Image 102: {\displaystyle \mu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9fd47b2a39f7a7856952afec1f1db72c67af6161) obtained in this way are qualitatively accurate for a number of simple gases. Slightly more sophisticated models, such as the [Lennard-Jones potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lennard-Jones_potential "Lennard-Jones potential"), or the more flexible [Mie potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mie_potential "Mie potential"), may provide better agreement with experiments, but only at the cost of a more opaque dependence on temperature. A further advantage of these more complex interaction potentials is that they can be used to develop accurate models for a wide variety of properties using the same potential parameters. In situations where little experimental data is available, this makes it possible to obtain model parameters from fitting to properties such as pure-fluid [vapour-liquid equilibria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vapor%E2%80%93liquid_equilibrium "Vapor–liquid equilibrium"), before using the parameters thus obtained to predict the viscosities of interest with reasonable accuracy.

In some systems, the assumption of [spherical symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_symmetry "Spherical symmetry") must be abandoned, as is the case for vapors with highly [polar molecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_molecules "Polar molecules") like [H 2 O](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Properties_of_water "Properties of water"). In these cases, the Chapman–Enskog analysis is significantly more complicated.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBirdStewartLightfoot200725%E2%80%9327-40)[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChapmanCowling1970235%E2%80%93237-41)

In the kinetic-molecular picture, a non-zero bulk viscosity arises in gases whenever there are non-negligible relaxational timescales governing the exchange of energy between the translational energy of molecules and their internal energy, e.g. [rotational](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_energy "Rotational energy") and [vibrational](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vibration "Vibration"). As such, the bulk viscosity is ![Image 103: {\displaystyle 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2aae8864a3c1fec9585261791a809ddec1489950) for a monatomic ideal gas, in which the internal energy of molecules is negligible, but is nonzero for a gas like [carbon dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_dioxide "Carbon dioxide"), whose molecules possess both rotational and vibrational energy.[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChapmanCowling1970197,_214%E2%80%93216-42)[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTECramer2012066102-2-43)

In contrast with gases, there is no simple yet accurate picture for the molecular origins of viscosity in liquids.

At the simplest level of description, the relative motion of adjacent layers in a liquid is opposed primarily by attractive molecular forces acting across the layer boundary. In this picture, one (correctly) expects viscosity to decrease with increasing temperature. This is because increasing temperature increases the random thermal motion of the molecules, which makes it easier for them to overcome their attractive interactions.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEReidSherwood1958202-44)

Building on this visualization, a simple theory can be constructed in analogy with the discrete structure of a solid: groups of molecules in a liquid are visualized as forming "cages" which surround and enclose single molecules.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBirdStewartLightfoot200729%E2%80%9331-45) These cages can be occupied or unoccupied, and stronger molecular attraction corresponds to stronger cages. Due to random thermal motion, a molecule "hops" between cages at a rate which varies inversely with the strength of molecular attractions. In [equilibrium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamic_equilibrium "Thermodynamic equilibrium") these "hops" are not biased in any direction. On the other hand, in order for two adjacent layers to move relative to each other, the "hops" must be biased in the direction of the relative motion. The force required to sustain this directed motion can be estimated for a given shear rate, leading to

![Image 104: {\displaystyle \mu \approx {\frac {N_{\text{A}}h}{V}}\operatorname {exp} \left(3.8{\frac {T_{\text{b}}}{T}}\right),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ef9ad28fb47b15a285aaedc90912f0ff5c82d660)1

where ![Image 105: {\displaystyle N_{\text{A}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/037f8d054f445451d8fee1b440cddba989e66ea1) is the [Avogadro constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Avogadro_constant "Avogadro constant"), ![Image 106: {\displaystyle h}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b26be3e694314bc90c3215047e4a2010c6ee184a) is the [Planck constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant "Planck constant"), ![Image 107: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) is the volume of a [mole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mole_(unit) "Mole (unit)") of liquid, and ![Image 108: {\displaystyle T_{\text{b}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9eb954fc0fb95dad925ac02b36d1f2192976383a) is the [normal boiling point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_boiling_point "Normal boiling point"). This result has the same form as the well-known empirical relation

![Image 109: {\displaystyle \mu =Ae^{B/T},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/89a68dde54867bc9ea90dcdfa1db0cd8004fa884)2

where ![Image 110: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3) and ![Image 111: {\displaystyle B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47136aad860d145f75f3eed3022df827cee94d7a) are constants fit from data.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBirdStewartLightfoot200729%E2%80%9331-45)[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEReidSherwood1958203%E2%80%93204-46) On the other hand, several authors express caution with respect to this model. Errors as large as 30% can be encountered using equation (**[1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#math_1)**), compared with fitting equation (**[2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#math_2)**) to experimental data.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBirdStewartLightfoot200729%E2%80%9331-45) More fundamentally, the physical assumptions underlying equation (**[1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#math_1)**) have been criticized.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHildebrand1977-47) It has also been argued that the exponential dependence in equation (**[1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#math_1)**) does not necessarily describe experimental observations more accurately than simpler, non-exponential expressions.[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHildebrand197737-48)[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEgelstaff1992264-49)

In light of these shortcomings, the development of a less ad hoc model is a matter of practical interest. Foregoing simplicity in favor of precision, it is possible to write rigorous expressions for viscosity starting from the fundamental equations of motion for molecules. A classic example of this approach is Irving–Kirkwood theory.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIrvingKirkwood1949817%E2%80%93829-50) On the other hand, such expressions are given as averages over multiparticle [correlation functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Correlation_function_(statistical_mechanics) "Correlation function (statistical mechanics)") and are therefore difficult to apply in practice.

In general, empirically derived expressions (based on existing viscosity measurements) appear to be the only consistently reliable means of calculating viscosity in liquids.[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEReidSherwood1958206%E2%80%93209-51)

Local atomic structure changes observed in undercooled liquids on cooling below the equilibrium melting temperature either in terms of radial distribution function _g_(_r_)[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-52) or structure factor _S_(_Q_)[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-53) are found to be directly responsible for the liquid fragility: deviation of the temperature dependence of viscosity of the undercooled liquid from the Arrhenius equation (2) through modification of the activation energy for viscous flow. At the same time equilibrium liquids follow the Arrhenius equation.

### Mixtures and blends

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Viscosity&action=edit&section=16 "Edit section: Mixtures and blends")]

The same molecular-kinetic picture of a single component gas can also be applied to a gaseous mixture. For instance, in the [Chapman–Enskog](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chapman%E2%80%93Enskog_theory "Chapman–Enskog theory") approach the viscosity ![Image 112: {\displaystyle \mu _{\text{mix}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1d69e7b4b7a37088007f427ae7c8d98009133acf) of a binary mixture of gases can be written in terms of the individual component viscosities ![Image 113: {\displaystyle \mu _{1,2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5dadd1becbc242ccd6b3dec28dba3a5e08bb58b2), their respective volume fractions, and the intermolecular interactions.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChapmanCowling1970-17)

As for the single-component gas, the dependence of ![Image 114: {\displaystyle \mu _{\text{mix}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1d69e7b4b7a37088007f427ae7c8d98009133acf) on the parameters of the intermolecular interactions enters through various collisional integrals which may not be expressible in [closed form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed-form_expression#Symbolic_integration "Closed-form expression"). To obtain usable expressions for ![Image 115: {\displaystyle \mu _{\text{mix}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1d69e7b4b7a37088007f427ae7c8d98009133acf) which reasonably match experimental data, the collisional integrals may be computed numerically or from correlations.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-:0-54) In some cases, the collision integrals are regarded as fitting parameters, and are fitted directly to experimental data.[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-55) This is a common approach in the development of [reference equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Reference_equations&action=edit&redlink=1 "Reference equations (page does not exist)") for gas-phase viscosities. An example of such a procedure is the Sutherland approach for the single-component gas, discussed above.

For gas mixtures consisting of simple molecules, [Revised Enskog Theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Revised_Enskog_theory "Revised Enskog theory") has been shown to accurately represent both the density- and temperature dependence of the viscosity over a wide range of conditions.[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-56)[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-:0-54)

As for pure liquids, the viscosity of a blend of liquids is difficult to predict from molecular principles. One method is to extend the molecular "cage" theory presented above for a pure liquid. This can be done with varying levels of sophistication. One expression resulting from such an analysis is the Lederer–Roegiers equation for a binary mixture:

![Image 116: {\displaystyle \ln \mu _{\text{blend}}={\frac {x_{1}}{x_{1}+\alpha x_{2}}}\ln \mu _{1}+{\frac {\alpha x_{2}}{x_{1}+\alpha x_{2}}}\ln \mu _{2},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/09e726b23a71eecf16a4b894dbd16aa7cad6b1cb)
where ![Image 117: {\displaystyle \alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b79333175c8b3f0840bfb4ec41b8072c83ea88d3) is an empirical parameter, and ![Image 118: {\displaystyle x_{1,2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a722bc42bd90aa8f4f6915385ccfe52a9f5f8c57) and ![Image 119: {\displaystyle \mu _{1,2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5dadd1becbc242ccd6b3dec28dba3a5e08bb58b2) are the respective [mole fractions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mole_fractions "Mole fractions") and viscosities of the component liquids.[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEZhmud201422-57)

Since blending is an important process in the lubricating and oil industries, a variety of empirical and proprietary equations exist for predicting the viscosity of a blend.[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEZhmud201422-57)

### Solutions and suspensions

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Viscosity&action=edit&section=19 "Edit section: Solutions and suspensions")]

Depending on the [solute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solute "Solute") and range of concentration, an aqueous [electrolyte](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrolyte "Electrolyte") solution can have either a larger or smaller viscosity compared with pure water at the same temperature and pressure. For instance, a 20% saline ([sodium chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride "Sodium chloride")) solution has viscosity over 1.5 times that of pure water, whereas a 20% [potassium iodide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_iodide "Potassium iodide") solution has viscosity about 0.91 times that of pure water.

In a suspension of solid particles (e.g. [micron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Micron "Micron")-size spheres suspended in oil), an effective viscosity ![Image 133: {\displaystyle \mu _{\text{eff}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7111a2b595cddff7203465b91f2e1a5b60549f6e) can be defined in terms of stress and strain components which are averaged over a volume large compared with the distance between the suspended particles, but small with respect to macroscopic dimensions.[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBirdStewartLightfoot200731%E2%80%9333-60) Such suspensions generally exhibit non-Newtonian behavior. However, for dilute systems in steady flows, the behavior is Newtonian and expressions for ![Image 134: {\displaystyle \mu _{\text{eff}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7111a2b595cddff7203465b91f2e1a5b60549f6e) can be derived directly from the particle dynamics. In a very dilute system, with volume fraction ![Image 135: {\displaystyle \phi \lesssim 0.02}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2f406c6c20b93c9a68575447bc99e3fb5612570a), interactions between the suspended particles can be ignored. In such a case one can explicitly calculate the flow field around each particle independently, and combine the results to obtain ![Image 136: {\displaystyle \mu _{\text{eff}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7111a2b595cddff7203465b91f2e1a5b60549f6e). For spheres, this results in the Einstein's effective viscosity formula:

![Image 137: {\displaystyle \mu _{\text{eff}}=\mu _{0}\left(1+{\frac {5}{2}}\phi \right),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8e8141492fe77c207ecc4d5ecc5402428a73e254)
where ![Image 138: {\displaystyle \mu _{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fe2fd9b8decb38a3cd158e7b6c0c6e2d987fefcc) is the viscosity of the suspending liquid. The linear dependence on ![Image 139: {\displaystyle \phi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/72b1f30316670aee6270a28334bdf4f5072cdde4) is a consequence of neglecting interparticle interactions. For dilute systems in general, one expects ![Image 140: {\displaystyle \mu _{\text{eff}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7111a2b595cddff7203465b91f2e1a5b60549f6e) to take the form

![Image 141: {\displaystyle \mu _{\text{eff}}=\mu _{0}\left(1+B\phi \right),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f2e3553335495441a9d1d7811c1871b71c48d5e5)
where the coefficient ![Image 142: {\displaystyle B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47136aad860d145f75f3eed3022df827cee94d7a) may depend on the particle shape (e.g. spheres, rods, disks).[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBirdStewartLightfoot200732-61) Experimental determination of the precise value of ![Image 143: {\displaystyle B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47136aad860d145f75f3eed3022df827cee94d7a) is difficult, however: even the prediction ![Image 144: {\displaystyle B=5/2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fd09fbc65fc2887b9c47c0adff0719f5b660247e) for spheres has not been conclusively validated, with various experiments finding values in the range ![Image 145: {\displaystyle 1.5\lesssim B\lesssim 5}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a7a4fe5715e37f1023e48165b10cd4eb3def44f4). This deficiency has been attributed to difficulty in controlling experimental conditions.[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMuellerLlewellinMader20091201%E2%80%931228-62)

In denser suspensions, ![Image 146: {\displaystyle \mu _{\text{eff}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7111a2b595cddff7203465b91f2e1a5b60549f6e) acquires a nonlinear dependence on ![Image 147: {\displaystyle \phi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/72b1f30316670aee6270a28334bdf4f5072cdde4), which indicates the importance of interparticle interactions. Various analytical and semi-empirical schemes exist for capturing this regime. At the most basic level, a term quadratic in ![Image 148: {\displaystyle \phi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/72b1f30316670aee6270a28334bdf4f5072cdde4) is added to ![Image 149: {\displaystyle \mu _{\text{eff}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7111a2b595cddff7203465b91f2e1a5b60549f6e):

![Image 150: {\displaystyle \mu _{\text{eff}}=\mu _{0}\left(1+B\phi +B_{1}\phi ^{2}\right),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e8e05805430bb3102b9dcd1f6db0a7e226f35a99)
and the coefficient ![Image 151: {\displaystyle B_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1fa091eb428443c9c5c5fcf32a69d3665c89e00c) is fit from experimental data or approximated from the microscopic theory. However, some authors advise caution in applying such simple formulas since non-Newtonian behavior appears in dense suspensions (![Image 152: {\displaystyle \phi \gtrsim 0.25}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/07d5061da46ecfc95f12debcf00f2203c52b97f4) for spheres),[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMuellerLlewellinMader20091201%E2%80%931228-62) or in suspensions of elongated or flexible particles.[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBirdStewartLightfoot200731%E2%80%9333-60)

There is a distinction between a suspension of solid particles, described above, and an [emulsion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emulsion "Emulsion"). The latter is a suspension of tiny droplets, which themselves may exhibit internal circulation. The presence of internal circulation can decrease the observed effective viscosity, and different theoretical or semi-empirical models must be used.[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBirdStewartLightfoot200733-63)

### Amorphous materials

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Viscosity&action=edit&section=22 "Edit section: Amorphous materials")]

[![Image 153](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Glassviscosityexamples.png/500px-Glassviscosityexamples.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Glassviscosityexamples.png)

Common [glass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glass "Glass") viscosity curves[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFluegel2007-64)

In the high and low temperature limits, viscous flow in [amorphous materials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amorphous_solid "Amorphous solid") (e.g. in [glasses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glass "Glass") and melts)[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDoremus20027619%E2%80%937629-65)[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOjovanTravisHand2007415107-66)[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOjovanLee20043803%E2%80%933810-67) has the [Arrhenius form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arrhenius_equation "Arrhenius equation"):

![Image 154: {\displaystyle \mu =Ae^{Q/(RT)},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c9b19a42b7eae1095445dc759dcace47b00368b0)
where Q is a relevant [activation energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Activation_energy "Activation energy"), given in terms of molecular parameters; T is temperature; R is the molar [gas constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas_constant "Gas constant"); and A is approximately a constant. The activation energy Q takes a different value depending on whether the high or low temperature limit is being considered: it changes from a high value _Q_ H at low temperatures (in the glassy state) to a low value _Q_ L at high temperatures (in the liquid state).

[![Image 155](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/B2O3_viscosoty.jpg/330px-B2O3_viscosoty.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:B2O3_viscosoty.jpg)

[Common logarithm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Common_logarithm "Common logarithm") of viscosity against temperature for [B 2 O 3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boron_trioxide "Boron trioxide"), showing two regimes

For intermediate temperatures, ![Image 156: {\displaystyle Q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8752c7023b4b3286800fe3238271bbca681219ed) varies nontrivially with temperature and the simple Arrhenius form fails. On the other hand, the two-exponential equation

![Image 157: {\displaystyle \mu =AT\exp \left({\frac {B}{RT}}\right)\left[1+C\exp \left({\frac {D}{RT}}\right)\right],}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38aa9224e9ac73624655cd20405e140af63a62eb)
where ![Image 158: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3), ![Image 159: {\displaystyle B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47136aad860d145f75f3eed3022df827cee94d7a), ![Image 160: {\displaystyle C}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4fc55753007cd3c18576f7933f6f089196732029), ![Image 161: {\displaystyle D}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f34a0c600395e5d4345287e21fb26efd386990e6) are all constants, provides a good fit to experimental data over the entire range of temperatures, while at the same time reducing to the correct Arrhenius form in the low and high temperature limits. This expression, also known as Duouglas-Doremus-[Ojovan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michael_Ojovan "Michael Ojovan") model,[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-68) can be motivated from various theoretical models of amorphous materials at the atomic level.[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOjovanTravisHand2007415107-66)

A two-exponential equation for the viscosity can be derived within the Dyre shoving model of supercooled liquids, where the Arrhenius energy barrier is identified with the high-frequency [shear modulus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shear_modulus "Shear modulus") times a characteristic shoving volume.[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDyreOlsenChristensen19962171-69)[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHecksherDyre2015-70) Upon specifying the temperature dependence of the shear modulus via thermal expansion and via the repulsive part of the intermolecular potential, another two-exponential equation is retrieved:[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKrausserSamwerZaccone201513762-71)

![Image 162: {\displaystyle \mu =\exp {\left\{{\frac {V_{c}C_{G}}{k_{B}T}}\exp {\left[(2+\lambda )\alpha _{T}T_{g}\left(1-{\frac {T}{T_{g}}}\right)\right]}\right\}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d7c6670713177337446c22a7976e9664d2008526)
where ![Image 163: {\displaystyle C_{G}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47f01459fd6bc92fead984540025151d0e431bf9) denotes the high-frequency [shear modulus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shear_modulus "Shear modulus") of the material evaluated at a temperature equal to the [glass transition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glass_transition "Glass transition") temperature ![Image 164: {\displaystyle T_{g}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f8e95177ce2147ed7b88f95acad4c46bfd195ee1), ![Image 165: {\displaystyle V_{c}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/338b595db7a169754c5e088d9881010c2225f597) is the so-called shoving volume, i.e. it is the characteristic volume of the group of atoms involved in the shoving event by which an atom/molecule escapes from the cage of nearest-neighbours, typically on the order of the volume occupied by few atoms. Furthermore, ![Image 166: {\displaystyle \alpha _{T}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1bae4c2d7a1f5097997a285df47dd01fb03ac1dc) is the [thermal expansion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermal_expansion "Thermal expansion") coefficient of the material, ![Image 167: {\displaystyle \lambda }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b43d0ea3c9c025af1be9128e62a18fa74bedda2a) is a parameter which measures the steepness of the power-law rise of the ascending flank of the first peak of the [radial distribution function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radial_distribution_function "Radial distribution function"), and is quantitatively related to the repulsive part of the [interatomic potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interatomic_potential "Interatomic potential").[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKrausserSamwerZaccone201513762-71) Finally, ![Image 168: {\displaystyle k_{B}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/70f38f7b73e53fd7b5d9ca64bec3a1438cc0eade) denotes the [Boltzmann constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boltzmann_constant "Boltzmann constant").

In the study of [turbulence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turbulence "Turbulence") in [fluids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluid "Fluid"), a common practical strategy is to ignore the small-scale [vortices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vortex "Vortex") (or [eddies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eddy_(fluid_dynamics) "Eddy (fluid dynamics)")) in the motion and to calculate a large-scale motion with an _effective_ viscosity, called the "eddy viscosity", which characterizes the transport and dissipation of [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy "Energy") in the smaller-scale flow (see [large eddy simulation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Large_eddy_simulation "Large eddy simulation")).[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBirdStewartLightfoot2007163-72)[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTELesieur20122%E2%80%93-73) In contrast to the viscosity of the fluid itself, which must be positive by the [second law of thermodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_law_of_thermodynamics "Second law of thermodynamics"), the eddy viscosity can be negative.[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTESivashinskyYakhot19851040-74)[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEXieLevchenko2019045434-75)

Because viscosity depends continuously on temperature and pressure, it cannot be fully characterized by a finite number of experimental measurements. Predictive formulas become necessary if experimental values are not available at the temperatures and pressures of interest. This capability is important for thermophysical simulations, in which the temperature and pressure of a fluid can vary continuously with space and time. A similar situation is encountered for mixtures of pure fluids, where the viscosity depends continuously on the concentration ratios of the constituent fluids

For the simplest fluids, such as dilute monatomic gases and their mixtures, _[ab initio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ab\_initio "Ab initio")_[quantum mechanical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics") computations can accurately predict viscosity in terms of fundamental atomic constants, i.e., without reference to existing viscosity measurements.[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTESharipovBenites2020-76) For the special case of dilute helium, [uncertainties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Measurement_uncertainty "Measurement uncertainty") in the _ab initio_ calculated viscosity are two order of magnitudes smaller than uncertainties in experimental values.[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTERowlandAl_GhafriMay2020-77)

For slightly more complex fluids and mixtures at moderate densities (i.e. [sub-critical densities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Critical_point_(thermodynamics) "Critical point (thermodynamics)")) [Revised Enskog Theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Revised_Enskog_theory "Revised Enskog theory") can be used to predict viscosities with some accuracy.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-:0-54) Revised Enskog Theory is predictive in the sense that predictions for viscosity can be obtained using parameters fitted to other, pure-fluid [thermodynamic properties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_thermodynamic_properties "List of thermodynamic properties") or [transport properties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transport_phenomena "Transport phenomena"), thus requiring no _a priori_ experimental viscosity measurements.

For most fluids, high-accuracy, first-principles computations are not feasible. Rather, theoretical or empirical expressions must be fit to existing viscosity measurements. If such an expression is fit to high-fidelity data over a large range of temperatures and pressures, then it is called a "reference correlation" for that fluid. Reference correlations have been published for many pure fluids; a few examples are [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water "Water"), [carbon dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_dioxide "Carbon dioxide"), [ammonia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia "Ammonia"), [benzene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene "Benzene"), and [xenon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xenon "Xenon").[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHuberPerkinsLaeseckeFriend2009-78)[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTELaeseckeMuzny2017-79)[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMonogenidouAssaelHuber2018-80)[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAvgeriAssaelHuberPerkins2014-81)[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVelliadouTasidouAntoniadisAssael2021-82) Many of these cover temperature and pressure ranges that encompass gas, liquid, and [supercritical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supercritical_fluid "Supercritical fluid") phases.

Thermophysical modeling software often relies on reference correlations for predicting viscosity at user-specified temperature and pressure. These correlations may be [proprietary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proprietary_software "Proprietary software"). Examples are [REFPROP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/REFPROP "REFPROP")[[82]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-83) (proprietary) and [CoolProp](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=CoolProp&action=edit&redlink=1 "CoolProp (page does not exist)")[[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBellWronskiQuoilinLemort2014-84) (open-source).

Viscosity can also be computed using formulas that express it in terms of the statistics of individual particle trajectories. These formulas include the [Green–Kubo relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green%E2%80%93Kubo_relations "Green–Kubo relations") for the linear shear viscosity and the _transient time correlation function_ expressions derived by Evans and Morriss in 1988.[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEvansMorriss2007-85)[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEvansMorriss1988-34) The advantage of these expressions is that they are formally exact and valid for general systems. The disadvantage is that they require detailed knowledge of particle trajectories, available only in computationally expensive simulations such as [molecular dynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_dynamics "Molecular dynamics"). An accurate model for interparticle interactions is also required, which may be difficult to obtain for complex molecules.[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMaginnMesserlyCarlsonRoe2019-86)

Selected substances
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Viscosity&action=edit&section=25 "Edit section: Selected substances")]

[![Image 169](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/University_of_Queensland_Pitch_drop_experiment-white_bg.jpg/250px-University_of_Queensland_Pitch_drop_experiment-white_bg.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:University_of_Queensland_Pitch_drop_experiment-white_bg.jpg)

In the University of Queensland [pitch drop experiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pitch_drop_experiment "Pitch drop experiment"), pitch has been dripping slowly through a funnel since 1927, at a rate of one drop roughly every decade. In this way the viscosity of pitch has been determined to be approximately 230 billion (2.3×10 11) times that of water.[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEdgeworthDaltonParnell1984198%E2%80%93200-87)

Observed values of viscosity vary over several orders of magnitude, even for common substances (see the order of magnitude table below). For instance, a 70% sucrose (sugar) solution has a viscosity over 400 times that of water, and 26,000 times that of air.[[87]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTERumble2018-88) More dramatically, [pitch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pitch_(resin) "Pitch (resin)") has been estimated to have a viscosity 230 billion times that of water.[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEdgeworthDaltonParnell1984198%E2%80%93200-87)

The [dynamic viscosity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynamic_viscosity "Dynamic viscosity")![Image 170: {\displaystyle \mu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9fd47b2a39f7a7856952afec1f1db72c67af6161) of [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water "Water") is about 0.89 mPa·s at room temperature (25°C). where _A_ = 0.02939 mPa·s, _B_ = 507.88 K, and _C_ = 149.3 K.[[88]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_viscosity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEViswanathNatarajan1989714%E2%80%93715-89) Experimentally determined values of the viscosity are also given in the table below.
