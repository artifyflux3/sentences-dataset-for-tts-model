**Stern–Gerlach experiment**: Silver atoms travelling through an inhomogeneous magnetic field, and being deflected up or down depending on their spin; (1) furnace, (2) beam of silver atoms, (3) inhomogeneous magnetic field, (4) classically expected result, (5) observed result

In [quantum physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_physics "Quantum physics"), the **Stern–Gerlach experiment** demonstrated that the spatial orientation of [angular momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum "Angular momentum") is [quantized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantization_(physics) "Quantization (physics)"). Thus an [atomic-scale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_spacing "Atomic spacing") system was shown to have intrinsically quantum properties. In the original experiment, silver [atoms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atom "Atom") were sent through a spatially-varying [magnetic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_field "Magnetic field"), which [deflected](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deflection_(physics) "Deflection (physics)") them before they struck a detector screen, such as a [glass slide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glass_slide "Glass slide"). Particles with non-zero [magnetic moment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_moment "Magnetic moment") were deflected, owing to the magnetic field [gradient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spatial_gradient "Spatial gradient"), from a straight path. The screen revealed discrete points of accumulation, rather than a continuous distribution,[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stern%E2%80%93Gerlach_experiment#cite_note-SG-1) owing to their quantized [spin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_(physics) "Spin (physics)"). Historically, this experiment was decisive in convincing physicists of the reality of angular-momentum quantization in all atomic-scale systems.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stern%E2%80%93Gerlach_experiment#cite_note-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stern%E2%80%93Gerlach_experiment#cite_note-FH2003-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stern%E2%80%93Gerlach_experiment#cite_note-4)

After its conception by [Otto Stern](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Otto_Stern "Otto Stern") in 1921, the experiment was first successfully conducted with [Walther Gerlach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Walther_Gerlach "Walther Gerlach") in early 1922.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stern%E2%80%93Gerlach_experiment#cite_note-SG-1)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stern%E2%80%93Gerlach_experiment#cite_note-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stern%E2%80%93Gerlach_experiment#cite_note-6)

Video explaining quantum spin versus classical magnet in the Stern–Gerlach experiment

The Stern–Gerlach experiment involves sending [silver](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silver "Silver") atoms through an [inhomogeneous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homogeneity_and_heterogeneity "Homogeneity and heterogeneity")[magnetic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_field "Magnetic field") and observing their deflection. Silver atoms were evaporated using an electric furnace in a vacuum. Using thin slits, the atoms were guided into a flat beam and the beam sent through an inhomogeneous magnetic field before colliding with a metallic plate. The laws of classical physics predict that the collection of condensed silver atoms on the plate should form a thin solid line in the same shape as the original beam. However, the inhomogeneous magnetic field caused the beam to split in two separate directions, creating two lines on the metallic plate.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stern%E2%80%93Gerlach_experiment#cite_note-FH2003-3)

The results show that particles possess an intrinsic [angular momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum "Angular momentum") that is closely analogous to the angular momentum of a classically spinning object, but that takes only certain quantized values. Another important result is that only one component of a particle's spin can be measured at one time, meaning that the measurement of the spin along the z-axis destroys information about a particle's spin along the x and y axis.

The experiment is normally conducted using electrically [neutral particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutral_particle "Neutral particle") such as silver atoms. This avoids the large deflection in the path of a charged particle moving through a magnetic field and allows spin-dependent effects to dominate.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stern%E2%80%93Gerlach_experiment#cite_note-7)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stern%E2%80%93Gerlach_experiment#cite_note-8)

If the particle is treated as a classical spinning [magnetic dipole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_dipole "Magnetic dipole"), it will [precess](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Larmor_precession "Larmor precession") in a magnetic field because of the torque that the magnetic field exerts on the dipole (see [torque-induced precession](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gyroscopic_precession "Gyroscopic precession")). If it moves through a homogeneous magnetic field, the forces exerted on opposite ends of the dipole cancel each other out and the trajectory of the particle is unaffected. However, if the magnetic field is inhomogeneous then the force on one end of the dipole will be slightly greater than the opposing force on the other end, so that there is a net force which deflects the particle's trajectory. If the particles were classical spinning objects, one would expect the distribution of their spin angular momentum vectors to be [random](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boltzmann_distribution "Boltzmann distribution") and [continuous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Random_variable "Random variable"). Each particle would be deflected by an amount proportional to the [dot product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dot_product "Dot product") of its magnetic moment with the external field gradient, producing some density distribution on the detector screen. Instead, the particles passing through the Stern–Gerlach apparatus are deflected either up or down by a specific amount. This was a measurement of the quantum [observable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable "Observable") now known as [spin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_operator "Spin operator") angular momentum, which demonstrated possible outcomes of a measurement where the observable has a discrete set of values or [point spectrum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_spectrum "Point spectrum").[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stern%E2%80%93Gerlach_experiment#cite_note-9)

Although some discrete quantum phenomena, such as [atomic spectra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_spectra "Atomic spectra"), were observed much earlier, the Stern–Gerlach experiment allowed scientists to directly observe separation between discrete quantum states for the first time.

Theoretically, [quantum angular momentum _of any kind_ has a discrete spectrum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum_operator#Quantization "Angular momentum operator"), which is sometimes briefly expressed as "angular momentum is quantized".

### Experiment using particles with +1/2 or −1/2 spin

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Stern%E2%80%93Gerlach_experiment&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Experiment using particles with +1/2 or −1/2 spin")]

If the experiment is conducted using charged particles like electrons, there will be a [Lorentz force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentz_force "Lorentz force") that tends to bend the trajectory in a circle. This force can be cancelled by an electric field of appropriate magnitude oriented transverse to the charged particle's path.

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Quantum_projection_of_S_onto_z_for_spin_half_particles.svg/150px-Quantum_projection_of_S_onto_z_for_spin_half_particles.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Quantum_projection_of_S_onto_z_for_spin_half_particles.svg)

Spin values for fermions

Electrons are [spin-1/2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin-1/2 "Spin-1/2") particles. These have only two possible spin angular momentum values measured along any axis, ![Image 3: {\displaystyle +{\frac {\hbar }{2}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e1855a611aa0d3572e2cf14dbb9464e591860d88) or ![Image 4: {\displaystyle -{\frac {\hbar }{2}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f6e155bab50678f12b4bea62d471aaa0923c478f), a purely quantum mechanical phenomenon. Because its value is always the same, it is regarded as an intrinsic property of electrons, and is sometimes known as "intrinsic angular momentum" (to distinguish it from orbital angular momentum, which can vary and depends on the presence of other particles). If one measures the spin along a vertical axis, electrons are described as "spin up" or "spin down", based on the magnetic moment pointing up or down, respectively.

To mathematically describe the experiment with spin-1/2 particles, it is easiest to use [Dirac](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Adrien_Maurice_Dirac "Paul Adrien Maurice Dirac")'s [bra–ket notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bra%E2%80%93ket_notation "Bra–ket notation"). As the particles pass through the Stern–Gerlach device, they are deflected either up or down, and observed by the detector which resolves to either spin up or spin down. These are described by the angular momentum quantum number ![Image 5: {\displaystyle j}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2f461e54f5c093e92a55547b9764291390f0b5d0), which can take on one of the two possible allowed values, either +1/2 or -1/2. The act of observing (measuring) the momentum along the ![Image 6: {\displaystyle z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf368e72c009decd9b6686ee84a375632e11de98) axis corresponds to the ![Image 7: {\displaystyle z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf368e72c009decd9b6686ee84a375632e11de98)-axis [angular momentum operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum_operator "Angular momentum operator"), often denoted ![Image 8: {\displaystyle J_{z}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5e4fc97aafeb0dc8febc33638f7644e68c2f68ac). In mathematical terms, the initial state of the particles is

![Image 9: {\displaystyle |\psi \rangle =c_{1}\left|\psi _{j=+{\frac {1}{2}}}\right\rangle +c_{2}\left|\psi _{j=-{\frac {1}{2}}}\right\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7426063a691ad97550b3a213abb76960fc3daec6)
where constants ![Image 10: {\displaystyle c_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/77b7dc6d279091d354e0b90889b463bfa7eb7247) and ![Image 11: {\displaystyle c_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0b30ba1b247fb8d334580cec68561e749d24aff2) are complex numbers. This initial state spin can point in any direction. The squares of the [absolute values](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_value "Absolute value")![Image 12: {\displaystyle |c_{1}|^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a9c67c5c8bf07a77edd704f880a64a35b8d9f1c5) and ![Image 13: {\displaystyle |c_{2}|^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/549e95b168be3d844845f4585e48735272eb4c97) are respectively the probabilities for a system in the state ![Image 14: {\displaystyle |\psi \rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cc27f1893b769a08cd6b296e115a29e61cab675e) to be found in ![Image 15: {\displaystyle \left|\psi _{j=+{\frac {1}{2}}}\right\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1819048f21a13752586e93633b7c93ec6456666c) and ![Image 16: {\displaystyle \left|\psi _{j=-{\frac {1}{2}}}\right\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8c0a35955bbe4cb52da6b78bd606d395e005669f) after the measurement along ![Image 17: {\displaystyle z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf368e72c009decd9b6686ee84a375632e11de98) axis is made. The constants ![Image 18: {\displaystyle c_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/77b7dc6d279091d354e0b90889b463bfa7eb7247) and ![Image 19: {\displaystyle c_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0b30ba1b247fb8d334580cec68561e749d24aff2) must also be normalized in order that the probability of finding either one of the values be unity, that is we must ensure that ![Image 20: {\displaystyle |c_{1}|^{2}+|c_{2}|^{2}=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a89569c66bde1474aa66103187b29122d0f5d619). However, this information is not sufficient to determine the values of ![Image 21: {\displaystyle c_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/77b7dc6d279091d354e0b90889b463bfa7eb7247) and ![Image 22: {\displaystyle c_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0b30ba1b247fb8d334580cec68561e749d24aff2), because they are complex numbers. Therefore, the measurement yields only the squared magnitudes of the constants, which are interpreted as probabilities.

Sequential experiments
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Stern%E2%80%93Gerlach_experiment&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Sequential experiments")]

If we link multiple Stern–Gerlach apparatuses (the rectangles containing _S-G_), we can clearly see that they do not act as simple selectors, i.e. filtering out particles with one of the states (pre-existing to the measurement) and blocking the others. Instead they alter the state by observing it (as in [light polarization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon_polarization "Photon polarization")). In the figure below, x and z name the directions of the (inhomogenous) magnetic field, with the x-z-plane being orthogonal to the particle beam. In the three S-G systems shown below, the cross-hatched squares denote the blocking of a given output, i.e. each of the S-G systems with a blocker allows only particles with one of two states to enter the next S-G apparatus in the sequence.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stern%E2%80%93Gerlach_experiment#cite_note-Sakurai1985-10)

[![Image 23](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Sg-seq.svg/640px-Sg-seq.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Sg-seq.svg)

[![Image 24: 3D model of 2 S-G analyzers in sequence, showing the path of neutrons. Both analyzers measure the z-axis](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Stern-Gerlach_Analyzer_Sequential_Series_E1.png/250px-Stern-Gerlach_Analyzer_Sequential_Series_E1.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Stern-Gerlach_Analyzer_Sequential_Series_E1.png)

Exp. 1 - Notice that no z- neutrons are detected at the second S-G analyzer

The top illustration shows that when a second, identical, S-G apparatus is placed at the exit of the first apparatus, **only z+ is seen** in the output of the second apparatus. This result is expected since all particles at this point are expected to have z+ spin, as only the z+ beam from the first apparatus entered the second apparatus.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stern%E2%80%93Gerlach_experiment#cite_note-:0-11)

[![Image 25: 3D model of 2 S-G analyzers in sequence, showing the path of neutrons. The first one measures the z-axis spin, and the second one the x-axis spin.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Stern-Gerlach_Analyzer_Sequential_Series_E2.png/250px-Stern-Gerlach_Analyzer_Sequential_Series_E2.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Stern-Gerlach_Analyzer_Sequential_Series_E2.png)

Exp. 2 - The z-spin is known, now measuring the x-spin.

The middle system shows what happens when a different S-G apparatus is placed at the exit of the z+ beam resulting of the first apparatus, the second apparatus measuring the deflection of the beams on the x axis instead of the z axis. The second apparatus produces x+ and x- outputs. Now classically we would expect to have one beam with the x characteristic oriented + and the z characteristic oriented +, and another with the x characteristic oriented - and the z characteristic oriented +.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stern%E2%80%93Gerlach_experiment#cite_note-:0-11)

[![Image 26: 3D model of 3 S-G analyzers in sequence, showing the path of neutrons through them. The first one measures the z-axis spin, and the second one the x-axis spin, and the third one the z-spin again.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Stern-Gerlach_Analyzer_Sequential_Series_E3.png/250px-Stern-Gerlach_Analyzer_Sequential_Series_E3.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Stern-Gerlach_Analyzer_Sequential_Series_E3.png)

Exp. 3 - Neutrons thought to have only z+ spin are measured again, finding that the z-spin has been 'reset'.

The bottom system contradicts that expectation. The output of the third apparatus which measures the deflection on the z axis again shows an **output of****z-** as well as z+. Given that the input to the second S-G apparatus consisted **only of z+**, it can be inferred that a S-G apparatus must be altering the states of the particles that pass through it. This experiment can be interpreted to exhibit the [uncertainty principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uncertainty_principle "Uncertainty principle"): since the angular momentum cannot be measured on two perpendicular directions at the same time, the measurement of the angular momentum on the x direction destroys the previous determination of the angular momentum in the z direction. That's why the third apparatus measures renewed z+ and z- beams like the x measurement really made a clean slate of the z+ output.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stern%E2%80%93Gerlach_experiment#cite_note-:0-11)

[![Image 27](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/SternGerlach2.jpg/250px-SternGerlach2.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:SternGerlach2.jpg)

A plaque at the Frankfurt institute commemorating the experiment

The Stern–Gerlach experiment was conceived by [Otto Stern](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Otto_Stern "Otto Stern") in 1921 and performed by him and [Walther Gerlach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Walther_Gerlach "Walther Gerlach") in [Frankfurt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frankfurt "Frankfurt") in 1922.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stern%E2%80%93Gerlach_experiment#cite_note-Sakurai1985-10) At the time of the experiment, the most prevalent model for describing the [atom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atom "Atom") was the [Bohr-Sommerfeld model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bohr-Sommerfeld_model "Bohr-Sommerfeld model"),[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stern%E2%80%93Gerlach_experiment#cite_note-12)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stern%E2%80%93Gerlach_experiment#cite_note-13) which described [electrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrons "Electrons") as going around the positively charged [nucleus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_nucleus "Atomic nucleus") only in certain discrete [atomic orbitals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_orbital "Atomic orbital") or [energy levels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_levels "Energy levels"). Since the electron was [quantized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantization_(physics) "Quantization (physics)") to be only in certain positions in space, the separation into distinct orbits was referred to as [space quantization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Old_quantum_theory#Rotator "Old quantum theory"). The Stern–Gerlach experiment was meant to test the [Bohr–Sommerfeld hypothesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Old_quantum_theory "Old quantum theory") that the direction of the angular momentum of a silver atom is quantized.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stern%E2%80%93Gerlach_experiment#cite_note-14)

The experiment was first performed with an electromagnet that allowed the non-uniform magnetic field to be turned on gradually from a null value.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stern%E2%80%93Gerlach_experiment#cite_note-SG-1) When the field was null, the silver atoms were deposited as a single band on the detecting glass slide. When the field was made stronger, the middle of the band began to widen and eventually to split into two, so that the glass-slide image looked like a lip-print, with an opening in the middle, and closure at either end.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stern%E2%80%93Gerlach_experiment#cite_note-15) In the middle, where the magnetic field was strong enough to split the beam into two, statistically half of the silver atoms had been deflected by the non-uniformity of the field.

Note that the experiment was performed several years before [George Uhlenbeck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_Uhlenbeck "George Uhlenbeck") and [Samuel Goudsmit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samuel_Goudsmit "Samuel Goudsmit") formulated their hypothesis about the existence of [electron spin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_(physics) "Spin (physics)") in 1925.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stern%E2%80%93Gerlach_experiment#cite_note-16) Even though the result of the Stern−Gerlach experiment has later turned out to be in agreement with the predictions of quantum mechanics for a spin-1/2 particle, the experimental result was also consistent with the [Bohr–Sommerfeld theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Old_quantum_theory "Old quantum theory").[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stern%E2%80%93Gerlach_experiment#cite_note-17)

In 1927, T.E. Phipps and J.B. Taylor reproduced the effect using [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen") atoms in their [ground state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ground_state "Ground state"), thereby eliminating any doubts that may have been caused by the use of [silver](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silver "Silver") atoms.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stern%E2%80%93Gerlach_experiment#cite_note-18) However, in 1926 the non-relativistic scalar [Schrödinger equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schr%C3%B6dinger_equation "Schrödinger equation") had incorrectly predicted the [magnetic moment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_moment "Magnetic moment") of hydrogen to be zero in its ground state. To correct this problem [Wolfgang Pauli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Pauli "Wolfgang Pauli") considered a spin-1/2 version of the Schrödinger equation using the 3 [Pauli matrices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pauli_matrices "Pauli matrices") which now bear his name, which was later shown by [Paul Dirac](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Dirac "Paul Dirac") in 1928 to be a consequence of his relativistic [Dirac equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dirac_equation "Dirac equation").

In the early 1930s Stern, together with [Otto Robert Frisch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Otto_Robert_Frisch "Otto Robert Frisch") and [Immanuel Estermann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immanuel_Estermann "Immanuel Estermann") improved the [molecular beam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_beam "Molecular beam") apparatus sufficiently to measure the magnetic moment of the [proton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton "Proton"), a value nearly 2000 times smaller than the electron moment. In 1931, theoretical analysis by [Gregory Breit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gregory_Breit "Gregory Breit") and [Isidor Isaac Rabi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isidor_Isaac_Rabi "Isidor Isaac Rabi") showed that this apparatus could be used to measure nuclear spin whenever the electronic configuration of the atom was known. The concept was applied by Rabi and Victor W. Cohen in 1934 to determine the ![Image 28: {\displaystyle 3/2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9a94d2d02968e6cbbc338b54f7762ccd20d74805) spin of [sodium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium "Sodium") atoms.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stern%E2%80%93Gerlach_experiment#cite_note-KelloggMillman-19)

In 1938 Rabi and coworkers inserted an oscillating magnetic field element into their apparatus, inventing [nuclear magnetic resonance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_magnetic_resonance "Nuclear magnetic resonance") spectroscopy.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stern%E2%80%93Gerlach_experiment#cite_note-:1-20)[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stern%E2%80%93Gerlach_experiment#cite_note-21) By tuning the frequency of the oscillator to the frequency of the nuclear precessions they could selectively tune into each quantum level of the material under study. Rabi was awarded the Nobel Prize in 1944 for this work.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stern%E2%80%93Gerlach_experiment#cite_note-22)

The Stern–Gerlach experiment was the first direct evidence of angular-momentum quantization in quantum mechanics,[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stern%E2%80%93Gerlach_experiment#cite_note-23) and it strongly influenced later developments in [modern physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modern_physics "Modern physics"):

*   In the decade that followed, scientists showed using similar techniques, that the nuclei of some atoms also have quantized angular momentum.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stern%E2%80%93Gerlach_experiment#cite_note-:1-20) It is the interaction of this nuclear angular momentum with the spin of the electron that is responsible for the [hyperfine structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperfine_structure "Hyperfine structure") of the spectroscopic lines.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stern%E2%80%93Gerlach_experiment#cite_note-24)
*   [Norman F. Ramsey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norman_F._Ramsey "Norman F. Ramsey") later modified the Rabi apparatus to improve its sensitivity (using the [separated oscillatory field method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ramsey_interferometry "Ramsey interferometry")).[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stern%E2%80%93Gerlach_experiment#cite_note-autobiog-25) In the early sixties, Ramsey, H. Mark Goldenberg, and [Daniel Kleppner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Daniel_Kleppner "Daniel Kleppner") used a Stern–Gerlach system to produce a beam of polarized hydrogen as the source of energy for the [hydrogen maser](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_maser "Hydrogen maser").[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stern%E2%80%93Gerlach_experiment#cite_note-26) This led to developing an extremely stable clock based on a hydrogen maser. From 1967 until 2019, [the second was defined](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2019_revision_of_the_SI "2019 revision of the SI") based on 9,192,631,770 Hz hyperfine transition of a [cesium-133](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cesium-133 "Cesium-133") atom; the [atomic clock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_clock "Atomic clock") which is used to set this standard is an application of Ramsey's work.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stern%E2%80%93Gerlach_experiment#cite_note-27)
*   The Stern–Gerlach experiment has become a prototype for _[quantum measurement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum\_measurement "Quantum measurement")_, demonstrating the observation of a discrete value (_[eigenvalue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eigenvalues\_and\_eigenvectors "Eigenvalues and eigenvectors"))_ of a physical property, previously assumed to be continuous.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stern%E2%80%93Gerlach_experiment#cite_note-28)[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stern%E2%80%93Gerlach_experiment#cite_note-29)[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stern%E2%80%93Gerlach_experiment#cite_note-30) Entering the Stern–Gerlach magnet, the direction of the silver atom's magnetic moment is indefinite, but when the atom is registered at the screen, it is observed to be at either one spot or the other, and this outcome cannot be predicted in advance. Because the experiment illustrates the character of quantum measurements, _[The Feynman Lectures on Physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The\_Feynman\_Lectures\_on\_Physics "The Feynman Lectures on Physics")_ use idealized Stern–Gerlach apparatuses to explain the basic mathematics of quantum theory.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stern%E2%80%93Gerlach_experiment#cite_note-31)[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stern%E2%80%93Gerlach_experiment#cite_note-32)[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stern%E2%80%93Gerlach_experiment#cite_note-33)
