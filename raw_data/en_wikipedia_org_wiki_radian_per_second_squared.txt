In [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics"), **angular acceleration** (symbol **α**, [alpha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha "Alpha")) is the [time rate of change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_rate_of_change "Time rate of change") of [angular velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_velocity "Angular velocity"). Following the two types of angular velocity, _spin angular velocity_ and _orbital angular velocity_, the respective types of angular acceleration are: **spin angular acceleration**, involving a [rigid body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rigid_body "Rigid body") about an [axis of rotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axis_of_rotation "Axis of rotation") intersecting the body's [centroid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centroid "Centroid"); and **orbital angular acceleration**, involving a point particle and an external axis.

Angular acceleration has [physical dimensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_dimension "Physical dimension") of angle per time squared, measured in [SI units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SI_unit "SI unit") of **radians per second squared** (**rad ⋅ s−2**). In two dimensions, angular acceleration is a [pseudoscalar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudoscalar "Pseudoscalar") whose sign is taken to be positive if the angular speed increases counterclockwise or decreases clockwise, and is taken to be negative if the angular speed increases clockwise or decreases counterclockwise. In three dimensions, angular acceleration is a [pseudovector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudovector "Pseudovector").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radian_per_second_squared#cite_note-ref1-1)

### Particle in two dimensions

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Angular_acceleration&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Particle in two dimensions")]

In two dimensions, the orbital angular acceleration is the rate at which the two-dimensional orbital angular velocity of the particle about the origin changes. The instantaneous angular velocity _ω_ at any point in time is given by

[![Image 2: {\displaystyle \omega ={\frac {v_{\perp }}{r}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/95c29a5398be29c77b66df211e844987301487fe)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:MathWikibase&qid=Q161635)
where ![Image 3: {\displaystyle r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d1ecb613aa2984f0576f70f86650b7c2a132538) is the distance from the origin and ![Image 4: {\displaystyle v_{\perp }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f80a8cf80254aa3ef2640555e94986487d5cba0b) is the cross-radial component of the instantaneous velocity (i.e. the component perpendicular to the position vector), which by convention is positive for counter-clockwise motion and negative for clockwise motion.

Therefore, the instantaneous angular acceleration _α_ of the particle is given by[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radian_per_second_squared#cite_note-ref2-2)

[![Image 5: {\displaystyle \alpha ={\frac {d}{dt}}\left({\frac {v_{\perp }}{r}}\right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/740f78ac5da016cc0566a575b33faf195b570c12)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:MathWikibase&qid=Q186300)
Expanding the right-hand-side using the product rule from differential calculus, this becomes

![Image 6: {\displaystyle \alpha ={\frac {1}{r}}{\frac {dv_{\perp }}{dt}}-{\frac {v_{\perp }}{r^{2}}}{\frac {dr}{dt}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/78b1fe69dcb3d3209b9994d0f9041e529ba1e671)
In the special case where the particle undergoes circular motion about the origin, ![Image 7: {\displaystyle {\frac {dv_{\perp }}{dt}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7dc80f148f43a4067098e8e5b819e04b6d6cb9e9) becomes just the tangential acceleration ![Image 8: {\displaystyle a_{\perp }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/56d1c35eb7a0cc9f1d81ddde26869569de60c118), and ![Image 9: {\displaystyle {\frac {dr}{dt}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6294bcbe6514c3283041284a3dd70111f259e8f0) vanishes (since the distance from the origin stays constant), so the above equation simplifies to

![Image 10: {\displaystyle \alpha ={\frac {a_{\perp }}{r}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5e71d1eb594f01f0f67c162d5554b38892c074c)
In two dimensions, angular acceleration is a number with plus or minus sign indicating orientation, but not pointing in a direction. The sign is conventionally taken to be positive if the angular speed increases in the counter-clockwise direction or decreases in the clockwise direction, and the sign is taken negative if the angular speed increases in the clockwise direction or decreases in the counter-clockwise direction. Angular acceleration then may be termed a [pseudoscalar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudoscalar "Pseudoscalar"), a numerical quantity which changes sign under a [parity inversion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parity_(physics) "Parity (physics)"), such as inverting one axis or switching the two axes.

### Particle in three dimensions

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Angular_acceleration&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Particle in three dimensions")]

In three dimensions, the orbital angular acceleration is the rate at which three-dimensional orbital angular velocity vector changes with time. The instantaneous angular velocity vector ![Image 11: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {\omega }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7cb8af7a2f64af348e559652b6b1f0d2415ba444) at any point in time is given by

![Image 12: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {\omega }}={\frac {\mathbf {r} \times \mathbf {v} }{r^{2}}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/55305ae9923ae62ddbfe3f817bc7bd28675a3556)
where ![Image 13: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eca0f46511c4c986c48b254073732c0bd98ae0c1) is the particle's position vector, ![Image 14: {\displaystyle r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d1ecb613aa2984f0576f70f86650b7c2a132538) its distance from the origin, and ![Image 15: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35c1866e359fbfd2e0f606c725ba5cc37a5195d6) its velocity vector.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radian_per_second_squared#cite_note-ref2-2)

Therefore, the orbital angular acceleration is the vector ![Image 16: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {\alpha }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a585d2bb19071162720ea56a7b087dab3ec17156) defined by

![Image 17: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {\alpha }}={\frac {d}{dt}}\left({\frac {\mathbf {r} \times \mathbf {v} }{r^{2}}}\right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ed8e4d6d00e9d3cbb6940fca5663c99e0b4084c3)
Expanding this derivative using the product rule for cross-products and the ordinary quotient rule, one gets:

![Image 18: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}{\boldsymbol {\alpha }}&={\frac {1}{r^{2}}}\left(\mathbf {r} \times {\frac {d\mathbf {v} }{dt}}+{\frac {d\mathbf {r} }{dt}}\times \mathbf {v} \right)-{\frac {2}{r^{3}}}{\frac {dr}{dt}}\left(\mathbf {r} \times \mathbf {v} \right)\\\\&={\frac {1}{r^{2}}}\left(\mathbf {r} \times \mathbf {a} +\mathbf {v} \times \mathbf {v} \right)-{\frac {2}{r^{3}}}{\frac {dr}{dt}}\left(\mathbf {r} \times \mathbf {v} \right)\\\\&={\frac {\mathbf {r} \times \mathbf {a} }{r^{2}}}-{\frac {2}{r^{3}}}{\frac {dr}{dt}}\left(\mathbf {r} \times \mathbf {v} \right).\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dfc1d3b7c2fd17a58432eeb9afb096f6105fc06f)
Since ![Image 19: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} \times \mathbf {v} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e0c08354c7b7f18ca3b8d0aaa45521d6b036eb8e) is just ![Image 20: {\displaystyle r^{2}{\boldsymbol {\omega }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bff6e6381c05ba13348d478f0fa3707416577718), the second term may be rewritten as ![Image 21: {\displaystyle -{\frac {2}{r}}{\frac {dr}{dt}}{\boldsymbol {\omega }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1a54325aac812d75d4214b3d4cc38d4b86aac2e4). In the case where the distance ![Image 22: {\displaystyle r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d1ecb613aa2984f0576f70f86650b7c2a132538) of the particle from the origin does not change with time (which includes circular motion as a subcase), the second term vanishes and the above formula simplifies to

![Image 23: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {\alpha }}={\frac {\mathbf {r} \times \mathbf {a} }{r^{2}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/51bad738987294cd28174ee909fec907d1ca037d)
From the above equation, one can recover the cross-radial acceleration in this special case as:

![Image 24: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} _{\perp }={\boldsymbol {\alpha }}\times \mathbf {r} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9819ec611f9c6915971d281a96a08d116c06983f)
Unlike in two dimensions, the angular acceleration in three dimensions need not be associated with a change in the angular _speed ![Image 25: {\displaystyle \omega =|{\boldsymbol {\omega }}|}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest\_v1/media/math/render/svg/164859a896988fa7134c1935d9cad90ba3dfd8ef)_: If the particle's position vector "twists" in space, changing its instantaneous plane of angular displacement, the change in the _direction_ of the angular velocity ![Image 26: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {\omega }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7cb8af7a2f64af348e559652b6b1f0d2415ba444) will still produce a nonzero angular acceleration. This cannot not happen if the position vector is restricted to a fixed plane, in which case ![Image 27: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {\omega }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7cb8af7a2f64af348e559652b6b1f0d2415ba444) has a fixed direction perpendicular to the plane.

The angular acceleration vector is more properly called a [pseudovector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudovector "Pseudovector"): It has three components which transform under rotations in the same way as the Cartesian coordinates of a point do, but which do not transform like Cartesian coordinates under reflections.

The net _[torque](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torque "Torque")_ on a point particle is defined to be the pseudovector

[![Image 28: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {\tau }}=\mathbf {r} \times \mathbf {F} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d78f7463314c4945dd5a3a81a7b138240d5866b6)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:MathWikibase&qid=Q48103)
where ![Image 29: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/da18bef8c979f3548bb0d8976f5844012d7b8256) is the net force on the particle.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radian_per_second_squared#cite_note-ref3-3)

Torque is the rotational analogue of force: it induces change in the rotational state of a system, just as force induces change in the translational state of a system. As force on a particle is connected to acceleration by the equation [![Image 30: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} =m\mathbf {a} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d0c60fab89e8c3193952047dc565bcf8d233d115)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:MathWikibase&qid=Q11402), one may write a similar equation connecting torque on a particle to angular acceleration, though this relation is necessarily more complicated.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radian_per_second_squared#cite_note-4)

First, substituting ![Image 31: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} =m\mathbf {a} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d0c60fab89e8c3193952047dc565bcf8d233d115) into the above equation for torque, one gets

![Image 32: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {\tau }}=m\left(\mathbf {r} \times \mathbf {a} \right)=mr^{2}\left({\frac {\mathbf {r} \times \mathbf {a} }{r^{2}}}\right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4f990ba97809be6a19691ccf13474453d3eb36da)
From the previous section:

![Image 33: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {\alpha }}={\frac {\mathbf {r} \times \mathbf {a} }{r^{2}}}-{\frac {2}{r}}{\frac {dr}{dt}}{\boldsymbol {\omega }},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2bed3e5f061f1c22a6273d2c8e20a10004215468)
where ![Image 34: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {\alpha }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a585d2bb19071162720ea56a7b087dab3ec17156) is orbital angular acceleration and ![Image 35: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {\omega }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7cb8af7a2f64af348e559652b6b1f0d2415ba444) is orbital angular velocity. Therefore:

![Image 36: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {\tau }}=mr^{2}\left({\boldsymbol {\alpha }}+{\frac {2}{r}}{\frac {dr}{dt}}{\boldsymbol {\omega }}\right)=mr^{2}{\boldsymbol {\alpha }}+2mr{\frac {dr}{dt}}{\boldsymbol {\omega }}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9181f7e4e4ea18449d5ea29e788faa3ca2554469)
In the special case of constant distance ![Image 37: {\displaystyle r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d1ecb613aa2984f0576f70f86650b7c2a132538) of the particle from the origin (![Image 38: {\displaystyle {\tfrac {dr}{dt}}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/057f34ed38bc264dedaa0550214136c557ec7fdd)), the second term in the above equation vanishes and the above equation simplifies to

![Image 39: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {\tau }}=mr^{2}{\boldsymbol {\alpha }},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eb20b634376be4112b64845d4a30472a1f9c34ab)
which can be interpreted as a "rotational analogue" to ![Image 40: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} =m\mathbf {a} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d0c60fab89e8c3193952047dc565bcf8d233d115), where the quantity ![Image 41: {\displaystyle mr^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ddd9d0ea2911509b014b72a7b536acb7376cb455) (known as the [moment of inertia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_of_inertia "Moment of inertia") of the particle) plays the role of the mass ![Image 42: {\displaystyle m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a07d98bb302f3856cbabc47b2b9016692e3f7bc). However, unlike ![Image 43: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} =m\mathbf {a} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d0c60fab89e8c3193952047dc565bcf8d233d115), this equation does _not_ apply to an arbitrary trajectory, only to a trajectory contained within a spherical shell about the origin.
