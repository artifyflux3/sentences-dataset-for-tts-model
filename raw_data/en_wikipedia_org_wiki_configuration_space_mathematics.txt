The configuration space of all unordered pairs of points on the circle is the [Möbius strip](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M%C3%B6bius_strip "Möbius strip").

In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), a **configuration space** is a construction closely related to [state spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_space "State space") or [phase spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phase_space "Phase space") in physics. In physics, these are used to describe the state of a whole system as a single point in a high-dimensional space. In mathematics, they are used to describe assignments of a collection of points to positions in a [topological space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_space "Topological space"). More specifically, configuration spaces in mathematics are particular examples of [configuration spaces in physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Configuration_space_(physics) "Configuration space (physics)") in the particular case of several non-colliding particles.

For a topological space ![Image 2: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) and a positive integer ![Image 3: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b), let ![Image 4: {\displaystyle X^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/268db8293666fefd75cfb00513706171948edf09) be the [Cartesian product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_product "Cartesian product") of ![Image 5: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) copies of ![Image 6: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab), equipped with the [product topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Product_topology "Product topology"). The **_n_ th (ordered) configuration space of ![Image 7: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab)** is the set of _n_-[tuples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tuple "Tuple") of pairwise distinct points in ![Image 8: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab):

![Image 9: {\displaystyle \operatorname {Conf} _{n}(X):=X^{n}\smallsetminus \{(x_{1},x_{2},\ldots ,x_{n})\in X^{n}\mid x_{i}=x_{j}\ {\text{for some }}i\neq j\}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/953f4b483465807939556230337ef42bc73231dd)[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Configuration_space_(mathematics)#cite_note-1)
This space is generally endowed with the subspace topology from the inclusion of ![Image 10: {\displaystyle \operatorname {Conf} _{n}(X)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35b22023740780d93fa0cf090c45d00524bf8084) into ![Image 11: {\displaystyle X^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/268db8293666fefd75cfb00513706171948edf09). It is also sometimes denoted ![Image 12: {\displaystyle F(X,n)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/701c1b9d9ac216126d9f019bd2ae4c14e978c4d8), ![Image 13: {\displaystyle F^{n}(X)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0fffeeea41db1b6194166c73d991879efd696bbb), or ![Image 14: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {C}}^{n}(X)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f616c5a0c7b2893b7544375745ec5effaa828831).[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Configuration_space_(mathematics)#cite_note-:0-2)

There is a natural [action](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_action_(mathematics) "Group action (mathematics)") of the [symmetric group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetric_group "Symmetric group")![Image 15: {\displaystyle S_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9f049ac28d4ac8097b625f9d71c1f22b2ebd1bc4) on the points in ![Image 16: {\displaystyle \operatorname {Conf} _{n}(X)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35b22023740780d93fa0cf090c45d00524bf8084) given by

![Image 17: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}S_{n}\times \operatorname {Conf} _{n}(X)&\longrightarrow \operatorname {Conf} _{n}(X)\\(\sigma ,x)&\longmapsto \sigma (x)=(x_{\sigma (1)},x_{\sigma (2)},\ldots ,x_{\sigma (n)}).\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/690dd74392caaa9be3fda76d13da7f1e012f3867)
This action gives rise to the **n th unordered configuration space of X**,

![Image 18: {\displaystyle \operatorname {UConf} _{n}(X):=\operatorname {Conf} _{n}(X)/S_{n},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68b25f2581be7b420151547ae063e3cad125f0d1)
which is the [orbit space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbit_space "Orbit space") of that action. The intuition is that this action "forgets the names of the points". The unordered configuration space is sometimes denoted ![Image 19: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {UC}}^{n}(X)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9cf2e79ff3bbeaf37e0376fb994d6ee5330fa9c1),[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Configuration_space_(mathematics)#cite_note-:0-2)![Image 20: {\displaystyle B_{n}(X)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/568cb691d28b21c0a3d8d645ba7ad8bcacf60a02), or ![Image 21: {\displaystyle C_{n}(X)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/71f5e3e55e67e5ade068a8039cafd358511128c8). The collection of unordered configuration spaces over all ![Image 22: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) is the [Ran space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ran_space "Ran space"), and comes with a natural topology.

For a topological space ![Image 23: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) and a finite set ![Image 24: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2), the **configuration space of X with particles labeled by S** is

![Image 25: {\displaystyle \operatorname {Conf} _{S}(X):=\{f\mid f\colon S\hookrightarrow X{\text{ is injective}}\}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f9658de452593096ba08e15a55d0a4bb3c5471c6)
For ![Image 26: {\displaystyle n\in \mathbb {N} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d059936e77a2d707e9ee0a1d9575a1d693ce5d0b), define ![Image 27: {\displaystyle \mathbf {n} :=\{1,2,\ldots ,n\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/782d39ceb2a41832df10aeaefb2e3c03747e08c9). Then the **n th configuration space of _X_** is denoted simply ![Image 28: {\displaystyle \operatorname {Conf} _{n}(X)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35b22023740780d93fa0cf090c45d00524bf8084).[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Configuration_space_(mathematics)#cite_note-3)

Connection to braid groups
--------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Configuration_space_(mathematics)&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Connection to braid groups")]

The **n-strand braid group** on a [connected](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connected_space "Connected space") topological space X is

![Image 29: {\displaystyle B_{n}(X):=\pi _{1}(\operatorname {UConf} _{n}(X)),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d9435914ac15a94b88e12d874e41fc5ef10ce188)
the [fundamental group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_group "Fundamental group") of the n th unordered configuration space of X. The **n-strand pure braid group** on X is[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Configuration_space_(mathematics)#cite_note-:0-2)

![Image 30: {\displaystyle P_{n}(X):=\pi _{1}(\operatorname {Conf} _{n}(X)).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9ef3558db90bd0476b08c3672719e6f34dd76246)
The first studied braid groups were the **Artin braid groups**![Image 31: {\displaystyle B_{n}\cong \pi _{1}(\operatorname {UConf} _{n}(\mathbf {R} ^{2}))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/89ec873ea4c6a76b68662520b0ceac9fdcf4b63d). While the above definition is not the one that [Emil Artin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emil_Artin "Emil Artin") gave, [Adolf Hurwitz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adolf_Hurwitz "Adolf Hurwitz") implicitly defined the Artin braid groups as fundamental groups of configuration spaces of the complex plane considerably before Artin's definition (in 1891).[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Configuration_space_(mathematics)#cite_note-5)

It follows from this definition and the fact that ![Image 32: {\displaystyle \operatorname {Conf} _{n}(\mathbf {R} ^{2})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/55e97af74460b53141e94846a5232caabd9c63e5) and ![Image 33: {\displaystyle \operatorname {UConf} _{n}(\mathbf {R} ^{2})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0cec6e86289badbf9c0e413e7453b6ea53d83565) are [Eilenberg–MacLane spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eilenberg%E2%80%93MacLane_space "Eilenberg–MacLane space") of type ![Image 34: {\displaystyle K(\pi ,1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/571aec7039bc895070dec4df8aa59aa70e8ce92a), that the unordered configuration space of the plane ![Image 35: {\displaystyle \operatorname {UConf} _{n}(\mathbf {R} ^{2})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0cec6e86289badbf9c0e413e7453b6ea53d83565) is a [classifying space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classifying_space "Classifying space") for the Artin braid group, and ![Image 36: {\displaystyle \operatorname {Conf} _{n}(\mathbf {R} ^{2})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/55e97af74460b53141e94846a5232caabd9c63e5) is a classifying space for the pure Artin braid group, when both are considered as [discrete groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discrete_group "Discrete group").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Configuration_space_(mathematics)#cite_note-6)

Configuration spaces of manifolds
---------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Configuration_space_(mathematics)&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Configuration spaces of manifolds")]

If the original space ![Image 37: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) is a [manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manifold "Manifold"), its ordered configuration spaces are open subspaces of the powers of ![Image 38: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) and are thus themselves manifolds. The configuration space of distinct unordered points is also a manifold, while the configuration space of _not necessarily distinct_[_[clarification needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Please\_clarify "Wikipedia:Please clarify")_] unordered points is instead an [orbifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbifold "Orbifold").

A configuration space is a type of [classifying space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classifying_space "Classifying space") or (fine) [moduli space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moduli_space "Moduli space"). In particular, there is a universal bundle ![Image 39: {\displaystyle \pi \colon E_{n}\to C_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b48e5ca588cf6c0d68b692894eba6e724449d6fb) which is a sub-bundle of the trivial bundle ![Image 40: {\displaystyle C_{n}\times X\to C_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f06956495a0eee7d59684f9c89bf47c32d126184), and which has the property that the fiber over each point ![Image 41: {\displaystyle p\in C_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/954fc4fd68ef6fedd644bbd0e7ad8234340ac4ff) is the _n_ element subset of ![Image 42: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) classified by _p_.

### Homotopy invariance

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Configuration_space_(mathematics)&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Homotopy invariance")]

The homotopy type of configuration spaces is not [homotopy invariant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homotopy_invariant "Homotopy invariant"). For example, the spaces ![Image 43: {\displaystyle \operatorname {Conf} _{n}(\mathbb {R} ^{m})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/55bb059489a1aa88d99429d29ffebbaafedbdb19) are not homotopy equivalent for any two distinct values of ![Image 44: {\displaystyle m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a07d98bb302f3856cbabc47b2b9016692e3f7bc): ![Image 45: {\displaystyle \mathrm {Conf} _{n}(\mathbb {R} ^{0})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/913b4c0c34bd2e2c33b8c3f37a5c5c3d0ab63552) is empty for ![Image 46: {\displaystyle n\geq 2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e6bf67f9d06ca3af619657f8d20ee1322da77174), ![Image 47: {\displaystyle \operatorname {Conf} _{n}(\mathbb {R} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5f0f97169b6896aa385e795f87406d0cab15e99) is not connected for ![Image 48: {\displaystyle n\geq 2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e6bf67f9d06ca3af619657f8d20ee1322da77174), ![Image 49: {\displaystyle \operatorname {Conf} _{n}(\mathbb {R} ^{2})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ee311322cc043f28c5c200291d73b0da2ab0c62e) is an [Eilenberg–MacLane space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eilenberg%E2%80%93MacLane_space "Eilenberg–MacLane space") of type ![Image 50: {\displaystyle K(\pi ,1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/571aec7039bc895070dec4df8aa59aa70e8ce92a), and ![Image 51: {\displaystyle \operatorname {Conf} _{n}(\mathbb {R} ^{m})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/55bb059489a1aa88d99429d29ffebbaafedbdb19) is [simply connected](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simply_connected_space "Simply connected space") for ![Image 52: {\displaystyle m\geq 3}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4610f29d2708d1febf1c0090f58ddd3986593545).

It used to be an open question whether there were examples of _compact_ manifolds which were homotopy equivalent but had non-homotopy equivalent configuration spaces: such an example was found only in 2005 by Riccardo Longoni and Paolo Salvatore. Their example are two three-dimensional [lens spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lens_space "Lens space"), and the configuration spaces of at least two points in them. That these configuration spaces are not homotopy equivalent was detected by [Massey products](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Massey_product "Massey product") in their respective universal covers.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Configuration_space_(mathematics)#cite_note-7) Homotopy invariance for configuration spaces of [simply connected](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simply_connected_space "Simply connected space") closed manifolds remains open in general, and has been proved to hold over the base field ![Image 53: {\displaystyle \mathbf {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5de85fcc2a00d8ba14aae84aeef812d7fef4b3d5).[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Configuration_space_(mathematics)#cite_note-8)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Configuration_space_(mathematics)#cite_note-9) Real homotopy invariance of simply connected compact [manifolds with simply connected boundary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manifold_with_boundary "Manifold with boundary") of dimension at least 4 was also proved.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Configuration_space_(mathematics)#cite_note-10)

Configuration spaces of mechanical linkages
-------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Configuration_space_(mathematics)&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Configuration spaces of mechanical linkages")]

One also defines the configuration space of a mechanical linkage with the graph ![Image 62: {\displaystyle \Gamma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4cfde86a3f7ec967af9955d0988592f0693d2b19) its underlying geometry. Such a graph is commonly assumed to be constructed as concatenation of rigid rods and hinges. The configuration space of such a linkage is defined as the totality of all its admissible positions in the Euclidean space equipped with a proper metric. The configuration space of a generic linkage is a smooth manifold, for example, for the trivial planar linkage made of ![Image 63: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) rigid rods connected with revolute joints, the configuration space is the n-torus ![Image 64: {\displaystyle T^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e7a5182df16f38b52918786915cbaa047bb46a02).[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Configuration_space_(mathematics)#cite_note-15)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Configuration_space_(mathematics)#cite_note-16) The simplest singularity point in such configuration spaces is a product of a cone on a homogeneous quadratic hypersurface by a Euclidean space. Such a singularity point emerges for linkages which can be divided into two sub-linkages such that their respective endpoints trace-paths intersect in a non-transverse manner, for example linkage which can be aligned (i.e. completely be folded into a line).[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Configuration_space_(mathematics)#cite_note-17)

The configuration space ![Image 65: {\displaystyle \operatorname {Conf} _{n}(X)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35b22023740780d93fa0cf090c45d00524bf8084) of distinct points is non-compact, having ends where the points tend to approach each other (become confluent). Many geometric applications require compact spaces, so one would like to [compactify](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compactification_(mathematics) "Compactification (mathematics)")![Image 66: {\displaystyle \operatorname {Conf} _{n}(X)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35b22023740780d93fa0cf090c45d00524bf8084), i.e., embed it as an open subset of a compact space with suitable properties. Approaches to this problem have been given by [Raoul Bott](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raoul_Bott "Raoul Bott") and [Clifford Taubes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_Taubes "Clifford Taubes"),[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Configuration_space_(mathematics)#cite_note-18) as well as [William Fulton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Fulton_(mathematician) "William Fulton (mathematician)") and [Robert MacPherson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_MacPherson_(mathematician) "Robert MacPherson (mathematician)").[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Configuration_space_(mathematics)#cite_note-19)
