Definition of a ruled surface: every point lies on a line

In [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry"), a [surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry_of_surfaces "Differential geometry of surfaces")S in [3-dimensional Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3-dimensional_Euclidean_space "3-dimensional Euclidean space") is **ruled** (also called a **scroll**) if through every [point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_(geometry) "Point (geometry)") of S, there is a [straight line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Straight_line "Straight line") that lies on S. Examples include the [plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_(mathematics) "Plane (mathematics)"), the lateral surface of a [cylinder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylinder_(geometry) "Cylinder (geometry)") or [cone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cone_(geometry) "Cone (geometry)"), a [conical surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conical_surface "Conical surface") with [elliptical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse "Ellipse")[directrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Directrix_(rational_normal_scroll) "Directrix (rational normal scroll)"), the [right conoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_conoid "Right conoid"), the [helicoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helicoid "Helicoid"), and the [tangent developable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_developable "Tangent developable") of a smooth [curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curve "Curve") in space.

A ruled surface can be described as the set of points swept by a moving straight line. For example, a cone is formed by keeping one point of a line fixed whilst moving another point along a [circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle "Circle"). A surface is **doubly ruled** if through every one of its points there are two distinct lines that lie on the surface. The [hyperbolic paraboloid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_paraboloid "Hyperbolic paraboloid") and the [hyperboloid of one sheet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperboloid_of_one_sheet "Hyperboloid of one sheet") are doubly ruled surfaces. The plane is the only surface which contains at least three distinct lines through each of its points ([Fuchs & Tabachnikov 2007](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ruled_surface#CITEREFFuchsTabachnikov2007)).

The properties of being ruled or doubly ruled are preserved by [projective maps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_map "Projective map"), and therefore are concepts of [projective geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_geometry "Projective geometry"). In [algebraic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_geometry "Algebraic geometry"), ruled surfaces are sometimes considered to be surfaces in [affine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_space "Affine space") or [projective space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_space "Projective space") over a [field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)"), but they are also sometimes considered as abstract algebraic surfaces without an [embedding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Embedding "Embedding") into affine or projective space, in which case "straight line" is understood to mean an affine or projective line.

Definition and parametric representation
----------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ruled_surface&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Definition and parametric representation")]

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Bez-regelfl.svg/330px-Bez-regelfl.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Bez-regelfl.svg)

Ruled surface generated by two [Bézier curves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/B%C3%A9zier_curve "Bézier curve") as directrices (red, green)

A [surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry_of_surfaces "Differential geometry of surfaces") in [3-dimensional Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3-dimensional_Euclidean_space "3-dimensional Euclidean space") is called a _ruled surface_ if it is the [union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Union_(set_theory) "Union (set theory)") of a differentiable one-parameter family of lines. Formally, a ruled surface is a surface in ![Image 3: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5) is described by a [parametric representation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parametric_representation "Parametric representation") of the form

![Image 4: {\displaystyle \quad \mathbf {x} (u,v)=\mathbf {c} (u)+v\mathbf {r} (u)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e2bd7d791858351caa963fa7188b73872132aceb)
for ![Image 5: {\displaystyle u}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3e6bb763d22c20916ed4f0bb6bd49d7470cffd8) varying over an interval and ![Image 6: {\displaystyle v}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e07b00e7fc0847fbd16391c778d65bc25c452597) ranging over the reals.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ruled_surface#cite_note-FOOTNOTEdo_Carmo1976188-1) It is required that ![Image 7: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} (u)\neq (0,0,0)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5dec774b51c27728fb474f7c12b9c94d22a31684), and both ![Image 8: {\displaystyle \mathbf {c} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8798d172f59e21f2ce193a3118d4063d19353ded) and ![Image 9: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eca0f46511c4c986c48b254073732c0bd98ae0c1) should be differentiable.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ruled_surface#cite_note-FOOTNOTEdo_Carmo1976188-1)

Any straight line ![Image 10: {\displaystyle v\mapsto \mathbf {x} (u_{0},v)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/870ddd7ed7e85be02bc298d1916f280156681d3d) with fixed parameter ![Image 11: {\displaystyle u=u_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e2ffea3ef298f4e8ccd41b4c77b931c7e9ad356f) is called a _generator_. The vectors ![Image 12: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} (u)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2320c3ce98b9a3b381579f6fd032aba11cb5da60) describe the directions of the generators. The curve ![Image 13: {\displaystyle u\mapsto \mathbf {c} (u)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38eb65492dcee83693b1c6075d773313bc8f9707) is called the _directrix_ of the representation. The directrix may collapse to a point (in case of a cone, see example below).

The ruled surface above may alternatively be described by

![Image 14: {\displaystyle \quad \mathbf {x} (u,v)=(1-v)\mathbf {c} (u)+v\mathbf {d} (u)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8e7220b5b32d28a4973917e22f9c1583cfeced68)
with the second directrix ![Image 15: {\displaystyle \mathbf {d} (u)=\mathbf {c} (u)+\mathbf {r} (u)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/25b55b3c2dc103d29d7369bcebf857f9f8f94cee). To go back to the first description starting with two non intersecting curves ![Image 16: {\displaystyle \mathbf {c} (u),\mathbf {d} (u)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/40cfcdbb29efb7d6f762545cbc1df733c7034842) as directrices, set ![Image 17: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} (u)=\mathbf {d} (u)-\mathbf {c} (u).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/59b975755a1957b68fe9a4e1238018330bf4d3c5)

The geometric shape of the directrices and generators are of course essential to the shape of the ruled surface they produce. However, the specific parametric representations of them also influence the shape of the ruled surface.

Developable surfaces
--------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ruled_surface&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Developable surfaces")]

For the determination of the normal vector at a point one needs the [partial derivatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_derivative "Partial derivative") of the representation ![Image 64: {\displaystyle \mathbf {x} (u,v)=\mathbf {c} (u)+v\mathbf {r} (u)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3f3cb672d0f6537b98ff45c95bbf925be040405c):

Hence the normal vector is

![Image 67: {\displaystyle \mathbf {n} =\mathbf {x} _{u}\times \mathbf {x} _{v}=\mathbf {\dot {c}} \times \mathbf {r} +v(\mathbf {\dot {r}} \times \mathbf {r} ).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/26132d304209e61454649aaa36c6357b8658ab72)

A smooth surface with zero [Gaussian curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaussian_curvature "Gaussian curvature") is called _developable into a plane_, or just _developable_. The determinant condition can be used to prove the following statement:

A ruled surface ![Image 76: {\displaystyle \mathbf {x} (u,v)=\mathbf {c} (u)+v\mathbf {r} (u)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3f3cb672d0f6537b98ff45c95bbf925be040405c) is developable if and only if ![Image 77: {\displaystyle \det(\mathbf {\dot {c}} ,\mathbf {\dot {r}} ,\mathbf {r} )=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d544c7c7a014904bc5d6625f3b682aafb29f768f)at every point.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ruled_surface#cite_note-4)
The generators of any ruled surface coalesce with one family of its asymptotic lines. For developable surfaces they also form one family of its [lines of curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_of_curvature "Line of curvature"). It can be shown that any developable surface is a cone, a cylinder, or a surface formed by all tangents of a space curve.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ruled_surface#cite_note-5)

Ruled surfaces in algebraic geometry
------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ruled_surface&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Ruled surfaces in algebraic geometry")]

In [algebraic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_geometry "Algebraic geometry"), ruled surfaces were originally defined as [projective surfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_surface "Projective surface") in [projective space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_space "Projective space") containing a straight line through any given point. This immediately implies that there is a projective line on the surface through any given point, and this condition is now often used as the definition of a ruled surface: ruled surfaces are defined to be abstract projective surfaces satisfying this condition that there is a projective line through any point. This is equivalent to saying that they are [birational](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Birational "Birational") to the product of a curve and a projective line. Sometimes a ruled surface is defined to be one satisfying the stronger condition that it has a [fibration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fibration "Fibration") over a curve with fibers that are projective lines. This excludes the projective plane, which has a projective line though every point but cannot be written as such a fibration.

Ruled surfaces appear in the [Enriques classification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enriques_classification "Enriques classification") of projective complex surfaces, because every algebraic surface of [Kodaira dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kodaira_dimension "Kodaira dimension")![Image 79: {\displaystyle -\infty }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ca2608c4b5fd3bffc73585f8c67e379b4e99b6f1) is a ruled surface (or a projective plane, if one uses the restrictive definition of ruled surface). Every minimal projective ruled surface other than the projective plane is the projective bundle of a 2-dimensional vector bundle over some curve. The ruled surfaces with base curve of genus 0 are the [Hirzebruch surfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hirzebruch_surface "Hirzebruch surface").

Ruled surfaces in architecture
------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ruled_surface&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Ruled surfaces in architecture")]

Doubly ruled surfaces are the inspiration for curved [hyperboloid structures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperboloid_structure "Hyperboloid structure") that can be built with a [latticework](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latticework "Latticework") of straight elements, namely:

*   Hyperbolic paraboloids, such as [saddle roofs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saddle_roof "Saddle roof").
*   Hyperboloids of one sheet, such as [cooling towers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cooling_tower "Cooling tower") and some [trash bins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Waste_container "Waste container").

The [RM-81 Agena](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RM-81_Agena "RM-81 Agena")[rocket engine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rocket_engine "Rocket engine") employed straight [cooling channels](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cooling_channel&action=edit&redlink=1 "Cooling channel (page does not exist)") that were laid out in a ruled surface to form the throat of the [nozzle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nozzle "Nozzle") section.
