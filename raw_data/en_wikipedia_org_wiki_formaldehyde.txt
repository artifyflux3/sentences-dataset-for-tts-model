**Formaldehyde** ( [_for-MAL-di-hide_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key "Help:Pronunciation respelling key"), [_fər-_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key "Help:Pronunciation respelling key")) ([systematic name](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Preferred_IUPAC_name "Preferred IUPAC name")**methanal**) is an [organic compound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_compound "Organic compound") with the [chemical formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_formula "Chemical formula")CH 2 O and structure H 2 C=O. The compound is a pungent, colourless gas that [polymerises](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polymerization "Polymerization") spontaneously into [paraformaldehyde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paraformaldehyde "Paraformaldehyde"). It is stored as aqueous solutions (**formalin**), which consists mainly of the hydrate CH 2(OH)2. It is the simplest of the [aldehydes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aldehyde "Aldehyde") (R−CHO). As a precursor to many other materials and chemical compounds, in 2006 the global production of formaldehyde was estimated at 12 million tons per year.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-14) It is mainly used in the production of industrial [resins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resin "Resin"), e.g., for [particle board](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_board "Particle board") and [coatings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coating "Coating").

Formaldehyde also occurs naturally. It is derived from the degradation of [serine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Serine "Serine"), [dimethylglycine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimethylglycine "Dimethylglycine"), and [lipids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lipid "Lipid"). [Demethylases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Demethylase "Demethylase") act by converting N-methyl groups to formaldehyde.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-15)

Formaldehyde is classified as a group 1 [carcinogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carcinogen "Carcinogen")[[note 1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-carcinogen-17)[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-18) and can cause respiratory and skin [irritation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irritation "Irritation") upon exposure.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-cancer.gov-16)

Formaldehyde is more complicated than many simple carbon compounds in that it adopts several diverse forms. These compounds can often be used interchangeably and can be interconverted.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

*   Molecular formaldehyde. A colorless gas with a characteristic pungent, irritating odor. It is stable at about 150°C, but it polymerizes when condensed to a liquid.
*   [1,3,5-Trioxane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1,3,5-Trioxane "1,3,5-Trioxane"), with the formula (CH 2 O)3. It is a white solid that dissolves without degradation in organic solvents. It is a [trimer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trimer_(chemistry) "Trimer (chemistry)") of molecular formaldehyde.
*   [Paraformaldehyde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paraformaldehyde "Paraformaldehyde"), with the formula HO(CH 2 O)n H. It is a white solid that is insoluble in most solvents.
*   [Methanediol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanediol "Methanediol"), with the formula CH 2(OH)2. This compound also exists in equilibrium with various [oligomers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oligomer "Oligomer") (short polymers), depending on the concentration and temperature. A saturated water solution, of about 40% formaldehyde by volume or 37% by mass, is called "100% formalin".

A small amount of [stabilizer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stabilizer_(chemistry) "Stabilizer (chemistry)"), such as [methanol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol "Methanol"), is usually added to suppress [oxidation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxidation "Oxidation") and [polymerization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polymerization "Polymerization"). A typical commercial-grade formalin may contain 10–12% methanol in addition to various metallic impurities.

"Formaldehyde" was first used as a [generic trademark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generic_trademark "Generic trademark") in 1893 following a previous trade name, "formalin".[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-19)

### Structure and bonding

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Formaldehyde&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Structure and bonding")]

Molecular formaldehyde contains a central carbon atom with a [double bond to the oxygen atom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonyl_group "Carbonyl group") and a [single bond to each hydrogen atom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon%E2%80%93hydrogen_bond "Carbon–hydrogen bond"). This structure is summarised by the [condensed formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Structural_formula#Condensed_formulas "Structural formula") H 2 C=O.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-Wells-20) The molecule is planar, Y-shaped and its [molecular symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_symmetry "Molecular symmetry") belongs to the [_C_ 2v point group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_symmetry#Common_point_groups "Molecular symmetry").[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-21) The precise [molecular geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_geometry "Molecular geometry") of gaseous formaldehyde has been determined by [gas electron diffraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas_electron_diffraction "Gas electron diffraction")[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-Wells-20)[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-CKIM-22) and [microwave spectroscopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microwave_spectroscopy "Microwave spectroscopy").[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-CRC-23)[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-Duncan-24) The [bond lengths](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bond_length "Bond length") are 1.21 [Å](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%85ngstrom "Ångstrom") for the [carbon–oxygen and around 1.11 Å for the [carbon–hydrogen bond](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon%E2%80%93hydrogen_bond "Carbon–hydrogen while the H–C–H [bond angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bond_angle "Bond angle") is planar molecule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trigonal_planar_molecular_geometry "Trigonal planar molecular geometry").[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-Wells-20) Some [excited electronic states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Excited_state "Excited state") of formaldehyde are [pyramidal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trigonal_pyramidal_molecular_geometry "Trigonal pyramidal molecular geometry") rather than planar as in the [ground state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ground_state "Ground state").[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-March-25)

Processes in the upper [atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere "Earth's atmosphere") contribute more than 80% of the total formaldehyde in the environment.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-26) Formaldehyde is an intermediate in the oxidation (or [combustion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Combustion "Combustion")) of [methane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane "Methane"), as well as of other carbon compounds, e.g. in [forest fires](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forest_fire "Forest fire"), [automobile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Automobile "Automobile") exhaust, and [tobacco smoke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tobacco_smoke "Tobacco smoke"). When produced in the atmosphere by the action of sunlight and [oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen "Oxygen") on [atmospheric methane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmospheric_methane "Atmospheric methane") and other [hydrocarbons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrocarbon "Hydrocarbon"), it becomes part of [smog](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smog "Smog"). Formaldehyde has also been detected in outer space.

Formaldehyde and its [adducts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adducts "Adducts") are ubiquitous in nature. Food may contain formaldehyde at levels 1–100 mg/kg.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-27) Formaldehyde, formed in the metabolism of the amino acids [serine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Serine "Serine") and [threonine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Threonine "Threonine"), is found in the bloodstream of humans and other primates at concentrations of approximately 50[micromolar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Micromolar "Micromolar").[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-Chang-28) Experiments in which animals are exposed to an atmosphere containing isotopically labeled formaldehyde have demonstrated that even in deliberately exposed animals, the majority of formaldehyde-DNA adducts found in non-respiratory tissues are derived from endogenously produced formaldehyde.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-29)

Formaldehyde does not accumulate in the environment, because it is broken down within a few hours by sunlight or by bacteria present in soil or water. Humans metabolize formaldehyde quickly, converting it to [formic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid "Formic acid").[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-30)[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-31) It nonetheless presents [significant health concerns](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#Safety), as a [contaminant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contaminant "Contaminant").

### Interstellar formaldehyde

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Formaldehyde&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Interstellar formaldehyde")]

Formaldehyde appears to be a useful probe in astrochemistry due to prominence of the 1 10←1 11 and 2 11←2 12 _K_-doublet transitions. It was the first polyatomic [organic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_chemistry "Organic chemistry") molecule detected in the [interstellar medium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_medium "Interstellar medium").[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-32) Since its initial detection in 1969, it has been observed in many regions of the [galaxy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milky_Way_galaxy "Milky Way galaxy"). Because of the widespread interest in interstellar formaldehyde, it has been extensively studied, yielding new extragalactic sources.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-mangum-33) A proposed mechanism for the formation is the hydrogenation of CO ice:[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-Woon-34)

H + CO → HCO HCO + H → CH 2 O
[HCN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide "Hydrogen cyanide"), [HNC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_isocyanide "Hydrogen isocyanide"), H 2 CO, and [dust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dust "Dust") have also been observed inside the [comae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coma_(cometary) "Coma (cometary)") of [comets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comet "Comet")[C/2012 F6 (Lemmon)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C/2012_F6_(Lemmon) "C/2012 F6 (Lemmon)") and [C/2012 S1 (ISON)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comet_ISON "Comet ISON").[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-NASA-20140811-35)[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-AJL-20140811-36)

Synthesis and industrial production
-----------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Formaldehyde&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Synthesis and industrial production")]

### Laboratory synthesis

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Formaldehyde&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Laboratory synthesis")]

Formaldehyde was discovered in 1859 by the Russian chemist [Aleksandr Butlerov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aleksandr_Butlerov "Aleksandr Butlerov") (1828–1886) when he attempted to synthesize methanediol ("methylene glycol") from [iodomethane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iodomethane "Iodomethane") and [silver oxalate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silver_oxalate "Silver oxalate").[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-37) In his paper, Butlerov referred to formaldehyde as "dioxymethylen" (methylene dioxide) because his empirical formula for it was incorrect, as atomic weights were not precisely determined until the [Karlsruhe Congress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karlsruhe_Congress "Karlsruhe Congress").

The compound was identified as an aldehyde by [August Wilhelm von Hofmann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/August_Wilhelm_von_Hofmann "August Wilhelm von Hofmann"), who first announced its production by passing methanol vapor in air over hot platinum wire.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-38)[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-39) With modifications, Hofmann's method remains the basis of the present day industrial route.

Solution routes to formaldehyde also entail oxidation of methanol or [iodomethane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iodomethane "Iodomethane").[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-40)

Formaldehyde is produced industrially by the catalytic oxidation of [methanol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol "Methanol"). The most common catalysts are [silver](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silver "Silver") metal (i.e. the [FASIL process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fasil_process "Fasil process")), [iron(III) oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron(III)_oxide "Iron(III) oxide"),[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-41) iron molybdenum [oxides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxide "Oxide") (e.g. iron(III) [molybdate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molybdate "Molybdate")) with a [molybdenum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molybdenum "Molybdenum")-enriched surface,[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-42) or [vanadium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vanadium_pentoxide "Vanadium pentoxide")[oxides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxide "Oxide"). In the commonly used [formox process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formox_process "Formox process"), methanol and oxygen react at c.250–400°C in presence of iron oxide in combination with molybdenum and/or vanadium to produce formaldehyde according to the [chemical equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_equation "Chemical equation"):[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-Ullmann-43)

2 CH 3 OH + O 2 → 2 CH 2 O + 2 H 2 O
The silver-based catalyst usually operates at a higher temperature, about 650°C. Two chemical reactions on it simultaneously produce formaldehyde: that shown above and the [dehydrogenation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dehydrogenation "Dehydrogenation") reaction:

CH 3 OH → CH 2 O + H 2
In principle, formaldehyde could be generated by oxidation of [methane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane "Methane"), but this route is not industrially viable because the methanol is more easily oxidized than methane.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-Ullmann-43)

Formaldehyde is produced via several enzyme-catalyzed routes.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-Kamps-44) Living beings, including humans, produce formaldehyde as part of their metabolism. Formaldehyde is key to several bodily functions (e.g. [epigenetics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epigenetic "Epigenetic")[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-Chang-28)), but its amount must also be tightly controlled to avoid self-poisoning.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-45)

*   [Serine hydroxymethyltransferase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Serine_hydroxymethyltransferase "Serine hydroxymethyltransferase") can decompose [serine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Serine "Serine") into formaldehyde and [glycine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine "Glycine"), according to this reaction: HOCH 2 CH(NH 2)CO 2 H → CH 2 O + H 2 C(NH 2)CO 2 H.
*   [Methylotrophic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methylotroph "Methylotroph") microbes convert methanol into formaldehyde and energy via [methanol dehydrogenase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol_dehydrogenase "Methanol dehydrogenase"): CH 3 OH → CH 2 O + 2e− + 2H+
*   Other routes to formaldehyde include oxidative [demethylations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Demethylation "Demethylation"), [semicarbazide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semicarbazide "Semicarbazide")-sensitive [amine oxidases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amine_oxidase "Amine oxidase"), [dimethylglycine dehydrogenases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimethylglycine_dehydrogenase "Dimethylglycine dehydrogenase"), [lipid peroxidases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lipid_peroxidase "Lipid peroxidase"), [P450 oxidases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/P450_oxidase "P450 oxidase"), and _N_-methyl group demethylases.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-Kamps-44)

Formaldehyde is catabolized by [alcohol dehydrogenase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alcohol_dehydrogenase "Alcohol dehydrogenase")[ADH5](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ADH5 "ADH5") and [aldehyde dehydrogenase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aldehyde_dehydrogenase "Aldehyde dehydrogenase")[ALDH2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ALDH2 "ALDH2").[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-46)

Formaldehyde is a building block in the synthesis of many other compounds of specialised and industrial significance. It exhibits most of the chemical properties of other aldehydes but is more reactive.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-47)

### Polymerization and hydration

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Formaldehyde&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Polymerization and hydration")]

Monomeric CH 2 O is a gas and is rarely encountered in the laboratory. Aqueous formaldehyde, unlike some other small aldehydes (which need specific conditions to oligomerize through [aldol condensation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aldol_condensation "Aldol condensation")) oligomerizes spontaneously at a common state. The trimer 1,3,5-trioxane, (CH 2 O)3, is a typical oligomer. Many cyclic [oligomers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oligomer "Oligomer") of other sizes have been isolated. Similarly, formaldehyde hydrates to give the geminal diol [methanediol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanediol "Methanediol"), which condenses further to form hydroxy-terminated oligomers HO(CH 2 O)_n_ H. The polymer is called [paraformaldehyde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paraformaldehyde "Paraformaldehyde"). The higher concentration of formaldehyde—the more equilibrium shifts towards polymerization. Diluting with water or increasing the solution temperature, as well as adding alcohols (such as methanol or ethanol) lowers that tendency.

Gaseous formaldehyde polymerizes at active sites on vessel walls, but the mechanism of the reaction is unknown.[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-48) Small amounts of [hydrogen chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_chloride "Hydrogen chloride"), [boron trifluoride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boron_trifluoride "Boron trifluoride"), or [stannic chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stannic_chloride "Stannic chloride") present in gaseous formaldehyde provide the catalytic effect and make the polymerization rapid.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-49)

Formaldehyde forms cross-links by first combining with a protein to form [methylol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methylol "Methylol"), which loses a water molecule to form a [Schiff base](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schiff_base "Schiff base").[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-pmid26354429-50) The Schiff base can then react with DNA or protein to create a cross-linked product.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-pmid26354429-50) This reaction is the basis for the most common process of [chemical fixation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fixation_(histology)#Chemical_fixation "Fixation (histology)").

### Oxidation and reduction

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Formaldehyde&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Oxidation and reduction")]

Formaldehyde is readily [oxidized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxidation "Oxidation") by atmospheric oxygen into [formic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid "Formic acid"). For this reason, commercial formaldehyde is typically contaminated with formic acid. Formaldehyde can be hydrogenated into [methanol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol "Methanol").

In the [Cannizzaro reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cannizzaro_reaction "Cannizzaro reaction"), formaldehyde and [base](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Base_(chemistry) "Base (chemistry)") react to produce [formic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid "Formic acid") and methanol, a [disproportionation reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disproportionation_reaction "Disproportionation reaction").

### Hydroxymethylation and chloromethylation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Formaldehyde&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Hydroxymethylation and chloromethylation")]

Formaldehyde reacts with many compounds, resulting in [hydroxymethylation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroxymethylation "Hydroxymethylation"):

X-H + CH 2 O → X-CH 2 OH(X = R 2 N, RC(O)NR', SH).
The resulting hydroxymethyl derivatives typically react further. Thus, amines give [hexahydro-1,3,5-triazines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hexahydro-1,3,5-triazine "Hexahydro-1,3,5-triazine"):

3 RNH 2 + 3 CH 2 O → (RNCH 2)3 + 3 H 2 O
Similarly, when combined with [hydrogen sulfide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide "Hydrogen sulfide"), it forms [trithiane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trithiane "Trithiane"):[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-51)

3 CH 2 O + 3 H 2 S → (CH 2 S)3 + 3 H 2 O
In the presence of acids, it participates in [electrophilic aromatic substitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrophilic_aromatic_substitution "Electrophilic aromatic substitution") reactions with [aromatic compounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aromatic_compound "Aromatic compound") resulting in hydroxymethylated derivatives:

ArH + CH 2 O → ArCH 2 OH
When conducted in the presence of hydrogen chloride, the product is the chloromethyl compound, as described in the [Blanc chloromethylation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blanc_chloromethylation "Blanc chloromethylation"). If the arene is electron-rich, as in phenols, elaborate condensations ensue. With 4-substituted phenols one obtains [calixarenes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calixarenes "Calixarenes").[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-52) Phenol results in polymers.

Many amino acids react with formaldehyde.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-Kamps-44) Cysteine converts to [thioproline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thioproline "Thioproline").

### Industrial applications

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Formaldehyde&action=edit&section=16 "Edit section: Industrial applications")]

Formaldehyde is a common precursor to more complex compounds and materials. In approximate order of decreasing consumption, products generated from formaldehyde include [urea formaldehyde resin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urea_formaldehyde_resin "Urea formaldehyde resin"), [melamine resin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Melamine_resin "Melamine resin"), [phenol formaldehyde resin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phenol_formaldehyde_resin "Phenol formaldehyde resin"), [polyoxymethylene plastics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyoxymethylene_plastic "Polyoxymethylene plastic"), [1,4-butanediol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1,4-butanediol "1,4-butanediol"), and [methylene diphenyl diisocyanate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methylene_diphenyl_diisocyanate "Methylene diphenyl diisocyanate").[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-Ullmann-43) The [textile industry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Textile_industry "Textile industry") uses formaldehyde-based resins as [finishers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finishing_(textiles) "Finishing (textiles)") to make fabrics crease-resistant.[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-53)

[![Image 26](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/UFresinSyn.svg/500px-UFresinSyn.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:UFresinSyn.svg)

Two steps in formation of urea-formaldehyde resin, which is widely used in the production of particle board

When condensed with [phenol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phenol "Phenol"), [urea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urea "Urea"), or [melamine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Melamine "Melamine"), formaldehyde produces, respectively, hard thermoset phenol formaldehyde resin, urea formaldehyde resin, and melamine resin. These polymers are permanent adhesives used in [plywood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plywood "Plywood") and [carpeting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carpet "Carpet"). They are also foamed to make [insulation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermal_insulation "Thermal insulation"), or [cast](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Casting "Casting") into moulded products. Production of formaldehyde resins accounts for more than half of formaldehyde consumption.

Formaldehyde is also a precursor to polyfunctional [alcohols](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alcohol_(chemistry) "Alcohol (chemistry)") such as [pentaerythritol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentaerythritol "Pentaerythritol"), which is used to make [paints](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paint "Paint") and [explosives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Explosive "Explosive"). Other formaldehyde derivatives include methylene diphenyl diisocyanate, an important component in [polyurethane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyurethane "Polyurethane") paints and foams, and [hexamine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hexamine "Hexamine"), which is used in phenol-formaldehyde resins as well as the explosive [RDX](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RDX "RDX").

Condensation with acetaldehyde affords [pentaerythritol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentaerythritol "Pentaerythritol"), a chemical necessary in synthesizing [PETN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PETN "PETN"), a high explosive:[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-54)

[![Image 27](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Pentaerythritol_Synthesis.svg/600px-Pentaerythritol_Synthesis.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Pentaerythritol_Synthesis.svg)

#### Disinfectant and biocide

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Formaldehyde&action=edit&section=18 "Edit section: Disinfectant and biocide")]

An aqueous solution of formaldehyde can be useful as a disinfectant as it kills most [bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacteria "Bacteria") and fungi (including their spores). It is used as an additive in vaccine manufacturing to inactivate toxins and pathogens.[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-55)[Formaldehyde releasers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde_releaser "Formaldehyde releaser") are used as biocides in personal care products such as cosmetics. Although present at levels not normally considered harmful, they are known to cause allergic [contact dermatitis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contact_dermatitis "Contact dermatitis") in certain sensitised individuals.[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-Formaldehyde_Releasers-56)

Aquarists use formaldehyde as a treatment for the parasites _[Ichthyophthirius multifiliis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ichthyophthirius\_multifiliis "Ichthyophthirius multifiliis")_ and _[Cryptocaryon irritans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cryptocaryon\_irritans "Cryptocaryon irritans")_.[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-57) Formaldehyde is one of the main disinfectants recommended for destroying [anthrax](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anthrax "Anthrax").[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-58)

Formaldehyde is also approved for use in the manufacture of animal feeds in the US. It is an antimicrobial agent used to maintain complete animal feeds or feed ingredients _Salmonella_ negative for up to 21 days.[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-59)

#### Tissue fixative and embalming agent

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Formaldehyde&action=edit&section=19 "Edit section: Tissue fixative and embalming agent")]

[![Image 28](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Steve_O%27Shea_injecting_formalin.jpg/250px-Steve_O%27Shea_injecting_formalin.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Steve_O%27Shea_injecting_formalin.jpg)

Injecting a [giant squid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giant_squid "Giant squid") specimen with formalin for preservation

Formaldehyde preserves or [fixes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fixation_(histology) "Fixation (histology)") tissue or cells. The process involves [cross-linking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross-link "Cross-link") of primary [amino groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amino_group "Amino group"). The European Union has banned the use of formaldehyde as a [biocide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biocide "Biocide") (including [embalming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Embalming "Embalming")) under the [Biocidal Products Directive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biocidal_Products_Directive "Biocidal Products Directive") (98/8/EC) due to its carcinogenic properties.[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-60)[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-61) Countries with a strong tradition of embalming corpses, such as Ireland and other colder-weather countries, have raised concerns. Despite reports to the contrary,[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-62) no decision on the inclusion of formaldehyde on Annex I of the Biocidal Products Directive for product-type 22 (embalming and taxidermist fluids) had been made as of September 2009.[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-63)

Formaldehyde-based crosslinking is exploited in [ChIP-on-chip](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ChIP-on-chip "ChIP-on-chip") or [ChIP-sequencing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ChIP-sequencing "ChIP-sequencing") genomics experiments, where DNA-binding proteins are cross-linked to their cognate binding sites on the chromosome and analyzed to determine what genes are regulated by the proteins. Formaldehyde is also used as a denaturing agent in [RNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RNA "RNA") gel [electrophoresis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrophoresis "Electrophoresis"), preventing RNA from forming secondary structures. A solution of 4% formaldehyde fixes pathology tissue specimens at about one mm per hour at room temperature.

Formaldehyde and 18[M](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concentration#Molality "Concentration") (concentrated) [sulfuric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfuric_acid "Sulfuric acid") makes [Marquis reagent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marquis_reagent "Marquis reagent")—which can identify [alkaloids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkaloid "Alkaloid") and other compounds.

In photography, formaldehyde is used in low concentrations for the process [C-41](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C-41_process "C-41 process") (color negative film) stabilizer in the final wash step,[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-64) as well as in the [process E-6](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/E-6_process "E-6 process") pre-bleach step, to make it unnecessary in the final wash. Due to improvements in dye coupler chemistry, more modern (2006 or later) E-6 and C-41 films do not need formaldehyde, as their dyes are already stable.

In view of its widespread use, toxicity, and volatility, formaldehyde poses a significant danger to human health.[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-IARC-65)[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-66) In 2011, the US [National Toxicology Program](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Toxicology_Program "National Toxicology Program") described formaldehyde as "known to be a human carcinogen".[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-Harris-67)[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-12Report2011-68)[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-roc2011-69)

Concerns are associated with chronic (long-term) exposure by inhalation as may happen from [thermal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermal_decomposition "Thermal decomposition") or [chemical decomposition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_decomposition "Chemical decomposition") of formaldehyde-based resins and the production of formaldehyde resulting from the [combustion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Combustion "Combustion") of a variety of organic compounds (for example, exhaust gases). As formaldehyde resins are used in many [construction materials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Construction_materials "Construction materials"),[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-70) it is one of the more common [indoor air pollutants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indoor_air_quality "Indoor air quality").[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-71)[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-72) At concentrations above 0.1 ppm in air, formaldehyde can irritate the eyes and [mucous membranes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mucous_membrane "Mucous membrane").[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-73) Formaldehyde inhaled at this concentration may cause headaches, a burning sensation in the throat, and difficulty breathing, and can trigger or aggravate asthma symptoms.[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-74)[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-75)

The [CDC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centers_for_Disease_Control_and_Prevention "Centers for Disease Control and Prevention") considers formaldehyde as a systemic poison. Formaldehyde poisoning can cause permanent changes in the [nervous system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nervous_system "Nervous system")'s functions.[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-cdc1-76)

A 1988 Canadian study of houses with [urea-formaldehyde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urea-formaldehyde "Urea-formaldehyde") foam insulation found that formaldehyde levels as low as 0.046 ppm were positively correlated with eye and nasal irritation.[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-77) A 2009 review of studies has shown a strong association between exposure to formaldehyde and the development of childhood [asthma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asthma "Asthma").[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-78)

A theory was proposed for the carcinogenesis of formaldehyde in 1978.[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-79) In 1987 the [United States Environmental Protection Agency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency "United States Environmental Protection Agency") (EPA) classified it as a _probable human carcinogen_, and after more studies the [WHO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WHO "WHO")[International Agency for Research on Cancer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Agency_for_Research_on_Cancer "International Agency for Research on Cancer") (IARC) in 1995 also classified it as a _probable human carcinogen_. Further information and evaluation of all known data led the IARC to reclassify formaldehyde as a _known human carcinogen_[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-International_Agency_for_Research_on_Cancer,_Monographs_Vol_88-80) associated with nasal sinus cancer and [nasopharyngeal cancer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nasopharyngeal_cancer "Nasopharyngeal cancer").[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-NCI-81) Studies in 2009 and 2010 have also shown a positive correlation between exposure to formaldehyde and the development of [leukemia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leukemia "Leukemia"), particularly [myeloid leukemia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Myeloid_leukemia "Myeloid leukemia").[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-82)[[82]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-83) Nasopharyngeal and sinonasal cancers are relatively rare, with a combined annual incidence in the United States of < 4,000 cases.[[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-84)[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-85) About 30,000 cases of myeloid leukemia occur in the United States each year.[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-86)[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-87) Some evidence suggests that workplace exposure to formaldehyde contributes to sinonasal cancers.[[87]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-cancer.org-88) Professionals exposed to formaldehyde in their occupation, such as funeral industry workers and [embalmers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Embalming "Embalming"), showed an increased risk of leukemia and brain cancer compared with the general population.[[88]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-89) Other factors are important in determining individual risk for the development of leukemia or nasopharyngeal cancer.[[87]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-cancer.org-88)[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-90)[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-91) In yeast, formaldehyde is found to perturb pathways for DNA repair and cell cycle.[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-92)

In the residential environment, formaldehyde exposure comes from a number of routes; formaldehyde can be emitted by treated [wood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wood "Wood") products, such as [plywood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plywood "Plywood") or [particle board](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_board "Particle board"), but it is produced by paints, [varnishes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Varnishes "Varnishes"), floor finishes, and [cigarette](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cigarette "Cigarette") smoking as well.[[92]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-93) In July 2016, the U.S. EPA released a prepublication version of its final rule on Formaldehyde Emission Standards for Composite Wood Products.[[93]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-94) These new rules impact manufacturers, importers, distributors, and retailers of products containing composite wood, including fiberboard, particleboard, and various laminated products, who must comply with more stringent record-keeping and labeling requirements.[[94]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-95)

The U.S. EPA allows no more than 0.016 ppm formaldehyde in the air in new buildings constructed for that agency.[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-96)[_[failed verification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability "Wikipedia:Verifiability")_] A U.S. EPA study found a new home measured 0.076 ppm when brand new and 0.045 ppm after 30 days.[[96]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-97) The [Federal Emergency Management Agency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federal_Emergency_Management_Agency "Federal Emergency Management Agency") (FEMA) has also announced limits on the formaldehyde levels in trailers purchased by that agency.[[97]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-98) The EPA recommends the use of "exterior-grade" pressed-wood products with phenol instead of urea resin to limit formaldehyde exposure, since pressed-wood products containing formaldehyde resins are often a significant source of formaldehyde in homes.[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-NCI-81)

The eyes are most sensitive to formaldehyde exposure: The lowest level at which many people can begin to smell formaldehyde ranges between 0.05 and 1 ppm. The maximum concentration value at the workplace is 0.3 ppm.[[98]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-99)[_[need quotation to verify](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability "Wikipedia:Verifiability")_] In controlled chamber studies, individuals begin to sense eye irritation at about 0.5 ppm; 5 to 20 percent report eye irritation at 0.5 to 1 ppm; and greater certainty for sensory irritation occurred at 1 ppm and above. While some agencies have used a level as low as 0.1 ppm as a threshold for irritation, the expert panel found that a level of 0.3 ppm would protect against nearly all irritation. In fact, the expert panel found that a level of 1.0 ppm would avoid eye irritation—the most sensitive endpoint—in 75–95% of all people exposed.[[99]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-100)

[![Image 31](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Rowenta_Intense_Pure_Air_air_purifier.jpg/250px-Rowenta_Intense_Pure_Air_air_purifier.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Rowenta_Intense_Pure_Air_air_purifier.jpg)

Some [air purifiers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Air_purifier "Air purifier") include filtering technology that is supposed to lower indoor formaldehyde concentration.

Formaldehyde levels in building environments are affected by a number of factors. These include the potency of formaldehyde-emitting products present, the ratio of the surface area of emitting materials to volume of space, environmental factors, product age, interactions with other materials, and ventilation conditions. Formaldehyde emits from a variety of construction materials, furnishings, and consumer products. The three products that emit the highest concentrations are [medium density fiberboard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medium_density_fiberboard "Medium density fiberboard"), hardwood plywood, and particle board. Environmental factors such as temperature and relative humidity can elevate levels because formaldehyde has a high [vapor pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vapor_pressure "Vapor pressure"). Formaldehyde levels from building materials are the highest when a building first opens because materials would have less time to off-gas. Formaldehyde levels decrease over time as the sources suppress.

In [operating rooms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operating_rooms "Operating rooms"), formaldehyde is produced as a byproduct of electrosurgery and is present in [surgical smoke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surgical_smoke "Surgical smoke"), exposing surgeons and healthcare workers to potentially unsafe concentrations.[[100]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-101)

Formaldehyde levels in air can be sampled and tested in several ways, including impinger, treated sorbent, and passive monitors.[[101]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-102) The [National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Institute_for_Occupational_Safety_and_Health "National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health") (NIOSH) has measurement methods numbered 2016, 2541, 3500, and 3800.[[102]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-103)

In June 2011, the twelfth edition of the [National Toxicology Program](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Toxicology_Program "National Toxicology Program") (NTP) Report on Carcinogens (RoC) changed the listing status of formaldehyde from "reasonably anticipated to be a human carcinogen" to "known to be a human carcinogen."[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-Harris-67)[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-12Report2011-68)[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-roc2011-69) Concurrently, a [National Academy of Sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_National_Academy_of_Sciences "United States National Academy of Sciences") (NAS) committee was convened and issued an independent review of the draft U.S. [EPA IRIS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/EPA_IRIS "EPA IRIS") assessment of formaldehyde, providing a comprehensive health effects assessment and quantitative estimates of human risks of adverse effects.[[103]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-104)

### Acute irritation and allergic reaction

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Formaldehyde&action=edit&section=24 "Edit section: Acute irritation and allergic reaction")]

[![Image 32](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Epikutanni-test.jpg/250px-Epikutanni-test.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Epikutanni-test.jpg)

[Patch test](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patch_test "Patch test")

For most people, irritation from formaldehyde is temporary and reversible, although formaldehyde can cause allergies and is part of the standard patch test series. In 2005–06, it was the seventh-most-prevalent [allergen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allergen "Allergen") in [patch tests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patch_test "Patch test") (9.0%).[[104]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-105) People with formaldehyde allergy are advised to avoid [formaldehyde releasers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde_releasers "Formaldehyde releasers") as well (e.g., [Quaternium-15](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quaternium-15 "Quaternium-15"), [imidazolidinyl urea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imidazolidinyl_urea "Imidazolidinyl urea"), and [diazolidinyl urea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diazolidinyl_urea "Diazolidinyl urea")).[[105]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-106) People who suffer allergic reactions to formaldehyde tend to display lesions on the skin in the areas that have had direct contact with the substance, such as the neck or thighs (often due to formaldehyde released from permanent press finished clothing) or [dermatitis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dermatitis "Dermatitis") on the face (typically from cosmetics).[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-Formaldehyde_Releasers-56) Formaldehyde has been banned in cosmetics in both [Sweden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sweden "Sweden")[[106]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-107) and [Japan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japan "Japan").[[107]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-108)

In humans, ingestion of as little as 30 millilitres (1.0 US fl oz) of 37% formaldehyde solution can cause death. Other symptoms associated with ingesting such a solution include gastrointestinal damage (vomiting, abdominal pain), and systematic damage (dizziness).[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-cdc1-76) Testing for formaldehyde is by blood and/or urine by [gas chromatography–mass spectrometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas_chromatography%E2%80%93mass_spectrometry "Gas chromatography–mass spectrometry"). Other methods to detect formaldehyde include infrared detection, gas detector tubes, gas detectors using electrochemical sensors, and [high-performance liquid chromatography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-performance_liquid_chromatography "High-performance liquid chromatography") (HPLC). HPLC is the most sensitive.[[108]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-109)

The fifteenth edition (2021) of the U.S. National Toxicology Program Report on Carcinogens notes that currently in the U.S. “The general population can be exposed to formaldehyde primarily from breathing indoor or outdoor air, from tobacco smoke, from use of cosmetic products containing formaldehyde, and, to a more limited extent, from ingestion of food and water.” Affected water includes groundwater, surface water, and bottled water. It also notes that occupational exposure can be significant. [[109]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-auto-110)

Contaminant in food
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Formaldehyde&action=edit&section=26 "Edit section: Contaminant in food")]

Formaldehyde in food can be present naturally, added as an inadvertent contaminant, or intentionally added as a preservative, disinfectant, or [bacteriostatic agent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacteriostatic_agent "Bacteriostatic agent"). Cooking and smoking food can also result in formaldehyde being produced in food. Foods that the [U.S. National Toxicology Program](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U.S._National_Toxicology_Program "U.S. National Toxicology Program") has reported to have higher levels compared to other foods are fish, seafood, and smoked ham. It also notes formaldehyde in food generally occurs in a bound form and that formaldehyde is unstable in an [aqueous solution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aqueous_solution "Aqueous solution"). [[109]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-auto-110)

Scandals have broken in both the [2005 Indonesia food scare](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2005_Indonesia_food_scare "2005 Indonesia food scare") and [2007 Vietnam food scare](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2007_Vietnam_food_scare "2007 Vietnam food scare") regarding the addition of formaldehyde to foods to extend shelf life. In 2011, after a four-year absence, Indonesian authorities found foods with formaldehyde being sold in markets in a number of regions across the country.[[110]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-111) In August 2011, at least at two [Carrefour](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carrefour "Carrefour") supermarkets, the Central [Jakarta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jakarta "Jakarta") Livestock and Fishery Sub-Department found [cendol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cendol "Cendol") containing 10 [parts per million](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parts_per_million "Parts per million") of formaldehyde.[[111]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-112) In 2014, the owner of two noodle factories in [Bogor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bogor "Bogor"), Indonesia, was arrested for using formaldehyde in noodles.[[112]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-113) Foods known to be contaminated included noodles, salted fish, and tofu. Chicken and beer were also rumored to be contaminated. In some places, such as China, manufacturers still use formaldehyde illegally as a preservative in foods, which exposes people to formaldehyde ingestion.[[113]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-114)

In 2011 in [Nakhon Ratchasima](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nakhon_Ratchasima "Nakhon Ratchasima"), Thailand, truckloads of rotten chicken were treated with formaldehyde for sale in which "a large network", including 11 slaughterhouses run by a criminal gang, were implicated.[[114]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-115) In 2012, 1 billion rupiah (almost US$100,000) of fish imported from [Pakistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pakistan "Pakistan") to [Batam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Batam "Batam"), Indonesia, were found laced with formaldehyde.[[115]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-116)

Formalin contamination of foods has been reported in [Bangladesh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bangladesh "Bangladesh"), with stores and supermarkets selling fruits, fishes, and vegetables that have been treated with formalin to keep them fresh.[[116]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-117) However, in 2015, a _Formalin Control Bill_ was passed in the [Parliament of Bangladesh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jatiyo_Sangshad "Jatiyo Sangshad") with a provision of life-term imprisonment as the maximum punishment as well as a maximum fine of 2,000,000 [BDT](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bangladeshi_taka "Bangladeshi taka") but not less than 500,000 [BDT](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bangladeshi_taka "Bangladeshi taka") for importing, producing, or hoarding formalin without a license.[[117]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-118)

In the early 1900s, formaldehyde was frequently added by US milk plants to milk bottles as a method of pasteurization due to the lack of knowledge and concern[[118]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-119) regarding formaldehyde's toxicity.[[119]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-120)[[120]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-121)

Formaldehyde was one of the chemicals used in 19th century industrialised food production that was investigated by Dr. [Harvey W. Wiley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harvey_W._Wiley "Harvey W. Wiley") with his famous 'Poison Squad' as part of the [US Department of Agriculture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/US_Department_of_Agriculture "US Department of Agriculture"). This led to the 1906 [Pure Food and Drug Act](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pure_Food_and_Drug_Act "Pure Food and Drug Act"), a landmark event in the [early history of food regulation in the United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Early_history_of_food_regulation_in_the_United_States "Early history of food regulation in the United States").[[121]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-122)

Formaldehyde is banned from use in certain applications (preservatives for liquid-cooling and processing systems, [slimicides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slimicide "Slimicide"), metalworking-fluid preservatives, and antifouling products) under the Biocidal Products Directive.[[122]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-123)[[123]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-124) In the EU, the maximum allowed concentration of formaldehyde in finished products is 0.2%, and any product that exceeds 0.05% has to include a warning that the product contains formaldehyde.[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-Formaldehyde_Releasers-56)

In the United States, [Congress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Congress "United States Congress") passed a bill July 7, 2010, regarding the use of formaldehyde in hardwood [plywood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plywood "Plywood"), [particle board](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_board "Particle board"), and [medium density fiberboard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medium_density_fiberboard "Medium density fiberboard"). The bill limited the allowable amount of formaldehyde emissions from these wood products to 0.09 ppm, and required companies to meet this standard by January 2013.[[124]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-125) The final U.S. EPA rule specified maximum emissions of "0.05 ppm formaldehyde for hardwood plywood, 0.09 ppm formaldehyde for particleboard, 0.11 ppm formaldehyde for medium-density fiberboard, and 0.13 ppm formaldehyde for thin medium-density fiberboard."[[125]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-126)

Formaldehyde was declared a toxic substance by the [1999 Canadian Environmental Protection Act](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1999_Canadian_Environmental_Protection_Act "1999 Canadian Environmental Protection Act").[[126]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-127)

The [FDA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/FDA "FDA") is proposing a ban on hair relaxers with formaldehyde due to cancer concerns.[[127]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde#cite_note-128)
