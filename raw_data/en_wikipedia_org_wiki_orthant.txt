In two dimensions, there are four orthants (called quadrants)

In [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry"), an **orthant**[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthant#cite_note-1) or **hyperoctant**[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthant#cite_note-2) is the analogue in _n_-dimensional [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space") of a [quadrant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrant_(plane_geometry) "Quadrant (plane geometry)") in the plane or an [octant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octant_(solid_geometry) "Octant (solid geometry)") in three dimensions.

In general an orthant in _n_-dimensions can be considered the intersection of _n_ mutually orthogonal [half-spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Half-space_(geometry) "Half-space (geometry)"). By independent selections of half-space signs, there are 2 _n_ orthants in _n_-dimensional space.

More specifically, a **closed orthant** in **R**_n_ is a subset defined by constraining each [Cartesian coordinate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate "Cartesian coordinate") to be nonnegative or nonpositive. Such a subset is defined by a system of inequalities:

ε 1 _x_ 1≥0  ε 2 _x_ 2≥0 ··· ε _n_ _x_ _n_≥0,
where each ε _i_ is +1 or −1.

Similarly, an **open orthant** in **R**_n_ is a subset defined by a system of strict inequalities

ε 1 _x_ 1>0  ε 2 _x_ 2>0 ··· ε _n_ _x_ _n_>0,
where each ε _i_ is +1 or −1.

By dimension:

*   In one dimension, an orthant is a [ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_(mathematics)#Ray "Line (mathematics)").
*   In two dimensions, an orthant is a [quadrant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system#Quadrants_and_octants "Cartesian coordinate system").
*   In three dimensions, an orthant is an [octant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octant_(solid_geometry) "Octant (solid geometry)").

[John Conway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Horton_Conway "John Horton Conway") and [Neil Sloane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neil_Sloane "Neil Sloane") defined the term _n_-[orthoplex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthoplex "Orthoplex") from **orthant complex** as a [regular polytope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polytope "Regular polytope") in _n_-dimensions with 2 _n_[simplex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simplex "Simplex")[facets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Facet_(geometry) "Facet (geometry)"), one per orthant.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthant#cite_note-3)

The **nonnegative orthant** is the generalization of the first [quadrant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrant_(plane_geometry) "Quadrant (plane geometry)") to _n_-dimensions and is important in many [constrained optimization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constrained_optimization "Constrained optimization") problems.

*   [Cross polytope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_polytope "Cross polytope") (or orthoplex) – a family of [regular polytopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polytope "Regular polytope") in _n_-dimensions which can be constructed with one [simplex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simplex "Simplex")[facets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Facet_(geometry) "Facet (geometry)") in each orthant space.
*   [Measure polytope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Measure_polytope "Measure polytope") (or hypercube) – a family of regular polytopes in _n_-dimensions which can be constructed with one [vertex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_(geometry) "Vertex (geometry)") in each orthant space.
*   [Orthotope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthotope "Orthotope") – generalization of a rectangle in _n_-dimensions, with one vertex in each orthant.
