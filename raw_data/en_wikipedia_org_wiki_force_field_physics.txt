Region of space in which a force acts

[![Image 12](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/GravityPotential.jpg/330px-GravityPotential.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:GravityPotential.jpg)

Plot of a two-dimensional slice of the gravitational potential in and around a uniform spherical body. The [inflection points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inflection_point "Inflection point") of the cross-section are at the surface of the body.

In [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics"), a **force field** is a [vector field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_field "Vector field") corresponding with a [non-contact force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-contact_force "Non-contact force") acting on a particle at various positions in [space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space "Space"). Specifically, a force field is a vector field F{\displaystyle \mathbf {F} }![Image 13: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/da18bef8c979f3548bb0d8976f5844012d7b8256), where F(r){\displaystyle \mathbf {F} (\mathbf {r} )}![Image 14: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} (\mathbf {r} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0b5f64c13605eb065971b62adf62deb92c2c9354) is the force that a particle would feel if it were at the position r{\displaystyle \mathbf {r} }![Image 15: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eca0f46511c4c986c48b254073732c0bd98ae0c1).[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Force_field_(physics)#cite_note-1)

Examples
--------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Force_field_(physics)&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Examples")]

*   [Gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity "Gravity") is the force of attraction between two objects. A gravitational force field models this influence that a massive body (or more generally, any quantity of [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass%E2%80%93energy_equivalence "Mass–energy equivalence")) extends into the space around itself.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Force_field_(physics)#cite_note-2) In [Newtonian gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newtonian_gravity "Newtonian gravity"), a particle of mass _M_ creates a [gravitational field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_field "Gravitational field")g=−G M r 2 r^{\displaystyle \mathbf {g} ={\frac {-GM}{r^{2}}}{\hat {\mathbf {r} }}}![Image 16: {\displaystyle \mathbf {g} ={\frac {-GM}{r^{2}}}{\hat {\mathbf {r} }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7500633b5500829caa42e0e8eaf6d8e969423cc1), where the radial [unit vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_vector "Unit vector")r^{\displaystyle {\hat {\mathbf {r} }}}![Image 17: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathbf {r} }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9740464b71653e12932278ee944540be8caa5b96) points away from the particle. The gravitational force experienced by a particle of light mass _m_, close to the surface of [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth "Earth") is given by F=m g{\displaystyle \mathbf {F} =m\mathbf {g} }![Image 18: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} =m\mathbf {g} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8e22142cef2e6bd8e2d46d86c89d232027687734), where _g_ is [Earth's gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_gravity "Earth's gravity").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Force_field_(physics)#cite_note-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Force_field_(physics)#cite_note-4)
*   An [electric field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_field "Electric field")E{\displaystyle \mathbf {E} }![Image 19: {\displaystyle \mathbf {E} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d7f22b39d51f780fc02859059c1757c606b9de2) exerts a force on a [point charge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_charge "Point charge")_q_, given by F=q E{\displaystyle \mathbf {F} =q\mathbf {E} }![Image 20: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} =q\mathbf {E} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a55ac3f5c34f199fc5327de0d034b5218a364269).[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Force_field_(physics)#cite_note-5)
*   In a [magnetic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_field "Magnetic field")B{\displaystyle \mathbf {B} }![Image 21: {\displaystyle \mathbf {B} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cafb0ef39b0f5ffa23c170aa7f7b4e718327c4d1), a point charge moving through it experiences a force perpendicular to its own velocity and to the direction of the field, following the relation: F=q v×B{\displaystyle \mathbf {F} =q\mathbf {v} \times \mathbf {B} }![Image 22: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} =q\mathbf {v} \times \mathbf {B} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8208e984160e97a12a3837a497561fd379bb048e).

Work
----

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Force_field_(physics)&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Work")]

Work is dependent on the displacement as well as the force acting on an object. As a particle moves through a force field along a path _C_, the [work](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Work_(physics) "Work (physics)") done by the force is a [line integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_integral "Line integral"): W=∫C F⋅d r{\displaystyle W=\int _{C}\mathbf {F} \cdot d\mathbf {r} }![Image 23: {\displaystyle W=\int _{C}\mathbf {F} \cdot d\mathbf {r} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a1436394c6e2fc80af8bd41bbf6efa019d6321c4)

This value is independent of the [velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity "Velocity")[/momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum "Momentum") that the particle travels along the path.

### Conservative force field

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Force_field_(physics)&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Conservative force field")]

For a [conservative force field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservative_force "Conservative force"), it is also independent of the path itself, depending only on the starting and ending points. Therefore, the work for an object travelling in a closed path is zero, since its starting and ending points are the same:

∮C F⋅d r=0{\displaystyle \oint _{C}\mathbf {F} \cdot d\mathbf {r} =0}![Image 24: {\displaystyle \oint _{C}\mathbf {F} \cdot d\mathbf {r} =0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/24a8efc07648d97ab75afff33d08f2145fd32663) If the field is conservative, the work done can be more easily evaluated by realizing that a conservative vector field can be written as the gradient of some scalar potential function:

F=−∇ϕ{\displaystyle \mathbf {F} =-\nabla \phi }![Image 25: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} =-\nabla \phi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dfad986cdfc9d6cce01477861ee4b0b4a0f96e86)

The work done is then simply the difference in the value of this potential in the starting and end points of the path. If these points are given by _x_ = _a_ and _x_ = _b_, respectively:

W=ϕ(b)−ϕ(a){\displaystyle W=\phi (b)-\phi (a)}![Image 26: {\displaystyle W=\phi (b)-\phi (a)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/98eff4f5dacb0c67a36d5136b3eba65cb34f9e88)
