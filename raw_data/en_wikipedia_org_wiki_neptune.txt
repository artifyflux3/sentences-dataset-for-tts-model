**Neptune** is the eighth and farthest known [planet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planet "Planet") orbiting the [Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun "Sun"). It is the [fourth-largest planet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_Solar_System_objects_by_size "List of Solar System objects by size") in the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System") by diameter, the third-most-massive planet, and the densest [giant planet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giant_planet "Giant planet"). It is 17 times the [mass of Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass_of_Earth "Mass of Earth"). Compared to [Uranus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uranus "Uranus"), its neighbouring [ice giant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ice_giant "Ice giant"), Neptune is slightly smaller, but more massive and denser. Being composed primarily of gases and liquids,[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-c102-25) it has no well-defined solid surface. Neptune orbits the Sun once every 164.8[years](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Julian_year_(astronomy) "Julian year (astronomy)") at an [orbital distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_distance "Orbital distance") of 30.1 [astronomical units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_unit "Astronomical unit") (4.5 billion kilometres; 2.8 billion miles). It is named after [the Roman god of the sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune_(mythology) "Neptune (mythology)") and has the [astronomical symbol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_symbol "Astronomical symbol")[![Image 4: ♆](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Neptune_symbol_%28fixed_width%29.svg/16px-Neptune_symbol_%28fixed_width%29.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Neptune_symbol_(fixed_width).svg "♆"), representing [Neptune's trident](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trident_of_Poseidon "Trident of Poseidon").[[e]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-26)

Neptune is not visible to the unaided eye and is the only planet in the Solar System that was not initially observed by direct [empirical observation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empirical_observation "Empirical observation"). Rather, unexpected changes in the orbit of Uranus led [Alexis Bouvard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexis_Bouvard "Alexis Bouvard") to hypothesise that its orbit was subject to gravitational [perturbation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perturbation_(astronomy) "Perturbation (astronomy)") by an unknown planet. After Bouvard's death, the position of Neptune was mathematically predicted from his observations, independently, by [John Couch Adams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Couch_Adams "John Couch Adams") and [Urbain Le Verrier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urbain_Le_Verrier "Urbain Le Verrier"). Neptune was subsequently directly observed with a telescope on 23 September 1846[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-Hamilton-3) by [Johann Gottfried Galle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johann_Gottfried_Galle "Johann Gottfried Galle") within a [degree](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_(angle) "Degree (angle)") of the position predicted by Le Verrier. Its largest moon, [Triton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triton_(moon) "Triton (moon)"), was discovered shortly thereafter, though none of the planet's [remaining moons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons_of_Neptune "Moons of Neptune") were located telescopically until the 20th century.

The planet's distance from Earth gives it a small [apparent size](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apparent_size "Apparent size"), and its distance from the Sun renders it very dim, making it challenging to study with Earth-based telescopes. Only the advent of the [Hubble Space Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope "Hubble Space Telescope") and of large ground-based telescopes with [adaptive optics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adaptive_optics "Adaptive optics") allowed for detailed observations. Neptune was visited by _[Voyager 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voyager\_2 "Voyager 2")_, which [flew by](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_flyby "Planetary flyby") the planet on 25 August 1989; _Voyager 2_ remains the only spacecraft to have visited it.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-NYT-20141018-KC-27)[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-28) Like the [gas giants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas_giant "Gas giant") ([Jupiter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jupiter "Jupiter") and [Saturn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saturn "Saturn")), Neptune's atmosphere is composed primarily of [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen") and [helium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium "Helium"), along with traces of [hydrocarbons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrocarbon "Hydrocarbon") and possibly [nitrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen "Nitrogen"), but contains a higher proportion of _[ices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volatile\_(astrogeology) "Volatile (astrogeology)")_ such as water, [ammonia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia "Ammonia") and [methane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane "Methane"). Similar to Uranus, its interior is primarily composed of ices and rock;[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-Podolak_Weizman_et_al._1995-29) both planets are normally considered "ice giants" to distinguish them.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-Lunine_1993-30) Along with [Rayleigh scattering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rayleigh_scattering "Rayleigh scattering"), traces of methane in the outermost regions make Neptune appear faintly blue.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-bluecolour-31)[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-32)

In contrast to the strongly seasonal atmosphere of Uranus, which can be featureless for long periods of time, Neptune's atmosphere has active and consistently visible weather patterns. At the time of the _Voyager 2_ flyby in 1989, the planet's southern hemisphere had a [Great Dark Spot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Dark_Spot "Great Dark Spot") comparable to the [Great Red Spot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Red_Spot "Great Red Spot") on Jupiter. In 2018, a newer main dark spot and smaller dark spot were identified and studied.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-NYT-20201222-33) These weather patterns are driven by the strongest sustained winds of any planet in the Solar System, as high as 2,100 km/h (580 m/s; 1,300 mph).[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-Suomi1991-34) Because of its great distance from the Sun, Neptune's outer atmosphere is one of the coldest places in the Solar System, with temperatures at its cloud tops approaching 55[K](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvin "Kelvin") (−218[°C](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celsius "Celsius"); −361[°F](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fahrenheit "Fahrenheit")). Temperatures at the planet's centre are approximately 5,400 K (5,100°C; 9,300°F).[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-hubbard-35)[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-nettelmann-36) Neptune has [a faint and fragmented ring system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rings_of_Neptune "Rings of Neptune") (labelled "arcs"), discovered in 1984 and confirmed by _Voyager 2_.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-ring1-37)

History
-------

### Discovery

[![Image 5](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Le_Verrier_colored.jpg/250px-Le_Verrier_colored.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Le_Verrier_colored.jpg)

[![Image 6](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/JohannGalle.jpg/250px-JohannGalle.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:JohannGalle.jpg)

[![Image 7](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/John_Couch_Adams_Cornwall.jpg/250px-John_Couch_Adams_Cornwall.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:John_Couch_Adams_Cornwall.jpg)

Some of the earliest known [telescopic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Telescope "Telescope") observations ever, Galileo's drawings on 28 December 1612 and 27 January 1613 ([New Style](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Old_Style_and_New_Style_dates "Old Style and New Style dates")) contain plotted points that match what is now known to have been the positions of Neptune on those dates. Both times, Galileo seems to have mistaken Neptune for a [fixed star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fixed_star "Fixed star") when it appeared close—in [conjunction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conjunction_(astronomy) "Conjunction (astronomy)")—to Jupiter in the [night sky](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Night_sky "Night sky").[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-38) Hence, he is not credited with Neptune's discovery. At his first observation in December 1612, Neptune was almost stationary in the sky because it had just turned [retrograde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apparent_retrograde_motion "Apparent retrograde motion") that day. This apparent backward motion is created when Earth's orbit takes it past an outer planet. Because Neptune was only beginning its yearly retrograde cycle, the motion of the planet was far too slight to be detected with Galileo's small telescope.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-39) In 2009, a study suggested that Galileo was at least aware that the "star" he had observed had moved relative to fixed stars.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-40)

In 1821, [Alexis Bouvard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexis_Bouvard "Alexis Bouvard") published astronomical tables of the [orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbit "Orbit") of [Uranus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uranus "Uranus").[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-41) Subsequent observations revealed substantial deviations from the tables, leading Bouvard to hypothesize that an unknown body was [perturbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perturbation_(astronomy) "Perturbation (astronomy)") the orbit through [gravitational](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitation "Gravitation") interaction.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-MNRAS7-42) In 1843, [John Couch Adams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Couch_Adams "John Couch Adams") began work on the orbit of Uranus using the data he had. He requested extra data from Sir [George Airy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_Biddell_Airy "George Biddell Airy"), the [Astronomer Royal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomer_Royal "Astronomer Royal"), who supplied it in February 1844. Adams continued to work in 1845–1846 and produced several different estimates for the position of an undiscovered planet beyond Uranus.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-43)[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-44)

Independently from Adams, [Urbain Le Verrier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urbain_Le_Verrier "Urbain Le Verrier") developed his own calculations in 1845–1846 that pointed to an undiscovered planet, but aroused no enthusiasm among his compatriots. In June 1846, upon seeing Le Verrier's first published estimate of a suspected undiscovered planet's longitude and its similarity to Adams's estimate, Airy persuaded [James Challis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Challis "James Challis") to search for it. Challis vainly scoured the sky throughout August and September.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-MNRAS7-42)[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-45) Challis had, in fact, observed Neptune a year before the planet's subsequent discoverer, [Johann Gottfried Galle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johann_Gottfried_Galle "Johann Gottfried Galle"), and on two occasions, 4 and 12 August 1845. However, his out-of-date star maps and poor observing techniques meant that he failed to recognize the observations as such until he carried out later analysis. Challis was full of remorse but blamed his neglect on his maps and the fact that he was distracted by his concurrent work on comet observations.[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-46)[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-MNRAS7-42)[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-47)

Meanwhile, Le Verrier sent a letter and urged [Berlin Observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berlin_Observatory "Berlin Observatory") astronomer Galle to search with the observatory's [refractor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Refractor "Refractor"). [Heinrich d'Arrest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heinrich_Louis_d%27Arrest "Heinrich Louis d'Arrest"), a student at the observatory, suggested to Galle that they could compare a recently drawn chart of the sky in the region of Le Verrier's predicted location with the current sky to seek the displacement characteristic of a [planet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planet "Planet"), as opposed to a fixed star. On the evening of 23 September 1846, the day Galle received the letter, he discovered Neptune just northeast of [Iota Aquarii](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iota_Aquarii "Iota Aquarii"), 1° from the "five degrees east of [Delta Capricorn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Delta_Capricorni "Delta Capricorni")" position Le Verrier had predicted it to be,[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-auto-48)[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-49) about 12° from Adams's prediction, and on the border of [Aquarius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aquarius_(constellation) "Aquarius (constellation)") and [Capricornus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capricornus "Capricornus") according to the modern [IAU constellation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IAU_designated_constellations "IAU designated constellations") boundaries.

[![Image 8](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Deutsches_Museum_M%C3%BCnchen_-_15803973590.jpg/250px-Deutsches_Museum_M%C3%BCnchen_-_15803973590.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Deutsches_Museum_M%C3%BCnchen_-_15803973590.jpg)

The 9" refractor used by Galle to discover Neptune

In the wake of the discovery, there was a nationalistic rivalry between the French and the British over who deserved credit for the discovery. Eventually, an international consensus emerged that Le Verrier and Adams deserved joint credit.[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-50) Since 1966, [Dennis Rawlins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dennis_Rawlins "Dennis Rawlins") has questioned the credibility of Adams's claim to co-discovery, and the issue was re-evaluated by historians with the return in 1998 of the "Neptune papers" (historical documents) to the [Royal Observatory, Greenwich](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Observatory,_Greenwich "Royal Observatory, Greenwich").[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-Neptdisc-51)[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-52)

### Naming

Shortly after its discovery, Neptune was referred to simply as "the planet exterior to Uranus" or as "Le Verrier's planet". The first suggestion for a name came from Galle, who proposed the name _[Janus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Janus\_(mythology) "Janus (mythology)")_. In England, Challis put forward the name _[Oceanus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oceanus "Oceanus")_.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-53)

Claiming the right to name his discovery, Le Verrier quickly proposed the name _Neptune_ for this new planet, though falsely stating that this had been officially approved by the French [Bureau des Longitudes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bureau_des_Longitudes "Bureau des Longitudes").[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-54) In October, he sought to name the planet _Le Verrier_, after himself, and he had loyal support in this from the observatory director, [François Arago](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Arago "François Arago"). This suggestion met with stiff resistance outside France.[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-55) French almanacs quickly reintroduced the name _Herschel_ for Uranus, after that planet's discoverer Sir[William Herschel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Herschel "William Herschel"), and _Leverrier_ for the new planet.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-56)

[Struve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Georg_Wilhelm_von_Struve "Friedrich Georg Wilhelm von Struve") came out in favour of the name _Neptune_ on 29 December 1846, to the [Saint Petersburg Academy of Sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_Academy_of_Sciences "Russian Academy of Sciences"),[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-57) after the colour of the planet as viewed through a telescope.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-58) Soon, _Neptune_ became the internationally accepted name. In [Roman mythology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_mythology "Roman mythology"), [Neptune](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune_(mythology) "Neptune (mythology)") was the god of the sea, identified with the Greek [Poseidon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poseidon "Poseidon"). The demand for a mythological name seemed to be in keeping with the nomenclature of the other planets, all of which were named for deities in [Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_mythology "Greek mythology") and Roman mythology.[[f]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-59)[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-USGS-60)

Most languages today use some variant of the name "Neptune" for the planet. In Chinese, Vietnamese, Japanese, and Korean, the planet's name was translated as "sea king star" (海王星).[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-61)[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-62) In [Mongolian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mongolian_language "Mongolian language"), Neptune is called _Dalain van_ (Далайн ван), reflecting its namesake god's role as the ruler of the sea. In modern [Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_language "Greek language"), the planet is called _Poseidon_ (Ποσειδώνας, _Poseidonas_), the Greek counterpart of Neptune.[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-63)[In Hebrew](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hebrew_astronomy "Hebrew astronomy"), _[Rahab](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rahab\_(term) "Rahab (term)")_ (רהב), from [a Biblical sea monster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rahab_(Egypt)#Jewish_folklore "Rahab (Egypt)") mentioned in the [Book of Psalms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Book_of_Psalms "Book of Psalms"), was selected in a vote managed by the [Academy of the Hebrew Language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Academy_of_the_Hebrew_Language "Academy of the Hebrew Language") in 2009 as the official name for the planet, even though the existing Latin term _Neptun_ (נפטון) is commonly used.[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-Haaretz-64)[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-Hayadan.org.il-65) In [Māori](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M%C4%81ori_language "Māori language"), the planet is called _Tangaroa_, named after the [Māori god of the sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangaroa "Tangaroa").[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-Appendix_5:_Planetary_Linguistics-66) In [Nahuatl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nahuatl_language "Nahuatl language"), the planet is called _Tlāloccītlalli_, named after the rain god [Tlāloc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tl%C4%81loc "Tlāloc").[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-Appendix_5:_Planetary_Linguistics-66) In [Thai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thai_language "Thai language"), Neptune is referred to by the Westernised name _Dao Nepchun/Nepjun_ (ดาวเนปจูน) but is also called _Dao Ket_ (ดาวเกตุ, lit.'star of Ketu'), after [Ketu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ketu_(mythology) "Ketu (mythology)") (केतु), the descending [lunar node](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunar_node "Lunar node"), who plays a role in [Hindu astrology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hindu_astrology "Hindu astrology"). In [Malay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malay_language "Malay language"), the name _Waruna_, after the [Hindu god of seas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Varuna "Varuna"), is attested as far back as the 1970s,[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-67) but was eventually superseded by the Latinate equivalents _Neptun_ (in [Malaysian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malaysian_language "Malaysian language")[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-68)) or _Neptunus_ (in [Indonesian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indonesian_language "Indonesian language")[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-69)).

The usual adjectival form is _Neptunian_. The [nonce](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nonce_word "Nonce word") form _Poseidean_ (), from [Poseidon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poseidon "Poseidon"), has also been used,[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-Poseidean-6) though the usual adjectival form of _Poseidon_ is _Poseidonian_ ().[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-70)

### Status

From its discovery in 1846 until the [discovery of Pluto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discovery_of_Pluto "Discovery of Pluto") in 1930, Neptune was the farthest known planet. When [Pluto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pluto "Pluto") was discovered, it was considered a planet, and Neptune thus became the second-farthest known planet, except for a 20-year period between 1979 and 1999 when Pluto's elliptical orbit brought it closer than Neptune to the Sun, making Neptune the ninth planet from the Sun during this period.[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-71)[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-72) The increasingly accurate estimations of Pluto's mass from ten times [that of Earth's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth_mass "Earth mass") to far less than [that of the Moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunar_mass "Lunar mass")[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-73) and the discovery of the [Kuiper belt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kuiper_belt "Kuiper belt") in 1992 led many astronomers to debate whether Pluto should be considered a planet or as part of the Kuiper belt.[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-74)[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-75) In 2006, the [International Astronomical Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union "International Astronomical Union")[defined the word "planet"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IAU_definition_of_planet "IAU definition of planet") for the first time, reclassifying Pluto as a "[dwarf planet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dwarf_planet "Dwarf planet")" and making Neptune once again the outermost-known planet in the Solar System.[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-76)

Physical characteristics
------------------------

[![Image 9](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Neptune%2C_Earth_size_comparison_true_color.jpg/250px-Neptune%2C_Earth_size_comparison_true_color.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Neptune,_Earth_size_comparison_true_color.jpg)

A size comparison of Neptune and Earth

Neptune's mass of 1.0243×10 26 kg[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-fact-10) is intermediate between Earth and the larger [gas giants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas_giant "Gas giant"): it is 17.15 times that of Earth but just 1/19th [that of Jupiter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jupiter_mass "Jupiter mass").[[g]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-mass-77) Its [gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity "Gravity") at 1 bar is 11.15 m/s 2, 1.14 times the [surface gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_gravity "Surface gravity") of Earth,[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-78) and surpassed only by Jupiter.[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-Uns%C3%B6ld_&_Baschek_2001-79) Neptune's [equatorial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equator "Equator") radius of 24,764 km[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-Seidelmann_Archinal_A'hearn_et_al._2007-13) is nearly four times [that of Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth_radius "Earth radius"). Neptune, like [Uranus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uranus "Uranus"), is an [ice giant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ice_giant "Ice giant"), a subclass of [giant planet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giant_planet "Giant planet"), because they are smaller and have higher concentrations of [volatiles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volatile_(astrogeology) "Volatile (astrogeology)") than Jupiter and Saturn.[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-Boss-80) In the search for [exoplanets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exoplanet "Exoplanet"), Neptune has been used as a [metonym](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metonymy "Metonymy"): discovered bodies of similar mass are often referred to as "Neptunes",[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-81) just as scientists refer to various extrasolar bodies as "Jupiters".

### Internal structure

Neptune's internal structure resembles [that of Uranus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uranus#Internal_structure "Uranus"). Its atmosphere forms about 5 to 10% of its mass and extends perhaps 10 to 20% of the way towards the core. Pressure in the atmosphere reaches about 10[GPa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pascal_(unit) "Pascal (unit)"), or about 10 5 atmospheres. Increasing concentrations of [methane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane "Methane"), [ammonia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia "Ammonia") and water are found in the lower regions of the atmosphere.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-hubbard-35)

[![Image 10](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Another_Neptune_diagram.svg/500px-Another_Neptune_diagram.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Another_Neptune_diagram.svg)

Illustration of the physical component of Neptune's interior and its surroundings in [false colours](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/False_color "False color")

The mantle is equivalent to 10 to 15 Earth masses and is rich in water, ammonia and methane.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-Hamilton-3) As is customary in planetary science, this mixture is called [icy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volatile_(astrogeology) "Volatile (astrogeology)") even though it is a hot, dense [supercritical fluid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supercritical_fluid "Supercritical fluid"). This fluid, which has a high electrical conductivity, is sometimes called a water–ammonia ocean.[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-Atreya2006-82) The mantle may consist of a layer of ionic water in which the water molecules break down into a soup of hydrogen and [oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen "Oxygen")[ions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ion "Ion"), and deeper down [superionic water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superionic_water "Superionic water") in which the oxygen crystallizes but the [hydrogen ions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_ion "Hydrogen ion") float around freely within the oxygen lattice.[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-83) At a depth of 7,000 km, the conditions may be such that methane decomposes into diamond crystals that rain downwards like hailstones.[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-84)[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-WP-20170825-85)[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-86) Scientists believe that this kind of diamond rain occurs on Jupiter, Saturn, and Uranus.[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-87)[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-WP-20170825-85) Very-high-pressure experiments at [Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lawrence_Livermore_National_Laboratory "Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory") suggest that the top of the mantle may be an ocean of liquid carbon with floating solid 'diamonds'.[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-88)[[82]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-Eggert_2004-89)[[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-Eggert_2010-90)

The [core](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_core "Planetary core") of Neptune is likely composed of iron, nickel and [silicates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicate "Silicate"), with an interior [model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_model "Scientific model") giving a mass about 1.2x that of Earth.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-Podolak_Weizman_et_al._1995-29) The pressure at the centre is 7[Mbar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bar_(unit) "Bar (unit)") (700 GPa), about twice as high as that at the centre of Earth, and the temperature may be 5,400 K (5,100°C; 9,300°F).[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-hubbard-35)[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-nettelmann-36)

### Atmosphere

[![Image 11](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Neptune_clouds.jpg/250px-Neptune_clouds.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Neptune_clouds.jpg)

Bands of high-altitude clouds cast shadows on Neptune's lower cloud deck. The colour is exaggerated to show the clouds more clearly.

At high altitudes, Neptune's atmosphere is 80% [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen") and 19% [helium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium "Helium").[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-hubbard-35) A trace amount of [methane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane "Methane") is present. Prominent absorption bands of methane exist at wavelengths above 600 nm, in the red and infrared portion of the spectrum. As with Uranus, this absorption of red light by atmospheric methane is part of what gives Neptune its faint blue hue,[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-92) which is more pronounced for Neptune's due to concentrated haze in Uranus's atmosphere.[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-NYT-20240104-93)[[87]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-:0-94)

Neptune's atmosphere is subdivided into two main regions: the lower [troposphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Troposphere "Troposphere"), where temperature decreases with altitude, and the [stratosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stratosphere "Stratosphere"), where temperature increases with altitude. The boundary between the two, the [tropopause](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tropopause "Tropopause"), lies at a pressure of 0.1 bars (10 kPa).[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-Lunine_1993-30) The stratosphere then gives way to the [thermosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermosphere "Thermosphere") at a pressure lower than 10−5 to 10−4 bars (1 to 10 Pa).[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-Lunine_1993-30) The thermosphere gradually transitions to the [exosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exosphere "Exosphere").[[88]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-l086-95)

Models suggest that Neptune's troposphere is banded by clouds of varying compositions depending on altitude.[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-NYT-20230818-91) The upper-level clouds lie at pressures below one bar, where the temperature is suitable for methane to condense. For pressures between one and five bars (100 and 500 kPa), clouds of [ammonia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia "Ammonia") and [hydrogen sulfide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide "Hydrogen sulfide") are thought to form. Above a pressure of five bars, the clouds may consist of ammonia, [ammonium sulfide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium_sulfide "Ammonium sulfide"), hydrogen sulfide and water. Deeper clouds of water ice should be found at pressures of about 50 bars (5.0 MPa), where the temperature reaches 273 K (0°C; 32°F). Underneath, clouds of ammonia and hydrogen sulfide may be found.[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-elkins-tanton-96)

High-altitude clouds on Neptune have been observed casting shadows on the opaque cloud deck below. There are high-altitude cloud bands that wrap around the planet at constant latitudes. These circumferential bands have widths of 50–150 km and lie about 50–110 km above the cloud deck.[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-apj125-97) These altitudes are in the layer where weather occurs, the troposphere. Weather does not occur in the higher stratosphere or thermosphere. In August 2023, the high-altitude clouds of Neptune vanished, prompting a study spanning thirty years of observations by the Hubble Space Telescope and ground-based telescopes. The study found that Neptune's high-altitude cloud activity is bound to [Solar cycles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_cycle "Solar cycle"), and not to the planet's seasons.[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-NYT-20230818-91)[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-98)[[92]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-99)

Neptune's [spectra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visible_spectrum "Visible spectrum") suggest that its lower stratosphere is hazy due to condensation of products of ultraviolet [photolysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photolysis "Photolysis") of methane, such as [ethane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethane "Ethane") and [ethyne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene "Acetylene").[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-Lunine_1993-30)[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-hubbard-35) The stratosphere is home to trace amounts of [carbon monoxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide "Carbon monoxide") and [hydrogen cyanide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide "Hydrogen cyanide").[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-Lunine_1993-30)[[93]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-Encrenaz_2003-100) The stratosphere of Neptune is warmer than that of Uranus due to the elevated concentration of hydrocarbons.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-Lunine_1993-30)

For reasons that remain obscure, the planet's thermosphere is at an anomalously high temperature of about 750 K (477°C; 890°F).[[94]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-Broadfoot19989-101)[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-Herbert_&_Sandel_1999-102) The planet is too far from the Sun for this heat to be generated by [ultraviolet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultraviolet "Ultraviolet") radiation. One candidate for a heating mechanism is atmospheric interaction with ions in the planet's [magnetic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetosphere "Magnetosphere"). Other candidates are [gravity waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity_wave "Gravity wave") from the interior that dissipate in the atmosphere. The thermosphere contains traces of [carbon dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_dioxide "Carbon dioxide") and water, which may have been deposited from external sources such as [meteorites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteorite "Meteorite") and dust.[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-elkins-tanton-96)[[93]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-Encrenaz_2003-100)

### Colour

Neptune's atmosphere is faintly blue in the [optical spectrum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visible_spectrum "Visible spectrum"), only slightly more saturated than the blue of Uranus's atmosphere. Early renderings of the two planets greatly exaggerated Neptune's colour contrast "to better reveal the clouds, bands and winds", making it seem deep blue compared to Uranus's off-white. The two planets had been imaged with different systems, making it hard to directly compare the resulting [composite images](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compositing "Compositing"). This was revisited with the colour normalised over time, most comprehensively in late 2023.[[87]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-:0-94)[[96]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-:1-103)

### Magnetosphere

Neptune's [magnetosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetosphere "Magnetosphere") consists of a [magnetic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_field "Magnetic field") that is strongly tilted relative to its [rotational axis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation "Rotation") at 47° and offset of at least 0.55 radius (~13,500 km) from the planet's physical centre—resembling Uranus's magnetosphere. Before the arrival of _Voyager 2_ to Neptune, it was hypothesised that Uranus's sideways rotation caused its tilted magnetosphere. In comparing the magnetic fields of the two planets, scientists now think the extreme orientation may be characteristic of flows in the planets' interiors. This field may be generated by [convective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convection "Convection") fluid motions in a thin spherical shell of [electrically conducting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrical_conductor "Electrical conductor") liquids (probably a combination of ammonia, methane and water),[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-elkins-tanton-96) resulting in a [dynamo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynamo "Dynamo") action.[[100]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-107)

The dipole component of the magnetic field at the magnetic equator of Neptune is about 14[microteslas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tesla_(unit) "Tesla (unit)") (0.14[G](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauss_(unit) "Gauss (unit)")).[[101]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-Connerney1991-108) The dipole [magnetic moment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_moment "Magnetic moment") of Neptune is about 2.2×10 17 T·m 3 (14 μT·_R_ _N_ 3, where _R_ _N_ is the radius of Neptune). Neptune's magnetic field has a complex geometry that includes relatively large contributions from non-dipolar components, including a strong [quadrupole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrupole "Quadrupole") moment that may exceed the [dipole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_dipole "Magnetic dipole") moment in strength. By contrast, Earth, Jupiter and Saturn have only relatively small quadrupole moments, and their fields are less tilted from the polar axis. The large quadrupole moment of Neptune may be the result of an offset from the planet's centre and geometrical constraints of the field's dynamo generator.[[102]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-science4936-109)[[103]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-110)

Neptune's [bow shock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bow_shock "Bow shock"), where the magnetosphere begins to slow the [solar wind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_wind "Solar wind"), occurs at a distance of 34.9 times the radius of the planet. The [magnetopause](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetopause "Magnetopause"), where the pressure of the magnetosphere counterbalances the solar wind, lies at a distance of 23–26.5 times the radius of Neptune. The tail of the magnetosphere extends out to at least 72 times the radius of Neptune, and likely much farther.[[102]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-science4936-109)

[![Image 15](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Neptune_with_Aurorae.png/250px-Neptune_with_Aurorae.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Neptune_with_Aurorae.png)

Left: a visible light image of Neptune taken by the Hubble Space Telescope.Right: the Hubble image composited with a near-infrared image taken by the James Webb Telescope. As the aurorae cannot be observed in the visible band, their image in near-infrared has been rendered as shades of cyan.

Measurements by _Voyager 2_ in extreme-ultraviolet and radio frequencies revealed that Neptune has faint and weak but complex and unique [aurorae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aurora "Aurora"); however, these observations were limited in time and did not contain infrared. Subsequent astronomers using the Hubble Space Telescope have not glimpsed the aurorae, in contrast to the more well-defined aurorae of Uranus.[[104]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-Lamy_2020_p._20190481-111)[[105]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-esa-112) In March 2025, aurorae on Neptune were pictured for the first time by combining [visible light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visible_light "Visible light") images from the Hubble Space Telescope with [near-infrared](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Near-infrared_spectroscopy "Near-infrared spectroscopy") (NIR) images from the [James Webb Space Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Webb_Space_Telescope "James Webb Space Telescope"). The relevant data were taken in June 2023. The James Webb Space Telescope attempted to learn the [spectrography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectrography "Spectrography") of Neptune's atmosphere and it was able to find [trihydrogen cations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trihydrogen_cation "Trihydrogen cation") (H+

3) which is generated during an aurora and is considered as a clear indicator of auroral activity on both gas giants and ice giants. The nature of Neptune's aurorae is greatly influenced by the peculiar nature of its magnetic field. Unlike the [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_magnetic_field "Earth's magnetic field"), [Jupiter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetosphere_of_Jupiter "Magnetosphere of Jupiter") or [Saturn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetosphere_of_Saturn "Magnetosphere of Saturn"), Neptune's magnetic poles are not aligned with the planet's rotational poles which is why Neptune's aurorae mostly occur around its mid-latitude areas instead of its poles like on Earth or Jupiter.[[106]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-113)

Climate
-------

[![Image 16](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Neptune_storms.jpg/250px-Neptune_storms.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Neptune_storms.jpg)

The [Great Dark Spot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Dark_Spot "Great Dark Spot") (top), Scooter (middle white cloud),[[107]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-scooter-114) and the [Small Dark Spot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Small_Dark_Spot "Small Dark Spot") (bottom), with contrast exaggerated

Neptune's weather is characterized by extremely dynamic storm systems, with winds reaching speeds of almost 600 m/s (2,200 km/h; 1,300 mph)—exceeding [supersonic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supersonic_speed "Supersonic speed") flow.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-Suomi1991-34) More typically, by tracking the motion of persistent clouds, wind speeds have been shown to vary from 20 m/s in the easterly direction to 325 m/s westward.[[108]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-Hammel1989-115) At the cloud tops, the prevailing winds range in speed from 400 m/s along the equator to 250 m/s at the poles.[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-elkins-tanton-96) Most of the winds on Neptune move in a direction opposite the planet's rotation.[[109]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-burgess2-116) The general pattern of winds showed prograde rotation at high latitudes vs. retrograde rotation at lower latitudes. The difference in flow direction is thought to be a "[skin effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skin_effect "Skin effect")" and not due to any deeper atmospheric processes.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-Lunine_1993-30) At 70°S latitude, a high-speed jet travels at a speed of 300 m/s.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-Lunine_1993-30) Due to seasonal changes, the cloud bands in the southern hemisphere of Neptune have been observed to increase in size and [albedo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albedo "Albedo"). This trend was first seen in 1980. The long orbital period of Neptune results in seasons lasting 40 Earth years.[[110]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-villard-117)

Neptune differs from Uranus in its typical level of [meteorological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteorology "Meteorology") activity. _Voyager 2_ observed weather phenomena on Neptune during its 1989 flyby,[[111]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-spot-118) but no comparable phenomena on Uranus during its 1986 flyby.

The abundance of methane, ethane and [acetylene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene "Acetylene") at Neptune's equator is 10–100 times greater than at the poles. This is interpreted as evidence for upwelling at the equator and subsidence near the poles, as photochemistry cannot account for the distribution without meridional circulation.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-Lunine_1993-30)

In 2007, it was discovered that the upper troposphere of Neptune's south pole was about 10 K warmer than the rest of its atmosphere, which averages about 73 K (−200°C). The temperature differential is enough to let methane, which elsewhere is frozen in the troposphere, escape into the stratosphere near the pole.[[112]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-119) The relative "hot spot" is due to Neptune's [axial tilt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axial_tilt "Axial tilt"), which has exposed the south pole to the [Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun "Sun") for the last quarter of Neptune's year, or roughly 40 Earth years. As Neptune slowly moves towards the opposite side of the Sun, the south pole will be darkened and the north pole illuminated, causing the methane release to shift to the north pole.[[113]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-120)

### Storms

[![Image 17](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Neptune%27s_Great_Dark_Spot.jpg/250px-Neptune%27s_Great_Dark_Spot.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Neptune%27s_Great_Dark_Spot.jpg)

The Great Dark Spot imaged by _Voyager 2_ in an enhanced colour image

In 1989, the [Great Dark Spot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Dark_Spot "Great Dark Spot"), an [anticyclonic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anticyclone "Anticyclone") storm system spanning 13,000 km ×6,600 km (8,100 mi ×4,100 mi),[[111]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-spot-118) was discovered by [NASA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NASA "NASA")'s _Voyager 2_ spacecraft. The storm resembled the [Great Red Spot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Red_Spot "Great Red Spot") of Jupiter. Some five years later, on 2 November 1994, the [Hubble Space Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope "Hubble Space Telescope") did not see the Great Dark Spot on the planet. Instead, a new storm similar to the Great Dark Spot was found in Neptune's northern hemisphere.[[114]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-121)

The Scooter is another storm, a white cloud group farther south than the Great Dark Spot. This nickname first arose during the months leading up to the _Voyager 2_ encounter in 1989, when they were observed moving at speeds faster than the Great Dark Spot (and images acquired later would subsequently reveal the presence of clouds moving even faster than those that had initially been detected by _Voyager 2_).[[109]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-burgess2-116) The [Small Dark Spot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Small_Dark_Spot "Small Dark Spot") is a southern cyclonic storm, the second-most-intense storm observed during the 1989 encounter. It was initially completely dark, but as _Voyager 2_ approached the planet, a bright core developed, which can be seen in most of the highest-resolution images.[[115]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-122) In 2018, a newer main dark spot and smaller dark spot were identified and studied.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-NYT-20201222-33) In 2023, the first ground-based observation of a dark spot on Neptune was announced.[[116]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-123)

[![Image 18: Neptune with its Northern Great Dark Spot visible.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/A_storm_is_coming_Neptune.tif/lossy-page1-172px-A_storm_is_coming_Neptune.tif.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:A_storm_is_coming_Neptune.tif)

The Northern Great Dark Spot imaged by Hubble in 2018

[![Image 19](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Neptune_Dark_Spot_Jr._Hubble.png/250px-Neptune_Dark_Spot_Jr._Hubble.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Neptune_Dark_Spot_Jr._Hubble.png)

The Northern Great Dark Spot and a smaller companion storm imaged by Hubble in 2020

[![Image 20](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Neptune%E2%80%99s_shrinking_vortex.jpg/500px-Neptune%E2%80%99s_shrinking_vortex.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Neptune%E2%80%99s_shrinking_vortex.jpg)

Neptune's SDS-2015[[117]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-124) vortex in around two years, which has appeared to be shrinking and disappearing

Neptune's dark spots are thought to occur in the [troposphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Troposphere "Troposphere") at lower altitudes than the brighter cloud features,[[118]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-125) so they appear as holes in the upper cloud decks. As they are stable features that can persist for several months, they are thought to be [vortex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vortex "Vortex") structures.[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-apj125-97) Often associated with dark spots are brighter, persistent methane clouds that form around the [tropopause](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tropopause "Tropopause") layer.[[119]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-126) The persistence of companion clouds shows that some former dark spots may continue to exist as cyclones even though they are no longer visible as a dark feature. Dark spots may dissipate when they migrate too close to the equator or possibly through some other, unknown mechanism.[[120]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-127)

[![Image 21](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/The_four_sides_of_Neptune_%28captured_by_the_Hubble_Space_Telescope%29.tif/lossy-page1-210px-The_four_sides_of_Neptune_%28captured_by_the_Hubble_Space_Telescope%29.tif.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:The_four_sides_of_Neptune_(captured_by_the_Hubble_Space_Telescope).tif)

Four images taken a few hours apart with the NASA/ESA _Hubble Space Telescope_'s[Wide Field Camera 3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wide_Field_Camera_3 "Wide Field Camera 3"). Near-infrared radiation data has been used as a red channel.[[121]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-128)

In 1989, _Voyager 2_'s [Planetary Radio Astronomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voyager_2#Scientific_instruments "Voyager 2") (PRA) experiment observed around 60 lightning flashes, or Neptunian electrostatic discharges emitting energies over 7×10 8[J](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joule "Joule").[[122]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-129) A plasma wave system (PWS) detected 16 electromagnetic wave events with a frequency range of 50–12 kHz at magnetic latitudes 7–33˚.[[123]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-Atmospheric_Electricity_at_the_Ice-130)[[124]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-Whistlers_in_Neptune's_magnetospher-131) These plasma wave detections were possibly triggered by lightning over 20 minutes in the ammonia clouds of the magnetosphere.[[124]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-Whistlers_in_Neptune's_magnetospher-131)

During _Voyager 2_’s closest approach to Neptune, the PWS instrument provided Neptune's first plasma wave detections at a sample rate of 28,800 samples per second.[[124]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-Whistlers_in_Neptune's_magnetospher-131) The measured plasma densities range from 10−3 – 10−1 cm 3.[[124]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-Whistlers_in_Neptune's_magnetospher-131)[[125]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-132) Neptunian lightning may occur in three cloud layers,[[126]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-133) with microphysical modelling suggesting that most of these occurrences happen in the water clouds of the troposphere or the shallow ammonia clouds of the magnetosphere.[[123]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-Atmospheric_Electricity_at_the_Ice-130)[[127]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-134) Neptune is predicted to have 1/19 the lightning flash rate of Jupiter and to display most of its lightning activity at high latitudes. However, lightning on Neptune seems to resemble lightning on Earth rather than Jovian lightning.[[128]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-135)

### Internal heating

Neptune's more varied weather when compared to Uranus is due in part to its higher [internal heating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internal_heating "Internal heating"). The upper regions of Neptune's troposphere reach a low temperature of 51.8 K (−221.3°C). At a depth where the [atmospheric pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure "Atmospheric pressure") equals 1[bar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bar_(unit) "Bar (unit)") (100[kPa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pascal_(unit) "Pascal (unit)")), the temperature is 72.00 K (−201.15°C).[[129]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-136) Deeper inside the layers of gas, the temperature rises steadily. As with Uranus, the source of this heating is unknown, but the discrepancy is larger: Uranus only radiates 1.1 times as much energy as it receives from the Sun;[[130]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-137) whereas Neptune radiates about 2.61 times as much energy as it receives from the Sun.[[131]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-138)

Neptune is over 50% farther from the Sun than Uranus and receives only ~40% of Uranus's amount of sunlight;[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-Lunine_1993-30) however, its internal energy is still enough for the fastest planetary winds in the Solar System. Depending on the thermal properties of its interior, the heat left over from Neptune's formation may be sufficient to explain its current heat flow, though it is harder to explain Uranus's lack of internal heat while preserving the apparent similarity between the two planets.[[132]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-139)

Orbit and rotation
------------------

[![Image 22](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Neptune_Orbit.gif/210px-Neptune_Orbit.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Neptune_Orbit.gif)

Neptune (red arc) completes one orbit around the Sun (centre) for every 164.79 orbits of Earth. The light blue dot represents Uranus.

The average distance between Neptune and the Sun is 4.5 billion km (about 30.1[astronomical units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_unit "Astronomical unit") (AU), the mean distance from the Earth to the Sun), and it completes an orbit on average every 164.79 years, subject to a variability of around ±0.1 years. The perihelion distance is 29.81 AU, and the aphelion distance is 30.33 AU.[[h]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-141) Neptune's [orbital eccentricity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity "Orbital eccentricity") is only 0.008678, making it the planet in the Solar System with the second most circular orbit after [Venus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Venus "Venus").[[134]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-142) The orbit of Neptune is [inclined](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_inclination "Orbital inclination") 1.77° compared to that of Earth.

On 11 July 2011, Neptune completed its first full [barycentric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barycenter "Barycenter") orbit since its discovery in 1846;[[135]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-obsjul711-143) it did not appear at its exact discovery position in the sky because Earth was in a different location in its 365.26-day orbit. Because of the motion of the Sun in relation to the barycentre of the Solar System, on 11 July, Neptune was not at its exact discovery position in relation to the Sun—if the more common [heliocentric coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_coordinate_systems "Solar coordinate systems") is used, the discovery longitude was reached on 12 July 2011.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-fact2-15)[[136]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-UT2010-08-144)[[137]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-145)[[138]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-Horizons2011-146)

The [axial tilt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axial_tilt "Axial tilt") of Neptune is 28.32°,[[139]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-147) which is similar to the tilts of Earth (23°) and Mars (25°). As a result, Neptune experiences seasonal changes similar to those on Earth. The long orbital period of Neptune means that the seasons last for forty Earth years.[[110]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-villard-117) Its sidereal rotation period (day) is roughly 16.11 hours.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-fact2-15) Because its axial tilt is comparable to Earth's, the variation in the length of its day over the course of its long year is not any more extreme.

Because Neptune is not a solid body, its atmosphere undergoes [differential rotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_rotation "Differential rotation"). The wide equatorial zone rotates with a period of about 18 hours, which is slower than the 16.1-hour rotation of the planet's magnetic field. By contrast, the reverse is true for the polar regions where the rotation period is 12 hours. This differential rotation is the most pronounced of any planet in the Solar System,[[140]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-148) and it results in strong latitudinal wind shear.[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-apj125-97)

Formation and resonances
------------------------

### Formation

[![Image 23](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Lhborbits.png/500px-Lhborbits.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Lhborbits.png)

A simulation showing the outer planets and Kuiper belt: a) before Jupiter and Saturn reached a 2:1 resonance; b) after inward scattering of Kuiper belt objects following the orbital shift of Neptune; c) after ejection of scattered Kuiper belt bodies by Jupiter

The formation of the ice giants, Neptune and Uranus, has been difficult to model precisely. Current models suggest that the matter density in the outer regions of the Solar System was too low to account for the formation of such large bodies from the traditionally accepted method of core [accretion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accretion_(astrophysics) "Accretion (astrophysics)"), and various hypotheses have been advanced to explain their formation. One is that the ice giants were not formed by core accretion but from instabilities within the original [protoplanetary disc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protoplanetary_disk "Protoplanetary disk") and later had their atmospheres blasted away by radiation from a nearby massive [OB star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OB_star "OB star").[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-Boss-80)

An alternative concept is that they formed closer to the Sun, where the matter density was higher, and then subsequently [migrated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_migration "Planetary migration") to their current orbits after the removal of the gaseous protoplanetary disc.[[141]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-149) This hypothesis of migration after formation is favoured due to its ability to better explain the occupancy of the populations of small objects observed in the trans-Neptunian region.[[142]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-150) The current most widely accepted[[143]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-Crida2009-151)[[144]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-Desch07-152)[[145]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-Smith2009-153) explanation of the details of this hypothesis is known as the [Nice model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nice_model "Nice model"), which is a [dynamical evolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formation_and_evolution_of_the_Solar_System "Formation and evolution of the Solar System") scenario that explores the potential effect of a migrating Neptune and the other giant planets on the structure of the Kuiper belt.

### Orbital resonances

[![Image 24](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/TheKuiperBelt_classes-en.svg/250px-TheKuiperBelt_classes-en.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:TheKuiperBelt_classes-en.svg)

A diagram showing the major orbital resonances in the [Kuiper belt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kuiper_belt "Kuiper belt") caused by Neptune: the highlighted regions are the 2:3 resonance ([plutinos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plutino "Plutino")), the nonresonant ["classical belt"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_Kuiper_belt_object "Classical Kuiper belt object") (cubewanos), and the 1:2 resonance ([twotinos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Twotino "Twotino")).

Neptune's orbit has a profound impact on the region directly beyond it, known as the [Kuiper belt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kuiper_belt "Kuiper belt"). The Kuiper belt is a ring of small icy worlds, similar to the [asteroid belt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asteroid_belt "Asteroid belt") but far larger, extending from Neptune's orbit at 30 AU out to about 55 AU from the Sun.[[146]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-154) Much in the same way that [Jupiter's gravity dominates the asteroid belt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jupiter#Impacts "Jupiter"), Neptune's gravity dominates the Kuiper belt. Over the age of the Solar System, certain regions of the Kuiper belt became destabilised by Neptune's gravity, creating gaps in its structure. The region between 40 and 42 AU is an example.[[147]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-155)

There do exist orbits within these empty regions where objects can survive for the age of the Solar System. These [resonances](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_resonance "Orbital resonance") occur when Neptune's orbital period is a precise fraction of that of the object, such as 1:2, or 3:4. If, say, an object orbits the Sun once for every two Neptune orbits, it will only complete half an orbit by the time Neptune returns to its original position. The most heavily populated resonance in the Kuiper belt, with over 200 known objects,[[148]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-156) is the 2:3 resonance. Objects in this resonance complete 2 orbits for every 3 of Neptune, and are known as [plutinos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plutino "Plutino") because the largest of the known Kuiper belt objects, [Pluto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pluto "Pluto"), is among them.[[149]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-157) Although Pluto crosses Neptune's orbit regularly, the 2:3 resonance makes it so that they can never collide.[[150]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-158) The 3:4, 3:5, 4:7 and 2:5 resonances are less populated.[[151]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-159)

Neptune has a number of known [trojan objects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trojan_(celestial_body) "Trojan (celestial body)") occupying both the [Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun "Sun")–Neptune [L 4](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrange_point#L4_and_L5 "Lagrange point") and [L 5](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrange_point#L4_and_L5 "Lagrange point")[Lagrangian points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrangian_point "Lagrangian point")—gravitationally stable regions leading and trailing Neptune in its orbit, respectively.[[152]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-160)[Neptune trojans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune_trojan "Neptune trojan") can be viewed as being in a 1:1 resonance with Neptune. Some Neptune trojans are remarkably stable in their orbits, and are likely to have formed alongside Neptune rather than being [captured](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_capture "Gravitational capture"). The first object identified as associated with Neptune's trailing [L 5](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrange_point#L4_and_L5 "Lagrange point") Lagrangian point was [2008 LC 18](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2008_LC18 "2008 LC18").[[153]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-Sheppard-161) Neptune has a temporary [quasi-satellite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasi-satellite "Quasi-satellite"), [(309239) 2007 RW 10](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/(309239)_2007_RW10 "(309239) 2007 RW10").[[154]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-quasi-162) The object has been a quasi-satellite of Neptune for about 12,500 years and it will remain in that dynamical state for another 12,500 years.[[154]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-quasi-162)

Moons
-----

[![Image 25: Shown in this image are Neptune and its moons: Triton, Galatea, Naiad, Thalassa, Despina, Proteus, and Larissa](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/New_Webb_Images_Capture_Rare_View_of_Neptune%E2%80%99s_Rings_%28Labeled%29.png/250px-New_Webb_Images_Capture_Rare_View_of_Neptune%E2%80%99s_Rings_%28Labeled%29.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:New_Webb_Images_Capture_Rare_View_of_Neptune%E2%80%99s_Rings_(Labeled).png)

An annotated picture of Neptune's many moons as captured by the [James Webb Space Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Webb_Space_Telescope "James Webb Space Telescope"). The bright blue [diffraction star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffraction_spike "Diffraction spike") is [Triton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triton_(moon) "Triton (moon)"), Neptune's largest moon.

Neptune has 16 known [moons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_satellite "Natural satellite").[[155]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-Sheppardmoons2024-163)[Triton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triton_(moon) "Triton (moon)") is the largest Neptunian moon, accounting for more than 99.5% of the mass in orbit around Neptune,[[i]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-164) and is the only one massive enough to be [spheroidal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spheroid "Spheroid"). Triton was discovered by [William Lassell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Lassell "William Lassell") just 17 days after the discovery of Neptune itself. Unlike all other large planetary moons in the Solar System, Triton has a [retrograde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Retrograde_and_prograde_motion "Retrograde and prograde motion") orbit, indicating that it was captured rather than forming in place; it was probably once a [dwarf planet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dwarf_planet "Dwarf planet") in the Kuiper belt.[[156]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-165) It is close enough to Neptune to be locked into a [synchronous rotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synchronous_rotation "Synchronous rotation"), and it is slowly spiralling inward because of [tidal acceleration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tidal_acceleration "Tidal acceleration"). It will eventually be torn apart, in about 3.6 billion years, when it reaches the [Roche limit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roche_limit "Roche limit").[[157]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-166) In 1989, Triton was the coldest object that had yet been measured in the Solar System,[[158]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-167) with estimated temperatures of 38 K (−235°C).[[159]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-168)[[160]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-169) This very low temperature is due to Triton's very high albedo which causes it to reflect a lot of sunlight instead of absorbing it.[[161]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-170)[[162]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-171)

Neptune's second-known satellite (by order of discovery), the irregular moon [Nereid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nereid_(moon) "Nereid (moon)"), has one of the most eccentric orbits of any satellite in the Solar System. The eccentricity of 0.7512 gives it an [apoapsis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apoapsis "Apoapsis") that is seven times its [periapsis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Periapsis "Periapsis") distance from Neptune.[[j]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-172)

From July to September 1989, _Voyager 2_ discovered six moons of Neptune.[[163]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-science4936-2-173) Of these, the irregularly shaped [Proteus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proteus_(moon) "Proteus (moon)") is notable for being as large as a body of its density can be without being pulled into a spherical shape by its own gravity.[[164]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-Brown-174) Although the second-most-massive Neptunian moon, it is only 0.25% the mass of Triton. Neptune's innermost four moons—[Naiad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naiad_(moon) "Naiad (moon)"), [Thalassa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thalassa_(moon) "Thalassa (moon)"), [Despina](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Despina_(moon) "Despina (moon)") and [Galatea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galatea_(moon) "Galatea (moon)")—orbit close enough to be within Neptune's rings. The next-farthest out, [Larissa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Larissa_(moon) "Larissa (moon)"), was originally discovered in 1981 when it had occulted a star. This occultation had been attributed to ring arcs, but when _Voyager 2_ observed Neptune in 1989, Larissa was found to have caused it. Five new irregular moons discovered between 2002 and 2003 were announced in 2004.[[165]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-HolmanKavelaarsGrav2004-175)[[166]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-176) A new moon and the smallest yet, [Hippocamp](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hippocamp_(moon) "Hippocamp (moon)"), was found in 2013 by combining multiple Hubble images.[[167]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-177) Because Neptune was the Roman god of the sea, Neptune's moons have been named after lesser sea gods.[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-USGS-60)

Planetary rings
---------------

[![Image 26](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/New_Webb_Image_Captures_Clearest_View_of_Neptune%E2%80%99s_Rings_in_Decades.png/250px-New_Webb_Image_Captures_Clearest_View_of_Neptune%E2%80%99s_Rings_in_Decades.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:New_Webb_Image_Captures_Clearest_View_of_Neptune%E2%80%99s_Rings_in_Decades.png)

Neptune's rings and moons viewed in infrared by the [James Webb Space Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Webb_Space_Telescope "James Webb Space Telescope"), in 2022.

Neptune has a [planetary ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_ring "Planetary ring") system, though one much less substantial than that of [Saturn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rings_of_Saturn "Rings of Saturn") and [Uranus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rings_of_Uranus "Rings of Uranus").[[168]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-NYT-20220921-178) The rings may consist of ice particles coated with silicates or carbon-based material, which most likely gives them a reddish hue.[[169]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-179) The three main rings are the narrow Adams Ring, 63,000 km from the centre of Neptune, the Le Verrier Ring, at 53,000 km, and the broader, fainter Galle Ring, at 42,000 km. A faint outward extension to the Le Verrier Ring has been named Lassell; it is bounded at its outer edge by the Arago Ring at 57,000 km.[[170]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-180)

The first of these planetary rings was detected in 1968 by a team led by [Edward Guinan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edward_Guinan "Edward Guinan").[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-ring1-37)[[171]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-181) In the early 1980s, analysis of this data along with newer observations led to the hypothesis that this ring might be incomplete.[[172]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-182) Evidence that the rings might have gaps first arose during a stellar [occultation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Occultation "Occultation") in 1984 when the rings obscured a star on immersion but not on emersion.[[173]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-Nicholson90-183) Images from _Voyager 2_ in 1989 settled the issue by showing several faint rings.

The outermost ring, Adams, contains five prominent arcs now named _Courage_, _Liberté_, _Egalité 1_, _Egalité 2_ and _Fraternité_ (Courage, Liberty, Equality and Fraternity).[[174]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-184) The existence of arcs was difficult to explain because the laws of motion would predict that arcs would spread out into a uniform ring over short timescales. Astronomers now estimate that the arcs are corralled into their current form by the gravitational effects of [Galatea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galatea_(moon) "Galatea (moon)"), a moon just inward from the ring.[[175]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-185)[[176]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-186)

Earth-based observations announced in 2005 appeared to show that Neptune's rings were much more unstable than previously thought. Images taken from the [W. M. Keck Observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/W._M._Keck_Observatory "W. M. Keck Observatory") in 2002 and 2003 show considerable decay in the rings when compared to images by _Voyager 2_. In particular, it seems that the _Liberté_ arc might disappear in as little as one century.[[177]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-187)

Observation
-----------

[![Image 27](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Neptunlauf_September_Oktober2022.png/250px-Neptunlauf_September_Oktober2022.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Neptunlauf_September_Oktober2022.png)

Movement of Neptune in front of the stars of [Aquarius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aquarius_(constellation) "Aquarius (constellation)") in 2022

Neptune brightened about 10% between 1980 and 2000 mostly due to the changing of the seasons.[[178]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-Schmude_et_al-188) Neptune may continue to brighten as it approaches perihelion in 2042. The [apparent magnitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apparent_magnitude "Apparent magnitude") currently ranges from 7.67 to 7.89 with a mean of 7.78 and a standard deviation of 0.06.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-Mallama_and_Hilton-22) Prior to 1980, the planet was as faint as magnitude 8.0.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-Mallama_and_Hilton-22) Neptune is too faint to be visible to the [naked eye](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naked_eye "Naked eye"). It can be outshone by Jupiter's [Galilean moons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galilean_moons "Galilean moons"), the [dwarf planet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dwarf_planet "Dwarf planet")[Ceres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ceres_(dwarf_planet) "Ceres (dwarf planet)") and the [asteroids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asteroid "Asteroid")[4 Vesta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/4_Vesta "4 Vesta"), [2 Pallas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2_Pallas "2 Pallas"), [7 Iris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/7_Iris "7 Iris"), [3 Juno](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3_Juno "3 Juno"), and [6 Hebe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/6_Hebe "6 Hebe").[[179]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-189) A telescope or strong binoculars will resolve Neptune as a small blue disk, similar in appearance to Uranus.[[180]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-190)

Because of the distance of Neptune from Earth, its [angular diameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_diameter "Angular diameter") only ranges from 2.2 to 2.4[arcseconds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arcsecond "Arcsecond"),[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-fact-10)[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-ephemeris-24) the smallest of the Solar System planets. Its small apparent size makes it challenging to study visually. Most telescopic data was fairly limited until the advent of the [Hubble Space Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope "Hubble Space Telescope") and large ground-based telescopes with [adaptive optics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adaptive_optics "Adaptive optics") (AO).[[181]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-191)[[182]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-192)[[183]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-193) The first scientifically useful observation of Neptune from ground-based telescopes using adaptive optics was commenced in 1997 from Hawaii.[[184]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-194) Neptune is currently approaching perihelion (closest approach to the Sun) and has been shown to be heating up, with increased atmospheric activity and brightness as a consequence. Combined with technological advancements, ground-based telescopes with adaptive optics are recording increasingly more detailed images of it. Both _Hubble_ and the adaptive-optics telescopes on Earth have made many new discoveries within the Solar System since the mid-1990s, with a large increase in the number of known satellites and moons around the outer planet, among others. In 2004 and 2005, five new small satellites of Neptune with diameters between 38 and 61 kilometres were discovered.[[185]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-195)

From Earth, Neptune goes through [apparent retrograde motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apparent_retrograde_motion "Apparent retrograde motion") every 367 days, resulting in a looping motion against the background stars during each [opposition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Opposition_(astronomy) "Opposition (astronomy)"). These loops carried it close to the 1846 discovery coordinates in April and July 2010 and again in October and November 2011.[[138]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-Horizons2011-146)

Neptune's 164-year orbital period means that the planet takes an average of 13 years to move through each constellation of the zodiac. In 2011, it completed its first full orbit of the Sun since being discovered and returned to where it was first spotted northeast of [Iota Aquarii](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iota_Aquarii "Iota Aquarii").[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-auto-48)

Observation of Neptune in the radio-frequency band shows that it is a source of both continuous emission and irregular bursts. Both sources are thought to originate from its rotating magnetic field.[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-elkins-tanton-96) In the [infrared](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infrared "Infrared") part of the spectrum, Neptune's storms appear bright against the cooler background, allowing the size and shape of these features to be readily tracked.[[186]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-196)

Exploration
-----------

[![Image 28](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Animation_of_Voyager_2_trajectory.gif/330px-Animation_of_Voyager_2_trajectory.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Animation_of_Voyager_2_trajectory.gif)

Animation of _Voyager 2_'s trajectory from 20 August 1977 to 30 December 2000 _Voyager 2_**·**[Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth "Earth")**·**[Jupiter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jupiter "Jupiter")**·**[Saturn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saturn "Saturn")**·**[Uranus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uranus "Uranus")**·**Neptune**·**[Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun "Sun")

_[Voyager 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voyager\_2 "Voyager 2")_ is the only spacecraft that has visited Neptune. The spacecraft's closest approach to the planet occurred on 25 August 1989. Because this was the last major planet the spacecraft could visit, it was decided to make a close flyby of the moon [Triton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triton_(moon) "Triton (moon)"), regardless of the consequences to the trajectory, similarly to what was done for _[Voyager 1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voyager\_1 "Voyager 1")_'s encounter with [Saturn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saturn "Saturn") and its moon [Titan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Titan_(moon) "Titan (moon)"). The images relayed back to Earth from _Voyager 2_ became the basis of a 1989 [PBS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PBS "PBS") all-night program, _[Neptune All Night](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune\_All\_Night "Neptune All Night")_.[[187]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-197)

During the encounter, signals from the spacecraft required 246 minutes to reach Earth. Hence, for the most part, _Voyager 2_'s mission relied on preloaded commands for the Neptune encounter. The spacecraft performed a near-encounter with the moon [Nereid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nereid_(moon) "Nereid (moon)") before it came within 4,400 km of Neptune's atmosphere on 25 August, then passed close to the planet's largest moon Triton later the same day.[[188]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-burgess-198)

The spacecraft verified the existence of a magnetic field surrounding the planet and discovered that the field was offset from the centre and tilted in a manner similar to the field around Uranus. Neptune's rotation period was determined using measurements of radio emissions and _Voyager 2_ showed that Neptune had a surprisingly active weather system. Six new moons were discovered, and the planet was shown to have more than one ring.[[163]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-science4936-2-173)[[188]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-burgess-198) The flyby provided the first accurate measurement of Neptune's mass which was found to be 0.5 per cent less than previously calculated. The new figure disproved the hypothesis that an undiscovered [Planet X](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planet_X "Planet X") acted upon the orbits of Neptune and Uranus.[[189]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-199)[[190]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-200)

Since 2018, the [China National Space Administration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_National_Space_Administration "China National Space Administration") has been studying a concept for a pair of _Voyager_-like interstellar probes tentatively known as _[Shensuo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shensuo\_(spacecraft) "Shensuo (spacecraft)")_.[[191]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-WuEtAl2019-201) Both probes would be launched in the 2020s and take differing paths to explore opposing ends of the [heliosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heliosphere "Heliosphere"); the second probe, _IHP-2_, would fly by Neptune in January 2038, passing only 1,000 km above the cloud tops, and potentially carry an atmospheric impactor to be released during its approach.[[192]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-SpaceNews_IHP-202) Afterward, it will continue its mission throughout the [Kuiper belt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kuiper_belt "Kuiper belt") toward the heliosphere tail, which is so far unexplored.

After _Voyager 2_ and _IHP-2_'s flybys, the next step in scientific exploration of the Neptunian system is considered to be an orbital mission; most proposals have been by [NASA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NASA "NASA"), most often for a [Flagship](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flagship_Program "Flagship Program") orbiter.[[193]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-Flagship-203) In 2003, there was a proposal in NASA's "Vision Missions Studies" for a "Neptune Orbiter with Probes" mission that does _[Cassini](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens "Cassini–Huygens")_-level science.[[194]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-204) A subsequent proposal, that was not selected, was for _[Argo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Argo\_(NASA\_spacecraft) "Argo (NASA spacecraft)")_, a flyby spacecraft to be launched in 2019, that would visit Jupiter, Saturn, Neptune, and a Kuiper belt object. The focus would have been on Neptune and its largest moon Triton to be investigated around 2029.[[195]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-argho1-205) The proposed _[New Horizons 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New\_Horizons\_2 "New Horizons 2")_ mission might have done a close flyby of the Neptunian system, but it was later scrapped. Currently a pending proposal for the [Discovery Program](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discovery_Program "Discovery Program"), the [_Trident_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trident_(spacecraft) "Trident (spacecraft)") spacecraft would conduct a flyby of Neptune and Triton;[[196]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-Trident_Description-206) however, the mission was not selected for Discovery 15 or 16. [Neptune Odyssey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune_Odyssey "Neptune Odyssey") is another concept for a Neptune orbiter and atmospheric probe that was studied as a possible [large strategic science mission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Large_strategic_science_mission "Large strategic science mission") by NASA; it would have launched between 2031 and 2033, and arrive at Neptune by 2049.[[197]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-Rymer-207) However, for logistical reasons the [Uranus Orbiter and Probe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uranus_Orbiter_and_Probe "Uranus Orbiter and Probe") mission was selected as the [ice giant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ice_giant "Ice giant") orbiter mission recommendation, with top priority ahead of the [Enceladus Orbilander](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enceladus_Orbilander "Enceladus Orbilander").[[198]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-2023decadal-208) Two notable proposals for a Triton-focused Neptune orbiter mission that would be costed right between the _Trident_ and _Odyssey_ missions (under the [New Frontiers program](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Frontiers_program "New Frontiers program")) are _Triton Ocean World Surveyor_ and _Nautilus_, with cruise stages taking place in the 2031–47 and 2041–56 time periods, respectively.[[199]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-209)[[200]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-Steckel_Conrad_Dekarske_Dolan_2023-210) Neptune is a potential target for China's _Tianwen-5_, which could arrive in 2058.[[201]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_note-211)

Notes

1.   **[^](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_ref-2)**Neptune's dark spots are not permanent features; the large dark spot observed by _Voyager 2_ was designated GDS-89 for "Great Dark Spot 1989".
2.   **[^](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune#cite_ref-barycentre_8-0)**Orbital elements refer to the Neptune barycentre and Solar System barycentre. These are the instantaneous [osculating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osculating_orbit "Osculating orbit") values at the precise [J2000](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J2000 "J2000") epoch. Barycentre quantities are given because, in contrast to the planetary centre, they do not experience appreciable changes on a day-to-day basis from the motion of the moons.
