From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

[![Image 1](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Hfspec1.svg/330px-Hfspec1.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Hfspec1.svg)

Computed energy level spectrum of hydrogen as a function of the electric field near _n_ = 15 for [magnetic quantum number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_quantum_number "Magnetic quantum number")_m_ = 0. Each [_n_ level](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principal_quantum_number "Principal quantum number") consists of _n_ − 1 [degenerate sublevels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degenerate_energy_level "Degenerate energy level"); application of an [electric field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_field "Electric field") breaks the degeneracy. Energy levels can cross due to [underlying symmetries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laplace%E2%80%93Runge%E2%80%93Lenz_vector "Laplace–Runge–Lenz vector") of motion in the [Coulomb potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coulomb_potential "Coulomb potential").

The **Stark effect** is the shifting and splitting of [spectral lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectral_line "Spectral line") of atoms and molecules due to the presence of an external [electric field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_field "Electric field"). It is the electric-field analogue of the [Zeeman effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zeeman_effect "Zeeman effect"), where a spectral line is split into several components due to the presence of the [magnetic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_field "Magnetic field"). Although initially coined for the static case, it is also used in the wider context to describe the effect of time-dependent electric fields. In particular, the Stark effect is responsible for the [pressure broadening](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectral_line#Pressure_broadening "Spectral line") (Stark broadening) of spectral lines by charged particles in [plasmas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasma_(physics) "Plasma (physics)"). For most spectral lines, the Stark effect is either linear (proportional to the applied electric field) or quadratic with a high accuracy.

The Stark effect can be observed both for emission and absorption lines. The latter was sometimes called the **inverse Stark effect**, but this term is no longer used in the modern literature.

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Lfspec1.jpg/500px-Lfspec1.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Lfspec1.jpg)

 Lithium [Rydberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rydberg_atom "Rydberg atom")-level spectrum as a function of the electric field near _n_ = 15 for _m_ = 0. Note how a complicated pattern of the energy levels emerges as the electric field increases, not unlike [bifurcations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bifurcation_theory "Bifurcation theory") of [closed orbits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptical_orbit "Elliptical orbit") in classical [dynamical systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynamical_system "Dynamical system") leading to [chaos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chaos_theory "Chaos theory"). [[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stark_effect#cite_note-Courtney1995-1)

The effect is named after the German physicist [Johannes Stark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johannes_Stark "Johannes Stark"), who discovered it in 1913. It was independently discovered in the same year by the Italian physicist [Antonino Lo Surdo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antonino_Lo_Surdo "Antonino Lo Surdo"). The discovery of this effect contributed importantly to the development of quantum theory and Stark was awarded with the [Nobel Prize in Physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physics "Nobel Prize in Physics") in the year 1919.

Inspired by the magnetic [Zeeman effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zeeman_effect "Zeeman effect"), and especially by [Hendrik Lorentz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hendrik_Lorentz "Hendrik Lorentz")'s explanation of it, [Woldemar Voigt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Woldemar_Voigt "Woldemar Voigt")[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stark_effect#cite_note-2) performed classical mechanical calculations of quasi-elastically bound electrons in an electric field. By using experimental indices of refraction he gave an estimate of the Stark splittings. This estimate was a few orders of magnitude too low. Not deterred by this prediction, Stark undertook measurements[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stark_effect#cite_note-3) on excited states of the hydrogen atom and succeeded in observing splittings.

By the use of the Bohr–Sommerfeld [("old") quantum theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Old_quantum_theory "Old quantum theory"), [Paul Epstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Sophus_Epstein "Paul Sophus Epstein")[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stark_effect#cite_note-4) and [Karl Schwarzschild](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karl_Schwarzschild "Karl Schwarzschild")[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stark_effect#cite_note-5) were independently able to derive equations for the linear and quadratic Stark effect in [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen"). Four years later, [Hendrik Kramers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hendrik_Anthony_Kramers "Hendrik Anthony Kramers")[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stark_effect#cite_note-6) derived formulas for intensities of spectral transitions. Kramers also included the effect of [fine structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fine_structure "Fine structure"), with corrections for relativistic kinetic energy and coupling between electron spin and orbital motion. The first quantum mechanical treatment (in the framework of [Werner Heisenberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Werner_Heisenberg "Werner Heisenberg")'s [matrix mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_mechanics "Matrix mechanics")) was by [Wolfgang Pauli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Pauli "Wolfgang Pauli").[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stark_effect#cite_note-7)[Erwin Schrödinger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erwin_Schr%C3%B6dinger "Erwin Schrödinger") discussed at length the Stark effect in his third paper[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stark_effect#cite_note-8) on quantum theory (in which he introduced his perturbation theory), once in the manner of the 1916 work of Epstein (but generalized from the old to the new quantum theory) and once by his (first-order) perturbation approach. Finally, Epstein reconsidered[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stark_effect#cite_note-epstein:1926a-9) the linear and quadratic Stark effect from the point of view of the new quantum theory. He derived equations for the line intensities which were a decided improvement over Kramers's results obtained by the old quantum theory.

While the first-order-perturbation (linear) Stark effect in hydrogen is in agreement with both the old Bohr–Sommerfeld model and the [quantum-mechanical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics") theory of the atom, higher-order corrections are not.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stark_effect#cite_note-epstein:1926a-9) Measurements of the Stark effect under high field strengths confirmed the correctness of the new quantum theory.

Imagine an atom with occupied 2s and 2p [electron states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_configuration "Electron configuration"). In the [Bohr model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bohr_model "Bohr model"), these states are [degenerate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degenerate_energy_levels "Degenerate energy levels"). However, in the presence of an external electric field, these electron orbitals will [hybridize](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_hybridisation "Orbital hybridisation") into eigenstates of the [perturbed Hamiltonian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perturbation_theory_(quantum_mechanics) "Perturbation theory (quantum mechanics)") (where each perturbed hybrid state can be written as a superpositon of unperturbed states). Since the 2s and 2p states have opposite [parity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parity_(physics) "Parity (physics)"), these hybrid states will lack inversion symmetry and will possess a time-averaged electric dipole moment. If this dipole moment is aligned with the electric field, the energy of the state will shift down; if this dipole moment is anti-aligned with the electric field, the energy of the state will shift up. Thus, the Stark effect causes a splitting of the original degeneracy.

Other things being equal, the effect of the electric field is greater for outer [electron shells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_shell "Electron shell") because the electron is more distant from the nucleus, resulting in a larger electric dipole moment upon hybridization.

### Multipole expansion

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Stark_effect&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Multipole expansion")]

The Stark effect originates from the interaction between a [charge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_charge "Electric charge") distribution (atom or molecule) and an external [electric field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_field "Electric field"). The interaction energy of a continuous charge distribution ![Image 3: {\displaystyle \rho (\mathbf {r} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/77f477411625125978c0a18946bdfae2c1f13bcb), confined within a finite volume ![Image 4: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {V}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47d69f309b6deb2e5008f6130ee11e09bbabd7b6), with an external [electrostatic potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrostatic#Electrostatic_potential "Electrostatic")![Image 5: {\displaystyle \phi (\mathbf {r} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b691651163c74d532577e18847c7cdd92c7c1b26) is ![Image 6: {\displaystyle V_{\mathrm {int} }=\int _{\mathcal {V}}\rho (\mathbf {r} )\phi (\mathbf {r} )\,d^{3}\mathbf {r} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6978ce297ff718b77930b07c0ef26b9a9f7be3a8) This expression is valid [classically](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_physics "Classical physics") and quantum-mechanically alike. If the potential varies weakly over the charge distribution, the [multipole expansion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multipole_expansion "Multipole expansion") converges fast, so only a few first terms give an accurate approximation. Namely, keeping only the zero- and first-order terms, ![Image 7: {\displaystyle \phi (\mathbf {r} )\approx \phi (\mathbf {0} )-\sum _{i=1}^{3}r_{i}F_{i},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/90ff9f6b4111a3e8ee2a59ccb50d4f669c98ada2) where we introduced the electric field ![Image 8: {\textstyle F_{i}\equiv -\left.\left({\frac {\partial \phi }{\partial r_{i}}}\right)\right|_{\mathbf {0} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6d77270c33dd42eda1bd7f8237219d9cc2dfb981) and assumed the origin **0** to be somewhere within ![Image 9: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {V}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47d69f309b6deb2e5008f6130ee11e09bbabd7b6). Therefore, the interaction becomes ![Image 10: {\displaystyle V_{\mathrm {int} }\approx \phi (\mathbf {0} )\int _{\mathcal {V}}\rho (\mathbf {r} )d^{3}r-\sum _{i=1}^{3}F_{i}\int _{\mathcal {V}}\rho (\mathbf {r} )r_{i}d^{3}r\equiv q\phi (\mathbf {0} )-\sum _{i=1}^{3}\mu _{i}F_{i}=q\phi (\mathbf {0} )-{\boldsymbol {\mu }}\cdot \mathbf {F} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c70682f4f780b4f8e16e8e77397b8972e99c31be) where ![Image 11: {\displaystyle q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/06809d64fa7c817ffc7e323f85997f783dbdf71d) and ![Image 12: {\displaystyle \mathbf {\mu } }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e407f1e8e83fffbed7e61f4112b7eef6b22b9e67) are, respectively, the total charge (zero [moment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_(physics) "Moment (physics)")) and the [dipole moment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dipole "Dipole") of the charge distribution.

Classical macroscopic objects are usually neutral or quasi-neutral (![Image 13: {\displaystyle q=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/654c2d5dc1a26e0af36dc0deb5fd252c6178977a)), so the first, monopole, term in the expression above is identically zero. This is also the case for a neutral atom or molecule. However, for an [ion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ion "Ion") this is no longer true. Nevertheless, it is often justified to omit it in this case, too. Indeed, the Stark effect is observed in spectral lines, which are emitted when an electron "jumps" between two [bound states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bound_state "Bound state"). Since such a transition only alters the internal [degrees of freedom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_of_freedom "Degree of freedom") of the radiator but not its charge, the effects of the monopole interaction on the initial and final states exactly cancel each other.

### Perturbation theory

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Stark_effect&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Perturbation theory")]

Turning now to quantum mechanics an atom or a molecule can be thought of as a collection of point charges (electrons and nuclei), so that the second definition of the dipole applies. The interaction of atom or molecule with a uniform external field is described by the operator ![Image 14: {\displaystyle V_{\mathrm {int} }=-\mathbf {F} \cdot {\boldsymbol {\mu }}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6af0dda41218212b53b00861ccfde8a106f3642c) This operator is used as a perturbation in first- and second-order [perturbation theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perturbation_theory "Perturbation theory") to account for the first- and second-order Stark effect.

Let the unperturbed atom or molecule be in a _g_-fold degenerate state with orthonormal zeroth-order state functions ![Image 15: {\displaystyle \psi _{1}^{0},\ldots ,\psi _{g}^{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/31157af7e98539291a1257d38766cc7cfa5f2af5). (Non-degeneracy is the special case _g_ = 1). According to perturbation theory the first-order energies are the eigenvalues of the _g_ × _g_ matrix with general element ![Image 16: {\displaystyle (\mathbf {V} _{\mathrm {int} })_{kl}=\langle \psi _{k}^{0}|V_{\mathrm {int} }|\psi _{l}^{0}\rangle =-\mathbf {F} \cdot \langle \psi _{k}^{0}|{\boldsymbol {\mu }}|\psi _{l}^{0}\rangle ,\qquad k,l=1,\ldots ,g.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b164742eac0ccb30752b64f3f2b1aa71f70db13) If _g_ = 1 (as is often the case for electronic states of molecules) the first-order energy becomes proportional to the expectation (average) value of the dipole operator ![Image 17: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {\mu }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a1aee7d7b4a36d96dfb35bfee9c7751bba1fdfbe), ![Image 18: {\displaystyle E^{(1)}=-\mathbf {F} \cdot \langle \psi _{1}^{0}|{\boldsymbol {\mu }}|\psi _{1}^{0}\rangle =-\mathbf {F} \cdot \langle {\boldsymbol {\mu }}\rangle .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/80729f72427757384779cd65129b6abd39ebe198)Since the electric dipole moment is a vector ([tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor "Tensor") of the first rank), the diagonal elements of the perturbation matrix **V**int vanish between states that have a definite [parity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parity_(physics) "Parity (physics)"). Atoms and molecules possessing inversion symmetry do not have a (permanent) dipole moment and hence do not show a linear Stark effect.

In order to obtain a non-zero matrix **V**int for systems with an inversion center it is necessary that some of the unperturbed functions ![Image 19: {\displaystyle \psi _{i}^{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9fc8ec2e53cdad87b1a8a4ac6b4b932cc0575a4a) have opposite parity (obtain plus and minus under inversion), because only functions of opposite parity give non-vanishing matrix elements. Degenerate zeroth-order states of opposite parity occur for excited hydrogen-like (one-electron) atoms or Rydberg states. Neglecting [fine-structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fine_structure "Fine structure") effects, such a state with the principal quantum number _n_ is _n_ 2-fold degenerate and ![Image 20: {\displaystyle n^{2}=\sum _{\ell =0}^{n-1}(2\ell +1),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c8d46bb13e6f8a4f03f0ddc343d505dd332530d1) where ![Image 21: {\displaystyle \ell }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f066e981e530bacc07efc6a10fa82deee985929e) is the azimuthal (angular momentum) quantum number. For instance, the excited _n_ = 4 state contains the following ![Image 22: {\displaystyle \ell }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f066e981e530bacc07efc6a10fa82deee985929e) states, ![Image 23: {\displaystyle 16=1+3+5+7\;\;\Longrightarrow \;\;n=4\;{\text{contains}}\;s\oplus p\oplus d\oplus f.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4ce7b9d3c71a68591d89377b5647d00a88ccb17a) The one-electron states with even ![Image 24: {\displaystyle \ell }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f066e981e530bacc07efc6a10fa82deee985929e) are even under parity, while those with odd ![Image 25: {\displaystyle \ell }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f066e981e530bacc07efc6a10fa82deee985929e) are odd under parity. Hence hydrogen-like atoms with _n_>1 show first-order Stark effect.

The first-order Stark effect occurs in rotational transitions of [symmetric top molecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy#Classification_of_molecular_rotors "Rotational spectroscopy") (but not for linear and asymmetric molecules). In first approximation a molecule may be seen as a rigid rotor. A symmetric top [rigid rotor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rigid_rotor "Rigid rotor") has the unperturbed eigenstates ![Image 26: {\displaystyle |JKM\rangle =(D_{MK}^{J})^{*}\quad {\text{with}}\quad M,K=-J,-J+1,\dots ,J}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d4bc95a790d096b2d96d3352d90e4322d51a31e9) with 2(2 _J_+1)-fold degenerate energy for |K| > 0 and (2 _J_+1)-fold degenerate energy for K=0. Here _D_ _J_ _MK_ is an element of the [Wigner D-matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wigner_D-matrix "Wigner D-matrix"). The first-order perturbation matrix on basis of the unperturbed rigid rotor function is non-zero and can be diagonalized. This gives shifts and splittings in the rotational spectrum. Quantitative analysis of these Stark shift yields the permanent [electric dipole moment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_dipole_moment "Electric dipole moment") of the symmetric top molecule.

As stated, the quadratic Stark effect is described by second-order perturbation theory. The zeroth-order [eigenproblem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eigenvalues_and_eigenvectors "Eigenvalues and eigenvectors")![Image 27: {\displaystyle H^{(0)}\psi _{k}^{0}=E_{k}^{(0)}\psi _{k}^{0},\quad k=0,1,\ldots ,\quad E_{0}^{(0)}<E_{1}^{(0)}\leq E_{2}^{(0)},\dots }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eeecf08c0233e7fa2231a283a40f220f5b090362) is assumed to be solved. The perturbation theory gives ![Image 28: {\displaystyle E_{k}^{(2)}=\sum _{k'\neq k}{\frac {\langle \psi _{k}^{0}|V_{\mathrm {int} }|\psi _{k^{\prime }}^{0}\rangle \langle \psi _{k'}^{0}|V_{\mathrm {int} }|\psi _{k}^{0}\rangle }{E_{k}^{(0)}-E_{k'}^{(0)}}}\equiv -{\frac {1}{2}}\sum _{i,j=1}^{3}\alpha _{ij}F_{i}F_{j}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38e057c730bebc383b13271a3ed7a715a432cc39) with the components of the [polarizability tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polarizability "Polarizability") α defined by ![Image 29: {\displaystyle \alpha _{ij}=-2\sum _{k'\neq k}{\frac {\langle \psi _{k}^{0}|\mu _{i}|\psi _{k'}^{0}\rangle \langle \psi _{k'}^{0}|\mu _{j}|\psi _{k}^{0}\rangle }{E_{k}^{(0)}-E_{k'}^{(0)}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2f5043c3bf09d7a8b4510c9a8aac8ba9ffb8b57b) The energy _E_(2) gives the quadratic Stark effect.

Neglecting the [hyperfine structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperfine_structure "Hyperfine structure") (which is often justified — unless extremely weak electric fields are considered), the polarizability tensor of atoms is isotropic, ![Image 30: {\displaystyle \alpha _{ij}\equiv \alpha _{0}\delta _{ij}\Longrightarrow E^{(2)}=-{\frac {1}{2}}\alpha _{0}F^{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/081216a16f23539033b6adf5bf9a06da344841bc) For some molecules this expression is a reasonable approximation, too.

For the ground state ![Image 31: {\displaystyle \alpha _{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a214eff2fcc322f780dd8837e7472b0edb994a13) is _always_ positive, i.e., the quadratic Stark shift is always negative.

The perturbative treatment of the Stark effect has some problems. In the presence of an electric field, states of atoms and molecules that were previously bound ([square-integrable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square-integrable "Square-integrable")), become formally (non-square-integrable) [resonances](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resonance "Resonance") of finite width. These resonances may decay in finite time via field ionization. For low lying states and not too strong fields the decay times are so long, however, that for all practical purposes the system can be regarded as bound. For highly excited states and/or very strong fields ionization may have to be accounted for. (See also the article on the [Rydberg atom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rydberg_atom "Rydberg atom")).[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

The Stark effect is at the basis of the spectral shift measured for [voltage-sensitive dyes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voltage-sensitive_dye "Voltage-sensitive dye") used for imaging of the firing activity of neurons.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stark_effect#cite_note-10)
