In algebraic geometry, the **dimension of a scheme** is a generalization of a [dimension of an algebraic variety](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension_of_an_algebraic_variety "Dimension of an algebraic variety"). [Scheme theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scheme_theory "Scheme theory") emphasizes the [relative point of view](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grothendieck%27s_relative_point_of_view "Grothendieck's relative point of view") and, accordingly, the **relative dimension** of a [morphism of schemes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morphism_of_schemes "Morphism of schemes") is also important.

By definition, the dimension of a scheme _X_ is the dimension of the underlying [topological space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_space "Topological space"): the supremum of the lengths _ℓ_ of chains of [irreducible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irreducible_space "Irreducible space") closed subsets:

![Image 1: {\displaystyle \emptyset \neq V_{0}\subsetneq V_{1}\subsetneq \cdots \subsetneq V_{\ell }\subset X.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/971bbf839438986b934ece4ef49f7686935e079c)[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension_of_a_scheme#cite_note-1)
In particular, if ![Image 2: {\displaystyle X=\operatorname {Spec} A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/729a50e2df83e0d1aaa004c24d5b445b65402b0a) is an [affine scheme](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_scheme "Affine scheme"), then such chains correspond to chains of [prime ideals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_ideal "Prime ideal") (inclusion reversed) and so the dimension of _X_ is precisely the [Krull dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Krull_dimension "Krull dimension") of _A_.

If _Y_ is an irreducible closed subset of a scheme _X_, then the codimension of _Y_ in _X_ is the supremum of the lengths _ℓ_ of chains of irreducible closed subsets:

![Image 3: {\displaystyle Y=V_{0}\subsetneq V_{1}\subsetneq \cdots \subsetneq V_{\ell }\subset X.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/153513a1f5e803b2d4df06f060589750a9ec4ff8)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension_of_a_scheme#cite_note-2)
An irreducible subset of _X_ is an [irreducible component](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irreducible_component "Irreducible component") of _X_ if and only if the codimension of it in _X_ is zero. If ![Image 4: {\displaystyle X=\operatorname {Spec} A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/729a50e2df83e0d1aaa004c24d5b445b65402b0a) is affine, then the codimension of _Y_ in _X_ is precisely the height of the prime ideal defining _Y_ in _X_.

Equidimensional scheme
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Dimension_of_a_scheme&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Equidimensional scheme")]

An **equidimensional scheme** (or, **pure dimensional scheme**) is a [scheme](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scheme_(mathematics) "Scheme (mathematics)") all of whose [irreducible components](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irreducible_component "Irreducible component") are of the same [dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension_(scheme) "Dimension (scheme)") (implicitly assuming the dimensions are all well-defined).

All irreducible schemes are equidimensional.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension_of_a_scheme#cite_note-7)

In [affine space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_space "Affine space"), the union of a line and a point not on the line is _not_ equidimensional. In general, if two closed subschemes of some scheme, neither containing the other, have unequal dimensions, then their union is not equidimensional.

If a scheme is [smooth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smooth_scheme "Smooth scheme") (for instance, [étale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%89tale_morphism "Étale morphism")) over Spec _k_ for some field _k_, then every [_connected_ component](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connected_component_(topology) "Connected component (topology)") (which is then in fact an irreducible component), is equidimensional.

Let ![Image 42: {\displaystyle f:X\rightarrow Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b215af1e965d0595a97ad2b21f7d0cbcf6281303) be a [morphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morphism "Morphism") locally of [finite type](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_type_scheme "Finite type scheme") between two [schemes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scheme_(mathematics) "Scheme (mathematics)")![Image 43: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) and ![Image 44: {\displaystyle Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/961d67d6b454b4df2301ac571808a3538b3a6d3f). The relative dimension of ![Image 45: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) at a point ![Image 46: {\displaystyle y\in Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cee1c0ec36a82f33f5e3d7434d5667881b4ec323) is the [dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension "Dimension") of the [fiber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiber_(mathematics) "Fiber (mathematics)")![Image 47: {\displaystyle f^{-1}(y)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8b357745fa4a2178733a502b4432072be8222fd4). If all the nonempty fibers [_[clarification needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Please\_clarify "Wikipedia:Please clarify")_] are purely of the same dimension ![Image 48: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b), then one says that ![Image 49: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) is of relative dimension ![Image 50: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b).[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension_of_a_scheme#cite_note-8)
