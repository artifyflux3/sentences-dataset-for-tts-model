[![Image 1: An infrared image of MoM-z14 from NASA's James Webb Space Telescope that was taken by the NIRCam](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/MoM-z14_Naidu_et_al._2025.png/250px-MoM-z14_Naidu_et_al._2025.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:MoM-z14_Naidu_et_al._2025.png)

[MoM-z14](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MoM-z14 "MoM-z14") has been determined to be at a redshift of 14.44, making it the record-holder for the most distant known [galaxy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy "Galaxy") as of May 2025.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_the_most_distant_astronomical_objects#cite_note-MoM-z14-1) This corresponds to a time less than 280 million years after the [big bang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang "Big Bang").

This article documents the most distant [astronomical objects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_object "Astronomical object") discovered and verified so far, and the time periods in which they were so classified.

For comparisons with the [light travel distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_travel_distance "Light travel distance") of the astronomical objects listed below, the [age of the universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Age_of_the_universe "Age of the universe") since the [Big Bang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang "Big Bang") is currently estimated as 13.787 ± 0.020 Gyr.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_the_most_distant_astronomical_objects#cite_note-Planck_2018age-2)

Distances to remote objects, other than those in nearby galaxies, are nearly always inferred by measuring the [cosmological redshift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmological_redshift "Cosmological redshift") of their light. By their nature, very distant objects tend to be very faint, and these distance determinations are difficult and subject to errors. An important distinction is whether the distance is determined via [spectroscopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopy "Spectroscopy") or using a [photometric redshift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photometric_redshift "Photometric redshift") technique. The former is generally both more precise and also more reliable, in the sense that photometric redshifts are more prone to being wrong due to confusion with lower redshift sources that may have unusual spectra. For that reason, a [spectroscopic redshift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_redshift "Spectroscopic redshift") is conventionally regarded as being necessary for an object's distance to be considered definitely known, whereas photometrically determined redshifts identify "candidate" very distant sources. Here, this distinction is indicated by a "p" subscript for photometric redshifts.

The [proper distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comoving_and_proper_distances "Comoving and proper distances") provides a measurement of how far a galaxy is at a fixed moment in time. At the present time the proper distance equals the [comoving distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comoving_and_proper_distances "Comoving and proper distances") since the cosmological [scale factor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scale_factor_(cosmology) "Scale factor (cosmology)") has value one: ![Image 2: {\displaystyle a(t_{0})=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b58ed1994b4bea79c6d38529fc4018f12f8f21ad). The proper distance represents the distance obtained as if one were able to freeze the flow of time (set ![Image 3: {\displaystyle dt=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/92c35cb8d714232bb64eaad31f6b1ac9dd05fe55) in the [FLRW](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedmann%E2%80%93Lema%C3%AEtre%E2%80%93Robertson%E2%80%93Walker_metric "Friedmann–Lemaître–Robertson–Walker metric") metric) and walk all the way to a galaxy while using a meter stick.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_the_most_distant_astronomical_objects#cite_note-3) For practical reasons, the proper distance is calculated as the distance traveled by light (set ![Image 4: {\displaystyle ds=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/883945c3f373e07e86b27113e72efb03196558e3) in the [FLRW](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedmann%E2%80%93Lema%C3%AEtre%E2%80%93Robertson%E2%80%93Walker_metric "Friedmann–Lemaître–Robertson–Walker metric") metric) from the time of emission by a galaxy to the time an observer (on Earth) receives the light signal. It differs from the "light travel distance" since the proper distance takes into account the expansion of the universe, i.e. the space expands as the light travels through it, resulting in numerical values which locate the most distant galaxies beyond the [Hubble sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble_volume "Hubble volume") and therefore with [recession velocities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recessional_velocity "Recessional velocity") greater than the speed of light _c_.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_the_most_distant_astronomical_objects#cite_note-4)

Candidate most distant objects
------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=List_of_the_most_distant_astronomical_objects&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Candidate most distant objects")]

Since the beginning of the [James Webb Space Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Webb_Space_Telescope "James Webb Space Telescope")'s (JWST) science operations in June 2022, numerous distant galaxies far beyond what could be seen by the [Hubble Space Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope "Hubble Space Telescope") (z = 11) have been discovered thanks to the JWST's capability of seeing far into the [infrared](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infrared "Infrared").[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_the_most_distant_astronomical_objects#cite_note-Adams2022-50)[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_the_most_distant_astronomical_objects#cite_note-Yan2023-51) Previously in 2012, there were about 50 possible objects z = 8 or farther, and another 100 candidates at z = 7, based on photometric redshift estimates released by the [Hubble eXtreme Deep Field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble_eXtreme_Deep_Field "Hubble eXtreme Deep Field") (XDF) project from observations made between mid-2002 and December 2012.[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_the_most_distant_astronomical_objects#cite_note-fg-52) Some objects included here have been observed spectroscopically, but had only one emission line tentatively detected, and are therefore still considered candidates by researchers.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_the_most_distant_astronomical_objects#cite_note-Harikane2022b-53)[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_the_most_distant_astronomical_objects#cite_note-Fujimoto2022-54)

Timeline of most distant astronomical object recordholders
----------------------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=List_of_the_most_distant_astronomical_objects&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Timeline of most distant astronomical object recordholders")]

Objects in this list were found to be the most distant object at the time of determination of their distance. This is frequently not the same as the date of their discovery.

Distances to astronomical objects may be determined through [parallax](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallax "Parallax") measurements, use of [standard references](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_candles "Standard candles") such as [cepheid variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cepheid_variable "Cepheid variable") or [Type Ia supernovas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Type_Ia_supernova "Type Ia supernova"), or [redshift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift "Redshift") measurement. [Spectroscopic redshift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_spectroscopy#Doppler_effect_and_redshift "Astronomical spectroscopy") measurement is preferred, while [photometric redshift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photometric_redshift "Photometric redshift") measurement is also used to identify candidate high redshift sources. The symbol _z_ represents redshift.

List of objects by year of discovery that turned out to be most distant
-----------------------------------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=List_of_the_most_distant_astronomical_objects&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: List of objects by year of discovery that turned out to be most distant")]

This list contains a list of most distant objects by year of discovery of the object, not the determination of its distance. Objects may have been discovered without distance determination, and were found subsequently to be the most distant known at that time. However, object must have been named or described. An object like [OJ 287](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OJ_287 "OJ 287") is ignored even though it was detected as early as 1891 using photographic plates, but ignored until the advent of radiotelescopes.
