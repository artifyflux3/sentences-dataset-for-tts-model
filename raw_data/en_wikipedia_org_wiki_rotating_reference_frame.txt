In the inertial frame of reference (upper part of the picture), the black ball moves in a straight line. However, the observer (red dot) who is standing in the rotating/non-inertial frame of reference (lower part of the picture) sees the object as following a curved path due to the Coriolis and centrifugal forces present in this frame.

A **rotating frame of reference** is a special case of a [non-inertial reference frame](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-inertial_reference_frame "Non-inertial reference frame") that is [rotating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation "Rotation") relative to an [inertial reference frame](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inertial_reference_frame "Inertial reference frame"). An everyday example of a rotating reference frame is the surface of the [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth "Earth"). (This article considers only frames rotating about a fixed axis. For more general rotations, see [Euler angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler_angles#Vehicles_and_moving_frames "Euler angles").)

All [non-inertial reference frames](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-inertial_reference_frame "Non-inertial reference frame") exhibit [fictitious forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fictitious_force "Fictitious force"); rotating reference frames are characterized by three:[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotating_reference_frame#cite_note-Arnold-1)

*   the [centrifugal force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centrifugal_force_(fictitious) "Centrifugal force (fictitious)"),
*   the [Coriolis force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coriolis_force "Coriolis force"),

and, for non-uniformly rotating reference frames,

*   the [Euler force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler_force "Euler force").

Scientists in a rotating box can measure the [rotation speed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation_speed "Rotation speed") and [axis of rotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axis_of_rotation "Axis of rotation") by measuring these fictitious forces. For example, [Léon Foucault](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L%C3%A9on_Foucault "Léon Foucault") was able to show the Coriolis force that results from Earth's rotation using the [Foucault pendulum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foucault_pendulum "Foucault pendulum"). If Earth were to rotate many times faster, these fictitious forces could be felt by humans, as they are when on a spinning [carousel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carousel "Carousel").

In [classical mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_mechanics "Classical mechanics"), _centrifugal force_ is an outward force associated with [rotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation "Rotation"). Centrifugal force is one of several so-called [pseudo-forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudo-force "Pseudo-force") (also known as [inertial forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inertial_force "Inertial force")), so named because, unlike [real forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_interaction "Fundamental interaction"), they do not originate in interactions with other bodies situated in the environment of the particle upon which they act. Instead, centrifugal force originates in the rotation of the frame of reference within which observations are made.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotating_reference_frame#cite_note-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotating_reference_frame#cite_note-Marsden-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotating_reference_frame#cite_note-Taylor_A-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotating_reference_frame#cite_note-Marion-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotating_reference_frame#cite_note-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotating_reference_frame#cite_note-7)

The mathematical expression for the Coriolis force appeared in an 1835 paper by a French scientist [Gaspard-Gustave Coriolis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaspard-Gustave_Coriolis "Gaspard-Gustave Coriolis") in connection with [hydrodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrodynamics "Hydrodynamics"), and also in the [tidal equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theory_of_tides "Theory of tides") of [Pierre-Simon Laplace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre-Simon_Laplace "Pierre-Simon Laplace") in 1778. Early in the 20th century, the term Coriolis force began to be used in connection with [meteorology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteorology "Meteorology").

Perhaps the most commonly encountered rotating reference frame is the [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth "Earth"). Moving objects on the surface of the Earth experience a Coriolis force, and appear to veer to the right in the [northern hemisphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northern_hemisphere "Northern hemisphere"), and to the left in the [southern](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southern_hemisphere "Southern hemisphere"). Movements of air in the atmosphere and water in the ocean are notable examples of this behavior: rather than flowing directly from areas of high pressure to low pressure, as they would on a non-rotating planet, winds and currents tend to flow to the right of this direction north of the [equator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equator "Equator"), and to the left of this direction south of the equator. This effect is responsible for the rotation of large [cyclones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclone#Structure "Cyclone") (see [Coriolis effects in meteorology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coriolis_effect#Meteorology "Coriolis effect")).

In [classical mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_mechanics "Classical mechanics"), the _Euler acceleration_ (named for [Leonhard Euler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leonhard_Euler "Leonhard Euler")), also known as _azimuthal acceleration_[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotating_reference_frame#cite_note-Morin-8) or _transverse acceleration_[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotating_reference_frame#cite_note-Fowles-9) is an [acceleration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acceleration "Acceleration") that appears when a non-uniformly rotating reference frame is used for analysis of motion and there is variation in the [angular velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_velocity "Angular velocity") of the [reference frame](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frame_of_reference "Frame of reference")'s axis. This article is restricted to a frame of reference that rotates about a fixed axis.

The _Euler force_ is a [fictitious force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fictitious_force "Fictitious force") on a body that is related to the Euler acceleration by **_F_**=_m**a**_, where **_a_** is the Euler acceleration and _m_ is the mass of the body.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotating_reference_frame#cite_note-Battin-10)[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotating_reference_frame#cite_note-11)

Relating rotating frames to stationary frames
---------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Rotating_reference_frame&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Relating rotating frames to stationary frames")]

The following is a derivation of the formulas for accelerations as well as fictitious forces in a rotating frame. It begins with the relation between a particle's coordinates in a rotating frame and its coordinates in an inertial (stationary) frame. Then, by taking time derivatives, formulas are derived that relate the velocity of the particle as seen in the two frames, and the acceleration relative to each frame. Using these accelerations, the fictitious forces are identified by comparing Newton's second law as formulated in the two different frames.

### Relation between accelerations in the two frames

Acceleration is the second time derivative of position, or the first time derivative of velocity

where subscript ![Image 62: {\displaystyle \mathrm {i} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/18f0f09f6fc40e634d34aed6e205ac0f7a40e062) means the inertial frame of reference, ![Image 63: {\displaystyle \mathrm {r} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e8c0f15bc8d24e551d7d0618c476a9b04448d695) the rotating frame of reference, and where the expression, again, ![Image 64: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {\Omega }}\times }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c2177ad64bc944ff9a0dd1a7a42ad25a7a0ec35c) in the bracketed expression on the left is to be interpreted as an [operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operator_(mathematics) "Operator (mathematics)") working onto the bracketed expression on the right.

As ![Image 65: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {\Omega }}\times {\boldsymbol {\Omega }}={\boldsymbol {0}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e40d07143fec074aaceef79dcbeb4ed3d6c10625), the first time derivatives of ![Image 66: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {\Omega }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9c95a28793dac7413de04992d822822f7e2dba16) inside either frame, when expressed with respect to the basis of e.g. the inertial frame, coincide. Carrying out the [differentiations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative "Derivative") and re-arranging some terms yields the acceleration _relative to the rotating_ reference frame, ![Image 67: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} _{\mathrm {r} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9add9775278740fc942e56055ffe445f462c3668)

![Image 68: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} _{\mathrm {r} }=\mathbf {a} _{\mathrm {i} }-2{\boldsymbol {\Omega }}\times \mathbf {v} _{\mathrm {r} }-{\boldsymbol {\Omega }}\times ({\boldsymbol {\Omega }}\times \mathbf {r} )-{\frac {\mathrm {d} {\boldsymbol {\Omega }}}{\mathrm {d} t}}\times \mathbf {r} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/837d3c3ceb6786ba58c1fe8bfccc35421fb44fd0)
where ![Image 69: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} _{\mathrm {r} }\ {\stackrel {\mathrm {def} }{=}}\ \left({\tfrac {\mathrm {d} ^{2}\mathbf {r} }{\mathrm {d} t^{2}}}\right)_{\mathrm {r} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2470ecfd8a458d8a17fbc8c7ffad450a64b7591f) is the apparent acceleration in the rotating reference frame, the term ![Image 70: {\displaystyle -{\boldsymbol {\Omega }}\times ({\boldsymbol {\Omega }}\times \mathbf {r} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d15c67e070622d658f4fc6de2d32059a93d878c4) represents [centrifugal acceleration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centrifugal_acceleration "Centrifugal acceleration"), and the term ![Image 71: {\displaystyle -2{\boldsymbol {\Omega }}\times \mathbf {v} _{\mathrm {r} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/df03842fe72be9bb50f9fc5f655c0bbccb16a61f) is the [Coriolis acceleration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coriolis_acceleration "Coriolis acceleration"). The last term, ![Image 72: {\displaystyle -{\tfrac {\mathrm {d} {\boldsymbol {\Omega }}}{\mathrm {d} t}}\times \mathbf {r} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6aedb652cb192fc06117dcc16e9ec557c13f9114), is the [Euler acceleration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler_acceleration "Euler acceleration") and is zero in uniformly rotating frames.

### Newton's second law in the two frames

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Rotating_reference_frame&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Newton's second law in the two frames")]

When the expression for acceleration is multiplied by the mass of the particle, the three extra terms on the right-hand side result in [fictitious forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fictitious_force "Fictitious force") in the rotating reference frame, that is, apparent forces that result from being in a [non-inertial reference frame](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-inertial_reference_frame "Non-inertial reference frame"), rather than from any physical interaction between bodies.

Using [Newton's second law of motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_laws_of_motion "Newton's laws of motion")![Image 73: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} =m\mathbf {a} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/93aa54e6c7e8df66d85d06b6eb0b0a2d3ec4ce20) we obtain:[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotating_reference_frame#cite_note-Arnold-1)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotating_reference_frame#cite_note-Lanczos-14)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotating_reference_frame#cite_note-Taylor-15)[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotating_reference_frame#cite_note-Landau-17)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotating_reference_frame#cite_note-Hand-18)

where ![Image 74: {\displaystyle m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a07d98bb302f3856cbabc47b2b9016692e3f7bc) is the mass of the object being acted upon by these [fictitious forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fictitious_force "Fictitious force"). Notice that all three forces vanish when the frame is not rotating, that is, when ![Image 75: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {\Omega }}=0\ .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/565018445fe417aa920fe15d1ef475f5e5a9a4b7)

For completeness, the inertial acceleration ![Image 76: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} _{\mathrm {i} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4e5f0b3c39727a761e8685fe7aa73e1b0a70a696) due to impressed external forces ![Image 77: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} _{\mathrm {imp} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/20c26eb013b20287f4a65be81984b08e1c733e89) can be determined from the total physical force in the inertial (non-rotating) frame (for example, force from physical interactions such as [electromagnetic forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetism "Electromagnetism")) using [Newton's second law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_laws_of_motion "Newton's laws of motion") in the inertial frame: ![Image 78: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} _{\mathrm {imp} }=m\mathbf {a} _{\mathrm {i} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ef6c27c6ef03780b6fe7b30524286f7a3b118408) Newton's law in the rotating frame then becomes

![Image 79: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F_{\mathrm {r} }} =\mathbf {F} _{\mathrm {imp} }+\mathbf {F} _{\mathrm {centrifugal} }+\mathbf {F} _{\mathrm {Coriolis} }+\mathbf {F} _{\mathrm {Euler} }=m\mathbf {a_{\mathrm {r} }} \ .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d77b0d4a69ffe37acf7b598727c37ed78eb95994)
In other words, to handle the laws of motion in a rotating reference frame:[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotating_reference_frame#cite_note-Hand-18)[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotating_reference_frame#cite_note-Pui-19)[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotating_reference_frame#cite_note-Taylor2-20)

> Treat the fictitious forces like real forces, and pretend you are in an inertial frame.

— Louis N. Hand, Janet D. Finch _Analytical Mechanics_, p. 267

> Obviously, a rotating frame of reference is a case of a non-inertial frame. Thus the particle in addition to the real force is acted upon by a fictitious force...The particle will move according to Newton's second law of motion if the total force acting on it is taken as the sum of the real and fictitious forces.

— HS Hans & SP Pui: _Mechanics_; p. 341

> This equation has exactly the form of Newton's second law, _except_ that in addition to **F**, the sum of all forces identified in the inertial frame, there is an extra term on the right...This means we can continue to use Newton's second law in the noninertial frame _provided_ we agree that in the noninertial frame we must add an extra force-like term, often called the **inertial force**.

— John R. Taylor: _Classical Mechanics_; p. 328

Use in magnetic resonance
-------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Rotating_reference_frame&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Use in magnetic resonance")]

It is convenient to consider [magnetic resonance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_magnetic_resonance "Nuclear magnetic resonance") in a frame that rotates at the [Larmor frequency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Larmor_frequency "Larmor frequency") of the spins. This is illustrated in the animation below. The [rotating wave approximation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotating_wave_approximation "Rotating wave approximation") may also be used.

[![Image 80](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Animated_Rotating_Frame.gif/250px-Animated_Rotating_Frame.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Animated_Rotating_Frame.gif)

Animation showing the rotating frame. The red arrow is a spin in the [Bloch sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bloch_sphere "Bloch sphere") which precesses in the laboratory frame due to a static magnetic field. In the rotating frame the spin remains still until a resonantly oscillating magnetic field drives magnetic resonance.
