**Lawrence Craig Evans** (born November 1, 1949) is an American [mathematician](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematician "Mathematician") and Professor of Mathematics at the [University of California, Berkeley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_California,_Berkeley "University of California, Berkeley").

His research is in the field of [nonlinear partial differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nonlinear_partial_differential_equation "Nonlinear partial differential equation"), primarily [elliptic equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptic_partial_differential_equation "Elliptic partial differential equation"). In 2004, he shared the [Leroy P. Steele Prize](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leroy_P._Steele_Prize "Leroy P. Steele Prize") for Seminal Contribution to Research with [Nicolai V. Krylov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nicolai_V._Krylov "Nicolai V. Krylov") for their proofs, found independently, that solutions of concave, fully nonlinear, uniformly elliptic equations are ![Image 2: {\displaystyle C^{2,\alpha }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e1a32ca3ddef474ba315e720e1e08b0d542a218f). Evans also made significant contributions to the development of the theory of [viscosity solutions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viscosity_solution "Viscosity solution") of nonlinear equations, to the understanding of the [Hamilton–Jacobi–Bellman equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamilton%E2%80%93Jacobi%E2%80%93Bellman_equation "Hamilton–Jacobi–Bellman equation") arising in stochastic [optimal control](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optimal_control "Optimal control") theory, and to the theory of [harmonic maps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harmonic_map "Harmonic map"). He is also well known as the author of the textbook _Partial Differential Equations,_[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lawrence_C._Evans#cite_note-1) which is considered as a standard introduction to the theory at the graduate level. His textbook _Measure theory and fine properties of functions_ (coauthored with Ronald Gariepy), an exposition on Hausdorff measure, capacity, Sobolev functions, and sets of finite perimeter, is also widely cited.

Evans is an [ISI highly cited researcher](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISI_highly_cited_researcher "ISI highly cited researcher").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lawrence_C._Evans#cite_note-2)

Lawrence Evans was born November 1, 1949, in [Atlanta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atlanta "Atlanta"), [Georgia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state) "Georgia (U.S. state)"). He received a BA from [Vanderbilt University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vanderbilt_University "Vanderbilt University") in 1971 and a PhD, with thesis advisor [Michael G. Crandall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michael_G._Crandall "Michael G. Crandall"), from the [University of California, Los Angeles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_California,_Los_Angeles "University of California, Los Angeles") in 1975. From 1975 to 1980, he worked at the [University of Kentucky](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Kentucky "University of Kentucky"); from 1980 to 1989, at the [University of Maryland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Maryland "University of Maryland"); and since 1989, at the [University of California, Berkeley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_California,_Berkeley "University of California, Berkeley").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lawrence_C._Evans#cite_note-cv-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lawrence_C._Evans#cite_note-NASonline-4)
