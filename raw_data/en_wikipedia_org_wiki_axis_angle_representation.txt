[![Image 1](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Angle_axis_vector.svg/150px-Angle_axis_vector.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Angle_axis_vector.svg)

The angle _Œ∏_ and axis unit vector **e** define a rotation, concisely represented by the rotation vector _Œ∏_**e**. 

In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), the **axis‚Äìangle representation** parameterizes a [rotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation_(mathematics) "Rotation (mathematics)") in a [three-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_space "Three-dimensional space")[Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space") by two quantities: a [unit vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_vector "Unit vector")**e** indicating the [direction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direction_(geometry) "Direction (geometry)") of an [axis of rotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axis_of_rotation "Axis of rotation"), and an [angle of rotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle_of_rotation "Angle of rotation")_Œ∏_ describing the magnitude and sense (e.g., [clockwise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clockwise "Clockwise")) of the [rotation about the axis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation_around_a_fixed_axis "Rotation around a fixed axis"). Only two numbers, not three, are needed to define the direction of a unit vector **e** rooted at the origin because the magnitude of **e** is constrained. For example, the [elevation and azimuth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horizontal_coordinate_system "Horizontal coordinate system") angles of **e** suffice to locate it in any particular Cartesian coordinate frame.

By [Rodrigues' rotation formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rodrigues%27_rotation_formula "Rodrigues' rotation formula"), the angle and axis determine a transformation that rotates three-dimensional vectors. The rotation occurs in the sense prescribed by the [right-hand rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right-hand_rule "Right-hand rule").

The rotation axis is sometimes called the **Euler axis**. The axis‚Äìangle representation is predicated on [Euler's rotation theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler%27s_rotation_theorem "Euler's rotation theorem"), which dictates that any rotation or sequence of rotations of a rigid body in a three-dimensional space is equivalent to a pure rotation about a single fixed axis.

It is one of many [rotation formalisms in three dimensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation_formalisms_in_three_dimensions "Rotation formalisms in three dimensions").

The axis‚Äìangle representation is equivalent to the more concise **rotation vector**, also called the **Euler vector** (not to be confused with a vector of [Euler angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler_angles "Euler angles")). In this case, both the rotation axis and the angle are represented by a vector codirectional with the rotation axis whose length is the rotation angle Œ∏, ![Image 2: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {\theta }}=\theta \mathbf {e} \,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0317bca704fd4a41e6792d9b396464f96261701d) It is used for the [exponential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_exponential "Matrix exponential") and [logarithm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logarithm_of_a_matrix "Logarithm of a matrix") maps involving this representation.

Many rotation vectors correspond to the same rotation. In particular, a rotation vector of length _Œ∏_ + 2 _œÄM_, for any integer M, encodes exactly the same rotation as a rotation vector of length Œ∏. Thus, there are at least a countable infinity of rotation vectors corresponding to any rotation. Furthermore, all rotations by 2 _œÄM_ are the same as no rotation at all, so, for a given integer M, all rotation vectors of length 2 _œÄM_, in all directions, constitute a two-parameter uncountable infinity of rotation vectors encoding the same rotation as the zero vector. These facts must be taken into account when inverting the exponential map, that is, when finding a rotation vector that corresponds to a given rotation matrix. The exponential map is _onto_ but not _one-to-one_.

Say you are standing on the ground and you pick the direction of gravity to be the negative _z_ direction. Then if you turn to your left, you will rotate ‚Å†-œÄ/2‚Å† radians (or [-90¬∞](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_angle "Right angle")) about the _-z_ axis. Viewing the axis-angle representation as an [ordered pair](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordered_pair "Ordered pair"), this would be ![Image 3: {\displaystyle (\mathrm {axis} ,\mathrm {angle} )=\left({\begin{bmatrix}e_{x}\\e_{y}\\e_{z}\end{bmatrix}},\theta \right)=\left({\begin{bmatrix}0\\0\\-1\end{bmatrix}},{\frac {-\pi }{2}}\right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/01e06cf582413eb67f55a9acbfcaebb9d9715c92)

The above example can be represented as a rotation vector with a magnitude of ‚Å†œÄ/2‚Å† pointing in the _z_ direction, ![Image 4: {\displaystyle {\begin{bmatrix}0\\0\\{\frac {\pi }{2}}\end{bmatrix}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b1625f7f5c797c46dad3a9dd2255eba902b600f8)

The axis‚Äìangle representation is convenient when dealing with [rigid-body dynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rigid-body_dynamics "Rigid-body dynamics"). It is useful to both characterize [rotations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation "Rotation"), and also for converting between different representations of rigid body [motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Motion_(physics) "Motion (physics)"), such as homogeneous transformations[_[clarification needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Please\_clarify "Wikipedia:Please clarify")_] and twists.

When a [rigid body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rigid_body "Rigid body") rotates [around a fixed axis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation_around_a_fixed_axis "Rotation around a fixed axis"), its axis‚Äìangle data are a [constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constant_function "Constant function") rotation axis and the rotation angle [continuously dependent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_function "Continuous function") on [time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time "Time").

Plugging the three eigenvalues 1 and _e_¬±_iŒ∏_ and their associated three orthogonal axes in a Cartesian representation into [Mercer's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mercer%27s_theorem "Mercer's theorem") is a convenient construction of the Cartesian representation of the Rotation Matrix in three dimensions.

[Rodrigues' rotation formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rodrigues%27_rotation_formula "Rodrigues' rotation formula"), named after [Olinde Rodrigues](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Olinde_Rodrigues "Olinde Rodrigues"), is an efficient algorithm for rotating a Euclidean vector, given a rotation axis and an angle of rotation. In other words, Rodrigues' formula provides an algorithm to compute the exponential map from ![Image 5: {\displaystyle {\mathfrak {so}}(3)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fb4f1d3d3bf3da64b92af1a1018ce00545808b9d) to SO(3) without computing the full matrix exponential.

If **v** is a vector in **R**3 and **e** is a [unit vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_vector "Unit vector") rooted at the origin describing an axis of rotation about which **v** is rotated by an angle Œ∏, Rodrigues' rotation formula to obtain the rotated vector is ![Image 6: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} _{\mathrm {rot} }=\mathbf {v} +(\sin \theta )(\mathbf {e} \times \mathbf {v} )+(1-\cos \theta )(\mathbf {e} \times (\mathbf {e} \times \mathbf {v} ))\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b42dafd0051b88aeadf728d3aa6b4870a524aeeb)

For the rotation of a single vector it may be more efficient than converting **e** and Œ∏ into a rotation matrix to rotate the vector.

Relationship to other representations
-------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Axis%E2%80%93angle_representation&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Relationship to other representations")]

There are several ways to represent a rotation. It is useful to understand how different representations relate to one another, and how to convert between them. Here the unit vector is denoted **œâ** instead of **e**.

### Exponential map from ùî∞ùî¨(3) to SO(3)

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Axis%E2%80%93angle_representation&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Exponential map from ùî∞ùî¨(3) to SO(3)")]

The [exponential map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponential_map_(Lie_theory) "Exponential map (Lie theory)") effects a transformation from the axis-angle representation of rotations to [rotation matrices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation_matrix "Rotation matrix"), ![Image 7: {\displaystyle \exp \colon {\mathfrak {so}}(3)\to \mathrm {SO} (3)\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/07a41749211d3efe9ceaeb3214d7ca5f8e5b824d)

Essentially, by using a [Taylor expansion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taylor_expansion "Taylor expansion") one derives a closed-form relation between these two representations. Given a unit vector ![Image 8: {\textstyle {\boldsymbol {\omega }}\in {\mathfrak {so}}(3)=\mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b02f0690a8eb8f9faf8d6936ac7e8191f049c971) representing the unit rotation axis, and an angle, _Œ∏_ ‚àà **R**, an equivalent rotation matrix R is given as follows, where **K** is the [cross product matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product#Conversion_to_matrix_multiplication "Cross product") of **œâ**, that is, **Kv** = _**œâ**_ √ó **v** for all vectors **v** ‚àà **R**3, ![Image 9: {\displaystyle R=\exp(\theta \mathbf {K} )=\sum _{k=0}^{\infty }{\frac {(\theta \mathbf {K} )^{k}}{k!}}=I+\theta \mathbf {K} +{\frac {1}{2!}}(\theta \mathbf {K} )^{2}+{\frac {1}{3!}}(\theta \mathbf {K} )^{3}+\cdots }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/206f5d22445504ed1a4c36271b3db8d959ed6513)

Because **K** is skew-symmetric, and the sum of the squares of its above-diagonal entries is 1, the [characteristic polynomial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Characteristic_polynomial "Characteristic polynomial")_P_(_t_) of **K** is _P_(_t_) = det(**K** ‚àí _t_**I**) = ‚àí(_t_ 3 + _t_). Since, by the [Cayley‚ÄìHamilton theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cayley%E2%80%93Hamilton_theorem "Cayley‚ÄìHamilton theorem"), _P_(**K**) = 0, this implies that ![Image 10: {\displaystyle \mathbf {K} ^{3}=-\mathbf {K} \,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b5100e40f8cd43e4b4470b7c26209565abf2ee04) As a result, **K**4 = ‚Äì**K**2, **K**5 = **K**, **K**6 = **K**2, **K**7 = ‚Äì**K**.

This cyclic pattern continues indefinitely, and so all higher powers of **K** can be expressed in terms of **K** and **K**2. Thus, from the above equation, it follows that ![Image 11: {\displaystyle R=I+\left(\theta -{\frac {\theta ^{3}}{3!}}+{\frac {\theta ^{5}}{5!}}-\cdots \right)\mathbf {K} +\left({\frac {\theta ^{2}}{2!}}-{\frac {\theta ^{4}}{4!}}+{\frac {\theta ^{6}}{6!}}-\cdots \right)\mathbf {K} ^{2}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fedae65d106fef38741695e07aa4e5fad2144206) that is, ![Image 12: {\displaystyle R=I+(\sin \theta )\mathbf {K} +(1-\cos \theta )\mathbf {K} ^{2}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5a14c692a6d26096fe3b03cf13851c6fa7df5c53)

by the [Taylor series formula for trigonometric functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taylor_series#Trigonometric_functions "Taylor series").

This is a Lie-algebraic derivation, in contrast to the geometric one in the article [Rodrigues' rotation formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rodrigues%27_rotation_formula "Rodrigues' rotation formula").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axis%E2%80%93angle_representation#cite_note-1)

Due to the existence of the above-mentioned exponential map, the unit vector **œâ** representing the rotation axis, and the angle _Œ∏_ are sometimes called the _exponential coordinates_ of the rotation matrix R.

### Log map from SO(3) to ùî∞ùî¨(3)

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Axis%E2%80%93angle_representation&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Log map from SO(3) to ùî∞ùî¨(3)")]

Let **K** continue to denote the 3√ó3 matrix that effects the cross product with the rotation axis **œâ**: **K**(**v**) = _**œâ**_ √ó **v** for all vectors **v** in what follows.

To retrieve the axis‚Äìangle representation of a [rotation matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation_matrix "Rotation matrix"), calculate the angle of rotation from the [trace of the rotation matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation_matrix#Determining_the_angle "Rotation matrix"): ![Image 13: {\displaystyle \theta =\arccos \left({\frac {\operatorname {Tr} (R)-1}{2}}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7edcb84b83d0afb83521974d624cfecefc6da2b0) and then use that to find the normalized axis, ![Image 14: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {\omega }}={\frac {1}{2\sin \theta }}{\begin{bmatrix}R_{32}-R_{23}\\R_{13}-R_{31}\\R_{21}-R_{12}\end{bmatrix}}~,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6557d003ca53d4047766afad451bde32e1b138b2)

where ![Image 15: {\displaystyle R_{ij}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/50382df1fd3bebf0baf22e2eab56a7a471d6621a) is the component of the rotation matrix, ![Image 16: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33), in the ![Image 17: {\displaystyle i}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/add78d8608ad86e54951b8c8bd6c8d8416533d20)-th row and ![Image 18: {\displaystyle j}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2f461e54f5c093e92a55547b9764291390f0b5d0)-th column.

The axis-angle representation is not unique since a rotation of ![Image 19: {\displaystyle -\theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1d715f4b31b39f52768eba6405b658be5dc573f5) about ![Image 20: {\displaystyle -{\boldsymbol {\omega }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af357243db0d88e78f769bd9e077f1611ffa8039) is the same as a rotation of ![Image 21: {\displaystyle \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e5ab2664b422d53eb0c7df3b87e1360d75ad9af) about ![Image 22: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {\omega }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7cb8af7a2f64af348e559652b6b1f0d2415ba444). Of course, adding any integer multiple of 2œÄ to ![Image 23: {\displaystyle \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e5ab2664b422d53eb0c7df3b87e1360d75ad9af) will also result in the identical rotation; a better method is to constrain ![Image 24: {\displaystyle \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e5ab2664b422d53eb0c7df3b87e1360d75ad9af) to the interval [0, 2œÄ) or (-œÄ, œÄ].

The above calculation of axis vector ![Image 25: {\displaystyle \omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/48eff443f9de7a985bb94ca3bde20813ea737be8)**does not work** if R is symmetric. Because, this is possible only when ![Image 26: {\displaystyle \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e5ab2664b422d53eb0c7df3b87e1360d75ad9af) = œÄ, so sin(![Image 27: {\displaystyle \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e5ab2664b422d53eb0c7df3b87e1360d75ad9af)) = 0, causing a division by 0 in the formula. However, the _limit_ of the formula for ![Image 28: {\displaystyle \omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/48eff443f9de7a985bb94ca3bde20813ea737be8), as ![Image 29: {\displaystyle \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e5ab2664b422d53eb0c7df3b87e1360d75ad9af) ‚Üí œÄ, gives the correct value for ![Image 30: {\displaystyle \omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/48eff443f9de7a985bb94ca3bde20813ea737be8). For the general case the ![Image 31: {\displaystyle \omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/48eff443f9de7a985bb94ca3bde20813ea737be8) may also be found using null space of R-I, see [rotation matrix#Determining the axis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation_matrix#Determining_the_axis "Rotation matrix").

The [matrix logarithm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_logarithm "Matrix logarithm") of the rotation matrix R is ![Image 32: {\displaystyle \log R={\begin{cases}0&{\text{if }}\theta =0\\{\dfrac {\theta }{2\sin \theta }}\left(R-R^{\mathsf {T}}\right)&{\text{if }}\theta \neq 0{\text{ and }}\theta \in (-\pi ,\pi )\end{cases}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/345e652b260168f00d3ee7c48c3c6eac66f1d6ee)

An exception occurs when _R_ has [eigenvalues](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eigenvalue "Eigenvalue") equal to [‚àí1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E2%88%921_(number) "‚àí1 (number)"). In this case, the log is not unique. However, even in the case where _Œ∏_ = _œÄ_ the [Frobenius norm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frobenius_norm "Frobenius norm") of the log is ![Image 33: {\displaystyle \|\log(R)\|_{\mathrm {F} }={\sqrt {2}}|\theta |\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/69f890256af1caf712ee8006f68ca931f2aeee0d) Given rotation matrices A and B, ![Image 34: {\displaystyle d_{g}(A,B):=\left\|\log \left(A^{\mathsf {T}}B\right)\right\|_{\mathrm {F} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e6c2c3608a480dc96fd588f0389da13395816471) is the geodesic distance on the 3D manifold of rotation matrices.

For small rotations, the above computation of Œ∏ may be numerically imprecise as the derivative of arccos goes to infinity as _Œ∏_ ‚Üí 0. In that case, the off-axis terms will actually provide better information about Œ∏ since, for small angles, _R_ ‚âà _I_ + _Œ∏_**K**. (This is because these are the first two terms of the Taylor series for exp(_Œ∏_**K**).)

This formulation also has numerical problems at _Œ∏_ = _œÄ_, where the off-axis terms do not give information about the rotation axis (which is still defined up to a sign ambiguity). In that case, we must reconsider the above formula.

![Image 35: {\displaystyle R=I+\mathbf {K} \sin \theta +\mathbf {K} ^{2}(1-\cos \theta )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/15251e1be89343a5217df1a53f165a6600e14bb8) At _Œ∏_ = _œÄ_, we have ![Image 36: {\displaystyle R=I+2\mathbf {K} ^{2}=I+2({\boldsymbol {\omega }}\otimes {\boldsymbol {\omega }}-I)=2{\boldsymbol {\omega }}\otimes {\boldsymbol {\omega }}-I}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/480d214d2208bb32eaac9e4e3a7680a85f71b7ec) and so let ![Image 37: {\displaystyle B:={\boldsymbol {\omega }}\otimes {\boldsymbol {\omega }}={\frac {1}{2}}(R+I)\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/559e37a4c3351cc9f56fd5009ca2ae8d39f4d79d) so the diagonal terms of _B_ are the squares of the elements of **œâ** and the signs (up to sign ambiguity) can be determined from the signs of the off-axis terms of B.

The following expression transforms axis‚Äìangle coordinates to [versors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Versor "Versor") (unit [quaternions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quaternion "Quaternion")): ![Image 38: {\displaystyle \mathbf {q} =\left(\cos {\tfrac {\theta }{2}},{\boldsymbol {\omega }}\sin {\tfrac {\theta }{2}}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ddd222df7d0ab2de7b9da8293c19670bf668cb67)

Given a versor **q** = _r_ + **v** represented with its [scalar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quaternion#Scalar_and_vector_parts "Quaternion")r and vector **v**, the axis‚Äìangle coordinates can be extracted using the following: ![Image 39: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\theta &=2\arccos r\\[8px]{\boldsymbol {\omega }}&={\begin{cases}{\dfrac {\mathbf {v} }{\sin {\tfrac {\theta }{2}}}},&{\text{if }}\theta \neq 0\\0,&{\text{otherwise}}.\end{cases}}\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ad2c6f96187fb9d15d62c44a33e4e2ed840c841a)

A more numerically stable expression of the rotation angle uses the [atan2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atan2 "Atan2") function: ![Image 40: {\displaystyle \theta =2\operatorname {atan2} (|\mathbf {v} |,r)\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a9f7c5a249779d13bf5455c320fd19abbc061984) where |**v**| is the [Euclidean norm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_norm "Euclidean norm") of the 3-vector **v**.
