A **triangle** is a [polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon "Polygon") with three corners and three sides, one of the basic [shapes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shapes "Shapes") in [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry"). The corners, also called [_vertices_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_(geometry) "Vertex (geometry)"), are zero-[dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimensional "Dimensional")[points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_(geometry) "Point (geometry)") while the sides connecting them, also called [_edges_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edge_(geometry) "Edge (geometry)"), are one-dimensional [line segments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_segment "Line segment"). A triangle has three [internal angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internal_angle "Internal angle"), each one bounded by a pair of adjacent edges; the [sum of angles of a triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sum_of_angles_of_a_triangle "Sum of angles of a triangle") always equals a [straight angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Straight_angle "Straight angle") (180 degrees or π radians). The triangle is a [plane figure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_figure "Plane figure") and its interior is a [planar region](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planar_region "Planar region"). Sometimes an arbitrary edge is chosen to be the [_base_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Base_(geometry) "Base (geometry)"), in which case the opposite vertex is called the [_apex_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apex_(geometry) "Apex (geometry)"); the shortest segment between the base and apex is the [_height_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Height_(triangle) "Height (triangle)"). The [area of a triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area_of_a_triangle "Area of a triangle") equals one-half the product of height and base length.

In [Euclidean geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_geometry "Euclidean geometry"), any two points determine a unique line segment situated within a unique [straight line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Straight_line "Straight line"), and any three points that do not [all lie on the same straight line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collinearity "Collinearity") determine a unique triangle situated within a unique flat [plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_(geometry) "Plane (geometry)"). More generally, four points in [three-dimensional Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_Euclidean_space "Three-dimensional Euclidean space") determine a [solid figure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_figure "Solid figure") called _[tetrahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron "Tetrahedron")_.

In [non-Euclidean geometries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-Euclidean_geometries "Non-Euclidean geometries"), three "straight" segments (having zero [curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic_curvature "Geodesic curvature")) also determine a "triangle", for instance, a [spherical triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_triangle "Spherical triangle") or [hyperbolic triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_triangle "Hyperbolic triangle"). A [geodesic triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic_triangle "Geodesic triangle") is a region of a general two-dimensional [surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_(mathematics) "Surface (mathematics)") enclosed by three sides that are straight relative to the surface ([geodesics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic "Geodesic")). A _curvilinear triangle_ is a shape with three [curved](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curved "Curved") sides, for instance, a _[circular triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circular\_triangle "Circular triangle")_ with [circular-arc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circular_arc "Circular arc") sides. (This article is about straight-sided triangles in Euclidean geometry, except where otherwise noted.)

Triangles are classified into different types based on their angles and the lengths of their sides. Relations between angles and side lengths are a major focus of [trigonometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trigonometry "Trigonometry"). In particular, the [sine, cosine, and tangent functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trigonometric_functions "Trigonometric functions") relate side lengths and angles in [right triangles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_triangle "Right triangle").

Definition, terminology, and types
----------------------------------

A triangle is a figure consisting of three line segments, each of whose endpoints are connected.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-FOOTNOTELangMurrow1988[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidpc_kBwAAQBAJpgPA4_4]-1) This forms a polygon with three sides and three angles. The terminology for categorizing triangles is more than two thousand years old, having been defined in Book One of [Euclid's Elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid%27s_Elements "Euclid's Elements").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-FOOTNOTEByrne2013xx%E2%80%93xxi-2) The names used for modern classification are either a direct transliteration of Euclid's Greek or their Latin translations.

Triangles have many types based on the length of the sides and the angles. A triangle whose sides are all the same length is an [equilateral triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equilateral_triangle "Equilateral triangle"),[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-3) a triangle with two sides having the same length is an [isosceles triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isosceles_triangle "Isosceles triangle"),[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-4)[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-7) and a triangle with three different-length sides is a _scalene triangle_.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-8) A triangle in which one of the angles is a [right angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_angle "Right angle") is a [right triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_triangle "Right triangle"), a triangle in which all of its angles are less than that angle is an [acute triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acute_triangle "Acute triangle"), and a triangle in which one of it angles is greater than that angle is an [obtuse triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_triangle "Obtuse triangle").[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-9) These definitions date back at least to [Euclid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid "Euclid").[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHeath1926Definition_20,_Definition_21-10)

Appearances
-----------

[![Image 8](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Triangular_dipyramid.png/250px-Triangular_dipyramid.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Triangular_dipyramid.png)

A [triangular bipyramid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangular_bipyramid "Triangular bipyramid") can be constructed by attaching two [tetrahedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron "Tetrahedron"). This polyhedron can be said to be a [simplicial polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simplicial_polyhedron "Simplicial polyhedron") because all of its faces are triangles. More specifically, when the faces are equilateral, it is categorized as a [deltahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deltahedron "Deltahedron").

All types of triangles are commonly found in real life. In man-made construction, the isosceles triangles may be found in the shape of [gables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gable "Gable") and [pediments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pediment "Pediment"), and the equilateral triangle can be found in the yield sign.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-11) The faces of the [Great Pyramid of Giza](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Pyramid_of_Giza "Great Pyramid of Giza") are sometimes considered to be equilateral, but more accurate measurements show they are isosceles instead.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHerz-Fischler2000-12) Other appearances are in [heraldic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heraldic "Heraldic") symbols as in the [flag of Saint Lucia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flag_of_Saint_Lucia "Flag of Saint Lucia") and [flag of the Philippines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flag_of_the_Philippines "Flag of the Philippines").[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuillermo2012[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidwmgX9M_yETICpgPA161_161]-13)

Triangles also appear in three-dimensional objects. A [polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyhedron "Polyhedron") is a solid whose boundary is covered by flat [polygonals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygonal "Polygonal") known as the faces, sharp corners known as the vertices, and line segments known as the edges. Polyhedra in some cases can be classified, judging from the shape of their faces. For example, when polyhedra have all equilateral triangles as their faces, they are known as [deltahedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deltahedron "Deltahedron").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-FOOTNOTECundy1952-14)[Antiprisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiprism "Antiprism") have alternating triangles on their sides.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMontroll2009[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidSeTqBgAAQBAJpgPA4_4]-15)[Pyramids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyramid_(geometry) "Pyramid (geometry)") and [bipyramids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bipyramid "Bipyramid") are polyhedra with polygonal bases and triangles for lateral faces; the triangles are isosceles whenever they are right pyramids and bipyramids. The [Kleetope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kleetope "Kleetope") of a polyhedron is a new polyhedron made by replacing each face of the original with a pyramid, and so the faces of a Kleetope will be triangles.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-16) More generally, triangles can be found in higher dimensions, as in the generalized notion of triangles known as the [simplex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simplex "Simplex"), and the [polytopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polytope "Polytope") with triangular [facets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Facet "Facet") known as the [simplicial polytopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simplicial_polytope "Simplicial polytope").[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-FOOTNOTECromwell1997341-17)

Properties
----------

### Points, lines, and circles associated with a triangle

Each triangle has many special points inside it, on its edges, or otherwise associated with it. They are constructed by finding three lines associated symmetrically with the three sides (or vertices) and then proving that the three lines meet in a single point. An important tool for proving the existence of these points is [Ceva's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ceva%27s_theorem "Ceva's theorem"), which gives a criterion for determining when three such lines are [concurrent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concurrent_lines "Concurrent lines").[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHolme2010[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidzXwQGo8jyHUCpgPA210_210]-18) Similarly, lines associated with a triangle are often constructed by proving that three symmetrically constructed points are [collinear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collinear "Collinear"); here [Menelaus' theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Menelaus%27_theorem "Menelaus' theorem") gives a useful general criterion.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHolme2010[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidzXwQGo8jyHUCpgPA143_143]-19) In this section, just a few of the most commonly encountered constructions are explained.

A [perpendicular bisector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bisection "Bisection") of a side of a triangle is a straight line passing through the [midpoint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Midpoint "Midpoint") of the side and being perpendicular to it, forming a right angle with it.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-FOOTNOTELangMurrow1988[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidpc_kBwAAQBAJpgPA126_126&ndash;127]-20) The three perpendicular bisectors meet in a single point, the triangle's [circumcenter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumcenter "Circumcenter"); this point is the center of the [circumcircle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumcircle "Circumcircle"), the circle passing through all three vertices.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-FOOTNOTELangMurrow1988[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidpc_kBwAAQBAJpgPA128_128]-21)[Thales' theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thales%27_theorem "Thales' theorem") implies that if the circumcenter is located on the side of the triangle, then the angle opposite that side is a right angle.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAnglinLambek1995[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidflblBwAAQBAJpgPA30_30]-22) If the circumcenter is located inside the triangle, then the triangle is acute; if the circumcenter is located outside the triangle, then the triangle is obtuse.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-FOOTNOTERyan2008[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidb_qM4HImlPgCpgPA105_105]-23)

[![Image 9](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Triangle.Circumcenter.svg/124px-Triangle.Circumcenter.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Triangle.Circumcenter.svg)

The intersection of perpendicular bisectors is the [circumcenter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumcenter "Circumcenter").

[![Image 10](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Triangle.Incircle.svg/144px-Triangle.Incircle.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Triangle.Incircle.svg)

The intersection of the angle bisectors is the [incenter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Incenter "Incenter")

[![Image 11](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Triangle.Centroid.svg/250px-Triangle.Centroid.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Triangle.Centroid.svg)

The intersection of the medians known as the [centroid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centroid "Centroid")

An [altitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Altitude_(triangle) "Altitude (triangle)") of a triangle is a straight line through a vertex and perpendicular to the opposite side. This opposite side is called the base of the altitude, and the point where the altitude intersects the base (or its extension) is called the foot of the altitude.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-24) The length of the altitude is the distance between the base and the vertex. The three altitudes intersect in a single point, called the [orthocenter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthocenter "Orthocenter") of the triangle.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKing2021[httpsbooksgooglecombooksid6UgrEAAAQBAJpgPA153_153]-25) The orthocenter lies inside the triangle if and only if the triangle is acute.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-FOOTNOTERyan2008[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidb_qM4HImlPgCpgPA106_106]-26)

[![Image 12](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Triangle.NinePointCircle.svg/250px-Triangle.NinePointCircle.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Triangle.NinePointCircle.svg)

[![Image 13](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Triangle.EulerLine.svg/250px-Triangle.EulerLine.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Triangle.EulerLine.svg)

[Nine-point circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nine-point_circle "Nine-point circle") demonstrates a symmetry where six points lie on the edge of the triangle. [Euler's line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler%27s_line "Euler's line") is a straight line through the orthocenter (blue), the center of the nine-point circle (red), centroid (orange), and circumcenter (green).

An [angle bisector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle_bisector "Angle bisector") of a triangle is a straight line through a vertex that cuts the corresponding angle in half. The three angle bisectors intersect in a single point, the [incenter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Incenter "Incenter"), which is the center of the triangle's [incircle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Incircle "Incircle"). The incircle is the circle that lies inside the triangle and touches all three sides. Its radius is called the inradius. There are three other important circles, the [excircles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Excircle "Excircle"); they lie outside the triangle and touch one side, as well as the extensions of the other two. The centers of the incircles and excircles form an [orthocentric system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthocentric_system "Orthocentric system").[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-FOOTNOTERyan2008[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidb_qM4HImlPgCpgPA104_104]-27) The midpoints of the three sides and the feet of the three altitudes all lie on a single circle, the triangle's [nine-point circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nine-point_circle "Nine-point circle").[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKing2021[httpsbooksgooglecombooksid6UgrEAAAQBAJpgPA155_155]-28) The remaining three points for which it is named are the midpoints of the portion of altitude between the vertices and the [orthocenter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthocenter "Orthocenter"). The radius of the nine-point circle is half that of the circumcircle. It touches the incircle (at the [Feuerbach point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nine-point_circle "Nine-point circle")) and the three [excircles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Excircle "Excircle"). The orthocenter (blue point), the center of the nine-point circle (red), the centroid (orange), and the circumcenter (green) all lie on a single line, known as [Euler's line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler%27s_line "Euler's line") (red line). The center of the nine-point circle lies at the midpoint between the orthocenter and the circumcenter, and the distance between the centroid and the circumcenter is half that between the centroid and the orthocenter.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKing2021[httpsbooksgooglecombooksid6UgrEAAAQBAJpgPA155_155]-28) Generally, the incircle's center is not located on Euler's line.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-29)[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-30)

A [median](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Median_(geometry) "Median (geometry)") of a triangle is a straight line through a [vertex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_(geometry) "Vertex (geometry)") and the [midpoint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Midpoint "Midpoint") of the opposite side, and divides the triangle into two equal areas. The three medians intersect in a single point, the triangle's [centroid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centroid "Centroid") or geometric barycenter. The centroid of a rigid triangular object (cut out of a thin sheet of uniform density) is also its [center of mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Center_of_mass "Center of mass"): the object can be balanced on its centroid in a uniform gravitational field.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-FOOTNOTERyan2008[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidb_qM4HImlPgCpgPA102_102]-31) The centroid cuts every median in the ratio 2:1, i.e. the distance between a vertex and the centroid is twice the distance between the centroid and the midpoint of the opposite side. If one reflects a median in the angle bisector that passes through the same vertex, one obtains a _[symmedian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmedian "Symmedian")_. The three symmedians intersect in a single point, the [symmedian point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmedian_point "Symmedian point") of the triangle.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHolme2010[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidzXwQGo8jyHUCpgPA240_240]-32)

### Angles

[![Image 14](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Triangle_sommeangles.svg/250px-Triangle_sommeangles.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Triangle_sommeangles.svg)

The measures of the interior angles of the triangle always add up to 180 degrees (same color to point out they are equal).

The [sum of the measures of the interior angles of a triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sum_of_angles_of_a_triangle "Sum of angles of a triangle") in [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space") is always 180 degrees.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHeath1926Proposition_32-33) This fact is equivalent to Euclid's [parallel postulate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_postulate "Parallel postulate"). This allows the determination of the measure of the third angle of any triangle, given the measure of two angles.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGonick2024107%E2%80%93109-34) An _[exterior angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior\_angle "Exterior angle")_ of a triangle is an angle that is a linear pair (and hence [supplementary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supplementary_angle "Supplementary angle")) to an interior angle. The measure of an exterior angle of a triangle is equal to the sum of the measures of the two interior angles that are not adjacent to it; this is the [exterior angle theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_angle_theorem "Exterior angle theorem").[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-FOOTNOTERamsayRichtmyer1995[httpsbooksgooglecombooksid4CDpBwAAQBAJpgPA38_38]-35) The sum of the measures of the three exterior angles (one for each vertex) of any triangle is 360 degrees, and indeed, this is true for any convex polygon, no matter how many sides it has.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGonick2024224%E2%80%93225-36)

Another relation between the internal angles and triangles creates a new concept of [trigonometric functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trigonometric_function "Trigonometric function"). The primary trigonometric functions are [sine and cosine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sine_and_cosine "Sine and cosine"), as well as the other functions. They can be defined as the [ratio between any two sides of a right triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sine_and_cosine#Right-angled_triangle_definition "Sine and cosine").[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-FOOTNOTEYoung2017[httpsbooksgooglecombooksid476ZDwAAQBAJpgPA27_27]-37) In a scalene triangle, the trigonometric functions can be used to find the unknown measure of either a side or an internal angle; methods for doing so use the [law of sines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law_of_sines "Law of sines") and the [law of cosines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law_of_cosines "Law of cosines").[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAxler2012[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidB5RxDwAAQBAJpgPA634_634]-38)

Any three angles that add to 180° can be the internal angles of a triangle. Infinitely many triangles have the same angles, since specifying the angles of a triangle does not determine its size. (A [degenerate triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degeneracy_(mathematics)#Triangle "Degeneracy (mathematics)"), whose vertices are [collinear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collinearity "Collinearity"), has internal angles of 0° and 180°; whether such a shape counts as a triangle is a matter of convention.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-39)[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-40)) The conditions for three angles ![Image 15: {\displaystyle \alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b79333175c8b3f0840bfb4ec41b8072c83ea88d3), ![Image 16: {\displaystyle \beta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7ed48a5e36207156fb792fa79d29925d2f7901e8), and ![Image 17: {\displaystyle \gamma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a223c880b0ce3da8f64ee33c4f0010beee400b1a), each of them between 0° and 180°, to be the angles of a triangle can also be stated using trigonometric functions. For example, a triangle with angles ![Image 18: {\displaystyle \alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b79333175c8b3f0840bfb4ec41b8072c83ea88d3), ![Image 19: {\displaystyle \beta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7ed48a5e36207156fb792fa79d29925d2f7901e8), and ![Image 20: {\displaystyle \gamma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a223c880b0ce3da8f64ee33c4f0010beee400b1a) exists [if and only if](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/If_and_only_if "If and only if")[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-41)![Image 21: {\displaystyle \cos ^{2}\alpha +\cos ^{2}\beta +\cos ^{2}\gamma +2\cos(\alpha )\cos(\beta )\cos(\gamma )=1.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a5f498f3240f7a06d5371e324386d827904799d6)

### Similarity and congruence

[![Image 22](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Angle-angle-side_triangle_congruence.svg/250px-Angle-angle-side_triangle_congruence.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Angle-angle-side_triangle_congruence.svg)

This diagram illustrates the geometric principle of angle-angle-side triangle congruence: given triangle ABC and triangle A'B'C', triangle ABC is congruent with triangle A'B'C' if and only if: angle CAB is congruent with angle C'A'B', and angle ABC is congruent with angle A'B'C', and BC is congruent with B'C'. Note [hatch marks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hatch_mark#Congruency_notation "Hatch mark") are used here to show angle and side equalities.

Two triangles are said to be _[similar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Similarity\_(geometry) "Similarity (geometry)")_, if every angle of one triangle has the same measure as the corresponding angle in the other triangle. The corresponding sides of similar triangles have lengths that are in the same proportion, and this property is also sufficient to establish similarity.[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGonick2024157%E2%80%93167-42)

Some basic [theorems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theorem "Theorem") about similar triangles are:

*   [If and only if](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/If_and_only_if "If and only if") one pair of internal angles of two triangles have the same measure as each other, and another pair also have the same measure as each other, the triangles are similar.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGonick2024167-43)
*   If and only if one pair of corresponding sides of two triangles are in the same proportion as another pair of corresponding sides, and their included angles have the same measure, then the triangles are similar.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGonick2024171-44) (The _included angle_ for any two sides of a polygon is the internal angle between those two sides.)
*   If and only if three pairs of corresponding sides of two triangles are all in the same proportion, then the triangles are similar.[[b]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-45)

Two triangles that are [congruent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congruence_(geometry) "Congruence (geometry)") have exactly the same size and shape. All pairs of congruent triangles are also similar, but not all pairs of similar triangles are congruent. Given two congruent triangles, all pairs of corresponding interior angles are equal in measure, and all pairs of corresponding sides have the same length. This is a total of six equalities, but three are often sufficient to prove congruence.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGonick202464-46)

Some individually [necessary and sufficient conditions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Necessary_and_sufficient_condition "Necessary and sufficient condition") for a pair of triangles to be congruent are:[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGonick202465,_72%E2%80%9373,_111-47)

*   SAS Postulate: Two sides in a triangle have the same length as two sides in the other triangle, and the included angles have the same measure.
*   ASA: Two interior angles and the side between them in a triangle have the same measure and length, respectively, as those in the other triangle. (This is the basis of [surveying by triangulation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangulation_(surveying) "Triangulation (surveying)").)
*   SSS: Each side of a triangle has the same length as the corresponding side of the other triangle.
*   AAS: Two angles and a corresponding (non-included) side in a triangle have the same measure and length, respectively, as those in the other triangle. (This is sometimes referred to as _AAcorrS_ and then includes ASA above.)

### Possible side lengths

The [triangle inequality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle_inequality "Triangle inequality") states that the sum of the lengths of any two sides of a triangle must be greater than or equal to the length of the third side.[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-52) Conversely, some triangle with three given positive side lengths exists if and only if those side lengths satisfy the triangle inequality.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith200086%E2%80%9387-53) The sum of two side lengths can equal the length of the third side only in the case of a [degenerate triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degenerate_triangle "Degenerate triangle"), one with collinear vertices.

### Rigidity

[![Image 47](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Structural_rigidity_basic_examples.svg/250px-Structural_rigidity_basic_examples.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Structural_rigidity_basic_examples.svg)

Rigidity of a triangle and square

Unlike a rectangle, which may collapse into a [parallelogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram "Parallelogram") from pressure to one of its points,[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJordanSmith2010[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidtevqDwAAQBAJpgPA834_834]-54) triangles are sturdy because specifying the lengths of all three sides determines the angles.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGonick2024125-55) Therefore, a triangle will not change shape unless its sides are bent or extended or broken or if its joints break; in essence, each of the three sides supports the other two. A rectangle, in contrast, is more dependent on the strength of its joints in a structural sense.

Triangles are strong in terms of rigidity, but while packed in a [tessellating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tessellation "Tessellation") arrangement triangles are not as strong as [hexagons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hexagon "Hexagon") under compression (hence the prevalence of hexagonal forms in [nature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nature "Nature")). Tessellated triangles still maintain superior strength for [cantilevering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cantilever "Cantilever"), however, which is why engineering makes use of [tetrahedral trusses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_frame "Space frame").[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

### Triangulation

[![Image 48](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Triangulation_3-coloring.svg/250px-Triangulation_3-coloring.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Triangulation_3-coloring.svg)

Triangulation in a simple polygon

[Triangulation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangulation_(geometry) "Triangulation (geometry)") means the partition of any planar object into a collection of triangles. For example, in [polygon triangulation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon_triangulation "Polygon triangulation"), a polygon is subdivided into multiple triangles that are attached edge-to-edge, with the property that their vertices coincide with the set of vertices of the polygon. In the case of a [simple polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simple_polygon "Simple polygon") with ![Image 49: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) sides, there are ![Image 50: {\displaystyle n-2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ff40d66ad535411eedb9c686a9008a5089c35ac0) triangles that are separated by ![Image 51: {\displaystyle n-3}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3ee3741ee7dd3d098f3f16980e15c0435471dda0) diagonals. Triangulation of a simple polygon has a relationship to the [ear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ear_(mathematics) "Ear (mathematics)"), a vertex connected by two other vertices, the diagonal between which lies entirely within the polygon. The [two ears theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two_ears_theorem "Two ears theorem") states that every simple polygon that is not itself a triangle has at least two ears.[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMeisters1975-57)

Location of a point
-------------------

One way to identify locations of points in (or outside) a triangle is to place the triangle in an arbitrary location and orientation in the [Cartesian plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_plane "Cartesian plane"), and to use Cartesian coordinates. While convenient for many purposes, this approach has the disadvantage of all points' coordinate values being dependent on the arbitrary placement in the plane.[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOldknow1995-58)

Two systems avoid that feature, so that the coordinates of a point are not affected by moving the triangle, rotating it, or reflecting it as in a mirror, any of which gives a congruent triangle, or even by rescaling it to a similar triangle:[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-59)

Every triangle has a unique [Steiner inellipse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steiner_inellipse "Steiner inellipse") which is interior to the triangle and tangent at the midpoints of the sides. [Marden's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marden%27s_theorem "Marden's theorem") shows how to find the [foci of this ellipse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse#Elements_of_an_ellipse "Ellipse").[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKalman2008-60) This ellipse has the greatest area of any ellipse tangent to all three sides of the triangle. The [Mandart inellipse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mandart_inellipse "Mandart inellipse") of a triangle is the ellipse inscribed within the triangle tangent to its sides at the contact points of its excircles. a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc). The smallest possible ratio of the side of one inscribed square to the side of another in the same non-obtuse triangle is ![Image 71: {\displaystyle 2{\sqrt {2}}/3}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/10d52fd129be9caa71b9b2b181e7aff1a962f6aa).[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOxmanStupel2013-66) Both of these extreme cases occur for the isosceles right triangle.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

[![Image 72](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Lemoine_Hexagon.svg/250px-Lemoine_Hexagon.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Lemoine_Hexagon.svg)

The Lemoine hexagon inscribed in a triangle

The [Lemoine hexagon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lemoine_hexagon "Lemoine hexagon") is a [cyclic hexagon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hexagon#Cyclic_hexagon "Hexagon") with vertices given by the six intersections of the sides of a triangle with the three lines that are parallel to the sides and that pass through its [symmedian point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmedian_point "Symmedian point"). In either its [simple form or its self-intersecting form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon#Convexity_and_types_of_non-convexity "Polygon"), the Lemoine hexagon is interior to the triangle with two vertices on each side of the triangle.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

Every [convex polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_polygon "Convex polygon") with area ![Image 73: {\displaystyle T}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ec7200acd984a1d3a3d7dc455e262fbe54f7f6e0) can be inscribed in a triangle of area at most equal to ![Image 74: {\displaystyle 2T}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dfa35bfda1f2223910d2d59ec206845be843bcf0). Equality holds only if the polygon is a [parallelogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram "Parallelogram").[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEggleston2007149%E2%80%93160-67)

### Figures circumscribed about a triangle

[![Image 75](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Tangential_triangle.svg/250px-Tangential_triangle.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Tangential_triangle.svg)

[![Image 76](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Steiner_ellipse.svg/250px-Steiner_ellipse.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Steiner_ellipse.svg)

The [tangential triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangential_triangle "Tangential triangle") of a reference triangle (other than a right triangle) is the triangle whose sides are on the [tangent lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_line "Tangent line") to the reference triangle's circumcircle at its vertices.[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-68)

As mentioned above, every triangle has a unique circumcircle, a circle passing through all three vertices, whose center is the intersection of the perpendicular bisectors of the triangle's sides. Furthermore, every triangle has a unique [Steiner circumellipse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steiner_ellipse "Steiner ellipse"), which passes through the triangle's vertices and has its center at the triangle's centroid. Of all ellipses going through the triangle's vertices, it has the smallest area.[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-69)

The [Kiepert hyperbola](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kiepert_hyperbola "Kiepert hyperbola") is unique [conic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conic "Conic") that passes through the triangle's three vertices, its centroid, and its circumcenter.[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-70)

Of all triangles contained in a given [convex polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_polygon "Convex polygon"), one with maximal area can be found in linear time; its vertices may be chosen as three of the vertices of the given polygon.[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChandranMount1992-71)

### Circular triangles

[![Image 77](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Circular_triangles_convex_concave.png/500px-Circular_triangles_convex_concave.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Circular_triangles_convex_concave.png)

Circular triangles with a mixture of convex and concave edges

A [circular triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circular_triangle "Circular triangle") is a triangle with circular [arc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arc_(geometry) "Arc (geometry)") edges. The edges of a circular triangle may be either convex (bending outward) or concave (bending inward).[[c]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-72) The intersection of three [disks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disk_(mathematics) "Disk (mathematics)") forms a circular triangle whose sides are all convex. An example of a circular triangle with three convex edges is a [Reuleaux triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reuleaux_triangle "Reuleaux triangle"), which can be made by intersecting three circles of equal size. The construction may be performed with a compass alone without needing a straightedge, by the [Mohr–Mascheroni theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mohr%E2%80%93Mascheroni_theorem "Mohr–Mascheroni theorem"). Alternatively, it can be constructed by rounding the sides of an equilateral triangle.[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-73)

A special case of concave circular triangle can be seen in a [pseudotriangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudotriangle "Pseudotriangle").[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVahedivan_der_Stappen2008[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidSLo6okq4wVgCpgPA73_73]-74) A pseudotriangle is a [simply-connected](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simply_connected_space "Simply connected space") subset of the plane lying between three mutually tangent convex regions. These sides are three smoothed curved lines connecting their endpoints called the _cusp points_. Any pseudotriangle can be partitioned into many pseudotriangles with the boundaries of convex disks and [bitangent lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitangent "Bitangent"), a process known as pseudo-triangulation. For ![Image 78: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) disks in a pseudotriangle, the partition gives ![Image 79: {\displaystyle 2n-2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a8593ff30008d016680ae67c34865b96087b62fa) pseudotriangles and ![Image 80: {\displaystyle 3n-3}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6179f584bafb96ca589fb8ea2f0278e5b3cf2350) bitangent lines.[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPocchiolaVegter1999[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidvtkaCAAAQBAJpgPA259_259]-75) The [convex hull](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_hull "Convex hull") of any pseudotriangle is a triangle.[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDevadossO'Rourke2011[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidInJL6iAaIQQCpgPA93_93]-76)

### Triangle in non-planar space

[![Image 81](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Hyperbolic_triangle.svg/250px-Hyperbolic_triangle.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Hyperbolic_triangle.svg)

[![Image 82](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Triangle_trirectangle.png/250px-Triangle_trirectangle.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Triangle_trirectangle.png)

A non-planar triangle is a triangle not embedded in a [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space"), roughly speaking a flat space. This means triangles may also be discovered in several spaces, as in [hyperbolic space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_space "Hyperbolic space") and [spherical geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_geometry "Spherical geometry"). A triangle in hyperbolic space is called a [hyperbolic triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_triangle "Hyperbolic triangle"), and it can be obtained by drawing on a negatively curved surface, such as a [saddle surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saddle_surface "Saddle surface"). Likewise, a triangle in spherical geometry is called a [spherical triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_triangle "Spherical triangle"), and it can be obtained by drawing on a positively curved surface such as a [sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere "Sphere").[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-FOOTNOTENielsen2021[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidhHMjEAAAQBAJpgPA154_154]-77)

The triangles in both spaces have properties different from the triangles in Euclidean space. For example, as mentioned above, the internal angles of a triangle in Euclidean space always add up to 180°. However, the sum of the internal angles of a hyperbolic triangle is less than 180°, and for any spherical triangle, the sum is more than 180°.[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-FOOTNOTENielsen2021[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidhHMjEAAAQBAJpgPA154_154]-77) In particular, it is possible to draw a triangle on a sphere such that the measure of each of its internal angles equals 90°, adding up to a total of 270°. By [Girard's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Girard%27s_theorem "Girard's theorem"), the sum of the angles of a triangle on a sphere is ![Image 83: {\displaystyle 180^{\circ }\times (1+4f)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b227f42aa6dbffb0f7c03d1afa48ca19805aeed0), where ![Image 84: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) is the fraction of the sphere's area enclosed by the triangle.[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-78)[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-79)

In more general spaces, there are [comparison theorems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comparison_theorem "Comparison theorem") relating the properties of a triangle in the space to properties of a corresponding triangle in a model space like hyperbolic or elliptic space.[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBerger2002134%E2%80%93139-80) For example, a [CAT(k) space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CAT(k)_space "CAT(k) space") is characterized by such comparisons.[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBallmann1995viii+112-81)

### Fractal geometry

[Fractal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal "Fractal") shapes based on triangles include the [Sierpiński gasket](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sierpi%C5%84ski_triangle "Sierpiński triangle") and the [Koch snowflake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Koch_snowflake "Koch snowflake").[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#cite_note-82)
