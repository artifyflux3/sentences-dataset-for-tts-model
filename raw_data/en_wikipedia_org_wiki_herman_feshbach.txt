**Herman Feshbach** (2 February 1917 ‚Äì 22 December 2000) was an American physicist. He was an Institute Professor Emeritus of physics at [MIT](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MIT "MIT"). Feshbach is best known for [Feshbach resonance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feshbach_resonance "Feshbach resonance") and for writing, with [Philip M. Morse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philip_M._Morse "Philip M. Morse"), _Methods of Theoretical Physics_.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herman_Feshbach#cite_note-1)

Background
----------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Herman_Feshbach&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Background")]

Feshbach was born in [New York City](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_York_City "New York City") and graduated from the [City College of New York](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/City_College_of_New_York "City College of New York") in 1937. He was a member of the same family as [Murray Feshbach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Murray_Feshbach "Murray Feshbach"), the [Sovietologist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sovietologist "Sovietologist") and retired Georgetown University professor. He then went on to receive his Ph.D. in [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics") from [MIT](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MIT "MIT") in 1942.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herman_Feshbach#cite_note-2) Feshbach attended the [Shelter Island Conference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shelter_Island_Conference "Shelter Island Conference") of 1947.

Career
------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Herman_Feshbach&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Career")]

Feshbach was invited to stay at MIT after he received his doctorate. He remained on the physics faculty for over fifty years. From 1967 to 1973, he was the director of MIT's [Center for Theoretical Physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MIT_Center_for_Theoretical_Physics "MIT Center for Theoretical Physics"), and from 1973 to 1983, he was chairman of the physics department. In 1983, Feshbach was named as an institute professor, the highest faculty honor at MIT.

### Activism

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Herman_Feshbach&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Activism")]

Feshbach was active in the [nuclear disarmament](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_disarmament "Nuclear disarmament") movement and was a founder and first chairman of the [Union of Concerned Scientists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Union_of_Concerned_Scientists "Union of Concerned Scientists"). In 1969, he participated in a protest against military research at MIT.

He became concerned about the condition of scientists behind the [Iron Curtain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron_Curtain "Iron Curtain"), and worked to establish contacts between Western scientists and their [Eastern Bloc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastern_Bloc "Eastern Bloc") counterparts. Prof. Feshbach also championed the cause of [Andrei Sakharov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andrei_Sakharov "Andrei Sakharov") and other Soviet [refuseniks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Refuseniks "Refuseniks"). He first met Sakharov in the mid-1970s; Feshbach wrote about meeting Sakharov after his release from internal exile, in an article that appeared in _[Physics Today](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics\_Today "Physics Today")_.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herman_Feshbach#cite_note-3)

Feshbach was a strong believer in equality of opportunity, especially within the scientific community. He worked to increase the number of women and minority members in both the physics department and at MIT in general. In the early 1990s, he was chairman of the MIT faculty's Equal Opportunity Committee, which made recommendations for recruiting and hiring more women and minority faculty members.

Death
-----

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Herman_Feshbach&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Death")]

Feshbach died of heart failure at Youville Hospital in [Cambridge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cambridge,_Massachusetts "Cambridge, Massachusetts"). He was 83.

-----------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Herman_Feshbach&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Awards and honors")]

Feshbach joined the [National Academy of Sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_National_Academy_of_Sciences "United States National Academy of Sciences") in 1969 and was president of the [American Physical Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Physical_Society "American Physical Society") from 1980 to 1981. From 1982 to 1986, he was president of the [American Academy of Arts and Sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Academy_of_Arts_and_Sciences "American Academy of Arts and Sciences").

In 1986, Feshbach was awarded the [National Medal of Science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Medal_of_Science "National Medal of Science").

In 1984, the physics department honored Feshbach for his decades of service by starting the annual Herman Feshbach Lectures. The physics department also has an endowed Herman Feshbach chair, established in 1999 to support theoretical physicists. It is currently held by [Frank Wilczek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frank_Wilczek "Frank Wilczek").

The American Physical Society awards the [Herman Feshbach Prize in Theoretical Nuclear Physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herman_Feshbach_Prize_in_Theoretical_Nuclear_Physics "Herman Feshbach Prize in Theoretical Nuclear Physics"); it is awarded annually and was inaugurated in 2014.
