**Alpha Centauri** (**α Centauri**, **α Cen**, or **Alpha Cen**) is a [star system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system "Star system") in the southern [constellation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constellation "Constellation") of [Centaurus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centaurus_(constellation) "Centaurus (constellation)"). It consists of three [stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star "Star"): Rigil Kentaurus (α Centauri A), Toliman (α Centauri B), and [Proxima Centauri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proxima_Centauri "Proxima Centauri") (α Centauri C).[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-WGSN-15) Proxima Centauri is the [closest star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_nearest_stars "List of nearest stars") to the [Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun "Sun") at 4.2465[light-years](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light-year "Light-year") (ly), which is 1.3020 [parsecs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parsec "Parsec") (pc).

Rigil Kentaurus and Toliman are [Sun-like](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun-like "Sun-like") stars ([class G](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G-type_main-sequence_star "G-type main-sequence star") and [K](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K-type_main-sequence_star "K-type main-sequence star"), respectively) that together form the [binary star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_star "Binary star") system α Centauri AB. To the [naked eye](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naked_eye "Naked eye"), these two main components appear to be a single star with an [apparent magnitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apparent_magnitude "Apparent magnitude") of −0.27. It is the brightest star in the constellation and the [third-brightest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_brightest_stars "List of brightest stars") in the [night sky](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Night_sky "Night sky"), outshone by only [Sirius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sirius "Sirius") and [Canopus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canopus "Canopus"). α Centauri AB is the nearest binary stars to the Sun at a distance of 4.344 ly (1.33 pc).

Rigil Kentaurus has 1.1 times the [mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_mass "Solar mass") (M☉) and 1.5 times the [luminosity of the Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_luminosity "Solar luminosity") (L☉), while Toliman is smaller and cooler, at 0.9 M☉ and less than 0.5 L☉.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-16) The pair orbit around a [common centre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barycentre "Barycentre") with an orbital period of 79 years.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-SixthCatOrbVisBin-17) Their elliptical orbit is [eccentric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity "Orbital eccentricity"), so that the distance between A and B varies from 35.6[astronomical units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_unit "Astronomical unit") (AU), or about the distance between [Pluto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pluto "Pluto") and the Sun, to 11.2 AU, or about the distance between [Saturn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saturn "Saturn") and the Sun.

[Proxima Centauri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proxima_Centauri "Proxima Centauri") is a small faint [red dwarf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_dwarf "Red dwarf") ([class M](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_classification#Class_M "Stellar classification")). Though not visible to the naked eye, Proxima Centauri is the closest star to the Sun at a distance of 4.24 ly (1.30 pc), slightly closer than α Centauri AB. The distance between Proxima Centauri and α Centauri AB is about 13,000[AU](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_unit "Astronomical unit") (0.21 ly),[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Kervella2017-18) equivalent to about 430 times the radius of [Neptune's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune "Neptune") orbit.

Proxima Centauri has two confirmed planets — [Proxima b](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proxima_Centauri_b "Proxima Centauri b") and [Proxima d](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proxima_Centauri_d "Proxima Centauri d"). The former is an [Earth-sized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terrestrial_planet "Terrestrial planet") planet in the [habitable zone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habitable_zone "Habitable zone") (though it is unlikely to be habitable) while the latter is a [sub-Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sub-Earth "Sub-Earth") which orbits very closely to the star.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Faria2022-19) A possible but disputed third planet, [Proxima c](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proxima_Centauri_c "Proxima Centauri c"), is a [mini-Neptune](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mini-Neptune "Mini-Neptune")1.5[astronomical units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_unit "Astronomical unit") away.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Artigau2022-20) Rigil Kentaurus may have a [Saturn-mass planet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri_Ab "Alpha Centauri Ab") in the habitable zone, though it is not yet known with certainty to be planetary in nature.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-WagnerBoehle2021-21)[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Aniket2025-22)[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Beichman2025-23) Toliman has no known planets.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Rajpaul2016-24)

Etymology and nomenclature
--------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Alpha_Centauri&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Etymology and nomenclature")]

α Centauri ([Latinised](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latinisation_of_names "Latinisation of names") to Alpha Centauri) is the system's [designation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bayer_designation "Bayer designation") given by [J. Bayer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johann_Bayer "Johann Bayer") in 1603. It belongs to the constellation [Centaurus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centaurus "Centaurus"), named after the [part human, part horse creature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centaur "Centaur") in Greek mythology; [Heracles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heracles "Heracles") accidentally wounded the centaur and placed him in the sky after his death. Alpha Centauri marks the right front hoof of the Centaur.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-25) The common name Rigil Kentaurus is a Latinisation of the Arabic translation رجل القنطورس _Rijl al-Qinṭūrus,_ meaning "the Foot of the Centaur".[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Kunitx-Smart-2006-26)[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-27)_Qinṭūrus_ is the Arabic transliteration of the Greek Κένταυρος (Kentaurus).[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-28) The name is frequently abbreviated to Rigil Kent () or even Rigil, though the latter name is better known for [Rigel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rigel "Rigel") (β Orionis).[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Allen-29)[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-30)[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-31)[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Kunitx-Smart-2006-26)[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-32)[[b]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-36)

An alternative name found in European sources, Toliman, is an approximation of the Arabic الظليمان _aẓ-Ẓalīmān_ (in older transcription, _aṭ-Ṭhalīmān_), meaning 'the (two male) Ostriches', an appellation [Zakariya al-Qazwini](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zakariya_al-Qazwini "Zakariya al-Qazwini") had applied to the pair of stars [Lambda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lambda_Sagittarii "Lambda Sagittarii") and [Mu Sagittarii](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mu_Sagittarii "Mu Sagittarii"); it was often unclear on old star maps which name was intended to go with which star (or stars), and the referents changed over time.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-37) The name Toliman originates with [Jacob Golius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacobus_Golius "Jacobus Golius")' 1669 edition of [Al-Farghani](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Farghani "Al-Farghani")'s _Compendium_. Tolimân is Golius' Latinisation of the Arabic name الظلمان _al-Ẓulmān_ "the ostriches", the name of an asterism of which Alpha Centauri formed the main star.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-38)[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-39)[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-40)[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-41)

α Centauri C was discovered in 1915 by [Robert T. A. Innes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_T._A._Innes "Robert T. A. Innes"),[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Innes1915-42) who suggested that it be named Proxima Centaurus,[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Innes1917-43) from [Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin_language "Latin language")'the nearest [star] of [Centaurus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centaurus "Centaurus")'.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-oxford2010-44) The name Proxima Centauri later became more widely used and is now listed by the [International Astronomical Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union "International Astronomical Union") (IAU) as the approved proper name;[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-aj39_913_20-45)[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-WGSNproxima-46) it is frequently abbreviated to Proxima.

In 2016, the [Working Group on Star Names](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IAU_Working_Group_on_Star_Names "IAU Working Group on Star Names") of the IAU,[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-WGSN-15) having decided to attribute proper names to individual component stars rather than to [multiple systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiple_star "Multiple star"),[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-TriRpt18-47) approved the name Rigil Kentaurus () as being restricted to α Centauri A and the name Proxima Centauri () for α Centauri C.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-IAU-LSN-48) On 10 August 2018, the [IAU](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union "International Astronomical Union") approved the name Toliman () for α Centauri B.[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-IAU-CSN-49)

During the 19th century, the northern amateur popularist E.H. Burritt used the now-obscure name Bungula ().[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-50) Its origin is not known, but it may have been coined from the Greek letter [beta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beta "Beta") (β) and Latin _ungula_ 'hoof', originally for [Beta Centauri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beta_Centauri "Beta Centauri") (the other hoof).[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Allen-29)[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Kunitx-Smart-2006-26)

In [Chinese astronomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_astronomy "Chinese astronomy"), 南門 _Nán Mén_, meaning [Southern Gate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horn_(Chinese_constellation) "Horn (Chinese constellation)"), refers to an [asterism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asterism_(astronomy) "Asterism (astronomy)") consisting of Alpha Centauri and [Epsilon Centauri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epsilon_Centauri "Epsilon Centauri"). Consequently, the [Chinese name](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_star_names "Chinese star names") for Alpha Centauri itself is 南門二 _Nán Mén Èr_, the Second Star of the Southern Gate.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-51)

To the Indigenous [Boorong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wergaia "Wergaia") people of northwestern [Victoria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Victoria_(Australia) "Victoria (Australia)") in Australia, Alpha Centauri and [Beta Centauri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beta_Centauri "Beta Centauri") are Bermbermgle,[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-hamacher-52) two brothers noted for their courage and destructiveness, who speared and killed Tchingal "The Emu" (the [Coalsack Nebula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coalsack_Nebula "Coalsack Nebula")).[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-stanbridge-53) The form in [Wotjobaluk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wergaia "Wergaia") is Bram-bram-bult.[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-hamacher-52)

Location of α Cen in Centaurus

[![Image 4: Two bright stars against a dense background of fainter stars, with one of the fainter stars circled in red](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Alpha%2C_Beta_and_Proxima_Centauri_%281%29.jpg/330px-Alpha%2C_Beta_and_Proxima_Centauri_%281%29.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Alpha,_Beta_and_Proxima_Centauri_(1).jpg)

Alpha Centauri AB (left) forms a triple star system with [Proxima Centauri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proxima_Centauri "Proxima Centauri") (below, south of, α Centauri AB), circled in red. The bright star to the right is [Beta Centauri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beta_Centauri "Beta Centauri").

To the naked eye, α Centauri AB appear to be a single star, the [brightest in the southern constellation of Centaurus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_stars_in_Centaurus "List of stars in Centaurus").[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Moore-2002-54) Their apparent angular separation varies over about 80 years between 2 and 22 [arcseconds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arcsecond "Arcsecond") (the [naked eye](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naked_eye "Naked eye") has a resolution of 60 arcsec),[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-55) but through much of the orbit, both are easily resolved in binoculars or small telescopes.[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-AOST2a-56) At −0.27 [apparent magnitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apparent_magnitude "Apparent magnitude") (combined for A and B magnitudes (see [Apparent magnitude §Magnitude addition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apparent_magnitude#Magnitude_addition "Apparent magnitude"))), Alpha Centauri is a [first-magnitude star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First-magnitude_star "First-magnitude star") and is fainter only than [Sirius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sirius "Sirius") and [Canopus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canopus "Canopus").[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Moore-2002-54) It is the outer star of The Pointers or The Southern Pointers,[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-AOST2a-56) so called because the line through [Beta Centauri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beta_Centauri "Beta Centauri") (Hadar/Agena),[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-NortonSA-57) some 4.5° west,[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-AOST2a-56) points to the constellation [Crux](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crux "Crux")—the Southern Cross.[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-AOST2a-56)[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-AOST2-58) The Pointers easily distinguish the true Southern Cross from the fainter [asterism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asterism_(astronomy) "Asterism (astronomy)") known as the [False Cross](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/False_Cross "False Cross").[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-59)

South of about 29° South latitude, α Cen is [circumpolar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumpolar_star "Circumpolar star") and never sets below the horizon.[[c]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-60) North of about 29° N latitude, Alpha Centauri never rises. Alpha Centauri lies close to the southern horizon when viewed from latitude 29° N to the [equator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equator "Equator") (close to [Hermosillo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermosillo "Hermosillo") and [Chihuahua City](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chihuahua_City "Chihuahua City") in [Mexico](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mexico "Mexico"); [Galveston, Texas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galveston,_Texas "Galveston, Texas"); [Ocala, Florida](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ocala,_Florida "Ocala, Florida"); and [Lanzarote](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lanzarote "Lanzarote"), the [Canary Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canary_Islands "Canary Islands") of [Spain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spain "Spain")), but only for a short time around its [culmination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Culmination "Culmination").[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-NortonSA-57) The star culminates each year at local midnight on 24 April and at local 9 p.m. on 8 June.[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-NortonSA-57)[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-61)

As seen from Earth, Proxima Centauri is 2.2° southwest from α Centauri AB; this distance is about four times the [angular diameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_diameter "Angular diameter") of the [Moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moon "Moon").[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Matt93-62) Proxima Centauri appears as a deep-red star of a typical apparent magnitude of 11.1 in a sparsely populated star field, requiring moderately sized telescopes to be seen. Listed as V645 Cen in the _[General Catalogue of Variable Stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General\_Catalogue\_of\_Variable\_Stars "General Catalogue of Variable Stars")_, version 4.2, this [UV Ceti star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UV_Ceti_star "UV Ceti star") or "flare star" can unexpectedly brighten rapidly by as much as 0.6 [magnitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnitude_(astronomy) "Magnitude (astronomy)") at visual wavelengths, then fade after only a few minutes.[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-bendict1998-63) Some amateur and professional astronomers regularly monitor for outbursts using either optical or radio telescopes.[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-64) In August 2015, the largest recorded flares of the star occurred, with the star becoming 8.3 times brighter than normal on 13 August, in the [B band (blue light region)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apparent_magnitude#Standard_reference_values "Apparent magnitude").[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-proximaflare-65)

### Observational history

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Alpha_Centauri&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Observational history")]

[![Image 5: Image of a very large telescope dome open to the night sky, with the Milky Way running diagonally across the sky above it and many southern stars and constellations labelled and connected by lines](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/The_Very_Large_Telescope_and_the_star_system_Alpha_Centauri.jpg/330px-The_Very_Large_Telescope_and_the_star_system_Alpha_Centauri.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:The_Very_Large_Telescope_and_the_star_system_Alpha_Centauri.jpg)

The [Very Large Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Very_Large_Telescope "Very Large Telescope") open to the night sky, with the Milky Way running diagonally across the sky above it and many southern stars and constellations labelled and connected by lines, including Alpha Centauri and the not visible Proxima Centauri.

Alpha Centauri is listed in the 2nd century star catalog appended to [Ptolemy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Claudius_Ptolemaeus "Claudius Ptolemaeus")'s _[Almagest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Almagest "Almagest")_. Ptolemy gave its [ecliptic coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecliptic_coordinates "Ecliptic coordinates"), but texts differ as to whether the ecliptic latitude reads 44° 10′ South or 41° 10′ South.[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-66) (Presently the ecliptic latitude is 43.5° South, but it has decreased by a fraction of a degree since [Ptolemy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ptolemy "Ptolemy")'s time due to [proper motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proper_motion "Proper motion").) In Ptolemy's time, Alpha Centauri was visible from [Alexandria, Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexandria,_Egypt "Alexandria, Egypt"), at 31° N, but, due to [precession](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Precession_of_the_equinoxes "Precession of the equinoxes"), its declination is now –60° 51′ South, and it can no longer be seen at that latitude. English explorer [Robert Hues](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Hues "Robert Hues") brought Alpha Centauri to the attention of European observers in his 1592 work _Tractatus de Globis_, along with Canopus and [Achernar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Achernar "Achernar"), noting:

> Now, therefore, there are but three [Stars of the first magnitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_magnitude_star "First magnitude star") that I could perceive in all those parts which are never seene here in [England](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/England "England"). The first of these is that bright Star in the sterne of [Argo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Argo_Navis "Argo Navis") which they call Canobus [Canopus]. The second [Achernar] is in the end of [Eridanus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eridanus_(constellation) "Eridanus (constellation)"). The third [Alpha Centauri] is in the right foote of the [Centaure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centaurus "Centaurus").[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-knobel-67)

The [binary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_star "Binary star") nature of Alpha Centauri AB was recognized in December 1689 by Jean Richaud, while observing a passing [comet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comet "Comet") from his station in [Puducherry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Puducherry_(union_territory) "Puducherry (union territory)"). Alpha Centauri was only the third binary star to be discovered, preceded by [Mizar AB](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mizar "Mizar") and [Acrux](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acrux "Acrux").[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-KameswaraRao1984-68)

The large proper motion of Alpha Centauri AB was discovered by [Manuel John Johnson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manuel_John_Johnson "Manuel John Johnson"), observing from [Saint Helena](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saint_Helena "Saint Helena"), who informed [Thomas Henderson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Henderson_(astronomer) "Thomas Henderson (astronomer)") at the [Royal Observatory, Cape of Good Hope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Observatory,_Cape_of_Good_Hope "Royal Observatory, Cape of Good Hope") of it. The [parallax](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallax_in_astronomy "Parallax in astronomy") of Alpha Centauri was subsequently determined by Henderson from many exacting positional observations of the AB system between April 1832 and May 1833. He withheld his results, however, because he suspected they were too large to be true, but eventually published them in 1839 after [Bessel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Wilhelm_Bessel "Friedrich Wilhelm Bessel") released his own accurately determined parallax for [61 Cygni](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/61_Cygni "61 Cygni") in 1838.[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-autogenerated345-69) For this reason, Alpha Centauri is sometimes considered as the second star to have its distance measured because Henderson's work was not fully acknowledged at first.[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-autogenerated345-69) (The distance of Alpha Centauri from the Earth is now reckoned at 4.396[light-years](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light-year "Light-year") or 4.159×10 13 km.)

[![Image 6](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/South_celestial_pole.png/250px-South_celestial_pole.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:South_celestial_pole.png)

Alpha Centauri (Rigel Kentaurus) around the [South celestial pole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_celestial_pole "South celestial pole")

[John Herschel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Herschel "John Herschel") made the first [micrometrical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Micrometer_(device) "Micrometer (device)") observations in 1834.[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-JHerschel1847-70) Since the early 20th century, measures have been made with [photographic plates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photographic_plates "Photographic plates").[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-adsabs.harvard.edu-71)

By 1926, [William Stephen Finsen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Stephen_Finsen "William Stephen Finsen") calculated the approximate [orbit elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_elements "Orbital elements") close to those now accepted for this system.[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Aitken-72) All future positions are now sufficiently accurate for visual observers to determine the relative places of the stars from a binary star [ephemeris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ephemeris "Ephemeris").[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-73) Others, like D. Pourbaix (2002), have regularly refined the precision of new published orbital elements.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-SixthCatOrbVisBin-17)

[Robert T. A. Innes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_T._A._Innes "Robert T. A. Innes") discovered [Proxima Centauri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proxima_Centauri "Proxima Centauri") in 1915 by blinking photographic plates taken at different times during a [proper motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proper_motion "Proper motion") survey. These showed large proper motion and parallax similar in both size and direction to those of α Centauri AB, which suggested that Proxima Centauri is part of the α Centauri system and slightly closer to Earth than α Centauri AB. As a result, Innes concluded that Proxima Centauri was the closest star to Earth yet discovered.

Location and motion
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Alpha_Centauri&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Location and motion")]

Alpha Centauri may be inside the [G-cloud](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G-cloud "G-cloud") of the [Local Bubble](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_Bubble "Local Bubble"),[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-74) and its nearest known system is the binary [brown dwarf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brown_dwarf "Brown dwarf") system [Luhman 16](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luhman_16 "Luhman 16"), at 3.6 [light-years](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light-year "Light-year") (1.1 [parsecs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parsec "Parsec")) distance.[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-substellarcompanion-75)[_[failed verification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability "Wikipedia:Verifiability")_]

Alpha Centauri (with unlabeled Proxima Centauri) on a radar map of all known stellar and [substellar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Substellar "Substellar") objects within 9 light years (ly), arranged clockwise in [hours](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hour_angle "Hour angle") of [right ascension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_ascension "Right ascension"), and marked by distance (▬) and position (◆). Positions are marked inward from their distance markings, connected by lines according to their [declinations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Declination "Declination") (doted when positive), representing the [arcs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circular_arc "Circular arc") of the declinations viewed edge-on.

All components of α Centauri display significant [proper motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proper_motion "Proper motion") against the background sky. Over centuries, this causes their apparent positions to slowly change.[[88]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-92) Proper motion was unknown to ancient astronomers. Most assumed that the stars were permanently fixed on the [celestial sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celestial_sphere "Celestial sphere"), as stated in the works of the philosopher Aristotle.[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-93) In 1718, [Edmond Halley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edmond_Halley "Edmond Halley") found that some stars had significantly moved from their ancient [astrometric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrometric "Astrometric") positions.[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-berry-94)

In the 1830s, [Thomas Henderson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Henderson_(astronomer) "Thomas Henderson (astronomer)") discovered the true distance to α Centauri by analysing his many astrometric mural circle observations.[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Henderson1839-76)[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-95) He then realised this system also likely had a high proper motion.[[92]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-96)[[93]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-97)[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Aitken-72) In this case, the apparent stellar motion was found using [Nicolas Louis de Lacaille](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nicolas_Louis_de_Lacaille "Nicolas Louis de Lacaille")'s astrometric observations of 1751–1752,[[94]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-98) by the observed differences between the two measured positions in different epochs.

Calculated proper motion of the centre of mass for α Centauri AB is about 3620 mas/y (milliarcseconds per year) toward the west and 694 mas/y toward the north, giving an overall motion of 3686 mas/y in a direction 11° north of west.[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Kervella2016-99)[[e]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-100) The motion of the centre of mass is about 6.1[arcmin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arcmin "Arcmin") each century, or 1.02[°](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_(angle) "Degree (angle)") each millennium. The speed in the western direction is 23.0 km/s (14.3 mi/s) and in the northerly direction 4.4 km/s (2.7 mi/s). Using [spectroscopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopy "Spectroscopy") the mean radial velocity has been determined to be around 22.4 km/s (13.9 mi/s) towards the Solar System.[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Kervella2016-99) This gives a speed with respect to the Sun of 32.4 km/s (20.1 mi/s), very close to the peak in the distribution of speeds of nearby stars.[[96]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-101)

Since α Centauri AB is almost exactly in the plane of the [Milky Way](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milky_Way "Milky Way") as viewed from Earth, many stars appear behind it. In early May 2028, α Centauri A will pass between the Earth and a distant red star, when there is a 45% probability that an [Einstein ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_ring "Einstein ring") will be observed. Other [conjunctions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conjunction_(astronomy) "Conjunction (astronomy)") will also occur in the coming decades, allowing accurate measurement of proper motions and possibly giving information on planets.[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Kervella2016-99)

#### Predicted future changes

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Alpha_Centauri&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Predicted future changes")]

[![Image 8: Line graph with x-axis in thousands of years and y-axis in light years, the lines on the graph being labelled with the names of stars.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Near-stars-past-future-en.svg/330px-Near-stars-past-future-en.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Near-stars-past-future-en.svg)

Distances of the [nearest stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_nearest_stars "List of nearest stars") from 20,000 years ago until 80,000 years in the future[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

[![Image 9: Animated image of a sky chart of the southern celestial hemisphere labelled with years.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Alpha_Cen_proper_motions.gif/330px-Alpha_Cen_proper_motions.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Alpha_Cen_proper_motions.gif)

Animation showing motion of α Centauri through the sky. (The other stars are held fixed for didactic reasons.) "Oggi" means today; "anni" means years.

Based on the system's common proper motion and radial velocities, α Centauri will continue to change its position in the sky significantly and will gradually brighten. For example, in about 6,200[CE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Common_Era "Common Era"), α Centauri's true motion will cause an extremely rare [first-magnitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First-magnitude_star "First-magnitude star") stellar conjunction with [Beta Centauri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beta_Centauri "Beta Centauri"), forming a brilliant optical [double star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double_star "Double star") in the southern sky.[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-AOST2-58) It will then pass just north of the Southern Cross or [Crux](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crux "Crux"), before moving northwest and up towards the present [celestial equator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celestial_equator "Celestial equator") and away from the [galactic plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galactic_plane "Galactic plane"). By about 26,700[CE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Common_Era "Common Era"), in the present-day constellation of [Hydra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydra_(constellation) "Hydra (constellation)"), α Centauri will reach perihelion at 0.90[pc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parsec "Parsec") or 2.9[ly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light-year "Light-year") away,[[97]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Matthews-102) though later calculations suggest that this will occur in 27,000 AD.[[98]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Bailer2015-103) At its nearest approach, α Centauri will attain a maximum [apparent magnitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apparent_magnitude "Apparent magnitude") of −0.86, comparable to present-day magnitude of [Canopus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canopus "Canopus"), but it will still not surpass that of [Sirius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sirius "Sirius"), which will brighten incrementally over the next 60,000 years, and will continue to be the brightest star as seen from Earth (other than the Sun) for the next 210,000 years.[[99]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-104)

Alpha Centauri is a triple star system, with its two main stars, A and B, together comprising a [binary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_star "Binary star") component. The _AB_ designation, or older _A×B_, denotes the mass centre of a main binary system relative to companion star(s) in a multiple star system.[[100]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-DoubleStarsHeintz-105)_AB-C_ refers to the component of Proxima Centauri in relation to the central binary, being the distance between the centre of mass and the outlying companion. Because the distance between Proxima (C) and either of Alpha Centauri A or B is similar, the AB binary system is sometimes treated as a single gravitational object.[[101]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-wds1996-106)

[![Image 10: Graphic image of a near-circle and a narrow ellipse labelled respectively as "B's real trajectory" and "B's apparent trajectory", with years marked along portions of the ellipses.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Orbit_Alpha_Centauri_AB_arcsec.png/500px-Orbit_Alpha_Centauri_AB_arcsec.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Orbit_Alpha_Centauri_AB_arcsec.png)

Apparent and true orbits of Alpha Centauri. The A component is held stationary, and the relative orbital motion of the B component is shown. The apparent orbit (thin ellipse) is the shape of the orbit as seen by an observer on Earth. The true orbit is the shape of the orbit viewed perpendicular to the plane of the orbital motion. According to the radial velocity versus time,[[102]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Pourbaix-107) the radial separation of A and B along the line of sight had reached a maximum in 2007, with B being further from Earth than A. The orbit is divided here into 80 points: each step refers to a timestep of approx. 0.99888 years or 364.84 days.

[![Image 11](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Orbital_plot_of_Proxima_Centauri.jpg/500px-Orbital_plot_of_Proxima_Centauri.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Orbital_plot_of_Proxima_Centauri.jpg)

Orbital plot of Proxima Centauri around the bright apparent star Alpha Centauri AB, with position change marked (in thousands of years).

The A and B components of Alpha Centauri have an orbital period of 79.762 years. Their orbit is moderately [eccentric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity "Orbital eccentricity"), as it has an eccentricity of almost 0.52;[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Akeson2021-6) their closest approach or [periastron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apsis "Apsis") is 11.2 AU (1.68×10 9 km), or about the distance between the Sun and Saturn; and their furthest separation or [apastron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apsis "Apsis") is 35.6 AU (5.33×10 9 km), about the distance between the Sun and Pluto.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-SixthCatOrbVisBin-17) The most recent [periastron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apsis "Apsis") was in August 1955 and the next will occur in May 2035; the most recent [apastron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apsis "Apsis") was in May 1995 and will next occur in 2075.

Viewed from Earth, the apparent orbit of A and B means that their separation and [position angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Position_angle "Position angle") (PA) are in continuous change throughout their projected orbit. Observed stellar positions in 2019 are separated by 4.92 [arcsec](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minute_and_second_of_arc "Minute and second of arc") through the PA of 337.1°, increasing to 5.49 arcsec through 345.3° in 2020.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-SixthCatOrbVisBin-17) The closest recent approach was in February 2016, at 4.0 arcsec through the PA of 300°.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-SixthCatOrbVisBin-17)[[103]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-108) The observed maximum separation of these stars is about 22 arcsec, while the minimum distance is 1.7 arcsec.[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Aitken-72) The widest separation occurred during February 1976, and the next will be in January 2056.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-SixthCatOrbVisBin-17)

Alpha Centauri C is about 13,000 AU (0.21 ly; 1.9×10 12 km) from Alpha Centauri AB, equivalent to about 5% of the distance between Alpha Centauri AB and the Sun.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Kervella2017-18)[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Matt93-62)[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-adsabs.harvard.edu-71) Until 2017, measurements of its small speed and its trajectory were of too little accuracy and duration in years to determine whether it is bound to Alpha Centauri AB or unrelated.

Radial velocity measurements made in 2017 were precise enough to show that Proxima Centauri and Alpha Centauri AB are gravitationally bound.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Kervella2017-18) The orbital period of Proxima Centauri is approximately 511 000+41 000

−30 000 years, with an eccentricity of 0.5, much more eccentric than [Mercury](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mercury_(planet) "Mercury (planet)")'s. Proxima Centauri comes within 4100+700

−600[AU](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_unit "Astronomical unit") of AB at periastron, and its apastron occurs at 12 300+200

−100[AU](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_unit "Astronomical unit").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Akeson2021-6)

### Physical properties

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Alpha_Centauri&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Physical properties")]

[![Image 12](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/ESO_-_Alpha_Centauri_in_the_HR-System_%28by%29.jpg/250px-ESO_-_Alpha_Centauri_in_the_HR-System_%28by%29.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:ESO_-_Alpha_Centauri_in_the_HR-System_(by).jpg)

ESO - Alpha Centauri in the HR-System (by)

[![Image 13](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Relative_sizes_of_the_Alpha_Centauri_components_and_other_objects_%28artist%E2%80%99s_impression%29.tif/lossy-page1-250px-Relative_sizes_of_the_Alpha_Centauri_components_and_other_objects_%28artist%E2%80%99s_impression%29.tif.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Relative_sizes_of_the_Alpha_Centauri_components_and_other_objects_(artist%E2%80%99s_impression).tif)

Relative sizes and colour of the Alpha Centauri A, B and C (Proxima) and other [local stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_nearest_stars_and_brown_dwarfs "List of nearest stars and brown dwarfs"), incl. the Sun and Jupiter (artist's impression)

[Asteroseismic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asteroseismology "Asteroseismology") studies, [chromospheric activity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chromospheric_activity "Chromospheric activity"), and stellar rotation ([gyrochronology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gyrochronology "Gyrochronology")) are all consistent with the Alpha Centauri system being similar in age to, or slightly older than, the Sun.[[104]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Mam08-109) Asteroseismic analyses that incorporate tight observational constraints on the stellar parameters for the Alpha Centauri stars have yielded age estimates of 4.85±0.5 Gyr,[[105]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Thevenin02-110)5.0±0.5 Gyr,[[106]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Bazot12-111)5.2 ± 1.9 Gyr,[[107]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Miglio05-112) 6.4 Gyr,[[108]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Thoul03-113) and 6.52±0.3 Gyr.[[109]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Eggenberger04-114) Age estimates for the stars based on chromospheric activity (Calcium H & K emission) yield 4.4 ± 2.1 Gyr, whereas gyrochronology yields 5.0±0.3 Gyr.[[104]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Mam08-109)[Stellar evolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_evolution "Stellar evolution") theory implies both stars are slightly older than the Sun at 5 to 6 billion years, as derived by their mass and spectral characteristics.[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Matt93-62)[[110]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-stellar-model-kim-115)

From the [orbital elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_elements "Orbital elements"), the total mass of Alpha Centauri AB is about 2.0[M☉](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_mass "Solar mass")[[f]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-116) – or twice that of the Sun.[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Aitken-72) The average individual stellar masses are about 1.08 M☉ and 0.91 M☉, respectively,[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Akeson2021-6) though slightly different masses have also been quoted in recent years, such as 1.14 M☉ and 0.92 M☉,[[87]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-RECONS-91) totaling 2.06 M☉. Alpha Centauri A and B have [absolute magnitudes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_magnitude "Absolute magnitude") of +4.38 and +5.71, respectively.

#### Alpha Centauri AB System

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Alpha_Centauri&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Alpha Centauri AB System")]

[![Image 14: Two white disks side by side, each with coloured fringes and prominent diffraction spikes](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Best_image_of_Alpha_Centauri_A_and_B.jpg/250px-Best_image_of_Alpha_Centauri_A_and_B.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Best_image_of_Alpha_Centauri_A_and_B.jpg)

α Centauri A (left) is of the same [stellar type](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_classification "Stellar classification")G2 as the Sun, while α Centauri B (right) is a K1-type star.[[111]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-117)

**Alpha Centauri A**, also known as **Rigil Kentaurus**, is the principal member, or primary, of the binary system. It is a solar-like [main-sequence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Main_sequence "Main sequence") star with a similar yellowish colour,[[112]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-csiro-118) whose [stellar classification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_classification "Stellar classification") is [spectral type](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectral_type "Spectral type") G2-V;[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-torres2006-4) it is about 10% more massive than the Sun,[[105]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Thevenin02-110) with a radius about 22% larger.[[113]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-kervella2017-119) When considered among the individual [brightest stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_brightest_stars "List of brightest stars") in the night sky, it is the fourth-brightest at an apparent magnitude of +0.01,[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-ducati-3) being slightly fainter than [Arcturus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arcturus "Arcturus") at an [apparent magnitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apparent_magnitude "Apparent magnitude") of −0.05.

The type of [magnetic activity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_activity "Magnetic activity") on Alpha Centauri A is comparable to that of the Sun, showing [coronal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_corona "Stellar corona") variability due to [star spots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_spot "Star spot"), as modulated by the rotation of the star. However, since 2005 the activity level has fallen into a deep minimum that might be similar to the Sun's historical [Maunder Minimum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maunder_Minimum "Maunder Minimum"). Alternatively, it may have a very long stellar activity cycle and is slowly recovering from a minimum phase.[[114]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Ayres2014-120)

**Alpha Centauri B**, also known as **Toliman**, is the secondary star of the binary system. It is a main-sequence star of spectral type K1-V, making it more an orange colour than Alpha Centauri A;[[112]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-csiro-118) it has around 90% of the mass of the Sun and a 14% smaller diameter. Although it has a lower luminosity than A, Alpha Centauri B emits more energy in the [X-ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray "X-ray") band.[[115]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Xrays-121) Its [light curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_curve "Light curve") varies on a short time scale, and there has been at least one observed [flare](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flare_star "Flare star").[[115]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Xrays-121) It is more magnetically active than Alpha Centauri A, showing a cycle of 8.2±0.2 yr compared to 11 years for the Sun, and has about half the minimum-to-peak variation in coronal luminosity of the Sun.[[114]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Ayres2014-120) This cycle was recently re-estimated based on more than 20 years of high-resolution spectroscopic observations of the CaIIH&K lines showing a cycle of 7.8±0.2 yr.[[116]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Cretignier2024-122) Alpha Centauri B has an apparent magnitude of +1.35, slightly dimmer than [Mimosa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mimosa_(star) "Mimosa (star)").[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-IAU-LSN-48)

Alpha Centauri C, better known as Proxima Centauri, is a small main-sequence [red dwarf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_dwarf "Red dwarf") of spectral class M6-Ve. It has an [absolute magnitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_magnitude "Absolute magnitude") of +15.60, over 20,000 times fainter than the Sun. Its mass is calculated to be 0.1221 M☉.[[117]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-KervellaTh%C3%A9venin2017-123) It is the closest star to the Sun but is too faint to be visible to the naked eye.[[118]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-ESA-124)

[![Image 15: Very wide rectangle with a dot labelled "Sun" on the left and two dots respectively labelled "Alpha Centauri AB" and "Proxima Centauri" on the right side, joined by lines labelled with distances and angles](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/5f/Relative_positions_of_Sun%2C_Alpha_Centauri_AB_and_Proxima_Centauri.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Relative_positions_of_Sun,_Alpha_Centauri_AB_and_Proxima_Centauri.png)

Relative positions of Sun, Alpha Centauri AB and Proxima Centauri. Grey dot is projection of Proxima Centauri, located at the same distance as Alpha Centauri AB.

The Alpha Centauri system as a whole has two confirmed planets, both of them around Proxima Centauri. While other planets have been claimed to exist around all of the stars, none of the discoveries have been confirmed.

In 2021, a candidate planet named Candidate 1 (or C1) was detected around Alpha Centauri A, thought to orbit at approximately 1.1 AU with a period of about one year, and to have a mass between that of Neptune and one-half that of Saturn, though it may be a dust disk or an artefact. The possibility of C1 being a background star has been ruled out.[[119]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-125)[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-WagnerBoehle2021-21) If this candidate is confirmed, the temporary name C1 will most likely be replaced with the scientific designation Alpha Centauri Ab in accordance with current naming conventions.[[120]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-IAUNamingRules-126)

GO Cycle 1 observations are planned for the [James Webb Space Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Webb_Space_Telescope "James Webb Space Telescope") (JWST) to search for planets around Alpha Centauri A, as well as observations of [Epsilon Muscae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epsilon_Muscae "Epsilon Muscae").[[121]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-:0-127) The coronographic observations, which occurred on July 26 and 27, 2023, were failures, though there are follow-up observations in March 2024.[[122]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-128) Pre-launch estimates predicted that JWST will be able to find planets with a radius of 5 [R🜨](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth_radius "Earth radius") at 1–3 AU. Multiple observations every 3–6 months could push the limit down to 3 R🜨.[[123]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-129) Post-launch estimates based on observations of [HIP 65426 b](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HIP_65426_b "HIP 65426 b") find that JWST will be able to find planets even closer to Alpha Centauri A and could find a 5 R🜨 planet at 0.5–2.5 AU.[[124]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-130) Candidate 1 has an estimated radius between 3.3–11 R🜨[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-WagnerBoehle2021-21) and orbits at 1.1 AU.

Observations with the James Webb Space Telescope in August 2024 uncovered a point source which might be an exoplanet at a separation of 2 astronomical units, believed to be the same object detected in 2021. This object is confirmed to be not a background object, and is unlikely to be an instrumental artifact, and therefore might be an exoplanet. It was not recovered and needs additional observations to be confirmed as a planet; there is a 52% chance it was not re-observed due to the orbital motion.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Beichman2025-23)[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Aniket2025-22) If it is an exoplanet, it should have a mass between 90 and 150 [Earth masses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth_mass "Earth mass"), a radius between 1.0 and 1.1 R J and a temperature of 225 K (−48°C; −55°F).[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Beichman2025-23)

### Planets of Alpha Centauri B

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Alpha_Centauri&action=edit&section=18 "Edit section: Planets of Alpha Centauri B")]

The first claim of a planet around Alpha Centauri B was that of [Alpha Centauri Bb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri_Bb "Alpha Centauri Bb") in 2012, which was proposed to be an Earth-mass planet in a 3.2-day orbit.[[125]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Dumusque-131) This was refuted in 2015 when the apparent planet was shown to be an artefact of the way the [radial velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doppler_spectroscopy "Doppler spectroscopy") data was processed.[[126]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-132)[[127]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-133)[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Rajpaul2016-24)

A search for [transits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_transit "Astronomical transit") of planet Bb was conducted with the [Hubble Space Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope "Hubble Space Telescope") from 2013 to 2014. This search detected one potential transit-like event, which could be associated with a different planet with a radius around 0.92[R🜨](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth_radius "Earth radius"). This planet would most likely orbit Alpha Centauri B with an orbital period of 20.4 days or less, with only a 5% chance of it having a longer orbit. The median of the likely orbits is 12.4 days. Its orbit would likely have an eccentricity of 0.24 or less.[[128]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Demory2015-134) It could have lakes of molten lava and would be far too close to Alpha Centauri B to harbour [life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extraterrestrial_life "Extraterrestrial life").[[129]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-135) If confirmed, this planet might be called Alpha Centauri Bc. However, the name has not been used in the literature, as it is not a claimed discovery.

### Planets of Proxima Centauri

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Alpha_Centauri&action=edit&section=19 "Edit section: Planets of Proxima Centauri")]

[Proxima Centauri b](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proxima_Centauri_b "Proxima Centauri b") or Alpha Centauri Cb is a terrestrial planet discovered in 2016 by astronomers at the [European Southern Observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Southern_Observatory "European Southern Observatory") (ESO). It has an estimated [minimum mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minimum_mass "Minimum mass") of 1.17 [M🜨](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth_mass "Earth mass") ([Earth masses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth_mass "Earth mass")) and orbits approximately 0.049 [AU](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_unit "Astronomical unit") from Proxima Centauri, placing it in the star's [habitable zone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumstellar_habitable_zone "Circumstellar habitable zone").[[130]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-proxima_b_discovery_paper-136)[[131]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Mascareno2020-137)

The discovery of [Proxima Centauri c](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proxima_Centauri_c "Proxima Centauri c") was formally published in 2020 and could be a [super-Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Super-Earth "Super-Earth") or [mini-Neptune](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mini-Neptune "Mini-Neptune").[[132]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-proxima_c_scientific_american-138)[[133]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-proxima_c_discovery_paper-139) It has a mass of roughly 7 M🜨 and orbits about 1.49 AU from Proxima Centauri with a period of 1,928 days (5.28 yr).[[134]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-proxima_c_mass-140) In June 2020, a possible direct imaging detection of the planet hinted at the presence of a large ring system.[[135]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-proxima_c_imaging-141) However, a 2022 study disputed the existence of this planet.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Artigau2022-20) As of 2025, evidence for Proxima c remains inconclusive; observations with the [NIRPS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ESO_3.6_m_Telescope "ESO 3.6 m Telescope") spectrograph were unable to confirm it, but found hints of a lower-amplitude signal with a similar period.[[136]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Su%C3%A1rezMascare%C3%B1o2025-142)

A 2020 paper refining Proxima b's mass excludes the presence of extra companions with masses above 0.6 M🜨 at periods shorter than 50 days, but the authors detected a radial-velocity curve with a periodicity of 5.15 days, suggesting the presence of a planet with a mass of about 0.29 M🜨.[[131]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Mascareno2020-137) This planet, [Proxima Centauri d](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proxima_Centauri_d "Proxima Centauri d"), was detected in 2022[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Faria2022-19)[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Artigau2022-20) and later confirmed in 2025.[[136]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Su%C3%A1rezMascare%C3%B1o2025-142)

### Hypothetical planets

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Alpha_Centauri&action=edit&section=20 "Edit section: Hypothetical planets")]

Additional planets may exist in the Alpha Centauri system, either orbiting Alpha Centauri A or Alpha Centauri B individually, or in large orbits around Alpha Centauri AB. Because both stars are fairly similar to the Sun (for example, in age and [metallicity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metallicity "Metallicity")), astronomers have been especially interested in making detailed searches for planets in the Alpha Centauri system. Several established planet-hunting teams have used various [radial velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radial_velocity "Radial velocity") or star [transit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_transit "Astronomical transit") methods in their searches around these two bright stars.[[137]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-universetoday.com-143) All the observational studies have so far failed to find evidence for [brown dwarfs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brown_dwarf "Brown dwarf") or [gas giants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas_giant "Gas giant").[[137]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-universetoday.com-143)[[138]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-144)

In 2009, computer simulations showed that a planet might have been able to form near the inner edge of Alpha Centauri B's habitable zone, which extends from 0.5–0.9 AU from the star. Certain special assumptions, such as considering that the Alpha Centauri pair may have initially formed with a wider separation and later moved closer to each other (as might be possible if they formed in a dense [star cluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_cluster "Open cluster")), would permit an accretion-friendly environment farther from the star.[[139]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Thebault-2009-145) Bodies around Alpha Centauri A would be able to orbit at slightly farther distances due to its stronger gravity. In addition, the lack of any brown dwarfs or gas giants in close orbits around Alpha Centauri make the likelihood of terrestrial planets greater than otherwise.[[140]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-lackofany-146) A theoretical study indicates that a radial velocity analysis might detect a hypothetical planet of 1.8[M🜨](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth_mass "Earth mass") in Alpha Centauri B's [habitable zone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumstellar_habitable_zone "Circumstellar habitable zone").[[141]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-guedesetal2008-147)

Radial velocity measurements of Alpha Centauri B made with the [High Accuracy Radial Velocity Planet Searcher](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High_Accuracy_Radial_Velocity_Planet_Searcher "High Accuracy Radial Velocity Planet Searcher")[spectrograph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectrograph "Spectrograph") were sufficiently sensitive to detect a 4[M🜨](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth_mass "Earth mass") planet within the habitable zone of the star (i.e. with an orbital period P = 200 days), but no planets were detected.[[125]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Dumusque-131)

Current estimates place the probability of finding an Earth-like planet around Alpha Centauri at roughly 75%.[[142]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-miniature-148) The observational thresholds for planet detection in the habitable zones by the radial velocity method are currently (2017) estimated to be about 53 M🜨 for Alpha Centauri A, 8.4 M🜨 for Alpha Centauri B, and 0.47 M🜨 for [Proxima Centauri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proxima_Centauri "Proxima Centauri").[[143]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Zhao2018-149)

Early computer-generated models of planetary formation predicted the existence of [terrestrial planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terrestrial_planet "Terrestrial planet") around [both Alpha Centauri A and B](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumbinary_planet "Circumbinary planet"),[[141]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-guedesetal2008-147)[[144]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Quintna-Lissr-2007-150) but most recent numerical investigations have shown that the gravitational pull of the companion star renders the accretion of planets difficult.[[139]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Thebault-2009-145)[[145]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-151) Despite these difficulties, given the similarities to the Sun in [spectral types](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectral_type "Spectral type"), star type, age and probable stability of the orbits, it has been suggested that this stellar system could hold one of the best possibilities for harbouring [extraterrestrial life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extraterrestrial_life "Extraterrestrial life") on a potential planet.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Wiegert-7)[[140]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-lackofany-146)[[146]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-152)[[144]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Quintna-Lissr-2007-150)

In the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System"), it was once thought that [Jupiter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jupiter "Jupiter") and [Saturn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saturn "Saturn") were probably crucial in perturbing [comets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comet "Comet") into the inner Solar System, providing the inner planets with a source of water and various other ices.[[147]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Croswell-153) However, since isotope measurements of the [deuterium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deuterium "Deuterium") to [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen") (D/H) ratio in comets [Halley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halley%27s_Comet "Halley's Comet"), [Hyakutake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comet_Hyakutake "Comet Hyakutake"), [Hale–Bopp](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comet_Hale%E2%80%93Bopp "Comet Hale–Bopp"), 2002T7, and Tuttle yield values approximately twice that of Earth's oceanic water, more recent models and research predict that less than 10% of Earth's water was supplied from comets. In the α Centauri system, Proxima Centauri may have influenced the planetary disk as the α Centauri system was forming, enriching the area around Alpha Centauri with volatile materials.[[148]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-154) This would be discounted if, for example, α Centauri B happened to have [gas giants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas_giant "Gas giant") orbiting α Centauri A (or vice versa), or if α Centauri A and B themselves were able to perturb comets into each other's inner systems, as Jupiter and Saturn presumably have done in the Solar System.[[147]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Croswell-153) Such icy bodies probably also reside in [Oort clouds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud "Oort cloud") of other planetary systems. When they are influenced gravitationally by either the gas giants or disruptions by passing nearby stars, many of these icy bodies then travel star-wards.[[147]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Croswell-153) Such ideas also apply to the close approach of Alpha Centauri or other stars to the Solar system, when, in the distant future, the [Oort Cloud](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_Cloud "Oort Cloud") might be disrupted enough to increase the number of active comets.[[97]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Matthews-102)

To be in the [habitable zone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habitable_zone "Habitable zone"), a planet around Alpha Centauri A would have an orbital radius of between about 1.2 and 2.1[AU](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_unit "Astronomical unit") so as to have similar planetary temperatures and conditions for liquid water to exist.[[149]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Kaltenegger2013-155) For the slightly less luminous and cooler α Centauri B, the habitable zone is between about 0.7 and 1.2 AU.[[149]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Kaltenegger2013-155)

With the goal of finding evidence of such planets, both Proxima Centauri and α Centauri AB were among the listed "Tier-1" target stars for [NASA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NASA "NASA")'s [Space Interferometry Mission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_Interferometry_Mission "Space Interferometry Mission") (S.I.M.). Detecting planets as small as three Earth-masses or smaller within two AU of a "Tier-1" target would have been possible with this new instrument.[[150]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-numbers-156) The S.I.M. mission, however, was cancelled due to financial issues in 2010.[[151]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-157)

### Circumstellar discs

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Alpha_Centauri&action=edit&section=21 "Edit section: Circumstellar discs")]

Based on observations between 2007 and 2012, a study found a slight excess of emissions in the 24[μm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Micrometre "Micrometre") (mid/far-infrared) band surrounding α Centauri AB, which may be interpreted as evidence for a sparse [circumstellar disc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumstellar_disc "Circumstellar disc") or dense [interplanetary dust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_dust_cloud "Interplanetary dust cloud").[[152]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Wiegart2014-158) The total mass was estimated to be between 10−7 to 10−6 the mass of the [Moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moon "Moon"), or 10–100 times the mass of the Solar System's [zodiacal cloud](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zodiacal_cloud "Zodiacal cloud").[[152]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Wiegart2014-158) If such a disc existed around both stars, α Centauri A's disc would likely be stable to 2.8 AU, and α Centauri B's would likely be stable to 2.5 AU [[152]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Wiegart2014-158) This would put A's disc entirely within the [frost line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frost_line_(astrophysics) "Frost line (astrophysics)"), and a small part of B's outer disc just outside.[[152]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Wiegart2014-158)

Interaction with surrounding
----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Alpha_Centauri&action=edit&section=22 "Edit section: Interaction with surrounding")]

There have been studies that there is a considerable amount of material exchanged with the far regions of the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System"),[[153]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-y745-159) resulting in possibly material coming from Alpha Centauri going also deeper into the Solar System. If proven the galaxy could form an interconnected network of larger material.[[154]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-l238-160)

View from this system
---------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Alpha_Centauri&action=edit&section=23 "Edit section: View from this system")]

[![Image 17: Simulated night-sky image centred on Orion labelled with constellation names in red and star names in yellow, including Sirius very close to Betelgeuse and the Sun near Cassiopeia.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/6d/Sky-from-alpha-centauri.jpg/330px-Sky-from-alpha-centauri.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Sky-from-alpha-centauri.jpg)

Looking towards the sky around [Orion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(constellation) "Orion (constellation)") from Alpha Centauri with [Sirius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sirius "Sirius") near [Betelgeuse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Betelgeuse "Betelgeuse"), [Procyon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Procyon "Procyon") in [Gemini](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gemini_(constellation) "Gemini (constellation)"), and the Sun in [Cassiopeia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cassiopeia_(constellation) "Cassiopeia (constellation)") generated by [Celestia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celestia "Celestia")

[![Image 18](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Sun_from_Alpha_Centauri.png/330px-Sun_from_Alpha_Centauri.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Sun_from_Alpha_Centauri.png)

Simulated night-sky image with a "W" of stars from [Cassiopeia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cassiopeia_(constellation) "Cassiopeia (constellation)") connected by lines, and the Sun, labeled "Sol", as it would appear to the left of the "W"

The sky from α Centauri AB would appear much as it does from the Earth, except that [Centaurus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centaurus "Centaurus")'s brightest star, being α Centauri AB itself, would be absent from the constellation. The Sun would appear as a white star of [apparent magnitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apparent_magnitude "Apparent magnitude") +0.5,[[155]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-161) roughly the same as the average brightness of [Betelgeuse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Betelgeuse "Betelgeuse") from Earth. It would be at the [antipodal point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antipodal_point "Antipodal point") of α Centauri AB's current [right ascension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_ascension "Right ascension") and [declination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Declination "Declination"), at 02 h 39 m 36 s +60°50′02.308″ (2000), in eastern [Cassiopeia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cassiopeia_(constellation) "Cassiopeia (constellation)"), easily outshining all the rest of the stars in the [constellation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constellation "Constellation"). With the placement of the Sun east of the magnitude 3.4 star [Epsilon Cassiopeiae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epsilon_Cassiopeiae "Epsilon Cassiopeiae"), nearly in front of the [Heart Nebula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heart_Nebula "Heart Nebula"), the "W" line of stars of Cassiopeia would have a "/W" shape.[[156]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-162)

Other nearby stars' placements may be affected somewhat drastically. [Sirius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sirius "Sirius"), at 9.2 light years away from the system, would still be the brightest star in the night sky, with a magnitude of -1.2, but would be located in [Orion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(constellation) "Orion (constellation)") less than a degree away from Betelgeuse. [Procyon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Procyon "Procyon"), which would also be at a slightly further distance than from the Sun, would move to outshine [Pollux](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pollux_(star) "Pollux (star)") in the middle of [Gemini](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gemini_(constellation) "Gemini (constellation)").

A planet around either α Centauri A or B would see the other star as a very bright secondary. For example, an Earth-like planet at 1.25 AU  from α Cen A (with a revolution period of 1.34 years) would get Sun-like illumination from its primary, and α Cen B would appear 5.7–8.6 magnitudes dimmer (−21.0 to −18.2), 190–2,700 times dimmer than α Cen A but still 150–2,100 times brighter than the full Moon. Conversely, an Earth-like planet at 0.71 AU  from α Cen B (with a revolution period of 0.63 years) would get nearly Sun-like illumination from its primary, and α Cen A would appear 4.6–7.3 magnitudes dimmer (−22.1 to −19.4), 70 to 840 times dimmer than α Cen B but still 470–5,700 times brighter than the full Moon.

Proxima Centauri would appear dim as one of many stars, being magnitude 4.5 at its current distance, and magnitude 2.6 at periastron.[[157]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-163)

[![Image 19: Series of partial circles centred on a small yellow disk labelled "Sun", each circle labelled with a distance, and several other small disks labelled with the names of stars](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/PIA18003-NASA-WISE-StarsNearSun-20140425-2_correction.png/330px-PIA18003-NASA-WISE-StarsNearSun-20140425-2_correction.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:PIA18003-NASA-WISE-StarsNearSun-20140425-2_correction.png)

Diagram of the [closest stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_nearest_stars "List of nearest stars") to the Sun, within 7.5 light years

Alpha Centauri is a first target for crewed or robotic [interstellar exploration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_travel "Interstellar travel"). Using current spacecraft technologies, crossing the distance between the Sun and Alpha Centauri would take several millennia, though the possibility of [nuclear pulse propulsion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_pulse_propulsion "Nuclear pulse propulsion") or laser [light sail](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_sail "Light sail") technology, as considered in the [Breakthrough Starshot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Breakthrough_Starshot "Breakthrough Starshot") program, could make the journey to Alpha Centauri in 20 years.[[158]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-NYT-20160412-db-164)[[159]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-165)[[160]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-166) An objective of such a mission would be to make a fly-by of, and possibly photograph, planets that might exist in the system.[[161]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-starshot-167)[[162]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-nytimes20160412-168) The existence of [Proxima Centauri b](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proxima_Centauri_b "Proxima Centauri b"), announced by the [European Southern Observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Southern_Observatory "European Southern Observatory") (ESO) in August 2016, would be a target for the Starshot program.[[161]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-starshot-167)[[163]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-nytimes20160824-169)

[NASA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NASA "NASA") released a mission concept in 2017 that would [send a spacecraft to Alpha Centauri in 2069](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2069_Alpha_Centauri_mission "2069 Alpha Centauri mission"), scheduled to coincide with the 100th anniversary of the first crewed lunar landing in 1969, [Apollo 11](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apollo_11 "Apollo 11"). Even at 10% of the [speed of light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light "Speed of light") (about 108 million km/h), which NASA experts say may be possible, it would take a spacecraft 44 years to reach the system, by the year 2113, and would take another 4 years for a signal, by the year 2117 to reach Earth. The concept received no further funding or development.[[164]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-170)[[165]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-171)

Alpha Centauri has been recognized and associated throughout history, particularly in the [Southern Hemisphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere "Southern Hemisphere"). [Polynesians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynesians "Polynesians") have been using Alpha Centauri for their [star navigation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynesian_navigation "Polynesian navigation") and have called it Kamailehope. In the [Ngarrindjeri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ngarrindjeri "Ngarrindjeri") culture of Australia, Alpha Centauri represents with [Beta Centauri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beta_Centauri "Beta Centauri") two sharks chasing a [stingray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stingray "Stingray"), the [Southern Cross](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southern_Cross "Southern Cross"), and in [Incan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inca "Inca") culture it with Beta Centauri form the eyes of a [llama](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Llama "Llama")-shaped [dark constellation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constellation#Dark_cloud_constellations "Constellation") embedded in the band of stars that the visible [Milky Way](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milky_Way "Milky Way") forms in the sky. In ancient Egypt it was also revered and in China it is known as part of the South Gate asterism.[[166]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-172)

The [Sagan Planet Walk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sagan_Planet_Walk "Sagan Planet Walk") in [Ithaca, New York](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ithaca,_New_York "Ithaca, New York"), is a walkable scale model of the solar system. An obelisk representing the scaled position of Alpha Centauri has been added at [ʻImiloa Astronomy Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%CA%BBImiloa_Astronomy_Center "ʻImiloa Astronomy Center") in Hawaii.[[167]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri#cite_note-Couillard-173)
