In [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics"), a **parity transformation** (also called **parity inversion**) is the flip in the sign of _one_[spatial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_space "Three-dimensional space")[coordinate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate "Coordinate"). In three dimensions, it can also refer to the simultaneous flip in the sign of all three spatial coordinates (a [point reflection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_reflection "Point reflection") or point inversion):

![Image 1: {\displaystyle \mathbf {P} :{\begin{pmatrix}x\\y\\z\end{pmatrix}}\mapsto {\begin{pmatrix}-x\\-y\\-z\end{pmatrix}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cc6c9d164a1297b4ae2d4099e9a7b05f69157bb4)

It can also be thought of as a test for [chirality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chirality_(physics) "Chirality (physics)") of a physical phenomenon, in that a parity inversion transforms a phenomenon into its mirror image.

All fundamental interactions of [elementary particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementary_particle "Elementary particle"), with the exception of the [weak interaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weak_interaction "Weak interaction"), are symmetric under parity transformation. As established by the [Wu experiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wu_experiment "Wu experiment") conducted at the US [National Bureau of Standards](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Bureau_of_Standards "National Bureau of Standards") by Chinese-American scientist [Chien-Shiung Wu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chien-Shiung_Wu "Chien-Shiung Wu"), the weak interaction is chiral and thus provides a means for probing chirality in physics. In her experiment, Wu took advantage of the controlling role of weak interactions in [radioactive decay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radioactive_decay "Radioactive decay") of atomic isotopes to establish the chirality of the weak force.

By contrast, in interactions that are symmetric under parity, such as electromagnetism in atomic and molecular physics, parity serves as a powerful controlling principle underlying quantum transitions.

A matrix representation of **P** (in any number of dimensions) has [determinant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Determinant "Determinant") equal to −1, and hence is distinct from a [rotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation "Rotation"), which has a determinant equal to 1. In a two-dimensional plane, a simultaneous flip of all coordinates in sign is _not_ a parity transformation; it is the same as a [180° rotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/180%C2%B0_rotation "180° rotation").

In quantum mechanics, wave functions that are unchanged by a parity transformation are described as [even](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Even_and_odd_functions "Even and odd functions") functions, while those that change sign under a parity transformation are odd functions.

Simple symmetry relations
-------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Parity_(physics)&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Simple symmetry relations")]

Under [rotations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation "Rotation"), classical geometrical objects can be classified into [scalars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_(physics) "Scalar (physics)"), [vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_vector "Euclidean vector"), and [tensors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor "Tensor") of higher rank. In [classical physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_physics "Classical physics"), physical configurations need to transform under [representations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_representation "Group representation") of every symmetry group.

[Quantum theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics") predicts that states in a [Hilbert space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space "Hilbert space") do not need to transform under representations of the [group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_(mathematics) "Group (mathematics)") of rotations, but only under [projective representations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_representation "Projective representation"). The word _projective_ refers to the fact that if one projects out the phase of each state, where we recall that the overall phase of a quantum state is not observable, then a projective representation reduces to an ordinary representation. All representations are also projective representations, but the converse is not true, therefore the projective representation condition on quantum states is weaker than the representation condition on classical states.

The projective representations of any group are isomorphic to the ordinary representations of a [central extension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_extension#Central_extension "Group extension") of the group. For example, projective representations of the 3-dimensional rotation group, which is the [special orthogonal group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_orthogonal_group "Special orthogonal group") SO(3), are ordinary representations of the [special unitary group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_unitary_group "Special unitary group") SU(2). Projective representations of the rotation group that are not representations are called [spinors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spinor "Spinor") and so quantum states may transform not only as tensors but also as spinors.

If one adds to this a classification by parity, these can be extended, for example, into notions of

*   _scalars_ (_P_ = +1) and _[pseudoscalars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudoscalar\_(physics) "Pseudoscalar (physics)")_ (_P_ = −1) which are rotationally invariant.
*   _vectors_ (_P_ = −1) and _axial vectors_ (also called _[pseudovectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudovector "Pseudovector")_) (_P_ = +1) which both transform as vectors under rotation.

One can define **reflections** such as

![Image 2: {\displaystyle V_{x}:{\begin{pmatrix}x\\y\\z\end{pmatrix}}\mapsto {\begin{pmatrix}-x\\y\\z\end{pmatrix}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/96851c74aa45f3ea62721b47e0d7adeeb93023a2)

which also have negative determinant and form a valid parity transformation. Then, combining them with rotations (or successively performing _x_-, _y_-, and _z_-reflections) one can recover the particular parity transformation defined earlier. The first parity transformation given does not work in an even number of dimensions, though, because it results in a positive determinant. In even dimensions only the latter example of a parity transformation (or any reflection of an odd number of coordinates) can be used.

Parity forms the [abelian group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abelian_group "Abelian group")![Image 3: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} _{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/92aedfb5c02eff978ab963421ce930f46801657e) due to the relation ![Image 4: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathcal {P}}}^{2}={\hat {1}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0adcc18eb7e652d8e74111772995cdf2ee948043). All Abelian groups have only one-dimensional [irreducible representations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irreducible_representations "Irreducible representations"). For ![Image 5: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} _{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/92aedfb5c02eff978ab963421ce930f46801657e), there are two irreducible representations: one is even under parity, ![Image 6: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathcal {P}}}\phi =+\phi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1fc0dbf904ced0507a602498239add379bd09452), the other is odd, ![Image 7: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathcal {P}}}\phi =-\phi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2351a72e8cd3cf053d5bc5e6b6d03a5b5f95b530). These are useful in quantum mechanics. However, as is elaborated below, in quantum mechanics states need not transform under actual representations of parity but only under projective representations and so in principle a parity transformation may rotate a state by any [phase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phase_(waves) "Phase (waves)").

### Representations of O(3)

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Parity_(physics)&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Representations of O(3)")]

*   scalars: ![Image 10: {\displaystyle \rho (R)=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/17c0a114f085a6fdb7e17d8c1e057b14265d6e68), the trivial representation
*   pseudoscalars: ![Image 11: {\displaystyle \rho (R)=\det(R)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9fae256c99c318b2c2c11f1446fd08556d098277)
*   vectors: ![Image 12: {\displaystyle \rho (R)=R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1266067697755beba03723709e27db797e87ffb4), the fundamental representation
*   pseudovectors: ![Image 13: {\displaystyle \rho (R)=\det(R)R.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/31cb9742f9d06174b6251d2c9ea863e541d3b322)

When the representation is restricted to ![Image 14: {\displaystyle {\text{SO}}(3)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/76ea569dfd0b7e75a686925ff43ea07e50e79526), scalars and pseudoscalars transform identically, as do vectors and pseudovectors.

Classical mechanics
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Parity_(physics)&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Classical mechanics")]

Newton's equation of motion ![Image 15: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} =m\mathbf {a} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d0c60fab89e8c3193952047dc565bcf8d233d115) (if the mass is constant) equates two vectors, and hence is invariant under parity. The law of gravity also involves only vectors and is also, therefore, invariant under parity.

However, angular momentum ![Image 16: {\displaystyle \mathbf {L} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6f5f750865376a1a4ae2b15a00b4ff9c75a66630) is an [axial vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axial_vector "Axial vector"), ![Image 17: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\mathbf {L} &=\mathbf {r} \times \mathbf {p} \\{\hat {P}}\left(\mathbf {L} \right)&=(-\mathbf {r} )\times (-\mathbf {p} )=\mathbf {L} .\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d93cb468289b4afff774b1d61f285817a168801b)

In classical [electrodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrodynamics "Electrodynamics"), the charge density ![Image 18: {\displaystyle \rho }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1f7d439671d1289b6a816e6af7a304be40608d64) is a scalar, the electric field, ![Image 19: {\displaystyle \mathbf {E} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d7f22b39d51f780fc02859059c1757c606b9de2), and current ![Image 20: {\displaystyle \mathbf {j} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ca5d874dbb32b8b33e83ca521f592808387a486e) are vectors, but the magnetic field, ![Image 21: {\displaystyle \mathbf {B} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cafb0ef39b0f5ffa23c170aa7f7b4e718327c4d1) is an axial vector. However, [Maxwell's equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxwell%27s_equations "Maxwell's equations") are invariant under parity because the [curl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curl_(mathematics) "Curl (mathematics)") of an axial vector is a vector.

Effect of spatial inversion on some variables of classical physics
------------------------------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Parity_(physics)&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Effect of spatial inversion on some variables of classical physics")]

The two major divisions of classical physical variables have either even or odd parity. The way into which particular variables and vectors sort out into either category depends on whether the _number of dimensions_ of space is either an odd or even number. The categories of _odd_ or _even_ given below for the _parity transformation_ is a different, but intimately related issue.

The answers given below are correct for 3 spatial dimensions. In a 2 dimensional space, for example, when constrained to remain on the surface of a planet, some of the variables switch sides.

### Possible eigenvalues

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Parity_(physics)&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Possible eigenvalues")]

[![Image 47](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Parity_1drep.png/250px-Parity_1drep.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Parity_1drep.png)

Two dimensional representations of parity are given by a pair of quantum states which go into each other under parity. However, this representation can always be reduced to linear combinations of states, each of which is either even or odd under parity. One says that all [irreducible representations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irreducible_representation "Irreducible representation") of parity are one-dimensional.

In quantum mechanics, spacetime transformations act on [quantum states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_states "Quantum states"). The parity transformation, ![Image 48: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathcal {P}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/908ff566c96e1f825f7350cd046f715a68218356), is a [unitary operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unitary_operator "Unitary operator"), in general acting on a state ![Image 49: {\displaystyle \psi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/45e5789e5d9c8f7c79744f43ecaaf8ba42a8553a) as follows: ![Image 50: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathcal {P}}}\,\psi {\left(r\right)}=e^{{i\phi }/{2}}\psi {\left(-r\right)}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2a23edcd47d7ddf1fab34e5f6c1554bb0cc63d45).

One must then have ![Image 51: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathcal {P}}}^{2}\,\psi {\left(r\right)}=e^{i\phi }\psi {\left(r\right)}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81ae29df0d7dc35e6cf172a355b0a7e5959df4d7), since an overall phase is unobservable. The operator ![Image 52: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathcal {P}}}^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e17eb77035ca391238c81bc97f177a2cf7903592), which reverses the parity of a state twice, leaves the spacetime invariant, and so is an internal symmetry which rotates its eigenstates by phases ![Image 53: {\displaystyle e^{i\phi }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/05c817052d719f5b21dd162c425e514232d14af6). If ![Image 54: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathcal {P}}}^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e17eb77035ca391238c81bc97f177a2cf7903592) is an element ![Image 55: {\displaystyle e^{iQ}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b5aeb6948a4c3ae87215daf3198cf09b7141912) of a continuous U(1) symmetry group of phase rotations, then ![Image 56: {\displaystyle e^{-iQ}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5fc5a698eef8426d1f2c945d3e3e5f2959fcabbf) is part of this U(1) and so is also a symmetry. In particular, we can define ![Image 57: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathcal {P}}}'\equiv {\hat {\mathcal {P}}}\,e^{-{iQ}/{2}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a78214ea19040051044de102c85120a912136112), which is also a symmetry, and so we can choose to call ![Image 58: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathcal {P}}}'}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9aa95d8ee6e30bf7ce60c3ba9c365be930c92584) our parity operator, instead of ![Image 59: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathcal {P}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/908ff566c96e1f825f7350cd046f715a68218356). Note that ![Image 60: {\displaystyle {{\hat {\mathcal {P}}}'}^{2}=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/433be2f077e9c0fdc8cfcae5a506e79a06341b8f) and so ![Image 61: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathcal {P}}}'}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9aa95d8ee6e30bf7ce60c3ba9c365be930c92584) has eigenvalues ![Image 62: {\displaystyle \pm 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0bfeaa85da53ad1947d8000926cfea33827ef1e0). Wave functions with eigenvalue ![Image 63: {\displaystyle +1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d04cf05c67d41d9f39dabf6a90722ce860a76958) under a parity transformation are [even functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Even_and_odd_functions "Even and odd functions"), while eigenvalue ![Image 64: {\displaystyle -1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/704fb0427140d054dd267925495e78164fee9aac) corresponds to odd functions.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parity_(physics)#cite_note-2) However, when no such symmetry group exists, it may be that all parity transformations have some eigenvalues which are phases other than ![Image 65: {\displaystyle \pm 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0bfeaa85da53ad1947d8000926cfea33827ef1e0).

For electronic wavefunctions, even states are usually indicated by a subscript g for _gerade_ (German: even) and odd states by a subscript u for _ungerade_ (German: odd). For example, the lowest energy level of the hydrogen molecule ion (H 2+) is labelled ![Image 66: {\displaystyle 1\sigma _{g}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d39f6370152c072040f371907381d0699540a4e8) and the next-closest (higher) energy level is labelled ![Image 67: {\displaystyle 1\sigma _{u}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/78d303cb25b98248a95e6bfca4d52dff2119e074).[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parity_(physics)#cite_note-3)

The wave functions of a particle moving into an external potential, which is [centrosymmetric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centrosymmetry "Centrosymmetry") (potential energy invariant with respect to a space inversion, symmetric to the origin), either remain invariable or change signs: these two possible states are called the even state or odd state of the wave functions.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parity_(physics)#cite_note-Andrew,_chapter_2-4)

The law of conservation of parity of particles states that, if an isolated ensemble of particles has a definite parity, then the parity remains invariable in the process of ensemble evolution. However this is not true for the [beta decay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beta_decay "Beta decay") of nuclei, because the [weak nuclear interaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weak_interaction#Violation_of_symmetry "Weak interaction") violates parity.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parity_(physics)#cite_note-5)

The parity of the states of a particle moving in a spherically symmetric external field is determined by the [angular momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum_operator "Angular momentum operator"), and the particle state is defined by three quantum numbers: total energy, angular momentum and the projection of angular momentum.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parity_(physics)#cite_note-Andrew,_chapter_2-4)

### Consequences of parity symmetry

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Parity_(physics)&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Consequences of parity symmetry")]

When parity generates the [Abelian group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abelian_group "Abelian group")![Image 68: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} _{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/92aedfb5c02eff978ab963421ce930f46801657e), one can always take linear combinations of quantum states such that they are either even or odd under parity (see the figure). Thus the parity of such states is ±1. The parity of a multiparticle state is the product of the parities of each state; in other words parity is a multiplicative quantum number.

In quantum mechanics, [Hamiltonians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamiltonian_(quantum_mechanics) "Hamiltonian (quantum mechanics)") are [invariant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invariant_(physics) "Invariant (physics)") (symmetric) under a parity transformation if ![Image 69: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathcal {P}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/908ff566c96e1f825f7350cd046f715a68218356)[commutes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutator "Commutator") with the Hamiltonian. In non-relativistic quantum mechanics, this happens for any scalar potential, i.e., ![Image 70: {\displaystyle V=V{\left(r\right)}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e8a4aaeadbd941c6398c4d544b0a7be82c9dd758), hence the potential is spherically symmetric. The following facts can be easily proven:

Some of the non-degenerate eigenfunctions of ![Image 71: {\displaystyle {\hat {H}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6bb06de5217295d7fbdbf68fb9c5309a513fc99e) are unaffected (invariant) by parity ![Image 72: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathcal {P}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/908ff566c96e1f825f7350cd046f715a68218356) and the others are merely reversed in sign when the Hamiltonian operator and the parity operator commute: ![Image 73: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathcal {P}}}|\psi \rangle =c\left|\psi \right\rangle ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e6f29484b36ed462c42b5847eaa39719ccd4ca69)

where ![Image 74: {\displaystyle c}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/86a67b81c2de995bd608d5b2df50cd8cd7d92455) is a constant, the [eigenvalue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eigenvalue "Eigenvalue") of ![Image 75: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathcal {P}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/908ff566c96e1f825f7350cd046f715a68218356), ![Image 76: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathcal {P}}}^{2}\left|\psi \right\rangle =c\,{\hat {\mathcal {P}}}\left|\psi \right\rangle .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/32accb93d1722850a0f2a4b4178b348c67180a42)

Many-particle systems: atoms, molecules, nuclei
-----------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Parity_(physics)&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Many-particle systems: atoms, molecules, nuclei")]

The overall parity of a many-particle system is the product of the parities of the one-particle states. It is −1 if an odd number of particles are in odd-parity states, and +1 otherwise. Different notations are in use to denote the parity of nuclei, atoms, and molecules.

[Atomic orbitals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_orbital "Atomic orbital") have parity (−1)_ℓ_, where the exponent ℓ is the [azimuthal quantum number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Azimuthal_quantum_number "Azimuthal quantum number"). The parity is odd for orbitals p, f, ... with ℓ = 1, 3, ..., and an atomic state has odd parity if an odd number of electrons occupy these orbitals. For example, the ground state of the nitrogen atom has the electron configuration 1s 2 2s 2 2p 3, and is identified by the term symbol 4 S o, where the superscript o denotes odd parity. However the third excited term at about 83,300 cm−1 above the ground state has electron configuration 1s 2 2s 2 2p 2 3s has even parity since there are only two 2p electrons, and its term symbol is 4 P (without an o superscript).[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parity_(physics)#cite_note-NIST-7)

The complete (rotational-vibrational-electronic-nuclear spin) electromagnetic Hamiltonian of any molecule commutes with (or is invariant to) the parity operation P (or E*, in the notation introduced by [Longuet-Higgins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christopher_Longuet-Higgins "Christopher Longuet-Higgins")[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parity_(physics)#cite_note-Longuet-Higgins1963-8)) and its eigenvalues can be given the parity symmetry label _**+**_ or _**−**_ as they are even or odd, respectively. The parity operation involves the inversion of electronic and nuclear spatial coordinates at the molecular center of mass.

Centrosymmetric molecules at equilibrium have a centre of symmetry at their midpoint (the nuclear center of mass). This includes all homonuclear [diatomic molecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diatomic_molecule "Diatomic molecule") as well as certain symmetric molecules such as [ethylene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene "Ethylene"), [benzene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene "Benzene"), [xenon tetrafluoride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xenon_tetrafluoride "Xenon tetrafluoride") and [sulphur hexafluoride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulphur_hexafluoride "Sulphur hexafluoride"). For centrosymmetric molecules, the point group contains the operation _**i**_ which is not to be confused with the parity operation. The operation _**i**_ involves the inversion of the electronic and vibrational displacement coordinates at the nuclear centre of mass. For centrosymmetric molecules the operation _**i**_ commutes with the rovibronic (rotation-vibration-electronic) Hamiltonian and can be used to label such states. Electronic and vibrational states of centrosymmetric molecules are either unchanged by the operation _**i**_, or they are changed in sign by _**i**_. The former are denoted by the subscript _**g**_ and are called _**gerade,**_ while the latter are denoted by the subscript _**u**_ and are called _**ungerade.**_ The complete electromagnetic Hamiltonian of a centrosymmetric molecule does not commute with the point group inversion operation _**i**_ because of the effect of the nuclear hyperfine Hamiltonian. The nuclear hyperfine Hamiltonian can mix the rotational levels of _**g**_ and _**u**_ vibronic states (called _ortho-para_ mixing) and give rise to _ortho_-_para_ transitions[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parity_(physics)#cite_note-9)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parity_(physics)#cite_note-Critchley2001-10)

In atomic nuclei, the state of each nucleon (proton or neutron) has even or odd parity, and nucleon configurations can be predicted using the [nuclear shell model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_shell_model "Nuclear shell model"). As for electrons in atoms, the nucleon state has odd overall parity if and only if the number of nucleons in odd-parity states is odd. The parity is usually written as a + (even) or − (odd) following the nuclear spin value. For example, the [oxygen isotope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotopes_of_oxygen "Isotopes of oxygen")17 O(5/2+) has spin 5/2 and even parity. The shell model explains this because the first 16 nucleons are paired so that each pair has spin zero and even parity, and the last nucleon is in the 1d 5/2 shell, which has even parity since ℓ = 2 for a d orbital.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parity_(physics)#cite_note-11)

Quantum field theory
--------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Parity_(physics)&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Quantum field theory")]

If one can show that the [vacuum state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vacuum_state "Vacuum state") is invariant under parity, ![Image 77: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathcal {P}}}\left|0\right\rangle =\left|0\right\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4971c7fd3f4f6d7e84e08fec0014528c3950d410), the Hamiltonian is parity invariant ![Image 78: {\displaystyle \left[{\hat {H}},{\hat {\mathcal {P}}}\right]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f7fa3c1b93528a6c270c12b65845f439a7bebca2) and the quantization conditions remain unchanged under parity, then it follows that every state has [good](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Good_quantum_number "Good quantum number") parity, and this parity is conserved in any reaction.

To show that [quantum electrodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_electrodynamics "Quantum electrodynamics") is invariant under parity, we have to prove that the action is invariant and the quantization is also invariant. For simplicity we will assume that [canonical quantization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canonical_quantization "Canonical quantization") is used; the vacuum state is then invariant under parity by construction. The invariance of the action follows from the classical invariance of Maxwell's equations. The invariance of the canonical quantization procedure can be worked out, and turns out to depend on the transformation of the annihilation operator:[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]![Image 79: {\displaystyle \mathbf {Pa} (\mathbf {p} ,\pm )\mathbf {P} ^{+}=\mathbf {a} (-\mathbf {p} ,\pm )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c7b0e358c78baa816de56da84bbaacf174afd139) where ![Image 80: {\displaystyle \mathbf {p} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dd73e3862cb92b016721b8c492eadb4e8a577527) denotes the momentum of a photon and ![Image 81: {\displaystyle \pm }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/869e366caf596564de4de06cb0ba124056d4064b) refers to its polarization state. This is equivalent to the statement that the photon has odd [intrinsic parity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intrinsic_parity "Intrinsic parity"). Similarly all [vector bosons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_boson "Vector boson") can be shown to have odd intrinsic parity, and all [axial-vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudovector_meson "Pseudovector meson") to have even intrinsic parity.

A straightforward extension of these arguments to scalar field theories shows that scalars have even parity. That is, ![Image 82: {\displaystyle {\mathsf {P}}\phi (-\mathbf {x} ,t){\mathsf {P}}^{-1}=\phi (\mathbf {x} ,t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf656ec2c3af2a348e7ce6318cdedb9cb305ebc4), since ![Image 83: {\displaystyle \mathbf {Pa} (\mathbf {p} )\mathbf {P} ^{+}=\mathbf {a} (-\mathbf {p} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/60989e0fd5799dc1c8d1e0f7fd022c001a98f0f2) This is true even for a complex scalar field. (Details of [spinors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spinor "Spinor") are dealt with in the article on the [Dirac equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dirac_equation "Dirac equation"), where it is shown that [fermions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermion "Fermion") and antifermions have opposite intrinsic parity.)

With [fermions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermion "Fermion"), there is a slight complication because there is more than one [spin group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_group "Spin group").

Parity in the Standard Model
----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Parity_(physics)&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: Parity in the Standard Model")]

### Fixing the global symmetries

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Parity_(physics)&action=edit&section=16 "Edit section: Fixing the global symmetries")]

Applying the parity operator twice leaves the coordinates unchanged, meaning that _P_ 2 must act as one of the internal symmetries of the theory, at most changing the phase of a state.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parity_(physics)#cite_note-12) For example, the [Standard Model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model "Standard Model") has three global [U(1)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle_group "Circle group") symmetries with charges equal to the [baryon number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baryon_number "Baryon number")_B_, the [lepton number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lepton_number "Lepton number")_L_, and the [electric charge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_charge "Electric charge")_Q_. Therefore, the parity operator satisfies _P_ 2 = _e_ _iαB_+_iβL_+_iγQ_ for some choice of α, β, and γ. This operator is also not unique in that a new parity operator P' can always be constructed by multiplying it by an internal symmetry such as P' = P _e_ _iαB_ for some _α_.

To see if the parity operator can always be defined to satisfy P 2 = 1, consider the general case when P 2 = Q for some internal symmetry  Q present in the theory. The desired parity operator would be P' = P Q−1/2. If Q is part of a continuous symmetry group then Q−1/2 exists, but if it is part of a [discrete symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discrete_symmetry "Discrete symmetry") then this element need not exist and such a redefinition may not be possible.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parity_(physics)#cite_note-13)

The Standard Model exhibits a (−1)_F_ symmetry, where _F_ is the [fermion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermion "Fermion")[number operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_number_operator "Particle number operator") counting how many fermions are in a state. Since all particles in the Standard Model satisfy _F_ = _B_ + _L_, the discrete symmetry is also part of the _e_ _iα_(_B_ + _L_) continuous symmetry group. If the parity operator satisfied P 2 = (−1)_F_, then it can be redefined to give a new parity operator satisfying P 2 = 1. But if the Standard Model is extended by incorporating [Majorana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Majorana_fermion "Majorana fermion")[neutrinos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutrino "Neutrino"), which have _F_ = 1 and _B_ + _L_ = 0, then the discrete symmetry (−1)_F_ is no longer part of the continuous symmetry group and the desired redefinition of the parity operator cannot be performed. Instead it satisfies P 4 = 1 so the Majorana neutrinos would have intrinsic parities of ±_i_.

In 1954, a paper by [William Chinowsky](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Chinowsky "William Chinowsky") and [Jack Steinberger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jack_Steinberger "Jack Steinberger") demonstrated that the [pion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pion "Pion") has negative parity.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parity_(physics)#cite_note-14) They studied the decay of an "atom" made from a [deuteron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deuteron "Deuteron") ([2 1 H+](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen-2 "Hydrogen-2")) and a negatively charged pion (π−

) in a state with zero orbital [angular momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum "Angular momentum")![Image 84: {\displaystyle ~\mathbf {L} ={\boldsymbol {0}}~}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0ab6efe0bd5806242f8baea0b685f3a912e592f3) into two [neutrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron "Neutron") (![Image 85: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)).

Neutrons are [fermions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermion "Fermion") and so obey [Fermi–Dirac statistics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermi%E2%80%93Dirac_statistics "Fermi–Dirac statistics"), which implies that the final state is antisymmetric. Using the fact that the deuteron has spin one and the pion spin zero together with the antisymmetry of the final state they concluded that the two neutrons must have orbital angular momentum ![Image 86: {\displaystyle ~L=1~.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c03b6b4d69db62f15a96475d86cf0eea2495d16a) The total parity is the product of the intrinsic parities of the particles and the extrinsic parity of the spherical harmonic function ![Image 87: {\displaystyle ~\left(-1\right)^{L}~.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/97bd66c1d3ca73ccbd4b0ddbfb13e2234c935943) Since the orbital momentum changes from zero to one in this process, if the process is to conserve the total parity then the products of the intrinsic parities of the initial and final particles must have opposite sign. A deuteron nucleus is made from a proton and a neutron, and so using the aforementioned convention that protons and neutrons have intrinsic parities equal to ![Image 88: {\displaystyle ~+1~}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/61432e540f3964b4af0195350f17102186778198) they argued that the parity of the pion is equal to minus the product of the parities of the two neutrons divided by that of the proton and neutron in the deuteron, explicitly ![Image 89: {\textstyle {\frac {(-1)(1)^{2}}{(1)^{2}}}=-1~,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/577a6b984e0c119d4225abb699225f6202bb691c) from which they concluded that the pion is a [pseudoscalar particle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudoscalar_particle "Pseudoscalar particle").

[![Image 90](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Parity_clocks_-_P-conservation.svg/330px-Parity_clocks_-_P-conservation.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Parity_clocks_-_P-conservation.svg)

P-symmetry: A clock built like its mirrored image behaves like the mirrored image of the original clock.

[![Image 91](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Parity_clocks_-_P-violation.svg/330px-Parity_clocks_-_P-violation.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Parity_clocks_-_P-violation.svg)

P-asymmetry: A clock built like its mirrored image that does _not_ behave like a mirrored image of the original clock.

Although parity is conserved in [electromagnetism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetism "Electromagnetism") and [gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity "Gravity"), it is violated in weak interactions, and perhaps, to some degree, in [strong interactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strong_interaction "Strong interaction").[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parity_(physics)#cite_note-15)[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parity_(physics)#cite_note-:0-16) The Standard Model incorporates **parity violation** by expressing the weak interaction as a [chiral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chirality_(physics) "Chirality (physics)") gauge interaction. Only the left-handed components of particles and right-handed components of antiparticles participate in charged weak interactions in the Standard Model. This implies that parity is not a symmetry of our universe, unless a [hidden mirror sector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mirror_matter "Mirror matter") exists in which parity is violated in the opposite way.

An obscure 1928 experiment, undertaken by [R. T. Cox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/R._T._Cox "R. T. Cox"), G. C. McIlwraith, and B. Kurrelmeyer, had in effect reported parity violation in [weak decays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weak_decay "Weak decay"), but, since the appropriate concepts had not yet been developed, those results had no impact.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parity_(physics)#cite_note-17) In 1929, [Hermann Weyl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Weyl "Hermann Weyl") explored, without any evidence, the existence of a two-component massless particle of spin one-half. This idea was rejected by [Pauli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Pauli "Wolfgang Pauli"), because it implied parity violation.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parity_(physics)#cite_note-18)

By the mid-20th century, it had been suggested by several scientists that parity might not be conserved (in different contexts), but without solid evidence these suggestions were not considered important. Then, in 1956, a careful review and analysis by theoretical physicists [Tsung-Dao Lee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tsung-Dao_Lee "Tsung-Dao Lee") and [Chen-Ning Yang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yang_Chen-Ning "Yang Chen-Ning")[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parity_(physics)#cite_note-19) went further, showing that while parity conservation had been verified in decays by the strong or [electromagnetic interactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_interaction "Electromagnetic interaction"), it was untested in the [weak interaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weak_interaction "Weak interaction"). They proposed several possible direct experimental tests. They were mostly ignored,[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] but Lee was able to convince his Columbia colleague [Chien-Shiung Wu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chien-Shiung_Wu "Chien-Shiung Wu") to try it.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] She needed special [cryogenic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cryogenic "Cryogenic") facilities and expertise, so the experiment was done at the [National Bureau of Standards](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Bureau_of_Standards "National Bureau of Standards").

[Wu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chien-Shiung_Wu "Chien-Shiung Wu"), [Ambler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ernest_Ambler "Ernest Ambler"), Hayward, Hoppes, and Hudson (1957) found a clear violation of parity conservation in the beta decay of [cobalt-60](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cobalt-60 "Cobalt-60").[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parity_(physics)#cite_note-Wu-Ambler-Hayward-etal-1957-20) As the experiment was winding down, with double-checking in progress, Wu informed Lee and Yang of their positive results, and saying the results need further examination, she asked them not to publicize the results first. However, Lee revealed the results to his Columbia colleagues on 4 January 1957 at a "Friday lunch" gathering of the Physics Department of Columbia.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parity_(physics)#cite_note-21) Three of them, [R. L. Garwin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Garwin "Richard Garwin"), [L. M. Lederman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leon_Lederman "Leon Lederman"), and R. M. Weinrich, modified an existing cyclotron experiment, and immediately verified the parity violation.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parity_(physics)#cite_note-22) They delayed publication of their results until after Wu's group was ready, and the two papers appeared back-to-back in the same physics journal.

The discovery of parity violation explained the outstanding [τ–θ puzzle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaon#Parity_violation "Kaon") in the physics of [kaons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaon "Kaon").

In 2010, it was reported that physicists working with the [Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_Heavy_Ion_Collider "Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider") had created a short-lived parity symmetry-breaking bubble in [quark–gluon plasmas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quark%E2%80%93gluon_plasma "Quark–gluon plasma"). An experiment conducted by several physicists in the [STAR collaboration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/STAR_collaboration "STAR collaboration"), suggested that parity may also be violated in the strong interaction.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parity_(physics)#cite_note-:0-16) It is predicted that this local parity violation manifests itself by [chiral magnetic effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chiral_magnetic_effect "Chiral magnetic effect").[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parity_(physics)#cite_note-23)[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parity_(physics)#cite_note-24)

### Intrinsic parity of hadrons

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Parity_(physics)&action=edit&section=19 "Edit section: Intrinsic parity of hadrons")]

To every particle one can assign an intrinsic parity as long as nature preserves parity. Although weak interactions do not, one can still assign a parity to any [hadron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hadron "Hadron") by examining the strong interaction reaction that produces it, or through decays not involving the weak interaction, such as [rho meson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rho_meson "Rho meson") decay to [pions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pion "Pion").
