In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), and specifically in [topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology "Topology"), a **CW complex** (also **cellular complex** or **cell complex**) is a [topological space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_space "Topological space") that is built by gluing together topological balls (so-called _cells_) of different dimensions in specific ways. The notion generalizes both [manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_manifold "Topological manifold") and [simplicial complexes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simplicial_complex "Simplicial complex") and has particular significance for [algebraic topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_topology "Algebraic topology").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CW_complexes#cite_note-1) It was initially introduced by [J. H. C. Whitehead](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J._H._C._Whitehead "J. H. C. Whitehead") to meet the needs of [homotopy theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homotopy_theory "Homotopy theory").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CW_complexes#cite_note-:2-2) CW complexes have better [categorical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category_theory "Category theory") properties than [simplicial complexes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simplicial_complex "Simplicial complex"), but still retain a combinatorial nature that allows for computation (often with a much smaller complex).

The C in CW stands for "closure-finite", and the W for "weak" topology.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CW_complexes#cite_note-:2-2)

A **CW complex** is constructed by taking the union of a sequence of topological spaces ![Image 1: {\displaystyle \emptyset =X_{-1}\subset X_{0}\subset X_{1}\subset \cdots }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0c18b7b1725009da29533875caf1f23f395b0087) such that each ![Image 2: {\displaystyle X_{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/33c25229c6989c235f9cbb7908331f6d01d0abfe) is obtained from ![Image 3: {\displaystyle X_{k-1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fb41e82ff1389c6d5f949e03c68e0c895d934a5a) by gluing copies of k-cells ![Image 4: {\displaystyle (e_{\alpha }^{k})_{\alpha }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5924ba99baf3710dd61de5306cd8a741eb5e1f94), each homeomorphic to the open unit [ball](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ball_(mathematics) "Ball (mathematics)")![Image 5: {\displaystyle B^{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d1f1ebbc79c2f3fe878d51107b5497de8f3c964c) in ![Image 6: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40)-dimensional Euclidean space, to ![Image 7: {\displaystyle X_{k-1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fb41e82ff1389c6d5f949e03c68e0c895d934a5a) by continuous gluing maps ![Image 8: {\displaystyle g_{\alpha }^{k}:\partial e_{\alpha }^{k}\to X_{k-1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b455b10c7570c7886e726a4c29616800d571ad05). The maps are also called [attaching maps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Attaching_map "Attaching map"). Thus as a set, ![Image 9: {\displaystyle X_{k}=X_{k-1}\sqcup _{\alpha }e_{\alpha }^{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bcace470494b9070ec11b0193a8dccfbbc43c473).

Each ![Image 10: {\displaystyle X_{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/33c25229c6989c235f9cbb7908331f6d01d0abfe) is called the **k-skeleton** of the complex.

### Regular CW complexes

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=CW_complex&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Regular CW complexes")]

A **regular CW complex** is a CW complex whose gluing maps are homeomorphisms. Accordingly, the partition of _X_ is also called a **regular cellulation**.

A [loopless](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Loop_(graph_theory) "Loop (graph theory)") graph is represented by a regular 1-dimensional CW-complex. A [closed 2-cell graph embedding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_embedding "Graph embedding") on a [surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface "Surface") is a regular 2-dimensional CW-complex. Finally, the 3-sphere regular cellulation conjecture claims that every [2-connected graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K-vertex-connected_graph "K-vertex-connected graph") is the 1-skeleton of a regular CW-complex on the [3-dimensional sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3-sphere "3-sphere").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CW_complexes#cite_note-3)

### Relative CW complexes

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=CW_complex&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Relative CW complexes")]

Roughly speaking, a _relative CW complex_ differs from a CW complex in that we allow it to have one extra building block that does not necessarily possess a cellular structure. This extra-block can be treated as a (−1)-dimensional cell in the former definition.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CW_complexes#cite_note-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CW_complexes#cite_note-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CW_complexes#cite_note-6)

### 0-dimensional CW complexes

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=CW_complex&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: 0-dimensional CW complexes")]

Every [discrete topological space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discrete_space "Discrete space") is a 0-dimensional CW complex.

### 1-dimensional CW complexes

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=CW_complex&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: 1-dimensional CW complexes")]

Some examples of 1-dimensional CW complexes are:[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CW_complexes#cite_note-:1-7)

*   **An interval**. It can be constructed from two points (_x_ and _y_), and the 1-dimensional ball _B_ (an interval), such that one endpoint of _B_ is glued to _x_ and the other is glued to _y_. The two points _x_ and _y_ are the 0-cells; the interior of _B_ is the 1-cell. Alternatively, it can be constructed just from a single interval, with no 0-cells.
*   **A circle**. It can be constructed from a single point _x_ and the 1-dimensional ball _B_, such that _both_ endpoints of _B_ are glued to _x_. Alternatively, it can be constructed from two points _x_ and _y_ and two 1-dimensional balls _A_ and _B_, such that the endpoints of _A_ are glued to _x_ and _y_, and the endpoints of _B_ are glued to _x_ and _y_ too.
*   **A graph.** Given a [graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multigraph "Multigraph"), a 1-dimensional CW complex can be constructed in which the 0-cells are the vertices and the 1-cells are the edges of the graph. The endpoints of each edge are identified with the incident vertices to it. This realization of a combinatorial graph as a topological space is sometimes called a **topological graph**. 
*   The _standard CW structure_ on the real numbers has as 0-skeleton the integers ![Image 19: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/449494a083e0a1fda2b61c62b2f09b6bee4633dc) and as 1-cells the intervals ![Image 20: {\displaystyle \{[n,n+1]:n\in \mathbb {Z} \}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/293be64a6365de868d30da25a098fd56128de763). Similarly, the standard CW structure on ![Image 21: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d) has cubical cells that are products of the 0 and 1-cells from ![Image 22: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc). This is the standard _[cubic lattice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer\_lattice "Integer lattice")_ cell structure on ![Image 23: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d).

### Infinite-dimensional CW complexes

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=CW_complex&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Infinite-dimensional CW complexes")]

*   An infinite-dimensional [Hilbert space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space "Hilbert space") is not a CW complex: it is a [Baire space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baire_space "Baire space") and therefore cannot be written as a countable union of _n_-skeletons, each of them being a closed set with empty interior. This argument extends to many other infinite-dimensional spaces.
*   The [hedgehog space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hedgehog_space "Hedgehog space")![Image 24: {\displaystyle \{re^{2\pi i\theta }:0\leq r\leq 1,\theta \in \mathbb {Q} \}\subseteq \mathbb {C} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4e3b1de3a73133e001230610709fade0ccd9241c) is homotopy equivalent to a CW complex (the point) but it does not admit a CW decomposition, since it is not [locally contractible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contractible_space#Locally_contractible_spaces "Contractible space").
*   The [Hawaiian earring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hawaiian_earring "Hawaiian earring") has no CW decomposition, because it is not locally contractible at origin. It is also not homotopy equivalent to a CW complex, because it has no good open cover.

*   CW complexes are locally contractible.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CW_complexes#cite_note-9)
*   If a space is [homotopy equivalent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homotopy_equivalent "Homotopy equivalent") to a CW complex, then it has a good open cover.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CW_complexes#cite_note-10) A good open cover is an open cover, such that every nonempty finite intersection is contractible.
*   CW complexes are [paracompact](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paracompact "Paracompact"). Finite CW complexes are [compact](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compact_space "Compact space"). A compact subspace of a CW complex is always contained in a finite subcomplex.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CW_complexes#cite_note-11)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CW_complexes#cite_note-12)
*   CW complexes satisfy the [Whitehead theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whitehead_theorem "Whitehead theorem"): a map between CW complexes is a homotopy equivalence if and only if it induces an isomorphism on all homotopy groups.
*   A [covering space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covering_space "Covering space") of a CW complex is also a CW complex.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CW_complexes#cite_note-13)
*   The product of two CW complexes can be made into a CW complex. Specifically, if _X_ and _Y_ are CW complexes, then one can form a CW complex _X_ × _Y_ in which each cell is a product of a cell in _X_ and a cell in _Y_, endowed with the [weak topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weak_topology "Weak topology"). The underlying set of _X_ × _Y_ is then the [Cartesian product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_product "Cartesian product") of _X_ and _Y_, as expected. In addition, the weak topology on this set often agrees with the more familiar [product topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Product_topology "Product topology") on _X_ × _Y_, for example if either _X_ or _Y_ is finite. However, the weak topology can be [finer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comparison_of_topologies "Comparison of topologies") than the product topology, for example if neither _X_ nor _Y_ is [locally compact](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Locally_compact_space "Locally compact space"). In this unfavorable case, the product _X_ × _Y_ in the product topology is _not_ a CW complex. On the other hand, the product of _X_ and _Y_ in the category of [compactly generated spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compactly_generated_space "Compactly generated space") agrees with the weak topology and therefore defines a CW complex.
*   Let _X_ and _Y_ be CW complexes. Then the [function spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_spaces "Function spaces") Hom(_X_,_Y_) (with the [compact-open topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compact-open_topology "Compact-open topology")) are _not_ CW complexes in general. If _X_ is finite then Hom(_X_,_Y_) is homotopy equivalent to a CW complex by a theorem of [John Milnor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Milnor "John Milnor") (1959).[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CW_complexes#cite_note-milnor-14) Note that _X_ and _Y_ are [compactly generated Hausdorff spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compactly_generated_Hausdorff_space "Compactly generated Hausdorff space"), so Hom(_X_,_Y_) is often taken with the [compactly generated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compactly_generated_space "Compactly generated space") variant of the compact-open topology; the above statements remain true.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CW_complexes#cite_note-15)
*   [Cellular approximation theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cellular_approximation_theorem "Cellular approximation theorem")

Homology and cohomology of CW complexes
---------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=CW_complex&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Homology and cohomology of CW complexes")]

[Singular homology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Singular_homology "Singular homology") and [cohomology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Singular_cohomology "Singular cohomology") of CW complexes is readily computable via [cellular homology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cellular_homology "Cellular homology"). Moreover, in the category of CW complexes and cellular maps, cellular homology can be interpreted as a [homology theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homology_theory "Homology theory"). To compute an [extraordinary (co)homology theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cohomology#Generalized_cohomology_theories "Cohomology") for a CW complex, the [Atiyah–Hirzebruch spectral sequence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atiyah%E2%80%93Hirzebruch_spectral_sequence "Atiyah–Hirzebruch spectral sequence") is the analogue of cellular homology.

Modification of CW structures
-----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=CW_complex&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Modification of CW structures")]

There is a technique, developed by Whitehead, for replacing a CW complex with a homotopy-equivalent CW complex that has a _simpler_ CW decomposition.

Consider, for example, an arbitrary CW complex. Its 1-skeleton can be fairly complicated, being an arbitrary [graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_(discrete_mathematics) "Graph (discrete mathematics)"). Now consider a maximal [forest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tree_(graph_theory) "Tree (graph theory)")_F_ in this graph. Since it is a collection of trees, and trees are contractible, consider the space ![Image 32: {\displaystyle X/{\sim }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/80508302af0d1ad574ebaaecbfd8b553e88149d9) where the equivalence relation is generated by ![Image 33: {\displaystyle x\sim y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bbd1014d850b7c883eb76301dd58c643e3c7e4eb) if they are contained in a common tree in the maximal forest _F_. The quotient map ![Image 34: {\displaystyle X\to X/{\sim }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/26f8404062c6934c87cc150abca35f4ab8d23609) is a homotopy equivalence. Moreover, ![Image 35: {\displaystyle X/{\sim }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/80508302af0d1ad574ebaaecbfd8b553e88149d9) naturally inherits a CW structure, with cells corresponding to the cells of ![Image 36: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) that are not contained in _F_. In particular, the 1-skeleton of ![Image 37: {\displaystyle X/{\sim }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/80508302af0d1ad574ebaaecbfd8b553e88149d9) is a disjoint union of wedges of circles.

Another way of stating the above is that a connected CW complex can be replaced by a homotopy-equivalent CW complex whose 0-skeleton consists of a single point.

Consider climbing up the connectivity ladder—assume _X_ is a simply-connected CW complex whose 0-skeleton consists of a point. Can we, through suitable modifications, replace _X_ by a homotopy-equivalent CW complex where ![Image 38: {\displaystyle X^{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6fca53d8f3bcc5469eece7dc6b23ada615470229) consists of a single point? The answer is yes. The first step is to observe that ![Image 39: {\displaystyle X^{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6fca53d8f3bcc5469eece7dc6b23ada615470229) and the attaching maps to construct ![Image 40: {\displaystyle X^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/df5c43e431c7e9c2c71cd2a0c59de0fb219e9d1e) from ![Image 41: {\displaystyle X^{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6fca53d8f3bcc5469eece7dc6b23ada615470229) form a [group presentation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Presentation_of_a_group "Presentation of a group"). The [Tietze theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tietze_transformations "Tietze transformations") for group presentations states that there is a sequence of moves we can perform to reduce this group presentation to the trivial presentation of the [trivial group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trivial_group "Trivial group"). There are two Tietze moves:

1) Adding/removing a generator. Adding a generator, from the perspective of the CW decomposition consists of adding a 1-cell and a 2-cell whose attaching map consists of the new 1-cell and the remainder of the attaching map is in ![Image 42: {\displaystyle X^{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6fca53d8f3bcc5469eece7dc6b23ada615470229). If we let ![Image 43: {\displaystyle {\tilde {X}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/19b6c2d2aa76b9cf010d897dc2ce988acf539624) be the corresponding CW complex ![Image 44: {\displaystyle {\tilde {X}}=X\cup e^{1}\cup e^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8e62fcefc2b8e05e5d0bc04124b0613c05e7b58c) then there is a homotopy equivalence ![Image 45: {\displaystyle {\tilde {X}}\to X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/953279905020b6c35426d3ce6670e8fb88e4198a) given by sliding the new 2-cell into _X_.2) Adding/removing a relation. The act of adding a relation is similar, only one is replacing _X_ by ![Image 46: {\displaystyle {\tilde {X}}=X\cup e^{2}\cup e^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/17c299f3a483b966f433eef2cb7ecaa28c55e3d8) where the new _3_-cell has an attaching map that consists of the new 2-cell and remainder mapping into ![Image 47: {\displaystyle X^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/df5c43e431c7e9c2c71cd2a0c59de0fb219e9d1e). A similar slide gives a homotopy-equivalence ![Image 48: {\displaystyle {\tilde {X}}\to X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/953279905020b6c35426d3ce6670e8fb88e4198a).
If a CW complex _X_ is [_n_-connected](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/N-connected_space "N-connected space") one can find a homotopy-equivalent CW complex ![Image 49: {\displaystyle {\tilde {X}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/19b6c2d2aa76b9cf010d897dc2ce988acf539624) whose _n_-skeleton ![Image 50: {\displaystyle X^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/268db8293666fefd75cfb00513706171948edf09) consists of a single point. The argument for ![Image 51: {\displaystyle n\geq 2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e6bf67f9d06ca3af619657f8d20ee1322da77174) is similar to the ![Image 52: {\displaystyle n=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d9ec7e1edc2e6d98f5aec2a39ae5f1c99d1e1425) case, only one replaces Tietze moves for the [fundamental group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_group "Fundamental group") presentation by [elementary matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementary_matrix "Elementary matrix") operations for the presentation matrices for ![Image 53: {\displaystyle H_{n}(X;\mathbb {Z} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0b03e60a250d31105073fadf3845a183b8e76946) (using the presentation matrices coming from [cellular homology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cellular_homology "Cellular homology"). i.e.: one can similarly realize elementary matrix operations by a sequence of addition/removal of cells or suitable homotopies of the attaching maps.

'The' homotopy category
-----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=CW_complex&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: 'The' homotopy category")]

The [homotopy category](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homotopy_category "Homotopy category") of CW complexes is, in the opinion of some experts, the best if not the only candidate for _the_ homotopy category (for technical reasons the version for [pointed spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pointed_space "Pointed space") is actually used).[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CW_complexes#cite_note-16) Auxiliary constructions that yield spaces that are not CW complexes must be used on occasion. One basic result is that the [representable functors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Representable_functor "Representable functor") on the homotopy category have a simple characterisation (the [Brown representability theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brown_representability_theorem "Brown representability theorem")).

*   [Abstract cell complex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abstract_cell_complex "Abstract cell complex")
*   The notion of CW complex has an adaptation to [smooth manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differentiable_manifold "Differentiable manifold") called a [handle decomposition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Handle_decomposition "Handle decomposition"), which is closely related to [surgery theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surgery_theory "Surgery theory").
