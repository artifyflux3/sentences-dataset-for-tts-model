[Coordinate surfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_system#Coordinate_surface "Coordinate system") of parabolic cylindrical coordinates. The red parabolic cylinder corresponds to σ=2, whereas the yellow parabolic cylinder corresponds to τ=1. The blue plane corresponds to _z_=2. These surfaces intersect at the point **P** (shown as a black sphere), which has [Cartesian coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system "Cartesian coordinate system") roughly (2, −1.5, 2).

In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), **parabolic cylindrical coordinates** are a three-dimensional [orthogonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_coordinates "Orthogonal coordinates")[coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_system "Coordinate system") that results from projecting the two-dimensional [parabolic coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parabolic_coordinates "Parabolic coordinates") in the perpendicular ![Image 2: {\displaystyle z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf368e72c009decd9b6686ee84a375632e11de98)-direction. Hence, the [coordinate surfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_system#Coordinate_surface "Coordinate system") are [confocal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confocal "Confocal")[parabolic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parabola "Parabola") cylinders. Parabolic cylindrical coordinates have found many applications, e.g., the [potential theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potential_theory "Potential theory") of edges.

[![Image 3](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Parabolic_coords.svg/250px-Parabolic_coords.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Parabolic_coords.svg)

Parabolic coordinate system showing curves of constant σ and τ the horizontal and vertical axes are the x and y coordinates respectively. These coordinates are projected along the z-axis, and so this diagram will hold for any value of the z coordinate.

The parabolic cylindrical coordinates (_σ_, _τ_, _z_) are defined in terms of the [Cartesian coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinates "Cartesian coordinates")(_x_, _y_, _z_) by:

![Image 4: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}x&=\sigma \tau \\y&={\frac {1}{2}}\left(\tau ^{2}-\sigma ^{2}\right)\\z&=z\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b7cf63de6820d1d43a22cfb229e2da3c8d9a841c)
The surfaces of constant _σ_ form confocal parabolic cylinders

![Image 5: {\displaystyle 2y={\frac {x^{2}}{\sigma ^{2}}}-\sigma ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/673e571139e5b22fccaf444c22145eb8d7e9f7d5)
that open towards +_y_, whereas the surfaces of constant _τ_ form confocal parabolic cylinders

![Image 6: {\displaystyle 2y=-{\frac {x^{2}}{\tau ^{2}}}+\tau ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e6a5c3e95d49388cafee36ab3252ee706cdec39d)
that open in the opposite direction, i.e., towards −_y_. The foci of all these parabolic cylinders are located along the line defined by _x_ = _y_ = 0. The radius _r_ has a simple formula as well

![Image 7: {\displaystyle r={\sqrt {x^{2}+y^{2}}}={\frac {1}{2}}\left(\sigma ^{2}+\tau ^{2}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/62614ec3bb0d098b72d7ece4bff0f59bf75f9577)
that proves useful in solving the [Hamilton–Jacobi equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamilton%E2%80%93Jacobi_equation "Hamilton–Jacobi equation") in parabolic coordinates for the [inverse-square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverse-square_law "Inverse-square law")[central force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_force "Central force") problem of [mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanics "Mechanics"); for further details, see the [Laplace–Runge–Lenz vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laplace%E2%80%93Runge%E2%80%93Lenz_vector "Laplace–Runge–Lenz vector") article.

The scale factors for the parabolic cylindrical coordinates _σ_ and _τ_ are:

![Image 8: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}h_{\sigma }&=h_{\tau }={\sqrt {\sigma ^{2}+\tau ^{2}}}\\h_{z}&=1\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8a9c421b9dca28b2185a5049ab69c358d66039b2)

Differential elements
---------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Parabolic_cylindrical_coordinates&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Differential elements")]

The infinitesimal element of volume is

![Image 9: {\displaystyle dV=h_{\sigma }h_{\tau }h_{z}d\sigma d\tau dz=(\sigma ^{2}+\tau ^{2})d\sigma \,d\tau \,dz}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ba94bdb8907146a848226b14a11fe701ca747588)
The differential displacement is given by:

![Image 10: {\displaystyle d\mathbf {l} ={\sqrt {\sigma ^{2}+\tau ^{2}}}\,d\sigma \,{\boldsymbol {\hat {\sigma }}}+{\sqrt {\sigma ^{2}+\tau ^{2}}}\,d\tau \,{\boldsymbol {\hat {\tau }}}+dz\,\mathbf {\hat {z}} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/17bc1b236c4800413b3ff965a2c789c75732b3f0)
The differential normal area is given by:

![Image 11: {\displaystyle d\mathbf {S} ={\sqrt {\sigma ^{2}+\tau ^{2}}}\,d\tau \,dz{\boldsymbol {\hat {\sigma }}}+{\sqrt {\sigma ^{2}+\tau ^{2}}}\,d\sigma \,dz{\boldsymbol {\hat {\tau }}}+\left(\sigma ^{2}+\tau ^{2}\right)\,d\sigma \,d\tau \mathbf {\hat {z}} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/79835b01556ad955690b28e528f0de6ea007526a)
Let _f_ be a scalar field. The [gradient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gradient "Gradient") is given by

![Image 12: {\displaystyle \nabla f={\frac {1}{\sqrt {\sigma ^{2}+\tau ^{2}}}}{\partial f \over \partial \sigma }{\boldsymbol {\hat {\sigma }}}+{\frac {1}{\sqrt {\sigma ^{2}+\tau ^{2}}}}{\partial f \over \partial \tau }{\boldsymbol {\hat {\tau }}}+{\partial f \over \partial z}\mathbf {\hat {z}} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fa166351be20958689832f03d30663a190ffde17)
The [Laplacian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laplacian "Laplacian") is given by

![Image 13: {\displaystyle \nabla ^{2}f={\frac {1}{\sigma ^{2}+\tau ^{2}}}\left({\frac {\partial ^{2}f}{\partial \sigma ^{2}}}+{\frac {\partial ^{2}f}{\partial \tau ^{2}}}\right)+{\frac {\partial ^{2}f}{\partial z^{2}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/05fa5630910165187edf7c3a944853800f35e30b)
Let **A** be a vector field of the form:

![Image 14: {\displaystyle \mathbf {A} =A_{\sigma }{\boldsymbol {\hat {\sigma }}}+A_{\tau }{\boldsymbol {\hat {\tau }}}+A_{z}\mathbf {\hat {z}} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c9951e7d8263a6b1f14ff9d1df467bb73e6a6f6c)
The [divergence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divergence "Divergence") is given by

![Image 15: {\displaystyle \nabla \cdot \mathbf {A} ={\frac {1}{\sigma ^{2}+\tau ^{2}}}\left({\partial ({\sqrt {\sigma ^{2}+\tau ^{2}}}A_{\sigma }) \over \partial \sigma }+{\partial ({\sqrt {\sigma ^{2}+\tau ^{2}}}A_{\tau }) \over \partial \tau }\right)+{\partial A_{z} \over \partial z}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/da59a71caa168f8fda3f8ff7c84774eb4b5c022b)
The [curl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curl_(mathematics) "Curl (mathematics)") is given by

![Image 16: {\displaystyle \nabla \times \mathbf {A} =\left({\frac {1}{\sqrt {\sigma ^{2}+\tau ^{2}}}}{\frac {\partial A_{z}}{\partial \tau }}-{\frac {\partial A_{\tau }}{\partial z}}\right){\boldsymbol {\hat {\sigma }}}-\left({\frac {1}{\sqrt {\sigma ^{2}+\tau ^{2}}}}{\frac {\partial A_{z}}{\partial \sigma }}-{\frac {\partial A_{\sigma }}{\partial z}}\right){\boldsymbol {\hat {\tau }}}+{\frac {1}{\sigma ^{2}+\tau ^{2}}}\left({\frac {\partial \left({\sqrt {\sigma ^{2}+\tau ^{2}}}A_{\tau }\right)}{\partial \sigma }}-{\frac {\partial \left({\sqrt {\sigma ^{2}+\tau ^{2}}}A_{\sigma }\right)}{\partial \tau }}\right)\mathbf {\hat {z}} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1fe59607fbe26dbd31ff2595d72491e7db689bb3)
Other differential operators can be expressed in the coordinates (_σ_, _τ_) by substituting the scale factors into the general formulae found in [orthogonal coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_coordinates "Orthogonal coordinates").

Parabolic cylinder harmonics
----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Parabolic_cylindrical_coordinates&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Parabolic cylinder harmonics")]

Since all of the surfaces of constant _σ_, _τ_ and _z_ are [conicoids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conicoid "Conicoid"), Laplace's equation is separable in parabolic cylindrical coordinates. Using the technique of the [separation of variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separation_of_variables "Separation of variables"), a separated solution to Laplace's equation may be written:

![Image 19: {\displaystyle V=S(\sigma )T(\tau )Z(z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d433047f4369439f846bc5868b12ee7e901419d5)
and Laplace's equation, divided by _V_, is written:

![Image 20: {\displaystyle {\frac {1}{\sigma ^{2}+\tau ^{2}}}\left[{\frac {\ddot {S}}{S}}+{\frac {\ddot {T}}{T}}\right]+{\frac {\ddot {Z}}{Z}}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/99190e6afe07d230b871dd5a2f159b9cf8179d2c)
Since the _Z_ equation is separate from the rest, we may write

![Image 21: {\displaystyle {\frac {\ddot {Z}}{Z}}=-m^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e42f2e6835bb3caa34a093783fb14ccd5584b7db)
where _m_ is constant. _Z_(_z_) has the solution:

![Image 22: {\displaystyle Z_{m}(z)=A_{1}\,e^{imz}+A_{2}\,e^{-imz}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2a93f119485317bfd0a3302918e2488892eeb33b)
Substituting −_m_ 2 for ![Image 23: {\displaystyle {\ddot {Z}}/Z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1862dcecd5909da6283488ae99db938b6aee1e8b), Laplace's equation may now be written:

![Image 24: {\displaystyle \left[{\frac {\ddot {S}}{S}}+{\frac {\ddot {T}}{T}}\right]=m^{2}(\sigma ^{2}+\tau ^{2})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/544c991ae2342d2a4f196e566d4357f7195a7c6c)
We may now separate the _S_ and _T_ functions and introduce another constant _n_ 2 to obtain:

![Image 25: {\displaystyle {\ddot {S}}-(m^{2}\sigma ^{2}+n^{2})S=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b855eeacb531a77081e063ee0a77bfa38a7e90f5)![Image 26: {\displaystyle {\ddot {T}}-(m^{2}\tau ^{2}-n^{2})T=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d0b433d2ca89c0bfc1c6fc8322719b9cb1e3865b)
The solutions to these equations are the [parabolic cylinder functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parabolic_cylinder_functions "Parabolic cylinder functions")

![Image 27: {\displaystyle S_{mn}(\sigma )=A_{3}y_{1}(n^{2}/2m,\sigma {\sqrt {2m}})+A_{4}y_{2}(n^{2}/2m,\sigma {\sqrt {2m}})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f657ece44c33c5eea35668e0617a6a0cc834d3ea)![Image 28: {\displaystyle T_{mn}(\tau )=A_{5}y_{1}(n^{2}/2m,i\tau {\sqrt {2m}})+A_{6}y_{2}(n^{2}/2m,i\tau {\sqrt {2m}})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/aa8fe883e7f88509702a25f50977d5460b2ca77a)
The parabolic cylinder harmonics for (_m_, _n_) are now the product of the solutions. The combination will reduce the number of constants and the general solution to Laplace's equation may be written:

![Image 29: {\displaystyle V(\sigma ,\tau ,z)=\sum _{m,n}A_{mn}S_{mn}T_{mn}Z_{m}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/638405e70f52a586da2cc4609feef1f279d3e329)
The classic applications of parabolic cylindrical coordinates are in solving [partial differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_differential_equations "Partial differential equations"), e.g., [Laplace's equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laplace%27s_equation "Laplace's equation") or the [Helmholtz equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helmholtz_equation "Helmholtz equation"), for which such coordinates allow a [separation of variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separation_of_variables "Separation of variables"). A typical example would be the [electric field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_field "Electric field") surrounding a flat semi-infinite conducting plate.
