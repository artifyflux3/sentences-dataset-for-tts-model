Relative motion man on train

The **relative velocity** of an object _**B**_ relative to an observer _**A**_, denoted ![Image 2: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} _{B\mid A}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2674082d33f0a396ab87732c47c7932868f44283) (also ![Image 3: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} _{BA}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/21410b6ad7f16cf34168a3e77d32346bbd56fadf) or ![Image 4: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} _{B\operatorname {rel} A}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/99f647a0a6fe3a200100029b0e9bb6a66ebb291b)), is the [velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity "Velocity")[vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_(physics) "Vector (physics)") of _B_ measured in the [rest frame](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rest_frame "Rest frame") of _A_. The **relative speed**![Image 5: {\displaystyle v_{B\mid A}=\|\mathbf {v} _{B\mid A}\|}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0511e6fa961a39c00d0bc425c4b79803f732e81b) is the [vector norm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_norm "Vector norm") of the relative velocity.

Classical mechanics
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Relative_velocity&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Classical mechanics")]

### In one dimension (non-relativistic)

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Relative_velocity&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: In one dimension (non-relativistic)")]

We begin with relative motion in the [classical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_mechanics "Classical mechanics"), (or non-[relativistic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_relativity "Special relativity"), or the [Newtonian approximation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_mechanics "Classical mechanics")) that all speeds are much less than the speed of light. This limit is associated with the [Galilean transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galilean_transformation "Galilean transformation"). The figure shows a man on top of a train, at the back edge. At 1:00 pm he begins to walk forward at a walking speed of 10 km/h (kilometers per hour). The train is moving at 40 km/h. The figure depicts the man and train at two different times: first, when the journey began, and also one hour later at 2:00 pm. The figure suggests that the man is 50 km from the starting point after having traveled (by walking and by train) for one hour. This, by definition, is 50 km/h, which suggests that the prescription for calculating relative velocity in this fashion is to add the two velocities.

The diagram displays clocks and rulers to remind the reader that while the logic behind this calculation seem flawless, it makes false assumptions about how clocks and rulers behave. (See [The train-and-platform thought experiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativity_of_simultaneity#The_train-and-platform "Relativity of simultaneity").) To recognize that this [classical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_mechanics "Classical mechanics") model of relative motion violates [special relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_relativity "Special relativity"), we generalize the example into an equation:

![Image 6: {\displaystyle \underbrace {\mathbf {v} _{M\mid E}} _{\text{50 km/h}}=\underbrace {\mathbf {v} _{M\mid T}} _{\text{10 km/h}}+\underbrace {\mathbf {v} _{T\mid E}} _{\text{40 km/h}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c670a068c6ad63ac644a21b904e898f56db61775)
where:

![Image 7: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} _{M\mid E}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/898714f5517008c59d55b7f299c71b847aaed2a0) is the velocity of the **M**an relative to **E**arth,![Image 8: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} _{M\mid T}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/298b6a03f9d7ac6a601d9eb9bb0635c28ede0dc8) is the velocity of the **M**an relative to the **T**rain,![Image 9: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} _{T\mid E}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5873cd16cb807d82fafe1384229ef7818d158831) is the velocity of the **T**rain relative to **E**arth.
Fully legitimate expressions for "the velocity of A relative to B" include "the velocity of A with respect to B" and "the velocity of A in the coordinate system where B is always at rest". The [violation of special relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postulates_of_special_relativity "Postulates of special relativity") occurs because this equation for relative velocity falsely predicts that different observers will measure different speeds when observing the motion of light. [[note 1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relative_velocity#cite_note-1)

### In two dimensions (non-relativistic)

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Relative_velocity&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: In two dimensions (non-relativistic)")]

[![Image 10](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Relative_velocity.svg/330px-Relative_velocity.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Relative_velocity.svg)

Relative velocities between two particles in classical mechanics

The figure shows two objects _A_ and _B_ moving at constant velocity. The equations of motion are:

![Image 11: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} _{A}=\mathbf {r} _{Ai}+\mathbf {v} _{A}t,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1a429554ee856604f6527f645811fb1d33bb8db5)![Image 12: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} _{B}=\mathbf {r} _{Bi}+\mathbf {v} _{B}t,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f55260567b153453d312ae41fad940f994ea089d)
where the subscript _i_ refers to the initial displacement (at time _t_ equal to zero). The difference between the two displacement vectors, ![Image 13: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} _{B}-\mathbf {r} _{A}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af4d58d764acaceb43a45ad3199bb072d83314ba), represents the location of B as seen from A.

![Image 14: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} _{B}-\mathbf {r} _{A}=\underbrace {\mathbf {r} _{Bi}-\mathbf {r} _{Ai}} _{\text{initial separation}}+\underbrace {(\mathbf {v} _{B}-\mathbf {v} _{A})t} _{\text{relative velocity}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/617d9c066cff8ea007bbb9011f77ca8da56516ef)
Hence:

![Image 15: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} _{B\mid A}=\mathbf {v} _{B}-\mathbf {v} _{A}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a8659e612a50012ccf4e1c51bad4004325b82312)
After making the substitutions ![Image 16: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} _{A|C}=\mathbf {v} _{A}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eecd9600b071ac3b1687931c983a36cb30653d0d) and ![Image 17: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} _{B|C}=\mathbf {v} _{B}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e19354aedfdd8979283cd33fb14074ddc57ab3b6), we have:

![Image 18: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} _{B\mid A}=\mathbf {v} _{B\mid C}-\mathbf {v} _{A\mid C}\Rightarrow }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5888ea8f9c9c70be36dae0109373b8bf72fe45ac)![Image 19: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} _{B\mid C}=\mathbf {v} _{B\mid A}+\mathbf {v} _{A\mid C}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5a0662b98ec3bc48ebaaa12df8efa2fe72a54e8c)

### Galilean transformation (non-relativistic)

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Relative_velocity&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Galilean transformation (non-relativistic)")]

To construct a theory of relative motion consistent with the theory of special relativity, we must adopt a different convention. Continuing to work in the (non-relativistic) [Newtonian limit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_mechanics "Classical mechanics") we begin with a [Galilean transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galilean_transformation "Galilean transformation") in one dimension:[[note 2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relative_velocity#cite_note-2)

![Image 20: {\displaystyle x'=x-vt}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/750ef5412025d2ea242170bb04644aaeed88dd7a)![Image 21: {\displaystyle t'=t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7827fb8419fde60614213d775219698ef599c39d)
where x' is the position as seen by a reference frame that is moving at speed, v, in the "unprimed" (x) reference frame.[[note 3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relative_velocity#cite_note-3) Taking the differential of the first of the two equations above, we have, ![Image 22: {\displaystyle dx'=dx-v\,dt}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/52e335f49a43d7b26c1d07e739d0fe725f53aaf9), and what may seem like the obvious[[note 4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relative_velocity#cite_note-4) statement that ![Image 23: {\displaystyle dt'=dt}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3edadb9d17ba2ea6c135fc66b3ff0f6f6df3d51d), we have:

![Image 24: {\displaystyle {\frac {dx'}{dt'}}={\frac {dx}{dt}}-v}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/981eb9370ec4ca9ce26b6849108f1bd1a3333619)
To recover the previous expressions for relative velocity, we assume that particle _A_ is following the path defined by dx/dt in the unprimed reference (and hence _dx_′/_dt_′ in the primed frame). Thus ![Image 25: {\displaystyle dx/dt=v_{A\mid O}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/977e8f445fa72f6997fa00c1e32c53dadfe91b42) and ![Image 26: {\displaystyle dx'/dt=v_{A\mid O'}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2f65217c16e714f9aeffc81c3e278fceae8d7b5f), where ![Image 27: {\displaystyle O}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9d70e1d0d87e2ef1092ea1ffe2923d9933ff18fc) and ![Image 28: {\displaystyle O'}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/470d46b4d626e35d82cd0d9f9aadc65ac47ab568) refer to motion of _A_ as seen by an observer in the unprimed and primed frame, respectively. Recall that _v_ is the motion of a stationary object in the primed frame, as seen from the unprimed frame. Thus we have ![Image 29: {\displaystyle v=v_{O'\mid O}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/967c6eaed08dbe6c65e685f173432cf0cfe99307), and:

![Image 30: {\displaystyle v_{A\mid O'}=v_{A\mid O}-v_{O'\mid O}\Rightarrow v_{A\mid O}=v_{A\mid O'}+v_{O'\mid O},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/76998599d7416edd94cf2832b1bd5710aff84fbc)
where the latter form has the desired (easily learned) symmetry.

As in classical mechanics, in special relativity the relative velocity ![Image 31: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} _{\mathrm {B|A} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/37f2a0bda72bf78c15d5fed853f071cd1ad06119) is the velocity of an object or observer **B** in the rest frame of another object or observer **A**. However, unlike the case of classical mechanics, in Special Relativity, it is generally _not_ the case that

![Image 32: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} _{\mathrm {B|A} }=-\mathbf {v} _{\mathrm {A|B} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/df29c05856eba1062a20043b8b4b539c54a22dcd)
This peculiar lack of symmetry is related to [Thomas precession](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_precession "Thomas precession") and the fact that two successive [Lorentz transformations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentz_transformation "Lorentz transformation") rotate the coordinate system. This rotation has no effect on the magnitude of a vector, and hence relative [speed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed "Speed") is symmetrical.

![Image 33: {\displaystyle \|\mathbf {v} _{\mathrm {B|A} }\|=\|\mathbf {v} _{\mathrm {A|B} }\|=v_{\mathrm {B|A} }=v_{\mathrm {A|B} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/df252cb3b559235d62b53f3dbb3f682c61e33c4f)

### Parallel velocities

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Relative_velocity&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Parallel velocities")]

In the case where two objects are traveling in parallel directions, the relativistic formula for relative velocity is similar in form to the formula for addition of relativistic velocities.

![Image 34: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} _{\mathrm {B|A} }={\frac {\mathbf {v} _{\mathrm {B} }-\mathbf {v} _{\mathrm {A} }}{1-{\frac {\mathbf {v} _{\mathrm {A} }\mathbf {v} _{\mathrm {B} }}{c^{2}}}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1c51bc36e66880436f969b0006d808af55dbe701)
The **relative speed** is given by the formula:

![Image 35: {\displaystyle v_{\mathrm {B|A} }={\frac {\left|\mathbf {v} _{\mathrm {B} }-\mathbf {v} _{\mathrm {A} }\right|}{1-{\frac {\mathbf {v} _{\mathrm {A} }\mathbf {v} _{\mathrm {B} }}{c^{2}}}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/94101e4d00bc2bbcd02adbd69bd305ad65f322d1)

### Perpendicular velocities

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Relative_velocity&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Perpendicular velocities")]

In the case where two objects are traveling in perpendicular directions, the relativistic relative velocity ![Image 36: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} _{\mathrm {B|A} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/37f2a0bda72bf78c15d5fed853f071cd1ad06119) is given by the formula:

![Image 37: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} _{\mathrm {B|A} }={\frac {\mathbf {v} _{\mathrm {B} }}{\gamma _{\mathrm {A} }}}-\mathbf {v} _{\mathrm {A} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4337c3c50921ed87df8a3effa6858c82eaa4837a)
where

![Image 38: {\displaystyle \gamma _{\mathrm {A} }={\frac {1}{\sqrt {1-\left({\frac {v_{\mathrm {A} }}{c}}\right)^{2}}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b76a7fc986bc9ef8362c31cc0c62f1d71e25188)
The relative speed is given by the formula

![Image 39: {\displaystyle v_{\mathrm {B|A} }={\frac {\sqrt {c^{4}-\left(c^{2}-v_{\mathrm {A} }^{2}\right)\left(c^{2}-v_{\mathrm {B} }^{2}\right)}}{c}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/88e8b80f7c1afbdc2c9d80926fe6c03bdcaab2ef)
The general formula for the relative velocity ![Image 40: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} _{\mathrm {B|A} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/37f2a0bda72bf78c15d5fed853f071cd1ad06119) of an object or observer **B** in the rest frame of another object or observer **A** is given by the formula:[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relative_velocity#cite_note-5)

![Image 41: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} _{\mathrm {B|A} }={\frac {1}{\gamma _{\mathrm {A} }\left(1-{\frac {\mathbf {v} _{\mathrm {A} }\mathbf {v} _{\mathrm {B} }}{c^{2}}}\right)}}\left[\mathbf {v} _{\mathrm {B} }-\mathbf {v} _{\mathrm {A} }+\mathbf {v} _{\mathrm {A} }(\gamma _{\mathrm {A} }-1)\left({\frac {\mathbf {v} _{\mathrm {A} }\cdot \mathbf {v} _{\mathrm {B} }}{v_{\mathrm {A} }^{2}}}-1\right)\right]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/41824d072f371a9f972d7b1f60cb764d1f288d17)
where

![Image 42: {\displaystyle \gamma _{\mathrm {A} }={\frac {1}{\sqrt {1-\left({\frac {v_{\mathrm {A} }}{c}}\right)^{2}}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b76a7fc986bc9ef8362c31cc0c62f1d71e25188)
The relative speed is given by the formula

![Image 43: {\displaystyle v_{\mathrm {B|A} }={\sqrt {1-{\frac {\left(c^{2}-v_{\mathrm {A} }^{2}\right)\left(c^{2}-v_{\mathrm {B} }^{2}\right)}{\left(c^{2}-\mathbf {v} _{\mathrm {A} }\cdot \mathbf {v} _{\mathrm {B} }\right)^{2}}}}}\cdot c}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4ef8b4190526e0ca3be9f2f9011d203fd5302a1f)
