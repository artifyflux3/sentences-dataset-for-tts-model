Some polygons of different kinds: open (excluding its boundary), boundary only (excluding interior), closed (including both boundary and interior), and self-intersecting.

In [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry"), a **polygon** () is a [plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_(mathematics) "Plane (mathematics)")[figure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shape "Shape") made up of [line segments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_segment "Line segment") connected to form a [closed polygonal chain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_polygonal_chain "Closed polygonal chain").

The segments of a closed polygonal chain are called its _[edges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edge\_(geometry) "Edge (geometry)")_ or _sides_. The points where two edges meet are the polygon's _[vertices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex\_(geometry) "Vertex (geometry)")_ or _corners_. An **_n_-gon** is a polygon with _n_ sides; for example, a [triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle "Triangle") is a 3-gon.

A [simple polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simple_polygon "Simple polygon") is one which does not intersect itself. More precisely, the only allowed intersections among the line segments that make up the polygon are the shared endpoints of consecutive segments in the polygonal chain. A simple polygon is the boundary of a region of the plane that is called a _solid polygon_. The interior of a solid polygon is its _body_, also known as a _**polygonal region**_ or _**polygonal area**_. In contexts where one is concerned only with simple and solid polygons, a _polygon_ may refer only to a simple polygon or to a solid polygon.

A polygonal chain may cross over itself, creating [star polygons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_polygon "Star polygon") and other [self-intersecting polygons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_self-intersecting_polygons "List of self-intersecting polygons"). Some sources also consider closed polygonal chains in [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space") to be a type of polygon (a [skew polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skew_polygon "Skew polygon")), even when the chain does not lie in a single plane.

A polygon is a 2-dimensional example of the more general [polytope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polytope "Polytope") in any number of dimensions. There are many more [generalizations of polygons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon#Generalizations) defined for different purposes.

Etymology
---------

The word _polygon_ derives from the [Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_language "Greek language") adjective πολύς (_polús_) 'much', 'many' and γωνία (_gōnía_) 'corner' or 'angle'. It has been suggested that γόνυ (_gónu_) 'knee' may be the origin of _gon_.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon#cite_note-1)

Classification
--------------

[![Image 3](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Polygon_types.svg/330px-Polygon_types.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Polygon_types.svg)

Some different types of polygon

### Number of sides

Polygons are primarily classified by the number of sides.

### Convexity and intersection

Polygons may be characterized by their convexity or type of non-convexity:

*   [Convex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_polygon "Convex polygon"): any line drawn through the polygon (and not tangent to an edge or corner) meets its boundary exactly twice. As a consequence, all its interior angles are less than 180°. Equivalently, any line segment with endpoints on the boundary passes through only interior points between its endpoints. This condition is true for polygons in any geometry, not just Euclidean.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon#cite_note-2)
*   Non-convex: a line may be found which meets its boundary more than twice. Equivalently, there exists a line segment between two boundary points that passes outside the polygon.
*   [Simple](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simple_polygon "Simple polygon"): the boundary of the polygon does not cross itself. All convex polygons are simple.
*   [Concave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concave_polygon "Concave polygon"): Non-convex and simple. There is at least one interior angle greater than 180°.
*   [Star-shaped](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star-shaped_polygon "Star-shaped polygon"): the whole interior is visible from at least one point, without crossing any edge. The polygon must be simple, and may be convex or concave. All convex polygons are star-shaped.
*   [Self-intersecting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_self-intersecting_polygons "List of self-intersecting polygons"): the boundary of the polygon crosses itself. The term _complex_ is sometimes used in contrast to _simple_, but this usage risks confusion with the idea of a _[complex polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex\_polytope "Complex polytope")_ as one which exists in the complex [Hilbert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space "Hilbert space") plane consisting of two [complex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number") dimensions.
*   [Star polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_polygon "Star polygon"): a polygon which self-intersects in a regular way. A polygon cannot be both a star and star-shaped.

### Equality and symmetry

*   [Equiangular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equiangular_polygon "Equiangular polygon"): all corner angles are equal.
*   [Equilateral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equilateral_polygon "Equilateral polygon"): all edges are of the same length.
*   [Regular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polygon "Regular polygon"): both equilateral and equiangular.
*   [Cyclic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclic_polygon "Cyclic polygon"): all corners lie on a single [circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle "Circle"), called the [circumcircle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumcircle "Circumcircle").
*   [Tangential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangential_polygon "Tangential polygon"): all sides are tangent to an [inscribed circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inscribed_circle "Inscribed circle").
*   Isogonal or [vertex-transitive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex-transitive "Vertex-transitive"): all corners lie within the same [symmetry orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry_orbit "Symmetry orbit"). The polygon is also cyclic and equiangular.
*   Isotoxal or [edge-transitive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edge-transitive "Edge-transitive"): all sides lie within the same [symmetry orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry_orbit "Symmetry orbit"). The polygon is also equilateral and tangential.

The property of regularity may be defined in other ways: a polygon is regular if and only if it is both isogonal and isotoxal, or equivalently it is both cyclic and equilateral. A non-convex regular polygon is called a _regular [star polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star\_polygon "Star polygon")_.

### Miscellaneous

*   [Rectilinear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectilinear_polygon "Rectilinear polygon"): the polygon's sides meet at right angles, i.e. all its interior angles are 90 or 270 degrees.
*   [Monotone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monotone_polygon "Monotone polygon") with respect to a given line _L_: every line [orthogonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_(geometry) "Orthogonal (geometry)") to L intersects the polygon not more than twice.

#### Simple polygons

If the polygon is non-self-intersecting (that is, [simple](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simple_polygon "Simple polygon")), the signed [area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area_(geometry) "Area (geometry)") is

or, using [determinants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Determinant "Determinant")

![Image 8: {\displaystyle 16A^{2}=\sum _{i=0}^{n-1}\sum _{j=0}^{n-1}{\begin{vmatrix}Q_{i,j}&Q_{i,j+1}\\Q_{i+1,j}&Q_{i+1,j+1}\end{vmatrix}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e3bf884f90ffb6da316eec33f98f8a61c6c45dc4)
where ![Image 9: {\displaystyle Q_{i,j}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f13dc1c4253af7122e2735c77ec965ac6ba7f64b) is the squared distance between ![Image 10: {\displaystyle (x_{i},y_{i})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d6dbb919b91ccacf17ed47898048428a1baf9703) and ![Image 11: {\displaystyle (x_{j},y_{j}).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5b6af53e3f51846ab5c2123240ca4a9e226e87ee)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon#cite_note-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon#cite_note-5)

The signed area depends on the ordering of the vertices and of the [orientation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orientation_(vector_space) "Orientation (vector space)") of the plane. Commonly, the positive orientation is defined by the (counterclockwise) rotation that maps the positive x-axis to the positive y-axis. If the vertices are ordered counterclockwise (that is, according to positive orientation), the signed area is positive; otherwise, it is negative. In either case, the area formula is correct in [absolute value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_value "Absolute value"). This is commonly called the _[shoelace formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shoelace\_formula "Shoelace formula")_ or _surveyor's formula_.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon#cite_note-6)

The area _A_ of a simple polygon can also be computed if the lengths of the sides, _a_ 1, _a_ 2, ..., _a n_ and the [exterior angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_angle "Exterior angle"), _θ_ 1, _θ_ 2, ..., _θ n_ are known, from:

![Image 12: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}A={\frac {1}{2}}(a_{1}[a_{2}\sin(\theta _{1})+a_{3}\sin(\theta _{1}+\theta _{2})+\cdots +a_{n-1}\sin(\theta _{1}+\theta _{2}+\cdots +\theta _{n-2})]\\{}+a_{2}[a_{3}\sin(\theta _{2})+a_{4}\sin(\theta _{2}+\theta _{3})+\cdots +a_{n-1}\sin(\theta _{2}+\cdots +\theta _{n-2})]\\{}+\cdots +a_{n-2}[a_{n-1}\sin(\theta _{n-2})]).\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/782046e07ca487a9d555c83a434d7c13aaf01c31)
The formula was described by Lopshits in 1963.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon#cite_note-lopshits-7)

If the polygon can be drawn on an equally spaced grid such that all its vertices are grid points, [Pick's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pick%27s_theorem "Pick's theorem") gives a simple formula for the polygon's area based on the numbers of interior and boundary grid points: the former number plus one-half the latter number, minus 1.

In every polygon with perimeter _p_ and area _A_, the [isoperimetric inequality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isoperimetric_inequality "Isoperimetric inequality")![Image 13: {\displaystyle p^{2}>4\pi A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3dc7bb97240acbcef4e43fe7855784a2096f5642) holds.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon#cite_note-8)

For any two simple polygons of equal area, the [Bolyai–Gerwien theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bolyai%E2%80%93Gerwien_theorem "Bolyai–Gerwien theorem") asserts that the first can be cut into polygonal pieces which can be reassembled to form the second polygon.

The lengths of the sides of a polygon do not in general determine its area.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon#cite_note-9) However, if the polygon is simple and cyclic then the sides _do_ determine the area.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon#cite_note-10) Of all _n_-gons with given side lengths, the one with the largest area is cyclic. Of all _n_-gons with a given perimeter, the one with the largest area is regular (and therefore cyclic).[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon#cite_note-11)

#### Self-intersecting

The area of a [self-intersecting polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_polygon "Complex polygon") can be defined in two different ways, giving different answers:

*   Using the formulas for simple polygons, we allow that particular regions within the polygon may have their area multiplied by a factor which we call the _density_ of the region. For example, the central convex pentagon in the center of a pentagram has density 2. The two triangular regions of a cross-quadrilateral (like a figure 8) have opposite-signed densities, and adding their areas together can give a total area of zero for the whole figure.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon#cite_note-14)
*   Considering the enclosed regions as point sets, we can find the area of the enclosed point set. This corresponds to the area of the plane covered by the polygon or to the area of one or more simple polygons having the same outline as the self-intersecting one. In the case of the cross-quadrilateral, it is treated as two simple triangles.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

Generalizations
---------------

The idea of a polygon has been generalized in various ways. Some of the more important include:

*   A [spherical polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_polygon "Spherical polygon") is a circuit of arcs of great circles (sides) and vertices on the surface of a sphere. It allows the [digon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digon "Digon"), a polygon having only two sides and two corners, which is impossible in a flat plane. Spherical polygons play an important role in [cartography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartography "Cartography") (map making) and in [Wythoff's construction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wythoff%27s_construction "Wythoff's construction") of the [uniform polyhedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uniform_polyhedra "Uniform polyhedra").
*   A [skew polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skew_polygon "Skew polygon") does not lie in a flat plane, but zigzags in three (or more) dimensions. The [Petrie polygons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petrie_polygon "Petrie polygon") of the regular polytopes are well known examples.
*   An [apeirogon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apeirogon "Apeirogon") is an infinite sequence of sides and angles, which is not closed but has no ends because it extends indefinitely in both directions.
*   A [skew apeirogon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skew_apeirogon "Skew apeirogon") is an infinite sequence of sides and angles that do not lie in a flat plane.
*   A [polygon with holes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon_with_holes "Polygon with holes") is an area-connected or multiply-connected planar polygon with one external boundary and one or more interior boundaries (holes).
*   A [complex polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_polytope "Complex polytope") is a [configuration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Configuration_(polytope) "Configuration (polytope)") analogous to an ordinary polygon, which exists in the [complex plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_plane "Complex plane") of two [real](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number") and two [imaginary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imaginary_number "Imaginary number") dimensions.
*   An [abstract polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abstract_polytope "Abstract polytope") is an algebraic [partially ordered set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partially_ordered_set "Partially ordered set") representing the various elements (sides, vertices, etc.) and their connectivity. A real geometric polygon is said to be a _realization_ of the associated abstract polygon. Depending on the mapping, all the generalizations described here can be realized.
*   A [polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyhedron "Polyhedron") is a three-dimensional solid bounded by flat polygonal faces, analogous to a polygon in two dimensions. The corresponding shapes in four or higher dimensions are called [polytopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polytope "Polytope").[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon#cite_note-15) (In other conventions, the words _polyhedron_ and _polytope_ are used in any dimension, with the distinction between the two that a polytope is necessarily bounded.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon#cite_note-16))

Naming
------

The word _polygon_ comes from [Late Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Late_Latin "Late Latin")_polygōnum_ (a noun), from [Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_language "Greek language") πολύγωνον (_polygōnon/polugōnon_), noun use of neuter of πολύγωνος (_polygōnos/polugōnos_, the masculine adjective), meaning "many-angled". Individual polygons are named (and sometimes classified) according to the number of sides, combining a [Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_language "Greek language")-derived [numerical prefix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Numerical_prefix "Numerical prefix") with the suffix _-gon_, e.g. _[pentagon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentagon "Pentagon")_, _[dodecagon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dodecagon "Dodecagon")_. The [triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle "Triangle"), [quadrilateral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral "Quadrilateral") and [nonagon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nonagon "Nonagon") are exceptions.

Beyond decagons (10-sided) and dodecagons (12-sided), mathematicians generally use numerical notation, for example 17-gon and 257-gon.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon#cite_note-mathworld-17)

Exceptions exist for side counts that are easily expressed in verbal form (e.g. 20 and 30), or are used by non-mathematicians. Some special polygons also have their own names; for example the [regular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polygon "Regular polygon")[star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_polygon "Star polygon")[pentagon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentagon "Pentagon") is also known as the [pentagram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentagram "Pentagram").

History
-------

[![Image 21](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Fotothek_df_tg_0003352_Geometrie_%5E_Dreieck_%5E_Viereck_%5E_Vieleck_%5E_Winkel.jpg/250px-Fotothek_df_tg_0003352_Geometrie_%5E_Dreieck_%5E_Viereck_%5E_Vieleck_%5E_Winkel.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Fotothek_df_tg_0003352_Geometrie_%5E_Dreieck_%5E_Viereck_%5E_Vieleck_%5E_Winkel.jpg)

Historical image of polygons (1699)

Polygons have been known since ancient times. The [regular polygons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polygon "Regular polygon") were known to the ancient Greeks, with the [pentagram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentagram "Pentagram"), a non-convex regular polygon ([star polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_polygon "Star polygon")), appearing as early as the 7th century B.C. on a [krater](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Krater "Krater") by [Aristophanes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristophanes_(vase_painter) "Aristophanes (vase painter)"), found at [Caere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caere "Caere") and now in the [Capitoline Museum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capitoline_Museum "Capitoline Museum").[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon#cite_note-40)[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon#cite_note-41)

The first known systematic study of non-convex polygons in general was made by [Thomas Bradwardine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Bradwardine "Thomas Bradwardine") in the 14th century.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon#cite_note-42)

In 1952, [Geoffrey Colin Shephard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geoffrey_Colin_Shephard "Geoffrey Colin Shephard") generalized the idea of polygons to the complex plane, where each [real](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number") dimension is accompanied by an [imaginary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imaginary_number "Imaginary number") one, to create [complex polygons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_polytope "Complex polytope").[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon#cite_note-43)

In nature
---------

[![Image 22](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Giants_causeway_closeup.jpg/250px-Giants_causeway_closeup.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Giants_causeway_closeup.jpg)

The [Giant's Causeway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giant%27s_Causeway "Giant's Causeway"), in [Northern Ireland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northern_Ireland "Northern Ireland")

Polygons appear in rock formations, most commonly as the flat facets of [crystals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystal "Crystal"), where the angles between the sides depend on the type of mineral from which the crystal is made.

Regular hexagons can occur when the cooling of [lava](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lava "Lava") forms areas of tightly packed columns of [basalt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basalt "Basalt"), which may be seen at the [Giant's Causeway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giant%27s_Causeway "Giant's Causeway") in [Northern Ireland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northern_Ireland "Northern Ireland"), or at the [Devil's Postpile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Devil%27s_Postpile "Devil's Postpile") in [California](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/California "California").

In [biology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology "Biology"), the surface of the wax [honeycomb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Honeycomb "Honeycomb") made by [bees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bee "Bee") is an array of [hexagons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hexagon "Hexagon"), and the sides and base of each cell are also polygons.

Computer graphics
-----------------

In [computer graphics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_graphics "Computer graphics"), a polygon is a [primitive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_primitive "Geometric primitive") used in modelling and rendering. They are defined in a database, containing arrays of [vertices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_(computer_graphics) "Vertex (computer graphics)") (the coordinates of the [geometrical vertices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_(geometry) "Vertex (geometry)"), as well as other attributes of the polygon, such as color, shading and texture), connectivity information, and [materials](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Material_(computer_graphics)&action=edit&redlink=1 "Material (computer graphics) (page does not exist)").[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon#cite_note-44)[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon#cite_note-45)

Any surface is modelled as a tessellation called [polygon mesh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon_mesh "Polygon mesh"). If a square mesh has _n_ + 1 points (vertices) per side, there are _n_ squared squares in the mesh, or 2 _n_ squared triangles since there are two triangles in a square. There are (_n_ + 1)2 / 2(_n_ 2) vertices per triangle. Where _n_ is large, this approaches one half. Or, each vertex inside the square mesh connects four edges (lines).

The imaging system calls up the structure of polygons needed for the scene to be created from the database. This is transferred to active memory and finally, to the display system (screen, TV monitors etc.) so that the scene can be viewed. During this process, the imaging system renders polygons in correct perspective ready for transmission of the processed data to the display system. Although polygons are two-dimensional, through the system computer they are placed in a visual scene in the correct three-dimensional orientation.

In computer graphics and [computational geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computational_geometry "Computational geometry"), it is often necessary to determine whether a given point ![Image 23: {\displaystyle P=(x_{0},y_{0})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a29b6070ac1b7ca23c822ac47b0b1bf4593ab526) lies inside a simple polygon given by a sequence of line segments. This is called the [point in polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_in_polygon "Point in polygon") test.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon#cite_note-46)
