From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

[![Image 1](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Classical_mechanics_roadmap.svg/250px-Classical_mechanics_roadmap.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Classical_mechanics_roadmap.svg)

Variational principles for classical mechanics was developed in parallel with Newtonian mechanics.

In [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics"), a **[variational principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variational_principle "Variational principle")** is an alternative method for determining the state or dynamics of a physical system, by identifying it as an extremum (minimum, maximum or saddle point) of a function or functional. Variational methods are exploited in many modern software applications to [simulate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simulation "Simulation") matter and light.

Since the development of [analytical mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytical_mechanics "Analytical mechanics") in the 18th century, the fundamental equations of physics have usually been established in terms of [action principles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stationary-action_principle "Stationary-action principle"), where the variational principle is applied to the [action](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_(physics) "Action (physics)") of a system in order to recover the fundamental equation of motion.

This article describes the historical development of such action principles and other variational methods applied in physics. See [History of physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_physics "History of physics") for an overview and [Outline of the history of physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outline_of_the_history_of_physics "Outline of the history of physics") for related histories.

Variational principles are found among earlier ideas in [surveying](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surveying "Surveying") and [optics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optics "Optics"). The [rope stretchers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rope_stretchers "Rope stretchers") of [ancient Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Egypt "Ancient Egypt") stretched corded ropes between two points to measure the path which minimized the distance of separation, and [Claudius Ptolemy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Claudius_Ptolemy "Claudius Ptolemy"), in his [Geographia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geographia_(Ptolemy) "Geographia (Ptolemy)") (Bk 1, Ch 2), emphasized that one must correct for "deviations from a straight course"; in [ancient Greece](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greece "Ancient Greece")[Euclid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid "Euclid") states in his _Catoptrica_ that, for the path of light reflecting from a mirror, the [angle of incidence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle_of_incidence_(optics) "Angle of incidence (optics)") equals the [angle of reflection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle_of_reflection "Angle of reflection"); and [Hero of Alexandria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hero_of_Alexandria "Hero of Alexandria") later showed that this path was the shortest length and least time.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_variational_principles_in_physics#cite_note-Kline1972-1): 580

First variational principles
----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=History_of_variational_principles_in_physics&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: First variational principles")]

### Principle of virtual work

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=History_of_variational_principles_in_physics&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Principle of virtual work")]

In the static analysis of objects under forces but fixed at [mechanical equilibrium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanical_equilibrium "Mechanical equilibrium"), the [principle of virtual work](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principle_of_virtual_work "Principle of virtual work") imagines tiny mathematical shifts away from equilibrium. Each shift does work—energy lost or gained—against the forces, but the sum of all these bits of virtual work must be zero. This principle was developed by [Johann Bernoulli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johann_Bernoulli "Johann Bernoulli") in a letter to [Pierre Varignon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre_Varignon "Pierre Varignon") in 1715, but never separately published.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_variational_principles_in_physics#cite_note-Coopersmith2017-2): 23 [Cornelius Lanczos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cornelius_Lanczos "Cornelius Lanczos") uses a slightly different definition as the single postulate for all analytic mechanics, showing thereby the power of energy based variational principles over Newtonian mechanics.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_variational_principles_in_physics#cite_note-Coopersmith2017-2): 87

### D'Alembert's principle

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=History_of_variational_principles_in_physics&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: D'Alembert's principle")]

In 1743 [Jean le Rond d'Alembert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_le_Rond_d%27Alembert "Jean le Rond d'Alembert") generalized the concept we now call [virtual work](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virtual_work "Virtual work") to dynamical systems with rigid constraints, like rods or string under tension, a form that became known as the [d'Alembert principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D%27Alembert_principle "D'Alembert principle").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_variational_principles_in_physics#cite_note-3): 190  In the case of static (in equilibrium) rigid bodies without friction, the principle of virtual work says the net work of all applied forces (![Image 2: {\displaystyle F_{i}^{(a)}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7ecdec1e0cd6c94f9917d3d189a7a2a55c09e5f4)) under variation of positions (![Image 3: {\displaystyle r_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a0b6d651eaf432dbf1f106021c8bb499ae83fd1f)) is zero:

![Image 4: {\displaystyle \Sigma _{i}F_{i}^{(a)}\cdot \delta r_{i}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8f891a0002b4e546618695416643f392a044527b)
A similar condition but valid for dynamics (systems in motion) introduces, for each force, the change in momentum ![Image 5: {\displaystyle {\dot {p}}_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/06d1fdf9542f461704e440cd1a76d8206f36a17b):

![Image 6: {\displaystyle \Sigma _{i}(F_{i}-{\dot {p}}_{i})\cdot \delta r_{i}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/94798925a55b58aac01fe718554fe03eb0ba977b)
which is d'Alembert's principle.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_variational_principles_in_physics#cite_note-Goldstein3-4): 17

### Principle of least time

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=History_of_variational_principles_in_physics&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Principle of least time")]

The earlier geometrical ideas in optics were generalized by [Pierre de Fermat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre_de_Fermat "Pierre de Fermat"), who, in the 17th century, refined the principle to "light travels between two given points along the path of shortest _time_"; now known as the principle of least time or [Fermat's principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermat%27s_principle "Fermat's principle"). Fermat showed that principle predicts the observed law of [refraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Refraction "Refraction"). His approach was metaphysical, arguing that Nature acts simply and economically.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_variational_principles_in_physics#cite_note-Kline1972-1): 580

### The brachystochrone problem

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=History_of_variational_principles_in_physics&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: The brachystochrone problem")]

[![Image 7](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Brachistochrone.gif/250px-Brachistochrone.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Brachistochrone.gif)

The brachistochrone problem. The path of the least time shown in red.

[![Image 8](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/18/Path_function_2.PNG)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Path_function_2.PNG)

Techniques based on small variations in the path of motion grew out of analysis of the brachistochrone problem.

In 1696 [Johann Bernoulli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johann_Bernoulli "Johann Bernoulli") posed a puzzle to European mathematicians: derive a curve for motion of a frictionless bead falling between a higher and a lower point in the least possible time. He named the curve the "[brachistochrone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brachistochrone "Brachistochrone")", (from _brachystos_, "shortest", and _chronos_, "time")[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_variational_principles_in_physics#cite_note-Goldstine-5): 31 [Isaac Newton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaac_Newton "Isaac Newton"), [Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gottfried_Wilhelm_Leibniz "Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz") and others contributed solutions, and in 1718 Johann Bernoulli published an analysis based on the solution created by his brother [James Bernoulli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Bernoulli "James Bernoulli"). All of these works, especially the approach taken by the Bernoullis, involved reasoning about small deviations in the path taken by the falling bead. Thus this became the first application of the variational technique, albeit as a special-case rather than a general principle.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_variational_principles_in_physics#cite_note-Goldstine-5): 68

### Principle of least action

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=History_of_variational_principles_in_physics&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Principle of least action")]

In 1744[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_variational_principles_in_physics#cite_note-6) and 1746,[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_variational_principles_in_physics#cite_note-7)[Pierre Louis Maupertuis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre_Louis_Maupertuis "Pierre Louis Maupertuis") generalized Fermat's concept to mechanics,[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_variational_principles_in_physics#cite_note-Whittaker-8): 97  in the form of a [principle of least action](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stationary-action_principle "Stationary-action principle"). Maupertuis argued metaphysically, he felt that "Nature is thrifty in all its actions", and applied the principle broadly:

> The laws of movement and of rest deduced from this principle being precisely the same as those observed in nature, we can admire the application of it to all phenomena. The movement of animals, the vegetative growth of plants ... are only its consequences; and the spectacle of the universe becomes so much the grander, so much more beautiful, the worthier of its Author, when one knows that a small number of laws, most wisely established, suffice for all movements.

— Pierre Louis Maupertuis[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_variational_principles_in_physics#cite_note-9)

This notion of Maupertuis, although somewhat deterministic today, does capture much of the essence of variational mechanics.

In application to physics, Maupertuis suggested that the quantity to be minimized was the product of the duration (time) of movement within a system by the [action](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_(physics) "Action (physics)"); his definitions of action varied with the problems he discussed.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_variational_principles_in_physics#cite_note-Kline1972-1): 581  One form he used was called "[vis viva](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vis_viva "Vis viva")",

**Maupertuis' principle**

![Image 9: {\displaystyle \delta \int 2T(t)dt=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e954e29ffa694e3befe0f47fbdaef253752f6f6f)

which is the integral of twice what we now call the [kinetic energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_energy "Kinetic energy")_T_ of the system.

[Leonhard Euler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leonhard_Euler "Leonhard Euler") corresponded with Maupertuis from 1740 to 1744;[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_variational_principles_in_physics#cite_note-Kline1972-1): 582  in 1744 Euler proposed a refined formulation of the least action principle in 1744.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_variational_principles_in_physics#cite_note-10) He writes[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_variational_principles_in_physics#cite_note-11)

"Let the mass of the projectile be _M_, and let its squared velocity resulting from its height be ![Image 10: {\displaystyle v}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e07b00e7fc0847fbd16391c778d65bc25c452597) while being moved over a distance _ds_. The body will have a momentum ![Image 11: {\displaystyle M{\sqrt {v}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a1ac31ec70334c08b873f03df7ec035f5d42d8de) that, when multiplied by the distance _ds_, will give ![Image 12: {\displaystyle Mds{\sqrt {v}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c257f088a9994c0847b51b8f2bd9dda024184f9b), the momentum of the body integrated over the distance _ds_. Now I assert that the curve thus described by the body to be the curve (from among all other curves connecting the same endpoints) that minimizes ![Image 13: {\displaystyle \int Mds{\sqrt {v}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/629d8f43204639d297c1cb3c90272bbb0470e60b) or, provided that _M_ is constant, ![Image 14: {\displaystyle \int ds{\sqrt {v}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0fa05249ace54fe3c1a538a2dfd6e0eb61018b)."[[Note 1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_variational_principles_in_physics#cite_note-12)
As Euler states, ![Image 15: {\displaystyle \int Mds{\sqrt {v}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/629d8f43204639d297c1cb3c90272bbb0470e60b) is the integral of the momentum over distance traveled (note that here ![Image 16: {\displaystyle v}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e07b00e7fc0847fbd16391c778d65bc25c452597) contrary to usual notation denotes the _squared_ velocity) which, in modern notation, equals the [abbreviated action](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_(physics)#abbreviated_action "Action (physics)"):[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_variational_principles_in_physics#cite_note-Goldstein3-4): 359

**Euler's principle**

![Image 17: {\displaystyle \delta \int p\,dq=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/33982758effb01c2221e2fe202eec2886f472b5d)

In rather general terms he wrote that "Since the fabric of the Universe is most perfect and is the work of a most wise Creator, nothing whatsoever takes place in the Universe in which some relation of maximum and minimum does not appear."

Euler continued to write on the topic; in his _Reflexions sur quelques loix generales de la nature_ (1748), he called the quantity "effort". His expression corresponds to what we would now call [potential energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potential_energy "Potential energy"), so that his statement of least action in statics is equivalent to the principle that a system of bodies at rest will adopt a configuration that minimizes total potential energy.

### Lagrangian mechanics

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=History_of_variational_principles_in_physics&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Lagrangian mechanics")]

The first use of the term "method of variations" came in 1755 through the work of a young [Joseph Louis Lagrange](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Louis_Lagrange "Joseph Louis Lagrange"); Euler presented Lagrange's approach to the [Berlin Academy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prussian_Academy_of_Sciences "Prussian Academy of Sciences") in 1756 as the "[calculus of variations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus_of_variations "Calculus of variations")". Unlike Euler, Lagrange's approach was purely analytic rather than geometrical. Lagrange introduced the idea of variation of entire curves or paths between the endpoints than of individual coordinates. For this he introduced a new form of a differential, written ![Image 18: {\displaystyle \delta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c5321cfa797202b3e1f8620663ff43c4660ea03a), that acts on integrals rather than ![Image 19: {\displaystyle d}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e85ff03cbe0c7341af6b982e47e9f90d235c66ab) acting on coordinates.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_variational_principles_in_physics#cite_note-Goldstine-5): 111  His notation continues to be used today.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_variational_principles_in_physics#cite_note-Kline1972-1): 583

### Hamilton-Jacobi mechanics

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=History_of_variational_principles_in_physics&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Hamilton-Jacobi mechanics")]

The variational principle was not used to derive the equations of motion until almost 75 years later, when [William Rowan Hamilton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Rowan_Hamilton "William Rowan Hamilton") in 1834 and 1835[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_variational_principles_in_physics#cite_note-13) applied the variational principle to the Lagrangian function ![Image 20: {\displaystyle L=T-V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/00644a2f8d4ddaa3b1ae36d472aabaeb63e4d9dc) (where _T_ is the kinetic energy and _V_ the potential energy of an object) to obtain what are now called the [Euler–Lagrange equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler%E2%80%93Lagrange_equation "Euler–Lagrange equation"). Hamilton believed his results were constrained by conservation of energy, which he called conservation of living force.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_variational_principles_in_physics#cite_note-NakaneFraser-14): 163

While few German scientists read English papers in this era, in 1836 the German mathematician [Carl Gustav Jacobi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Gustav_Jacobi "Carl Gustav Jacobi") read of Hamilton's work and immediately began new mathematical work, publishing ground breaking work on the variational principle in the following year.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_variational_principles_in_physics#cite_note-15) Among Jacobi's results was the extension of Hamilton's method to time-dependent potentials (or "force functions" as they were known at that time).[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_variational_principles_in_physics#cite_note-NakaneFraser-14): 201

### Extensions by Gauss and Hertz

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=History_of_variational_principles_in_physics&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Extensions by Gauss and Hertz")]

Other extremal principles of [classical mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_mechanics "Classical mechanics") were formulated, such as [Carl Friedrich Gauss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Friedrich_Gauss "Carl Friedrich Gauss")'s 1829 [principle of least constraint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauss%27s_principle_of_least_constraint "Gauss's principle of least constraint") and its corollary, [Heinrich Hertz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heinrich_Hertz "Heinrich Hertz")'s 1896 [principle of least curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hertz_principle_of_least_curvature "Hertz principle of least curvature").

Modern action principles
------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=History_of_variational_principles_in_physics&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Modern action principles")]

In 1915 [David Hilbert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Hilbert "David Hilbert") applied variational principles to derive the [gravitational field equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_field_equations "Einstein field equations") of [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity") in agreement with [Albert Einstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Einstein "Albert Einstein")'s derivation.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_variational_principles_in_physics#cite_note-MehraEinsteinHilbert-23) (Einstein and Hilbert discussed Einstein's work on general relativity in person and letters throughout 1915.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_variational_principles_in_physics#cite_note-Straumann-24)) Hilbert's approach required accepting the variational principle as "axiomatic", a broadly accepted requirement today but questionable to the physicists of 1915. Hilbert's variations were based on what became known as the [Einstein–Hilbert action](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein%E2%80%93Hilbert_action "Einstein–Hilbert action"), given by

![Image 30: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {S}}[g]={\frac {1}{2\kappa }}\int R{\sqrt {-g}}\,\mathrm {d} ^{4}x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ad4450861e4d24359c8b0955851e50c783ead335),
where _κ_ is [Einstein gravitational constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_gravitational_constant "Einstein gravitational constant"), ![Image 31: {\displaystyle g=\det(g_{\alpha \beta })}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e7741db09090aed4f5699f1e0023b5b3ccc84618) is the determinant of a spacetime [Lorentz metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentz_metric "Lorentz metric") and ![Image 32: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) is the [scalar curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_curvature "Scalar curvature").

### In quantum mechanics

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=History_of_variational_principles_in_physics&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: In quantum mechanics")]

Variational principles played decisive roles at critical times in the development of quantum mechanics.

Following [Max Planck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Max_Planck "Max Planck")'s proposal that quantum radiators explain the [blackbody radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blackbody_radiation "Blackbody radiation") spectrum and [Albert Einstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Einstein "Albert Einstein") hypothesis of quantum radiation to explain the [photoelectric effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect "Photoelectric effect"), [Niels Bohr](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Niels_Bohr "Niels Bohr") proposed quantized energy levels for the orbits in his model of the atom, thereby explaining the [Balmer series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Balmer_series "Balmer series") for absorption of radiation by atoms. However this hypothesis involved no mechanical model. [Arnold Sommerfeld](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arnold_Sommerfeld "Arnold Sommerfeld") then showed that quantization of the action of orbits for Hydrogen predicted the Balmer series, complete with relativistic corrections leading to [fine structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fine_structure "Fine structure") in spectral lines. However, this approach could not be extended to atoms with more electrons and, more fundamentally, the quantum hypothesis itself had no explanation from this classical mechanics solution.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_variational_principles_in_physics#cite_note-YourgrauMandelstam1979-22): 97

#### Schrödinger's equation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=History_of_variational_principles_in_physics&action=edit&section=17 "Edit section: Schrödinger's equation")]

Combining Einstein's relativity and photoelectric effect results, [De Broglie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_Broglie "De Broglie") suggested that Sommerfeld's quantized action may relate to quantized wave effects; [Erwin Schrödinger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erwin_Schr%C3%B6dinger "Erwin Schrödinger") took up this idea, applying [Hamilton's optico-mechanical analogy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamilton%27s_optico-mechanical_analogy "Hamilton's optico-mechanical analogy") to connect the quantized action to [Hamilton-Jacobi equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamilton-Jacobi_equations "Hamilton-Jacobi equations") for the action. Hamilton's connection between light rays and light waves now became a connection between matter trajectories and de Broglie [matter waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matter_waves "Matter waves").[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_variational_principles_in_physics#cite_note-YourgrauMandelstam1979-22): 119  The resulting [Schrödinger equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schr%C3%B6dinger_equation "Schrödinger equation") became the first successful quantum mechanics.

#### Dirac's quantum action

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=History_of_variational_principles_in_physics&action=edit&section=18 "Edit section: Dirac's quantum action")]

The work that built on Schrödinger's equation relied on analogies to [Hamiltonian mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamiltonian_mechanics "Hamiltonian mechanics"). In 1933 [Paul Dirac](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Dirac "Paul Dirac") published a paper seeking an alternative formulation based on [Lagrangian mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrangian_mechanics "Lagrangian mechanics"). He was motivated by the power of the action principle and the relativistic invariance of the action itself.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_variational_principles_in_physics#cite_note-25) Dirac was able to show that the wavefunctions probability amplitude at ![Image 33: {\displaystyle x_{1},t_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0e78817658f1547cd3a33a70ac052b42bf5d82ab) was related to the amplitude at ![Image 34: {\displaystyle x_{2},t_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/55e4769fef5d422cd7969bb431666820b3c74d61) through a complex exponential function of the action.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_variational_principles_in_physics#cite_note-MerhaRechenberg-26): 1025

#### Feynman's least action mechanics

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=History_of_variational_principles_in_physics&action=edit&section=19 "Edit section: Feynman's least action mechanics")]

In 1942, nearly a decade after Dirac's work, [Richard Feynman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Feynman "Richard Feynman") built a new quantum mechanics [formulation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Path_integral_formulation "Path integral formulation") on the action principle. Feynman interpreted Dirac's formula as a physical recipe for the probability amplitude contributions from every possible path between ![Image 35: {\displaystyle x_{1},t_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0e78817658f1547cd3a33a70ac052b42bf5d82ab) and ![Image 36: {\displaystyle x_{2},t_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/55e4769fef5d422cd7969bb431666820b3c74d61). These possibilities [interfere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wave_interference "Wave interference"); constructive interference gives the paths with the most amplitude. In the classical limit with large values of action compared to ![Image 37: {\displaystyle h}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b26be3e694314bc90c3215047e4a2010c6ee184a), the single classical path given by the action principle results.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_variational_principles_in_physics#cite_note-MerhaRechenberg-26): 1027

#### Schwinger's quantum action principle

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=History_of_variational_principles_in_physics&action=edit&section=20 "Edit section: Schwinger's quantum action principle")]

In 1950, [Julian Schwinger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Julian_Schwinger "Julian Schwinger") revisited Dirac's Lagrangian paper to develop the action principle in a different direction.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_variational_principles_in_physics#cite_note-MerhaRechenberg-26): 1082  Unlike Feynman's focus on paths, Schwinger's approach was "differential" or local.

### In particle physics

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=History_of_variational_principles_in_physics&action=edit&section=21 "Edit section: In particle physics")]

The [Standard Model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model "Standard Model") is defined in terms of a [Lagrangian density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrangian_density "Lagrangian density") that includes all known [elementary particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementary_particle "Elementary particle"), the [Higgs field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Higgs_field "Higgs field") and three of the [fundamental interactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_interaction "Fundamental interaction") ([electromagnetism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetism "Electromagnetism"), [weak interaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weak_interaction "Weak interaction") and [strong interaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strong_interaction "Strong interaction"), not including gravitational interaction). Its formulation started in the 1970s and has successfully explained almost all experimental results related to microscopic physics.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_variational_principles_in_physics#cite_note-27)

Teleology in action principles
------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=History_of_variational_principles_in_physics&action=edit&section=22 "Edit section: Teleology in action principles")]

The breadth of physical phenomena subject to study by action principles lead scientists from all centuries to view these concepts as especially fundamental; the connection of two points by paths lead some to suggest a "purpose" to the selection of one particular path.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_variational_principles_in_physics#cite_note-St%C3%B6ltzner1994-28) This [teleological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teleology "Teleology") viewpoint runs from the earliest physics through Fermat, Maupertuis, and on up to [Max Planck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Max_Planck "Max Planck"), without, however, any scientific backing.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_variational_principles_in_physics#cite_note-YourgrauMandelstam1979-22): 162  The use of colorful language continues in the modern era with phrases like "Nature's command (to) _Explore all paths!_"[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_variational_principles_in_physics#cite_note-29) or "It isn't that a particle takes the path of least action but that it smells all the paths in the neighborhood...".[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_variational_principles_in_physics#cite_note-FeynmanII-18): II:19

Variational methods
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=History_of_variational_principles_in_physics&action=edit&section=23 "Edit section: Variational methods")]

### Ritz's work on elasticity and waves

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=History_of_variational_principles_in_physics&action=edit&section=24 "Edit section: Ritz's work on elasticity and waves")]

[Lord Rayleigh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_William_Strutt,_3rd_Baron_Rayleigh "John William Strutt, 3rd Baron Rayleigh") was the first to popularly adapt the variational principles for the search of [eigenvalues and eigenvectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eigenvalues_and_eigenvectors "Eigenvalues and eigenvectors") for the study of [elasticity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elasticity_(physics) "Elasticity (physics)") and classical [waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wave "Wave") in his 1877 _Theory of Sound_.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_variational_principles_in_physics#cite_note-:0-30) The Rayleigh method allows approximation of the fundamental frequencies without full knowledge of the material composition and without the requirement of computational power.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_variational_principles_in_physics#cite_note-:0-30) From 1903 to 1908, [Walther Ritz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Walther_Ritz "Walther Ritz") introduced a series of improved methods for static and free vibration problems based on the optimization of an [ansatz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ansatz "Ansatz") or trial function. Ritz demonstrated his use in the [Euler–Bernoulli beam theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler%E2%80%93Bernoulli_beam_theory "Euler–Bernoulli beam theory") and the determination of [Chladni figures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chladni_Figures "Chladni Figures").[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_variational_principles_in_physics#cite_note-:0-30)

For years, Ritz works were poorly cited in Western Europe and would only become popular after Ritz death in 1909.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_variational_principles_in_physics#cite_note-:1-31) In Russia, physicists like [Ivan Bubnov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ivan_Bubnov "Ivan Bubnov") (in 1913) and [Boris Galerkin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boris_Galerkin "Boris Galerkin") (in 1915) would rediscover and popularize some of Ritz's methods from 1908. In 1940, Georgii I. Petrov improved these approximations.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_variational_principles_in_physics#cite_note-:1-31) These methods are now known under different names, including Bubnov–Galerkin, [Petrov–Galerkin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petrov%E2%80%93Galerkin_method "Petrov–Galerkin method") and Ritz–Galerkin methods.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_variational_principles_in_physics#cite_note-:0-30)

In 1911, Rayleigh complemented Ritz for his method for solving Chladni's problem, but complained for the lack of citation of his earlier work. However the similarity between Rayleigh's and Ritz's method has sometimes been challenged.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_variational_principles_in_physics#cite_note-:0-30)[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_variational_principles_in_physics#cite_note-32)[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_variational_principles_in_physics#cite_note-:1-31) Ritz's methods are sometimes referred as [Rayleigh–Ritz method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rayleigh%E2%80%93Ritz_method "Rayleigh–Ritz method") or simply [Ritz method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ritz_method "Ritz method"), depending on the procedure.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_variational_principles_in_physics#cite_note-:0-30)[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_variational_principles_in_physics#cite_note-:1-31) Ritz's method led to the development of [finite element method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_element_method "Finite element method") for the numerical solution of [partial differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_differential_equation "Partial differential equation") in physics.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_variational_principles_in_physics#cite_note-:1-31)

### For quantum systems

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=History_of_variational_principles_in_physics&action=edit&section=25 "Edit section: For quantum systems")]

The variational method of Ritz would found his use [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics") with the development of [Hellmann–Feynman theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hellmann%E2%80%93Feynman_theorem "Hellmann–Feynman theorem"). The theorem was first discussed by Schrödinger in 1926, the first proof was given by Paul Güttinger in 1932, and later rediscovered independently by [Wolfgang Pauli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Pauli "Wolfgang Pauli") and [Hans Hellmann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hans_Hellmann "Hans Hellmann") in 1933, and by Feynman in 1939.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

In [quantum chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_chemistry "Quantum chemistry") and [condensed matter physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics "Condensed matter physics"), [variational methods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variational_method_(quantum_mechanics) "Variational method (quantum mechanics)") were developed to study atoms, molecules, nuclei and solids under a quantum mechanical framework. Some of these include the use of Ritz methods for the determination of the spectra of the [helium atom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_atom "Helium atom"), 1930 [Hartree–Fock method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hartree%E2%80%93Fock_method "Hartree–Fock method"), 1964 [density functional theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Density_functional_theory "Density functional theory") and [variational Monte Carlo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variational_Monte_Carlo "Variational Monte Carlo") and 1992 [density matrix renormalization group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Density_matrix_renormalization_group "Density matrix renormalization group") (DMRG).[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

In 2014, variational principles were part of a hybrid strategy, called [noisy intermediate-scale quantum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noisy_intermediate-scale_quantum_era "Noisy intermediate-scale quantum era") (NISQ) computing, to combine powerful but imperfect quantum computers coupled with classical computers.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_variational_principles_in_physics#cite_note-33) The first proposals included a [variational quantum eigensolver](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variational_quantum_eigensolver "Variational quantum eigensolver")[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_variational_principles_in_physics#cite_note-34) to exploit quantum phenomena to simulate atoms and small molecules using variational methods and an approximate optimization algorithm.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_variational_principles_in_physics#cite_note-35)[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_variational_principles_in_physics#cite_note-36)
