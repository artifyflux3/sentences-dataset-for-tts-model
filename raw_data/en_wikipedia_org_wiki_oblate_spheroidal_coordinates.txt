Figure 1: Coordinate isosurfaces for a point _P_ (shown as a black sphere) in oblate spheroidal coordinates (_μ_, _ν_, _φ_). The _z_-axis is vertical, and the foci are at±2. The red oblate spheroid (flattened sphere) corresponds to _μ_ = 1, whereas the blue half-hyperboloid corresponds to _ν_ = 45°. The [Cartesian coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system "Cartesian coordinate system") of **P** are roughly (1.09, −1.89, 1.66).

**Oblate spheroidal coordinates** are a three-dimensional [orthogonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_coordinates "Orthogonal coordinates")[coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_system "Coordinate system") that results from rotating the two-dimensional [elliptic coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptic_coordinates "Elliptic coordinates") about the non-focal axis of the ellipse, i.e., the symmetry axis that separates the foci. Thus, the two foci are transformed into a ring of radius ![Image 2: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc) in the _x_-_y_ plane. (Rotation about the other axis produces [prolate spheroidal coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prolate_spheroidal_coordinates "Prolate spheroidal coordinates").) Oblate spheroidal coordinates can also be considered as a [limiting case](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limiting_case_(mathematics) "Limiting case (mathematics)") of [ellipsoidal coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipsoidal_coordinates "Ellipsoidal coordinates") in which the two largest [semi-axes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semi-axis "Semi-axis") are equal in length.

Oblate spheroidal coordinates are often useful in solving [partial differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_differential_equation "Partial differential equation") when the boundary conditions are defined on an [oblate spheroid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oblate_spheroid "Oblate spheroid") or a [hyperboloid of revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperboloid "Hyperboloid"). For example, they played an important role in the calculation of the [Perrin friction factors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perrin_friction_factors "Perrin friction factors"), which contributed to the awarding of the 1926 [Nobel Prize in Physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physics "Nobel Prize in Physics") to [Jean Baptiste Perrin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_Baptiste_Perrin "Jean Baptiste Perrin"). These friction factors determine the [rotational diffusion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_diffusion "Rotational diffusion") of molecules, which affects the feasibility of many techniques such as [protein NMR](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protein_NMR "Protein NMR") and from which the hydrodynamic volume and shape of molecules can be inferred. Oblate spheroidal coordinates are also useful in problems of electromagnetism (e.g., dielectric constant of charged oblate molecules), acoustics (e.g., scattering of sound through a circular hole), fluid dynamics (e.g., the flow of water through a firehose nozzle), the diffusion of materials and heat (e.g., cooling of a red-hot coin in a water bath), and relativistic physics, such as the spacetime distortion around [rotating black holes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kerr_metric "Kerr metric").

Figure 2: Plot of the oblate spheroidal coordinates μ and ν in the _x_-_z_ plane, where φ is zero and _a_ equals one. The curves of constant _μ_ form red ellipses, whereas those of constant _ν_ form cyan half-hyperbolae in this plane. The _z_-axis runs vertically and separates the foci; the coordinates _z_ and ν always have the same sign. The most common definition of oblate spheroidal coordinates ![Image 4: {\displaystyle (\mu ,\nu ,\varphi )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/883c554397980b4faad844056ec12cce80c4452d) is ![Image 5: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}x&=a\ \cosh \mu \ \cos \nu \ \cos \varphi \\y&=a\ \cosh \mu \ \cos \nu \ \sin \varphi \\z&=a\ \sinh \mu \ \sin \nu \end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b7452fcff1a931577c11824488f4bdcaca07e18b)

where ![Image 6: {\displaystyle \mu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9fd47b2a39f7a7856952afec1f1db72c67af6161) is a nonnegative real number and the angle ![Image 7: {\displaystyle \nu \in \left[-\pi /2,\pi /2\right]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/794f2b514a169668de4bd0738c71118a7389f8bc). The azimuthal angle ![Image 8: {\displaystyle \varphi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/33ee699558d09cf9d653f6351f9fda0b2f4aaa3e) can fall anywhere on a full circle, between ![Image 9: {\displaystyle \pm \pi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/526d4dfa032be40e7af5c8e7e27132397765fb36). These coordinates are favored over the alternatives below because they are not degenerate; the set of coordinates ![Image 10: {\displaystyle (\mu ,\nu ,\varphi )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/883c554397980b4faad844056ec12cce80c4452d) describes a unique point in Cartesian coordinates ![Image 11: {\displaystyle (x,y,z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/22a8c93372e8f8b6e24d523bd5545aed3430baf4). The reverse is also true, except on the ![Image 12: {\displaystyle z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf368e72c009decd9b6686ee84a375632e11de98)-axis and the disk in the ![Image 13: {\displaystyle xy}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c72eb345e496513fb8b2fa4aa8c4d89b855f9a01)-plane inside the focal ring.

### Coordinate surfaces

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Oblate_spheroidal_coordinates&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Coordinate surfaces")]

The surfaces of constant μ form [oblate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oblate_spheroid "Oblate spheroid")[spheroids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spheroids "Spheroids"), by the trigonometric identity ![Image 14: {\displaystyle {\frac {x^{2}+y^{2}}{a^{2}\cosh ^{2}\mu }}+{\frac {z^{2}}{a^{2}\sinh ^{2}\mu }}=\cos ^{2}\nu +\sin ^{2}\nu =1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/229e2c7fd0468e890744a263784ea8797be45f2e)

since they are [ellipses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse "Ellipse") rotated about the _z_-axis, which separates their foci. An ellipse in the _x_-_z_ plane (Figure 2) has a [major semiaxis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semi-major_axis "Semi-major axis") of length _a_ cosh μ along the _x_-axis, whereas its [minor semiaxis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semi-minor_axis "Semi-minor axis") has length _a_ sinh μ along the _z_-axis. The foci of all the ellipses in the _x_-_z_ plane are located on the _x_-axis at ±_a_.

Similarly, the surfaces of constant ν form one-sheet half [hyperboloids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperboloid "Hyperboloid") of revolution by the hyperbolic trigonometric identity

![Image 15: {\displaystyle {\frac {x^{2}+y^{2}}{a^{2}\cos ^{2}\nu }}-{\frac {z^{2}}{a^{2}\sin ^{2}\nu }}=\cosh ^{2}\mu -\sinh ^{2}\mu =1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a3226388cf49ad35233e7eaedfa73adce88d268f)

For positive ν, the half-hyperboloid is above the _x_-_y_ plane (i.e., has positive _z_) whereas for negative ν, the half-hyperboloid is below the _x_-_y_ plane (i.e., has negative _z_). Geometrically, the angle ν corresponds to the angle of the [asymptotes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asymptote "Asymptote") of the hyperbola. The foci of all the hyperbolae are likewise located on the _x_-axis at ±_a_.

### Inverse transformation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Oblate_spheroidal_coordinates&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Inverse transformation")]

The (μ, ν, φ) coordinates may be calculated from the Cartesian coordinates (_x_, _y_, _z_) as follows. The azimuthal angle φ is given by the formula ![Image 16: {\displaystyle \tan \phi ={\frac {y}{x}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e4759fd030edbc60fe8fceab8681a50d45f8cd57)

The cylindrical radius ρ of the point P is given by ![Image 17: {\displaystyle \rho ^{2}=x^{2}+y^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ba4a1eb59b3df89c2a9de68ae9411c844fe6c8b4) and its distances to the foci in the plane defined by φ is given by ![Image 18: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}d_{1}^{2}=(\rho +a)^{2}+z^{2}\\d_{2}^{2}=(\rho -a)^{2}+z^{2}\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0da965d0d603ff7a3724048d1fc4d795e68a6622)

The remaining coordinates μ and ν can be calculated from the equations ![Image 19: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\cosh \mu &={\frac {d_{1}+d_{2}}{2a}}\\\cos \nu &={\frac {d_{1}-d_{2}}{2a}}\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/20ad258b83683f7e9d2c23541a326672be11d2a3)

where the sign of μ is always non-negative, and the sign of ν is the same as that of _z_.

Another method to compute the inverse transform is

![Image 20: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\mu &=\operatorname {Re} \operatorname {arcosh} {\frac {\rho +zi}{a}}\\\nu &=\operatorname {Im} \operatorname {arcosh} {\frac {\rho +zi}{a}}\\\phi &=\arctan {\frac {y}{x}}\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fbe500556bbaa2540ea3d25223e1365fd0149587)

where ![Image 21: {\displaystyle \rho ={\sqrt {x^{2}+y^{2}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6639197208572a96358af21debdbdaf96ae306a4)

The scale factors for the coordinates μ and ν are equal ![Image 22: {\displaystyle h_{\mu }=h_{\nu }=a{\sqrt {\sinh ^{2}\mu +\sin ^{2}\nu }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/83ef5b578241ab1267b6442b0617b1c50a5a96b9) whereas the azimuthal scale factor equals ![Image 23: {\displaystyle h_{\phi }=a\cosh \mu \ \cos \nu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b46767d114562cb7b84f83cf623d7349e38ce6b8)

Consequently, an infinitesimal volume element equals ![Image 24: {\displaystyle dV=a^{3}\cosh \mu \ \cos \nu \ \left(\sinh ^{2}\mu +\sin ^{2}\nu \right)d\mu \,d\nu \,d\phi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6964400d7e36236b48d1030026582fbc3e504caf) and the Laplacian can be written ![Image 25: {\displaystyle \nabla ^{2}\Phi ={\frac {1}{a^{2}\left(\sinh ^{2}\mu +\sin ^{2}\nu \right)}}\left[{\frac {1}{\cosh \mu }}{\frac {\partial }{\partial \mu }}\left(\cosh \mu {\frac {\partial \Phi }{\partial \mu }}\right)+{\frac {1}{\cos \nu }}{\frac {\partial }{\partial \nu }}\left(\cos \nu {\frac {\partial \Phi }{\partial \nu }}\right)\right]+{\frac {1}{a^{2}\cosh ^{2}\mu \cos ^{2}\nu }}{\frac {\partial ^{2}\Phi }{\partial \phi ^{2}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1af95c5adbce0fca4f50966f022afeaf00e9154b)

Other differential operators such as ![Image 26: {\displaystyle \nabla \cdot \mathbf {F} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1f4f5b9e6560d7fdaf1a69d9fdeff6beb1f327cc) and ![Image 27: {\displaystyle \nabla \times \mathbf {F} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c6e2429b7e3cb6965d1a6651703ff5ef0c88b378) can be expressed in the coordinates (μ, ν, φ) by substituting the scale factors into the general formulae found in [orthogonal coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_coordinates "Orthogonal coordinates").

The orthonormal basis vectors for the ![Image 28: {\displaystyle \mu ,\nu ,\phi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/178bd9299b6dd0f7eb7d6a7d217b36bc6396f7d3) coordinate system can be expressed in Cartesian coordinates as

![Image 29: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}{\hat {e}}_{\mu }&={\frac {1}{\sqrt {\sinh ^{2}\mu +\sin ^{2}\nu }}}\left(\sinh \mu \cos \nu \cos \phi {\boldsymbol {\hat {i}}}+\sinh \mu \cos \nu \sin \phi {\boldsymbol {\hat {j}}}+\cosh \mu \sin \nu {\boldsymbol {\hat {k}}}\right)\\{\hat {e}}_{\nu }&={\frac {1}{\sqrt {\sinh ^{2}\mu +\sin ^{2}\nu }}}\left(-\cosh \mu \sin \nu \cos \phi {\boldsymbol {\hat {i}}}-\cosh \mu \sin \nu \sin \phi {\boldsymbol {\hat {j}}}+\sinh \mu \cos \nu {\boldsymbol {\hat {k}}}\right)\\{\hat {e}}_{\phi }&=-\sin \phi {\boldsymbol {\hat {i}}}+\cos \phi {\boldsymbol {\hat {j}}}\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8d21e9cb0cf196079c926ee3e5ec06da1788cbf4)

where ![Image 30: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {\hat {i}}},{\boldsymbol {\hat {j}}},{\boldsymbol {\hat {k}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/96e697b243f2250210c6b8890421543f82728f23) are the Cartesian unit vectors. Here, ![Image 31: {\displaystyle {\hat {e}}_{\mu }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d383f29f7cb47dc055932e887546e749c79f398f) is the outward normal vector to the oblate spheroidal surface of constant ![Image 32: {\displaystyle \mu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9fd47b2a39f7a7856952afec1f1db72c67af6161), ![Image 33: {\displaystyle {\hat {e}}_{\phi }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6c7cb9869eaa145ee1cd532b7235c6f90757621b) is the same azimuthal unit vector from spherical coordinates, and ![Image 34: {\displaystyle {\hat {e}}_{\nu }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6c4c931c6fda5cec32bc5f25f85dcd9865789820) lies in the tangent plane to the oblate spheroid surface and completes the right-handed basis set.

Definition (ζ, ξ, φ)
--------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Oblate_spheroidal_coordinates&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Definition (ζ, ξ, φ)")]

Another set of oblate spheroidal coordinates ![Image 35: {\displaystyle (\zeta ,\xi ,\phi )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/69d6a96d0e3c9dc022d96720300321c0fdccaf9a) are sometimes used where ![Image 36: {\displaystyle \zeta =\sinh \mu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f51205298a1939392b556b120fb2a82ee3ed8a73) and ![Image 37: {\displaystyle \xi =\sin \nu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1942b51ee9b442cf7d2a7c03088566f42504bf53) (Smythe 1968). The curves of constant ![Image 38: {\displaystyle \zeta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d5c3916703cae7938143d38865f78f27faadd4ae) are oblate spheroids and the curves of constant ![Image 39: {\displaystyle \xi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e0b461aaf61091abd5d2c808931c48b8ff9647db) are the hyperboloids of revolution. The coordinate ![Image 40: {\displaystyle \zeta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d5c3916703cae7938143d38865f78f27faadd4ae) is restricted by ![Image 41: {\displaystyle 0\leq \zeta <\infty }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f934433426080b1698e86ac2114a78abc33e659a) and ![Image 42: {\displaystyle \xi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e0b461aaf61091abd5d2c808931c48b8ff9647db) is restricted by ![Image 43: {\displaystyle -1\leq \xi <1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/50d18b7293513be513a91c58c394492afbb34f16).

The relationship to [Cartesian coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinates "Cartesian coordinates") is ![Image 44: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}x=a{\sqrt {(1+\zeta ^{2})(1-\xi ^{2})}}\,\cos \phi \\y=a{\sqrt {(1+\zeta ^{2})(1-\xi ^{2})}}\,\sin \phi \\z=a\zeta \xi \end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/873103397cb57a58df15f231fdcd2558f6042d76)

The scale factors for ![Image 45: {\displaystyle (\zeta ,\xi ,\phi )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/69d6a96d0e3c9dc022d96720300321c0fdccaf9a) are: ![Image 46: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}h_{\zeta }&=a{\sqrt {\frac {\zeta ^{2}+\xi ^{2}}{1+\zeta ^{2}}}}\\h_{\xi }&=a{\sqrt {\frac {\zeta ^{2}+\xi ^{2}}{1-\xi ^{2}}}}\\h_{\phi }&=a{\sqrt {(1+\zeta ^{2})(1-\xi ^{2})}}\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c60bb429e0237fe4694dd9c0be66143c4fd7a938)

Knowing the scale factors, various functions of the coordinates can be calculated by the general method outlined in the [orthogonal coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_coordinates "Orthogonal coordinates") article. The infinitesimal volume element is: ![Image 47: {\displaystyle dV=a^{3}\left(\zeta ^{2}+\xi ^{2}\right)d\zeta \,d\xi \,d\phi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a73ab5e831232627745b9e7f791a38855583c25d)

The gradient is: ![Image 48: {\displaystyle \nabla V={\frac {1}{h_{\zeta }}}{\frac {\partial V}{\partial \zeta }}\,{\hat {\zeta }}+{\frac {1}{h_{\xi }}}{\frac {\partial V}{\partial \xi }}\,{\hat {\xi }}+{\frac {1}{h_{\phi }}}{\frac {\partial V}{\partial \phi }}\,{\hat {\phi }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6868d2c46d1d163849381762d42dee787c579580)

The divergence is: ![Image 49: {\displaystyle \nabla \cdot \mathbf {F} ={\frac {1}{a(\zeta ^{2}+\xi ^{2})}}\left\{{\frac {\partial }{\partial \zeta }}\left({\sqrt {1+\zeta ^{2}}}{\sqrt {\zeta ^{2}+\xi ^{2}}}F_{\zeta }\right)+{\frac {\partial }{\partial \xi }}\left({\sqrt {1-\xi ^{2}}}{\sqrt {\zeta ^{2}+\xi ^{2}}}F_{\xi }\right)\right\}+{\frac {1}{a{\sqrt {1+\zeta ^{2}}}{\sqrt {1-\xi ^{2}}}}}{\frac {\partial F_{\phi }}{\partial \phi }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/252ba00239a9f2d30b5f89783c2b448f0f38a910)

and the Laplacian equals ![Image 50: {\displaystyle \nabla ^{2}V={\frac {1}{a^{2}\left(\zeta ^{2}+\xi ^{2}\right)}}\left\{{\frac {\partial }{\partial \zeta }}\left[\left(1+\zeta ^{2}\right){\frac {\partial V}{\partial \zeta }}\right]+{\frac {\partial }{\partial \xi }}\left[\left(1-\xi ^{2}\right){\frac {\partial V}{\partial \xi }}\right]\right\}+{\frac {1}{a^{2}\left(1+\zeta ^{2}\right)\left(1-\xi ^{2}\right)}}{\frac {\partial ^{2}V}{\partial \phi ^{2}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b357b2c9bd2183b7e40d3f74589b6b2b948608de)

### Oblate spheroidal harmonics

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Oblate_spheroidal_coordinates&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Oblate spheroidal harmonics")]

As is the case with [spherical coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_coordinates "Spherical coordinates") and [spherical harmonics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_harmonics "Spherical harmonics"), Laplace's equation may be solved by the method of [separation of variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separation_of_variables "Separation of variables") to yield solutions in the form of **oblate spheroidal harmonics**, which are convenient to use when boundary conditions are defined on a surface with a constant oblate spheroidal coordinate.

Following the technique of [separation of variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separation_of_variables "Separation of variables"), a solution to Laplace's equation is written: ![Image 51: {\displaystyle V=Z(\zeta )\,\Xi (\xi )\,\Phi (\phi )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4462e96cd3da829220f7d0a4c30d456b47a70cee)

This yields three separate differential equations in each of the variables: ![Image 52: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}{\frac {d}{d\zeta }}\left[(1+\zeta ^{2}){\frac {dZ}{d\zeta }}\right]+{\frac {m^{2}Z}{1+\zeta ^{2}}}-n(n+1)Z=0\\{\frac {d}{d\xi }}\left[(1-\xi ^{2}){\frac {d\Xi }{d\xi }}\right]-{\frac {m^{2}\Xi }{1-\xi ^{2}}}+n(n+1)\Xi =0\\{\frac {d^{2}\Phi }{d\phi ^{2}}}=-m^{2}\Phi \end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c85dd354b4d9a6700849d004f4d96866e959a8b9) where _m_ is a constant which is an integer because the φ variable is periodic with period 2π. _n_ will then be an integer. The solution to these equations are: ![Image 53: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}Z_{mn}&=A_{1}P_{n}^{m}(i\zeta )+A_{2}Q_{n}^{m}(i\zeta )\\[1ex]\Xi _{mn}&=A_{3}P_{n}^{m}(\xi )+A_{4}Q_{n}^{m}(\xi )\\[1ex]\Phi _{m}&=A_{5}e^{im\phi }+A_{6}e^{-im\phi }\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b9cb74392adae88641e5f8d5cee0b7c2f34c7fb3) where the ![Image 54: {\displaystyle A_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1aed3b5def921afbe6cc48aaf8f9b11c6f1c1e2d) are constants and ![Image 55: {\displaystyle P_{n}^{m}(z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b5052318378856897b27b6693d84e9824198c06f) and ![Image 56: {\displaystyle Q_{n}^{m}(z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0203c81c710a660caef6e647dc3139d9faf55e22) are [associated Legendre polynomials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Associated_Legendre_polynomial "Associated Legendre polynomial") of the first and second kind respectively. The product of the three solutions is called an _oblate spheroidal harmonic_ and the general solution to Laplace's equation is written: ![Image 57: {\displaystyle V=\sum _{n=0}^{\infty }\sum _{m=0}^{\infty }\,Z_{mn}(\zeta )\,\Xi _{mn}(\xi )\,\Phi _{m}(\phi )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5911cc9fe5198eaf98dad5b66f7df29a1ac8f438)

The constants will combine to yield only four independent constants for each harmonic.

Definition (σ, τ, φ)
--------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Oblate_spheroidal_coordinates&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Definition (σ, τ, φ)")]

[![Image 58](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Oblate_spheroidal_coordinates_full_hyperboloid.png/350px-Oblate_spheroidal_coordinates_full_hyperboloid.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Oblate_spheroidal_coordinates_full_hyperboloid.png)

Figure 3: Coordinate isosurfaces for a point P (shown as a black sphere) in the alternative oblate spheroidal coordinates (σ, τ, φ). As before, the oblate spheroid corresponding to σ is shown in red, and φ measures the azimuthal angle between the green and yellow half-planes. However, the surface of constant τ is a full one-sheet hyperboloid, shown in blue. This produces a two-fold degeneracy, shown by the two black spheres located at (_x_, _y_, ±_z_).

An alternative and geometrically intuitive set of oblate spheroidal coordinates (σ, τ, φ) are sometimes used, where σ = [cosh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosh_(mathematical_function) "Cosh (mathematical function)") μ and τ = [cos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosine "Cosine") ν.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oblate_spheroidal_coordinates#cite_note-1) Therefore, the coordinate σ must be greater than or equal to one, whereas τ must lie between ±1, inclusive. The surfaces of constant σ are oblate spheroids, as were those of constant μ, whereas the curves of constant τ are full hyperboloids of revolution, including the half-hyperboloids corresponding to ±ν. Thus, these coordinates are degenerate; _two_ points in Cartesian coordinates (_x_, _y_, ±_z_) map to _one_ set of coordinates (σ, τ, φ). This two-fold degeneracy in the sign of _z_ is evident from the equations transforming from oblate spheroidal coordinates to the [Cartesian coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinates "Cartesian coordinates")![Image 59: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}x&=a\sigma \tau \cos \phi \\y&=a\sigma \tau \sin \phi \\z^{2}&=a^{2}\left(\sigma ^{2}-1\right)\left(1-\tau ^{2}\right)\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/88d178d28ed3a802493560649444937216f233cb)

The coordinates ![Image 60: {\displaystyle \sigma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/59f59b7c3e6fdb1d0365a494b81fb9a696138c36) and ![Image 61: {\displaystyle \tau }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38a7dcde9730ef0853809fefc18d88771f95206c) have a simple relation to the distances to the focal ring. For any point, the _sum_![Image 62: {\displaystyle d_{1}+d_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/028d9a26c6c05e0531d8476389cb73cc8d5e8520) of its distances to the focal ring equals ![Image 63: {\displaystyle 2a\sigma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3c33546d2cc2f56c633756909048382e5283eb2c), whereas their _difference_![Image 64: {\displaystyle d_{1}-d_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/de2341ef255cb1d411333857ddd1f0dbf76c43f4) equals ![Image 65: {\displaystyle 2a\tau }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/99604fd548ab96ea6fe35259c07a0631fcf3cad5). Thus, the "far" distance to the focal ring is ![Image 66: {\displaystyle a(\sigma +\tau )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9723f0deeb63d3cddabdc059f2fee30c96c85818), whereas the "near" distance is ![Image 67: {\displaystyle a(\sigma -\tau )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7b6a3c2b6f276dca7a3991f109af701f5858c3d1).

### Coordinate surfaces

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Oblate_spheroidal_coordinates&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Coordinate surfaces")]

Similar to its counterpart μ, the surfaces of constant σ form [oblate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oblate_spheroid "Oblate spheroid")[spheroids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spheroids "Spheroids")![Image 68: {\displaystyle {\frac {x^{2}+y^{2}}{a^{2}\sigma ^{2}}}+{\frac {z^{2}}{a^{2}\left(\sigma ^{2}-1\right)}}=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/649ed2b8f57697f9233796737de6fdc2d8882c11)

Similarly, the surfaces of constant τ form full one-sheet [hyperboloids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperboloid "Hyperboloid") of revolution ![Image 69: {\displaystyle {\frac {x^{2}+y^{2}}{a^{2}\tau ^{2}}}-{\frac {z^{2}}{a^{2}\left(1-\tau ^{2}\right)}}=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3de47ff1ae2b775d0c7b1ad69ecc53e8ade7d969)

The scale factors for the alternative oblate spheroidal coordinates ![Image 70: {\displaystyle (\sigma ,\tau ,\phi )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3dc37b484c959a66b0cc4f4e1be4b8f022cc715b) are ![Image 71: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}h_{\sigma }=a{\sqrt {\frac {\sigma ^{2}-\tau ^{2}}{\sigma ^{2}-1}}}\\h_{\tau }=a{\sqrt {\frac {\sigma ^{2}-\tau ^{2}}{1-\tau ^{2}}}}\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/847532e8372838d3f7a9549bce2dbcb3e5305110) whereas the azimuthal scale factor is ![Image 72: {\displaystyle h_{\phi }=a\sigma \tau }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/01b39afd3ba7f082fbf7e2e2411facaaa6abc77a).

Hence, the infinitesimal volume element can be written ![Image 73: {\displaystyle dV=a^{3}\sigma \tau {\frac {\sigma ^{2}-\tau ^{2}}{\sqrt {\left(\sigma ^{2}-1\right)\left(1-\tau ^{2}\right)}}}\,d\sigma \,d\tau \,d\phi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fcb677bdb6548c31227ab5517f8ac20a0f29eaa3) and the Laplacian equals ![Image 74: {\displaystyle \nabla ^{2}\Phi ={\frac {1}{a^{2}\left(\sigma ^{2}-\tau ^{2}\right)}}\left\{{\frac {\sqrt {\sigma ^{2}-1}}{\sigma }}{\frac {\partial }{\partial \sigma }}\left[\left(\sigma {\sqrt {\sigma ^{2}-1}}\right){\frac {\partial \Phi }{\partial \sigma }}\right]+{\frac {\sqrt {1-\tau ^{2}}}{\tau }}{\frac {\partial }{\partial \tau }}\left[\left(\tau {\sqrt {1-\tau ^{2}}}\right){\frac {\partial \Phi }{\partial \tau }}\right]\right\}+{\frac {1}{a^{2}\sigma ^{2}\tau ^{2}}}{\frac {\partial ^{2}\Phi }{\partial \phi ^{2}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/14964682baacf6ce7e88a813cf299106742a402b)

Other differential operators such as ![Image 75: {\displaystyle \nabla \cdot \mathbf {F} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1f4f5b9e6560d7fdaf1a69d9fdeff6beb1f327cc) and ![Image 76: {\displaystyle \nabla \times \mathbf {F} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c6e2429b7e3cb6965d1a6651703ff5ef0c88b378) can be expressed in the coordinates ![Image 77: {\displaystyle (\sigma ,\tau )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c2d60596d44a9bc5043b60b76c1fac959eb65eb0) by substituting the scale factors into the general formulae found in [orthogonal coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_coordinates "Orthogonal coordinates").

As is the case with [spherical coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_coordinates "Spherical coordinates"), Laplaces equation may be solved by the method of [separation of variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separation_of_variables "Separation of variables") to yield solutions in the form of **oblate spheroidal harmonics**, which are convenient to use when boundary conditions are defined on a surface with a constant oblate spheroidal coordinate (See Smythe, 1968).
