Title: Tests of general relativity

URL Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity

Published Time: 2005-04-23T09:58:04Z

Markdown Content:
**Tests of general relativity** serve to establish observational evidence for the [theory of general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theory_of_general_relativity "Theory of general relativity"). The first three tests, proposed by [Albert Einstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Einstein "Albert Einstein") in 1915, concerned the "anomalous" [precession](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Precession "Precession") of the [perihelion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perihelion "Perihelion") of [Mercury](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mercury_(planet) "Mercury (planet)"), the bending of light in [gravitational fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_field "Gravitational field"), and the [gravitational redshift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_redshift "Gravitational redshift"). The precession of Mercury was already known; experiments showing light bending in accordance with the predictions of [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity") were performed in 1919, with increasingly precise measurements made in subsequent tests; and scientists claimed to have measured the gravitational redshift in 1925, although measurements sensitive enough to actually confirm the theory were not made until 1954. A more accurate program starting in 1959 tested general relativity in the weak gravitational field limit, severely limiting possible deviations from the theory.

In the 1970s, scientists began to make additional tests, starting with [Irwin Shapiro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irwin_I._Shapiro "Irwin I. Shapiro")'s measurement of the relativistic time delay in radar signal travel time near the Sun. Beginning in 1974, [Hulse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russell_Alan_Hulse "Russell Alan Hulse"), [Taylor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Hooton_Taylor,_Jr. "Joseph Hooton Taylor, Jr.") and others studied the behaviour of [binary pulsars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_pulsar "Binary pulsar") experiencing much stronger gravitational fields than those found in the Solar System. Both in the weak field limit (as in the Solar System) and with the stronger fields present in systems of binary pulsars the predictions of general relativity have been extremely well tested.

In February 2016, the [Advanced LIGO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LIGO "LIGO") team announced that they had [directly detected gravitational waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_wave_detection "Gravitational wave detection") from a black hole merger.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-Discovery_2016-1) This discovery, along with additional detections announced in June 2016 and June 2017,[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-conover2017-2) tested general relativity in the very strong field limit, observing to date no deviations from theory.

[Albert Einstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Einstein "Albert Einstein") proposed[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-Ein1916-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-Ein1916eng-4) three tests of general relativity, subsequently called the "classical tests" of general relativity, in 1916:

1.   the perihelion precession of [Mercury](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mercury_(planet) "Mercury (planet)")'s orbit
2.   the [deflection of light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_lens "Gravitational lens") by the [Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun "Sun")
3.   the [gravitational redshift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_redshift "Gravitational redshift") of light

In the letter to _[The Times](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The\_Times "The Times")_ (of London) on November 28, 1919, he described the theory of relativity and thanked his English colleagues for their understanding and testing of his work. He also mentioned three classical tests with comments:[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-5)

"The chief attraction of the theory lies in its logical completeness. If a single one of the conclusions drawn from it proves wrong, it must be given up; to modify it without destroying the whole structure seems to be impossible."

### Perihelion precession of Mercury

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tests_of_general_relativity&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Perihelion precession of Mercury")]

[![Image 1](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Mercury_transit_2.jpg/250px-Mercury_transit_2.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Mercury_transit_2.jpg)

Transit of Mercury on November 8, 2006 with [sunspots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sunspot "Sunspot") #921, 922, and 923

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Apsidendrehung.png/250px-Apsidendrehung.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Apsidendrehung.png)

The perihelion precession of Mercury

Under [Newtonian physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newtonian_physics "Newtonian physics"), an object in an (isolated) two-body system, consisting of the object orbiting a spherical mass, would trace out an [ellipse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse "Ellipse") with the center of mass of the system at a [focus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Focus_(geometry) "Focus (geometry)") of the ellipse. The point of closest approach, called the [periapsis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Periapsis "Periapsis") (or when the central body is the Sun, [perihelion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perihelion "Perihelion")), is fixed. Hence the major axis of the ellipse remains fixed in space. Both objects orbit around the center of mass of this system, so they each have their own ellipse. However, a number of effects in the Solar System cause the perihelia of planets to precess (rotate) around the Sun in the plane of their orbits, or equivalently, cause the major axis to rotate about the center of mass, hence changing its orientation in space.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-6) The principal cause is the presence of other planets which [perturb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perturbation_(astronomy) "Perturbation (astronomy)") one another's orbit. Another (much less significant) effect is solar [oblateness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oblate_spheroid "Oblate spheroid").

[Mercury](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mercury_(planet) "Mercury (planet)") deviates from the precession predicted from these Newtonian effects. This anomalous rate of precession of the perihelion of Mercury's orbit was first recognized in 1859 as a problem in [celestial mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celestial_mechanics "Celestial mechanics"), by [Urbain Le Verrier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urbain_Le_Verrier "Urbain Le Verrier"). His re-analysis of available timed observations of transits of Mercury over the Sun's disk from 1697 to 1848 showed that the actual rate of the precession disagreed from that predicted from Newton's theory by 38″ ([arcseconds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arcseconds "Arcseconds")) per [tropical century](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tropical_year "Tropical year") (later re-estimated at 43″ by [Simon Newcomb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simon_Newcomb "Simon Newcomb") in 1882).[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-Verrier1859-7) A number of _ad hoc_ and ultimately unsuccessful solutions were proposed, but they tended to introduce more problems. Le Verrier suggested that another hypothetical planet might exist to account for Mercury's behavior.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-Verrier1859-7) The previously successful search for Neptune based on its perturbations of the orbit of Uranus led astronomers to place some faith in this possible explanation, and the hypothetical planet was even named [Vulcan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vulcan_(hypothetical_planet) "Vulcan (hypothetical planet)"). Finally, in 1908, [W. W. Campbell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/W._W._Campbell "W. W. Campbell"), Director of the Lick Observatory, after the comprehensive photographic observations by Lick astronomer, [Charles D. Perrine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_D._Perrine "Charles D. Perrine"), at three solar eclipse expeditions, stated, "In my opinion, Dr. Perrine's work at the three eclipses of 1901, 1905, and 1908 brings the observational side of the famous intramercurial-planet problem definitely to a close."[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-8)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-9) Subsequently, no evidence of Vulcan was found and Einstein's 1915 general theory accounted for Mercury's anomalous precession. Einstein wrote to [Michele Besso](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michele_Besso "Michele Besso"), "Perihelion motions explained quantitatively... you will be astonished".[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-10)

In general relativity, this remaining [precession](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_precession "Stellar precession"), or change of orientation of the orbital ellipse within its orbital plane, is explained by gravitation being mediated by the curvature of spacetime. Einstein showed that general relativity[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-Ein1916-3) agrees closely with the observed amount of perihelion shift. This was a powerful factor motivating the adoption of general relativity.

Although earlier measurements of planetary orbits were made using conventional telescopes, more accurate measurements are now made with [radar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radar_astronomy "Radar astronomy"). The total observed precession of Mercury is (574.10 ± 0.65)″ per century[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-Clemence-11) relative to the inertial [ICRF](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Celestial_Reference_Frame "International Celestial Reference Frame"). This precession can be attributed to the following causes:

| --- | --- |
| 532.3035 | gravitational tugs of other solar bodies |
| 0.0286 | oblateness of the Sun ([quadrupole moment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrupole#Gravitational_quadrupole "Quadrupole")) |
| 42.9799 | gravitoelectric effects (Schwarzschild-like), a general relativity effect |
| −0.0020 | [Lense–Thirring precession](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lense%E2%80%93Thirring_precession "Lense–Thirring precession") |
| 575.31[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-Park-12) | total predicted |
| 574.10 ± 0.65[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-Clemence-11) | observed |

The correction by (42.980±0.001)″/cy is the prediction of [post-Newtonian theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parameterized_post-Newtonian_formalism "Parameterized post-Newtonian formalism") with [parameters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parameterized_post-Newtonian_formalism#Beta-delta_notation "Parameterized post-Newtonian formalism")![Image 3: {\displaystyle \gamma =\beta =1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fb96b68c1387c6ca1938c729d89302b6cdd5ceb4).[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-13) Thus the effect can be fully explained by general relativity. More recent calculations based on more precise measurements have not materially changed the situation.

In general relativity the perihelion shift _σ_, expressed in radians per revolution, is approximately given by:[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-14)

![Image 4: {\displaystyle \sigma ={\frac {24\pi ^{3}L^{2}}{T^{2}c^{2}\left(1-e^{2}\right)}}\ ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cd8646fc9858f61c8acd497d3eb8fe23c38a8200)
where _L_ is the [semi-major axis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semi-major_axis "Semi-major axis"), _T_ is the [orbital period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_period "Orbital period"), _c_ is the speed of light, and _e_ is the [orbital eccentricity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity "Orbital eccentricity") (see: [Two-body problem in general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two-body_problem_in_general_relativity "Two-body problem in general relativity")).

The other planets experience perihelion shifts as well, but, since they are farther from the Sun and have longer periods, their shifts are lower, and could not be observed accurately until long after Mercury's. For example, the perihelion shift of Earth's orbit due to general relativity is theoretically 3.83868″ per century and experimentally (3.8387±0.0004)″/cy, Venus's is 8.62473″/cy and (8.6247 ± 0.0005)″/cy and Mars' is (1.351±0.001)″/cy. Both values have now been measured, with results in good agreement with theory.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-Biswas-15) The [periapsis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Periapsis "Periapsis") shift has also now been measured for binary pulsar systems, with [PSR 1913+16](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PSR_1913%2B16 "PSR 1913+16") amounting to 4.2° per year.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-Matzner-16) These observations are consistent with general relativity.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-17) It is also possible to measure periapsis shift in binary star systems which do not contain ultra-dense stars, but it is more difficult to model the classical effects precisely – for example, the alignment of the stars' spin to their orbital plane needs to be known and is hard to measure directly. A few systems, such as [DI Herculis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DI_Herculis "DI Herculis"),[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-skyandtelescope2009-18) have been measured as test cases for general relativity.

### Deflection of light by the Sun

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tests_of_general_relativity&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Deflection of light by the Sun")]

[![Image 5](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/1919_eclipse_negative.jpg/250px-1919_eclipse_negative.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:1919_eclipse_negative.jpg)

One of [Eddington](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sir_Arthur_Eddington "Sir Arthur Eddington")'s photographs of the [1919 solar eclipse experiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eddington_experiment "Eddington experiment"), presented in his 1920 paper announcing its success

[Henry Cavendish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry_Cavendish "Henry Cavendish") in 1784 (in an unpublished manuscript) and [Johann Georg von Soldner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johann_Georg_von_Soldner "Johann Georg von Soldner") in 1801 (published in 1804) had pointed out that Newtonian gravity predicts that starlight will bend around a massive object.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-19)[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-20) The same value as Soldner's was calculated by Einstein in 1911 based on the equivalence principle alone. However, Einstein noted in 1915 in the process of completing general relativity, that his 1911 result (and thus Soldner's 1801 result) is only half of the correct value. Einstein became the first to calculate the correct value for light bending: 1.75 [arcseconds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arcsecond "Arcsecond") for light that grazes the Sun.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-21)[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-22)

The first observation of light deflection was performed by noting the change in position of [stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star "Star") as they passed near the Sun on the [celestial sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celestial_sphere "Celestial sphere"). The observations were performed by [Arthur Eddington](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sir_Arthur_Eddington "Sir Arthur Eddington") and his collaborators (see [Eddington experiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eddington_experiment "Eddington experiment")) during the total [solar eclipse of May 29, 1919](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_eclipse_of_May_29,_1919 "Solar eclipse of May 29, 1919"),[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-Eddington1920-23) when the stars near the Sun (at that time in the constellation [Taurus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation) "Taurus (constellation)")) could be observed.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-Eddington1920-23) Observations were made simultaneously in the cities of [Sobral, Ceará](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sobral,_Cear%C3%A1 "Sobral, Ceará"), Brazil and in [São Tomé and Príncipe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Tom%C3%A9_and_Pr%C3%ADncipe "São Tomé and Príncipe") on the west coast of Africa.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-24) The result was considered spectacular news and made the front page of most major newspapers. It made Einstein and his theory of general relativity world-famous. When asked by his assistant what his reaction would have been if general relativity had not been confirmed by Eddington and Dyson in 1919, Einstein famously made the quip: "Then I would feel sorry for the dear Lord. The theory is correct anyway."[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-25)

The early accuracy, however, was poor and there was doubt that the small number of measured star locations and instrument questions could produce a reliable result. The results were argued by some[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-26) to have been plagued by [systematic error](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Systematic_error "Systematic error") and possibly [confirmation bias](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confirmation_bias "Confirmation bias"), although modern reanalysis of the dataset[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-27) suggests that Eddington's analysis was accurate.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-28)[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-PhysToday-29) The measurement was repeated by a team from the [Lick Observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lick_Observatory "Lick Observatory") led by the Director [W. W. Campbell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/W._W._Campbell "W. W. Campbell") in the [1922 eclipse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_eclipse_of_September_21,_1922 "Solar eclipse of September 21, 1922") as observed in remote Australian station of [Wallal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wallal "Wallal"),[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-30) with results based on hundreds of star positions that agreed with the 1919 results[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-PhysToday-29) and has been repeated several times since, most notably in 1953 by [Yerkes Observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yerkes_Observatory "Yerkes Observatory") astronomers[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-31) and in 1973 by a team from the [University of Texas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Texas "University of Texas").[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-32) Considerable uncertainty remained in these measurements for almost fifty years, until observations started being made at [radio frequencies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radio_astronomy "Radio astronomy").[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-33)[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-34) The deflection of starlight by the nearby [white dwarf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_dwarf "White dwarf") star [Stein 2051 B](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stein_2051_B "Stein 2051 B") has also been measured.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-35)

### Gravitational redshift of light

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tests_of_general_relativity&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Gravitational redshift of light")]

[![Image 6](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Gravitational_red-shifting2.png/250px-Gravitational_red-shifting2.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Gravitational_red-shifting2.png)

The gravitational redshift of a light wave as it moves upwards against a gravitational field (caused by the yellow star below).

Einstein predicted the [gravitational redshift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_redshift "Gravitational redshift") of light from the [equivalence principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equivalence_principle "Equivalence principle") in 1907, and it was predicted that this effect might be measured in the spectral lines of a [white dwarf star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_dwarf_star "White dwarf star"), which has a very high gravitational field. Initial attempts to measure the gravitational redshift of the spectrum of [Sirius-B](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sirius-B "Sirius-B") were done by [Walter Sydney Adams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Walter_Sydney_Adams "Walter Sydney Adams") in 1925, but the result was criticized as being unusable due to the contamination from light from the (much brighter) primary star, [Sirius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sirius "Sirius").[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-Hetherington1980-36)[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-Holberg2010-37) The first accurate measurement of the gravitational redshift of a white dwarf was done by Popper in 1954, measuring a 21 km/s gravitational redshift of [40 Eridani](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/40_Eridani "40 Eridani") B.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-Holberg2010-37)

The redshift of Sirius B was finally measured by Greenstein _et al._ in 1971, obtaining the value for the gravitational redshift of 89±16 km/s, with more accurate measurements by the Hubble Space Telescope showing 80.4±4.8 km/s.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-38)

### Tests of special relativity

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tests_of_general_relativity&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Tests of special relativity")]

The general theory of relativity incorporates Einstein's [special theory of relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_relativity "Special relativity"), and hence tests of special relativity are also testing aspects of general relativity. As a consequence of the [equivalence principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equivalence_principle "Equivalence principle"), [Lorentz invariance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentz_invariance "Lorentz invariance") holds locally in non-rotating, freely falling reference frames. Experiments related to Lorentz invariance special relativity (that is, when gravitational effects can be neglected) are described in [tests of special relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_special_relativity "Tests of special relativity").

The modern era of testing general relativity was ushered in largely at the impetus of [Dicke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_H._Dicke "Robert H. Dicke") and [Schiff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leonard_Isaac_Schiff "Leonard Isaac Schiff") who laid out a framework for testing general relativity.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-39)[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-40)[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-41) They emphasized the importance not only of the classical tests, but of null experiments, testing for effects which in principle could occur in a theory of gravitation, but do not occur in general relativity. Other important theoretical developments included the inception of [alternative theories to general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alternatives_to_general_relativity "Alternatives to general relativity"), in particular, [scalar–tensor theories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar%E2%80%93tensor_theory "Scalar–tensor theory") such as the [Brans–Dicke theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brans%E2%80%93Dicke_theory "Brans–Dicke theory");[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-42) the [parameterized post-Newtonian formalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parameterized_post-Newtonian_formalism "Parameterized post-Newtonian formalism") in which deviations from general relativity can be quantified; and the framework of the [equivalence principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equivalence_principle "Equivalence principle").

Experimentally, new developments in [space exploration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_exploration "Space exploration"), [electronics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronics "Electronics") and [condensed matter physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics "Condensed matter physics") have made additional precise experiments possible, such as the Pound–Rebka experiment, laser interferometry and [lunar rangefinding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunar_Laser_Ranging_experiment "Lunar Laser Ranging experiment").

### Post-Newtonian tests of gravity

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tests_of_general_relativity&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Post-Newtonian tests of gravity")]

Early tests of general relativity were hampered by the lack of viable competitors to the theory: it was not clear what sorts of tests would distinguish it from its competitors. General relativity was the only known relativistic theory of gravity compatible with special relativity and observations. This changed in 1960 with the introduction of [Brans–Dicke theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brans%E2%80%93Dicke_theory "Brans–Dicke theory") which provided an alternative theory also in agreement with experimental observations.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-Will-43): 3  Ultimately, this led to the development of the [parametrized post-Newtonian formalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PPN_formalism "PPN formalism") by [Nordtvedt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kenneth_Nordtvedt "Kenneth Nordtvedt") and [Will](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_Martin_Will "Clifford Martin Will"), which parametrizes, in terms of ten adjustable parameters, all the possible departures from Newton's law of universal gravitation to first order in the velocity of moving objects (_i.e._ to first order in ![Image 7: {\displaystyle v/c}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bd5fbfe05e197127354e233f6c813e6b5d846672), where _v_ is the velocity of an object and _c_ is the speed of light). This approximation allows the possible deviations from general relativity, for slowly moving objects in weak gravitational fields, to be systematically analyzed. Much effort has been put into constraining the post-Newtonian parameters, and deviations from general relativity are at present severely limited.

The experiments testing gravitational lensing and light time delay limits the same post-Newtonian parameter, the so-called Eddington parameter γ, which is a straightforward parametrization of the amount of deflection of light by a gravitational source. It is equal to one for general relativity, and takes different values in other theories (such as Brans–Dicke theory). It is the best constrained of the ten post-Newtonian parameters, but there are other experiments designed to constrain the others. Precise observations of the perihelion shift of Mercury constrain other parameters, as do tests of the strong equivalence principle.

One of the goals of the [BepiColombo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BepiColombo "BepiColombo") mission to Mercury, is to test the general relativity theory by measuring the parameters gamma and beta of the parametrized post-Newtonian formalism with high accuracy.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-44)[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-45) The experiment is part of the Mercury Orbiter Radio science Experiment (MORE).[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-46)[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-47) The spacecraft was launched in October 2018 and is expected to enter orbit around Mercury in November 2026.

### Gravitational lensing

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tests_of_general_relativity&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Gravitational lensing")]

One of the most important tests is [gravitational lensing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_lensing "Gravitational lensing"). It has been observed in distant astrophysical sources, but these are poorly controlled and it is uncertain how they constrain general relativity. The most precise tests are analogous to Eddington's 1919 experiment: they measure the deflection of radiation from a distant source by the Sun. The sources that can be most precisely analyzed are distant [radio sources](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radio_astronomy "Radio astronomy"). In particular, some [quasars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasar "Quasar") are very strong radio sources. The directional resolution of any telescope is in principle limited by diffraction; for radio telescopes this is also the practical limit. An important improvement in obtaining positional high accuracies (from milli-arcsecond to micro-arcsecond) was obtained by combining radio telescopes across Earth. The technique is called [very long baseline interferometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/VLBI "VLBI") (VLBI). With this technique radio observations couple the phase information of the radio signal observed in telescopes separated over large distances. Recently, these telescopes have measured the deflection of radio waves by the Sun to extremely high precision, confirming the amount of deflection predicted by general relativity aspect to the 0.03% level.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-48) At this level of precision systematic effects have to be carefully taken into account to determine the precise location of the telescopes on Earth. Some important effects are Earth's [nutation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nutation "Nutation"), rotation, atmospheric refraction, tectonic displacement and tidal waves. Another important effect is refraction of the radio waves by the [solar corona](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_corona "Solar corona"). Fortunately, this effect has a characteristic [spectrum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectrum "Spectrum"), whereas gravitational distortion is independent of wavelength. Thus, careful analysis, using measurements at several frequencies, can subtract this source of error.

The entire sky is slightly distorted due to the gravitational deflection of light caused by the Sun (the anti-Sun direction excepted). This effect has been observed by the [European Space Agency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Space_Agency "European Space Agency") astrometric satellite [Hipparcos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hipparcos "Hipparcos"). It measured the positions of about 10 5 stars. During the full mission about 3.5×10 6 relative positions have been determined, each to an accuracy of typically 3 milliarcseconds (the accuracy for an 8–9 magnitude star). Since the gravitation deflection perpendicular to the Earth–Sun direction is already 4.07 milliarcseconds, corrections are needed for practically all stars. Without systematic effects, the error in an individual observation of 3 milliarcseconds, could be reduced by the square root of the number of positions, leading to a precision of 0.0016 milliarcseconds. Systematic effects, however, limit the accuracy of the determination to 0.3% (Froeschlé, 1997).

Launched in 2013, the [_Gaia_ spacecraft](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaia_(spacecraft) "Gaia (spacecraft)") will conduct a census of one billion [stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star "Star") in the [Milky Way](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milky_Way "Milky Way") and measure their positions to an accuracy of 24 microarcseconds. Thus it will also provide stringent new tests of gravitational deflection of light caused by the [Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun "Sun") which was predicted by General relativity.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-49)

### Light travel time delay testing

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tests_of_general_relativity&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Light travel time delay testing")]

[Irwin I. Shapiro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irwin_I._Shapiro "Irwin I. Shapiro") proposed another test, beyond the classical tests, which could be performed within the Solar System. It is sometimes called the fourth "classical" test of [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity"). He predicted a relativistic time delay ([Shapiro delay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shapiro_delay "Shapiro delay")) in the round-trip travel time for radar signals reflecting off other planets.[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-50) The mere curvature of the path of a [photon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon "Photon") passing near the Sun is too small to have an observable delaying effect (when the round-trip time is compared to the time taken if the photon had followed a straight path), but general relativity predicts a time delay that becomes progressively larger when the photon passes nearer to the Sun due to the [time dilation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_dilation "Time dilation") in the [gravitational potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_potential "Gravitational potential") of the Sun. Observing radar reflections from Mercury and Venus just before and after they are eclipsed by the Sun agrees with general relativity theory at the 5% level.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-51)

More recently, the [Cassini probe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens "Cassini–Huygens") has undertaken a similar experiment which gave agreement with general relativity at the 0.002% level.[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-52) However, the following detailed studies[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-53)[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-54) revealed that the measured value of the PPN parameter gamma is affected by a [gravitomagnetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitoelectromagnetism "Gravitoelectromagnetism") effect caused by the orbital motion of Sun around the [barycenter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barycenter "Barycenter") of the solar system. The gravitomagnetic effect in the [Cassini](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens "Cassini–Huygens") radioscience experiment was implicitly postulated by B. Bertotti as having a pure general relativistic origin but its theoretical value has never been tested in the experiment which effectively makes the experimental uncertainty in the measured value of gamma actually larger (by a factor of 10) than 0.002% claimed by B. Bertotti and co-authors in Nature.

[Very Long Baseline Interferometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Very_Long_Baseline_Interferometry "Very Long Baseline Interferometry") has measured velocity-dependent (gravitomagnetic) corrections to the Shapiro time delay in the field of moving Jupiter[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-55)[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-56) and Saturn.[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-57)

### Equivalence principle

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tests_of_general_relativity&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Equivalence principle")]

The equivalence principle, in its simplest form, asserts that the trajectories of falling bodies in a gravitational field should be independent of their mass and internal structure, provided they are small enough not to disturb the environment or be affected by [tidal forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tidal_forces "Tidal forces"). This idea has been tested to extremely high precision by [Eötvös torsion balance experiments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/E%C3%B6tv%C3%B6s_experiment "Eötvös experiment"), which look for a differential acceleration between two test masses. Constraints on this, and on the existence of a composition-dependent fifth force or gravitational [Yukawa interaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yukawa_interaction "Yukawa interaction") are very strong, and are discussed under [fifth force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fifth_force "Fifth force") and [weak equivalence principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weak_equivalence_principle "Weak equivalence principle").

A version of the equivalence principle, called the [strong equivalence principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strong_equivalence_principle "Strong equivalence principle"), asserts that self-gravitation falling bodies, such as stars, planets or black holes (which are all held together by their gravitational attraction) should follow the same trajectories in a gravitational field, provided the same conditions are satisfied. This is called the [Nordtvedt effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nordtvedt_effect "Nordtvedt effect") and is most precisely tested by the [Lunar Laser Ranging Experiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunar_Laser_Ranging_Experiment "Lunar Laser Ranging Experiment").[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-58)[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-59) Since 1969, it has continuously measured the distance from several rangefinding stations on Earth to reflectors on the Moon to approximately centimeter accuracy.[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-60) These have provided a strong constraint on several of the other post-Newtonian parameters.

Another part of the strong equivalence principle is the requirement that Newton's gravitational constant be constant in time, and have the same value everywhere in the universe. There are many independent observations limiting the possible variation of Newton's [gravitational constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_constant "Gravitational constant"),[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-61) but one of the best comes from lunar rangefinding which suggests that the gravitational constant does not change by more than one part in 10 11 per year. The constancy of the other constants is discussed in the [Einstein equivalence principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_equivalence_principle "Einstein equivalence principle") section of the equivalence principle article.

#### Gravitational redshift and time dilation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tests_of_general_relativity&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Gravitational redshift and time dilation")]

The first of the classical tests discussed above, the [gravitational redshift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_redshift "Gravitational redshift"), is a simple consequence of the [Einstein equivalence principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_equivalence_principle "Einstein equivalence principle") and was predicted by Einstein in 1907. As such, it is not a test of general relativity in the same way as the post-Newtonian tests, because any theory of gravity obeying the equivalence principle should also incorporate the gravitational redshift. Nonetheless, confirming the existence of the effect was an important substantiation of relativistic gravity, since the absence of gravitational redshift would have strongly contradicted relativity. The first observation of the gravitational redshift was the measurement of the shift in the spectral lines from the [white dwarf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_dwarf "White dwarf") star [Sirius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sirius "Sirius") B by Adams in 1925, discussed above, and follow-on measurements of other white dwarfs. Because of the difficulty of the astrophysical measurement, however, experimental verification using a known terrestrial source was preferable.

Experimental verification of gravitational redshift using terrestrial sources took several decades, because it is difficult to find clocks (to measure [time dilation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_dilation "Time dilation")) or sources of electromagnetic radiation (to measure redshift) with a frequency that is known well enough that the effect can be accurately measured. It was confirmed experimentally for the first time in 1959 using measurements of the change in wavelength of gamma-ray photons generated with the [Mössbauer effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M%C3%B6ssbauer_effect "Mössbauer effect"), which generates radiation with a very narrow line width. The [Pound–Rebka experiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pound%E2%80%93Rebka_experiment "Pound–Rebka experiment") measured the relative redshift of two sources situated at the top and bottom of Harvard University's Jefferson tower.[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-62)[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-63) The result was in excellent agreement with general relativity. This was one of the first precision experiments testing general relativity. The experiment was later improved to better than the 1% level by Pound and Snider.[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-64)

The blueshift of a falling photon can be found by assuming it has an equivalent mass based on its frequency _E_ = _hf_ (where _h_ is the [Planck constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant "Planck constant")) along with _E_ = _mc_ 2, a result of special relativity. Such simple derivations ignore the fact that in general relativity the experiment compares clock rates, rather than energies. In other words, the "higher energy" of the photon after it falls can be equivalently ascribed to the slower running of clocks deeper in the gravitational potential well. To fully validate general relativity, it is important to also show that the rate of arrival of the photons is greater than the rate at which they are emitted. A very accurate gravitational redshift experiment, which deals with this issue, was performed in 1976,[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-65) where a [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen")[maser](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maser "Maser") clock on a rocket was launched to a height of 10,000 km, and its rate compared with an identical clock on the ground. It tested the gravitational redshift to 0.007%.

Although the [Global Positioning System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_Positioning_System "Global Positioning System") (GPS) is not designed as a test of fundamental physics, it must account for the gravitational redshift in its timing system, and physicists have analyzed timing data from the GPS to confirm other tests. When the first satellite was launched, some engineers resisted the prediction that a noticeable gravitational time dilation would occur, so the first satellite was launched without the clock adjustment that was later built into subsequent satellites. It showed the predicted shift of 38 microseconds per day. This rate of discrepancy is sufficient to substantially impair function of GPS within hours if not accounted for. An excellent account of the role played by general relativity in the design of GPS can be found in Ashby 2003.[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-66)

Other precision tests of general relativity,[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-67) not discussed here, are the [Gravity Probe A](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity_Probe_A "Gravity Probe A") satellite, launched in 1976, which showed gravity and velocity affect the ability to synchronize the rates of clocks orbiting a central mass and the [Hafele–Keating experiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hafele%E2%80%93Keating_experiment "Hafele–Keating experiment"), which used atomic clocks in circumnavigating aircraft to test general relativity and special relativity together.[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-68)[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-69)

### Frame-dragging tests

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tests_of_general_relativity&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Frame-dragging tests")]

[![Image 8](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/LAGEOS-NASA.jpg/250px-LAGEOS-NASA.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:LAGEOS-NASA.jpg)

The LAGEOS-1 satellite. ([D](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diameter "Diameter")=60 cm)

Tests of the [Lense–Thirring precession](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lense%E2%80%93Thirring_precession "Lense–Thirring precession"), consisting of small secular [precessions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Precession "Precession") of the orbit of a test particle in motion around a central rotating mass, for example, a planet or a star, have been performed with the [LAGEOS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LAGEOS "LAGEOS") satellites,[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-70) but many aspects of them remain controversial. The same effect may have been detected in the data of the [Mars Global Surveyor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mars_Global_Surveyor "Mars Global Surveyor") (MGS) spacecraft, a former probe in orbit around [Mars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mars "Mars"); also such a test raised a debate.[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-71) First attempts to detect the [Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun "Sun")'s Lense–Thirring effect on the [perihelia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perihelia "Perihelia") of the inner [planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planet "Planet") have been recently reported as well. Frame dragging would cause the orbital plane of stars orbiting near a [supermassive black hole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supermassive_black_hole "Supermassive black hole") to precess about the black hole spin axis. This effect should be detectable within the next few years via [astrometric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrometry "Astrometry") monitoring of stars at the center of the [Milky Way](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milky_Way "Milky Way") galaxy.[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-72) By comparing the rate of orbital precession of two stars on different orbits, it is possible in principle to test the [no-hair theorems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/No-hair_theorem "No-hair theorem") of general relativity.[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-73)

The [Gravity Probe B](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity_Probe_B "Gravity Probe B") satellite, launched in 2004 and operated until 2005, detected frame-dragging and the [geodetic effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodetic_effect "Geodetic effect"). The experiment used four quartz spheres the size of ping pong balls coated with a superconductor. Data analysis continued through 2011 due to high noise levels and difficulties in modelling the noise accurately so that a useful signal could be found. Principal investigators at [Stanford University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stanford_University "Stanford University") reported on May 4, 2011, that they had accurately measured the frame dragging effect relative to the distant star [IM Pegasi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IM_Pegasi "IM Pegasi"), and the calculations proved to be in line with the prediction of Einstein's theory. The results, published in _[Physical Review Letters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical\_Review\_Letters "Physical Review Letters")_ measured the [geodetic effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodetic_effect "Geodetic effect") with an error of about 0.2 percent. The results reported the frame dragging effect (caused by Earth's rotation) added up to 37 milliarcseconds with an error of about 19 percent.[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-Everitt-74) Investigator Francis Everitt explained that a milliarcsecond "is the width of a human hair seen at the distance of 10 miles".[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-75)

In January 2012, [LARES](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LARES_(satellite) "LARES (satellite)") satellite was launched on a [Vega](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vega_(rocket) "Vega (rocket)") rocket[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-76) to measure [Lense–Thirring effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lense%E2%80%93Thirring_effect "Lense–Thirring effect") with an accuracy of about 1%, according to its proponents.[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-77) This evaluation of the actual accuracy obtainable is a subject of debate.[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-Ciufolini2009-78)[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-79)[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-80)

### Tests of the gravitational potential at small distances

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tests_of_general_relativity&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Tests of the gravitational potential at small distances")]

It is possible to test whether the gravitational potential continues with the inverse square law at very small distances. Tests so far have focused on a divergence from GR in the form of a [Yukawa potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yukawa_potential "Yukawa potential")![Image 9: {\textstyle V(r)=V_{0}\left(1+\alpha e^{-r/\lambda }\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ff80958ad823d4e16ce42177ea416cbfd898c49f), but no evidence for a potential of this kind has been found. The Yukawa potential with ![Image 10: {\displaystyle \alpha =1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/03d67a45a44be8b8f15e99b7def2b0cf0aba1717) has been ruled out down to _λ_ = 5.6×10−5 m.[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-81)

#### Mössbauer rotor experiment

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tests_of_general_relativity&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Mössbauer rotor experiment")]

It was conceived as a means to measure the [time dilation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_dilation "Time dilation") effect on Earth after being motivated by Einstein's [equivalence principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equivalence_principle "Equivalence principle") that implies a rotating observer will be subject to the same transformations as an observer in a gravitational field.[[82]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-82) Mössbauer rotor experiments hence permit a precise terrestrial test of the [relativistic Doppler effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_Doppler_effect "Relativistic Doppler effect"). From a radioactive source fixed at the center of a spinning disc or rod, [gamma rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamma_rays "Gamma rays") travel to an absorber at the rim (in some variations of the experiment this scheme was reversed) and an unabsorbed number of them pass through depending on the rotational speed to arrive at a stationary counter (_i.e._, detector of gamma quanta resting in the lab frame). In lieu with the [Clock hypothesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clock_hypothesis "Clock hypothesis"), Einstein's [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity") predicts that the moving absorber's clock at the rim should retard by a specific amount due to time dilation on account of centrifugal binding alone compared to a rest frame absorber. So the transmission of gamma photons through the absorber should increase during rotation, which can be subsequently measured by the stationary counter beyond the absorber. This prediction was actually observed using the [Mössbauer effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M%C3%B6ssbauer_effect "Mössbauer effect"), since the equivalence principle, as originally suggested by Einstein, implicitly allows the association of the time dilation due to rotation (calculated as a result of the change in the detector's count rate) with gravitational time dilation. Such experiments were pioneered by Hay _et al._ (1960),[[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-83) Champeney _et al._ (1965),[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-84) and Kündig (1963),[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-85) and all of them had declared confirmation of the prediction of Einstein's theory of relativity.

Be that as it may, an early 21st Century re-examination of these endeavors called into question the validity of the past obtained results claiming to have verified time dilation as predicted by Einstein's relativity theory,[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-86)[[87]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-87) whereby novel experimentations were carried out that uncovered an _extra energy shift_ between emitted and absorbed radiation next to the classical relativistic dilation of time.[[88]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-88)[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-89) This discovery was first explained as discrediting [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity") and successfully confirming at the laboratory scale the predictions of an alternative theory of gravity developed by T. Yarman and his colleagues.[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-90) Against this development, a contentious attempt was made to explain the disclosed extra energy shift as arising from a so-far unknown and allegedly missed _clock synchronization effect_,[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-91)[[92]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-92) which was unusually awarded a prize in 2018 by the [Gravity Research Foundation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity_Research_Foundation "Gravity Research Foundation") for having secured _a new proof of general relativity_.[[93]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-93) However, at the same time period, it was revealed that said author committed several mathematical errors in his calculations,[[94]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-94) and the supposed contribution of the so-called clock synchronization to the measured time dilation is in fact practically null.[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-95)[[96]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-96)[[97]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-97)[[98]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-98)[[99]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-99)[[100]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-100) As a consequence, a general relativistic explanation for the outcomes of Mössbauer rotor experiments remains open.

The very strong gravitational fields that are present close to [black holes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_hole "Black hole"), especially those [supermassive black holes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supermassive_black_hole "Supermassive black hole") which are thought to power [active galactic nuclei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Active_galactic_nuclei "Active galactic nuclei") and the more active [quasars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasar "Quasar"), belong to a field of intense active research. Observations of these quasars and active galactic nuclei are difficult, and interpretation of the observations is heavily dependent upon astrophysical models other than general relativity or competing fundamental [theories of gravitation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alternatives_to_general_relativity "Alternatives to general relativity"), but they are qualitatively consistent with the black hole concept as modeled in general relativity.

[Pulsars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulsars "Pulsars") are rapidly rotating [neutron stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron_star "Neutron star") which emit regular radio pulses as they rotate. As such they act as clocks which allow very precise monitoring of their orbital motions. Observations of pulsars in orbit around other stars have all demonstrated substantial [periapsis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Periapsis "Periapsis") precessions that cannot be accounted for classically but can be accounted for by using general relativity. For example, the Hulse–Taylor [binary pulsar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_pulsar "Binary pulsar")[PSR B1913+16](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PSR_B1913%2B16 "PSR B1913+16") (a pair of neutron stars in which one is detected as a pulsar) has an observed precession of over 4° of arc per year (periastron shift per orbit only about 10−6). This precession has been used to compute the masses of the components.

Similarly to the way in which atoms and molecules emit electromagnetic radiation, a gravitating mass that is in [quadrupole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrupole "Quadrupole") type or higher order vibration, or is asymmetric and in rotation, can emit gravitational waves.[[101]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-101) These [gravitational waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_waves "Gravitational waves") are predicted to travel at the [speed of light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light "Speed of light"). For example, planets orbiting the Sun constantly lose energy via gravitational radiation, but this effect is so small that it is unlikely it will be observed in the near future (Earth radiates about 200 watts of [gravitational radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_wave "Gravitational wave")).

The radiation of gravitational waves has been inferred from the [Hulse–Taylor binary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PSR_B1913%2B16 "PSR B1913+16") (and other binary pulsars).[[102]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-102) Precise timing of the pulses shows that the stars orbit only approximately according to [Kepler's Laws](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler%27s_Laws "Kepler's Laws"): over time they gradually spiral towards each other, demonstrating an [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy "Energy") loss in close agreement with the predicted energy radiated by gravitational waves.[[103]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-103)[[104]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-104) For their discovery of the first binary pulsar and measuring its orbital decay due to gravitational-wave emission, [Hulse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russell_Alan_Hulse "Russell Alan Hulse") and [Taylor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Hooton_Taylor,_Jr. "Joseph Hooton Taylor, Jr.") won the 1993 [Nobel Prize in Physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physics "Nobel Prize in Physics").[[105]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-105)

A "double pulsar" discovered in 2003, [PSR J0737-3039](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PSR_J0737-3039 "PSR J0737-3039"), has a periastron precession of 16.90° per year; unlike the Hulse–Taylor binary, both [neutron stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron_star "Neutron star") are detected as pulsars, allowing precision timing of both members of the system. Due to this, the tight orbit, the fact that the system is almost edge-on, and the very low transverse velocity of the system as seen from Earth, J0737−3039 provides by far the best system for strong-field tests of general relativity known so far. Several distinct relativistic effects are observed, including orbital decay as in the Hulse–Taylor system. After observing the system for two and a half years, four independent tests of general relativity were possible, the most precise (the Shapiro delay) confirming the general relativity prediction within 0.05%[[106]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-106) (nevertheless the periastron shift per orbit is only about 0.0013% of a circle and thus it is not a higher-order relativity test).

In 2013, an international team of astronomers reported new data from observing a pulsar-white dwarf system [PSR J0348+0432](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PSR_J0348%2B0432 "PSR J0348+0432"), in which they have been able to measure a change in the orbital period of 8 millionths of a second per year, and confirmed GR predictions in a regime of extreme gravitational fields never probed before;[[107]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-107) but there are still some competing theories that would agree with these data.[[108]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-108)

### Direct detection of gravitational waves

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tests_of_general_relativity&action=edit&section=17 "Edit section: Direct detection of gravitational waves")]

A number of [gravitational-wave detectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational-wave_detector "Gravitational-wave detector") have been built with the intent of directly detecting the gravitational waves emanating from such astronomical events as the merger of two [neutron stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron_star "Neutron star") or [black holes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_hole "Black hole"). In February 2016, the [Advanced LIGO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Advanced_LIGO "Advanced LIGO") team announced that they had [directly detected gravitational waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_wave_detection "Gravitational wave detection") from a stellar [binary black hole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_black_hole "Binary black hole") merger,[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-Discovery_2016-1)[[109]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-109)[[110]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-NSF-110) with additional detections announced in June 2016, June 2017, and August 2017.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-conover2017-2)[[111]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-111)

General relativity predicts gravitational waves, as does any theory of gravitation in which changes in the gravitational field propagate at a finite speed.[[112]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-112) Then, the LIGO response function could discriminate among the various theories.[[113]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-113)[[114]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-114) Since gravitational waves can be directly detected,[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-Discovery_2016-1)[[110]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-NSF-110) it is possible to use them to learn about the Universe. This is [gravitational-wave astronomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational-wave_astronomy "Gravitational-wave astronomy"). Gravitational-wave astronomy can test general relativity by verifying that the observed waves are of the form predicted (for example, that they only have two transverse polarizations), and by checking that [black holes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_hole "Black hole") are the objects described by solutions of the [Einstein field equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_field_equations "Einstein field equations").[[115]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-115)[[116]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-116)[[117]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-tests-117)

Gravitational-wave astronomy can also test Maxwell-Einstein field equations. This version of the field equations predicts that spinning magnetars (i.e., neutron stars with extremely strong magnetic dipole field) should emit gravitational waves.[[118]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-118)

"These amazing observations are the confirmation of a lot of theoretical work, including Einstein's general theory of relativity, which predicts gravitational waves", said Stephen Hawking.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-Discovery_2016-1)

### Direct observation of black holes

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tests_of_general_relativity&action=edit&section=18 "Edit section: Direct observation of black holes")]

[![Image 11](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Black_hole_-_Messier_87_crop_max_res.jpg/500px-Black_hole_-_Messier_87_crop_max_res.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Black_hole_-_Messier_87_crop_max_res.jpg)

A bright ring of material surrounding a dark center that marks the shadow of the M87's [supermassive black hole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supermassive_black_hole "Supermassive black hole"). The image also provided a key confirmation of General relativity.[[119]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-First_M87_EHT-119)

The galaxy M87 was the subject of observation by the [Event Horizon Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Event_Horizon_Telescope "Event Horizon Telescope") (EHT) in 2017; the 10 April 2019 issue of _[Astrophysical Journal Letters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrophysical\_Journal\_Letters "Astrophysical Journal Letters")_ (vol. 875, No. 1) was dedicated to the EHT results, publishing six [open-access](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open-access "Open-access") papers. The [event horizon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Event_horizon "Event horizon") of the black hole at the center of M87 was directly imaged at the wavelength of radio waves by the EHT; the image was revealed in a press conference on 10 April 2019, the first image of a black hole's event horizon.[[120]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-120)[[119]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-First_M87_EHT-119) In May 2022, the EHT provided the first image of the super massive black hole Sagittarius A* in the center of our own Milky Way galaxy.

### Gravitational redshift and orbit precession of star in strong gravity field

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tests_of_general_relativity&action=edit&section=19 "Edit section: Gravitational redshift and orbit precession of star in strong gravity field")]

Gravitational redshift in light from the [S2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S2_(star) "S2 (star)") star orbiting the supermassive black hole [Sagittarius A*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sagittarius_A* "Sagittarius A*") in the center of the Milky Way has been measured with the [Very Large Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Very_Large_Telescope "Very Large Telescope") using GRAVITY, NACO and SIFONI instruments.[[121]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-121)[[122]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-122) Additionally, there has now been detection of the Schwarzschild precession in the orbit of the star S2 near the Galactic centre massive black hole.[[123]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-123)

### Strong equivalence principle

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tests_of_general_relativity&action=edit&section=20 "Edit section: Strong equivalence principle")]

The strong equivalence principle of general relativity requires universality of free fall to apply even to bodies with strong self-gravity. Direct tests of this principle using Solar System bodies are limited by the weak self-gravity of the bodies, and tests using pulsar–white-dwarf binaries have been limited by the weak gravitational pull of the Milky Way. With the discovery of a triple star system called [PSR J0337+1715](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PSR_J0337%2B1715 "PSR J0337+1715"), located about 4,200 light-years from Earth, the strong equivalence principle can be tested with a high accuracy. This system contains a [neutron star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron_star "Neutron star") in a 1.6-day orbit with a [white dwarf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_dwarf "White dwarf") star, and the pair in a 327-day orbit with another white dwarf further away. This system permits a test that compares how the gravitational pull of the outer white dwarf affects the pulsar, which has strong self-gravity, and the inner white dwarf. The result shows that the accelerations of the pulsar and its nearby white-dwarf companion differ fractionally by no more than 2.6×10−6 (95% [confidence level](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confidence_interval "Confidence interval")).[[124]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-124)[[125]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-125)[[126]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-126)

This technique is based on the idea that [photon trajectories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesics_in_general_relativity "Geodesics in general relativity") are modified in the presence of a gravitational body. A very common astrophysical system in the universe is a [black hole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_hole "Black hole") surrounded by an [accretion disk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accretion_disk "Accretion disk"). The radiation from the general neighborhood, including the accretion disk, is affected by the nature of the central black hole. Assuming Einstein's theory is correct, astrophysical black holes are described by the Kerr metric. (A consequence of the [no-hair theorems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/No-hair_theorem "No-hair theorem").) Thus, by analyzing the radiation from such systems, it is possible to test Einstein's theory.

Most of the radiation from these black hole – accretion disk systems (e.g., [black hole binaries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray_binary "X-ray binary") and [active galactic nuclei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Active_galactic_nucleus "Active galactic nucleus")) arrives in the form of X-rays. When modeled, the radiation is decomposed into several components. Tests of Einstein's theory are possible with the thermal spectrum (only for black hole binaries) and the reflection spectrum (for both black hole binaries and active galactic nuclei). The former is not expected to provide strong constraints,[[127]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-127) while the latter is much more promising.[[128]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-128) In both cases, systematic uncertainties might make such tests more challenging.[[129]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-129)

Tests of general relativity on the largest scales are not nearly so stringent as Solar System tests.[[130]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-130) The earliest such test was the prediction and discovery of the [expansion of the universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expansion_of_the_universe "Expansion of the universe").[[131]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-Rudnicki28-131) In 1922, [Alexander Friedmann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_Friedmann "Alexander Friedmann") found that the Einstein equations have non-stationary solutions (even in the presence of the [cosmological constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmological_constant "Cosmological constant")).[[132]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-Pauli1-132)[[133]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-133) In 1927, [Georges Lemaître](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georges_Lema%C3%AEtre "Georges Lemaître") showed that static solutions of the Einstein equations, which are possible in the presence of the cosmological constant, are unstable, and therefore the static universe envisioned by Einstein could not exist (it must either expand or contract).[[132]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-Pauli1-132) Lemaître made an explicit prediction that the universe should expand.[[134]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-Kragh,_2003,_p._153-134) He also derived a redshift-distance relationship, which is now known as the [Hubble Law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble_Law "Hubble Law").[[134]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-Kragh,_2003,_p._153-134) Later, in 1931, Einstein himself agreed with the results of Friedmann and Lemaître.[[132]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-Pauli1-132) The expansion of the universe discovered by [Edwin Hubble](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edwin_Hubble "Edwin Hubble") in 1929[[132]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-Pauli1-132) was then considered by many (and continues to be considered by some now) as a direct confirmation of general relativity.[[135]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-135) In the 1930s, largely due to the work of [E. A. Milne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/E._A._Milne "E. A. Milne"), it was realised that the linear relationship between redshift and distance derives from the general assumption of uniformity and isotropy rather than specifically from general relativity.[[131]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-Rudnicki28-131) However the prediction of a non-static universe was non-trivial, indeed dramatic, and primarily motivated by general relativity.[[136]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-136)

Some other cosmological tests include searches for primordial gravitational waves generated during [cosmic inflation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_inflation "Cosmic inflation"), which may be detected in the [cosmic microwave background](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background "Cosmic microwave background")[polarization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polarization_(waves) "Polarization (waves)")[[137]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-137) or by a proposed space-based [gravitational-wave interferometer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational-wave_detector "Gravitational-wave detector") called the [Big Bang Observer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_Observer "Big Bang Observer"). Other tests at high redshift are constraints on other theories of gravity,[[138]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-138)[[139]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-139) and the variation of the gravitational constant since [Big Bang nucleosynthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_nucleosynthesis "Big Bang nucleosynthesis") (it varied by no more than 40% since then).[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

In August 2017, the findings of tests conducted by astronomers using the [European Southern Observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Southern_Observatory "European Southern Observatory")'s [Very Large Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Very_Large_Telescope "Very Large Telescope") (VLT), among other instruments, were released, and positively demonstrated gravitational effects predicted by Albert Einstein. One of these tests observed the orbit of the stars circling around [Sagittarius A*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sagittarius_A* "Sagittarius A*"), a black hole about 4 million times as massive as the sun. Einstein's theory suggested that large objects bend the space around them, causing other objects to diverge from the straight lines they would otherwise follow. Although previous studies have validated Einstein's theory, this was the first time his theory had been tested on such a gigantic object. The findings were published in _[The Astrophysical Journal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The\_Astrophysical\_Journal "The Astrophysical Journal")_.[[140]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-Einstein-TheoryOfRelativity-140)[[141]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-SeanDuffy2017-141)

### Gravitational lensing

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tests_of_general_relativity&action=edit&section=23 "Edit section: Gravitational lensing")]

Astronomers using the Hubble Space Telescope and the Very Large Telescope have made precise tests of general relativity on galactic scales. The nearby galaxy [ESO 325-G004](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ESO_325-G004 "ESO 325-G004") acts as a strong gravitational lens, distorting light from a distant galaxy behind it to create an [Einstein ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_ring "Einstein ring") around its centre. By comparing the mass of ESO 325-G004 (from measurements of the motions of stars inside this galaxy) with the curvature of space around it, astronomers found that gravity behaves as predicted by general relativity on these astronomical length-scales.[[142]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-142)[[143]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_note-143)

101.   **[^](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#cite_ref-101)**In general relativity, a perfectly spherical star (in vacuum) that expands or contracts while remaining perfectly spherical _cannot_ emit any gravitational waves (similar to the lack of e/m radiation from a pulsating charge), as [Birkhoff's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Birkhoff%27s_theorem_(relativity) "Birkhoff's theorem (relativity)") says that the geometry remains the same exterior to the star.
