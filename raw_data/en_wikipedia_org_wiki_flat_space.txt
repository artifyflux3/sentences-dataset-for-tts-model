A migrating wild-type _[Dictyostelium discoideum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dictyostelium\_discoideum "Dictyostelium discoideum")_ cell whose boundary is colored by curvature. Scale bar: 5 Î¼m.

In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), **curvature** is any of several strongly related concepts in [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry") that intuitively measure the amount by which a [curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curve "Curve") deviates from being a [straight line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Straight_line "Straight line") or by which a [surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_(mathematics) "Surface (mathematics)") deviates from being a [plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_(geometry) "Plane (geometry)"). If a curve or surface is contained in a larger space, curvature can be defined _extrinsically_ relative to the ambient space. [Curvature of Riemannian manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvature_of_Riemannian_manifolds "Curvature of Riemannian manifolds") of dimension at least two can be defined _intrinsically_ without reference to a larger space.

For curves, the canonical example is that of a [circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle "Circle"), which has a curvature equal to the [reciprocal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplicative_inverse "Multiplicative inverse") of its [radius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radius "Radius"). Smaller circles bend more sharply, and hence have higher curvature. The curvature _at a point_ of a [differentiable curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differentiable_curve "Differentiable curve") is the curvature of its [osculating circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osculating_circle "Osculating circle") â€” that is, the circle that best approximates the curve near this point. The curvature of a straight line is zero. In contrast to the [tangent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent "Tangent"), which is a vector quantity, the curvature at a point is typically a [scalar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_(mathematics) "Scalar (mathematics)") quantity, that is, it is expressed by a single [real number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number").

For surfaces (and, more generally for higher-dimensional [manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manifold "Manifold")), that are [embedded](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Embedding "Embedding") in a [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space"), the concept of curvature is more complex, as it depends on the choice of a direction on the surface or manifold. This leads to the concepts of _maximal curvature_, _[minimal curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minimal\_surface "Minimal surface")_, and _[mean curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mean\_curvature "Mean curvature")_.

In _Tractatus de configurationibus qualitatum et motuum,_[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flat_space#cite_note-1) the 14th-century philosopher and mathematician [Nicole Oresme](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nicole_Oresme "Nicole Oresme") introduces the concept of curvature as a measure of departure from straightness; for circles he has the curvature as being inversely proportional to the radius; and he attempts to extend this idea to other curves as a continuously varying magnitude.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flat_space#cite_note-2)

The curvature of a [differentiable curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differentiable_curve "Differentiable curve") was originally defined through [osculating circles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osculating_circle "Osculating circle"). In this setting, [Augustin-Louis Cauchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Augustin-Louis_Cauchy "Augustin-Louis Cauchy") showed that the center of curvature is the intersection point of two infinitely close [normal lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_(geometry) "Normal (geometry)") to the curve.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flat_space#cite_note-Cauchy-3)

Intuitively, the curvature describes for any part of a curve how much the curve direction changes over a small distance travelled (e.g. angle per length in [rad/m](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radians_per_metre "Radians per metre")), so it is a measure of the [instantaneous rate of change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Instantaneous_rate_of_change "Instantaneous rate of change") of _direction_ of a point that moves on the curve: the larger the curvature, the larger this rate of change. In other words, the curvature measures how fast the unit tangent vector to the curve at point P rotates[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flat_space#cite_note-4) when point P moves at unit speed along the curve. In fact, it can be proved that this instantaneous rate of change of direction is exactly the curvature.

More precisely, suppose that the point is moving on the curve at a constant speed of one unit per time, that is, the position of the point _P_(_s_) is a unit-speed function of the parameter s. (The parameter may be thought as time or as [arc length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arc_length "Arc length") from a given origin.) Let **T**(_s_) be a [unit tangent vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_tangent_vector "Unit tangent vector") of the curve at _P_(_s_), which is also the [derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative "Derivative") of _P_(_s_) with respect to s. Then, the derivative of **T**(_s_) with respect to s is a vector that is normal to the curve and whose length is the curvature.

To be meaningful, the definition of the curvature and its different characterizations require that the curve is [continuously differentiable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuously_differentiable "Continuously differentiable") near P, for having a tangent that varies continuously; it requires also that the curve is twice differentiable at P, for insuring the existence of the involved limits, and of the derivative of **T**(_s_).

The characterization of the curvature in terms of the derivative of the unit tangent vector is probably less intuitive than the definition in terms of the osculating circle, but formulas for computing the curvature are easier to deduce. Therefore, and also because of its use in [kinematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematics "Kinematics"), this characterization is often given as a definition of the curvature.

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Osculating.svg/250px-Osculating.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Osculating.svg)

Historically, the curvature of a differentiable curve was defined through the [osculating circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osculating_circle "Osculating circle"), which is the circle that best approximates the curve at a point. More precisely, given a point P on a curve, every other point Q of the curve defines a circle (or sometimes a line) passing through Q and [tangent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_(geometry) "Tangent (geometry)") to the curve at P. The osculating circle is the [limit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limit_(mathematics) "Limit (mathematics)"), if it exists, of this circle when Q tends to P. Then the _center_ and the _radius of curvature_ of the curve at P are the center and the radius of the osculating circle. The curvature is the [reciprocal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplicative_inverse "Multiplicative inverse") of radius of curvature. That is, the curvature is

![Image 3: {\displaystyle \kappa ={\frac {1}{R}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8a9ed4e46b193ab70e99c22b61477e74e8188eb2)
where R is the radius of curvature[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flat_space#cite_note-5) (the whole circle has this curvature, it can be read as turn 2Ï€ over the length 2Ï€ R).

This definition is difficult to manipulate and to express in formulas. Therefore, other equivalent definitions have been introduced.

In the general case of a curve, the sign of the signed curvature is somewhat arbitrary, as it depends on the orientation of the curve. In the case of the graph of a function, there is a natural orientation by increasing values of x. This makes significant the sign of the signed curvature.

The sign of the signed curvature is the same as the sign of the second derivative of f. If it is positive then the graph has an upward concavity, and, if it is negative the graph has a downward concavity. If it is zero, then one has an [inflection point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inflection_point "Inflection point") or an [undulation point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Undulation_point "Undulation point").

When the [slope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slope "Slope") of the graph (that is the derivative of the function) is small, the signed curvature is well approximated by the second derivative. ![Image 19: {\displaystyle k(x)=y''{\Bigl (}1+O{\bigl (}{\textstyle y'}^{2}{\bigr )}{\Bigr )}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9fd9ac56898fe613dd943b195d4e876e3ce30df6)
It is common in [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics") and [engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineering "Engineering") to approximate the curvature with the second derivative, for example, in [beam theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beam_theory "Beam theory") or for deriving the [wave equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wave_equation "Wave equation") of a string under tension, and other applications where small slopes are involved. This often allows systems that are otherwise [nonlinear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nonlinear_system "Nonlinear system") to be treated approximately as linear.

The curvature of curves drawn on a [surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_(mathematics) "Surface (mathematics)") is the main tool for the defining and studying the curvature of the surface.

For a curve drawn on a surface (embedded in three-dimensional [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space")), several curvatures are defined, which relates the direction of curvature to the surface's unit [normal vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_vector "Normal vector"), including the:

*   [normal curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_curvature "Normal curvature")
*   [geodesic curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic_curvature "Geodesic curvature")
*   [geodesic torsion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic_torsion "Geodesic torsion")

Any non-singular curve on a smooth surface has its tangent vector **T** contained in the [tangent plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_plane "Tangent plane") of the surface. The **normal curvature**, _k_ n, is the curvature of the curve projected onto the plane containing the curve's tangent **T** and the surface normal **u**; the **geodesic curvature**, _k_ g, is the curvature of the curve projected onto the surface's tangent plane; and the **geodesic torsion** (or **relative torsion**), _Ï„_ r, measures the rate of change of the surface normal around the curve's tangent.

The above quantities are related by:

#### Principal curvature

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Curvature&action=edit&section=19 "Edit section: Principal curvature")]

[![Image 48](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Minimal_surface_curvature_planes-en.svg/330px-Minimal_surface_curvature_planes-en.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Minimal_surface_curvature_planes-en.svg)

[Saddle surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saddle_surface "Saddle surface") with normal planes in directions of principal curvatures

All curves on the surface with the same tangent vector at a given point will have the same normal curvature, which is the same as the curvature of the curve obtained by intersecting the surface with the plane containing **T** and **u**. Taking all possible tangent vectors, the maximum and minimum values of the normal curvature at a point are called the **principal curvatures**, _k_ 1 and _k_ 2, and the directions of the corresponding tangent vectors are called **principal normal directions**.

### Developable surfaces

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Curvature&action=edit&section=21 "Edit section: Developable surfaces")]

Some curved surfaces, such as those made from a smooth sheet of paper, can be flattened down into the plane without distorting their intrinsic features in any way. Such [developable surfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Developable_surface "Developable surface") have zero Gaussian curvature (see below).[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flat_space#cite_note-10)

In contrast to curves, which do not have intrinsic curvature, but do have extrinsic curvature (they only have a curvature given an embedding), surfaces can have intrinsic curvature, independent of an embedding. The [Gaussian curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaussian_curvature "Gaussian curvature"), named after [Carl Friedrich Gauss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Friedrich_Gauss "Carl Friedrich Gauss"), is equal to the product of the principal curvatures, _k_ 1 _k_ 2. It has a dimension of lengthâˆ’2 and is positive for [spheres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere "Sphere"), negative for one-sheet [hyperboloids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperboloid "Hyperboloid") and zero for planes and [cylinders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylinder#Cylindrical_surfaces "Cylinder"). It determines whether a surface is [locally](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Locally "Locally")[convex](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/convex "wikt:convex") (when it is positive) or locally saddle-shaped (when it is negative).

Gaussian curvature is an _intrinsic_ property of the surface, meaning it does not depend on the particular [embedding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Embedding "Embedding") of the surface; intuitively, this means that ants living on the surface could determine the Gaussian curvature. For example, an ant living on a sphere could measure the sum of the interior angles of a triangle and determine that it was greater than 180 degrees, implying that the space it inhabited had positive curvature. On the other hand, an ant living on a cylinder would not detect any such departure from [Euclidean geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_geometry "Euclidean geometry"); in particular the ant could not detect that the two surfaces have different mean curvatures (see below), which is a purely extrinsic type of curvature.

Formally, Gaussian curvature only depends on the [Riemannian metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_metric "Riemannian metric") of the surface. This is [Gauss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Friedrich_Gauss "Carl Friedrich Gauss")'s celebrated [Theorema Egregium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theorema_Egregium "Theorema Egregium"), which he found while concerned with geographic surveys and mapmaking.

An intrinsic definition of the Gaussian curvature at a point P is the following: imagine an ant which is tied to P with a short thread of length r. It runs around P while the thread is completely stretched and measures the length _C_(_r_) of one complete trip around P. If the surface were flat, the ant would find _C_(_r_) = 2Ï€ _r_. On curved surfaces, the formula for _C_(_r_) will be different, and the Gaussian curvature K at the point P can be computed by the [Bertrandâ€“Diguetâ€“Puiseux theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bertrand%E2%80%93Diguet%E2%80%93Puiseux_theorem "Bertrandâ€“Diguetâ€“Puiseux theorem") as

![Image 49: {\displaystyle K=\lim _{r\to 0^{+}}3\left({\frac {2\pi r-C(r)}{\pi r^{3}}}\right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fb2e38d0cd6c64c767d6f0f88fe3cde6a644f43f)
The [integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral "Integral") of the Gaussian curvature over the whole surface is closely related to the surface's [Euler characteristic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler_characteristic "Euler characteristic"); see the [Gaussâ€“Bonnet theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauss%E2%80%93Bonnet_theorem "Gaussâ€“Bonnet theorem").

The discrete analog of curvature, corresponding to curvature being concentrated at a point and particularly useful for [polyhedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyhedra "Polyhedra"), is the [(angular) defect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Defect_(geometry) "Defect (geometry)"); the analog for the [Gaussâ€“Bonnet theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauss%E2%80%93Bonnet_theorem "Gaussâ€“Bonnet theorem") is [Descartes' theorem on total angular defect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Defect_(geometry)#Descartes.27_theorem "Defect (geometry)").

Because (Gaussian) curvature can be defined without reference to an embedding space, it is not necessary that a surface be embedded in a higher-dimensional space in order to be curved. Such an intrinsically curved two-dimensional surface is a simple example of a [Riemannian manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_manifold "Riemannian manifold").

The mean curvature is an _extrinsic_ measure of curvature equal to half the sum of the [principal curvatures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principal_curvature "Principal curvature"), â _k_ 1 + _k_ 2/2â . It has a dimension of lengthâˆ’1. Mean curvature is closely related to the first variation of [surface area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_area "Surface area"). In particular, a [minimal surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minimal_surface "Minimal surface") such as a [soap film](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soap_film "Soap film") has mean curvature zero and a [soap bubble](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soap_bubble "Soap bubble") has constant mean curvature. Unlike Gauss curvature, the mean curvature is extrinsic and depends on the embedding, for instance, a [cylinder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylinder_(geometry) "Cylinder (geometry)") and a plane are locally [isometric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isometry "Isometry") but the mean curvature of a plane is zero while that of a cylinder is nonzero.

### Second fundamental form

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Curvature&action=edit&section=24 "Edit section: Second fundamental form")]

The intrinsic and extrinsic curvature of a surface can be combined in the second fundamental form. This is a [quadratic form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadratic_form "Quadratic form") in the tangent plane to the surface at a point whose value at a particular tangent vector **X** to the surface is the normal component of the acceleration of a curve along the surface tangent to **X**; that is, it is the normal curvature to a curve tangent to **X** (see [above](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flat_space#Curves_on_surfaces)). Symbolically,

![Image 50: {\displaystyle \operatorname {I\!I} (\mathbf {X} ,\mathbf {X} )=\mathbf {N} \cdot (\nabla _{\mathbf {X} }\mathbf {X} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ebf01f82537f24c445e8c58c5d470f6701100942)
where **N** is the unit normal to the surface. For unit tangent vectors **X**, the second fundamental form assumes the maximum value _k_ 1 and minimum value _k_ 2, which occur in the principal directions **u**1 and **u**2, respectively. Thus, by the [principal axis theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principal_axis_theorem "Principal axis theorem"), the second fundamental form is

![Image 51: {\displaystyle \operatorname {I\!I} (\mathbf {X} ,\mathbf {X} )=k_{1}\left(\mathbf {X} \cdot \mathbf {u} _{1}\right)^{2}+k_{2}\left(\mathbf {X} \cdot \mathbf {u} _{2}\right)^{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9ea9c35e6ccb45df77ce10075734c210ff1f5514)
Thus the second fundamental form encodes both the intrinsic and extrinsic curvatures.

An encapsulation of surface curvature can be found in the shape operator, _S_, which is a [self-adjoint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-adjoint_operator "Self-adjoint operator")[linear operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_operator "Linear operator") from the tangent plane to itself (specifically, the differential of the [Gauss map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauss_map "Gauss map")).

For a surface with tangent vectors **X** and normal **N**, the shape operator can be expressed compactly in [index summation notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_notation "Einstein notation") as

(Compare the [alternative expression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flat_space#Frenet%E2%80%93Serret_formulas_for_plane_curves) of curvature for a plane curve.)

The [Weingarten equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weingarten_equations "Weingarten equations") give the value of _S_ in terms of the coefficients of the [first](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_fundamental_form "First fundamental form") and [second fundamental forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_fundamental_form "Second fundamental form") as

By extension of the former argument, a space of three or more dimensions can be intrinsically curved. The curvature is _intrinsic_ in the sense that it is a property defined at every point in the space, rather than a property defined with respect to a larger space that contains it. In general, a curved space may or may not be conceived as being embedded in a higher-dimensional [ambient space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ambient_space "Ambient space"); if not then its curvature can only be defined intrinsically.

After the discovery of the intrinsic definition of curvature, which is closely connected with [non-Euclidean geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-Euclidean_geometry "Non-Euclidean geometry"), many mathematicians and scientists questioned whether ordinary physical space might be curved, although the success of Euclidean geometry up to that time meant that the radius of curvature must be astronomically large. In the theory of [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity"), which describes [gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity "Gravity") and [cosmology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_cosmology "Physical cosmology"), the idea is slightly generalised to the "curvature of [spacetime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spacetime "Spacetime")"; in relativity theory spacetime is a [pseudo-Riemannian manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudo-Riemannian_manifold "Pseudo-Riemannian manifold"). Once a time coordinate is defined, the three-dimensional space corresponding to a particular time is generally a curved Riemannian manifold; but since the time coordinate choice is largely arbitrary, it is the underlying spacetime curvature that is physically significant.

Although an arbitrarily curved space is very complex to describe, the curvature of a space which is locally [isotropic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotropic "Isotropic") and [homogeneous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homogeneous_space "Homogeneous space") is described by a single Gaussian curvature, as for a surface; mathematically these are strong conditions, but they correspond to reasonable physical assumptions (all points and all directions are indistinguishable). A positive curvature corresponds to the inverse square radius of curvature; an example is a sphere or [hypersphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypersphere "Hypersphere"). An example of negatively curved space is [hyperbolic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_geometry "Hyperbolic geometry") (see also: [non-positive curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-positive_curvature "Non-positive curvature")). A space or space-time with zero curvature is called _**flat**_. For example, [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space") is an example of a flat space, and [Minkowski space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minkowski_space "Minkowski space") is an example of a flat spacetime. There are other examples of flat geometries in both settings, though. A [torus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torus "Torus") or a [cylinder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylinder_(geometry) "Cylinder (geometry)") can both be given flat metrics, but differ in their [topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology "Topology"). Other topologies are also possible for curved space (see also: [Shape of the universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shape_of_the_universe "Shape of the universe")).

[![Image 54](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Parallel_Transport.svg/250px-Parallel_Transport.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Parallel_Transport.svg)

Moving a vector along a curve from A â†’ N â†’ B â†’ A produces another vector. The inability to return to the initial vector is measured by the holonomy of the surface. In a space with no curvature, the angle Î± is 0 degrees, and in a space with curvature, the angle Î± is greater than 0 degrees. The more space is curved, the greater the magnitude of the angle Î±. 

The mathematical notion of _curvature_ is also defined in much more general contexts.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flat_space#cite_note-11) Many of these generalizations emphasize different aspects of the curvature as it is understood in lower dimensions.

One such generalization is kinematic. The curvature of a curve can naturally be considered as a kinematic quantity, representing the force felt by a certain observer moving along the curve; analogously, curvature in higher dimensions can be regarded as a kind of [tidal force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tidal_force "Tidal force") (this is one way of thinking of the [sectional curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sectional_curvature "Sectional curvature")). This generalization of curvature depends on how nearby test particles diverge or converge when they are allowed to move freely in the space; see [Jacobi field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacobi_field "Jacobi field").

Another broad generalization of curvature comes from the study of [parallel transport](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_transport "Parallel transport") on a surface. For instance, if a vector is moved around a loop on the surface of a sphere keeping parallel throughout the motion, then the final position of the vector may not be the same as the initial position of the vector. This phenomenon is known as [holonomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holonomy "Holonomy").[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flat_space#cite_note-12) Various generalizations capture in an abstract form this idea of curvature as a measure of holonomy; see [curvature form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvature_form "Curvature form"). A closely related notion of curvature comes from [gauge theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauge_theory "Gauge theory") in physics, where the curvature represents a field and a [vector potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_potential "Vector potential") for the field is a quantity that is in general path-dependent: it may change if an observer moves around a loop.

Two more generalizations of curvature are the [scalar curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_curvature "Scalar curvature") and [Ricci curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_curvature "Ricci curvature"). In a curved surface such as the sphere, the area of a disc on the surface differs from the area of a disc of the same radius in flat space. This difference (in a suitable limit) is measured by the scalar curvature. The difference in area of a sector of the disc is measured by the Ricci curvature. Each of the scalar curvature and Ricci curvature are defined in analogous ways in three and higher dimensions. They are particularly important in relativity theory, where they both appear on the side of [Einstein's field equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein%27s_field_equations "Einstein's field equations") that represents the geometry of spacetime (the other side of which represents the presence of matter and energy). These generalizations of curvature underlie, for instance, the notion that curvature can be a property of a [measure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Measure_(mathematics) "Measure (mathematics)"); see [curvature of a measure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvature_of_a_measure "Curvature of a measure").

Another generalization of curvature relies on the ability to [compare](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comparison_theorem "Comparison theorem") a curved space with another space that has _constant_ curvature. Often this is done with triangles in the spaces. The notion of a triangle makes senses in [metric spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space "Metric space"), and this gives rise to [CAT(_k_) spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CAT(k)_space "CAT(k) space").
