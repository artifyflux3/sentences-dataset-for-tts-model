Movement of each of the components of the Boulton & Watt Steam Engine (1784) can be described by a set of equations of kinematics and kinetics.

In the [physical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics") science of [dynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynamics_(mechanics) "Dynamics (mechanics)"), **rigid-body dynamics** studies the movement of [systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_system "Physical system") of interconnected [bodies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_object "Physical object") under the action of external [forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Force "Force"). The assumption that the bodies are _[rigid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rigid\_body "Rigid body")_ (i.e. they do not [deform](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deformation_(physics) "Deformation (physics)") under the action of applied forces) simplifies analysis, by reducing the parameters that describe the configuration of the system to the translation and rotation of [reference frames](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frame_of_reference "Frame of reference") attached to each body.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rigid_body_dynamics#cite_note-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rigid_body_dynamics#cite_note-2) This excludes bodies that display [fluid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluid "Fluid"), highly [elastic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elasticity_(physics) "Elasticity (physics)"), and [plastic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasticity_(physics) "Plasticity (physics)") behavior.

The dynamics of a rigid body system is described by the laws of [kinematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematics "Kinematics") and by the application of Newton's second law ([kinetics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_laws_of_motion "Newton's laws of motion")) or their derivative form, [Lagrangian mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrangian_mechanics "Lagrangian mechanics"). The solution of these equations of motion provides a description of the position, the motion and the acceleration of the individual components of the system, and overall the system itself, as a [function of time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time-variant_system "Time-variant system"). The formulation and solution of rigid body dynamics is an important tool in the computer simulation of [mechanical systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanical_system "Mechanical system").

Planar rigid body dynamics
--------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Rigid_body_dynamics&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Planar rigid body dynamics")]

If a system of particles moves parallel to a fixed plane, the system is said to be constrained to planar movement. In this case, Newton's laws (kinetics) for a rigid system of N particles, P _i_, _i_=1,...,_N_, simplify because there is no movement in the _k_ direction. Determine the [resultant force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resultant_force "Resultant force") and [torque](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torque "Torque") at a reference point **R**, to obtain ![Image 2: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} =\sum _{i=1}^{N}m_{i}\mathbf {A} _{i},\quad \mathbf {T} =\sum _{i=1}^{N}(\mathbf {r} _{i}-\mathbf {R} )\times m_{i}\mathbf {A} _{i},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/427c0602ef18cd5d68ce4758df14a23993d3410b)

where **r**i denotes the planar trajectory of each particle.

The [kinematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematics "Kinematics") of a rigid body yields the formula for the acceleration of the particle P i in terms of the position **R** and acceleration **A** of the reference particle as well as the angular velocity vector _**ω**_ and [angular acceleration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_acceleration "Angular acceleration") vector _**α**_ of the rigid system of particles as, ![Image 3: {\displaystyle \mathbf {A} _{i}={\boldsymbol {\alpha }}\times (\mathbf {r} _{i}-\mathbf {R} )+{\boldsymbol {\omega }}\times ({\boldsymbol {\omega }}\times (\mathbf {r} _{i}-\mathbf {R} ))+\mathbf {A} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f1e346d5faec580dfe0b2e119af0afe7b58b4dd9)

For systems that are constrained to planar movement, the angular velocity and angular acceleration vectors are directed along **k** perpendicular to the plane of movement, which simplifies this acceleration equation. In this case, the acceleration vectors can be simplified by introducing the unit vectors **e**i from the reference point **R** to a point **r**i and the unit vectors ![Image 4: {\textstyle \mathbf {t} _{i}=\mathbf {k} \times \mathbf {e} _{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d1d018ac0267e984cc8663b6daf5cfdcabb9b136), so ![Image 5: {\displaystyle \mathbf {A} _{i}=\alpha (\Delta r_{i}\mathbf {t} _{i})-\omega ^{2}(\Delta r_{i}\mathbf {e} _{i})+\mathbf {A} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2a139c0293ebb8bd310e9f5ce2c4cc9eb33a1206)

This yields the resultant force on the system as ![Image 6: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} =\alpha \sum _{i=1}^{N}m_{i}\left(\Delta r_{i}\mathbf {t} _{i}\right)-\omega ^{2}\sum _{i=1}^{N}m_{i}\left(\Delta r_{i}\mathbf {e} _{i}\right)+\left(\sum _{i=1}^{N}m_{i}\right)\mathbf {A} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1762f3d507e1ce24001e61e673740e51646adce5) and torque as ![Image 7: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\mathbf {T} ={}&\sum _{i=1}^{N}(m_{i}\Delta r_{i}\mathbf {e} _{i})\times \left(\alpha (\Delta r_{i}\mathbf {t} _{i})-\omega ^{2}(\Delta r_{i}\mathbf {e} _{i})+\mathbf {A} \right)\\{}={}&\left(\sum _{i=1}^{N}m_{i}\Delta r_{i}^{2}\right)\alpha \mathbf {k} +\left(\sum _{i=1}^{N}m_{i}\Delta r_{i}\mathbf {e} _{i}\right)\times \mathbf {A} ,\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9dd9a902e179308b39676fe5218f18847edf18f2)

where ![Image 8: {\textstyle \mathbf {e} _{i}\times \mathbf {e} _{i}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8d364c8ca3b6399323c7768edf9864d56ecb18bb) and ![Image 9: {\textstyle \mathbf {e} _{i}\times \mathbf {t} _{i}=\mathbf {k} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c6e9fb743937f8a462948e9e63f830f7c3a9eb09) is the unit vector perpendicular to the plane for all of the particles P i.

Use the [center of mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Center_of_mass "Center of mass")**C** as the reference point, so these equations for Newton's laws simplify to become ![Image 10: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} =M\mathbf {A} ,\quad \mathbf {T} =I_{\textbf {C}}\alpha \mathbf {k} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8a26121f508b3cdde196a17d60739a7c75579040)

where M is the total mass and _I_**C** is the [moment of inertia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_of_inertia "Moment of inertia") about an axis perpendicular to the movement of the rigid system and through the center of mass.

Rigid body in three dimensions
------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Rigid_body_dynamics&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Rigid body in three dimensions")]

### Orientation or attitude descriptions

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Rigid_body_dynamics&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Orientation or attitude descriptions")]

Several methods to describe orientations of a rigid body in three dimensions have been developed. They are summarized in the following sections.

The first attempt to represent an orientation is attributed to [Leonhard Euler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leonhard_Euler "Leonhard Euler"). He imagined three reference frames that could rotate one around the other, and realized that by starting with a fixed reference frame and performing three rotations, he could get any other reference frame in the space (using two rotations to fix the vertical axis and another to fix the other two axes). The values of these three rotations are called [Euler angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler_angles "Euler angles"). Commonly, ![Image 11: {\displaystyle \psi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/45e5789e5d9c8f7c79744f43ecaaf8ba42a8553a) is used to denote precession, ![Image 12: {\displaystyle \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e5ab2664b422d53eb0c7df3b87e1360d75ad9af) nutation, and ![Image 13: {\displaystyle \phi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/72b1f30316670aee6270a28334bdf4f5072cdde4) intrinsic rotation.

Tait–Bryan angles, another way to describe orientation

These are three angles, also known as yaw, pitch and roll, Navigation angles and Cardan angles. Mathematically they constitute a set of six possibilities inside the twelve possible sets of Euler angles, the ordering being the one best used for describing the orientation of a vehicle such as an airplane. In aerospace engineering they are usually referred to as Euler angles.

#### Orientation vector

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Rigid_body_dynamics&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Orientation vector")]

Euler also realized that the composition of two rotations is equivalent to a single rotation about a different fixed axis ([Euler's rotation theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler%27s_rotation_theorem "Euler's rotation theorem")). Therefore, the composition of the former three angles has to be equal to only one rotation, whose axis was complicated to calculate until matrices were developed.

Based on this fact he introduced a vectorial way to describe any rotation, with a vector on the rotation axis and module equal to the value of the angle. Therefore, any orientation can be represented by a rotation vector (also called Euler vector) that leads to it from the reference frame. When used to represent an orientation, the rotation vector is commonly called orientation vector, or attitude vector.

A similar method, called [axis-angle representation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axis-angle_representation "Axis-angle representation"), describes a rotation or orientation using a [unit vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_vector "Unit vector") aligned with the rotation axis, and a separate value to indicate the angle (see figure).

With the introduction of matrices the Euler theorems were rewritten. The rotations were described by [orthogonal matrices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_matrix "Orthogonal matrix") referred to as rotation matrices or direction cosine matrices. When used to represent an orientation, a [rotation matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation_matrix "Rotation matrix") is commonly called orientation matrix, or attitude matrix.

The above-mentioned Euler vector is the [eigenvector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eigenvector "Eigenvector") of a rotation matrix (a rotation matrix has a unique real [eigenvalue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eigenvalue "Eigenvalue")). The product of two rotation matrices is the composition of rotations. Therefore, as before, the orientation can be given as the rotation from the initial frame to achieve the frame that we want to describe.

The [configuration space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Configuration_space_(physics) "Configuration space (physics)") of a non-[symmetrical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry "Symmetry") object in _n_-dimensional space is [SO(_n_)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_group "Orthogonal group")[×](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Product_topology "Product topology")[**R**_n_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space"). Orientation may be visualized by attaching a basis of [tangent vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_space "Tangent space") to an object. The direction in which each vector points determines its orientation.

#### Orientation quaternion

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Rigid_body_dynamics&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Orientation quaternion")]

Another way to describe rotations is using [rotation quaternions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quaternions_and_spatial_rotation "Quaternions and spatial rotation"), also called versors. They are equivalent to rotation matrices and rotation vectors. With respect to rotation vectors, they can be more easily converted to and from matrices. When used to represent orientations, rotation quaternions are typically called orientation quaternions or attitude quaternions.

### Newton's second law in three dimensions

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Rigid_body_dynamics&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Newton's second law in three dimensions")]

To consider rigid body dynamics in three-dimensional space, Newton's second law must be extended to define the relationship between the movement of a rigid body and the system of forces and torques that act on it.

Newton formulated his second law for a particle as, "The change of motion of an object is proportional to the force impressed and is made in the direction of the straight line in which the force is impressed."[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rigid_body_dynamics#cite_note-3) Because Newton generally referred to mass times velocity as the "motion" of a particle, the phrase "change of motion" refers to the mass times acceleration of the particle, and so this law is usually written as ![Image 18: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} =m\mathbf {a} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/93aa54e6c7e8df66d85d06b6eb0b0a2d3ec4ce20) where **F** is understood to be the only external force acting on the particle, _m_ is the mass of the particle, and **a** is its acceleration vector. The extension of Newton's second law to rigid bodies is achieved by considering a rigid system of particles.

### Rigid system of particles

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Rigid_body_dynamics&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Rigid system of particles")]

If a system of _N_ particles, P i, i=1,...,_N_, are assembled into a rigid body, then Newton's second law can be applied to each of the particles in the body. If **F**i is the external force applied to particle P i with mass _m_ i, then ![Image 19: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} _{i}+\sum _{j=1}^{N}\mathbf {F} _{ij}=m_{i}\mathbf {a} _{i},\quad i=1,\ldots ,N,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5e0c18eeacb4686829f2754866a81e1c341fede4) where **F**ij is the internal force of particle P j acting on particle P i that maintains the constant distance between these particles.

[![Image 20](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Rigid_bodies.jpg/250px-Rigid_bodies.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Rigid_bodies.jpg)

Human body modelled as a system of rigid bodies of geometrical solids. Representative bones were added for better visualization of the walking person.

An important simplification to these force equations is obtained by introducing the [resultant force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resultant_force "Resultant force") and torque that acts on the rigid system. This resultant force and torque is obtained by choosing one of the particles in the system as a reference point, **R**, where each of the external forces are applied with the addition of an associated torque. The resultant force **F** and torque **T** are given by the formulas, ![Image 21: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} =\sum _{i=1}^{N}\mathbf {F} _{i},\quad \mathbf {T} =\sum _{i=1}^{N}(\mathbf {R} _{i}-\mathbf {R} )\times \mathbf {F} _{i},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d367b694d2ec9354d0452887a816c9f459d390a0) where **R**i is the vector that defines the position of particle P i.

Newton's second law for a particle combines with these formulas for the resultant force and torque to yield, ![Image 22: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} =\sum _{i=1}^{N}m_{i}\mathbf {a} _{i},\quad \mathbf {T} =\sum _{i=1}^{N}(\mathbf {R} _{i}-\mathbf {R} )\times (m_{i}\mathbf {a} _{i}),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b02a1883901152253bc9e64a555210337ea1153f) where the internal forces **F**_ij_ cancel in pairs. The [kinematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematics "Kinematics") of a rigid body yields the formula for the acceleration of the particle P i in terms of the position **R** and acceleration **a** of the reference particle as well as the angular velocity vector ω and angular acceleration vector α of the rigid system of particles as, ![Image 23: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} _{i}=\alpha \times (\mathbf {R} _{i}-\mathbf {R} )+\omega \times (\omega \times (\mathbf {R} _{i}-\mathbf {R} ))+\mathbf {a} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/61448a42bfa530c743d6def306fb46af75e662bd)

The mass properties of the rigid body are represented by its [center of mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Center_of_mass "Center of mass") and [inertia matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_of_inertia "Moment of inertia"). Choose the reference point **R** so that it satisfies the condition ![Image 24: {\displaystyle \sum _{i=1}^{N}m_{i}(\mathbf {R} _{i}-\mathbf {R} )=0,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7eeebb0a4e1d8d7d7b02c8266f80f479bdadfd11)

then it is known as the center of mass of the system.

The inertia matrix [I R] of the system relative to the reference point **R** is defined by ![Image 25: {\displaystyle [I_{R}]=\sum _{i=1}^{N}m_{i}\left(\mathbf {I} \left(\mathbf {S} _{i}^{\textsf {T}}\mathbf {S} _{i}\right)-\mathbf {S} _{i}\mathbf {S} _{i}^{\textsf {T}}\right),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d212d70cb031aa272e6fef3690439553a1a07450)

where ![Image 26: {\displaystyle \mathbf {S} _{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/926b0315a51772356bb6ec4c269f87e390989944) is the column vector **R**i − **R**; ![Image 27: {\displaystyle \mathbf {S} _{i}^{\textsf {T}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2d53f2a737342097c01569b5ecba028b99e77ed0) is its transpose, and ![Image 28: {\displaystyle \mathbf {I} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8a458c8aeb096ce732abf346ae8edf3e4f53a126) is the 3 by 3 [identity matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Identity_matrix "Identity matrix").

![Image 29: {\displaystyle \mathbf {S} _{i}^{\textsf {T}}\mathbf {S} _{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d85c6340f01b6b5318489627ed47ee272e258763) is the scalar product of ![Image 30: {\displaystyle \mathbf {S} _{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/926b0315a51772356bb6ec4c269f87e390989944) with itself, while ![Image 31: {\displaystyle \mathbf {S} _{i}\mathbf {S} _{i}^{\textsf {T}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/04ff75eee88e2532d11c2441a769bf6962c37fd7) is the tensor product of ![Image 32: {\displaystyle \mathbf {S} _{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/926b0315a51772356bb6ec4c269f87e390989944) with itself.

### Force-torque equations

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Rigid_body_dynamics&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Force-torque equations")]

Using the center of mass and inertia matrix, the force and torque equations for a single rigid body take the form ![Image 33: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} =m\mathbf {a} ,\quad \mathbf {T} =[I_{R}]\alpha +\omega \times [I_{R}]\omega ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/04cb3ac3348dc67b9f1d15aa50079857a33a8dcf) and are known as Newton's second law of motion for a rigid body.

The dynamics of an interconnected system of rigid bodies, _B_ _i_, _j_ = 1, ..., _M_, is formulated by isolating each rigid body and introducing the interaction forces. The resultant of the external and interaction forces on each body, yields the force-torque equations ![Image 34: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} _{j}=m_{j}\mathbf {a} _{j},\quad \mathbf {T} _{j}=[I_{R}]_{j}\alpha _{j}+\omega _{j}\times [I_{R}]_{j}\omega _{j},\quad j=1,\ldots ,M.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eb22fbde3580ac19702abca9caee8df4f187f576)

Newton's formulation yields 6 _M_ equations that define the dynamics of a system of _M_ rigid bodies.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rigid_body_dynamics#cite_note-4)

### Rotation in three dimensions

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Rigid_body_dynamics&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Rotation in three dimensions")]

A rotating object, whether under the influence of torques or not, may exhibit the behaviours of [precession](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Precession "Precession") and [nutation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nutation "Nutation"). The fundamental equation describing the behavior of a rotating solid body is [Euler's equation of motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler%27s_equation_of_motion "Euler's equation of motion"): ![Image 35: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {\tau }}={\frac {D\mathbf {L} }{Dt}}={\frac {d\mathbf {L} }{dt}}+{\boldsymbol {\omega }}\times \mathbf {L} ={\frac {d(I{\boldsymbol {\omega }})}{dt}}+{\boldsymbol {\omega }}\times {I{\boldsymbol {\omega }}}=I{\boldsymbol {\alpha }}+{\boldsymbol {\omega }}\times {I{\boldsymbol {\omega }}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6c5a8badfbc980772529b28cfe3ea6ef1ee430df) where the [pseudovectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudovector "Pseudovector")**τ** and **L** are, respectively, the [torques](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torque "Torque") on the body and its [angular momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum "Angular momentum"), the scalar _I_ is its [moment of inertia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_of_inertia "Moment of inertia"), the vector **ω** is its angular velocity, the vector **α** is its angular acceleration, D is the differential in an inertial reference frame and d is the differential in a relative reference frame fixed with the body.

The solution to this equation when there is no applied torque is discussed in the articles [Euler's equation of motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler%27s_equation_of_motion "Euler's equation of motion") and [Poinsot's ellipsoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poinsot%27s_ellipsoid "Poinsot's ellipsoid").

It follows from Euler's equation that a torque **τ** applied perpendicular to the axis of rotation, and therefore perpendicular to **L**, results in a rotation about an axis perpendicular to both **τ** and **L**. This motion is called _precession_. The angular velocity of precession **Ω**P is given by the [cross product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product "Cross product"):[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]![Image 36: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {\tau }}={\boldsymbol {\Omega }}_{\mathrm {P} }\times \mathbf {L} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6887b5e83576087b8f141908c3a027eb962dc600)

[![Image 37](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Gyroscope_precession.gif/250px-Gyroscope_precession.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Gyroscope_precession.gif)

Precession of a [gyroscope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gyroscope "Gyroscope")

Precession can be demonstrated by placing a spinning top with its axis horizontal and supported loosely (frictionless toward precession) at one end. Instead of falling, as might be expected, the top appears to defy gravity by remaining with its axis horizontal, when the other end of the axis is left unsupported and the free end of the axis slowly describes a circle in a horizontal plane, the resulting precession turning. This effect is explained by the above equations. The torque on the top is supplied by a couple of forces: gravity acting downward on the device's centre of mass, and an equal force acting upward to support one end of the device. The rotation resulting from this torque is not downward, as might be intuitively expected, causing the device to fall, but perpendicular to both the gravitational torque (horizontal and perpendicular to the axis of rotation) and the axis of rotation (horizontal and outwards from the point of support), i.e., about a vertical axis, causing the device to rotate slowly about the supporting point.

Under a constant torque of magnitude _τ_, the speed of precession _Ω_ P is inversely proportional to _L_, the magnitude of its angular momentum: ![Image 38: {\displaystyle \tau ={\mathit {\Omega }}_{\mathrm {P} }L\sin \theta ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/83feb43e05c26be25916b5cc256a22ffeeb0eac2) where _θ_ is the angle between the vectors **Ω**P and **L**. Thus, if the top's spin slows down (for example, due to friction), its angular momentum decreases and so the rate of precession increases. This continues until the device is unable to rotate fast enough to support its own weight, when it stops precessing and falls off its support, mostly because friction against precession cause another precession that goes to cause the fall.

By convention, these three vectors – torque, spin, and precession – are all oriented with respect to each other according to the [right-hand rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right-hand_rule "Right-hand rule").

Virtual work of forces acting on a rigid body
---------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Rigid_body_dynamics&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Virtual work of forces acting on a rigid body")]

An alternate formulation of rigid body dynamics that has a number of convenient features is obtained by considering the [virtual work](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virtual_work "Virtual work") of forces acting on a rigid body.

The virtual work of forces acting at various points on a single rigid body can be calculated using the velocities of their point of application and the [resultant force and torque](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resultant_force "Resultant force"). To see this, let the forces **F**1, **F**2 ... **F**_n_ act on the points **R**1, **R**2 ... **R**_n_ in a rigid body.

The trajectories of **R**_i_, _i_ = 1, ..., _n_ are defined by the movement of the rigid body. The velocity of the points **R**_i_ along their trajectories are ![Image 39: {\displaystyle \mathbf {V} _{i}={\boldsymbol {\omega }}\times (\mathbf {R} _{i}-\mathbf {R} )+\mathbf {V} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/666f1a6403fe2bcfc884d6c19acb47d90d65fc45) where **ω** is the angular velocity vector of the body.

Work is computed from the [dot product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dot_product "Dot product") of each force with the displacement of its point of contact ![Image 40: {\displaystyle \delta W=\sum _{i=1}^{n}\mathbf {F} _{i}\cdot \delta \mathbf {r} _{i}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8a3f8b0f46e6e55d58d67c73c0fb3105f05414ca) If the trajectory of a rigid body is defined by a set of [generalized coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generalized_coordinates "Generalized coordinates")_q_ _j_, _j_ = 1, ..., _m_, then the virtual displacements _δ_**r**i are given by ![Image 41: {\displaystyle \delta \mathbf {r} _{i}=\sum _{j=1}^{m}{\frac {\partial \mathbf {r} _{i}}{\partial q_{j}}}\delta q_{j}=\sum _{j=1}^{m}{\frac {\partial \mathbf {V} _{i}}{\partial {\dot {q}}_{j}}}\delta q_{j}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5fd39eb7eefb474eaf220ee0bc4a702d53c5945e) The virtual work of this system of forces acting on the body in terms of the generalized coordinates becomes ![Image 42: {\displaystyle \delta W=\mathbf {F} _{1}\cdot \left(\sum _{j=1}^{m}{\frac {\partial \mathbf {V} _{1}}{\partial {\dot {q}}_{j}}}\delta q_{j}\right)+\dots +\mathbf {F} _{n}\cdot \left(\sum _{j=1}^{m}{\frac {\partial \mathbf {V} _{n}}{\partial {\dot {q}}_{j}}}\delta q_{j}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/664f819be796b2574eaff040224e20bfb624a1b8)

or collecting the coefficients of _δq j_![Image 43: {\displaystyle \delta W=\left(\sum _{i=1}^{n}\mathbf {F} _{i}\cdot {\frac {\partial \mathbf {V} _{i}}{\partial {\dot {q}}_{1}}}\right)\delta q_{1}+\dots +\left(\sum _{1=1}^{n}\mathbf {F} _{i}\cdot {\frac {\partial \mathbf {V} _{i}}{\partial {\dot {q}}_{m}}}\right)\delta q_{m}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/214a6323e22af25a49da16609b24620245ff5c34)

For simplicity consider a trajectory of a rigid body that is specified by a single generalized coordinate q, such as a rotation angle, then the formula becomes ![Image 44: {\displaystyle \delta W=\left(\sum _{i=1}^{n}\mathbf {F} _{i}\cdot {\frac {\partial \mathbf {V} _{i}}{\partial {\dot {q}}}}\right)\delta q=\left(\sum _{i=1}^{n}\mathbf {F} _{i}\cdot {\frac {\partial ({\boldsymbol {\omega }}\times (\mathbf {R} _{i}-\mathbf {R} )+\mathbf {V} )}{\partial {\dot {q}}}}\right)\delta q.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/700375171a7e560adfc388d5a6e9df99a0a19489)

Introduce the resultant force **F** and torque **T** so this equation takes the form ![Image 45: {\displaystyle \delta W=\left(\mathbf {F} \cdot {\frac {\partial \mathbf {V} }{\partial {\dot {q}}}}+\mathbf {T} \cdot {\frac {\partial {\boldsymbol {\omega }}}{\partial {\dot {q}}}}\right)\delta q.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f2d6c88843bf69c44ce323bb17d0f1892c1b686f)

The quantity _Q_ defined by ![Image 46: {\displaystyle Q=\mathbf {F} \cdot {\frac {\partial \mathbf {V} }{\partial {\dot {q}}}}+\mathbf {T} \cdot {\frac {\partial {\boldsymbol {\omega }}}{\partial {\dot {q}}}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/298137edfc43383451cb7d53a41f6c669df1229c)

is known as the [generalized force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generalized_forces "Generalized forces") associated with the virtual displacement δq. This formula generalizes to the movement of a rigid body defined by more than one generalized coordinate, that is ![Image 47: {\displaystyle \delta W=\sum _{j=1}^{m}Q_{j}\delta q_{j},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a679d873e64385d0b6211b80a7ce3c53aeba369) where ![Image 48: {\displaystyle Q_{j}=\mathbf {F} \cdot {\frac {\partial \mathbf {V} }{\partial {\dot {q}}_{j}}}+\mathbf {T} \cdot {\frac {\partial {\boldsymbol {\omega }}}{\partial {\dot {q}}_{j}}},\quad j=1,\ldots ,m.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ea4b730e85542e125203bb0f39381881812ccff2)

It is useful to note that conservative forces such as gravity and spring forces are derivable from a potential function _V_(_q_ 1, ..., _q_ _n_), known as a [potential energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potential_energy "Potential energy"). In this case the generalized forces are given by ![Image 49: {\displaystyle Q_{j}=-{\frac {\partial V}{\partial q_{j}}},\quad j=1,\ldots ,m.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4308ff5cfb56cc37b94786a305f4ee3023d3ce89)

D'Alembert's form of the principle of virtual work
--------------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Rigid_body_dynamics&action=edit&section=17 "Edit section: D'Alembert's form of the principle of virtual work")]

The equations of motion for a mechanical system of rigid bodies can be determined using D'Alembert's form of the principle of virtual work. The principle of virtual work is used to study the static equilibrium of a system of rigid bodies, however by introducing acceleration terms in Newton's laws this approach is generalized to define dynamic equilibrium.

The static equilibrium of a mechanical system rigid bodies is defined by the condition that the virtual work of the applied forces is zero for any virtual displacement of the system. This is known as the _principle of virtual work._[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rigid_body_dynamics#cite_note-Torby1984-5) This is equivalent to the requirement that the generalized forces for any virtual displacement are zero, that is _Q_ _i_=0.

Let a mechanical system be constructed from n rigid bodies, B _i_, _i_ = 1, ..., _n_, and let the resultant of the applied forces on each body be the force-torque pairs, **F**_i_ and **T**_i_, _i_ = 1, ..., _n_. Notice that these applied forces do not include the reaction forces where the bodies are connected. Finally, assume that the velocity **V**_i_ and angular velocities _**ω**_ _i_, _i_ = 1, ..., _n_, for each rigid body, are defined by a single generalized coordinate q. Such a system of rigid bodies is said to have one [degree of freedom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_of_freedom_(mechanics) "Degree of freedom (mechanics)").

The virtual work of the forces and torques, **F**_i_ and **T**_i_, applied to this one degree of freedom system is given by ![Image 50: {\displaystyle \delta W=\sum _{i=1}^{n}\left(\mathbf {F} _{i}\cdot {\frac {\partial \mathbf {V} _{i}}{\partial {\dot {q}}}}+\mathbf {T} _{i}\cdot {\frac {\partial {\boldsymbol {\omega }}_{i}}{\partial {\dot {q}}}}\right)\delta q=Q\delta q,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7e44163221edb0e5c11f6026697e5501bbb04bd1) where ![Image 51: {\displaystyle Q=\sum _{i=1}^{n}\left(\mathbf {F} _{i}\cdot {\frac {\partial \mathbf {V} _{i}}{\partial {\dot {q}}}}+\mathbf {T} _{i}\cdot {\frac {\partial {\boldsymbol {\omega }}_{i}}{\partial {\dot {q}}}}\right),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a765c7283c8ed365074f043606b1d0102ad01538) is the generalized force acting on this one degree of freedom system.

If the mechanical system is defined by m generalized coordinates, _q_ _j_, _j_ = 1, ..., _m_, then the system has m degrees of freedom and the virtual work is given by, ![Image 52: {\displaystyle \delta W=\sum _{j=1}^{m}Q_{j}\delta q_{j},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a679d873e64385d0b6211b80a7ce3c53aeba369) where ![Image 53: {\displaystyle Q_{j}=\sum _{i=1}^{n}\left(\mathbf {F} _{i}\cdot {\frac {\partial \mathbf {V} _{i}}{\partial {\dot {q}}_{j}}}+\mathbf {T} _{i}\cdot {\frac {\partial {\boldsymbol {\omega }}_{i}}{\partial {\dot {q}}_{j}}}\right),\quad j=1,\ldots ,m.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c868f553e7c0828fae1ef13c45003042f6839dc2) is the generalized force associated with the generalized coordinate _q_ _j_. The principle of virtual work states that static equilibrium occurs when these generalized forces acting on the system are zero, that is ![Image 54: {\displaystyle Q_{j}=0,\quad j=1,\ldots ,m.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c7858fae475a047df5a6ff2f68ef2d0d3749f5db)

These m equations define the static equilibrium of the system of rigid bodies.

### Generalized inertia forces

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Rigid_body_dynamics&action=edit&section=19 "Edit section: Generalized inertia forces")]

Consider a single rigid body which moves under the action of a resultant force **F** and torque **T**, with one degree of freedom defined by the generalized coordinate _q_. Assume the reference point for the resultant force and torque is the center of mass of the body, then the generalized inertia force _Q*_ associated with the generalized coordinate q is given by ![Image 55: {\displaystyle Q^{*}=-(M\mathbf {A} )\cdot {\frac {\partial \mathbf {V} }{\partial {\dot {q}}}}-\left([I_{R}]{\boldsymbol {\alpha }}+{\boldsymbol {\omega }}\times [I_{R}]{\boldsymbol {\omega }}\right)\cdot {\frac {\partial {\boldsymbol {\omega }}}{\partial {\dot {q}}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/df57abd9197f239cfcfa19770f34cfeeb699b1e3)

This inertia force can be computed from the kinetic energy of the rigid body, ![Image 56: {\displaystyle T={\tfrac {1}{2}}M\mathbf {V} \cdot \mathbf {V} +{\tfrac {1}{2}}{\boldsymbol {\omega }}\cdot [I_{R}]{\boldsymbol {\omega }},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9746f56c6d59a6412cccc00201d6b3846f7af724) by using the formula ![Image 57: {\displaystyle Q^{*}=-\left({\frac {d}{dt}}{\frac {\partial T}{\partial {\dot {q}}}}-{\frac {\partial T}{\partial q}}\right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5011d97e0cbee68fa732ce8991a61549118888e0)

A system of n rigid bodies with m generalized coordinates has the kinetic energy ![Image 58: {\displaystyle T=\sum _{i=1}^{n}\left({\tfrac {1}{2}}M\mathbf {V} _{i}\cdot \mathbf {V} _{i}+{\tfrac {1}{2}}{\boldsymbol {\omega }}_{i}\cdot [I_{R}]{\boldsymbol {\omega }}_{i}\right),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cab5ab8718b6844af72b9fa8b6ba4324a1152d60) which can be used to calculate the m generalized inertia forces[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rigid_body_dynamics#cite_note-6)![Image 59: {\displaystyle Q_{j}^{*}=-\left({\frac {d}{dt}}{\frac {\partial T}{\partial {\dot {q}}_{j}}}-{\frac {\partial T}{\partial q_{j}}}\right),\quad j=1,\ldots ,m.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/52f16308afd5d1ea4567b0e47ebb0ee3f37b1a1a)

### Dynamic equilibrium

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Rigid_body_dynamics&action=edit&section=20 "Edit section: Dynamic equilibrium")]

D'Alembert's form of the principle of virtual work states that a system of rigid bodies is in dynamic equilibrium when the virtual work of the sum of the applied forces and the inertial forces is zero for any virtual displacement of the system. Thus, dynamic equilibrium of a system of n rigid bodies with m generalized coordinates requires that ![Image 60: {\displaystyle \delta W=\left(Q_{1}+Q_{1}^{*}\right)\delta q_{1}+\dots +\left(Q_{m}+Q_{m}^{*}\right)\delta q_{m}=0,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7bd5c1b4fe5c2de2649857b9721d370261a23b38) for any set of virtual displacements _δq_ _j_. This condition yields m equations, ![Image 61: {\displaystyle Q_{j}+Q_{j}^{*}=0,\quad j=1,\ldots ,m,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/abdc1026adbba50c42e5d87d85441336a6f2d9f4) which can also be written as ![Image 62: {\displaystyle {\frac {d}{dt}}{\frac {\partial T}{\partial {\dot {q}}_{j}}}-{\frac {\partial T}{\partial q_{j}}}=Q_{j},\quad j=1,\ldots ,m.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/588bfb13eedb4ccb044296df1df54a9a409cfb53) The result is a set of m equations of motion that define the dynamics of the rigid body system.

### Lagrange's equations

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Rigid_body_dynamics&action=edit&section=21 "Edit section: Lagrange's equations")]

If the generalized forces Q _j_ are derivable from a potential energy _V_(_q_ 1, ..., _q_ _m_), then these equations of motion take the form ![Image 63: {\displaystyle {\frac {d}{dt}}{\frac {\partial T}{\partial {\dot {q}}_{j}}}-{\frac {\partial T}{\partial q_{j}}}=-{\frac {\partial V}{\partial q_{j}}},\quad j=1,\ldots ,m.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7cd686304a9c80e9af12e8f88db50df653ab7906)

In this case, introduce the [Lagrangian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrangian_mechanics "Lagrangian mechanics"), _L_ = _T_ − _V_, so these equations of motion become ![Image 64: {\displaystyle {\frac {d}{dt}}{\frac {\partial L}{\partial {\dot {q}}_{j}}}-{\frac {\partial L}{\partial q_{j}}}=0\quad j=1,\ldots ,m.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b21b186aa6c6042e89c4f950413bac44a96000b1) These are known as [Lagrange's equations of motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrangian_mechanics "Lagrangian mechanics").

Linear and angular momentum
---------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Rigid_body_dynamics&action=edit&section=22 "Edit section: Linear and angular momentum")]

### System of particles

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Rigid_body_dynamics&action=edit&section=23 "Edit section: System of particles")]

The linear and angular momentum of a rigid system of particles is formulated by measuring the position and velocity of the particles relative to the center of mass. Let the system of particles P i, _i_ = 1, ..., _n_ be located at the coordinates **r**_i_ and velocities **v**_i_. Select a reference point **R** and compute the relative position and velocity vectors, ![Image 65: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} _{i}=\left(\mathbf {r} _{i}-\mathbf {R} \right)+\mathbf {R} ,\quad \mathbf {v} _{i}={\frac {d}{dt}}(\mathbf {r} _{i}-\mathbf {R} )+\mathbf {V} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/596822f0cf74caddd7b22d744d530faf7d2be438)

The total linear and angular momentum vectors relative to the reference point **R** are ![Image 66: {\displaystyle \mathbf {p} ={\frac {d}{dt}}\left(\sum _{i=1}^{n}m_{i}\left(\mathbf {r} _{i}-\mathbf {R} \right)\right)+\left(\sum _{i=1}^{n}m_{i}\right)\mathbf {V} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/edeb445aaf99af1181fcab5266f04d6f8b815bf7) and ![Image 67: {\displaystyle \mathbf {L} =\sum _{i=1}^{n}m_{i}\left(\mathbf {r} _{i}-\mathbf {R} \right)\times {\frac {d}{dt}}\left(\mathbf {r} _{i}-\mathbf {R} \right)+\left(\sum _{i=1}^{n}m_{i}\left(\mathbf {r} _{i}-\mathbf {R} \right)\right)\times \mathbf {V} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9d0eddfc8baf6959f02c443fed85202311a0f8a7)

If **R** is chosen as the center of mass these equations simplify to ![Image 68: {\displaystyle \mathbf {p} =M\mathbf {V} ,\quad \mathbf {L} =\sum _{i=1}^{n}m_{i}\left(\mathbf {r} _{i}-\mathbf {R} \right)\times {\frac {d}{dt}}\left(\mathbf {r} _{i}-\mathbf {R} \right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d98504f3d24d209f8a11597a8b7d5acee03f15ad)

### Rigid system of particles

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Rigid_body_dynamics&action=edit&section=24 "Edit section: Rigid system of particles")]

To specialize these formulas to a rigid body, assume the particles are rigidly connected to each other so P i, i=1,...,n are located by the coordinates **r**i and velocities **v**i. Select a reference point **R** and compute the relative position and velocity vectors, ![Image 69: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} _{i}=(\mathbf {r} _{i}-\mathbf {R} )+\mathbf {R} ,\quad \mathbf {v} _{i}=\omega \times (\mathbf {r} _{i}-\mathbf {R} )+\mathbf {V} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7a90412b356e44c087004a14a7aa1c364ac00f50) where ω is the angular velocity of the system.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rigid_body_dynamics#cite_note-7)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rigid_body_dynamics#cite_note-8)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rigid_body_dynamics#cite_note-9)

The [linear momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_momentum "Linear momentum") and [angular momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum "Angular momentum") of this rigid system measured relative to the center of mass **R** is ![Image 70: {\displaystyle \mathbf {p} =\left(\sum _{i=1}^{n}m_{i}\right)\mathbf {V} ,\quad \mathbf {L} =\sum _{i=1}^{n}m_{i}(\mathbf {r} _{i}-\mathbf {R} )\times \mathbf {v} _{i}=\sum _{i=1}^{n}m_{i}(\mathbf {r} _{i}-\mathbf {R} )\times (\omega \times (\mathbf {r} _{i}-\mathbf {R} )).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c1231ff3858788338ec01fc7879a7fe8a6b41dad)

These equations simplify to become, ![Image 71: {\displaystyle \mathbf {p} =M\mathbf {V} ,\quad \mathbf {L} =[I_{R}]\omega ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f2ef7db20a056f0716e9e45d93ee4c733871f5fc) where M is the total mass of the system and [I R] is the [moment of inertia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_of_inertia "Moment of inertia") matrix defined by ![Image 72: {\displaystyle [I_{R}]=-\sum _{i=1}^{n}m_{i}[r_{i}-R][r_{i}-R],}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/abe90de29df41040a0385f1f1f79d2f99af97e03) where [r _i_ − R] is the skew-symmetric matrix constructed from the vector **r**_i_ − **R**.
