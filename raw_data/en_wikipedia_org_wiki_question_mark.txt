The **question mark****?** (also known as **interrogation point**, **query**, or **eroteme** in [journalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Journalism "Journalism")[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Question_mark#cite_note-FOOTNOTETruss2003139-1)) is a [punctuation mark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Punctuation "Punctuation") that indicates a [question](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Question "Question") or [interrogative clause](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interrogative_clause "Interrogative clause") or phrase in many [languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Language "Language").

The history of the question mark is contested. One popular theory posits that the shape of the symbol is inspired by the crook in a cat's tail, often attributed to the ancient Egyptians.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Question_mark#cite_note-burb-2) However, [Egyptian hieroglyphics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egyptian_hieroglyphics "Egyptian hieroglyphics") did not utilize punctuation marks.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Question_mark#cite_note-3)

In the fifth century, [Syriac](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_Syriac "Classical Syriac") Bible manuscripts used question markers, according to a 2011 theory by manuscript specialist Chip Coakley: he believes the _zagwa elaya_ ("upper pair"), a vertical double dot over a word at the start of a sentence, indicates that the sentence is a question.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Question_mark#cite_note-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Question_mark#cite_note-5)

[![Image 1](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Btv1b6000718s.png/250px-Btv1b6000718s.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Btv1b6000718s.png)

8th century _punctus interrogativus_ from the [Godescalc Evangelistary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Godescalc_Evangelistary "Godescalc Evangelistary"). ([BnF NAL 1203, f. 6v](https://gallica.bnf.fr/ark:/12148/btv1b6000718s/f12).)

From around 783, in [Godescalc Evangelistary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Godescalc_Evangelistary "Godescalc Evangelistary"), a mark described as "a lightning flash, striking from right to left" is attested.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Question_mark#cite_note-grammarphobia-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Question_mark#cite_note-FOOTNOTETruss2003159-7) This mark is later called a _[punctus interrogativus](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/punctus\_interrogativus#English "wikt:punctus interrogativus")_. According to some [paleographers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palaeography "Palaeography"), it may have indicated [intonation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intonation_(linguistics) "Intonation (linguistics)"), perhaps associated with early musical notation like [neumes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neume "Neume").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Question_mark#cite_note-grammarphobia-6) Another theory, is that the "lightning flash" was originally a [tilde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tilde "Tilde") or [titlo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Titlo "Titlo"), as in ¬∑~, one of many wavy or more or less slanted marks used in [medieval](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval "Medieval") texts for denoting things such as [abbreviations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abbreviations "Abbreviations"), which would later become various [diacritics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diacritics "Diacritics") or [ligatures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Typographic_ligature "Typographic ligature").[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Question_mark#cite_note-8)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Question_mark#cite_note-9) The creation of the _punctus interrogativus_ has also been attributed to [Alcuin of York](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alcuin_of_York "Alcuin of York"), an advisor to [Charlemagne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charlemagne "Charlemagne").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Question_mark#cite_note-burb-2)

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Punctus_interrogativus_from_Bern%2C_B%C3%BCrgerbibliothek_Cod._162%2C_f._15r.jpg/250px-Punctus_interrogativus_from_Bern%2C_B%C3%BCrgerbibliothek_Cod._162%2C_f._15r.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Punctus_interrogativus_from_Bern,_B%C3%BCrgerbibliothek_Cod._162,_f._15r.jpg)

An 11th century _punctus interrogativus_; in the third line, before "tamen". ([Burgerbibliothek Bern](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burgerbibliothek_of_Berne "Burgerbibliothek of Berne"), [Cod. 162, f. 15r](https://www.e-codices.ch/en/bbb/0162/15r).)

From the 10th century, the pitch-defining element (if it ever existed) seems to have been gradually forgotten, so that the "lightning flash" sign (with the stroke sometimes slightly curved) is often seen indifferently at the end of clauses, whether they embody a question or not.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

In the early 13th century, when the growth of communities of scholars ([universities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University "University")) in Paris and other major cities led to an expansion and streamlining of the book-production trade,[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Question_mark#cite_note-10) punctuation was rationalized by assigning the "lightning flash" specifically to [interrogatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interrogative "Interrogative"); by this time, the stroke was more sharply curved and can easily be recognized as the modern question mark (see, for example, _[De Aetna](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=De\_Aetna&action=edit&redlink=1 "De Aetna (page does not exist)")[[it](https://it.wikipedia.org/wiki/De\_Aetna "it:De Aetna")]_ (1496) printed by [Aldo Manuzio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aldus_Manutius "Aldus Manutius") in [Venice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Venice "Venice")[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Question_mark#cite_note-11)).

In 1598, the English term _point of interrogation_ is attested in an [Italian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italian_language "Italian language")‚ÄìEnglish dictionary by [John Florio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Florio "John Florio").[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Question_mark#cite_note-12)

In the 1850s, the term _question mark_ is attested:[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Question_mark#cite_note-13)

> The mark which you are to notice in this lesson is of this shape **?** You see it is made by placing a little crooked mark over a period.... The name of this mark is the _Question Mark_, because it is always put after a question. Sometimes it is called by a longer and harder name. The long and hard name is the _Interrogation Point_.

In English, the question mark typically occurs at the end of a sentence, where it replaces the [full stop](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Full_stop "Full stop") (period). However, the question mark may also occur at the end of a clause or phrase, where it replaces the comma (see also [Question comma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Question_comma "Question comma")):

"Is it good in form? style? meaning?"
or:

"Showing off for him, for all of them, not out of hubris‚Äîhubris? him? what did he have to be hubrid about?‚Äîbut from mood and nervousness."‚Äî [Stanley Elkin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stanley_Elkin "Stanley Elkin").[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Question_mark#cite_note-14)
This is quite common in [Spanish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spanish_(language) "Spanish (language)"), where the use of bracketing question marks explicitly indicates the scope of interrogation.

_En el caso de que no puedas ir con ellos, ¬øquieres ir con nosotros?_ ('In case you cannot go with them, would you like to go with us?')
A question mark may also appear immediately after questionable data, such as dates:

_Genghis Khan (1162?‚Äì1227)_

Typological variants of _?_
---------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Question_mark&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Typological variants of¬†?")]

The rhetorical question mark or percontation point (see [Irony punctuation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irony_punctuation "Irony punctuation")) was invented by [Henry Denham](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry_Denham "Henry Denham") in the 1580s and was used at the end of a [rhetorical question](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhetorical_question "Rhetorical question");[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Question_mark#cite_note-FOOTNOTETruss2003142-35) however, it became obsolete in the 17th century. It was the reverse of an ordinary question mark, so that instead of the main opening pointing back into the sentence, it opened away from it.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Question_mark#cite_note-FOOTNOTETruss2003142-35) This character can be represented using U+2E2E‚∏Æ[REVERSED QUESTION MARK](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irony_punctuation#Irony_mark "Irony punctuation").

Bracketed question marks can be used for rhetorical questions, for example Oh, really(?), in informal contexts such as [closed captioning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_captioning "Closed captioning").

The question mark can also be used as a [meta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meta_(prefix) "Meta (prefix)")-sign to signal uncertainty regarding what precedes it. It is usually put between brackets: (?). The uncertainty may concern either a superficial level (such as unsure spelling), or a deeper [truth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Truth-conditional_semantics "Truth-conditional semantics") (real [meaning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meaning_(philosophy_of_language) "Meaning (philosophy of language)")).

In recent [XFree86](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/XFree86 "XFree86") and [X.Org](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X.Org_Server "X.Org Server") incarnations of the [X Window System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X_Window_System "X Window System"), it can be accessed as a compose sequence of two straight question marks, i.e. pressing [Compose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compose_key "Compose key")?? yields ¬ø. In [classic Mac OS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classic_Mac_OS "Classic Mac OS") and [Mac OS X](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mac_OS_X "Mac OS X") (macOS), the key combination [Option](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Option_key "Option key") Shift? produces an inverted question mark.

In shell and scripting languages, the question mark is often utilized as a [wildcard character](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wildcard_character "Wildcard character"): a symbol that can be used to substitute for any other character or characters in a [string](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/String_(computer_science) "String (computer science)"). In particular, [filename globbing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glob_(programming) "Glob (programming)") uses "?" as a substitute for any one character, as opposed to the [asterisk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asterisk "Asterisk"), "*", which matches zero or more characters in a string.

The question mark is used in ASCII renderings of the [International Phonetic Alphabet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Phonetic_Alphabet "International Phonetic Alphabet"), such as [SAMPA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SAMPA "SAMPA"), in place of the [glottal stop](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glottal_stop "Glottal stop") symbol, ** î**, (which resembles "**?**" without the dot), and corresponds to Unicode code point U+0294  î LATIN LETTER GLOTTAL STOP.

In [computer programming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_programming "Computer programming"), the symbol "**?**" has a special meaning in many [programming languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Programming_language "Programming language"). In [C](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C_(programming_language) "C (programming language)")-descended languages, `?` is part of the [`?:`](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%3F: "?:") operator, which is used to evaluate simple [boolean conditions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boolean_domain "Boolean domain"). In [C#](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C_Sharp_(programming_language) "C Sharp (programming language)") 2.0, the `?` modifier is used to handle [nullable data types](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nullable_type "Nullable type") and `??` is the [null coalescing operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Null_coalescing_operator "Null coalescing operator"). In the [POSIX](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/POSIX "POSIX") syntax for [regular expressions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_expression "Regular expression"), such as that used in [Perl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perl "Perl") and [Python](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Python_(programming_language) "Python (programming language)"), `?` stands for "zero or one instance of the previous subexpression", i.e. an optional element. It can also make a quantifier like `{x,y}`, `+` or `*` match as few characters as possible, making it lazy, e.g. `/^.*?px/` will match the substring `165px` in `165px 17px` instead of matching `165px 17px`.[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Question_mark#cite_note-pcre-qm-Uflag-38) In certain implementations of the [BASIC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BASIC "BASIC") programming language, the `?` character may be used as a shorthand for the "print" function; in others (notably the [BBC BASIC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BBC_BASIC "BBC BASIC") family), `?` is used to address a single-byte memory location. In [OCaml](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OCaml "OCaml"), the question mark precedes the label for an optional parameter. In [Scheme](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scheme_(programming_language) "Scheme (programming language)"), as a convention, symbol names ending in `?` are used for predicates, such as `odd?`, `null?`, and `eq?`. Similarly, in [Ruby](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ruby_(programming_language) "Ruby (programming language)"), method names ending in `?` are used for predicates. In [Swift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swift_(programming_language) "Swift (programming language)") a type followed by `?` denotes an [option type](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Option_type "Option type"); `?` is also used in "optional chaining", where if an option value is nil, it ignores the following operations. Similarly, in [Kotlin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kotlin_(programming_language) "Kotlin (programming language)"), a type followed by `?` is [nullable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nullable_type "Nullable type") and functions similar to option chaining are supported. In [APL](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/APL_(programming_language) "APL (programming language)"), `?` generates random numbers or a random subset of indices. In [Rust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rust_(programming_language) "Rust (programming language)"), a `?` suffix on a function or method call indicates error handling. In [SPARQL](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SPARQL "SPARQL"), the question mark is used to introduce variable names, such as `?name`. In [MUMPS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MUMPS "MUMPS"), it is the pattern match operator.

In the [Xbase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xbase "Xbase") family of programming languages, which includes [dBase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DBase "DBase") and [FoxPro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/FoxPro "FoxPro"), either one or two question marks at the start of a line of code serve as a shorthand for the Print function. The effect is to evaluate the following expression(s) and to send the result(s) either to the screen or a printer. A single question mark sends a carriage return and line feed before the output; this is not the case with a double question mark.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Question_mark#cite_note-39)[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Question_mark#cite_note-40)

In many [Web browsers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Web_browser "Web browser") and other computer programs, when converting text between encodings, it may not be possible to map some characters into the target [character set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Character_set "Character set"). In this situation it is common to replace each unmappable character with a question mark ?, inverted question mark ¬ø, or the Unicode [replacement character](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Replacement_character "Replacement character"), usually rendered as a white question mark in a black diamond: U+FFFDÔøΩREPLACEMENT CHARACTER. This commonly occurs for apostrophes and quotation marks when they are written with software that uses its own proprietary non-standard code for these characters, such as [Microsoft Office](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microsoft_Office "Microsoft Office")'s ["smart quotes"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quotation_marks_in_English#Smart_quotes "Quotation marks in English").

The generic [URL](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uniform_Resource_Locator "Uniform Resource Locator") syntax allows for a [query string](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Query_string "Query string") to be appended to a resource location in a Web address so that additional information can be passed to a script; the query mark, `?`, is used to indicate the start of a query string. A query string is usually made up of a number of different field/value pairs, each separated by the [ampersand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ampersand "Ampersand") symbol, `&`, as seen in this URL:

`http://www.example.com/search.php?query=testing&database=English`

Here, a script on the page search.php on the server www.example.com is to provide a response to the query string containing the pairs query=testing and database=English.

In [algebraic chess notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_chess_notation "Algebraic chess notation"), some [chess punctuation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chess_punctuation "Chess punctuation") conventions include: "**?**" denotes a bad move, "**??**" a [blunder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blunder_(chess) "Blunder (chess)"), "**?!**" a [dubious](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dubious "Dubious") move, and "**!?**" an interesting move.

In [Scrabble](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scrabble "Scrabble"), a question mark indicates a blank tile.[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Question_mark#cite_note-41)

In most areas of [linguistics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linguistics "Linguistics"), but especially in [syntax](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syntax "Syntax"), a question mark in front of a word, phrase or sentence indicates that the form in question is strongly dispreferred, "questionable" or "strange", but not outright [ungrammatical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ungrammatical "Ungrammatical").[[b]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Question_mark#cite_note-45) (The [asterisk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asterisks_in_linguistics "Asterisks in linguistics") is used to indicate outright ungrammaticality.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Question_mark#cite_note-Graffi-46): 332 )

Other sources go further and use several symbols (e.g. the question mark and the asterisk plus `?*` or the [degree symbol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_symbol "Degree symbol")`¬∞`) to indicate gradations or a continuum of acceptability.[[c]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Question_mark#cite_note-48)

Yet others use double question marks `??` to indicate a degree of strangeness between those indicated by a single question mark and that indicated by the combination of question mark and asterisk.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Question_mark#cite_note-49)[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Question_mark#cite_note-50)

Mathematics and formal logic
----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Question_mark&action=edit&section=16 "Edit section: Mathematics and formal logic")]

In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), "**?**" commonly denotes [Minkowski's question mark function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minkowski%27s_question_mark_function "Minkowski's question mark function").

In [linear logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_logic "Linear logic"), the question mark denotes one of the exponential modalities that control weakening and contraction.

When placed above the relational symbol in an [equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equation "Equation") or [inequality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inequality_(mathematics) "Inequality (mathematics)"), a question-mark annotation means that the stated relation is "questioned". This can be used to ask whether the relation might be true or to point out the relation's possible invalidity.

*   U+2A7B‚©ªLESS-THAN WITH QUESTION MARK ABOVE
*   U+2A7C‚©ºGREATER-THAN WITH QUESTION MARK ABOVE

A question mark is used in English medical notes to suggest a possible [diagnosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diagnosis "Diagnosis"). It facilitates the recording of a doctor's impressions regarding a patient's symptoms and signs. For example, for a patient presenting with left [lower abdominal pain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lower_abdominal_pain "Lower abdominal pain"), a differential diagnosis might include _?[diverticulitis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diverticulitis "Diverticulitis")_ (read as "query diverticulitis").
