A diagram of dimensions 1, 2, 3, and 4

In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), the **dimension** of a [vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space")_V_ is the [cardinality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardinality "Cardinality") (i.e., the number of vectors) of a [basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basis_(linear_algebra) "Basis (linear algebra)") of _V_ over its base [field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension_(vector_space)#cite_note-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension_(vector_space)#cite_note-2) It is sometimes called **Hamel dimension** (after [Georg Hamel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georg_Hamel "Georg Hamel")) or **algebraic dimension** to distinguish it from other types of [dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension "Dimension").

For every vector space there exists a basis,[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension_(vector_space)#cite_note-3) and all bases of a vector space have equal cardinality;[[b]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension_(vector_space)#cite_note-4) as a result, the dimension of a vector space is uniquely defined. We say ![Image 2: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) is **finite-dimensional** if the dimension of ![Image 3: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) is [finite](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/finite "wiktionary:finite"), and **infinite-dimensional** if its dimension is [infinite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinity "Infinity").

The dimension of the vector space ![Image 4: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) over the field ![Image 5: {\displaystyle F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/545fd099af8541605f7ee55f08225526be88ce57) can be written as ![Image 6: {\displaystyle \dim _{F}(V)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fd65db775dec7f7ec645780c5ff6b08ab6023f5d) or as ![Image 7: {\displaystyle [V:F],}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4cb93bcc0bc1570b7c50bfbc2947be3b487a5f9c) read "dimension of ![Image 8: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) over ![Image 9: {\displaystyle F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/545fd099af8541605f7ee55f08225526be88ce57)". When ![Image 10: {\displaystyle F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/545fd099af8541605f7ee55f08225526be88ce57) can be inferred from context, ![Image 11: {\displaystyle \dim(V)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e7a31b94c41961050c330be4cbd9ab0f1aab11e8) is typically written.

The vector space ![Image 12: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5) has ![Image 13: {\displaystyle \left\{{\begin{pmatrix}1\\0\\0\end{pmatrix}},{\begin{pmatrix}0\\1\\0\end{pmatrix}},{\begin{pmatrix}0\\0\\1\end{pmatrix}}\right\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/44d47376a411c2afc05c78de34fa78825d11ad6e) as a [standard basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_basis "Standard basis"), and therefore ![Image 14: {\displaystyle \dim _{\mathbb {R} }(\mathbb {R} ^{3})=3.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/25789fab4dc10088d3bde6e2397e3cb9978f2e8f) More generally, ![Image 15: {\displaystyle \dim _{\mathbb {R} }(\mathbb {R} ^{n})=n,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0e8c3da5388a5b130c2d007facec2458486354f1) and even more generally, ![Image 16: {\displaystyle \dim _{F}(F^{n})=n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5f866303e3a365583704b04a3a44b80b866b77a0) for any [field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)")![Image 17: {\displaystyle F.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a6b34655c2ef19b56c81af0e6d1f2f6df0d3ed33)

The [complex numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number")![Image 18: {\displaystyle \mathbb {C} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f9add4085095b9b6d28d045fd9c92c2c09f549a7) are both a real and complex vector space; we have ![Image 19: {\displaystyle \dim _{\mathbb {R} }(\mathbb {C} )=2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38503bf9869be655bc222f6c20b340a9c893ad94) and ![Image 20: {\displaystyle \dim _{\mathbb {C} }(\mathbb {C} )=1.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8f0cfcb2005c385d558e24d0619ef207351037f6) So the dimension depends on the base field.

The only vector space with dimension ![Image 21: {\displaystyle 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2aae8864a3c1fec9585261791a809ddec1489950) is ![Image 22: {\displaystyle \{0\},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/24e3d43e3dc8ecfabf8f9c40c9ab4965b1c92298) the vector space consisting only of its zero element.

If ![Image 23: {\displaystyle W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54a9c4c547f4d6111f81946cad242b18298d70b7) is a [linear subspace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_subspace "Linear subspace") of ![Image 24: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) then ![Image 25: {\displaystyle \dim(W)\leq \dim(V).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dd29db56bf3e10e78d1d59b9ec06cf8daca6b795)

To show that two finite-dimensional vector spaces are equal, the following criterion can be used: if ![Image 26: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) is a finite-dimensional vector space and ![Image 27: {\displaystyle W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54a9c4c547f4d6111f81946cad242b18298d70b7) is a linear subspace of ![Image 28: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) with ![Image 29: {\displaystyle \dim(W)=\dim(V),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/24e73dbdcf8de72c1a6ed4075e522b5806d565e6) then ![Image 30: {\displaystyle W=V.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ab5055219898898debedf4fa33ef6d82a3907ea3)

The space ![Image 31: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d) has the standard basis ![Image 32: {\displaystyle \left\{e_{1},\ldots ,e_{n}\right\},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1d5f02e6a61ce1730b6c3a8c3b7fee4cd333658e) where ![Image 33: {\displaystyle e_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ebdc3a9cb1583d3204eff8918b558c293e0d2cf3) is the ![Image 34: {\displaystyle i}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/add78d8608ad86e54951b8c8bd6c8d8416533d20)-th column of the corresponding [identity matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Identity_matrix "Identity matrix"). Therefore, ![Image 35: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d) has dimension ![Image 36: {\displaystyle n.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e59df02a9f67a5da3c220f1244c99a46cc4eb1c6)

Any two finite dimensional vector spaces over ![Image 37: {\displaystyle F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/545fd099af8541605f7ee55f08225526be88ce57) with the same dimension are [isomorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isomorphic "Isomorphic"). Any [bijective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bijective "Bijective") map between their bases can be uniquely extended to a bijective linear map between the vector spaces. If ![Image 38: {\displaystyle B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47136aad860d145f75f3eed3022df827cee94d7a) is some set, a vector space with dimension ![Image 39: {\displaystyle |B|}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/09a17e400743ffd99fb026d81d8298a6e6344352) over ![Image 40: {\displaystyle F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/545fd099af8541605f7ee55f08225526be88ce57) can be constructed as follows: take the set ![Image 41: {\displaystyle F(B)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8782985e694c972847a883de7ad1124001a8813a) of all functions ![Image 42: {\displaystyle f:B\to F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/284ba5f8b497858fa26480a17681a14854956b54) such that ![Image 43: {\displaystyle f(b)=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1610a50af0798418cfc95060ede1e9a88e8bc691) for all but finitely many ![Image 44: {\displaystyle b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f11423fbb2e967f986e36804a8ae4271734917c3) in ![Image 45: {\displaystyle B.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0eccf5bca7cdc1fa4439af2d31831db6bde00473) These functions can be added and multiplied with elements of ![Image 46: {\displaystyle F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/545fd099af8541605f7ee55f08225526be88ce57) to obtain the desired ![Image 47: {\displaystyle F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/545fd099af8541605f7ee55f08225526be88ce57)-vector space.

An important result about dimensions is given by the [rank–nullity theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rank%E2%80%93nullity_theorem "Rank–nullity theorem") for [linear maps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_map "Linear map").

If ![Image 48: {\displaystyle F/K}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/02620f6632f0e6ba3cf67f966cd198b3b3b2d340) is a [field extension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_extension "Field extension"), then ![Image 49: {\displaystyle F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/545fd099af8541605f7ee55f08225526be88ce57) is in particular a vector space over ![Image 50: {\displaystyle K.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1bb0e178e42abf16ef4e4c0b0f22aa235ad6e6e5) Furthermore, every ![Image 51: {\displaystyle F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/545fd099af8541605f7ee55f08225526be88ce57)-vector space ![Image 52: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) is also a ![Image 53: {\displaystyle K}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b76fce82a62ed5461908f0dc8f037de4e3686b0)-vector space. The dimensions are related by the formula ![Image 54: {\displaystyle \dim _{K}(V)=\dim _{K}(F)\dim _{F}(V).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/819af586fbbf5ccbebd7bf457b807a11d6a590a0) In particular, every complex vector space of dimension ![Image 55: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) is a real vector space of dimension ![Image 56: {\displaystyle 2n.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/21e85bad71f97a39b9abced77f06d46b44c97640)

Some formulae relate the dimension of a vector space with the [cardinality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardinality "Cardinality") of the base field and the cardinality of the space itself. If dim ![Image 61: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) is finite then ![Image 62: {\displaystyle |V|=|F|^{\dim V}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8c29ddaee2741c43b2cd4799204de20cd2e3c1f)If dim ![Image 63: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) is infinite then ![Image 64: {\displaystyle |V|=\max(|F|,\dim V).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8819c770923790c999b98d62f755bbc066fc459)
A vector space can be seen as a particular case of a [matroid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matroid "Matroid"), and in the latter there is a well-defined notion of dimension. The [length of a module](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Length_of_a_module "Length of a module") and the [rank of an abelian group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rank_of_an_abelian_group "Rank of an abelian group") both have several properties similar to the dimension of vector spaces.

The [Krull dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Krull_dimension "Krull dimension") of a commutative [ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ring_(algebra) "Ring (algebra)"), named after [Wolfgang Krull](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Krull "Wolfgang Krull") (1899–1971), is defined to be the maximal number of strict inclusions in an increasing chain of [prime ideals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_ideal "Prime ideal") in the ring.

The dimension of a vector space may alternatively be characterized as the [trace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trace_(linear_algebra) "Trace (linear algebra)") of the [identity operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Identity_operator "Identity operator"). For instance, ![Image 65: {\displaystyle \operatorname {tr} \ \operatorname {id} _{\mathbb {R} ^{2}}=\operatorname {tr} \left({\begin{smallmatrix}1&0\\0&1\end{smallmatrix}}\right)=1+1=2.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6802a3f37c7b96132402cb1586f3d3b5d232e377) This appears to be a [circular definition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circular_definition "Circular definition"), but it allows useful generalizations.

Firstly, it allows for a definition of a notion of dimension when one has a trace but no natural sense of basis. For example, one may have an [algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra_over_a_field "Algebra over a field")![Image 66: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3) with maps ![Image 67: {\displaystyle \eta :K\to A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2d66f18939977f688feb718c2b1b622961ba25b3) (the inclusion of scalars, called the _unit_) and a map ![Image 68: {\displaystyle \epsilon :A\to K}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7d3f2ab61f8c7fbc82a83b3faa6e0f206ed3e903) (corresponding to trace, called the _[counit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Counit "Counit")_). The composition ![Image 69: {\displaystyle \epsilon \circ \eta :K\to K}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/feffe1f8d9b017724a35b2ef4600e0eba607304e) is a scalar (being a linear operator on a 1-dimensional space) corresponds to "trace of identity", and gives a notion of dimension for an abstract algebra. In practice, in [bialgebras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bialgebra "Bialgebra"), this map is required to be the identity, which can be obtained by normalizing the counit by dividing by dimension (![Image 70: {\displaystyle \epsilon :=\textstyle {\frac {1}{n}}\operatorname {tr} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7574a1aefb8955749063b1788e230595a6926e93)), so in these cases the normalizing constant corresponds to dimension.

Alternatively, it may be possible to take the trace of operators on an infinite-dimensional space; in this case a (finite) trace is defined, even though no (finite) dimension exists, and gives a notion of "dimension of the operator". These fall under the rubric of "[trace class](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trace_class "Trace class") operators" on a [Hilbert space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space "Hilbert space"), or more generally [nuclear operators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_operator "Nuclear operator") on a [Banach space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Banach_space "Banach space").

A subtler generalization is to consider the trace of a _family_ of operators as a kind of "twisted" dimension. This occurs significantly in [representation theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Representation_theory "Representation theory"), where the [character](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Character_(mathematics) "Character (mathematics)") of a representation is the trace of the representation, hence a scalar-valued function on a [group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_(mathematics) "Group (mathematics)")![Image 71: {\displaystyle \chi :G\to K,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/669957e186392d17c22c8765cd8921290bc7db1a) whose value on the identity ![Image 72: {\displaystyle 1\in G}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/392d9c2ae44dacbd511d505381d19b4c70234656) is the dimension of the representation, as a representation sends the identity in the group to the identity matrix: ![Image 73: {\displaystyle \chi (1_{G})=\operatorname {tr} \ I_{V}=\dim V.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/126ba02209d29c2aac30292c56147660c6d70fac) The other values ![Image 74: {\displaystyle \chi (g)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/535a8c5628523914dd156e399c515362073ffa32) of the character can be viewed as "twisted" dimensions, and find analogs or generalizations of statements about dimensions to statements about characters or representations. A sophisticated example of this occurs in the theory of [monstrous moonshine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monstrous_moonshine "Monstrous moonshine"): the [![Image 75: {\displaystyle j}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2f461e54f5c093e92a55547b9764291390f0b5d0)-invariant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J-invariant "J-invariant") is the [graded dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graded_dimension "Graded dimension") of an infinite-dimensional graded representation of the [monster group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monster_group "Monster group"), and replacing the dimension with the character gives the [McKay–Thompson series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/McKay%E2%80%93Thompson_series "McKay–Thompson series") for each element of the Monster group.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension_(vector_space)#cite_note-gannon-5)
