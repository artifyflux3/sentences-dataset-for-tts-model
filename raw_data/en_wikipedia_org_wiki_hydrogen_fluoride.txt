Chemical compound

**Hydrogen fluoride** (fluorane) is an [inorganic compound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inorganic_chemistry "Inorganic chemistry") with [chemical formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_formula "Chemical formula")[H](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen")[F](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluorine "Fluorine"). It is a very poisonous, colorless gas or liquid that dissolves in water to yield [hydrofluoric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrofluoric_acid "Hydrofluoric acid"). It is the principal industrial source of [fluorine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluorine "Fluorine"), often in the form of hydrofluoric acid, and is an important [feedstock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feedstock "Feedstock") in the preparation of many important compounds including pharmaceuticals and [polymers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polymer "Polymer") such as [polytetrafluoroethylene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polytetrafluoroethylene "Polytetrafluoroethylene") (PTFE). HF is also widely used in the [petrochemical industry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petrochemical_industry "Petrochemical industry") as a component of [superacids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superacid "Superacid"). Due to strong and extensive [hydrogen bonding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_bond "Hydrogen bond"), it boils near room temperature, a much higher temperature than other [hydrogen halides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_halide "Hydrogen halide").

Hydrogen fluoride is an extremely dangerous gas, forming [corrosive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corrosive "Corrosive") and penetrating [hydrofluoric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrofluoric_acid "Hydrofluoric acid") upon contact with [moisture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moisture "Moisture"). The gas can also cause [blindness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blindness "Blindness") by rapid destruction of the [corneas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cornea "Cornea").

History
-------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen_fluoride&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: History")]

In 1771 [Carl Wilhelm Scheele](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Wilhelm_Scheele "Carl Wilhelm Scheele") prepared the aqueous solution, [hydrofluoric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrofluoric_acid "Hydrofluoric acid") in large quantities, although hydrofluoric acid had been known in the [glass industry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glass_industry "Glass industry") before then. French chemist [Edmond Frémy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edmond_Fr%C3%A9my "Edmond Frémy") (1814–1894) is credited with discovering hydrogen fluoride (HF) while trying to isolate [fluorine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluorine "Fluorine").

Structure and reactions
-----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen_fluoride&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Structure and reactions")]

[![Image 46](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Hydrogen-fluoride-solid-2D-dimensions.png/330px-Hydrogen-fluoride-solid-2D-dimensions.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Hydrogen-fluoride-solid-2D-dimensions.png)

The structure of chains of HF in crystalline hydrogen fluoride.

HF is diatomic in the gas-phase. As a liquid, HF forms relatively strong [hydrogen bonds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_bond "Hydrogen bond"), hence its relatively high boiling point. Solid HF consists of zig-zag chains of HF molecules. The HF molecules, with a short covalent H–F bond of 95 pm length, are linked to neighboring molecules by intermolecular H–F distances of 155 pm.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_fluoride#cite_note-4) Liquid HF also consists of chains of HF molecules, but the chains are shorter, consisting on average of only five or six molecules.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_fluoride#cite_note-5)

### Comparison with other hydrogen halides

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen_fluoride&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Comparison with other hydrogen halides")]

Hydrogen fluoride does not boil until 20°C in contrast to the heavier hydrogen halides, which boil between −85°C (−120°F) and −35°C (−30°F).[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_fluoride#cite_note-Pauling_HF_hydrogen_bonds-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_fluoride#cite_note-Atkins_HF-7)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_fluoride#cite_note-New_Scientist_HF-8) This hydrogen bonding between HF molecules gives rise to high [viscosity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viscosity "Viscosity") in the liquid phase and lower than expected pressure in the gas phase.

### Aqueous solutions

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen_fluoride&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Aqueous solutions")]

Main article: [Hydrofluoric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrofluoric_acid "Hydrofluoric acid")

HF is [miscible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Miscible "Miscible") with water (dissolves in any proportion). In contrast, the other hydrogen halides exhibit limiting solubilities in water. Hydrogen fluoride forms a monohydrate HF.H 2 O with melting point −40°C (−40°F), which is 44°C (79°F) above the melting point of pure HF.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_fluoride#cite_note-9)

**HF and H 2 O similarities**
[![Image 47: graph showing trend-breaking water and HF boiling points: big jogs up versus a trend that is down with lower molecular weight for the other series members.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Boiling-points_Chalcogen-Halogen.svg/330px-Boiling-points_Chalcogen-Halogen.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Boiling-points_Chalcogen-Halogen.svg)[![Image 48: graph showing humps of melting temperature, most prominent is at HF 50% mole fraction](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/HF-H2O_Phase-Diagram.svg/250px-HF-H2O_Phase-Diagram.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:HF-H2O_Phase-Diagram.svg)
Boiling points of the hydrogen halides (blue) and [hydrogen chalcogenides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_chalcogenide "Hydrogen chalcogenide") (red): HF and H 2 O break trends.Freezing point of HF/ H 2 O mixtures: arrows indicate compounds in the solid state.

Aqueous solutions of HF are called [hydrofluoric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrofluoric_acid "Hydrofluoric acid"). When dilute, hydrofluoric acid behaves like a weak acid, unlike the other hydrohalic acids, due to the formation of hydrogen-bonded [ion pairs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ion_pair "Ion pair") [H 3 O+·F−]. However concentrated solutions are strong acids, because [bifluoride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bifluoride "Bifluoride") anions are predominant, instead of ion pairs. In liquid anhydrous HF, [self-ionization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_autoionization "Molecular autoionization") occurs:[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_fluoride#cite_note-10)[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_fluoride#cite_note-11)

3 HF ⇌ H 2 F+ + HF−2
which forms an extremely acidic liquid ([_H_ 0](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hammett_acidity_function "Hammett acidity function")=−15.1).

### Reactions with Lewis acids

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen_fluoride&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Reactions with Lewis acids")]

Like water, HF can act as a weak base, reacting with [Lewis acids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lewis_acid "Lewis acid") to give [superacids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superacid "Superacid"). A [Hammett acidity function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hammett_acidity_function "Hammett acidity function") (_H_ 0) of −21 is obtained with [antimony pentafluoride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antimony_pentafluoride "Antimony pentafluoride") (SbF 5), forming [fluoroantimonic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluoroantimonic_acid "Fluoroantimonic acid").[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_fluoride#cite_note-Jolly-12)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_fluoride#cite_note-Cotton_109-13)

Production
----------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen_fluoride&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Production")]

Hydrogen fluoride is typically produced by the reaction between [sulfuric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfuric_acid "Sulfuric acid") and pure grades of the mineral [fluorite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluorite "Fluorite"):[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_fluoride#cite_note-AigueperseMollard2000-14)

CaF 2 + H 2 SO 4 → 2 HF + CaSO 4
About 20% of manufactured HF is a byproduct of fertilizer production, which generates [hexafluorosilicic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hexafluorosilicic_acid "Hexafluorosilicic acid"). This acid can be degraded to release HF thermally and by hydrolysis:

H 2 SiF 6 → 2 HF + SiF 4 SiF 4 + 2 H 2 O → 4 HF + SiO 2

Use
---

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen_fluoride&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Use")]

In general, anhydrous hydrogen fluoride is more common industrially than its aqueous solution, [hydrofluoric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrofluoric_acid "Hydrofluoric acid"). Its main uses, on a tonnage basis, are as a precursor to [organofluorine compounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organofluorine_compound "Organofluorine compound") and a precursor to [cryolite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cryolite "Cryolite") for the electrolysis of aluminium.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_fluoride#cite_note-AigueperseMollard2000-14)

### Precursor to organofluorine compounds

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen_fluoride&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Precursor to organofluorine compounds")]

HF reacts with chlorocarbons to give fluorocarbons. An important application of this reaction is the production of [tetrafluoroethylene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrafluoroethylene "Tetrafluoroethylene") (TFE), precursor to [Teflon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teflon "Teflon"). Chloroform is fluorinated by HF to produce [chlorodifluoromethane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chlorodifluoromethane "Chlorodifluoromethane") (R-22):[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_fluoride#cite_note-AigueperseMollard2000-14)

CHCl 3 + 2 HF → CHClF 2 + 2 HCl
Pyrolysis of chlorodifluoromethane (at 550-750°C) yields TFE.

HF is a reactive solvent in the [electrochemical fluorination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrochemical_fluorination "Electrochemical fluorination") of organic compounds. In this approach, HF is oxidized in the presence of a [hydrocarbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrocarbon "Hydrocarbon") and the fluorine replaces C–H bonds with [C–F bonds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon%E2%80%93fluorine_bond "Carbon–fluorine bond"). [Perfluorinated carboxylic acids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perfluorinated_carboxylic_acid "Perfluorinated carboxylic acid") and [sulfonic acids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfonic_acid "Sulfonic acid") are produced in this way.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_fluoride#cite_note-Ullmann-15)

[1,1-Difluoroethane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1,1-Difluoroethane "1,1-Difluoroethane") is produced by adding HF to [acetylene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene "Acetylene") using mercury as a catalyst.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_fluoride#cite_note-Ullmann-15)

HC≡CH + 2 HF → CH 3 CHF 2
The intermediate in this process is [vinyl fluoride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vinyl_fluoride "Vinyl fluoride") or fluoroethylene, the [monomeric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monomer "Monomer") precursor to [polyvinyl fluoride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyvinyl_fluoride "Polyvinyl fluoride").

### Precursor to metal fluorides and fluorine

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen_fluoride&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Precursor to metal fluorides and fluorine")]

The [electrowinning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrowinning "Electrowinning") of [aluminium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aluminium "Aluminium") relies on the electrolysis of aluminium fluoride in molten cryolite. Several kilograms of HF are consumed per ton of Al produced. Other metal fluorides are produced using HF, including [uranium tetrafluoride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uranium_tetrafluoride "Uranium tetrafluoride").[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_fluoride#cite_note-AigueperseMollard2000-14)

HF is the precursor to elemental [fluorine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluorine "Fluorine"), F 2, by [electrolysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrolysis "Electrolysis") of a solution of HF and [potassium bifluoride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_bifluoride "Potassium bifluoride"). The potassium bifluoride is needed because anhydrous HF does not conduct electricity. Several thousand tons of F 2 are produced annually.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_fluoride#cite_note-16)

### Catalyst

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen_fluoride&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Catalyst")]

HF serves as a [catalyst](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catalyst "Catalyst") in [alkylation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkylation "Alkylation") processes in refineries. It is used in the majority of the installed [linear alkyl benzene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_alkyl_benzene "Linear alkyl benzene") production facilities in the world. The process involves dehydrogenation of _n_-paraffins to olefins, and subsequent reaction with benzene using HF as catalyst. For example, in [oil refineries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oil_refineries "Oil refineries") "alkylate", a component of high-[octane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octane "Octane") petrol ([gasoline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gasoline "Gasoline")), is generated in alkylation units, which combine C 3 and C 4 olefins and [_iso_-butane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methylpropane "Methylpropane").[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_fluoride#cite_note-AigueperseMollard2000-14)

### Solvent

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen_fluoride&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Solvent")]

Hydrogen fluoride is an excellent solvent. Reflecting the ability of HF to participate in hydrogen bonding, even proteins and carbohydrates dissolve in HF and can be recovered from it. In contrast, most non-fluoride inorganic chemicals react with HF rather than dissolving.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_fluoride#cite_note-17)

Health effects
--------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen_fluoride&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Health effects")]

[![Image 49: left and right hands, two views, burned index fingers](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/HF_burned_hands.jpg/250px-HF_burned_hands.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:HF_burned_hands.jpg)

HF burns, not evident until a day after

Main articles: [Hydrofluoric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrofluoric_acid "Hydrofluoric acid") and [Hydrofluoric acid burn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrofluoric_acid_burn "Hydrofluoric acid burn")

Hydrogen fluoride is highly corrosive and a powerful contact poison. Exposure requires immediate medical attention.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_fluoride#cite_note-emergency.cdc.gov-18) It can cause blindness by rapid destruction of the [corneas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cornea "Cornea"). Breathing in hydrogen fluoride at high levels or in combination with skin contact can cause death from an [irregular heartbeat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardiac_dysrhythmia "Cardiac dysrhythmia") or from [pulmonary edema](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulmonary_edema "Pulmonary edema") (fluid buildup in the lungs).[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_fluoride#cite_note-emergency.cdc.gov-18) Exposure of the intestinal system to HF solution is known to cause fulminant acute colitis requiring surgical intervention.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_fluoride#cite_note-19)
