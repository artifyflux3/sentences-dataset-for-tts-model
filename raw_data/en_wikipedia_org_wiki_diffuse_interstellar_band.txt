Relative strengths of observed diffuse interstellar bands

**Diffuse interstellar bands** (DIBs) are [absorption](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absorption_(optics) "Absorption (optics)") features seen in the [spectra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_spectroscopy "Astronomical spectroscopy") of [astronomical objects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_object "Astronomical object") in the [Milky Way](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milky_Way "Milky Way") and other galaxies. They are caused by the absorption of light by the [interstellar medium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_medium "Interstellar medium"). Circa 500 bands have now been seen, in [ultraviolet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultraviolet "Ultraviolet"), [visible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visible_light "Visible light") and [infrared](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infrared "Infrared") wavelengths.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffuse_interstellar_band#cite_note-1)

The origin of most DIBs remains unknown, with common suggestions being [polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polycyclic_aromatic_hydrocarbon "Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbon") and other large [carbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon "Carbon")-bearing molecules.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffuse_interstellar_band#cite_note-nature1997-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffuse_interstellar_band#cite_note-3) Only one DIB carrier has been identified: ionised [buckminsterfullerene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene "Buckminsterfullerene") (C 60+), which is responsible for several DIBs in the near-infrared.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffuse_interstellar_band#cite_note-nature.com-4) The carriers of most DIBs remain unidentified.

Discovery and history
---------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Diffuse_interstellar_bands&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Discovery and history")]

Much astronomical work relies on the study of [spectra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum "Electromagnetic spectrum") - the light from [astronomical objects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_object "Astronomical object") dispersed using a [prism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prism_(optics) "Prism (optics)") or, more usually, a [diffraction grating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffraction_grating "Diffraction grating"). A typical stellar spectrum will consist of a [continuum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuum_(theory) "Continuum (theory)"), containing [absorption lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absorption_line "Absorption line"), each of which is attributed to a particular [atomic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atom "Atom")[energy level](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_level "Energy level") transition in the atmosphere of the star.

The appearances of all astronomical objects are affected by [extinction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction_(astronomy) "Extinction (astronomy)"), the absorption and scattering of [photons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon "Photon") by the [interstellar medium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_medium "Interstellar medium"). Relevant to DIBs is interstellar absorption, which predominantly affects the whole spectrum in a continuous way, rather than causing absorption lines. In 1922, though, astronomer [Mary Lea Heger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mary_Lea_Heger "Mary Lea Heger")[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffuse_interstellar_band#cite_note-5) first observed a number of line-like absorption features which seemed to be interstellar in origin.

Their interstellar nature was shown by the fact that the strength of the observed absorption was roughly proportional to the extinction, and that in objects with widely differing [radial velocities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radial_velocity "Radial velocity") the absorption bands were not affected by [Doppler shifting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doppler_shift "Doppler shift"), implying that the absorption was not occurring in or around the object concerned.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffuse_interstellar_band#cite_note-BibcodeARAHdoiannurevaa-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffuse_interstellar_band#cite_note-7)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffuse_interstellar_band#cite_note-8) The name diffuse interstellar band, or DIB for short, was coined to reflect the fact that the absorption features are much broader than the normal absorption lines seen in stellar spectra.

The first DIBs observed were those at wavelengths 578.0 and 579.7 nanometers (visible light corresponds to a wavelength range of 400 - 700 nanometers). Other strong DIBs are seen at 628.4, 661.4 and 443.0 nm. The 443.0 nm DIB is particularly broad at about 1.2 nm across - typical intrinsic stellar absorption features are 0.1 nm or less across.

Later [spectroscopic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_spectroscopy "Astronomical spectroscopy") studies at higher [spectral resolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectral_resolution "Spectral resolution") and sensitivity revealed more and more DIBs; a catalogue of them in 1975 contained 25 known DIBs, and a decade later the number known had more than doubled. The first detection-limited survey was published by [Peter Jenniskens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Jenniskens "Peter Jenniskens") and Xavier Desert in 1994 (see Figure above),[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffuse_interstellar_band#cite_note-9) which led to the first conference on The Diffuse Interstellar Bands at the University of Colorado in Boulder on May 16â€“19, 1994. Today circa 500 have been detected.

In recent years, very high resolution [spectrographs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectrograph "Spectrograph") on the world's most powerful [telescopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Telescope "Telescope") have been used to observe and analyse DIBs.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffuse_interstellar_band#cite_note-10) Spectral resolutions of 0.005 nm are now routine using instruments at observatories such as the [European Southern Observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Southern_Observatory "European Southern Observatory") at [Cerro Paranal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cerro_Paranal "Cerro Paranal"), [Chile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chile "Chile"), and the [Anglo-Australian Observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anglo-Australian_Observatory "Anglo-Australian Observatory") in [Australia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Australia "Australia"), and at these high resolutions, many DIBs are found to contain considerable sub-structure.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffuse_interstellar_band#cite_note-11)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffuse_interstellar_band#cite_note-12)

The nature of the carriers
--------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Diffuse_interstellar_bands&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: The nature of the carriers")]

The great problem with DIBs, apparent from the earliest observations, was that their central wavelengths did not correspond with any known [spectral lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectral_line "Spectral line") of any [ion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ion "Ion") or [molecule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecule "Molecule"), and so the material which was responsible for the absorption could not be identified. A large number of theories were advanced as the number of known DIBs grew, and determining the nature of the absorbing material (the 'carrier') became a crucial problem in [astrophysics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrophysics "Astrophysics").

One important observational result is that the strengths of most DIBs are not strongly correlated with each other. This means that there must be many carriers, rather than one carrier responsible for all DIBs. Also significant is that the strength of DIBs is broadly correlated with the [interstellar extinction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_extinction "Interstellar extinction"). Extinction is caused by [interstellar dust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_dust "Interstellar dust"); however, DIBs, are not likely to be caused by dust grains.

The existence of sub-structure in DIBs supports the idea that they are caused by molecules. Substructure results from band heads in the rotational band contour and from isotope substitution. In a molecule containing, say, three [carbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon "Carbon") atoms, some of the carbon will be in the form of the [carbon-13](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon-13 "Carbon-13")[isotope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotope "Isotope"), so that while most molecules will contain three [carbon-12](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon-12 "Carbon-12") atoms, some will contain two 12 C atoms and one 13 C atom, much less will contain one 12 C and two 13 C, and a very small fraction will contain three 13 C molecules. Each of these forms of the molecule will create an absorption line at a slightly different rest wavelength.

The most likely candidate molecules for producing DIBs are thought to be large carbon-bearing molecules, which are common in the interstellar medium. [Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polycyclic_aromatic_hydrocarbon "Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbon"), long carbon-chain molecules such as [polyynes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyyne "Polyyne"), and [fullerenes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fullerenes "Fullerenes") are all potentially important.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffuse_interstellar_band#cite_note-BibcodeARAHdoiannurevaa-6)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffuse_interstellar_band#cite_note-13) These types of molecule experience rapid and efficient deactivation when excited by a photon, which both broadens the spectral lines and makes them stable enough to exist in the interstellar medium.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffuse_interstellar_band#cite_note-14)[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffuse_interstellar_band#cite_note-15)

Identification of C 60+ as a carrier
------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Diffuse_interstellar_bands&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Identification of C60+ as a carrier")]

As of 2021 the only molecule confirmed to be a DIB carrier is the [buckminsterfullerene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene "Buckminsterfullerene") ion, C 60+. Soon after [Harry Kroto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harry_Kroto "Harry Kroto") discovered [fullerenes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fullerene "Fullerene") in the 1980s, he proposed that they could be DIB carriers.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffuse_interstellar_band#cite_note-kroto88-16) Kroto pointed out that the ionised form C 60+ was more likely to survive in the diffuse interstellar medium.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffuse_interstellar_band#cite_note-17)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffuse_interstellar_band#cite_note-kroto88-16) However, the lack of a reliable laboratory spectrum of gas-phase C 60+ made this proposal difficult to test.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffuse_interstellar_band#cite_note-18)

In the early 1990s, laboratory spectra of C 60+ were obtained by embedding the molecule in solid ices, which showed strong bands in the near-infrared. In 1994, [Bernard Foing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bernard_Foing "Bernard Foing") and [Pascale Ehrenfreund](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pascale_Ehrenfreund "Pascale Ehrenfreund") detected new DIBs with wavelengths close to those in the laboratory spectra, and argued that the difference was due to an offset between the gas-phase and solid-phase wavelengths.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffuse_interstellar_band#cite_note-19) However, this conclusion was disputed by other researchers, such as [Peter Jenniskens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Jenniskens "Peter Jenniskens"), on multiple spectroscopic and observational grounds.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffuse_interstellar_band#cite_note-20)

A laboratory gas-phase spectrum of C 60+ was obtained in 2015 by a group led by [John Maier](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=John_Maier&action=edit&redlink=1 "John Maier (page does not exist)").[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffuse_interstellar_band#cite_note-:0-21) Their results matched the band wavelengths that had been observed by Foing and Ehrenfreund in 1994.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffuse_interstellar_band#cite_note-:0-21) Three weaker bands of C 60+ were found in interstellar spectra soon afterwards, resolving one of the earlier objections raised by Jenniskens.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffuse_interstellar_band#cite_note-22) New objections were raised by other researchers,[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffuse_interstellar_band#cite_note-23) but by 2019 the C 60+ bands and their assignment had been confirmed by multiple groups of astronomers[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffuse_interstellar_band#cite_note-24)[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffuse_interstellar_band#cite_note-25) and laboratory chemists.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffuse_interstellar_band#cite_note-26)
