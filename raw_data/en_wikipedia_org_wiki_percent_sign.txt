The **percent sign**% (sometimes **per cent sign** in [British English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_English "British English")) is the symbol used to indicate a [percentage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Percentage "Percentage"), a number or [ratio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ratio "Ratio") as a [fraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fraction_(mathematics) "Fraction (mathematics)") of 100. Related signs include the [permille](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permille "Permille") (per thousand) sign ‰ and the [permyriad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basis_point#Permyriad "Basis point") (per ten thousand) sign ‱ (also known as a [basis point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basis_point "Basis point")), which indicate that a number is divided by one thousand or ten thousand, respectively. Higher proportions use [parts-per notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parts-per_notation "Parts-per notation").

English style guides prescribe writing the percent sign following the number without any space between (e.g. 50%).[[sources 1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Percent_sign#cite_note-8) However, the [International System of Units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Units "International System of Units") and [ISO 31-0](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO_31-0 "ISO 31-0") standard prescribe a space between the number and percent sign,[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Percent_sign#cite_note-9)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Percent_sign#cite_note-10)[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Percent_sign#cite_note-11) in line with the general practice of using a [non-breaking space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-breaking_space "Non-breaking space") between a [numerical value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Numerical_value "Numerical value") and its corresponding [unit of measurement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_of_measurement "Unit of measurement").

Other languages have other rules for spacing in front of the percent sign:

*   In [Czech](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Czech_language "Czech language") and in [Slovak](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slovak_language "Slovak language"), the percent sign is spaced with a non-breaking space if the number is used as a noun.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Percent_sign#cite_note-12) In Czech, no space is inserted if the number is used as an adjective (e.g. "a 50% increase"),[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Percent_sign#cite_note-13) whereas Slovak uses a non-breaking space in this case as well.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Percent_sign#cite_note-14)
*   In [Croatian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Croatian_language "Croatian language"), the percent sign is spaced[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Percent_sign#cite_note-15) with a non-breaking space.
*   In [Finnish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finnish_language "Finnish language"), the percent sign is always spaced, and a [case suffix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finnish_noun_cases "Finnish noun cases") can be attached to it using the [colon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colon_(punctuation) "Colon (punctuation)") (e.g. _50%:n kasvu_ 'an increase of 50%').[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Percent_sign#cite_note-16)
*   In [French](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_language "French language"), the percent sign must be spaced with a non-breaking space.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Percent_sign#cite_note-17)[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Percent_sign#cite_note-18)
*   According to the [Real Academia Española](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_Academia_Espa%C3%B1ola "Real Academia Española"), in [Spanish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spanish_language "Spanish language"), the percent sign should be spaced now, despite the fact that it is not the linguistic norm.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Percent_sign#cite_note-19) Despite that, in North American Spanish ([Mexico](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mexican_Spanish "Mexican Spanish") and the [US](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spanish_language_in_the_United_States "Spanish language in the United States")), several style guides and institutions either recommend the percent sign be written following the number without any space between or do so in their own publications in accordance with common usage in that region.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Percent_sign#cite_note-20)[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Percent_sign#cite_note-21)
*   In [Russian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_language "Russian language"), the percent sign is rarely spaced, contrary to the guidelines of the GOST 8.417-2002 state standard.
*   According to the [Swedish Language Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swedish_Language_Council "Swedish Language Council"), the percent sign should be preceded by a space in [Swedish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swedish_language "Swedish language"), as all other units.
*   In [German](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_language "German language"), the space is prescribed by the regulatory body in the national standard [DIN 5008](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DIN_5008 "DIN 5008").
*   In [Turkish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turkish_language "Turkish language") and some other [Turkic languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turkic_languages "Turkic languages"), the percent sign precedes rather than follows the number, without an intervening space.
*   In [Persian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persian_language "Persian language") texts, the percent sign may either precede or follow the number, in either case without a space.
*   In [Arabic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabic_language "Arabic language"), the percent sign follows the number; as Arabic is written from [right to left](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_to_left "Right to left"), this means that the percent sign is to the left of the number, usually without a space.
*   In [Hebrew](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hebrew_language "Hebrew language"), the percent sign is written to the right of the number, just as in English, without an intervening space. This is because numbers in Hebrew (which otherwise is written from right to left) are written from left to right, as in English.
*   In [Dutch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dutch_language "Dutch language"), the official rule ([NBN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bureau_of_Normalization "Bureau of Normalization") Z 01-002) is to place a space between the number and the sign (e.g. "een stijging van 50%"), but most of the time, the space is missing (e.g. "een stijging van 50%").[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Percent_sign#cite_note-22)

It is often recommended that the percent sign only be used in tables and other places with space restrictions. In running text, it should be spelled out as _percent_ or _per cent_ (often in newspapers). For example, not "Sales increased by 24% over 2006" but "Sales increased by 24 percent over 2006".[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Percent_sign#cite_note-23)[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Percent_sign#cite_note-24)[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Percent_sign#cite_note-25)

Prior to 1425, there is no known evidence of a special symbol being used for percentages. The [Italian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italian_language "Italian language") term _per cento_, "for a hundred", was used as well as several different abbreviations (e.g. "per 100", "p 100", "p cento"). Examples of this can be seen in the 1339 arithmetic text (author unknown) depicted below.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Percent_sign#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith1898p._437-26) The letter p with its [descender](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Descender "Descender") crossed by a horizontal or diagonal stroke (to indicate abbreviation) [conventionally stood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scribal_abbreviation "Scribal abbreviation") for per, por, par, or pur in Medieval and Renaissance [palaeography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palaeography "Palaeography").[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Percent_sign#cite_note-27)

[![Image 1: 1339 arithmetic text](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Percent_sign_in_1339.png/450px-Percent_sign_in_1339.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Percent_sign_in_1339.png)

1339 arithmetic text in _Rara Arithmetica_, p. 437

At some point, a scribe used the abbreviation _pc_ with a tiny loop or circle (depicting the ending _-o_ used in [Italian ordinals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Masculine_ordinal "Masculine ordinal"), as in _primo_, _secondo_; it is analogous to the English _-th_ as in _25th_). This appears in some additional pages of a 1425 text which were probably added around 1435.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Percent_sign#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith1898pp._439%E2%80%93440-28)

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Percent_sign_in_1425.png/330px-Percent_sign_in_1425.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Percent_sign_in_1425.png)

1425 arithmetic text in _Rara Arithmetica_, p. 440[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Percent_sign#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith1898p._440-29)

The _pc_ with a loop eventually evolved into a horizontal fraction sign by 1650 (see below for an example in a 1684 text)[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Percent_sign#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith1898p._441-30) and thereafter lost the _per_.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Percent_sign#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith1898p._440-29)

[![Image 3: 1684 arithmetic text](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Percent_sign_in_1648.png/250px-Percent_sign_in_1648.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Percent_sign_in_1648.png)

1684 arithmetic text in _Rara Arithmetica_, p. 441

In 1925, D. E. Smith wrote, "The [solidus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solidus_(punctuation) "Solidus (punctuation)") form ([![Image 4: A modern percentage sign, a diagonal line with a zero on either side](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Percentsign_in_Poor_Richard_font.png/20px-Percentsign_in_Poor_Richard_font.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Percentsign_in_Poor_Richard_font.png)) is modern."[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Percent_sign#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith1925Vol._2,_p._250-31)

The [Unicode](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unicode "Unicode") code points are:

*   U+0025%PERCENT SIGN (HTML `&#37;`, `&percnt;`[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Percent_sign#cite_note-32)),
*   U+FF05％[FULLWIDTH PERCENT SIGN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halfwidth_and_fullwidth_forms "Halfwidth and fullwidth forms")
*   U+FE6A﹪[SMALL PERCENT SIGN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Small_Form_Variants "Small Form Variants")
*   U+066A٪ARABIC PERCENT SIGN, which has the circles replaced by square dots set on edge, the shape of the digit 0 in [Eastern Arabic numerals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastern_Arabic_numerals "Eastern Arabic numerals").
*   U+332B㌫SQUARE PAASENTO, パーセント (_pāsento_, from English "percent") in one square character.

The [ASCII code](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ASCII_code "ASCII code") for the percent [character](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Character_(computing) "Character (computing)") is 37, or 0x25 in [hexadecimal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hexadecimal "Hexadecimal").

Names for the percent sign include percent sign (in [ITU-T](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ITU-T "ITU-T")), [mod](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modulo_operation "Modulo operation"), grapes (in [hacker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hacker_(programmer_subculture) "Hacker (programmer subculture)")[jargon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jargon "Jargon")),[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Percent_sign#cite_note-33) and the humorous double-oh-seven (in [INTERCAL](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/INTERCAL "INTERCAL")).

In [computing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computing "Computing"), the percent [character](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Character_(computing) "Character (computing)") is also used for the [modulo operation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modulo_operation "Modulo operation") in [programming languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Programming_language "Programming language") that derive their syntax from the [C programming language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C_(programming_language) "C (programming language)"), which in turn acquired this usage from the earlier [B](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/B_(programming_language) "B (programming language)").[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Percent_sign#cite_note-34)

In the textual representation of [URIs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uniform_Resource_Identifier "Uniform Resource Identifier"), a% immediately followed by a 2-digit [hexadecimal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hexadecimal "Hexadecimal") number denotes an octet specifying (part of) a character that might otherwise not be allowed in URIs (see [percent-encoding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Percent-encoding "Percent-encoding")).

In [SQL](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SQL "SQL"), the percent sign is a [wildcard character](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wildcard_character "Wildcard character") in "LIKE" expressions, for example `SELECT * FROM table WHERE fullname LIKE 'Lisa %'` will fetch all records whose names start with "Lisa ".

In [TeX](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TeX "TeX") (and therefore also in [LaTeX](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LaTeX "LaTeX")) and [PostScript](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PostScript "PostScript"), and in [GNU Octave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GNU_Octave "GNU Octave") and [MATLAB](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MATLAB "MATLAB"),[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Percent_sign#cite_note-35) a% denotes a line [comment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comment_(computing) "Comment (computing)").

In [BASIC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BASIC "BASIC"), [Visual Basic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visual_Basic "Visual Basic"), [ASP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Active_Server_Pages "Active Server Pages"), and [VBA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visual_Basic_for_Applications "Visual Basic for Applications") a trailing% after a variable name marks it as an [integer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer").

In [ASP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Active_Server_Pages "Active Server Pages"), the percent sign can be used to indicate the start and end of the ASP code `<%...... %>`

In [Perl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perl "Perl")% is the [sigil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sigil_(computer_programming) "Sigil (computer programming)") for [hashes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hash_table "Hash table").

In many programming languages' string formatting operations (performed by functions such as `printf` and `scanf`), the percent sign denotes parts of the template string that will be replaced with arguments. (See [printf format string](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Printf_format_string "Printf format string").) In [Python](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Python_(programming_language) "Python (programming language)") and [Ruby](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ruby_(programming_language) "Ruby (programming language)") the percent sign is also used as the string formatting operator.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Percent_sign#cite_note-36)[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Percent_sign#cite_note-37)[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Percent_sign#cite_note-38)

In the [command processors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Command_processor "Command processor")[COMMAND.COM](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/COMMAND.COM "COMMAND.COM") (DOS) and [CMD.EXE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CMD.EXE "CMD.EXE") (OS/2 and Windows), %1, %2,... stand for the first, second,... parameters of a [batch file](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Batch_file "Batch file"). %0 stands for the specification of the batch file itself as typed on the command line. The% sign is also used similarly in the FOR command. %VAR1% represents the value of an [environment variable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Environment_variable "Environment variable") named VAR1. Thus: `set PATH=c:\;%PATH%` sets a new value for PATH, that being the old value preceded by "`c:\;`". Because these uses give the percent sign special meaning, the sequence%% (two percent signs) is used to represent a literal percent sign, so that: `set PATH=c:\;%%PATH%%` would set PATH to the literal value "`c:\;%PATH%`".

In the [C Shell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C_Shell "C Shell") and [Z shell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Z_shell "Z shell"),% is part of the default [command prompt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prompt_(computing) "Prompt (computing)").

In linguistics, the percent sign is prepended to an example sentence or other [string](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/String_(computer_science) "String (computer science)") to show that it is judged [well-formed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Well-formed_formula "Well-formed formula") (grammatical) by some speakers and ill-formed by others. This may be due to differences in [dialect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dialect "Dialect") or even individual [idiolects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Idiolect "Idiolect").[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Percent_sign#cite_note-39)[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Percent_sign#cite_note-40) This use is similar to those of the [asterisk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asterisk#Ungrammaticality "Asterisk") to mark ill-formed strings, the [question mark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Question_mark#Linguistics "Question mark") to mark strings where well-formedness is unclear, and the [number sign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_sign#Other_uses "Number sign") to mark strings that are syntactically well-formed but semantically or pragmatically nonsensical.
