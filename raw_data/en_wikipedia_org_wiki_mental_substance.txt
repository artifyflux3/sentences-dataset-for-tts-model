**Mental substance**, according to the idea held by [dualists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mind-body_dualism "Mind-body dualism") and [idealists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Idealism "Idealism"), is a non-physical substance of which minds are composed. This substance is often referred to as [consciousness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Consciousness "Consciousness").

This is opposed to the view of [materialists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Materialism "Materialism"), who hold that what we normally think of as mental substance is ultimately physical matter (i.e., brains).

[Ren√© Descartes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Descartes "Ren√© Descartes"), who was most famous for the assertion "[I think therefore I am](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/I_think_therefore_I_am "I think therefore I am")", played a major role in developing the [mind‚Äìbody problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mind%E2%80%93body_problem "Mind‚Äìbody problem"). He describes his theory of mental substance (which he calls _**res cogitans**_ distinguishing it from the _[res extensa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Res\_extensa "Res extensa")_) in the _Second [Meditation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meditations\_on\_First\_Philosophy "Meditations on First Philosophy")_ (II.8) and in _[Principia Philosophiae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principia\_Philosophiae "Principia Philosophiae")_ (2.002).

He used a more precise definition of the word "substance" than is currently popular: that a substance is something which can exist without the existence of any other substance. For many philosophers, this word or the phrase "mental substance" has a special meaning.

History
-------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Mental_substance&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: History")]

According to Descartes, God first created eternal truths and then the world from nothing, governing it with His [divine providence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divine_providence "Divine providence"). He took special care of human creatures, placing innate ideas in their thought, starting with the ideas of [perfection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perfection "Perfection") and [infinity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinity "Infinity").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mental_substance#cite_note-1)

[Gottfried Leibniz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gottfried_Leibniz "Gottfried Leibniz"), belonging to the generation immediately after Descartes, held the position that the mental world was built up of [monads](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monad_(philosophy) "Monad (philosophy)"), mental objects that are not part of the physical world (see _[Monadology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monadology "Monadology")_).

The distinction between _res cogitans_ and _res extensa_ was taken up in [Spinoza](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spinoza "Spinoza")'s _[Ethics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethics\_(Spinoza\_book) "Ethics (Spinoza book)")_, written between 1661 and 1675, according to which Thought and [Extension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Res_extensa "Res extensa") are two infinite attributes of the one divine Substance. Soul and body are in turn two finite modes of Thought and Extension.
