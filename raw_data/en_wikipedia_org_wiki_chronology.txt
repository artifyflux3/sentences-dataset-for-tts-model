[Joseph Scaliger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Scaliger "Joseph Scaliger")'s _De emendatione temporum_ (1583) began the modern science of chronology[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chronology#cite_note-1)

**Chronology** (from [Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin "Latin")_chronologia_, from [Ancient Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek "Ancient Greek")[χρόνος](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CF%87%CF%81%CF%8C%CE%BD%CE%BF%CF%82 "wikt:χρόνος"), _chrónos_, 'time'; and -λογία, _[-logia](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-logia "wikt:-logia")_)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chronology#cite_note-2) is the [science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Science "Science") of arranging events in their order of occurrence in [time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time "Time"). Consider, for example, the use of a [timeline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline "Timeline") or [sequence of events](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sequence_of_events "Sequence of events"). It is also "the determination of the actual temporal sequence of past events".[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chronology#cite_note-wordnet-3)

Chronology is a part of [periodization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Periodization "Periodization"). It is also a part of the discipline of [history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History "History") including [earth history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth_history "Earth history"), the [earth sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth_sciences "Earth sciences"), and study of the [geologic time scale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geologic_time_scale "Geologic time scale").

Related fields
--------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Chronology&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Related fields")]

Chronology is the science of locating historical events in time. It relies mostly upon [chronometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chronometry "Chronometry"), which is also known as timekeeping, and [historiography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Historiography "Historiography"), which examines the writing of history and the use of historical methods. [Radiocarbon dating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiocarbon_dating "Radiocarbon dating") estimates the age of formerly living things by measuring the proportion of [carbon-14](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon-14 "Carbon-14")[isotope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotope "Isotope") in their [carbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon "Carbon") content. [Dendrochronology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dendrochronology "Dendrochronology") estimates the age of trees by [correlation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Correlation "Correlation") of the various [growth rings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Growth_rings "Growth rings") in their wood to known year-by-year reference sequences in the region to reflect year-to-year climatic variation. Dendrochronology is used in turn as a [calibration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calibration "Calibration") reference for [radiocarbon dating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiocarbon_dating "Radiocarbon dating") curves.

Calendar and era
----------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Chronology&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Calendar and era")]

Main article: [Calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calendar "Calendar")

The familiar terms _[calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calendar "Calendar")_ and _[era](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Era "Era")_ (within the meaning of a coherent system of numbered calendar years) concern two complementary fundamental concepts of chronology. For example, during eight centuries the calendar belonging to the [Christian era](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christian_era "Christian era"), which era was taken in use in the 8th century by [Bede](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bede "Bede"), was the Julian calendar, but after the year 1582 it was the Gregorian calendar. [Dionysius Exiguus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dionysius_Exiguus "Dionysius Exiguus") (about the year 500) was the founder of that era, which is nowadays the most widespread dating system on earth. An [epoch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epoch_(reference_date) "Epoch (reference date)") is the date (year usually) when an era begins.

### Ab Urbe condita era

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Chronology&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Ab Urbe condita era")]

Main article: [Ab urbe condita](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ab_urbe_condita "Ab urbe condita")

_Ab Urbe condita_ is [Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin "Latin") for "from [the founding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Founding_of_Rome "Founding of Rome") of the City ([Rome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rome "Rome"))",[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chronology#cite_note-4) traditionally set in 753 BC. It was used to identify the Roman year by a few Roman historians. Modern historians use it much more frequently than the Romans themselves did; the dominant method of identifying Roman years was to name the two [consuls](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Consul "Consul") who held office that year. Before the advent of the modern critical edition of historical Roman works, AUC was indiscriminately added to them by earlier editors, making it appear more widely used than it actually was.

It was used systematically for the first time only about the year 400, by the Iberian historian [Orosius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paulus_Orosius "Paulus Orosius"). [Pope Boniface IV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pope_Boniface_IV "Pope Boniface IV"), in about the year 600, seems to have been the first who made a connection between this era and [Anno Domini](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anno_Domini "Anno Domini"). (AD 1 = AUC 754.)

### Astronomical era

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Chronology&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Astronomical era")]

Main article: [Astronomical year numbering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_year_numbering "Astronomical year numbering")

Dionysius Exiguus' Anno Domini era (which contains only calendar years _AD_) was extended by [Bede](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bede "Bede") to the complete [Christian era](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christian_era "Christian era") (which contains, in addition all calendar years _BC_, but no _year zero_). Ten centuries after Bede, the French astronomers [Philippe de la Hire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philippe_de_la_Hire "Philippe de la Hire") (in the year 1702) and [Jacques Cassini](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacques_Cassini "Jacques Cassini") (in the year 1740), purely to simplify certain calculations, put the [Julian Dating System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Julian_Date "Julian Date") (proposed in the year 1583 by [Joseph Scaliger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Scaliger "Joseph Scaliger")) and with it an astronomical era into use, which contains a [leap year](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leap_year "Leap year") zero, which precedes the year 1 (AD).[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chronology#cite_note-5)

Prehistory
----------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Chronology&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Prehistory")]

While of critical importance to the historian, methods of determining chronology are used in most disciplines of science, especially [astronomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy "Astronomy"), [geology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geology "Geology"), [paleontology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paleontology "Paleontology") and [archaeology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archaeology "Archaeology").

In the absence of [written history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Written_history "Written history"), with its [chronicles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chronicle "Chronicle") and [king lists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category:Regnal_lists "Category:Regnal lists"), late 19th century archaeologists found that they could develop relative chronologies based on pottery techniques and styles. In the field of [Egyptology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egyptology "Egyptology"), [William Flinders Petrie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Flinders_Petrie "William Flinders Petrie") pioneered sequence dating to penetrate pre-dynastic [Neolithic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neolithic "Neolithic") times, using groups of contemporary artefacts deposited together at a single time in graves and working backwards methodically from the earliest historical phases of Egypt. This method of dating is known as [seriation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seriation_(archaeology) "Seriation (archaeology)").

Known wares discovered at strata in sometimes quite distant sites, the product of trade, helped extend the network of chronologies. Some cultures have retained the name applied to them in reference to characteristic forms, for lack of an idea of what they called themselves: "The [Beaker People](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beaker_People "Beaker People")" in northern Europe during the 3rd millennium BCE, for example. The study of the means of placing pottery and other cultural artifacts into some kind of order proceeds in two phases, classification and typology: Classification creates categories for the purposes of description, and typology seeks to identify and analyse changes that allow artifacts to be placed into sequences.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chronology#cite_note-6)

Laboratory techniques developed particularly after mid-20th century helped constantly revise and refine the chronologies developed for specific cultural areas. Unrelated dating methods help reinforce a chronology, an axiom of corroborative [evidence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evidence "Evidence"). Ideally, archaeological materials used for dating a site should complement each other and provide a means of cross-checking. Conclusions drawn from just one unsupported technique are usually regarded as unreliable.

Synchronism
-----------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Chronology&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Synchronism")]

The fundamental problem of chronology is to synchronize events. By synchronizing an event it becomes possible to relate it to the current time and to compare the event to other events. Among historians, a typical need is to synchronize the reigns of kings and leaders in order to relate the history of one country or region to that of another. For example, the [Chronicon of Eusebius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chronicon_(Eusebius) "Chronicon (Eusebius)") (325 A.D.) is one of the major works of historical synchronism. This work has two sections. The first contains narrative chronicles of nine different kingdoms: Chaldean, Assyrian, Median, Lydian, Persian, Hebrew, Greek, Peloponnesian, Asian, and Roman. The second part is a long table synchronizing the events from each of the nine kingdoms in parallel columns.

By comparing the parallel columns, the reader can determine which events were contemporaneous, or how many years separated two different events. To place all the events on the same time scale, Eusebius used an [Anno Mundi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anno_Mundi "Anno Mundi") (A.M.) era, meaning that events were dated from the supposed beginning of the world as computed from the [Book of Genesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Book_of_Genesis "Book of Genesis") in the Hebrew [Pentateuch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentateuch "Pentateuch"). According to the computation Eusebius used, this occurred in 5199 B.C. The Chronicon of Eusebius was widely used in the medieval world to establish the dates and times of historical events. Subsequent chronographers, such as [George Syncellus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_Syncellus "George Syncellus") (died circa 811), analyzed and elaborated on the Chronicon by comparing with other chronologies. The last great chronographer was [Joseph Justus Scaliger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Justus_Scaliger "Joseph Justus Scaliger") (1540-1609) who reconstructed the lost Chronicon and synchronized all of ancient history in his two major works, _De emendatione temporum_ (1583) and _Thesaurus temporum_ (1606). Much of modern historical datings and chronology of the ancient world ultimately derives from these two works.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chronology#cite_note-Grafton-7) Scaliger invented the concept of the [Julian Day](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Julian_day "Julian day") which is still used as the standard unified scale of time for both historians and astronomers.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

In addition to the literary methods of synchronism used by traditional chronographers such as Eusebius, Syncellus and Scaliger, it is possible to synchronize events by archaeological or astronomical means. For example, the [Eclipse of Thales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eclipse_of_Thales "Eclipse of Thales"), described in the first book of [Herodotus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herodotus "Herodotus") can potentially be used to date the Lydian War because the eclipse took place during the middle of an important battle in that war. Likewise, various eclipses and other astronomical events described in ancient records can be used to astronomically synchronize historical events.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chronology#cite_note-8) Another method to synchronize events is the use of archaeological findings, such as pottery, to do [sequence dating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dating_methodologies_in_archaeology "Dating methodologies in archaeology").
