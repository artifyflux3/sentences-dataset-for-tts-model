For the mathematical concept, see [Moment (mathematics)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_(mathematics) "Moment (mathematics)"). For the moment of a force, sometimes shortened to "moment", see [Torque](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torque "Torque").

A **moment** is a mathematical expression involving the product of a distance and a physical quantity such as a [force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Force "Force") or [electric charge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_charge "Electric charge"). Moments are usually defined with respect to a fixed reference point and refer to physical quantities located some distance from the reference point. For example, the moment of force, often called [torque](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torque "Torque"), is the product of a force on an object and the distance from the reference point to the object. In principle, any physical quantity can be multiplied by a distance to produce a moment. Commonly used quantities include forces, [masses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass "Mass"), and [electric charge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_charge "Electric charge") distributions; [a list of examples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_(physics)#See_also) is provided later.

In its most basic form, a moment is the [product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Product_(mathematics) "Product (mathematics)") of the distance to a point, raised to a power, and a physical quantity (such as force or electrical charge) at that point:

![Image 1: {\displaystyle \mu _{n}=r^{n}\,Q,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8969a071b94aaa8a2ac662dd7667d2f965e18a72)
where ![Image 2: {\displaystyle Q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8752c7023b4b3286800fe3238271bbca681219ed) is the physical quantity such as a force applied at a point, or a point charge, or a point mass, etc. If the quantity is not concentrated solely at a single point, the moment is the [integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral "Integral") of that quantity's density over space:

![Image 3: {\displaystyle \mu _{n}=\int r^{n}\rho (r)\,dr}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c4706323a7a3bdfcbde5b82b629688d6afa2ecc2)
where ![Image 4: {\displaystyle \rho }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1f7d439671d1289b6a816e6af7a304be40608d64) is the distribution of the density of charge, mass, or whatever quantity is being considered.

More complex forms take into account the angular relationships between the distance and the physical quantity, but the above equations capture the essential feature of a moment, namely the existence of an underlying ![Image 5: {\displaystyle r^{n}\rho (r)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9307da19a37c5697c214159d087aee99b4a4135b) or equivalent term. This implies that there are multiple moments (one for each value of _n_) and that the moment generally depends on the reference point from which the distance ![Image 6: {\displaystyle r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d1ecb613aa2984f0576f70f86650b7c2a132538) is measured, although for certain moments (technically, the lowest non-zero moment) this dependence vanishes and the moment becomes independent of the reference point.

Each value of _n_ corresponds to a different moment: the 1st moment corresponds to _n_=1; the 2nd moment to _n_=2, etc. The 0th moment (_n_=0) is sometimes called the _monopole moment_; the 1st moment (_n_=1) is sometimes called the _dipole moment_, and the 2nd moment (_n_=2) is sometimes called the _[quadrupole moment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrupole\_moment "Quadrupole moment")_, especially in the context of electric charge distributions.

Moments of mass:

Assuming a density function that is finite and localized to a particular region, outside that region a 1/_r_[potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_potential "Scalar potential") may be expressed as a series of [spherical harmonics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_harmonics "Spherical harmonics"):

The coefficients ![Image 8: {\displaystyle q_{\ell m}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8ed1d7e2e013705098720354ef5e11007fac2125) are known as _multipole moments_, and take the form:

![Image 9: {\displaystyle q_{\ell m}=\int (r')^{\ell }\,\rho (\mathbf {r'} )\,Y_{\ell m}^{*}(\theta ',\varphi ')\,d^{3}r'}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/066e85b109004eef582e88421cfd69c2306092b0)
where ![Image 10: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} '}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1317b633a9366fab48e4b85ddec1cd1c0a8c31b3) expressed in spherical coordinates ![Image 11: {\displaystyle \left(r',\varphi ',\theta '\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0f1f6065124f85c5768bad44b82fbdf508fd179c) is a variable of integration. A more complete treatment may be found in pages describing [multipole expansion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multipole_expansion "Multipole expansion") or [spherical multipole moments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_multipole_moments "Spherical multipole moments"). (The convention in the above equations was taken from Jackson[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_(physics)#cite_note-1) – the conventions used in the referenced pages may be slightly different.)

When ![Image 12: {\displaystyle \rho }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1f7d439671d1289b6a816e6af7a304be40608d64) represents an electric charge density, the ![Image 13: {\displaystyle q_{lm}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6a4f49930c198f3a99ddbbcd72e5def4cf2fc8d6) are, in a sense, projections of the moments of electric charge: ![Image 14: {\displaystyle q_{00}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/71b5ad8f450dd81480021224b712ecd9602cf3f2) is the monopole moment; the ![Image 15: {\displaystyle q_{1m}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5928c31dd778ff397093f6bb87793516a485599) are projections of the dipole moment, the ![Image 16: {\displaystyle q_{2m}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c2b4bdffa546a7f68e8775b0af026b5535ab1b9b) are projections of the quadrupole moment, etc.

Applications of multipole moments
---------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Moment_(physics)&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Applications of multipole moments")]

The multipole expansion applies to 1/_r_ scalar potentials, examples of which include the [electric potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_potential "Electric potential") and the [gravitational potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_potential "Gravitational potential"). For these potentials, the expression can be used to approximate the strength of a field produced by a localized distribution of charges (or mass) by calculating the first few moments. For sufficiently large _r_, a reasonable approximation can be obtained from just the monopole and dipole moments. Higher fidelity can be achieved by calculating higher order moments. Extensions of the technique can be used to [calculate interaction energies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_multipole_moments#Interaction_energies_of_spherical_multipoles "Spherical multipole moments") and intermolecular forces.

The technique can also be used to determine the properties of an unknown distribution ![Image 17: {\displaystyle \rho }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1f7d439671d1289b6a816e6af7a304be40608d64). Measurements pertaining to multipole moments may be taken and used to infer properties of the underlying distribution. This technique applies to small objects such as molecules,[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_(physics)#cite_note-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_(physics)#cite_note-3) but has also been applied to the universe itself,[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_(physics)#cite_note-4) being for example the technique employed by the [WMAP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WMAP "WMAP") and [Planck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_(spacecraft) "Planck (spacecraft)") experiments to analyze the [cosmic microwave background](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background "Cosmic microwave background") radiation.

A lever in balance

In works believed to stem from [Ancient Greece](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greece "Ancient Greece"), the concept of a moment is alluded to by the word [ῥοπή](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E1%BF%A5%CE%BF%CF%80%CE%AE "wikt:ῥοπή") (_rhopḗ_, lit. "inclination") and composites like [ἰσόρροπα](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E1%BC%B0%CF%83%CF%8C%CF%81%CF%81%CE%BF%CF%80%CE%B1 "wikt:ἰσόρροπα") (_isorropa_, lit. "of equal inclinations").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_(physics)#cite_note-mersenne-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_(physics)#cite_note-clagett2-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_(physics)#cite_note-lsj1-7) The context of these works is [mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanics "Mechanics") and [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry") involving the [lever](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lever "Lever").[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_(physics)#cite_note-clagett1-8) In particular, in extant works attributed to [Archimedes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archimedes "Archimedes"), the moment is pointed out in phrasings like:

"[Commensurable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commensurability_(mathematics) "Commensurability (mathematics)") magnitudes ([σύμμετρα](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CF%83%CF%8D%CE%BC%CE%BC%CE%B5%CF%84%CF%81%CE%B1#Ancient_Greek "wikt:σύμμετρα")[μεγέθεα](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%BC%CE%B5%CE%B3%CE%AD%CE%B8%CE%B5%CE%B1#Ancient_Greek "wikt:μεγέθεα")) [A and B] are equally balanced ([ἰσορροπέοντι](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E1%BC%B0%CF%83%CE%BF%CF%81%CF%81%CE%BF%CF%80%CE%AD%CE%BF%CE%BD%CF%84%CE%B9#Ancient_Greek "wikt:ἰσορροπέοντι"))[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_(physics)#cite_note-9) if their distances [to the center Γ, i.e., ΑΓ and ΓΒ] are [inversely proportional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proportionality_(mathematics)#Inverse_proportionality "Proportionality (mathematics)") ([ἀντιπεπονθότως](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E1%BC%80%CE%BD%CF%84%CE%B9%CF%80%CE%B5%CF%80%CE%BF%CE%BD%CE%B8%CF%8C%CF%84%CF%89%CF%82#Ancient_Greek "wikt:ἀντιπεπονθότως")) to their weights ([βάρεσιν](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%B2%CE%AC%CF%81%CE%B5%CF%83%CE%B9%CE%BD#Ancient_Greek "wikt:βάρεσιν"))."[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_(physics)#cite_note-clagett2-6)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_(physics)#cite_note-10)
Moreover, in extant texts such as _[The Method of Mechanical Theorems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The\_Method\_of\_Mechanical\_Theorems "The Method of Mechanical Theorems")_, moments are used to infer the [center of gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Center_of_gravity "Center of gravity"), area, and volume of geometric figures.

In 1269, [William of Moerbeke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_of_Moerbeke "William of Moerbeke") translates various works of [Archimedes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archimedes "Archimedes") and [Eutocious](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eutocius_of_Ascalon "Eutocius of Ascalon") into [Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin "Latin"). The term ῥοπή is [transliterated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transliteration "Transliteration") into _ropen_.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_(physics)#cite_note-clagett2-6)

Around 1450, [Jacobus Cremonensis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iacopo_da_San_Cassiano "Iacopo da San Cassiano") translates ῥοπή in similar texts into the [Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin "Latin") term _momentum_ (lit. "movement"[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_(physics)#cite_note-11)).[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_(physics)#cite_note-12)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_(physics)#cite_note-clagett2-6): 331  The same term is kept in a 1501 translation by [Giorgio Valla](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giorgio_Valla "Giorgio Valla"), and subsequently by [Francesco Maurolico](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Francesco_Maurolico "Francesco Maurolico"), [Federico Commandino](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federico_Commandino "Federico Commandino"), [Guidobaldo del Monte](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guidobaldo_del_Monte "Guidobaldo del Monte"), [Adriaan van Roomen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adriaan_van_Roomen "Adriaan van Roomen"), [Florence Rivault](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Florence_Rivault "Florence Rivault"), [Francesco Buonamici](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Francesco_Buonamici_(philosopher) "Francesco Buonamici (philosopher)"), [Marin Mersenne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marin_Mersenne "Marin Mersenne")[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_(physics)#cite_note-mersenne-5), and [Galileo Galilei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galileo_Galilei "Galileo Galilei"). That said, why was the word _momentum_ chosen for the translation? One clue, according to [Treccani](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treccani "Treccani"), is that _momento_ in medieval Italy, the place the early translators lived, in a transferred sense meant both a "moment of time" and a "moment of weight" (a small amount of weight that turns the [scale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steelyard_balance "Steelyard balance")).[[b]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_(physics)#cite_note-13)

In 1554, [Francesco Maurolico](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Francesco_Maurolico "Francesco Maurolico") clarifies the Latin term _momentum_ in the work _Prologi sive sermones_. Here is a Latin to English translation as given by [Marshall Clagett](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marshall_Clagett "Marshall Clagett"):[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_(physics)#cite_note-clagett2-6)

> "[...] equal weights at unequal distances do not weigh equally, but unequal weights [at these unequal distances may] weigh equally. For a weight suspended at a greater distance is heavier, as is obvious in a [balance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steelyard_balance "Steelyard balance"). Therefore, there exists a certain third kind of power or third difference of magnitude—one that differs from both body and weight—and this they call **moment**.[[c]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_(physics)#cite_note-14) Therefore, a body acquires weight from both quantity [i.e., size] and quality [i.e., material], but a weight receives its moment from the distance at which it is suspended. Therefore, when distances are reciprocally proportional to weights, the moments [of the weights] are equal, as [Archimedes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archimedes "Archimedes") demonstrated in _[The Book on Equal Moments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/On\_the\_Equilibrium\_of\_Planes "On the Equilibrium of Planes")_.[[d]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_(physics)#cite_note-15) Therefore, weights or [rather] moments like other continuous quantities, are joined at some common terminus, that is, at something common to both of them like the center of weight, or at a point of equilibrium. Now the [center of gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Center_of_gravity "Center of gravity") in any weight is that point which, no matter how often or whenever the body is suspended, always inclines perpendicularly toward the universal center.
> 
> 
> In addition to body, weight, and moment, there is a certain fourth power, which can be called impetus or force.[[e]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_(physics)#cite_note-16)[Aristotle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotle "Aristotle") investigates it in _On Mechanical Questions_, and it is completely different from [the] three aforesaid [powers or magnitudes]. [...]"

in 1586, [Simon Stevin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simon_Stevin "Simon Stevin") uses the [Dutch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dutch_language "Dutch language") term _staltwicht_ ("parked weight") for momentum in _[De Beghinselen Der Weeghconst](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De\_Beghinselen\_Der\_Weeghconst "De Beghinselen Der Weeghconst")_.

In 1632, [Galileo Galilei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galileo_Galilei "Galileo Galilei") publishes _[Dialogue Concerning the Two Chief World Systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dialogue\_Concerning\_the\_Two\_Chief\_World\_Systems "Dialogue Concerning the Two Chief World Systems")_ and uses the [Italian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italian_language "Italian language")_momento_ with many meanings, including the one of his predecessors.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_(physics)#cite_note-17)

In 1643, Thomas Salusbury translates some of Galilei's works into [English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English_language "English language"). Salusbury translates Latin _momentum_ and Italian _momento_ into the English term _moment_.[[f]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_(physics)#cite_note-18)

In 1765, the [Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin "Latin") term _momentum inertiae_ ([English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English_language "English language"): _[moment of inertia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment\_of\_inertia "Moment of inertia")_) is used by [Leonhard Euler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leonhard_Euler "Leonhard Euler") to refer to one of [Christiaan Huygens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens "Christiaan Huygens")'s quantities in _[Horologium Oscillatorium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horologium\_Oscillatorium "Horologium Oscillatorium")_.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_(physics)#cite_note-euler-19) Huygens 1673 work involving finding the [center of oscillation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Center_of_oscillation "Center of oscillation") had been stimulated by [Marin Mersenne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marin_Mersenne "Marin Mersenne"), who suggested it to him in 1646.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_(physics)#cite_note-20)[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_(physics)#cite_note-21)

In 1811, the French term _moment d'une force_ ([English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English_language "English language"): _moment of a force_) with respect to a point and plane is used by [Siméon Denis Poisson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sim%C3%A9on_Denis_Poisson "Siméon Denis Poisson") in _Traité de mécanique_.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_(physics)#cite_note-22)[An English translation](https://babel.hathitrust.org/cgi/pt?id=mdp.39015067064249) appears in 1842.

In 1884, the term _[torque](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torque "Torque")_ is suggested by [James Thomson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Thomson_(engineer) "James Thomson (engineer)") in the context of measuring rotational forces of [machines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Machine "Machine") (with [propellers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propeller "Propeller") and [rotors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotor_(electric) "Rotor (electric)")).[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_(physics)#cite_note-23)[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_(physics)#cite_note-24) Today, a [dynamometer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynamometer "Dynamometer") is used to measure the torque of machines.

In 1893, [Karl Pearson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karl_Pearson "Karl Pearson") uses the term _n-th moment_ and ![Image 19: {\displaystyle \mu _{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/267d03f9351dcc8d3d3ac7cad59ea3ba4fecbfef) in the context of [curve-fitting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curve_fitting "Curve fitting") scientific measurements.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_(physics)#cite_note-25) Pearson wrote in response to [John Venn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Venn "John Venn"), who, some years earlier, observed a peculiar pattern involving [meteorological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteorology "Meteorology") data and asked for an explanation of its cause.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_(physics)#cite_note-26) In Pearson's response, this analogy is used: the mechanical "center of gravity" is the [mean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mean "Mean") and the "distance" is the [deviation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deviation_(statistics) "Deviation (statistics)") from the mean. This later evolved into [moments in mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_(mathematics) "Moment (mathematics)"). The analogy between the mechanical concept of a moment and the [statistical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistics "Statistics") function involving the sum of the n th powers of deviations was noticed by several earlier, including [Laplace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre-Simon_Laplace "Pierre-Simon Laplace"), [Kramp](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christian_Kramp "Christian Kramp"), [Gauss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Friedrich_Gauss "Carl Friedrich Gauss"), [Encke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johann_Franz_Encke "Johann Franz Encke"), [Czuber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emanuel_Czuber "Emanuel Czuber"), [Quetelet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adolphe_Quetelet "Adolphe Quetelet"), and [De Forest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erastus_L._De_Forest "Erastus L. De Forest").[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_(physics)#cite_note-27)
