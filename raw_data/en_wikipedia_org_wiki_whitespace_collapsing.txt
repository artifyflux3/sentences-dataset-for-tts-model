A **whitespace character** is a [character](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Character_(computing) "Character (computing)")[data element](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_element "Data element") that represents [white space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_space_(visual_arts) "White space (visual arts)") when [text](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Text_string "Text string") is [rendered](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rendering_(computer_graphics) "Rendering (computer graphics)") for display by a [computer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer "Computer").

For example, a _[space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space\_(punctuation) "Space (punctuation)")_ character (U+0020 SPACE, [ASCII](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ASCII "ASCII") 32) represents blank space such as a [word divider](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Word_divider "Word divider") in a Western [script](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Writing_system "Writing system").

A [printable character](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Printable_character "Printable character") results in output when rendered, but a whitespace character does not. Instead, whitespace characters define the layout of text to a limited degree, interrupting the normal sequence of rendering characters next to each other. The output of subsequent characters is typically shifted to the right (or to the left for [right-to-left script](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right-to-left_script "Right-to-left script")) or to the start of the next line. The effect of multiple sequential whitespace characters is cumulative such that the next printable character is rendered at a location based on the accumulated effect of preceding whitespace characters.

The origin of the term _whitespace_ is rooted in the common practice of rendering text on white [paper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paper "Paper"). Normally, a whitespace character is _not_ rendered as white. It affects rendering, but it is not itself rendered.

[![Image 1](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Punctuation-Spaces.svg/250px-Punctuation-Spaces.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Punctuation-Spaces.svg)

Width of various [Unicode](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unicode "Unicode") space characters

A space character typically inserts horizontal space that is about as wide as a letter. For a [monospaced font](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monospaced_font "Monospaced font") the width is the width of a letter, and for a [variable-width font](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variable-width_font "Variable-width font") the width is font-specific. Some fonts support multiple space characters that have different widths.

A tab character typically inserts horizontal space that is based on [tab stops](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tab_stop "Tab stop") which vary by [application](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Application_software "Application software").

A [newline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newline "Newline") character sequence typically moves the render output location to the beginning of the next line. If one follows text, it does not actually result in whitespace. But, two sequential newline sequences between text blocks results in a blank line between the blocks. The height of the blank line varies by application.

Using whitespace characters to layout text is a convention. Applications sometimes render whitespace characters as visible markup so that a user can see what is normally not visible.

Typically, a [user](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_user "Computer user") types a space character by pressing spacebar, a tab character by pressing Tab ↹ and newline by pressing ↵ Enter.

The table below lists the twenty-five characters defined as whitespace ("WSpace=Y", "WS") characters in the [Unicode](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unicode "Unicode") Character Database.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whitespace_collapsing#cite_note-1) Seventeen use a definition of whitespace consistent with the algorithm for [bidirectional writing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unicode_character_property#Bidirectional_writing "Unicode character property") ("Bidirectional Character Type=WS") and are known as "Bidi-WS" characters. The remaining characters may also be used, but are not of this "Bidi" type.

Whitespace and digital typography
---------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Whitespace_character&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Whitespace and digital typography")]

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Traditional_spacing_examples_from_the_1911_Chicago_Manual_of_Style.png/250px-Traditional_spacing_examples_from_the_1911_Chicago_Manual_of_Style.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Traditional_spacing_examples_from_the_1911_Chicago_Manual_of_Style.png)

[The Chicago Manual of Style](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Chicago_Manual_of_Style "The Chicago Manual of Style") has rules for using the different sized whitespaces.

[Text editors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Text_editor "Text editor"), [word processors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Word_processor "Word processor"), and [desktop publishing software](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Desktop_publishing_software "Desktop publishing software") differ in how they represent whitespace on the screen, and how they represent spaces at the ends of lines longer than the screen or column width. In some cases, spaces are shown simply as blank space; in other cases they may be represented by an [interpunct](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interpunct "Interpunct") or other symbols. Many different characters (described below) could be used to produce spaces, and non-character functions (such as margins and tab settings) can also affect whitespace.

Many of the Unicode space characters were created for compatibility with classic print typography.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whitespace_collapsing#cite_note-31)

Even if digital typography has algorithmic kerning and justification, those space characters can be used to supplement the electronic formatting when needed.

### Variable-width general-purpose space

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Whitespace_character&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Variable-width general-purpose space")]

In computer [character encodings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Character_encoding "Character encoding"), there is a normal **general-purpose space** (Unicode character U+0020) whose width will vary according to the design of the typeface. Typical values range from 1/5 em to 1/3 em (in digital typography an [em](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Em_(typography) "Em (typography)") is equal to the nominal size of the font, so for a 10-point font the space will probably be between 2 and 3.3 points). Sophisticated fonts may have differently sized spaces for bold, italic, and small-caps faces, and often compositors will manually adjust the width of the space depending on the size and prominence of the text.

In addition to this general-purpose space, it is possible to encode a space of a specific width. See the table [above](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whitespace_collapsing#Unicode) for a complete list.

### Hair spaces around dashes

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Whitespace_character&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Hair spaces around dashes")]

[Em dashes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Em_dash "Em dash") used as parenthetical dividers, and [en dashes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/En_dash "En dash") when used as word joiners, are usually set continuous with the text.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whitespace_collapsing#cite_note-32) However, such a dash can optionally be surrounded with a **hair space**, U+200A, or **[thin space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thin_space "Thin space")**, U+2009. The hair space can be written in HTML by using the [numeric character references](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Numeric_character_reference "Numeric character reference")`&#x200A;` or `&#8202;`, or the named entity `&hairsp;`. The [thin space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thin_space "Thin space") is named entity `&thinsp;` and numeric references `&#x2009;` or `&#8201;`. These spaces are much thinner than a normal space (except in a [monospaced (non-proportional) font](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monospaced_font "Monospaced font")), with the hair space in particular being the thinnest of horizontal whitespace characters.

Computing applications
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Whitespace_character&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Computing applications")]

### Programming languages

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Whitespace_character&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Programming languages")]

In most [programming language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Programming_language "Programming language")[syntax](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syntax "Syntax"), whitespace characters can be used to separate [tokens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Token_(parser) "Token (parser)"). For a [free-form language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free-form_language "Free-form language"), whitespace characters are ignored by code processors (i.e. [compiler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compiler "Compiler")). Even when language syntax requires white space, often multiple whitespace characters are treated the same as a single. In an [off-side rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Off-side_rule "Off-side rule") language, [indentation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indentation "Indentation") white space is syntactically significant. In the [satirical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satirical "Satirical") and contrarian language called [Whitespace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whitespace_(programming_language) "Whitespace (programming language)"), whitespace characters are the only significant characters and normal text is ignored.

Good use of white space in [source code](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Source_code "Source code") can group related logic and make the code easier to understand. Excessive use of whitespace, including at the end of a line where it provides no rendering behavior, is considered a nuisance.

Most languages only recognize whitespace characters that have an ASCII code. They disallow most or all of the Unicode codes listed above. The [C language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C_language "C language") defines whitespace characters to be "space, horizontal tab, new-line, vertical tab, and form-feed".[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whitespace_collapsing#cite_note-33) The [HTTP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypertext_Transfer_Protocol "Hypertext Transfer Protocol") network protocol requires different types of whitespace to be used in different parts of the protocol, such as: only the space character in the [status](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HTTP_status "HTTP status") line, CRLF at the end of a line, and "linear whitespace" in header values.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whitespace_collapsing#cite_note-34)

### Command-line parsing

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Whitespace_character&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Command-line parsing")]

Typical [command-line parsers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Command_processor "Command processor") use the space character to [delimit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Delimiter "Delimiter")[arguments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parameter_(computer_programming) "Parameter (computer programming)"). A value with an embedded space character is problematic since it causes the value to parse as multiple arguments. Typically, a parser allows for escaping the normal argument parsing by enclosing the text in quotes.

Consider that one wants to list the files in directory named "foo bar". This command instead lists the files matching either "foo" or "bar":

ls foo bar

This command correctly specifies a single argument:

ls "foo bar"

Some [markup languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Markup_language "Markup language"), such as [SGML](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SGML "SGML"), preserve whitespace as written.

Web markup languages such as [XML](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/XML "XML") and [HTML](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HTML "HTML") treat whitespace characters specially, including space characters, for programmers' convenience. One or more space characters read by conforming display-time processors of those markup languages are collapsed to 0 or 1 space, depending on their semantic context. For example, double (or more) spaces within text are collapsed to a single space, and spaces which appear on either side of the "`=`" that separates an attribute name from its value have no effect on the interpretation of the document. Element end tags can contain trailing spaces, and empty-element tags in XML can contain spaces before the "`/>`". In these languages, unnecessary whitespace increases the file size, and so may slow network transfers. On the other hand, unnecessary whitespace can also inconspicuously mark code, similar to, but less obvious than comments in code. This can be desirable to prove an [infringement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Copyright_infringement "Copyright infringement") of license or copyright that was committed by [copying and pasting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Copying_and_pasting "Copying and pasting").

#### Preserving whitespace

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Whitespace_character&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Preserving whitespace")]

In XML attribute values, sequences of whitespace characters are treated as a single space when the document is read by a parser.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whitespace_collapsing#cite_note-35) Whitespace in XML element content is not changed in this way by the parser, but an application receiving information from the parser may choose to apply similar rules to element content. An XML document author can use the `xml:space="preserve"` attribute on an element to instruct the parser to discourage the downstream application from altering whitespace in that element's content.

In most [HTML elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HTML_element "HTML element"), a sequence of whitespace characters is treated as a single _inter-word separator_, which may manifest as a single space character when rendering text in a language that normally inserts such space between words.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whitespace_collapsing#cite_note-36) Conforming HTML renderers apply literal whitespace behaviour to certain elements: those inside `<pre>...</pre>` tags, and those where [CSS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cascading_Style_Sheets "Cascading Style Sheets") property `white-space` is set to pre or pre-wrap. In these elements, space characters will not be "collapsed" into inter-word separators.

In [MediaWiki](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MediaWiki "MediaWiki") markup, as well as the `<pre>...</pre>` there is an optional `<poem>...</poem>` tag, which also preserves whitespace. It requires Extension:Poem.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whitespace_collapsing#cite_note-37)

In both XML and HTML, the [non-breaking space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-breaking_space "Non-breaking space") character, along with other "non-standard" spaces, is not treated as collapsible whitespace.

Such usage is similar to multiword file names written for operating systems and applications that are confused by embedded space codes—such file names instead use an [underscore](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Underscore "Underscore") (_) as a word separator, as_in_this_phrase.

Another such symbol was U+2422␢BLANK SYMBOL. This was used in the early years of computer programming when writing on coding forms. [Keypunch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Keypunch "Keypunch") operators immediately recognized the symbol as an "explicit space".[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whitespace_collapsing#cite_note-Mackenzie_1980-19) It was used in [BCDIC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BCDIC "BCDIC"),[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whitespace_collapsing#cite_note-Mackenzie_1980-19)[EBCDIC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/EBCDIC "EBCDIC"),[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whitespace_collapsing#cite_note-Mackenzie_1980-19) and [ASCII-1963](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ASCII-1963 "ASCII-1963").[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whitespace_collapsing#cite_note-Mackenzie_1980-19)

The fixed-width space characters (U+2000..U+200A) are derived from conventional (hot lead) typography. Algorithmic kerning and justification in computerized typography do not use these characters. However, where they are used (for example, in typesetting mathematical formulae), their width is generally font-specified, and they typically do not expand during justification. The exception is U+2009 thin space, which sometimes gets adjusted.
