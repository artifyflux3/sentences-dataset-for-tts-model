In [mathematical analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_analysis "Mathematical analysis"), a **domain** or **region** is a [non-empty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empty_set "Empty set"), [connected](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connected_(topology) "Connected (topology)"), and [open set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_set "Open set") in a [topological space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_space "Topological space"). In particular, it is any non-empty connected open [subset](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subset "Subset") of the [real coordinate space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_coordinate_space "Real coordinate space")**R**_n_ or the [complex coordinate space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_coordinate_space "Complex coordinate space")**C**_n_. A connected open subset of [coordinate space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_space "Coordinate space") is frequently used for the [domain of a function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_of_a_function "Domain of a function").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_(mathematical_analysis)#cite_note-1)

The basic idea of a connected subset of a space dates from the 19th century, but precise definitions vary slightly from generation to generation, author to author, and edition to edition, as concepts developed and terms were translated between German, French, and English works. In English, some authors use the term _domain_,[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_(mathematical_analysis)#cite_note-2) some use the term _region_,[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_(mathematical_analysis)#cite_note-3) some use both terms interchangeably,[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_(mathematical_analysis)#cite_note-4) and some define the two terms slightly differently;[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_(mathematical_analysis)#cite_note-5) some avoid ambiguity by sticking with a phrase such as _non-empty connected open subset_.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_(mathematical_analysis)#cite_note-6)

One common convention is to define a _domain_ as a connected open set but a _region_ as the [union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Union_(set_theory) "Union (set theory)") of a domain with none, some, or all of its [limit points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limit_point "Limit point").[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_(mathematical_analysis)#cite_note-7) A **closed region** or **closed domain** is the union of a domain and all of its limit points.

Various degrees of smoothness of the [boundary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boundary_(mathematics) "Boundary (mathematics)") of the domain are required for various properties of functions defined on the domain to hold, such as integral theorems ([Green's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green%27s_theorem "Green's theorem"), [Stokes theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stokes_theorem "Stokes theorem")), properties of [Sobolev spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sobolev_space "Sobolev space"), and to define [measures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Measure_(mathematics) "Measure (mathematics)") on the boundary and spaces of [traces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trace_operator "Trace operator") (generalized functions defined on the boundary). Commonly considered types of domains are domains with [continuous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_function "Continuous function") boundary, [Lipschitz boundary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lipschitz_domain "Lipschitz domain"), [_C_ 1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smooth_function "Smooth function") boundary, and so forth.

A **bounded domain** is a domain that is [bounded](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bounded_set "Bounded set"), i.e., contained in some ball. **Bounded region** is defined similarly. An **exterior domain** or **external domain** is a domain whose [complement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complement_(set_theory) "Complement (set theory)") is bounded; sometimes smoothness conditions are imposed on its boundary.

In [complex analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_analysis "Complex analysis"), a **complex domain** (or simply **domain**) is any connected open subset of the [complex plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_plane "Complex plane")**C**. For example, the entire complex plane is a domain, as is the open [unit disk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_disk "Unit disk"), the open [upper half-plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Upper_half-plane "Upper half-plane"), and so forth. Often, a complex domain serves as the [domain of definition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_of_a_function "Domain of a function") for a [holomorphic function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holomorphic_function "Holomorphic function"). In the study of [several complex variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_of_several_complex_variables "Function of several complex variables"), the definition of a domain is extended to include any connected open subset of **C**_n_.

In [Euclidean spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space"), [one-](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One-dimensional "One-dimensional"), [two-](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two-dimensional "Two-dimensional"), and [three-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional "Three-dimensional") regions are [curves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curve "Curve"), [surfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_(mathematics) "Surface (mathematics)"), and [solids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_(mathematics) "Solid (mathematics)"), whose extent are called, respectively, _[length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arc\_length "Arc length")_, _[area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area "Area")_, and _[volume](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume "Volume")_.

> **Definition**. An open set is connected if it cannot be expressed as the sum of two open sets. An open connected set is called a domain. [German](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_language "German language"): _Eine offene Punktmenge heißt zusammenhängend, wenn man sie nicht als Summe von zwei offenen Punktmengen darstellen kann. Eine offene zusammenhängende Punktmenge heißt ein Gebiet._

According to [Hans Hahn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hans_Hahn_(mathematician) "Hans Hahn (mathematician)"),[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_(mathematical_analysis)#cite_note-8) the concept of a domain as an open connected set was introduced by [Constantin Carathéodory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constantin_Carath%C3%A9odory "Constantin Carathéodory") in his famous book ([Carathéodory 1918](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_(mathematical_analysis)#CITEREFCarath%C3%A9odory1918)). In this definition, Carathéodory considers obviously [non-empty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empty_set "Empty set")[disjoint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disjoint_sets "Disjoint sets") sets. Hahn also remarks that the word "_Gebiet_" ("_Domain_") was occasionally previously used as a [synonym](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synonym "Synonym") of [open set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_set "Open set").[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_(mathematical_analysis)#cite_note-9) The rough concept is older. In the 19th and early 20th century, the terms _domain_ and _region_ were often used informally (sometimes interchangeably) without explicit definition.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_(mathematical_analysis)#cite_note-10)

However, the term "domain" was occasionally used to identify closely related but slightly different concepts. For example, in his influential [monographs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monograph "Monograph") on [elliptic partial differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptic_partial_differential_equation "Elliptic partial differential equation"), [Carlo Miranda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carlo_Miranda "Carlo Miranda") uses the term "region" to identify an open connected set,[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_(mathematical_analysis)#cite_note-M&Mp1-11)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_(mathematical_analysis)#cite_note-12) and reserves the term "domain" to identify an internally connected,[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_(mathematical_analysis)#cite_note-13)[perfect set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perfect_set "Perfect set"), each point of which is an accumulation point of interior points,[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_(mathematical_analysis)#cite_note-M&Mp1-11) following his former master [Mauro Picone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mauro_Picone "Mauro Picone"):[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_(mathematical_analysis)#cite_note-14) according to this convention, if a set _A_ is a region then its [closure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closure_(topology) "Closure (topology)")_A_ is a domain.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_(mathematical_analysis)#cite_note-M&Mp1-11)

10.   **[^](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_(mathematical_analysis)#cite_ref-10 "Jump up")**For example ([Forsyth 1893](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_(mathematical_analysis)#CITEREFForsyth1893)) uses the term _region_ informally throughout (e.g. [§16, p. 21](https://archive.org/details/theoryoffunction00fors/page/21/)) alongside the informal expression _part of the z-plane_, and defines the _domain_ of a point a for a function f to be the largest [r-neighborhood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neighbourhood_(mathematics)#In_a_metric_space "Neighbourhood (mathematics)") of a in which f is [holomorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holomorphic_function "Holomorphic function") ([§32, p. 52](https://archive.org/details/theoryoffunction00fors/page/52/)). The first edition of the [influential textbook](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A_Course_of_Modern_Analysis "A Course of Modern Analysis") ([Whittaker 1902](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_(mathematical_analysis)#CITEREFWhittaker1902)) uses the terms _domain_ and _region_ informally and apparently interchangeably. By the second edition ([Whittaker & Watson 1915](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_(mathematical_analysis)#CITEREFWhittakerWatson1915), [§3.21, p. 44](https://archive.org/details/courseofmodernan00whituoft/page/44/)) define an _open region_ to be the [interior of a simple closed curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jordan_curve_theorem "Jordan curve theorem"), and a _closed region_ or _domain_ to be the open region along with its boundary curve. ([Goursat 1905](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_(mathematical_analysis)#CITEREFGoursat1905), [§262, p. 10](https://archive.org/details/courseinmathemat02gouruoft/page/10/)) defines _région_ [region] or _aire_ [area] as a connected portion of the plane. ([Townsend 1915](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_(mathematical_analysis)#CITEREFTownsend1915), [§10, p. 20](https://archive.org/details/functionsofcompl00towniala/page/20/)) defines a _region_ or _domain_ to be a connected portion of the complex plane consisting only of inner points.
