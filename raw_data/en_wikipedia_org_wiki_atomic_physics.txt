**Atomic physics** is the field of [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics") that studies [atoms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atom "Atom") as an isolated system of [electrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron "Electron") and an [atomic nucleus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_nucleus "Atomic nucleus"). Atomic physics typically refers to the study of atomic structure and the interaction between atoms.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_physics#cite_note-1) It is primarily concerned with [the way in which electrons are arranged around the nucleus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_configuration "Electron configuration") and the processes by which these arrangements change. This comprises [ions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ion "Ion"), neutral atoms and, unless otherwise stated, it can be assumed that the term _atom_ includes ions.

The term _atomic physics_ can be associated with [nuclear power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power "Nuclear power") and [nuclear weapons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_weapon "Nuclear weapon"), due to the [synonymous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synonym "Synonym") use of _atomic_ and _nuclear_ in [standard English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_English "Standard English"). Physicists distinguish between atomic physics—which deals with the atom as a system consisting of a nucleus and electrons—and [nuclear physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_physics "Nuclear physics"), which studies [nuclear reactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_reaction "Nuclear reaction") and special properties of atomic nuclei.

As with many scientific fields, strict delineation can be highly contrived and atomic physics is often considered in the wider context of [atomic, molecular, and optical physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic,_molecular,_and_optical_physics "Atomic, molecular, and optical physics"). Physics research groups are usually so classified.

Isolated atoms
--------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Atomic_physics&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Isolated atoms")]

Atomic physics primarily considers atoms in isolation. Atomic models will consist of a single nucleus that may be surrounded by one or more bound electrons. It is not concerned with the formation of [molecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecule "Molecule") (although much of the physics is identical), nor does it examine atoms in a [solid state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid-state_physics "Solid-state physics") as [condensed matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter "Condensed matter"). It is concerned with processes such as [ionization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ionization "Ionization") and [excitation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Excited_state "Excited state") by photons or collisions with atomic particles.

While modelling atoms in isolation may not seem realistic, if one considers atoms in a [gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas "Gas") or [plasma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasma_(physics) "Plasma (physics)") then the time-scales for atom-atom interactions are huge in comparison to the atomic processes that are generally considered. This means that the individual atoms can be treated as if each were in isolation, as the vast majority of the time they are. By this consideration, atomic physics provides the underlying theory in plasma physics and [atmospheric physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmospheric_physics "Atmospheric physics"), even though both deal with very large numbers of atoms.

Electronic configuration
------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Atomic_physics&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Electronic configuration")]

Electrons form notional [shells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_shells "Electron shells") around the nucleus. These are normally in a [ground state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ground_state "Ground state") but can be excited by the absorption of energy from light ([photons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon "Photon")), [magnetic fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_field "Magnetic field"), or interaction with a colliding particle (typically ions or other electrons).

[![Image 19](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Bohr_atom_model.svg/250px-Bohr_atom_model.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Bohr_atom_model.svg)

In the Bohr model, the transition of an electron with n=3 to the shell n=2 is shown, where a photon is emitted. An electron from shell (n=2) must have been removed beforehand by ionization

Electrons that populate a shell are said to be in a [bound state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bound_state "Bound state"). The energy necessary to remove an electron from its shell (taking it to infinity) is called the [binding energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binding_energy "Binding energy"). Any quantity of energy absorbed by the electron in excess of this amount is converted to [kinetic energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_energy "Kinetic energy") according to the [conservation of energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservation_of_energy "Conservation of energy"). The atom is said to have undergone the process of ionization.

If the electron absorbs a quantity of energy less than the binding energy, it will be transferred to an excited state. After a certain time, the electron in an excited state will "jump" (undergo a transition) to a lower state. In a neutral atom, the system will emit a photon of the difference in energy, since energy is conserved.

If an inner electron has absorbed more than the binding energy (so that the atom ionizes), then a more outer electron may undergo a transition to fill the inner orbital. In this case, a visible photon or a [characteristic X-ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Characteristic_x-ray "Characteristic x-ray") is emitted, or a phenomenon known as the [Auger effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Auger_effect "Auger effect") may take place, where the released energy is transferred to another bound electron, causing it to go into the continuum. The Auger effect allows one to multiply ionize an atom with a single photon.

There are rather strict [selection rules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Selection_rule "Selection rule") as to the electronic configurations that can be reached by excitation by light — however, there are no such rules for excitation by collision processes.

### Bohr model of the atom

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Atomic_physics&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Bohr model of the atom")]

The Bohr model, proposed by **Niels Bohr in 1913**, is a revolutionary theory describing the structure of the hydrogen atom. It introduced the idea of quantized orbits for electrons, combining classical and quantum physics.

Key Postulates of the Bohr Model
1.   Electrons Move in Circular Orbits 
    *   Electrons revolve around the nucleus in fixed, circular paths called **orbits** or **energy levels**.
    *   These orbits are **stable** and do not radiate energy.

2.   **Quantization of Angular Momentum:**
    *   The angular momentum of an electron is quantized and given by: L=m e v r=n ℏ,n=1,2,3,…{\displaystyle \ L=m_{\text{e}}vr=n\hbar ,\quad n=1,2,3,\ldots }![Image 20: {\displaystyle \ L=m_{\text{e}}vr=n\hbar ,\quad n=1,2,3,\ldots }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5aa7fa9135791b1dd3564cc1dc4107eb581dfa23) where: m e{\displaystyle m_{\text{e}}}![Image 21: {\displaystyle m_{\text{e}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c45a5eb082ea4dafd3cb43ca39e033989e4a52eb): electron mass v{\displaystyle v}![Image 22: {\displaystyle v}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e07b00e7fc0847fbd16391c778d65bc25c452597): velocity of the electron r{\displaystyle r}![Image 23: {\displaystyle r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d1ecb613aa2984f0576f70f86650b7c2a132538): radius of the orbit ℏ{\displaystyle \hbar }![Image 24: {\displaystyle \hbar }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/de68de3a92517953436c93b5a76461d49160cc41): reduced Planck constant (ℏ=h/2 π{\displaystyle \hbar ={h}/{2\pi }}![Image 25: {\displaystyle \hbar ={h}/{2\pi }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b0708c18b4e13fd8e8399ee3d0d6378e7e3ec487))n{\displaystyle n}![Image 26: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b): principal quantum number, representing the orbit

3.   Energy Levels 
    *   Each orbit has a specific energy. The total energy of an electron in the n{\displaystyle n}![Image 27: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)th orbit is: E n=−13.6 e V n 2,{\displaystyle \ E_{n}=-{\frac {\mathrm {13.6~eV} }{n^{2}}},}![Image 28: {\displaystyle \ E_{n}=-{\frac {\mathrm {13.6~eV} }{n^{2}}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b404d36fc47f4c5052cfa47a288b963c832e71a9) where 13.6 e V{\displaystyle \mathrm {13.6~eV} }![Image 29: {\displaystyle \mathrm {13.6~eV} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/86f5b6b0b5e781c43e249999122ac40e10845e4d)is the ground-state energy of the hydrogen atom.

History and developments
------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Atomic_physics&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: History and developments")]

Main article: [Atomic theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_theory "Atomic theory")

One of the earliest steps towards atomic physics was the recognition that matter was composed of _atoms_. It forms a part of the texts written in 6th century BC to 2nd century BC, such as those of [Democritus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democritus "Democritus") or _\_[Vaiśeṣika Sūtra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vai%C5%9Be%E1%B9%A3ika\\_S%C5%ABtra "Vaiśeṣika Sūtra")\__ written by [Kaṇāda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ka%E1%B9%87%C4%81da_(philosopher) "Kaṇāda (philosopher)").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_physics#cite_note-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_physics#cite_note-3) This theory was later developed in the modern sense of the basic unit of a [chemical element](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_element "Chemical element") by the British chemist and physicist [John Dalton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Dalton "John Dalton") in the 18th century.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_physics#cite_note-4) At this stage, it was not clear what atoms were, although they could be described and classified by their properties (in bulk). The invention of the [periodic system of elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Periodic_system_of_elements "Periodic system of elements") by [Dmitri Mendeleev](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dmitri_Mendeleev "Dmitri Mendeleev") was another great step forward.

The true beginning of atomic physics is marked by the discovery of [spectral lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectral_line "Spectral line") and attempts to describe the phenomenon, most notably by [Joseph von Fraunhofer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_von_Fraunhofer "Joseph von Fraunhofer").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_physics#cite_note-5) The study of these lines led to the [Bohr atom model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bohr_atom_model "Bohr atom model") and to the birth of [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics"). In seeking to explain atomic spectra, an entirely new mathematical model of matter was revealed. As far as atoms and their electron shells were concerned, not only did this yield a better overall description, i.e. the [atomic orbital model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_orbital_model "Atomic orbital model"), but it also provided a new theoretical basis for [chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemistry "Chemistry") ([quantum chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_chemistry "Quantum chemistry")) and [spectroscopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopy "Spectroscopy").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_physics#cite_note-6)

Since the [Second World War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II "World War II"), both theoretical and experimental fields have advanced at a rapid pace. This can be attributed to progress in computing technology, which has allowed larger and more sophisticated models of atomic structure and associated collision processes.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_physics#cite_note-7)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_physics#cite_note-8) Similar technological advances in [accelerators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerator_physics "Accelerator physics"), detectors, magnetic field generation and [lasers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laser "Laser") have greatly assisted experimental work.

Beyond the well-known phenomena which can be describe with regular quantum mechanics [chaotic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chaos_theory "Chaos theory") processes[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_physics#cite_note-9) can occur which need different descriptions.
