The **Hurst exponent** is used as a measure of [long-term memory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Long-range_dependency "Long-range dependency") of [time series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_series "Time series"). It relates to the [autocorrelations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autocorrelation "Autocorrelation") of the time series, and the rate at which these decrease as the lag between pairs of values increases. Studies involving the Hurst exponent were originally developed in [hydrology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrology "Hydrology") for the practical matter of determining optimum dam sizing for the [Nile river](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nile_river "Nile river")'s volatile rain and drought conditions that had been observed over a long period of time.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hurst_exponent#cite_note-:0-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hurst_exponent#cite_note-2) The name "Hurst exponent", or "Hurst coefficient", derives from [Harold Edwin Hurst](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harold_Edwin_Hurst "Harold Edwin Hurst") (1880–1978), who was the lead researcher in these studies; the use of the standard notation _H_ for the coefficient also relates to his name.

In [fractal geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_geometry "Fractal geometry"), the **generalized Hurst exponent** has been denoted by [_H_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/H_(disambiguation) "H (disambiguation)") or _H q_ in honor of both Harold Edwin Hurst and [Ludwig Otto Hölder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Otto_Ludwig_Holder "Otto Ludwig Holder") (1859–1937) by [Benoît Mandelbrot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beno%C3%AEt_Mandelbrot "Benoît Mandelbrot") (1924–2010).[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hurst_exponent#cite_note-:1-3)_H_ is directly related to [fractal dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension "Fractal dimension"), _D_, and is a measure of a data series' "mild" or "wild" randomness.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hurst_exponent#cite_note-4)

The Hurst exponent is referred to as the "index of dependence" or "index of long-range dependence". It quantifies the relative tendency of a time series either to regress strongly to the mean or to cluster in a direction.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hurst_exponent#cite_note-5) A value _H_ in the range 0.5–1 indicates a time series with long-term positive autocorrelation, meaning that the decay in autocorrelation is slower than exponential, following a [power law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power-law_tail "Power-law tail"); for the series it means that a high value tends to be followed by another high value and that future excursions to more high values do occur. A value in the range 0 – 0.5 indicates a time series with long-term switching between high and low values in adjacent pairs, meaning that a single high value will probably be followed by a low value and that the value after that will tend to be high, with this tendency to switch between high and low values lasting a long time into the future, also following a power law. A value of _H_=0.5 indicates [short-memory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Long-range_dependence#Short-range_dependence_versus_long-range_dependence "Long-range dependence"), with (absolute) autocorrelations decaying exponentially quickly to zero.

Relation to Fractal Dimension
-----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hurst_exponent&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Relation to Fractal Dimension")]

For self-similar time series, _H_ is directly related to [fractal dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension "Fractal dimension"), _D_, where 1 <_D_< 2, such that _D_ = 2 - _H_. The values of the Hurst exponent vary between 0 and 1, with higher values indicating a smoother trend, less volatility, and less roughness.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hurst_exponent#cite_note-8)

For more general time series or multi-dimensional process, the Hurst exponent and fractal dimension can be chosen independently, as the Hurst exponent represents structure over asymptotically longer periods, while fractal dimension represents structure over asymptotically shorter periods.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hurst_exponent#cite_note-9)

Estimating the exponent
-----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hurst_exponent&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Estimating the exponent")]

A number of estimators of long-range dependence have been proposed in the literature. The oldest and best-known is the so-called [rescaled range](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rescaled_range "Rescaled range") (R/S) analysis popularized by Mandelbrot and Wallis[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hurst_exponent#cite_note-:1-3)[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hurst_exponent#cite_note-10) and based on previous hydrological findings of Hurst.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hurst_exponent#cite_note-:0-1) Alternatives include [DFA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Detrended_fluctuation_analysis "Detrended fluctuation analysis"), [Periodogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Periodogram "Periodogram") regression,[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hurst_exponent#cite_note-11) aggregated variances,[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hurst_exponent#cite_note-12) local Whittle's estimator,[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hurst_exponent#cite_note-13) wavelet analysis,[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hurst_exponent#cite_note-14)[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hurst_exponent#cite_note-15) both in the [time domain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_domain "Time domain") and [frequency domain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frequency_domain "Frequency domain").

### Rescaled range (R/S) analysis

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hurst_exponent&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Rescaled range (R/S) analysis")]

To estimate the Hurst exponent, one must first estimate the dependence of the [rescaled range](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rescaled_range "Rescaled range") on the time span _n_ of observation.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hurst_exponent#cite_note-Feder-7) A time series of full length _N_ is divided into a number of nonoverlapping shorter time series of length _n_, where _n_ takes values _N_, _N_/2, _N_/4, ... (in the convenient case that _N_ is a power of 2). The average rescaled range is then calculated for each value of _n_.

For each such time series of length ![Image 2: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b), ![Image 3: {\displaystyle X=X_{1},X_{2},\dots ,X_{n}\,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5453b04dd982611ee2c478767473e7ead417da5d), the rescaled range is calculated as follows:[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hurst_exponent#cite_note-Qian-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hurst_exponent#cite_note-Feder-7)

1.   Calculate the [mean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mean "Mean"); ![Image 4: {\displaystyle m={\frac {1}{n}}\sum _{i=1}^{n}X_{i}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b0bb9bcaebc0e796c95bbbf5896abca30e70cfd9)
2.   Create a mean-adjusted series; ![Image 5: {\displaystyle Y_{t}=X_{t}-m\quad {\text{  for }}t=1,2,\dots ,n\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/64e44d5373996038586268c1d0a423e92e86692d)
3.   Calculate the cumulative deviate series ![Image 6: {\displaystyle Z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1cc6b75e09a8aa3f04d8584b11db534f88fb56bd); ![Image 7: {\displaystyle Z_{t}=\sum _{i=1}^{t}Y_{i}\quad {\text{   for }}t=1,2,\dots ,n\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/864197fba72d0cee5681bbd609b484c74df04343)
4.   Compute the range ![Image 8: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33); ![Image 9: {\displaystyle R(n)=\operatorname {max} \left(Z_{1},Z_{2},\dots ,Z_{n}\right)-\operatorname {min} \left(Z_{1},Z_{2},\dots ,Z_{n}\right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d806128b12073a865935531777f5ed716db453f9)
5.   Compute the [standard deviation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_deviation "Standard deviation")![Image 10: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2); ![Image 11: {\displaystyle S(n)={\sqrt {{\frac {1}{n}}\sum _{i=1}^{n}\left(X_{i}-m\right)^{2}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b31ba28dd766f521cfefaa7be26d776f5c6fb473)
6.   Calculate the rescaled range ![Image 12: {\displaystyle R(n)/S(n)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/414d81a86d4e8223ede9d86bb3b2596523031461) and average over all the partial time series of length ![Image 13: {\displaystyle n.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e59df02a9f67a5da3c220f1244c99a46cc4eb1c6)

The Hurst exponent is estimated by fitting the [power law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_law "Power law")![Image 14: {\displaystyle \mathbb {E} [R(n)/S(n)]=Cn^{H}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d65330dda2923c2ebdee79c9447fca3b3953a478) to the data. This can be done by plotting ![Image 15: {\displaystyle \log[R(n)/S(n)]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/176342767e457ccbcdb73cda46dabdf85791a72c) as a function of ![Image 16: {\displaystyle \log n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/317ab5292da7c7935aec01a570461fe0613b21d5), and fitting a straight line; the slope of the line gives ![Image 17: {\displaystyle H}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/75a9edddcca2f782014371f75dca39d7e13a9c1b). A more principled approach would be to fit the power law in a maximum-likelihood fashion.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hurst_exponent#cite_note-16) Such a graph is called a [box plot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Box_plot "Box plot"). However, this approach is known to produce biased estimates of the power-law exponent.[_[clarification needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Please\_clarify "Wikipedia:Please clarify")_] For small ![Image 18: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) there is a significant deviation from the 0.5 slope.[_[clarification needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Please\_clarify "Wikipedia:Please clarify")_] Anis and Lloyd[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hurst_exponent#cite_note-:2-17) estimated the theoretical (i.e., for white noise)[_[clarification needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Please\_clarify "Wikipedia:Please clarify")_] values of the R/S statistic to be:

where ![Image 20: {\displaystyle \Gamma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4cfde86a3f7ec967af9955d0988592f0693d2b19) is the [Euler gamma function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler_Gamma_Function "Euler Gamma Function").[_[clarification needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Please\_clarify "Wikipedia:Please clarify")_] The Anis-Lloyd corrected R/S Hurst exponent[_[clarification needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Please\_clarify "Wikipedia:Please clarify")_] is calculated as 0.5 plus the slope of ![Image 21: {\displaystyle R(n)/S(n)-\mathbb {E} [R(n)/S(n)]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6b359472554077d9c4a41143a1218d0894ec3771).

### Confidence intervals

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hurst_exponent&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Confidence intervals")]

No asymptotic distribution theory has been derived for most of the Hurst exponent estimators so far. However, Weron[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hurst_exponent#cite_note-18) used [bootstrapping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bootstrapping_(statistics) "Bootstrapping (statistics)") to obtain approximate functional forms for confidence intervals of the two most popular methods, i.e., for the Anis-Lloyd[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hurst_exponent#cite_note-:2-17) corrected R/S analysis:

| Level | Lower bound | Upper bound |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 90% | 0.5 − exp(−7.35 log(log M) + 4.06) | exp(−7.07 log(log M) + 3.75) + 0.5 |
| 95% | 0.5 − exp(−7.33 log(log M) + 4.21) | exp(−7.20 log(log M) + 4.04) + 0.5 |
| 99% | 0.5 − exp(−7.19 log(log M) + 4.34) | exp(−7.51 log(log M) + 4.58) + 0.5 |

and for [DFA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Detrended_fluctuation_analysis "Detrended fluctuation analysis"):

| Level | Lower bound | Upper bound |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 90% | 0.5 − exp(−2.99 log M + 4.45) | exp(−3.09 log M + 4.57) + 0.5 |
| 95% | 0.5 − exp(−2.93 log M + 4.45) | exp(−3.10 log M + 4.77) + 0.5 |
| 99% | 0.5 − exp(−2.67 log M + 4.06) | exp(−3.19 log M + 5.28) + 0.5 |

Here ![Image 22: {\displaystyle M=\log _{2}N}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/07b8b291f6b1f0d5e975b7d5dbd0b88c0ebd991b) and ![Image 23: {\displaystyle N}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5e3890c981ae85503089652feb48b191b57aae3) is the series length. In both cases only subseries of length ![Image 24: {\displaystyle n>50}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a7fd27e88c5493f3425fbc5fd932b6a54407f68b) were considered for estimating the Hurst exponent; subseries of smaller length lead to a high variance of the R/S estimates.

Generalized exponent
--------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hurst_exponent&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Generalized exponent")]

The basic Hurst exponent can be related to the expected size of changes, as a function of the lag between observations, as measured by E(|_X_ _t_+_τ_−_X_ _t_|2). For the generalized form of the coefficient, the exponent here is replaced by a more general term, denoted by _q_.

There are a variety of techniques that exist for estimating _H_, however assessing the accuracy of the estimation can be a complicated issue. Mathematically, in one technique, the Hurst exponent can be estimated such that:[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hurst_exponent#cite_note-19)[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hurst_exponent#cite_note-20)![Image 25: {\displaystyle H_{q}=H(q),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7b04dd6e1304b3e3f9519430c025952c79cb6bfd) for a time series ![Image 26: {\displaystyle g(t),t=1,2,\dots }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a3a249cd85c9bf93b1022f93c6d77a2704043c40) may be defined by the scaling properties of its [structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_structure "Algebraic structure") functions ![Image 27: {\displaystyle S_{q}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/66227ac26289a99fd55f640226b18e977d4bf9f8) (![Image 28: {\displaystyle \tau }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38a7dcde9730ef0853809fefc18d88771f95206c)): ![Image 29: {\displaystyle S_{q}=\left\langle \left|g(t+\tau )-g(t)\right|^{q}\right\rangle _{t}\sim \tau ^{qH(q)},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d51613aa7cfb9a1dbe1264b353346ee970a7dfaa) where ![Image 30: {\displaystyle q>0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/482e0a33d9e8fd6307b5f68a5182c2d0d14efc9c), ![Image 31: {\displaystyle \tau }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38a7dcde9730ef0853809fefc18d88771f95206c) is the time lag and averaging is over the time window ![Image 32: {\displaystyle t\gg \tau ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c5f15a03df73c9e7943e18d456a6ce6c416fd31d) usually the largest time scale of the system.

Practically, in nature, there is no limit to time, and thus _H_ is non-deterministic as it may only be estimated based on the observed data; e.g., the most dramatic daily move upwards ever seen in a stock market index can always be exceeded during some subsequent day.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hurst_exponent#cite_note-21)

In the above mathematical estimation technique, the function _H_(_q_) contains information about averaged generalized volatilities at scale ![Image 33: {\displaystyle \tau }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38a7dcde9730ef0853809fefc18d88771f95206c) (only _q_ = 1, 2 are used to define the volatility). In particular, the _H_ 1 exponent indicates persistent (_H_ 1>1⁄2) or antipersistent (_H_ 1<1⁄2) behavior of the trend.

The Hurst exponent for [white noise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_noise "White noise") is dimension dependent,[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hurst_exponent#cite_note-22) and for 1D and 2D it is ![Image 38: {\displaystyle H_{q}^{1D}=-{\frac {1}{2}},\quad H_{q}^{2D}=-1.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/130216f432ff9819b119ecd896ecff7199575137)

[Multifractal detrended fluctuation analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Multifractal_detrended_fluctuation_analysis&action=edit&redlink=1 "Multifractal detrended fluctuation analysis (page does not exist)")[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hurst_exponent#cite_note-23) is one method to estimate ![Image 43: {\displaystyle H(q)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bb7258e7274db86188844940fad02593b9ec5666) from non-stationary time series. When ![Image 44: {\displaystyle H(q)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bb7258e7274db86188844940fad02593b9ec5666) is a non-linear function of q the time series is a [multifractal system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multifractal_system "Multifractal system").

In the above definition two separate requirements are mixed together as if they would be one.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hurst_exponent#cite_note-24) Here are the two independent requirements: (i) [stationarity of the increments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stationary_increments "Stationary increments"), _x_(_t_+_T_) − _x_(_t_) = _x_(_T_) − _x_(0) in distribution. This is the condition that yields longtime autocorrelations. (ii) [Self-similarity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-similarity "Self-similarity") of the [stochastic process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stochastic_process "Stochastic process") then yields variance scaling, but is not needed for longtime memory. E.g., both [Markov processes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Markov_process "Markov process") (i.e., memory-free processes) and [fractional Brownian motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractional_Brownian_motion "Fractional Brownian motion") scale at the level of 1-point densities (simple averages), but neither scales at the level of pair correlations or, correspondingly, the 2-point probability density.[_[clarification needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Please\_clarify "Wikipedia:Please clarify")_]

An efficient market requires a [martingale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Martingale_(probability_theory) "Martingale (probability theory)") condition, and unless the variance is linear in the time this produces nonstationary increments, _x_(_t_+_T_) − _x_(_t_) ≠ _x_(_T_) − _x_(0). Martingales are Markovian at the level of pair correlations, meaning that pair correlations cannot be used to beat a martingale market. Stationary increments with nonlinear variance, on the other hand, induce the longtime pair memory of [fractional Brownian motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractional_Brownian_motion "Fractional Brownian motion") that would make the market beatable at the level of pair correlations. Such a market would necessarily be far from "efficient".

An analysis of economic time series by means of the Hurst exponent using [rescaled range](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rescaled_range "Rescaled range") and [Detrended fluctuation analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Detrended_fluctuation_analysis "Detrended fluctuation analysis") is conducted by econophysicist A.F. Bariviera.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hurst_exponent#cite_note-25) This paper studies the time varying character of [Long-range dependency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Long-range_dependency "Long-range dependency") and, thus of informational efficiency.

Hurst exponent has also been applied to the investigation of [long-range dependency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Long-range_dependency "Long-range dependency") in [DNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNA "DNA"),[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hurst_exponent#cite_note-26) and photonic [band gap](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Band_gap "Band gap") materials.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hurst_exponent#cite_note-27)

*   Matlab code for computing R/S, DFA, periodogram regression and wavelet estimates of the Hurst exponent and their corresponding confidence intervals is available from RePEc: [https://ideas.repec.org/s/wuu/hscode.html](https://ideas.repec.org/s/wuu/hscode.html)
*   Implementation of R/S in Python: [https://github.com/Mottl/hurst](https://github.com/Mottl/hurst) and of DFA and MFDFA in Python: [https://github.com/LRydin/MFDFA](https://github.com/LRydin/MFDFA)
*   Matlab code for computing real Hurst and complex Hurst: [https://www.mathworks.com/matlabcentral/fileexchange/49803-calculate-complex-hurst](https://www.mathworks.com/matlabcentral/fileexchange/49803-calculate-complex-hurst)
*   Excel sheet can also be used to do so: [https://www.researchgate.net/publication/272792633_Excel_Hurst_Calculator](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/272792633_Excel_Hurst_Calculator)
