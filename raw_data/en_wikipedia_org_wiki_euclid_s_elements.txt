The _**Elements**_ ([Ancient Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language "Ancient Greek language"): Στοιχεῖα _Stoikheîa_) is a [mathematical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics")[treatise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treatise "Treatise") written c. 300 BC by the [Ancient Greek mathematician](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek_mathematics "Ancient Greek mathematics")[Euclid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid "Euclid").

_Elements_ is the oldest extant large-scale deductive treatment of mathematics. Drawing on the works of earlier mathematicians such as [Hippocrates of Chios](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hippocrates_of_Chios "Hippocrates of Chios"), [Eudoxus of Cnidus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eudoxus_of_Cnidus "Eudoxus of Cnidus") and [Theaetetus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theaetetus_(mathematician) "Theaetetus (mathematician)"), the _Elements_ is a collection in 13 books of definitions, [postulates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postulates "Postulates"), [propositions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propositions "Propositions") and [mathematical proofs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_proof "Mathematical proof") that covers plane and solid [Euclidean geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_geometry "Euclidean geometry"), elementary [number theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_theory "Number theory"), and [incommensurability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commensurability_(mathematics) "Commensurability (mathematics)"). These include the [Pythagorean theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagorean_theorem "Pythagorean theorem"), [Thales' theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thales%27_theorem "Thales' theorem"), the [Euclidean algorithm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_algorithm "Euclidean algorithm") for [greatest common divisors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greatest_common_divisor "Greatest common divisor"), [Euclid's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid%27s_theorem "Euclid's theorem") that there are infinitely many prime numbers, and the [construction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compass-and-straightedge_construction "Compass-and-straightedge construction") of [regular polygons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polygons "Regular polygons") and [polyhedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polyhedra "Regular polyhedra").

Often referred to as the most successful [textbook](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Textbook "Textbook") ever written, the _Elements_ has continued to be used for introductory geometry from the time it was written up through the present day. It was translated into Arabic and Latin in the medieval period, where it exerted a great deal of influence on [mathematics in the medieval Islamic world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics_in_the_medieval_Islamic_world "Mathematics in the medieval Islamic world") and in Western Europe, and has proven instrumental in the development of [logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logic "Logic") and modern [science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Science "Science"), where its logical rigor was not [surpassed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_theory "Set theory") until the 19th century.

Euclid's _Elements_ is the oldest extant large-scale deductive treatment of mathematics.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGrant2002-1)[Proclus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proclus "Proclus") (412–485 AD), a Greek mathematician who lived around seven centuries after Euclid, wrote in his commentary on the _Elements_: "Euclid, who put together the _Elements_, collecting many of [Eudoxus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eudoxus_of_Cnidus "Eudoxus of Cnidus")' theorems, perfecting many of [Theaetetus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theaetetus_(mathematician) "Theaetetus (mathematician)")', and also bringing to irrefragable demonstration the things which were only somewhat loosely proved by his predecessors".[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-translation-2) Scholars believe that the _Elements_ is largely a compilation of propositions based on books by earlier Greek mathematicians,[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVan_der_Waerden1975197-3) including [Eudoxus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eudoxus_of_Cnidus "Eudoxus of Cnidus"), [Hippocrates of Chios](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hippocrates_of_Chios "Hippocrates of Chios"), [Thales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thales "Thales") and [Theaetetus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theaetetus_(mathematician) "Theaetetus (mathematician)"), while other theorems are mentioned by Plato and Aristotle.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAsper2010%C2%A7_para._6-4) It is difficult to differentiate the work of Euclid from that of his predecessors, especially because the _Elements_ essentially superseded much earlier and now-lost Greek mathematics.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTETaisbakVan_der_Waerden2021%C2%A7_%22Sources_and_contents_of_the_''Elements''%22-5) The _Elements_ version available today also includes "post-Euclidean" mathematics, probably added later by later editors such as the mathematician [Theon of Alexandria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theon_of_Alexandria "Theon of Alexandria") in the 4th century.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAsper2010%C2%A7_para._6-4) The classicist Markus Asper concludes that "apparently Euclid's achievement consists of assembling accepted mathematical knowledge into a cogent order and adding new proofs to fill in the gaps" and the historian [Serafina Cuomo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Serafina_Cuomo "Serafina Cuomo") described it as a "reservoir of results".[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTECuomo2005131-6)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAsper2010%C2%A7_para._6-4) Despite this, Sialaros furthers that "the remarkably tight structure of the _Elements_ reveals authorial control beyond the limits of a mere editor".[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTESialaros2021%C2%A7_%22Works%22-7)

[Pythagoras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras "Pythagoras") (c. 570–495 BC) was probably the source for most of books I and II, [Hippocrates of Chios](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hippocrates_of_Chios "Hippocrates of Chios") (c. 470–410 BC, not to be confused with the contemporaneous physician [Hippocrates of Kos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hippocrates "Hippocrates")) for book III, and [Eudoxus of Cnidus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eudoxus_of_Cnidus "Eudoxus of Cnidus") (c. 408–355 BC) for book V, while books IV, VI, XI, and XII probably came from other Pythagorean or Athenian mathematicians.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBall191515-8) The _Elements_ may have been based on an earlier textbook by Hippocrates of Chios, who also may have originated the use of letters to refer to figures.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBall191538-9)

Other similar works are also reported to have been written by [Theudius of Magnesia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theudius_of_Magnesia "Theudius of Magnesia"), [Leon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leon_(mathematician) "Leon (mathematician)"), and [Hermotimus of Colophon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermotimus_of_Colophon "Hermotimus of Colophon").[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-10)

The _Elements_ does not exclusively discuss geometry as is sometimes believed.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTETaisbakVan_der_Waerden2021%C2%A7_%22Sources_and_contents_of_the_''Elements''%22-5) It is traditionally divided into three topics: [plane geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_geometry "Plane geometry") (books I–VI), basic [number theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_theory "Number theory") (books VII–X) and [solid geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_geometry "Solid geometry") (books XI–XIII)—though book V (on proportions) and X (on [incommensurability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commensurability_(mathematics) "Commensurability (mathematics)")) do not exactly fit this scheme.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArtmann20123-11)[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAsper2010%C2%A7_para._4-12) The heart of the text is the [theorems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theorem "Theorem") scattered throughout.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAsper2010%C2%A7_para._2-13) Using Aristotle's terminology, these may be generally separated into two categories: "first principles" and "second principles".[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTESialaros2021%C2%A7_%22The_''Elements''%22-14) The first group includes statements labeled as a "definition" ([Ancient Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language "Ancient Greek language"): ὅρος or ὁρισμός), "postulate" (αἴτημα), or a "common notion" (κοινὴ ἔννοια).[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTESialaros2021%C2%A7_%22The_''Elements''%22-14)[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJahnke201018-15) The postulates (that is, [axioms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiom "Axiom")) and common notions occur only in book I.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTETaisbakVan_der_Waerden2021%C2%A7_%22Sources_and_contents_of_the_''Elements''%22-5) Close study of [Proclus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proclus "Proclus") suggests that older versions of the _Elements_ may have followed the same distinctions but with different terminology, instead calling the definitions hypotheses and the common notions axioms.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJahnke201018-15) The second group consists of propositions, presented alongside [mathematical proofs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_proof "Mathematical proof") and diagrams.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTESialaros2021%C2%A7_%22The_''Elements''%22-14) It is unknown if Euclid intended the _Elements_ as a textbook, but its method of presentation makes it a natural fit.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTESialaros2021%C2%A7_%22Works%22-7) As a whole, the [authorial voice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Authorial_voice "Authorial voice") remains general and impersonal.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAsper2010%C2%A7_para._6-4)

### Books I to VI: Plane geometry

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Euclid%27s_Elements&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Books I to VI: Plane geometry")]

Book I of the _Elements_ is foundational for the entire text.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTETaisbakVan_der_Waerden2021%C2%A7_%22Sources_and_contents_of_the_''Elements''%22-5) It begins with a series of 20 definitions for basic geometric concepts such as [lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_(geometry) "Line (geometry)"), [angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle "Angle") and various [regular polygons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polygon "Regular polygon").[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArtmann20123%E2%80%934-17) Euclid then presents 10 assumptions (see table, right), grouped into five postulates (axioms) and five common notions.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWolfe19454-18) These assumptions are intended to provide the logical basis for every subsequent theorem, i.e. serve as an [axiomatic system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiomatic_system "Axiomatic system").[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPickover200956-19) The common notions exclusively concern the comparison of [magnitudes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnitude_(mathematics) "Magnitude (mathematics)").[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArtmann20124-20) While postulates 1 through 4 are relatively straightforward, the 5th is known as the [parallel postulate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_postulate "Parallel postulate") and particularly famous.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArtmann20124-20)

Book I also includes 48 propositions, which can be loosely divided into: basic theorems and constructions of plane geometry and [triangle congruence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle_congruence "Triangle congruence") (1–26), [parallel lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_line "Parallel line") (27-34), the [area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area "Area") of [triangles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle "Triangle") and [parallelograms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram "Parallelogram") (35–45), and the [Pythagorean theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagorean_theorem "Pythagorean theorem") (46–48).[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArtmann20124-20)

The last of these includes the earliest surviving proof of the Pythagorean theorem, described by Sialaros as "remarkably delicate".[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTESialaros2021%C2%A7_%22The_''Elements''%22-14)

[P.Oxy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxyrhynchus_Papyri "Oxyrhynchus Papyri"). 5299 is a 3rd-century CE papyrus containing fragments of propositions 8-11 and 14-25.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-21)

The second book has a more focused scope and mostly provides algebraic theorems to accompany various geometric shapes.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTETaisbakVan_der_Waerden2021%C2%A7_%22Sources_and_contents_of_the_''Elements''%22-5)[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArtmann20124-20) It focuses on the area of [rectangles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle "Rectangle") and [squares](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square "Square") (see [Quadrature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrature_(geometry) "Quadrature (geometry)")), and leads up to a geometric precursor of the [law of cosines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law_of_cosines "Law of cosines").[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKatzMichalowicz202059-22) Book II is traditionally understood as concerning "[geometric algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_geometric_algebra "Greek geometric algebra")", though this interpretation has been heavily debated since the 1970s; critics describe the characterization as anachronistic, since the foundations of even nascent algebra occurred many centuries later.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTESialaros2021%C2%A7_%22The_''Elements''%22-14)

Book III begins with a list of 11 definitions, and follows with 37 propositions that deal with circles and their properties: finding the center (1), [chords](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chord_(geometry) "Chord (geometry)"), intersecting and [tangent circles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_circles "Tangent circles") (2-15), [tangent lines to circles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_lines_to_circles "Tangent lines to circles") (16-19), [inscribed angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inscribed_angle "Inscribed angle") (20-22), chords, arcs, and angles (23-30), angles in circles, including [Thales' theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thales%27_theorem "Thales' theorem") (31-34), and intersecting chords and tangents, including the [intersecting secants theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intersecting_secants_theorem "Intersecting secants theorem") and the [tangent-secant theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent-secant_theorem "Tangent-secant theorem") (35-39).[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArtmann201279-23)

Book IV treats four problems systematically for different polygons: Inscribing a polygon within a circle, Circumscribing a polygon about a circle, [inscribing a circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Incircle "Incircle") within a polygon, [circumscribing a circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumcircle "Circumcircle") about a polygon.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArtmann20125-24) These problems are solved in sequence for triangles, as well as [regular polygons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polygon "Regular polygon") with 4, 5, 6, and 15 sides.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTETaisbakVan_der_Waerden2021%C2%A7_%22Sources_and_contents_of_the_''Elements''%22-5)

Book V, which is independent of the previous four books, concerns proportions of [magnitudes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnitude_(mathematics) "Magnitude (mathematics)").[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArtmann20125%E2%80%936-25)

Much of Book V was probably ascertained from earlier mathematicians, perhaps Eudoxus, [[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTESialaros2021%C2%A7_%22The_''Elements''%22-14) although certain propositions, such as V.16, dealing with "alternation" (if _a_: _b_:: _c_: _d_, then _a_: _c_:: _b_: _d_) likely predate him.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArtmann20125-6-26)

Book VI utilizes the theory of proportions from Book V in the context of plane geometry,[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTETaisbakVan_der_Waerden2021%C2%A7_%22Sources_and_contents_of_the_''Elements''%22-5) especially the construction and recognition of [similar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Similarity_(geometry) "Similarity (geometry)") figures. It is built almost entirely of its first proposition:[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArtmann20126-27) "Triangles and parallelograms which are under the same height are to one another as their bases".[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHeath1908b191-28)

### Books VII to X: Number theory

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Euclid%27s_Elements&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Books VII to X: Number theory")]

[Number theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_theory "Number theory") is covered by books VII to X, the former beginning with a set of 22 definitions for [parity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parity_(mathematics) "Parity (mathematics)"), [prime numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_number "Prime number") and other arithmetic-related concepts.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTETaisbakVan_der_Waerden2021%C2%A7_%22Sources_and_contents_of_the_''Elements''%22-5)

Book VII deals with elementary number theory, and includes 39 propositions, which can be loosely divided into: [Euclidean algorithm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_algorithm "Euclidean algorithm"), a method for finding the [greatest common divisor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greatest_common_divisor "Greatest common divisor") (1-4), fractions (5-10), theory of proportions for numbers (11-19), prime and [relatively prime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relatively_prime "Relatively prime") numbers (20-32), and [Least common multiples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Least_common_multiple "Least common multiple") (33-39).

Book VIII deals with the construction and existence of [geometric sequences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_progression "Geometric progression") of integers. Propositions 1 to 10 deal with geometric progressions in general, while 11 to 27 deal with square and cube numbers.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArtmann20127-29)

Book IX applies the results of the preceding two books and gives the [infinitude of prime numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinitude_of_prime_numbers "Infinitude of prime numbers") (Euclid's theorem) and a construction for even [perfect numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perfect_number "Perfect number").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTETaisbakVan_der_Waerden2021%C2%A7_%22Sources_and_contents_of_the_''Elements''%22-5)[Alhazen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alhazen "Alhazen") conjectured c. 1000, and in the 18th century [Leonhard Euler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leonhard_Euler "Leonhard Euler") proved, that this construction generates _all_ even perfect numbers. This result is the [Euclid–Euler theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid%E2%80%93Euler_theorem "Euclid–Euler theorem").[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-30)

Of the _Elements_, book X is by far the largest and most complex, dealing with irrational numbers in the context of magnitudes.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTESialaros2021%C2%A7_%22The_''Elements''%22-14) Book X proves the irrationality of the square roots of non-square integers such as ![Image 2: {\displaystyle {\sqrt {2}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4afc1e27d418021bf10898eb44a7f5f315735ff) and classifies the square roots of [incommensurable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commensurability_(mathematics) "Commensurability (mathematics)") lines into thirteen disjoint categories. Euclid here introduces the term "irrational", which has a different meaning than the modern concept of [irrational numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irrational_number "Irrational number"). He also gives a [formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid%27s_formula "Euclid's formula") to produce [Pythagorean triples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagorean_triples "Pythagorean triples").[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJoyce1997-31)

### Books XI to XIII: Solid geometry

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Euclid%27s_Elements&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: Books XI to XIII: Solid geometry")]

[![Image 3](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Platonic_Solids_Transparent.svg/250px-Platonic_Solids_Transparent.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Platonic_Solids_Transparent.svg)

The five [Platonic solids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platonic_solids "Platonic solids"), foundational components of [solid geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_geometry "Solid geometry") which feature in Books 11–13

The final three books primarily discuss [solid geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_geometry "Solid geometry").[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArtmann20123-11) By introducing a list of 37 definitions, Book XI contextualizes the next two.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArtmann20129-32) Although its foundational character resembles Book I, unlike the latter it features no axiomatic system or postulates.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArtmann20129-32)

Book XI generalizes the results of book VI to solid figures: perpendicularity, parallelism, volumes and similarity of [parallelepipeds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelepiped "Parallelepiped"). The three sections of Book XI include content on: solid geometry (1-19), solid angles (20-23), and [parallelepipeds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelepiped "Parallelepiped") (24-37).[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArtmann20129-32)

Book XII studies the volumes of [cones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cone_(geometry) "Cone (geometry)"), [pyramids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyramid_(geometry) "Pyramid (geometry)"), and [cylinders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylinder_(geometry) "Cylinder (geometry)") in detail by using the [method of exhaustion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Method_of_exhaustion "Method of exhaustion"),[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArtmann20129-32) a precursor to [integration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral "Integral"), and shows, for example, that the volume of a cone is a third of the volume of the corresponding cylinder. It concludes by showing that the volume of a [sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere "Sphere") is proportional to the cube of its radius (in modern language) by approximating its volume by a union of many pyramids.

Book XIII constructs the five [Platonic solids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platonic_solid "Platonic solid") (regular polyhedra) inscribed in a sphere and compares the ratios of their edges to the radius of the sphere.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArtmann201210-33)

Two additional books, that were not written by Euclid, Books XIV and XV, have been transmitted in the manuscripts of the Elements:[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoyer1991118%E2%80%93119-34)

*   Book XIV, probably written by [Hypsicles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypsicles "Hypsicles") on the basis of a treatise by [Apollonius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apollonius_of_Perga "Apollonius of Perga"), continues Euclid's comparison of regular solids inscribed in spheres, with the chief result being that the ratio of the surfaces of the [dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dodecahedron "Dodecahedron") and [icosahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Icosahedron "Icosahedron") inscribed in the same sphere is the same as the ratio of their volumes, the ratio being

![Image 4: {\displaystyle {\sqrt {\frac {10}{3(5-{\sqrt {5}})}}}={\sqrt {\frac {5+{\sqrt {5}}}{6}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/79000800482e38e87a5f85c10f0594f15302dadd)

*   Book XV, probably written, at least in part, by [Isidore of Miletus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isidore_of_Miletus "Isidore of Miletus"), covers topics such as counting the number of edges and solid angles in the regular solids, and finding the measure of dihedral angles of faces that meet at an edge.

It was not uncommon in ancient times to attribute works to celebrated authors that were not written by them. It is by these means that the [apocryphal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apocrypha "Apocrypha") books of the _Elements_ were sometimes included in the collection.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoyer1991118%E2%80%93119-34)

Euclid's method and style of presentation
-----------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Euclid%27s_Elements&action=edit&section=20 "Edit section: Euclid's method and style of presentation")]

> • To draw a straight line from any point to any point.
> 
>  • To describe a circle with any center and distance.

Euclid, _Elements_, Book I, Postulates 1 & 3.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHartshorne200018-35)

[![Image 5](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/cf/HexagonConstructionAni.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:HexagonConstructionAni.gif)

An animation showing how Euclid constructed a hexagon (Book IV, Proposition 15). Every two-dimensional figure in the _Elements_ can be constructed using only a compass and straightedge.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHartshorne200018-35)

Euclid's [axiomatic approach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiomatic_system#Axiomatic_method "Axiomatic system") and [constructive methods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_geometry#Methods_of_proof "Euclidean geometry") were widely influential.

Many of Euclid's propositions were constructive, demonstrating the existence of some figure by detailing the steps he used to construct the object using a [compass and straightedge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compass-and-straightedge_construction "Compass-and-straightedge construction"). His constructive approach appears even in his geometry's postulates, as the first and third postulates stating the existence of a line and circle are constructive. Instead of stating that lines and circles exist per his prior definitions, he states that it is possible to 'construct' a line and circle. It also appears that, for him to use a figure in one of his proofs, he needs to construct it in an earlier proposition. For example, he proves the Pythagorean theorem by first inscribing a square on the sides of a right triangle, but only after constructing a square on a given line one proposition earlier.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHartshorne200018%E2%80%9320-36)

The presentation of each result is given in a stylized form, which, although not invented by Euclid, is recognized as typically classical. It has six different parts: First is the 'enunciation', which states the result in general terms (i.e., the statement of the proposition). Then comes the 'setting-out', which gives the figure and denotes particular geometrical objects by letters. Next comes the 'definition' or 'specification', which restates the enunciation in terms of the particular figure. Then the 'construction' or 'machinery' follows. Here, the original figure is extended to forward the proof. Then, the 'proof' itself follows. Finally, the 'conclusion' connects the proof to the enunciation by stating the specific conclusions drawn in the proof, in the general terms of the enunciation.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHeath1931216-37)

No indication is given of the method of reasoning that led to the result, although the _[Data](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data\_(Euclid) "Data (Euclid)")_ does provide instruction about how to approach the types of problems encountered in the first four books of the _Elements_.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBall191554-38) Some scholars have tried to find fault in Euclid's use of figures in his proofs, accusing him of writing proofs that depended on the specific figures drawn rather than the general underlying logic, especially concerning Proposition II of Book I. However, Euclid's original proof of this proposition, is general, valid, and does not depend on the figure used as an example to illustrate one given configuration.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEToussaint199312%E2%80%9323-39)

As was common in ancient mathematical texts, when a proposition needed [proof](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_proof "Mathematical proof") in several different cases, Euclid often proved only one of them (often the most difficult), leaving the others to the reader. Later editors such as [Theon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theon_of_Alexandria "Theon of Alexandria") often interpolated their own proofs of these cases.

Euclid's presentation was limited by the mathematical ideas and notations in common currency in his era, and this causes the treatment to seem awkward to the modern reader in some places. For example, there was no notion of an angle greater than two right angles,[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBall191555-40) the number 1 was sometimes treated separately from other positive integers, and as multiplication was treated geometrically he did not use the product of more than 3 different numbers. The geometrical treatment of number theory may have been because the alternative would have been the extremely awkward [Alexandrian system of numerals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_numerals "Greek numerals").[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBall191554,_58,_127-41)

[![Image 6](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Woman_teaching_geometry.jpg/250px-Woman_teaching_geometry.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Woman_teaching_geometry.jpg)

An illumination from a manuscript based on [Adelard of Bath](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adelard_of_Bath "Adelard of Bath")'s translation of the _Elements_, c. 1309–1316; Adelard's is the oldest surviving translation of the _Elements_ into Latin, done in the 12th-century work and translated from Arabic.[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTERussell2013177-42)

Euclid's _Elements_ has been referred to as the most successful [textbook](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Textbook "Textbook") ever written.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoyer1991119-43) The _Elements_ is often considered after the [Bible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bible "Bible") as the most frequently translated, published, and studied book in history.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTETaisbakVan_der_Waerden2021%C2%A7_%22Legacy%22-44) With Aristotle's _[Metaphysics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metaphysics\_(Aristotle) "Metaphysics (Aristotle)")_, the _Elements_ is perhaps the most successful ancient Greek text, and was the dominant mathematical textbook in the Medieval Islamic world and Western Europe.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTETaisbakVan_der_Waerden2021%C2%A7_%22Legacy%22-44) In historical context, it has proven enormously influential in many areas of [science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Science "Science"). It was one of the very earliest mathematical works to be printed after the [invention of the printing press](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Movable_type "Movable type") and has been estimated to be second only to the [Bible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bible "Bible") in the number of editions published since the first printing in 1482,[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoyer1991100-45) the number reaching well over one thousand.[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBuntJonesBedient1988142-46) Scientists [Nicolaus Copernicus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nicolaus_Copernicus "Nicolaus Copernicus"), [Johannes Kepler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johannes_Kepler "Johannes Kepler"), [Galileo Galilei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galileo_Galilei "Galileo Galilei"), [Albert Einstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Einstein "Albert Einstein") and Sir [Isaac Newton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaac_Newton "Isaac Newton") were all influenced by the _Elements_, and applied their knowledge of it to their work.

The success of the _Elements_ is due primarily to its logical presentation of most of the mathematical knowledge available to Euclid. Much of the material is not original to him, although many of the proofs are his. However, Euclid's systematic development of his subject, from a small set of axioms to deep results, and the consistency of his approach throughout the _Elements_, encouraged its use as a textbook for about 2,000 years. The _Elements_ still influences modern geometry books. Furthermore, its logical, axiomatic approach and rigorous proofs remain the cornerstone of mathematics. Mathematicians and philosophers, such as [Thomas Hobbes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Hobbes "Thomas Hobbes"), [Baruch Spinoza](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baruch_Spinoza "Baruch Spinoza"), [Alfred North Whitehead](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alfred_North_Whitehead "Alfred North Whitehead"), and [Bertrand Russell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bertrand_Russell "Bertrand Russell"), have attempted to create their own foundational "Elements" for their respective disciplines, by adopting the axiomatized deductive structures that Euclid's work introduced.

### In Classical antiquity

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Euclid%27s_Elements&action=edit&section=22 "Edit section: In Classical antiquity")]

The oldest extant evidence for Euclid's Elements are a set of six ostraca found among the [Elephantine papyri and ostraca](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elephantine_papyri_and_ostraca "Elephantine papyri and ostraca"), from the 3rd century BC that deal with propositions XIII.10 and XIII.16, on the construction of a dodecahedron.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFowler1999209%E2%80%93210-47) A papyrus recovered from [Herculaneum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herculaneum "Herculaneum")[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-48) contains an essay by the Epicurean philosopher [Demetrius Lacon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Demetrius_Lacon "Demetrius Lacon") on Euclid's Elements.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFowler1999209%E2%80%93210-47) The earliest extant papyrus containing the actual text of the Elements is [Papyrus Oxyrhynchus 29](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Papyrus_Oxyrhynchus_29 "Papyrus Oxyrhynchus 29"), a fragment containing the text of Book II, Proposition 5 and an accompanying diagram, dated to c. 75–125 AD.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFowler1999210%E2%80%93211-49)

These ancient texts which refer to the _Elements_ itself, and to other mathematical theories that were current at the time it was written, are also important in reconstructing the history of the text. Such analyses are conducted by [J. L. Heiberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johan_Ludvig_Heiberg_(historian) "Johan Ludvig Heiberg (historian)") and Sir [Thomas Little Heath](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Little_Heath "Thomas Little Heath") in their editions of the text.

In the 4th century AD, [Theon of Alexandria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theon_of_Alexandria "Theon of Alexandria") produced an edition of Euclid which was so widely used that it became the only surviving source until [François Peyrard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Peyrard "François Peyrard")'s 1808 discovery at the [Vatican](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vatican_Library "Vatican Library") of a manuscript not derived from Theon's. This manuscript, the [Heiberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johan_Ludvig_Heiberg_(historian) "Johan Ludvig Heiberg (historian)") manuscript, is from a Byzantine workshop around 900 and is the basis of modern editions.[[b]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-surviving1-50)[[c]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-surviving2-51)[Papyrus Oxyrhynchus 29](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Papyrus_Oxyrhynchus_29 "Papyrus Oxyrhynchus 29") is a tiny fragment of an even older manuscript, but only contains the statement of one proposition.

Although Euclid was known to [Cicero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cicero "Cicero"), for instance, no record exists of the text having been translated into Latin prior to [Boethius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boethius "Boethius") in the fifth or sixth century.[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTERussell2013177-42) The Arabs received the _Elements_ from the Byzantines around 760; this version was translated into [Arabic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabic "Arabic") under [Harun al-Rashid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harun_al-Rashid "Harun al-Rashid") (c. 800).[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTERussell2013177-42) The Byzantine scholar [Arethas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arethas_of_Caesarea "Arethas of Caesarea") commissioned the copying of one of the extant Greek manuscripts of Euclid in the late ninth century.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEReynoldsWilson199157-52) Although known in Byzantium, the _Elements_ was lost to Western Europe until about 1120, when the English monk [Adelard of Bath](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adelard_of_Bath "Adelard of Bath") translated it into Latin from an Arabic translation.[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBall1915165-53) A relatively recent discovery was made of a Greek-to-Latin translation from the 12th century at Palermo, Sicily. The name of the translator is not known other than he was an anonymous medical student from Salerno who was visiting Palermo in order to translate the [Almagest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Almagest "Almagest") to Latin. The Euclid manuscript is extant and quite complete.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMurdoch1967-54)

After the translation by Adelard of Bath (known as Adelard I), there was a flurry of translations from Arabic. Notable translators in this period include [Herman of Carinthia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herman_of_Carinthia "Herman of Carinthia") who wrote an edition around 1140, Robert of Chester (his manuscripts are referred to collectively as Adelard II, written on or before 1251), Johannes de Tinemue,[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKnorr1990-55) possibly also known as [John of Tynemouth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_of_Tynemouth_(geometer) "John of Tynemouth (geometer)") (his manuscripts are referred to collectively as Adelard III), late 12th century, and [Gerard of Cremona](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gerard_of_Cremona "Gerard of Cremona") (sometime after 1120 but before 1187). The exact details concerning these translations is still an active area of research.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBusard20051%E2%80%9340Survey_of_the_Arabic%E2%80%93Latin_and_Greek%E2%80%93Latin_translations-56)[Campanus of Novara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Campanus_of_Novara "Campanus of Novara") relied heavily on these Arabic translations to create his edition (sometime before 1260) which ultimately came to dominate Latin editions until the availability of Greek manuscripts in the 16th century. There are more than 100 pre-1482 Campanus manuscripts still available today.[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFolkerts1989-57)[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-58)

Copies of the Greek text still exist, some of which can be found in the [Vatican Library](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vatican_Library "Vatican Library") and the [Bodleian Library](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bodleian_Library "Bodleian Library") in Oxford. The manuscripts available are of variable quality, and invariably incomplete. By careful analysis of the translations and originals, hypotheses have been made about the contents of the original text. Also of importance are the [scholia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scholia "Scholia"), or annotations to the text. These additions, which often distinguished themselves from the main text (depending on the manuscript), gradually accumulated over time as opinions varied upon what was worthy of explanation or further study.

### In modern mathematics

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Euclid%27s_Elements&action=edit&section=25 "Edit section: In modern mathematics")]

The _Elements_ is still considered a masterpiece in the application of [logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logic "Logic") to [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"). The mathematician [Oliver Byrne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oliver_Byrne_(mathematician) "Oliver Byrne (mathematician)") published a well-known version of the _Elements_ in 1847 entitled _The First Six Books of the Elements of Euclid in Which Coloured Diagrams and Symbols Are Used Instead of Letters for the Greater Ease of Learners_, which included colored diagrams intended to increase its pedagogical effect.[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHawesKolpas2015-65)[David Hilbert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Hilbert "David Hilbert") authored a [modern axiomatization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert%27s_axioms "Hilbert's axioms") of the _Elements_.[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEH%C3%A4hlPeters2022%C2%A7_para._1-66)

#### Non-Euclidean geometry

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Euclid%27s_Elements&action=edit&section=26 "Edit section: Non-Euclidean geometry")]

[![Image 8](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Noneuclid.svg/330px-Noneuclid.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Noneuclid.svg)

The different versions of the parallel postulate result in different geometries.

The geometrical system established by the _Elements_ long dominated the field; however, today that system is often referred to as '[Euclidean geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_geometry "Euclidean geometry")' to distinguish it from other [non-Euclidean geometries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-Euclidean_geometry "Non-Euclidean geometry") discovered in the early 19th century.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTETaisbakVan_der_Waerden2021%C2%A7_%22Legacy%22-44)

One of the most notable influences of Euclid on modern mathematics is the discussion of the [parallel postulate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_postulate "Parallel postulate"). In Book I, Euclid lists five postulates, the fifth of which stipulates

> If a [line segment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_segment "Line segment") intersects two straight [lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_(mathematics) "Line (mathematics)") forming two interior angles on the same side that sum to less than two [right angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_angle "Right angle"), then the two lines, if extended indefinitely, meet on that side on which the angles sum to less than two right angles.

This postulate plagued mathematicians for centuries due to its apparent complexity compared with the other four postulates. Many attempts were made to prove the fifth postulate based on the other four, but they never succeeded. Eventually in 1829, mathematician [Nikolai Lobachevsky](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nikolai_Lobachevsky "Nikolai Lobachevsky") published a description of acute geometry (or [hyperbolic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_geometry "Hyperbolic geometry")), a geometry which assumed a different form of the parallel postulate. It is in fact possible to create a valid geometry without the fifth postulate entirely, or with different versions of the fifth postulate ([elliptic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptic_geometry "Elliptic geometry")). If one takes the fifth postulate as a given, the result is [Euclidean geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_geometry "Euclidean geometry").[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTELaubenbacherPengelley1999-67)

Some of the foundational theorems are proved using axioms that Euclid did not state explicitly, such as [Pasch's axiom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pasch%27s_axiom "Pasch's axiom"). Early attempts to construct a more complete set of axioms include [Hilbert's geometry axioms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert%27s_axioms "Hilbert's axioms") and [Tarski's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tarski%27s_axioms "Tarski's axioms"). Later editors have added Euclid's implicit axiomatic assumptions in their list of formal axioms.[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHeath190862-68) In 2017, Michael Beeson et al. used computer [proof assistants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proof_assistant "Proof assistant") to create a new set of axioms similar to Euclid's and generate proofs that were valid with those axioms.[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBeesonNarbouxWiedijk2019-69)

A few proofs also rely on assumptions that are intuitive but not explicitly proven. For example, in the first construction of Book 1, Euclid used a premise that was neither postulated nor proved: that two circles with centers at the distance of their radius will intersect in two points.[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHeath1908242-70) Mathematician and historian [W. W. Rouse Ball](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/W._W._Rouse_Ball "W. W. Rouse Ball") put the criticisms in perspective, remarking that "the fact that for two thousand years [the _Elements_] was the usual text-book on the subject raises a strong presumption that it is not unsuitable for that purpose."[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid's_Elements#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBall191555-40)
