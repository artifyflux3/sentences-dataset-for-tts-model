This article is about natural satellites in general. For orbiting artificial spacecrafts, see [Satellite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satellite "Satellite").

[![Image 1](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Dscovrepicmoontransitfull.gif/500px-Dscovrepicmoontransitfull.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Dscovrepicmoontransitfull.gif)

The [Moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moon "Moon") orbiting around [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth "Earth") (observed by the [Deep Space Climate Observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deep_Space_Climate_Observatory "Deep Space Climate Observatory"))

A **natural satellite** is, in the most common usage, an [astronomical body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_object "Astronomical object") that [orbits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbit "Orbit") a [planet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planet "Planet"), [dwarf planet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dwarf_planet "Dwarf planet"), or [small Solar System body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Small_Solar_System_body "Small Solar System body") (or sometimes another natural satellite). Natural satellites are colloquially referred to as **moons**, a derivation from the [Moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moon "Moon") of [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth "Earth").

In the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System"), there are six planetary [satellite systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satellite_system_(astronomy) "Satellite system (astronomy)"), altogether comprising 418 natural satellites with confirmed orbits. Seven objects commonly considered [dwarf planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dwarf_planet "Dwarf planet") by astronomers are also known to have natural satellites: [Orcus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orcus_(dwarf_planet) "Orcus (dwarf planet)"), [Pluto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons_of_Pluto "Moons of Pluto"), [Haumea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons_of_Haumea "Moons of Haumea"), [Quaoar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quaoar "Quaoar"), [Makemake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Makemake "Makemake"), [Gonggong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gonggong_(dwarf_planet) "Gonggong (dwarf planet)"), and [Eris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eris_(dwarf_planet) "Eris (dwarf planet)").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-WGPSN-1) As of January 2022, there are 447 other [minor planets known to have natural satellites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minor-planet_moon "Minor-planet moon").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-Johnston-2)

A planet usually has at least around 10,000 times the mass of any natural satellites that orbit it, with a correspondingly much larger diameter.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-2006Natur.441..834C-3) The [Earth–Moon system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%E2%80%93Moon_system "Earth–Moon system") is a unique exception in the Solar System; at 3,474 kilometres (2,158 miles) across, the Moon is 0.273 times the [diameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diameter "Diameter") of Earth and about 1⁄80 of its mass.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-GuinnessWR2014-4) The next largest ratios are the [Neptune](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune "Neptune")–[Triton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triton_(moon) "Triton (moon)") system at 0.055 (with a mass ratio of about 1 to 4790), the [Saturn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saturn "Saturn")–[Titan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Titan_(moon) "Titan (moon)") system at 0.044 (with the second mass ratio next to the Earth–Moon system, 1 to 4220), the [Jupiter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jupiter "Jupiter")–[Ganymede](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ganymede_(moon) "Ganymede (moon)") system at 0.038, and the [Uranus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uranus "Uranus")–[Titania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Titania_(moon) "Titania (moon)") system at 0.031. For the category of [dwarf planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dwarf_planet "Dwarf planet"), [Charon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charon_(moon) "Charon (moon)") has the largest ratio, being 0.52 the diameter and 12.2% the mass of [Pluto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pluto "Pluto").

The first known natural satellite was the [Moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moon "Moon"), but it was considered a "planet" until [Copernicus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nicolaus_Copernicus "Nicolaus Copernicus")' introduction of _[De revolutionibus orbium coelestium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De\_revolutionibus\_orbium\_coelestium "De revolutionibus orbium coelestium")_ in 1543. Until the discovery of the [Galilean satellites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galilean_moons "Galilean moons") in 1610 there was no opportunity for referring to such objects as a class. [Galileo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galileo_Galilei "Galileo Galilei") chose to refer to his discoveries as _Planetæ_ ("planets"), but later discoverers chose other terms to distinguish them from the objects they orbited.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-metzger22-5)

The first to use the term _satellite_ to describe orbiting bodies was the German astronomer [Johannes Kepler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johannes_Kepler "Johannes Kepler") in his pamphlet _Narratio de Observatis a se quatuor Iouis satellitibus erronibus_ ("Narration About Four Satellites of Jupiter Observed") in 1610. He derived the term from the [Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin "Latin") word _satelles_, meaning "guard", "attendant", or "companion", because the _satellites_ accompanied their primary planet in their journey through the heavens.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-NASA-firstsatellites-6)

The term _satellite_ thus became the normal one for referring to an object orbiting a planet, as it avoided the ambiguity of "moon". In 1957, however, the launching of the artificial object [Sputnik](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sputnik "Sputnik") created a need for new terminology.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-NASA-firstsatellites-6) The terms _man-made satellite_ and _artificial moon_ were very quickly abandoned in favor of the simpler _satellite_. As a consequence, the term has become linked with artificial objects flown in space.

Because of this shift in meaning, the term _moon_, which had continued to be used in a generic sense in works of popular science and fiction, has regained respectability and is now used interchangeably with _natural satellite_, even in scientific articles. When it is necessary to avoid both the ambiguity of confusion with Earth's natural satellite the Moon and the natural satellites of the other planets on the one hand, and artificial satellites on the other, the term _natural satellite_ (using "natural" in a sense opposed to "artificial") is used. To further avoid ambiguity, the convention is to capitalize the word Moon when referring to Earth's natural satellite (a [proper noun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proper_noun "Proper noun")), but not when referring to other natural satellites ([common nouns](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Common_noun "Common noun")).

Many authors define "satellite" or "natural satellite" as orbiting some planet or minor planet, synonymous with "moon"– by such a definition all natural satellites are moons, but Earth and other planets are not satellites.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-7)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-8)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-9) A few recent authors define "moon" as "a satellite of a planet or minor planet", and "planet" as "a satellite of a star"– such authors consider Earth as a "natural satellite of the Sun".[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-10)[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-11)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-NASA-what-is-a-satellite-12)

### Definition of a moon

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Natural_satellite&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Definition of a moon")]

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Moon%2C_Earth_size_comparison.jpg/250px-Moon%2C_Earth_size_comparison.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Moon,_Earth_size_comparison.jpg)

Size comparison of [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth "Earth") and the [Moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moon "Moon")

There is no established lower limit on what is considered a "moon". Every natural celestial body with an identified orbit around a planet of the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System"), some as small as a kilometer across, has been considered a moon,[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-13) though objects a tenth that size within Saturn's rings, which have not been directly observed, have been called _[moonlets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moonlet "Moonlet")_. Small [asteroid moons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minor-planet_moon "Minor-planet moon") (natural satellites of asteroids), such as [Dactyl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dactyl_(moon) "Dactyl (moon)"), have also been called moonlets.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-Marchis05-14)

The upper limit is also vague. Two orbiting bodies are sometimes described as a [double planet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double_planet "Double planet") rather than a primary and satellite; the Earth-Moon system is used as an example.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-15)[Asteroids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asteroid "Asteroid") such as [90 Antiope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/90_Antiope "90 Antiope") are considered double asteroids,[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-16) but they have not forced a clear definition of what constitutes a moon. Some authors consider the Pluto–Charon system to be a double (dwarf) planet,[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-Ksanfomality_2016-17) with one argument being that the [barycentre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barycentre "Barycentre") lies above the surface of the larger body.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-Rijsdijk_2007-18) In contrast, the barycenter of all planetary moons of the Solar System are located within the radius of their host planet.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-Hahn_2020-19)

Origin and orbital characteristics
----------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Natural_satellite&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Origin and orbital characteristics")]

The natural satellites orbiting relatively close to the planet on [prograde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Retrograde_and_prograde_motion "Retrograde and prograde motion"), uninclined circular orbits ([_regular_ satellites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_moon "Regular moon")) are generally thought to have been formed out of the same [collapsing region](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumplanetary_disk "Circumplanetary disk") of the [protoplanetary disk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protoplanetary_disk "Protoplanetary disk") that created its primary.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-arxiv0812-20)[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-dangelo_podolak_2015-21) In contrast, [irregular satellites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irregular_satellite "Irregular satellite") (generally orbiting on distant, [inclined](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_inclination "Orbital inclination"), [eccentric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity "Orbital eccentricity") and/or [retrograde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Retrograde_motion "Retrograde motion") orbits) are thought to be captured [asteroids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asteroid "Asteroid") possibly further fragmented by collisions. Most of the major natural satellites of the Solar System have regular orbits, while most of the small natural satellites have irregular orbits.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-22) The [Moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moon "Moon") and the [Moons of Pluto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons_of_Pluto "Moons of Pluto") are exceptions among large bodies in that they are thought to have originated from the collision of two large protoplanetary objects early in the Solar System's history (see the [giant impact hypothesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giant_impact_hypothesis "Giant impact hypothesis")).[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-23)[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-24) The material that would have been placed in orbit around the central body is predicted to have reaccreted to form one or more orbiting natural satellites. As opposed to planetary-sized bodies, [asteroid moons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asteroid_moon "Asteroid moon") are thought to commonly form by this process.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-Durda_et_al_2004-25)[Triton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triton_(moon) "Triton (moon)") is another exception; although large and in a close, circular orbit, its motion is retrograde and it is thought to be a captured [dwarf planet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dwarf_planet "Dwarf planet").[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-26)

Two natural satellites are known to have small companions at both their [L 4](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrange_point#L4_and_L5 "Lagrange point") and [L 5](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrange_point#L4_and_L5 "Lagrange point")[Lagrangian points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrangian_point "Lagrangian point"), sixty degrees ahead and behind the body in its orbit. These companions are called [trojan moons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trojan_moon "Trojan moon"), as their orbits are analogous to the [trojan asteroids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trojan_asteroid "Trojan asteroid") of [Jupiter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jupiter "Jupiter"). The trojan moons are [Telesto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Telesto_(moon) "Telesto (moon)") and [Calypso](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calypso_(moon) "Calypso (moon)"), which are the leading and following companions, respectively, of the Saturnian moon [Tethys](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tethys_(moon) "Tethys (moon)"); and [Helene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helene_(moon) "Helene (moon)") and [Polydeuces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polydeuces_(moon) "Polydeuces (moon)"), the leading and following companions of the Saturnian moon [Dione](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dione_(moon) "Dione (moon)").[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-27)

### Temporary satellites

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Natural_satellite&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Temporary satellites")]

The capture of an asteroid from a heliocentric orbit is not always permanent. According to simulations, [temporary satellites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temporary_satellite "Temporary satellite") should be a common phenomenon.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-Carlisle-28)[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-Fedorets-29) As of 2023, the observed minor bodies that have displayed transient co-orbital motion with Earth are: [1991 VG](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1991_VG "1991 VG"), [2006 RH 120](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2006_RH120 "2006 RH120"), [2020 CD 3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2020_CD3 "2020 CD3"), and [2022 NX 1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2022_NX1 "2022 NX1").[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-30)

[2006 RH 120](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2006_RH120 "2006 RH120") was a temporary satellite of Earth for nine months in 2006 and 2007.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-Shefford-31)[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-Sinott-32)

Most [regular moons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_moon "Regular moon") (natural satellites following relatively close and prograde orbits with small orbital inclination and eccentricity) in the Solar System are tidally locked to their respective primaries, meaning that the same side of the natural satellite always faces its planet. This phenomenon comes about through a loss of energy due to tidal forces raised by the planet, slowing the rotation of the satellite until it is negligible.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-Barnes_2010-33) Exceptions are known; one such exception is [Saturn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saturn "Saturn")'s natural satellite [Hyperion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperion_(moon) "Hyperion (moon)"), which rotates chaotically because of the gravitational influence of [Titan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Titan_(moon) "Titan (moon)").[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-34) Pluto's four, circumbinary small moons also rotate chaotically due to Charon's influence.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-NASA2015-35)

In contrast, the outer natural satellites of the giant planets (irregular satellites) are too far away to have become locked. For example, Jupiter's [Himalia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Himalia_(moon) "Himalia (moon)"), Saturn's [Phoebe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phoebe_(moon) "Phoebe (moon)"), and Neptune's [Nereid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nereid_(moon) "Nereid (moon)") have rotation periods in the range of ten hours, whereas their orbital periods are hundreds of days.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-36)

Satellites of satellites
------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Natural_satellite&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Satellites of satellites")]

[![Image 3](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Rhean_rings_PIA10246_Full_res.jpg/250px-Rhean_rings_PIA10246_Full_res.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Rhean_rings_PIA10246_Full_res.jpg)

Artist impression of [Rhea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhea_(moon) "Rhea (moon)")'s proposed rings

No "moons of moons" or subsatellites (natural satellites that orbit a natural satellite of a planet) are currently known. In most cases, the tidal effects of the planet would make such a system unstable. Potential exceptions include large moons on wide orbits, including Titan, Iapetus, Callisto, and the Moon. However, other sources of dynamical instability may remove such submoons, such as [mascons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mascons "Mascons") on the Moon.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-37)

Calculations performed after the 2008 detection[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-38) of a possible [ring system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rings_of_Rhea "Rings of Rhea") around Saturn's moon [Rhea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhea_(moon) "Rhea (moon)") indicate that satellites orbiting Rhea could have stable orbits. Furthermore, the suspected rings are thought to be narrow,[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-39) a phenomenon normally associated with [shepherd moons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shepherd_moon "Shepherd moon"). However, targeted images taken by the _[Cassini](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens "Cassini–Huygens")_ spacecraft failed to detect rings around Rhea.[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-40)

It has also been proposed that Saturn's moon [Iapetus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iapetus_(moon) "Iapetus (moon)") had a satellite in the past; this is one of several hypotheses that have been put forward to account for its [equatorial ridge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equatorial_ridge_on_Iapetus "Equatorial ridge on Iapetus").[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-41)

Light-curve analysis suggests that Saturn's irregular satellite [Kiviuq](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kiviuq_(moon) "Kiviuq (moon)") is extremely prolate, and is likely a [contact binary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contact_binary_(small_Solar_System_body) "Contact binary (small Solar System body)") or even a binary moon.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-LPSC2654-42)

[![Image 4](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Masses_of_all_moons_in_the_Solar_System.png/330px-Masses_of_all_moons_in_the_Solar_System.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Masses_of_all_moons_in_the_Solar_System.png)

The relative masses of the natural satellites of the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System"). [Mimas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mimas_(moon) "Mimas (moon)"), [Enceladus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enceladus "Enceladus"), and [Miranda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Miranda_(moon) "Miranda (moon)") are too small to be visible at this scale. All the irregularly shaped natural satellites, even added together, would also be too small to be visible.

Neptune's moon [Proteus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proteus_(moon) "Proteus (moon)") is the largest irregularly shaped natural satellite, and is about as large as an icy moon can become before becoming [relaxed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relaxation_(physics) "Relaxation (physics)") into a [spheroidal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spheroid "Spheroid") shape.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-Croft_402-43) The shapes of moons in synchronous orbit are expected to [asymptotically](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asymptote "Asymptote") change shape into rounded [ellipsoids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipsoid "Ellipsoid") under [hydrostatic equilibrium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrostatic_equilibrium "Hydrostatic equilibrium"), although this may not happen in the age of the Solar System. For example, the larger Saturnian moons are in equilibrium, while [Iapetus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iapetus_(moon) "Iapetus (moon)"), Mimas, and Enceladus are apparently not.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-44)[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-45)The "round/rounded satellites" are sometimes categorized as [planetary-mass moons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary-mass_moon "Planetary-mass moon").[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] The shapes of Eris' moon [Dysnomia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dysnomia_(moon) "Dysnomia (moon)") and [Orcus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orcus_(dwarf_planet) "Orcus (dwarf planet)")' moon [Vanth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vanth_(moon) "Vanth (moon)") are presently unknown, although Dysnomia's density is high enough that it is probably a solid ellipsoid as well.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

The larger natural satellites, being tidally locked, tend toward [ovoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oval "Oval") (egg-like) shapes: squat at their poles and with longer equatorial axes in the direction of their primaries (their planets) than in the direction of their motion.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-Rambaux_Castillo-Rogez_2012-46): 184–187  Saturn's moon [Mimas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mimas_(moon) "Mimas (moon)"), for example, has a major axis 9% greater than its polar axis and 5% greater than its other equatorial axis.[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-Thomas_2010-47)[Methone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methone_(moon) "Methone (moon)"), another of Saturn's moons, is only around 3 km in diameter and [visibly egg-shaped](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Methone_PIA14633.jpg "File:Methone PIA14633.jpg").[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-48) The effect is smaller on the largest natural satellites, where their gravity is greater relative to the effects of tidal distortion, especially those that orbit less massive planets or, as in the case of the Moon, at greater distances.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-Rambaux_Castillo-Rogez_2012-46): 167–170

Geological activity
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Natural_satellite&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Geological activity")]

Of the nineteen known natural satellites in the Solar System that are large enough to be gravitationally rounded, several remain geologically active today. [Io](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Io_(moon) "Io (moon)") is the most volcanically active body in the Solar System,[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-Lainey_et_al_2009-49) while [Europa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Europa_(moon) "Europa (moon)"),[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-Cook_et_al_2013-50)[Enceladus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enceladus "Enceladus"),[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-Roberts_Nimmo_2008-51) and [Triton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triton_(moon) "Triton (moon)") display evidence of ongoing [tectonic activity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tectonics "Tectonics") and [cryovolcanism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cryovolcano "Cryovolcano"). In the first three cases, the geological activity is powered by the [tidal heating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tidal_heating#Tidal_heating "Tidal heating") resulting from having [eccentric orbits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity "Orbital eccentricity") close to their giant-planet primaries.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-Lainey_et_al_2009-49)[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-Cook_et_al_2013-50)[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-Roberts_Nimmo_2008-51) (This mechanism would have also operated on Triton in the past before its orbit was [circularized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tidal_circularization "Tidal circularization").[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-52)) Many other natural satellites, such as Earth's Moon, [Ganymede](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ganymede_(moon) "Ganymede (moon)"), [Titan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Titan_(moon) "Titan (moon)"), Tethys, and Miranda show evidence of past geological activity, resulting from energy sources such as the [decay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radioactive_decay "Radioactive decay") of their [primordial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primordial_nuclide "Primordial nuclide")[radioisotopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radionuclide "Radionuclide"), greater past orbital eccentricities (due in some cases to past [orbital resonances](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_resonance "Orbital resonance")), or the [differentiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_differentiation "Planetary differentiation") or freezing of their interiors.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-Nimmo_2025-53) Enceladus and Triton both have active features resembling [geysers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geysers "Geysers"), although in the case of Triton solar heating appears to provide the energy. Titan and Triton have significant atmospheres; Titan also has [hydrocarbon lakes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lakes_of_Titan "Lakes of Titan").[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-Nimmo_2025-53) All four of the Galilean moons have atmospheres, though they are extremely thin.[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-54)[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-EuropaAtmosphere-55)[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-JPLAtmosphere-56) Four of the largest natural satellites, Europa,[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-Cook_et_al_2013-50) Ganymede, [Callisto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Callisto_(moon) "Callisto (moon)"), and Titan, are thought to have subsurface oceans of liquid water,[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-Nimmo_2025-53) while smaller Enceladus also supports a global subsurface ocean of liquid water.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-Roberts_Nimmo_2008-51)

Occurrence in the Solar System
------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Natural_satellite&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Occurrence in the Solar System")]

[![Image 5](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Moons_of_the_Solar_System_To_Scale_%2843564841545%29.png/330px-Moons_of_the_Solar_System_To_Scale_%2843564841545%29.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Moons_of_the_Solar_System_To_Scale_(43564841545).png)

Largest moons to scale with their parent planets and dwarf planet

Of the inner planets, [Mercury](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mercury_(planet) "Mercury (planet)") and [Venus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Venus "Venus") have no natural satellites; Earth has one large natural satellite, known as the Moon; and [Mars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mars "Mars") has two tiny natural satellites, [Phobos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phobos_(moon) "Phobos (moon)") and [Deimos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deimos_(moon) "Deimos (moon)"). The [giant planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giant_planet "Giant planet") have extensive systems of natural satellites, including half a dozen comparable in size to Earth's Moon: the four [Galilean moons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galilean_moon "Galilean moon"), Saturn's Titan, and [Neptune](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune "Neptune")'s Triton.[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-Kane_et_al_2013-57) Saturn has an additional six mid-sized natural satellites massive enough to have achieved [hydrostatic equilibrium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrostatic_equilibrium "Hydrostatic equilibrium"), and [Uranus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uranus "Uranus") has five. It has been suggested that some [satellites may potentially harbour life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_satellite_habitability "Natural satellite habitability").[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-WRD-20150127-58)

Among the objects generally agreed by astronomers to be dwarf planets, [Ceres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ceres_(dwarf_planet) "Ceres (dwarf planet)") and [Sedna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sedna_(dwarf_planet) "Sedna (dwarf planet)") have no known natural satellites. Pluto has the relatively large natural satellite Charon and four smaller natural satellites; [Styx](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Styx_(moon) "Styx (moon)"), [Nix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nix_(moon) "Nix (moon)"), [Kerberos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kerberos_(moon) "Kerberos (moon)"), and [Hydra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydra_(moon) "Hydra (moon)").[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-esahubble-59)[Haumea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haumea "Haumea") has two natural satellites; [Orcus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orcus_(dwarf_planet) "Orcus (dwarf planet)"), [Quaoar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quaoar "Quaoar"), [Makemake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Makemake "Makemake"), [Gonggong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gonggong_(dwarf_planet) "Gonggong (dwarf planet)"), and [Eris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eris_(dwarf_planet) "Eris (dwarf planet)") have one each.[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-Upadhyay_2025-60) The Pluto–Charon system is unusual in that the [center of mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Center_of_mass#Barycenter_in_astrophysics_and_astronomy "Center of mass") lies in open space between the two, a characteristic sometimes associated with a [double-planet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double_planet "Double planet") system.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-Ksanfomality_2016-17)

Planets around other stars are likely to have satellites as well, and although numerous candidates have been detected to date, as of 2024 none have yet been confirmed.[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-61)

### Non-planetary satellites

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Natural_satellite&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Non-planetary satellites")]

The discovery of [243 Ida](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/243_Ida "243 Ida")'s natural satellite [Dactyl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dactyl_(moon) "Dactyl (moon)") in the early 1990s confirmed that some [asteroids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asteroid "Asteroid") have natural satellites;[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-62) indeed, [87 Sylvia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/87_Sylvia "87 Sylvia") has two.[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-63) Some, such as [90 Antiope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/90_Antiope "90 Antiope"), are double asteroids with two comparably sized components.[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-64) For astronomers, a useful aspect of an asteroid satellite is that it can be used to determine the density of the primary asteroid, without the need for a spacecraft fly-by mission.[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-65)

Besides planets and dwarf planets objects within the Solar System known to have natural satellites are 76 in the [asteroid belt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asteroid_belt "Asteroid belt") (five with two each), four [Jupiter trojans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jupiter_trojan "Jupiter trojan"), 39 [near-Earth objects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Near-Earth_object "Near-Earth object") (two with two satellites each), and 14 [Mars-crossers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_Mars-crossing_minor_planets "List of Mars-crossing minor planets").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-Johnston-2) There are also 84 known natural satellites of [trans-Neptunian objects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trans-Neptunian_objects "Trans-Neptunian objects").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-Johnston-2) Some 150 additional small bodies have been observed within the [rings of Saturn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rings_of_Saturn "Rings of Saturn"), but only a few were tracked long enough to establish orbits.

The seven largest [natural satellites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_natural_satellites "List of natural satellites") in the Solar System (those bigger than 2,500 km across) are Jupiter's [Galilean moons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galilean_moon "Galilean moon") (Ganymede, [Callisto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Callisto_(moon) "Callisto (moon)"), Io, and [Europa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Europa_(moon) "Europa (moon)")), Saturn's moon Titan, Earth's moon, and Neptune's captured natural satellite Triton.[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-Burns_Matthews_1986-66) Of these, Ganymede and Titan are larger than the planet Mercury, while Callisto is about the same size.[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-Koupelis_2010-67) The next size group of nine mid-sized natural satellites, between 1,000 km and 1,600 km across, consists of [Titania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Titania_(moon) "Titania (moon)"), [Oberon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oberon_(moon) "Oberon (moon)"), [Rhea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhea_(moon) "Rhea (moon)"), [Iapetus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iapetus_(moon) "Iapetus (moon)"), Charon, [Ariel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ariel_(moon) "Ariel (moon)"), [Umbriel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Umbriel "Umbriel"), [Dione](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dione_(moon) "Dione (moon)"), and Tethys, the smallest.[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-Hussman_et_al_2010-68) As well as the natural satellites of the various planets, there are hundreds of known natural satellites of the [dwarf planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dwarf_planet "Dwarf planet"), [minor planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minor_planet "Minor planet") and other [small Solar System bodies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Small_Solar_System_body "Small Solar System body").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons#cite_note-Johnston-2)
