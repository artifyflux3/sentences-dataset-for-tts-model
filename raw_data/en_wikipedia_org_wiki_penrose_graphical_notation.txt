From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Graphical notation for multilinear algebra calculations

[![Image 10](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/80/Matrix_product_state_obc_tikz.svg/250px-Matrix_product_state_obc_tikz.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Matrix_product_state_obc_tikz.svg)

Penrose graphical notation (tensor diagram notation) of a [matrix product state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_product_state "Matrix product state") of five particles

In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics") and [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics"), **Penrose graphical notation** or **tensor diagram notation** is a (usually handwritten) visual depiction of [multilinear functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multilinear_function "Multilinear function") or [tensors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor "Tensor") proposed by [Roger Penrose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roger_Penrose "Roger Penrose") in 1971.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Penrose_graphical_notation#cite_note-1) A diagram in the notation consists of several shapes linked together by lines.

The notation widely appears in modern [quantum theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics"), particularly in [matrix product states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_product_state "Matrix product state") and [quantum circuits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_circuit "Quantum circuit"). In particular, [categorical quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Categorical_quantum_mechanics "Categorical quantum mechanics") (which includes [ZX-calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ZX-calculus "ZX-calculus")) is a fully comprehensive reformulation of quantum theory in terms of Penrose diagrams.

The notation has been studied extensively by [Predrag Cvitanović](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Predrag_Cvitanovi%C4%87 "Predrag Cvitanović"), who used it, along with [Feynman's diagrams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feynman_diagram "Feynman diagram") and other related notations in developing "birdtracks", a group-theoretical diagram to classify the [classical Lie groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_Lie_groups "Classical Lie groups").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Penrose_graphical_notation#cite_note-2) Penrose's notation has also been generalized using [representation theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Representation_theory "Representation theory") to [spin networks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_network "Spin network") in physics, and with the presence of [matrix groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_group "Matrix group") to [trace diagrams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trace_diagram "Trace diagram") in [linear algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_algebra "Linear algebra").

Interpretations
---------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Penrose_graphical_notation&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Interpretations")]

### Multilinear algebra

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Penrose_graphical_notation&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Multilinear algebra")]

In the language of [multilinear algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multilinear_algebra "Multilinear algebra"), each shape represents a [multilinear function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multilinear_function "Multilinear function"). The lines attached to shapes represent the inputs or outputs of a function, and attaching shapes together in some way is essentially the [composition of functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Composition_of_functions "Composition of functions").

### Tensors

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Penrose_graphical_notation&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Tensors")]

In the language of [tensor algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor "Tensor"), a particular tensor is associated with a particular shape with many lines projecting upwards and downwards, corresponding to [abstract](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abstract_index_notation "Abstract index notation")[upper and lower](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covariance_and_contravariance_of_vectors "Covariance and contravariance of vectors") indices of tensors respectively. Connecting lines between two shapes corresponds to [contraction of indices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_contraction "Tensor contraction"). One advantage of this [notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_notation "Mathematical notation") is that one does not have to invent new letters for new indices. This notation is also explicitly [basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basis_(linear_algebra) "Basis (linear algebra)")-independent.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Penrose_graphical_notation#cite_note-3)

### Matrices

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Penrose_graphical_notation&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Matrices")]

Each shape represents a matrix, and [tensor multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_product "Tensor product") is done horizontally, and [matrix multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_multiplication "Matrix multiplication") is done vertically.

---------------------------------

### Metric tensor

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Penrose_graphical_notation&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Metric tensor")]

The [metric tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_tensor "Metric tensor") is represented by a U-shaped loop or an upside-down U-shaped loop, depending on the type of tensor that is used.

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Penrose_graphical_notation&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Covariant derivative")]

Tensor manipulation
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Penrose_graphical_notation&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: Tensor manipulation")]

The diagrammatic notation is useful in manipulating tensor algebra. It usually involves a few simple "[identities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Identity_(mathematics) "Identity (mathematics)")" of tensor manipulations.

For example, ε a...c ε a...c=n!{\displaystyle \varepsilon _{a...c}\varepsilon ^{a...c}=n!}![Image 45: {\displaystyle \varepsilon _{a...c}\varepsilon ^{a...c}=n!}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a3cdfdf42179ca8d6a8ff6ab8c7503403dd28388), where _n_ is the number of dimensions, is a common "identity".
