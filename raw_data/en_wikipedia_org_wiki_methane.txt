**Methane** ( [_METH-ayn_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key "Help:Pronunciation respelling key"), [_MEE-thayn_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key "Help:Pronunciation respelling key")) is a [chemical compound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_compound "Chemical compound") with the [chemical formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_formula "Chemical formula")CH 4 (one [carbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon "Carbon") atom bonded to four [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen") atoms). It is a [group-14 hydride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group-14_hydride "Group-14 hydride"), the simplest [alkane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkane "Alkane"), and the main constituent of [natural gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_gas "Natural gas"). The abundance of methane on [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth "Earth") makes it an economically attractive [fuel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fuel "Fuel"), although capturing and storing it is difficult because it is a [gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas "Gas") at [standard temperature and pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_temperature_and_pressure "Standard temperature and pressure"). In the Earth's atmosphere methane is transparent to visible light but absorbs [infrared radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infrared_radiation "Infrared radiation"), acting as a [greenhouse gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenhouse_gas "Greenhouse gas"). Methane is an [organic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_chemistry "Organic chemistry")[compound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_compound "Organic compound"), and among the simplest of organic compounds. Methane is also a [hydrocarbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrocarbon "Hydrocarbon").

Naturally occurring methane is found both below ground and under the [seafloor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seafloor "Seafloor") and is formed by both geological and biological processes. The largest [reservoir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reservoir "Reservoir") of methane is under the seafloor in the form of [methane clathrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane_clathrate "Methane clathrate"). When methane reaches the surface and the [atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth "Atmosphere of Earth"), it is known as [atmospheric methane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmospheric_methane "Atmospheric methane").[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-Khalil-1999-10)

The Earth's atmospheric methane concentration [has increased](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane_emissions "Methane emissions") by about 160% since 1750, with the overwhelming percentage caused by human activity.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-UNEP_2022-11) It accounted for 20% of the total [radiative forcing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiative_forcing "Radiative forcing") from all of the long-lived and globally mixed [greenhouse gases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenhouse_gas "Greenhouse gas"), according to the 2021 [Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change "Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change") report.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-Technical_summary2-12) Strong, rapid and sustained reductions in methane emissions could limit near-term warming and improve air quality by reducing global surface ozone.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-13)

Methane has also been detected on other planets, including [Mars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mars "Mars"), which has implications for [astrobiology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology "Astrobiology") research.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-Etiope-2013-14)

Properties and bonding
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Methane&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Properties and bonding")]

[![Image 19](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Covalent.svg/250px-Covalent.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Covalent.svg)

[Covalently bonded](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covalently_bonded "Covalently bonded") hydrogen and carbon in a molecule of methane.

Methane is a [tetrahedral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedral_molecular_geometry "Tetrahedral molecular geometry") molecule with four equivalent [C–H bonds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon%E2%80%93hydrogen_bond "Carbon–hydrogen bond"). Its [electronic structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_configuration "Electron configuration") is described by four bonding molecular orbitals (MOs) resulting from the overlap of the valence orbitals on [C](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon "Carbon") and [H](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen"). The lowest-energy MO is the result of the overlap of the [2s orbital](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_orbital "Atomic orbital") on carbon with the in-phase combination of the 1s orbitals on the four hydrogen atoms. Above this energy level is a triply degenerate set of MOs that involve overlap of the 2p orbitals on carbon with various linear combinations of the 1s orbitals on hydrogen. The resulting "three-over-one" bonding scheme is consistent with photoelectron spectroscopic measurements.

Methane is an odorless, colourless and transparent gas at [standard temperature and pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_temperature_and_pressure "Standard temperature and pressure").[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-15) It does absorb visible light, especially at the red end of the spectrum, due to [overtone bands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Overtone_band "Overtone band"), but the effect is only noticeable if the light path is very long. This is what gives [Uranus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uranus "Uranus") and [Neptune](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune "Neptune") their blue or bluish-green colors, as light passes through their atmospheres containing methane and is then scattered back out.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-16)

The familiar smell of natural gas as used in homes is achieved by the addition of an [odorant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Odorizer "Odorizer"), usually blends containing [_tert_-butylthiol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tert-butylthiol "Tert-butylthiol"), as a safety measure. Methane has a boiling point of −161.5[°C](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_Celsius "Degree Celsius") at a pressure of one [atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_(unit) "Atmosphere (unit)").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-crc-3) As a gas, it is [flammable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flammable "Flammable") over a range of concentrations (5.4%–17%) in air at [standard pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_pressure "Standard pressure").

Solid methane exists in several [modifications](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polymorphism_(materials_science) "Polymorphism (materials science)"), of which nine are known.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-BiniPratesi-17) Cooling methane at normal pressure results in the formation of methane I. This substance crystallizes in the cubic system ([space group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_group "Space group") Fm 3 m). The positions of the hydrogen atoms are not fixed in methane I, i.e. methane molecules may rotate freely. Therefore, it is a [plastic crystal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plastic_crystal "Plastic crystal").[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-18)

The primary chemical reactions of methane are [combustion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Combustion "Combustion"), [steam reforming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steam_reforming "Steam reforming") to [syngas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syngas "Syngas"), and [halogenation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halogenation "Halogenation"). In general, methane reactions are difficult to control.

### Selective oxidation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Methane&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Selective oxidation")]

Partial [oxidation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redox "Redox") of methane to [methanol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol "Methanol") ([C](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon "Carbon")[H](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen")3[O](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen "Oxygen")[H](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen")), a more convenient, liquid fuel, is challenging because the reaction typically progresses all the way to [carbon dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_dioxide "Carbon dioxide") and [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water "Water") even with an insufficient supply of [oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen "Oxygen"). The [enzyme](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enzyme "Enzyme")[methane monooxygenase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane_monooxygenase "Methane monooxygenase") produces methanol from methane, but cannot be used for industrial-scale reactions.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-19) Some homogeneously [catalyzed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catalysis "Catalysis") systems and heterogeneous systems have been developed, but all have significant drawbacks. These generally operate by generating protected products which are shielded from overoxidation. Examples include the [Catalytica system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane_functionalization#The_Catalytica_system "Methane functionalization"), copper [zeolites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zeolite "Zeolite"), and iron zeolites stabilizing the [alpha-oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha-oxygen "Alpha-oxygen") active site.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-20)

One group of [bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacteria "Bacteria") catalyze methane oxidation with [nitrite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrite "Nitrite") as the [oxidant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxidizing_agent "Oxidizing agent") in the absence of [oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen "Oxygen"), giving rise to the so-called [anaerobic oxidation of methane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anaerobic_oxidation_of_methane "Anaerobic oxidation of methane").[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-21)

### Acid–base reactions

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Methane&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Acid–base reactions")]

Like other [hydrocarbons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrocarbon "Hydrocarbon"), methane is an extremely [weak acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weak_acid "Weak acid"). Its [p _K_ a](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PKa "PKa") in [DMSO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimethyl_sulfoxide "Dimethyl sulfoxide") is estimated to be 56.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-22) It cannot be [deprotonated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deprotonation "Deprotonation") in solution, but the [conjugate base](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conjugate_base "Conjugate base") is known in forms such as [methyllithium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methyllithium "Methyllithium").

A variety of [positive ions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cation "Cation") derived from methane have been observed, mostly as unstable species in low-pressure gas mixtures. These include [methenium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methenium "Methenium") or methyl cation CH+3, methane cation CH+4, and [methanium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanium "Methanium") or protonated methane CH+5. Some of these have been [detected in outer space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_interstellar_and_circumstellar_molecules "List of interstellar and circumstellar molecules"). Methanium can also be produced as diluted solutions from methane with [superacids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superacid "Superacid"). [Cations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cation "Cation") with higher charge, such as CH 2+6 and CH 3+7, have been studied theoretically and conjectured to be stable.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-Rasul-23)

Despite the [strength](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bond_strength "Bond strength") of its C–H bonds, there is intense interest in [catalysts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catalysts "Catalysts") that facilitate [C–H bond activation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C%E2%80%93H_bond_activation "C–H bond activation") in methane (and other lower numbered [alkanes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkanes "Alkanes")).[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-24)

[![Image 20: A young woman holding a flame in her hands](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/The_fire_within_her.jpg/250px-The_fire_within_her.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:The_fire_within_her.jpg)

Methane bubbles can be burned on a wet hand without injury.

Methane's [heat of combustion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat_of_combustion "Heat of combustion") is 55.5 MJ/kg.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-25)[Combustion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Combustion "Combustion") of methane is a multiple step reaction summarized as follows:

CH 4 + 2 O 2 → CO 2 + 2 H 2 O Δ _H_ = −802 [kJ](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kilojoule "Kilojoule")/[mol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mole_(unit) "Mole (unit)"), at standard conditions (for water vapor, Δ _H_ = −891 [kJ](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kilojoule "Kilojoule")/[mol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mole_(unit) "Mole (unit)") for liquid water)
[Peters four-step chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peters_four-step_chemistry "Peters four-step chemistry") is a systematically reduced four-step chemistry that explains the burning of methane.

### Methane radical reactions

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Methane&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Methane radical reactions")]

Given appropriate conditions, methane reacts with [halogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halogen "Halogen")[radicals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radical_(chemistry) "Radical (chemistry)") as follows:

•X + CH 4 → HX + •CH 3•CH 3 + X 2 → CH 3 X + •X
where X is a [halogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halogen "Halogen"): [fluorine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluorine "Fluorine") (F), [chlorine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chlorine "Chlorine") (Cl), [bromine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bromine "Bromine") (Br), or [iodine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iodine "Iodine") (I). This mechanism for this process is called [free radical halogenation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_radical_halogenation "Free radical halogenation"). It is initiated when [UV light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UV_light "UV light") or some other [radical initiator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radical_initiator "Radical initiator") (like [peroxides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peroxides "Peroxides")) produces a halogen [atom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atom "Atom"). A two-step [chain reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chain_reaction "Chain reaction") ensues in which the halogen atom abstracts a hydrogen atom from a methane molecule, resulting in the formation of a [hydrogen halide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_halide "Hydrogen halide") molecule and a [methyl radical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methyl_radical "Methyl radical") (•CH 3). The methyl radical then reacts with a molecule of the halogen to form a molecule of the halomethane, with a new halogen atom as byproduct.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-26) Similar reactions can occur on the halogenated product, leading to replacement of additional hydrogen atoms by halogen atoms with [dihalomethane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihalomethane "Dihalomethane"), [trihalomethane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trihalomethane "Trihalomethane"), and ultimately, [tetrahalomethane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahalomethane "Tetrahalomethane") structures, depending upon reaction conditions and the halogen-to-methane ratio.

This reaction is commonly used with chlorine to produce [dichloromethane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dichloromethane "Dichloromethane") and [chloroform](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chloroform "Chloroform") via [chloromethane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chloromethane "Chloromethane"). [Carbon tetrachloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_tetrachloride "Carbon tetrachloride") can be made with excess chlorine.

Methane may be transported as a refrigerated liquid (liquefied natural gas, or [LNG](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LNG "LNG")). While leaks from a refrigerated liquid container are initially heavier than air due to the increased density of the cold gas, the gas at ambient temperature is lighter than air. [Gas pipelines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pipeline_transport "Pipeline transport") distribute large amounts of natural gas, of which methane is the principal component.

Methane is used as a [fuel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fuel "Fuel") for ovens, homes, water heaters, kilns, automobiles,[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-27)[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-Cornell-2008-28) rockets, turbines, etc.

As the major constituent of [natural gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_gas "Natural gas"), methane is important for [electricity generation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electricity_generation "Electricity generation") by burning it as a fuel in a [gas turbine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas_turbine "Gas turbine") or [steam generator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boiler_(power_generation) "Boiler (power generation)"). Compared to other [hydrocarbon fuels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fossil_fuel "Fossil fuel"), methane produces less [carbon dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_dioxide "Carbon dioxide") for each unit of heat released. At about 891 kJ/mol, methane's [heat of combustion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat_of_combustion "Heat of combustion") is lower than that of any other hydrocarbon, but the ratio of the heat of combustion (891 kJ/mol) to the molecular mass (16.0 g/mol, of which 12.0 g/mol is carbon) shows that methane, being the simplest hydrocarbon, produces more heat per mass unit (55.7 kJ/g) than other complex hydrocarbons. In many areas with a dense enough population, methane is piped into homes and businesses for [heating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heating "Heating"), cooking, and industrial uses. In this context it is usually known as [natural gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_gas "Natural gas"), which is considered to have an energy content of 39 [megajoules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Megajoule "Megajoule") per cubic meter, or 1,000 [BTU](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_thermal_unit "British thermal unit") per [standard cubic foot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_cubic_foot "Standard cubic foot"). [Liquefied natural gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liquefied_natural_gas "Liquefied natural gas") (LNG) is predominantly methane (CH 4) converted into liquid form for ease of storage or transport.

Refined **liquid methane** as well as LNG is [used as](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liquid_rocket_propellants#Bipropellants "Liquid rocket propellants") a [rocket fuel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rocket_fuel "Rocket fuel"),[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-aiaa2004-29) when combined with [liquid oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liquid_oxygen "Liquid oxygen"), as in the [TQ-12](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TQ-12 "TQ-12"), [BE-4](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BE-4 "BE-4"), [Raptor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raptor_(rocket_engine_family) "Raptor (rocket engine family)"), [YF-215](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/YF-215 "YF-215"), and [Aeon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativity_Space#Rocket_engines "Relativity Space") engines.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-30) Due to the similarities between methane and LNG such engines are commonly grouped together under the term _methalox_.

As a [liquid rocket propellant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liquid_rocket_propellant "Liquid rocket propellant"), a methane/[liquid oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liquid_oxygen "Liquid oxygen") combination offers the advantage over [kerosene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kerosene "Kerosene")/[liquid oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liquid_oxygen "Liquid oxygen") combination, or kerolox, of producing small exhaust molecules, reducing coking or deposition of [soot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soot "Soot") on engine components. Methane is easier to store than hydrogen due to its higher boiling point and density, as well as its lack of [hydrogen embrittlement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_embrittlement "Hydrogen embrittlement").[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-pbt20140219-31)[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-nsf20140307-32) The lower [molecular weight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_weight "Molecular weight") of the exhaust also increases the fraction of the heat energy which is in the form of kinetic energy available for propulsion, increasing the [specific impulse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Specific_impulse "Specific impulse") of the rocket. Compared to [liquid hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liquid_hydrogen "Liquid hydrogen"), the [specific energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Specific_energy "Specific energy") of methane is lower but this disadvantage is offset by methane's greater density and temperature range, allowing for smaller and lighter tankage for a given fuel mass. Liquid methane has a temperature range (91–112 K) nearly compatible with [liquid oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liquid_oxygen "Liquid oxygen") (54–90 K). The fuel currently sees use in operational launch vehicles such as [Zhuque-2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zhuque-2 "Zhuque-2"), [Vulcan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vulcan_Centaur "Vulcan Centaur") and [New Glenn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Glenn "New Glenn") as well as in-development launchers such as [Starship](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SpaceX_Starship "SpaceX Starship"), [Neutron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rocket_Lab_Neutron "Rocket Lab Neutron"), [Terran R](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terran_R "Terran R"), [Nova](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stoke_Space_Nova "Stoke Space Nova"), and [Long March 9](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Long_March_9 "Long March 9").[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-33)

[Natural gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_gas "Natural gas"), which is mostly composed of methane, is used to produce hydrogen gas on an industrial scale. [Steam methane reforming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steam_reforming "Steam reforming") (SMR), or simply known as steam reforming, is the standard industrial method of producing commercial bulk hydrogen gas. More than 50 million metric tons are produced annually worldwide (2013), principally from the SMR of natural gas.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-34) Much of this hydrogen is used in [petroleum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petroleum "Petroleum")[refineries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Refinery "Refinery"), in the production of chemicals and in food processing. Very large quantities of hydrogen are used in the [industrial synthesis of ammonia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia_production "Ammonia production").

At high temperatures (700–1100°C) and in the presence of a [metal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metal "Metal")-based [catalyst](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catalyst "Catalyst") ([nickel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nickel "Nickel")), steam reacts with methane to yield a mixture of [CO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide "Carbon monoxide") and [H 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihydrogen "Dihydrogen"), known as "water gas" or "[syngas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syngas "Syngas")":

CH 4 + H 2 O ⇌ CO + 3 H 2
This reaction is strongly [endothermic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endothermic "Endothermic") (consumes heat, Δ _H_ r = 206 kJ/mol). Additional hydrogen is obtained by the reaction of [CO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide "Carbon monoxide") with water via the [water-gas shift reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water-gas_shift_reaction "Water-gas shift reaction"):

CO + H 2 O ⇌ CO 2 + H 2
This reaction is mildly [exothermic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exothermic "Exothermic") (produces heat, Δ _H_ r = −41 kJ/mol).

Methane is also subjected to free-radical [chlorination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chlorination_reaction "Chlorination reaction") in the production of chloromethanes, although [methanol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol "Methanol") is a more typical precursor.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-Ullmann-35)

Hydrogen can also be produced via the direct decomposition of methane, also known as methane [pyrolysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrolysis "Pyrolysis"), which, unlike steam reforming, produces no [greenhouse gases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenhouse_gas "Greenhouse gas") (GHG). The heat needed for the reaction can also be GHG emission free, e.g. from concentrated sunlight, renewable electricity, or burning some of the produced hydrogen. If the methane is from [biogas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biogas "Biogas") then the process can be a [carbon sink](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_sink "Carbon sink"). Temperatures in excess of 1200°C are required to break the bonds of methane to produce hydrogen gas and solid carbon.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-36)

However, through the use of a suitable catalyst the reaction temperature can be reduced to between 550 and 900°C depending on the chosen catalyst. Dozens of catalysts have been tested, including unsupported and supported metal catalysts, carbonaceous and metal-carbon catalysts.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-37)

The reaction is moderately endothermic as shown in the reaction equation below.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-38)

CH 4(g) → C(s) + 2 H 2(g)(ΔH° = 74.8 [kJ/mol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joule_per_mole "Joule per mole"))
As a [refrigerant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Refrigerant "Refrigerant"), methane has the [ASHRAE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ASHRAE "ASHRAE") designation [R-50](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_refrigerants#:~:text=R-50 "List of refrigerants").

[![Image 21](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Global_Methane_Budget_2017.jpg/250px-Global_Methane_Budget_2017.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Global_Methane_Budget_2017.jpg)

Global methane budget (2017). Shows natural sources and sinks (green), anthropogenic sources (orange), and mixed natural and anthropogenic sources (hatched orange-green for 'biomass and biofuel burning').

Methane can be generated through geological, biological or industrial routes.

[![Image 22](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Origins_of_Biotic_and_Abiotic_Methane.jpg/500px-Origins_of_Biotic_and_Abiotic_Methane.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Origins_of_Biotic_and_Abiotic_Methane.jpg)

Abiotic sources of methane[_[example needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:AUDIENCE "Wikipedia:AUDIENCE")_] have been found in more than 20 countries and in several deep ocean regions so far.

The two main routes for geological methane generation are (i) organic (thermally generated, or thermogenic) and (ii) inorganic ([abiotic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abiotic_component "Abiotic component")).[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-Etiope-2013-14) Thermogenic methane occurs due to the breakup of organic matter at elevated temperatures and pressures in deep sedimentary [strata](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stratum "Stratum"). Most methane in sedimentary basins is thermogenic; therefore, thermogenic methane is the most important source of natural gas. Thermogenic methane components are typically considered to be relic (from an earlier time). Generally, formation of thermogenic methane (at depth) can occur through organic matter breakup, or organic synthesis. Both ways can involve microorganisms ([methanogenesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanogenesis "Methanogenesis")), but may also occur inorganically. The processes involved can also consume methane, with and without microorganisms.

The more important source of methane at depth (crystalline bedrock) is abiotic. Abiotic means that methane is created from inorganic compounds, without biological activity, either through magmatic processes[_[example needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:AUDIENCE "Wikipedia:AUDIENCE")_] or via water-rock reactions that occur at low temperatures and pressures, like [serpentinization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Serpentinite "Serpentinite").[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-Kiet%C3%A4v%C3%A4inen-2015-39)[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-Cramer-2005-40)

Most of Earth's methane is [biogenic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biogenic_substance "Biogenic substance") and is produced by [methanogenesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanogenesis "Methanogenesis"),[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-Lessner-2009-41)[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-Thiel-2018-42) a form of anaerobic respiration only known to be conducted by some members of the domain [Archaea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archaea "Archaea").[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-Dean-2018-2-43) Methanogens occur in [landfills](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Landfill "Landfill") and [soils](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soil_gas "Soil gas"),[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-Serrano-Silva-2014-44)[ruminants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ruminants "Ruminants") (for example, [cattle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cattle "Cattle")),[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-Sirohi-2010-45) the guts of termites, and the [anoxic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anoxic_waters "Anoxic waters") sediments below the seafloor and the bottom of lakes.

This multistep process is used by these microorganisms for energy. The net reaction of methanogenesis is:

CO 2 + 4 H 2 → CH 4 + 2 H 2 O
The final step in the process is catalyzed by the enzyme [methyl coenzyme M reductase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coenzyme-B_sulfoethylthiotransferase "Coenzyme-B sulfoethylthiotransferase") (MCR).[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-46)

Testing Australian sheep for exhaled methane production (2001), [CSIRO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CSIRO "CSIRO")

Wetlands are the largest natural sources of methane to the atmosphere,[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-47) accounting for approximately 20 – 30% of atmospheric methane.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-:1-48) Climate change is increasing the amount of methane released from wetlands due to increased temperatures and altered rainfall patterns. This phenomenon is called _wetland methane feedback_.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-:0-49)

[Rice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rice "Rice") cultivation generates as much as 12% of total global methane emissions due to the long-term flooding of rice fields.[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-50)

Ruminants such as cattle belch out methane, accounting for about 22% of the U.S. annual methane emissions to the atmosphere.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-Inventory_of_U.S._Greenhouse_Gas_Emissions_and_Sinks:_1990-20142-51) One study reported that the livestock sector in general (primarily cattle, chickens, and pigs) produces 37% of all human-induced methane.[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-FAO-2006-52) A 2013 study estimated that livestock accounted for 44% of human-induced methane and about 15% of human-induced greenhouse gas emissions.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-Tempio-2013-53) Many efforts are underway to reduce livestock methane production, such as medical treatments and dietary adjustments,[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-Roach-2002-54)[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-55) and to trap the gas to use its combustion energy.[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-Silverman-2007-56)

Most of the subseafloor is [anoxic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anoxic_waters "Anoxic waters") because oxygen is removed by [aerobic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aerobic_respiration "Aerobic respiration") microorganisms within the first few centimeters of the [sediment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seafloor_sediment "Seafloor sediment"). Below the oxygen-replete seafloor, [methanogens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanogen "Methanogen") produce methane that is either used by other organisms or becomes trapped in [gas hydrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clathrate_hydrate "Clathrate hydrate").[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-Dean-2018-2-43) These other organisms that utilize methane for energy are known as [methanotrophs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanotroph "Methanotroph") ('methane-eating'), and are the main reason why little methane generated at depth reaches the sea surface.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-Dean-2018-2-43) Consortia of Archaea and Bacteria have been found to oxidize methane via [anaerobic oxidation of methane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anaerobic_oxidation_of_methane "Anaerobic oxidation of methane") (AOM); the organisms responsible for this are anaerobic [methanotrophic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanotroph "Methanotroph") Archaea (ANME) and [sulfate-reducing bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfate-reducing_microorganism "Sulfate-reducing microorganism") (SRB).[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-Knittel-2019-57)

[![Image 25](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Diagram_of_sustainable_methane_fuel_production-en.svg/500px-Diagram_of_sustainable_methane_fuel_production-en.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Diagram_of_sustainable_methane_fuel_production-en.svg)

This diagram shows a method for producing methane sustainably. See: [electrolysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrolysis "Electrolysis"), [Sabatier reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sabatier_reaction "Sabatier reaction")

Given its cheap abundance in natural gas, there is little incentive to produce methane industrially. Methane can be produced by [hydrogenating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogenation "Hydrogenation") carbon dioxide through the [Sabatier process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sabatier_process "Sabatier process"). Methane is also a side product of the hydrogenation of carbon monoxide in the [Fischer–Tropsch process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fischer%E2%80%93Tropsch_process "Fischer–Tropsch process"), which is practiced on a large scale to produce longer-chain molecules than methane.

An example of large-scale coal-to-methane gasification is the [Great Plains Synfuels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Plains_Synfuels "Great Plains Synfuels") plant, started in 1984 in [Beulah, North Dakota](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beulah,_North_Dakota "Beulah, North Dakota") as a way to develop abundant local resources of low-grade [lignite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lignite "Lignite"), a resource that is otherwise difficult to transport for its weight, [ash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coal_assay#Ash "Coal assay") content, low calorific value and propensity to [spontaneous combustion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spontaneous_combustion "Spontaneous combustion") during storage and transport. A number of similar plants exist around the world, although mostly these plants are targeted towards the production of long chain alkanes for use as [gasoline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gasoline "Gasoline"), [diesel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diesel_fuel "Diesel fuel"), or feedstock to other processes.

[Power to methane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_to_gas#Power_to_methane "Power to gas") is a technology that uses [electrical power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electricity "Electricity") to produce hydrogen from water by [electrolysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrolysis "Electrolysis") and uses the [Sabatier reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sabatier_reaction "Sabatier reaction") to combine hydrogen with [carbon dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_dioxide "Carbon dioxide") to produce methane.

#### Laboratory synthesis

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Methane&action=edit&section=19 "Edit section: Laboratory synthesis")]

Methane can be produced by [protonation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protonation "Protonation") of [methyl lithium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methyl_lithium "Methyl lithium") or a methyl [Grignard reagent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grignard_reagent "Grignard reagent") such as [methylmagnesium chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methylmagnesium_chloride "Methylmagnesium chloride"). It can also be made from anhydrous [sodium acetate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_acetate "Sodium acetate") and dry [sodium hydroxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide "Sodium hydroxide"), mixed and heated above 300°C (with [sodium carbonate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_carbonate "Sodium carbonate") as byproduct).[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] In practice, a requirement for pure methane can easily be fulfilled by steel gas bottle from standard gas suppliers.

Methane is the major component of natural gas, about 87% by volume. The major source of methane is extraction from geological deposits known as [natural gas fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_gas_fields "Natural gas fields"), with [coal seam gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coal_seam_gas "Coal seam gas") extraction becoming a major source (see [coal bed methane extraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coal_bed_methane_extraction "Coal bed methane extraction"), a method for extracting methane from a [coal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coal "Coal") deposit, while [enhanced coal bed methane recovery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enhanced_coal_bed_methane_recovery "Enhanced coal bed methane recovery") is a method of recovering methane from non-mineable coal seams). It is associated with other [hydrocarbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrocarbon "Hydrocarbon") fuels, and sometimes accompanied by [helium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium "Helium") and [nitrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen "Nitrogen"). Methane is produced at shallow levels (low pressure) by [anaerobic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anaerobic_organism "Anaerobic organism")[decay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decomposition "Decomposition") of [organic matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_matter "Organic matter") and reworked methane from deep under the Earth's surface. In general, the [sediments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sediment "Sediment") that generate natural gas are buried deeper and at higher temperatures than those that contain [oil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petroleum "Petroleum").

Methane is generally transported in bulk by [pipeline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pipeline_transport "Pipeline transport") in its natural gas form, or by LNG carriers in its liquefied form; few countries transport it by truck.

Atmospheric methane and climate change
--------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Methane&action=edit&section=21 "Edit section: Atmospheric methane and climate change")]

[![Image 26](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/CH4_mm.png/330px-CH4_mm.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:CH4_mm.png)

Methane (CH 4) measured by the Advanced Global Atmospheric Gases Experiment ([AGAGE](http://agage.mit.edu/)) in the lower atmosphere ([troposphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Troposphere "Troposphere")) at stations around the world. Abundances are given as pollution free monthly mean mole fractions in [parts-per-billion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parts-per_notation "Parts-per notation").

Methane is an important [greenhouse gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenhouse_gas "Greenhouse gas"), responsible for around 30% of the rise in global temperatures since the industrial revolution.[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-58)

Methane has a [global warming potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_warming_potential "Global warming potential") (GWP) of 29.8 ± 11 compared to CO 2 (potential of 1) over a 100-year period, and 82.5 ± 25.8 over a 20-year period.[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-ar6-59) This means that, for example, a [leak](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fugitive_gas_emissions "Fugitive gas emissions") of one tonne of methane is equivalent to emitting 82.5 tonnes of carbon dioxide. Burning methane and producing carbon dioxide also reduces the greenhouse gas impact compared to simply venting methane to the atmosphere.

[![Image 27](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Sources_of_methane_emissions%2C_2021.jpg/250px-Sources_of_methane_emissions%2C_2021.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Sources_of_methane_emissions,_2021.jpg)

Sources of global methane emissions

As methane is gradually converted into carbon dioxide (and water) in the atmosphere, these values include the climate forcing from the carbon dioxide produced from methane over these timescales.

Annual global methane emissions are currently approximately 580 Mt,[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-60) 40% of which is from natural sources and the remaining 60% originating from human activity, known as anthropogenic emissions. The largest anthropogenic source is [agriculture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agriculture "Agriculture"), responsible for around one quarter of emissions, closely followed by the [energy sector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_industry "Energy industry"), which includes emissions from coal, oil, natural gas and biofuels.[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-61)

[Historic methane concentrations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Keeling_Curve "Keeling Curve") in the world's atmosphere have ranged between 300 and 400 nmol/mol during glacial periods commonly known as [ice ages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ice_ages "Ice ages"), and between 600 and 700 nmol/mol during the warm [interglacial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interglacial "Interglacial") periods. A 2012 NASA website said the oceans were a potential important source of Arctic methane,[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-NASA_2012-62) but more recent studies associate increasing methane levels as caused by human activity.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-UNEP_2022-11)

Global monitoring of atmospheric methane concentrations began in the 1980s.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-UNEP_2022-11) The Earth's atmospheric methane concentration has increased 160% since preindustrial levels in the mid-18th century.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-UNEP_2022-11) In 2013, atmospheric methane accounted for 20% of the total [radiative forcing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiative_forcing "Radiative forcing") from all of the long-lived and globally mixed greenhouse gases.[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-63) Between 2011 and 2019 the annual average increase of methane in the atmosphere was 1866 ppb.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-Technical_summary2-12) From 2015 to 2019 sharp rises in levels of atmospheric methane were recorded.[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-64)[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-65)

In 2019, the atmospheric methane concentration was higher than at any time in the last 800,000 years. As stated in the [AR6](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IPCC_Sixth_Assessment_Report "IPCC Sixth Assessment Report") of the [IPCC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change "Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change"), "Since 1750, increases in CO 2 (47%) and CH 4 (156%) concentrations far exceed, and increases in N 2 O (23%) are similar to, the natural multi-millennial changes between glacial and interglacial periods over at least the past 800,000 years (very high confidence)".[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-Technical_summary2-12)[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-66)[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-IPCC_2013-67)

In February 2020, it was reported that [fugitive emissions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fugitive_emissions "Fugitive emissions") and [gas venting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas_venting "Gas venting") from the [fossil fuel industry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fossil_fuel_industry "Fossil fuel industry") may have been significantly underestimated.[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-Hmiel_2020-68)[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-Harvey_2020-69) The largest annual increase occurred in 2021 with the overwhelming percentage caused by human activity.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-UNEP_2022-11)

Climate change can increase atmospheric methane levels by increasing methane production in natural ecosystems, forming a [climate change feedback](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change_feedback "Climate change feedback").[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-Dean-2018-2-43)[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-70) Another explanation for the rise in methane emissions could be a slowdown of the chemical reaction that removes methane from the atmosphere.[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-71)

Over 100 countries have signed the [Global Methane Pledge](https://www.globalmethanepledge.org/), launched in 2021, promising to cut their methane emissions by 30% by 2030.[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-72) This could avoid 0.2°C of warming globally by 2050, although there have been calls for higher commitments in order to reach this target.[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-73) The [International Energy Agency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Energy_Agency "International Energy Agency")'s 2022 report states "the most cost-effective opportunities for methane abatement are in the energy sector, especially in oil and gas operations".[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-74)

[Methane clathrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane_clathrate "Methane clathrate") (also known as methane hydrates) are solid cages of water molecules that trap single molecules of methane. Significant reservoirs of methane clathrates have been found in arctic permafrost and along [continental margins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continental_margin "Continental margin") beneath the [ocean floor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seabed "Seabed") within the [gas clathrate stability zone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas_hydrate_stability_zone "Gas hydrate stability zone"), located at high pressures (1 to 100 MPa; lower end requires lower temperature) and low temperatures (< 15°C; upper end requires higher pressure).[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-Bohrmann-2006-75) Methane clathrates can form from biogenic methane, thermogenic methane, or a mix of the two. These deposits are both a potential source of methane fuel as well as a potential contributor to global warming.[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-Miller-2007-76)[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-Dean-2018-77) The global mass of carbon stored in gas clathrates is still uncertain and has been estimated as high as 12,500 [Gt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gigaton "Gigaton") carbon and as low as 500 Gt carbon.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-:0-49) The estimate has declined over time with a most recent estimate of ≈1800 Gt carbon.[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-Ruppel-2017-78) A large part of this uncertainty is due to our knowledge gap in sources and sinks of methane and the distribution of methane clathrates at the global scale. For example, a source of methane was discovered relatively recently in an [ultraslow spreading ridge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mid-ocean_ridge "Mid-ocean ridge") in the Arctic.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-:1-48) Some climate models suggest that today's methane emission regime from the ocean floor is potentially similar to that during the period of the [Paleocene–Eocene Thermal Maximum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paleocene%E2%80%93Eocene_Thermal_Maximum "Paleocene–Eocene Thermal Maximum") ([PETM](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PETM "PETM")) around 55.5 million years ago, although there are no data indicating that methane from clathrate dissociation currently reaches the atmosphere.[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-Ruppel-2017-78)[Arctic methane release](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arctic_methane_release "Arctic methane release") from [permafrost](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permafrost "Permafrost") and seafloor methane clathrates is a potential consequence and further cause of [global warming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_warming "Global warming"); this is known as the [clathrate gun hypothesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clathrate_gun_hypothesis "Clathrate gun hypothesis").[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-NSF-2010-79)[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-Connor-2011-80)[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-NSIDC-2012-81)[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-UN_Environment-82) Data from 2016 indicate that Arctic permafrost thaws faster than predicted.[[82]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-83)

Public safety and the environment
---------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Methane&action=edit&section=23 "Edit section: Public safety and the environment")]

[![Image 28](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Abatement_potential_of_policy_measures%2C_2021.jpg/250px-Abatement_potential_of_policy_measures%2C_2021.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Abatement_potential_of_policy_measures,_2021.jpg)

An International Energy Agency graphic showing the potential of various emission reduction policies for addressing global methane emissions.

Methane "degrades air quality and adversely impacts human health, agricultural yields, and ecosystem productivity".[[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-Schindell_2012-84)

Methane is extremely flammable and may form [explosive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Explosive "Explosive") mixtures with air. Methane gas explosions are responsible for many deadly mining disasters.[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-85) A methane gas explosion was the cause of the [Upper Big Branch coal mine disaster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Upper_Big_Branch_Mine_disaster "Upper Big Branch Mine disaster") in [West Virginia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/West_Virginia "West Virginia") on April 5, 2010, killing 29.[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-86)[Natural gas accidental release](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_gas_accidental_release "Natural gas accidental release") has also been a major focus in the field of [safety engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Safety_engineering "Safety engineering"), due to past accidental releases that concluded in the formation of [jet fire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jet_fire "Jet fire") disasters.[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-87)[[87]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-88)

The 2015–2016 [methane gas leak in Aliso Canyon, California](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aliso_Canyon_gas_leak "Aliso Canyon gas leak") was considered to be the worst in terms of its environmental effect in American history.[[88]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-LATimes_2016-89)[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-90)[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-91) It was also described as more damaging to the environment than [Deepwater Horizon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deepwater_Horizon_oil_spill "Deepwater Horizon oil spill")'s leak in the Gulf of Mexico.[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-92)

In May 2023 _[The Guardian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The\_Guardian "The Guardian")_ published a report blaming [Turkmenistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turkmenistan "Turkmenistan") as the worst in the world for methane _super emitting_. The data collected by Kayrros researchers indicate that two large Turkmen fossil fuel fields [leaked](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane_leak "Methane leak") 2.6 million and 1.8 million [metric tonnes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_tonne "Metric tonne") of methane in 2022 alone, pumping the [CO 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_dioxide "Carbon dioxide") equivalent of 366 million tonnes into the atmosphere, surpassing the annual CO 2 emissions of the [United Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Kingdom "United Kingdom").[[92]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-93)

Methane is also an [asphyxiant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asphyxiant_gas "Asphyxiant gas") if the oxygen concentration is reduced to below about 16% by displacement, as most people can [tolerate a reduction from 21% to 16% without ill effects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cabin_pressurization#Need_for_cabin_pressurization "Cabin pressurization"). The concentration of methane at which asphyxiation risk becomes significant is much higher than the 5–15% concentration in a flammable or explosive mixture. Methane off-gas can penetrate the interiors of buildings near [landfills](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Landfills "Landfills") and expose occupants to significant levels of methane. Some buildings have specially engineered recovery systems below their basements to actively capture this gas and vent it away from the building.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

### Interstellar medium

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Methane&action=edit&section=25 "Edit section: Interstellar medium")]

[![Image 29](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/6c/Wiki_letter_w.svg/44px-Wiki_letter_w.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Wiki_letter_w.svg)This section **is missing information** about where extraterrestrial abiotic methane comes from (Big Bang? supernova? mineral deposits reacting?). Please expand the section to include this information. Further details may exist on the [talk page](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Talk:Methane "Talk:Methane")._(June 2024)_

Methane is abundant in many parts of the Solar System and potentially could be harvested on the surface of another Solar System body (in particular, using [methane production from local materials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/In_situ_resource_utilization "In situ resource utilization") found on [Mars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mars "Mars")[[93]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-zubrin20121215-94) or [Titan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Titan_(moon) "Titan (moon)")), providing fuel for a return journey.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-aiaa2004-29)[[94]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-nasa20070504-95)

[Negative methane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Negative_methane "Negative methane"), the [negative ion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ion "Ion") of methane, is also known to exist in [interstellar space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_space "Interstellar space").[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-96) Its mechanism of formation is not fully understood.

Methane has been detected on all planets of the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System") and most of the larger moons.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] With the possible exception of [Mars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life_on_Mars "Life on Mars"), it is believed to have come from [abiotic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abiogenic_petroleum_origin "Abiogenic petroleum origin") processes.[[96]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-NYT-20121102-97)[[97]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-98)

[![Image 30](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/PIA19088-MarsCuriosityRover-MethaneSource-20141216.png/250px-PIA19088-MarsCuriosityRover-MethaneSource-20141216.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:PIA19088-MarsCuriosityRover-MethaneSource-20141216.png)

[Methane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Mars#Methane "Atmosphere of Mars") (CH 4) on Mars– potential sources and sinks

The [_Curiosity_ rover](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curiosity_(rover) "Curiosity (rover)") has documented seasonal fluctuations of [atmospheric methane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Mars "Atmosphere of Mars") levels on Mars. These fluctuations peaked at the end of the Martian summer at 0.6 parts per billion.[[98]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-NASA-20180607-99)[[99]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-NASA-20180607vid-100)[[100]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-SPC-20180607-101)[[101]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-NYT-20180607-102)[[102]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-SCI-20180607-103)[[103]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-SCI-20180608a-104)[[104]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-SCI-20180608b-105)[[105]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-SCI-20180608c-106)

Methane has been proposed as a possible [rocket propellant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rocket_propellant "Rocket propellant") on future [Mars missions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_mission_to_Mars "Human mission to Mars") due in part to the possibility of synthesizing it on the planet by [in situ resource utilization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/In_situ_resource_utilization#Mars "In situ resource utilization").[[106]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-107) An adaptation of the [Sabatier methanation reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sabatier_reaction "Sabatier reaction") may be used with a mixed catalyst bed and a [reverse water-gas shift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water-gas_shift_reaction#Reverse_water-gas_shift "Water-gas shift reaction") in a single reactor to produce methane and [oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen "Oxygen") from the raw materials available on Mars, utilizing water from the [Martian subsoil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Martian_soil "Martian soil") and [carbon dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_dioxide "Carbon dioxide") in the [Martian atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Mars "Atmosphere of Mars").[[93]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-zubrin20121215-94)

Methane could be produced by a non-biological process called [serpentinization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Serpentinite "Serpentinite")[[b]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-serpentinization-108) involving water, carbon dioxide, and the mineral [olivine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Olivine "Olivine"), which is known to be common on Mars.[[107]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-olivine-109)

[![Image 31](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/PIA22481-SaturnMoon-Titan-Lakes-20170911.jpg/250px-PIA22481-SaturnMoon-Titan-Lakes-20170911.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:PIA22481-SaturnMoon-Titan-Lakes-20170911.jpg)

Titan lakes (September 11, 2017)

Methane has been detected in vast abundance on [Titan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Titan_(moon) "Titan (moon)"), the largest moon of [Saturn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saturn "Saturn"). It comprises a significant portion of [its atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Titan "Atmosphere of Titan") and also exists in a liquid form on its surface, where it comprises the majority of the liquid in Titan's [vast lakes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lakes_of_Titan "Lakes of Titan") of hydrocarbons, the [second largest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ligeia_Mare "Ligeia Mare") of which is believed to be almost pure methane in composition.[[108]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-MethaneJPL2016-110)

The presence of stable lakes of liquid methane on Titan, as well as the surface of Titan being highly chemically active and rich in organic compounds, has led scientists to consider the [possibility of life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life_on_Titan#Hydrocarbons_as_solvents "Life on Titan") existing within Titan's lakes, using methane as a solvent in the place of water for Earth-based life[[109]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-methanesolvent-111) and using hydrogen in the atmosphere to derive energy with [acetylene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene "Acetylene").[[110]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-mckay-112)

[![Image 32](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/ETH-BIB-Volta%2C_Alessandro_%281745-1827%29-Portrait-Portr_02303.tif/lossy-page1-250px-ETH-BIB-Volta%2C_Alessandro_%281745-1827%29-Portrait-Portr_02303.tif.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:ETH-BIB-Volta,_Alessandro_(1745-1827)-Portrait-Portr_02303.tif)

[Alessandro Volta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alessandro_Volta "Alessandro Volta")

The discovery of methane is credited to [Italian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italian_people "Italian people") physicist [Alessandro Volta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alessandro_Volta "Alessandro Volta"), who characterized numerous properties including its [flammability limit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flammability_limit "Flammability limit") and origin from decaying organic matter.[[111]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-Volta-113)

Volta was initially motivated by reports of inflammable air present in marshes by his friend Father Carlo Giuseppe Campi. While on a fishing trip to [Lake Maggiore](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lake_Maggiore "Lake Maggiore") straddling [Italy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italy "Italy") and [Switzerland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Switzerland "Switzerland") in November 1776, he noticed the presence of bubbles in the nearby marshes and decided to investigate. Volta collected the gas rising from the marsh and demonstrated that the gas was inflammable.[[111]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-Volta-113)[[112]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-114)

Volta notes similar observations of inflammable air were present previously in scientific literature, including a letter written by [Benjamin Franklin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benjamin_Franklin "Benjamin Franklin").[[113]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-115)

Following the [Felling mine disaster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Felling_mine_disasters#1812_disaster "Felling mine disasters") of 1812 in which 92 men perished, Sir [Humphry Davy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Humphry_Davy "Humphry Davy") established that the feared [firedamp](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Firedamp "Firedamp") was in fact largely methane.[[114]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-116)

The name "methane" was coined in 1866 by the German chemist [August Wilhelm von Hofmann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/August_Wilhelm_von_Hofmann "August Wilhelm von Hofmann").[[115]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-117)[[116]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-118) The name was derived from [methanol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#History "Methanol").

Etymologically, the word _methane_ is coined from the chemical suffix "_[-ane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/-ane "-ane")_", which denotes substances belonging to the alkane family; and the word _methyl_, which is derived from the German _Methyl_ (1840) or directly from the French _méthyle_, which is a back-formation from the French _méthylène_ (corresponding to English "methylene"), the root of which was coined by [Jean-Baptiste Dumas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_Dumas "Jean-Baptiste Dumas") and [Eugène Péligot](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Eug%C3%A8ne_P%C3%A9ligot&action=edit&redlink=1 "Eugène Péligot (page does not exist)") in 1834 from the Greek μέθυ _méthy_ (wine) (related to English "mead") and ὕλη _hýlē_ (meaning "wood"). The radical is named after this because it was first detected in [methanol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol "Methanol"), an alcohol first isolated by distillation of wood. The chemical suffix _-ane_ is from the coordinating chemical suffix _-ine_ which is from Latin feminine suffix _-ina_ which is applied to represent abstracts. The coordination of "-ane", "[-ene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/-ene "-ene")", "[-one](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/-one "-one")", etc. was proposed in 1866 by German chemist [August Wilhelm von Hofmann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/August_Wilhelm_von_Hofmann "August Wilhelm von Hofmann").[[117]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-119)

The abbreviation CH 4-C can mean the mass of carbon contained in a mass of methane, and the mass of methane is always 1.33 times the mass of CH 4-C.[[118]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-methane-c-120)[[119]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-epa-ag-121)CH 4-C can also mean the methane-carbon ratio, which is 1.33 by mass.[[120]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-mcratio-122) Methane at scales of the atmosphere is commonly measured in teragrams (Tg CH 4) or millions of metric tons (MMT CH 4), which mean the same thing.[[121]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane#cite_note-epa90-20-123) Other standard units are also used, such as nanomole (nmol, one billionth of a mole), [mole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mole_(unit) "Mole (unit)") (mol), [kilogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kilogram "Kilogram"), and [gram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gram "Gram").
