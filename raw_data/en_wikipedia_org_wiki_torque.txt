In [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics") and [mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanics "Mechanics"), **torque** is the [rotational](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational "Rotational") analogue of linear [force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Force "Force").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torque#cite_note-1) It is also referred to as the **moment of force** (also abbreviated to **moment**). The symbol for torque is typically ![Image 4: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {\tau }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/beb1359149db88a28c86f0a3030894b71610a224), the lowercase [Greek letter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_alphabet "Greek alphabet")_[tau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tau "Tau")_. When being referred to as [moment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_(physics) "Moment (physics)") of force, it is commonly denoted by M. Just as a linear force is a push or a pull applied to a body, a torque can be thought of as a twist applied to an object with respect to a chosen point; for example, driving a [screw](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Screw "Screw") uses torque to force it into an object, which is applied by the [screwdriver](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Screwdriver "Screwdriver") rotating around its [axis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axis_of_rotation "Axis of rotation") to the [drives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_screw_drives "List of screw drives") on the head.

Historical terminology
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Torque&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Historical terminology")]

The term _torque_ (from [Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin "Latin")_[torquēre](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/torquere#Latin "wikt:torquere")_, 'to twist') is said to have been suggested by [James Thomson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Thomson_(engineer) "James Thomson (engineer)") and appeared in print in April, 1884.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torque#cite_note-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torque#cite_note-thompson1-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torque#cite_note-oed1933-4) Usage is attested the same year by [Silvanus P. Thompson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silvanus_P._Thompson "Silvanus P. Thompson") in the first edition of _Dynamo-Electric Machinery_.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torque#cite_note-oed1933-4) Thompson describes his usage of the term as follows:[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torque#cite_note-thompson1-3)

> Just as the Newtonian definition of _[force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Force "Force")_ is that which produces or tends to produce [motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Motion "Motion") (along a line), so _torque_ may be defined as that which produces or tends to produce _[torsion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torsion\_(mechanics) "Torsion (mechanics)")_ (around an axis). It is better to use a term which treats this action as a single definite entity than to use terms like "[couple](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Couple_(mechanics) "Couple (mechanics)")" and "[moment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_(physics) "Moment (physics)")", which suggest more complex ideas. The single notion of a twist applied to turn a shaft is better than the more complex notion of applying a linear force (or a pair of forces) with a certain leverage.

Today, torque is referred to using different vocabulary depending on geographical location and field of study. This article follows the definition used in US physics in its usage of the word _torque_.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torque#cite_note-Hendricks-5)

In the UK and in US [mechanical engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanical_engineering "Mechanical engineering"), torque is referred to as _moment of force_, usually shortened to _moment_.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torque#cite_note-Kane-6) This terminology can be traced back to at least 1811 in [Siméon Denis Poisson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sim%C3%A9on_Denis_Poisson "Siméon Denis Poisson")'s _Traité de mécanique_.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torque#cite_note-7) An English translation of Poisson's work appears in 1842.

Definition and relation to other physical quantities
----------------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Torque&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Definition and relation to other physical quantities")]

[![Image 5](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Torque%2C_position%2C_and_force.svg/250px-Torque%2C_position%2C_and_force.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Torque,_position,_and_force.svg)

A particle is located at position **r** relative to its axis of rotation. When a force **F** is applied to the particle, only the [perpendicular component](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangential_and_normal_components "Tangential and normal components")**F**⊥ produces a torque. This torque _**τ**_ = **r** × **F** has magnitude _τ_ = |**r**| |**F**⊥| = |**r**| |**F**| sin _θ_ and is directed outward from the page.

A force applied [perpendicularly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular "Perpendicular") to a lever multiplied by its distance from the [lever's fulcrum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lever "Lever") (the length of the **lever arm**) is its torque. Therefore, torque is defined as the product of the magnitude of the perpendicular component of the force and the distance of the [line of action](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_of_action "Line of action") of a force from the point around which it is being determined. In three dimensions, the torque is a [pseudovector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudovector "Pseudovector"); for [point particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_particles "Point particles"), it is given by the [cross product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product "Cross product") of the [displacement vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Displacement_(geometry) "Displacement (geometry)") and the [force vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Force "Force"). The direction of the torque can be determined by using the [right-hand grip rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right-hand_rule#Amp%C3%A8re's_right-hand_grip_rule "Right-hand rule"): if the fingers of the right hand are curled from the direction of the lever arm to the direction of the force, then the thumb points in the direction of the torque.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torque#cite_note-8) It follows that the **torque vector** is perpendicular to both the _[position](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Position\_(geometry) "Position (geometry)")_ and _force_ vectors, and defines the plane in which the two vectors lie. The resulting torque vector direction is determined by the [right-hand rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right-hand_rule "Right-hand rule"). Therefore any force directed parallel to the particle's position vector does not produce a torque.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torque#cite_note-halliday_184-85-9)[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torque#cite_note-10) The magnitude of torque applied to a [rigid body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rigid_body "Rigid body") depends on three quantities: the force applied, the **lever arm vector**[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torque#cite_note-11) connecting the point about which the torque is being measured to the point of force application, and the angle between the force and lever arm vectors. In symbols:

![Image 6: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {\tau }}=\mathbf {r} \times \mathbf {F} \implies \tau =rF_{\perp }=rF\sin \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cef96724dd731be6ba7ff9506bde7b9b25d96a6a)

where

The [SI unit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SI_units "SI units") for torque is the [newton-metre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton-metre "Newton-metre") (N⋅m). For more on the units of torque, see _[§Units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torque#Units)_.

where **L** is the angular momentum vector and _t_ is time. For the motion of a point particle,

![Image 8: {\displaystyle \mathbf {L} =I{\boldsymbol {\omega }},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/afb18c91ec4284e8e4142e3b7fea1b3cb54baf40)

It follows that

#### Proof of the equivalence of definitions

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Torque&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Proof of the equivalence of definitions")]

The definition of angular momentum for a single point particle is: ![Image 14: {\displaystyle \mathbf {L} =\mathbf {r} \times \mathbf {p} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/22179a9b81408e19de312b5fbfec30ff62cefa4a) where **p** is the particle's [linear momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_momentum "Linear momentum") and **r** is the position vector from the origin. The time-derivative of this is:

![Image 15: {\displaystyle {\frac {\mathrm {d} \mathbf {L} }{\mathrm {d} t}}=\mathbf {r} \times {\frac {\mathrm {d} \mathbf {p} }{\mathrm {d} t}}+{\frac {\mathrm {d} \mathbf {r} }{\mathrm {d} t}}\times \mathbf {p} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5e42c08b67078ba87951167815a73a90ee397271)

This result can easily be proven by splitting the vectors into components and applying the [product rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Product_rule "Product rule"). But because the rate of change of linear momentum is force ![Image 16: {\textstyle \mathbf {F} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/52b52fc142fd1b3f80ad9ff08f11c64562cd041d) and the rate of change of position is velocity ![Image 17: {\textstyle \mathbf {v} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5a3231f52246f12ebad1bdba6b54136c7a581396),

![Image 18: {\displaystyle {\frac {\mathrm {d} \mathbf {L} }{\mathrm {d} t}}=\mathbf {r} \times \mathbf {F} +\mathbf {v} \times \mathbf {p} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fec449c4d6e1c9025876d1c37cef0e25eee936e4)

The cross product of momentum ![Image 19: {\displaystyle \mathbf {p} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dd73e3862cb92b016721b8c492eadb4e8a577527) with its associated velocity ![Image 20: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35c1866e359fbfd2e0f606c725ba5cc37a5195d6) is zero because velocity and momentum are parallel, so the second term vanishes. Therefore, torque on a particle is _equal_ to the [first derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative#Notation_for_differentiation "Derivative") of its angular momentum with respect to time. If multiple forces are applied, according [Newton's second law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_second_law "Newton's second law") it follows that![Image 21: {\displaystyle {\frac {\mathrm {d} \mathbf {L} }{\mathrm {d} t}}=\mathbf {r} \times \mathbf {F} _{\mathrm {net} }={\boldsymbol {\tau }}_{\mathrm {net} }.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b19bcdc94f36841245e56b11e842ce78fcbd17d3)

This is a general proof for point particles, but it can be generalized to a system of point particles by applying the above proof to each of the point particles and then summing over all the point particles. Similarly, the proof can be generalized to a continuous mass by applying the above proof to each point within the mass, and then [integrating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral_calculus "Integral calculus") over the entire mass.

### Derivatives of torque

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Torque&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Derivatives of torque")]

In [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics"), **rotatum** is the derivative of torque with respect to [time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time "Time")[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torque#cite_note-12)

> ![Image 22: {\displaystyle \mathbf {P} ={\frac {\mathrm {d} {\boldsymbol {\tau }}}{\mathrm {d} t}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0799089cfde0b618bd0636e7876325f16a126584)

where **τ** is torque.

This word is derived from the [Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin "Latin") word _rotātus_ meaning 'to rotate'. The term _rotatum_ is not universally recognized but is commonly used. Using the cross product definition of torque, an alternative expression for rotatum is:

Because the rate of change of force is yank ![Image 24: {\textstyle \mathbf {Y} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a38eeaacff400d0c2dab8de25d97a4e3bc288e6f) and the rate of change of position is velocity ![Image 25: {\textstyle \mathbf {v} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5a3231f52246f12ebad1bdba6b54136c7a581396), the expression can be further simplified to:

### Relationship with power and energy

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Torque&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Relationship with power and energy")]

The law of [conservation of energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservation_of_energy "Conservation of energy") can also be used to understand torque. If a [force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Force "Force") is allowed to act through a distance, it is doing [mechanical work](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanical_work "Mechanical work"). Similarly, if torque is allowed to act through an [angular displacement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_displacement "Angular displacement"), it is doing work. Mathematically, for rotation about a fixed axis through the [center of mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Center_of_mass "Center of mass"), the work _W_ can be expressed as

[![Image 27: {\displaystyle W=\int _{\theta _{1}}^{\theta _{2}}\tau \ \mathrm {d} \theta ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c14fe7f128dede145aefc5387bd106c6ad2f9798)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:MathWikibase&qid=Q104145165)

where _τ_ is torque, and _θ_ 1 and _θ_ 2 represent (respectively) the initial and final [angular positions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_position "Angular position") of the body.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torque#cite_note-kleppner_267-68-13)

It follows from the [work–energy principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Work%E2%80%93energy_principle "Work–energy principle") that _W_ also represents the change in the [rotational kinetic energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_energy "Rotational energy")_E_ r of the body, given by

[![Image 28: {\displaystyle E_{\mathrm {r} }={\tfrac {1}{2}}I\omega ^{2},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/13cee50b145f623d79b04ddc98c343d2a04685a5)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:MathWikibase&qid=Q104145205)

where _I_ is the [moment of inertia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_of_inertia "Moment of inertia") of the body and _ω_ is its [angular speed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_speed "Angular speed").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torque#cite_note-kleppner_267-68-13)

[Power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_(physics) "Power (physics)") is the work per unit [time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time "Time"), given by

[![Image 29: {\displaystyle P={\boldsymbol {\tau }}\cdot {\boldsymbol {\omega }},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/abbe6d5570b3818abf4a6a6d31aee4639e242ada)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:MathWikibase&qid=Q104145185)

where _P_ is power, _**τ**_ is torque, _**ω**_ is the [angular velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_velocity "Angular velocity"), and ![Image 30: {\displaystyle \cdot }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ba2c023bad1bd39ed49080f729cbf26bc448c9ba) represents the [scalar product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_product "Scalar product").

Algebraically, the equation may be rearranged to compute torque for a given angular speed and power output. The power injected by the torque depends only on the instantaneous angular speed – not on whether the angular speed increases, decreases, or remains constant while the torque is being applied (this is equivalent to the linear case where the power injected by a force depends only on the instantaneous speed – not on the resulting acceleration, if any).

However, the infinitesimal linear displacement ![Image 34: {\displaystyle \mathrm {d} \mathbf {s} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0b53965c8f86e8a184dff634475ea13b8fa6c722) is related to a corresponding angular displacement ![Image 35: {\displaystyle \mathrm {d} {\boldsymbol {\theta }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/637422c2dfcb36c2f35c3ea90ce64dff69e679b0) and the radius vector ![Image 36: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eca0f46511c4c986c48b254073732c0bd98ae0c1) as

Principle of moments
--------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Torque&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Principle of moments")]

The principle of moments, also known as [Varignon's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Varignon%27s_theorem_(mechanics) "Varignon's theorem (mechanics)") (not to be confused with the [geometrical theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Varignon%27s_theorem "Varignon's theorem") of the same name) states that the resultant torques due to several forces applied to about a point is equal to the sum of the contributing torques:

![Image 43: {\displaystyle \tau =\mathbf {r} _{1}\times \mathbf {F} _{1}+\mathbf {r} _{2}\times \mathbf {F} _{2}+\ldots +\mathbf {r} _{N}\times \mathbf {F} _{N}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b834b639074ee057bdfeaee18ece2ed8a2b0ada2)

From this it follows that the torques resulting from N number of forces acting around a pivot on an object are balanced when

![Image 44: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} _{1}\times \mathbf {F} _{1}+\mathbf {r} _{2}\times \mathbf {F} _{2}+\ldots +\mathbf {r} _{N}\times \mathbf {F} _{N}=\mathbf {0} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a3a6e29f3e394deb549a7e9d606c0e3e4f9dcfb2)

Torque has the [dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension_(physics) "Dimension (physics)") of force times [distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distance "Distance"), symbolically T−2 L 2 M and those fundamental dimensions are the same as that for [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy "Energy") or [work](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanical_work "Mechanical work"). Official [SI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SI "SI") literature indicates _[newton-metre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton-metre "Newton-metre")_, is properly denoted N⋅m, as the unit for torque; although this is [dimensionally equivalent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimensional_analysis "Dimensional analysis") to the [joule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joule "Joule"), which is not used for torque.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torque#cite_note-BIPM222-14)[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torque#cite_note-BIPM_5.1-15) In the case of torque, the unit is assigned to a [vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_(geometric) "Vector (geometric)"), whereas for [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy "Energy"), it is assigned to a [scalar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_(physics) "Scalar (physics)"). This means that the dimensional equivalence of the newton-metre and the joule may be applied in the former but not in the latter case. This problem is addressed in [orientational analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimensional_analysis#Siano's_extension:_orientational_analysis "Dimensional analysis"), which treats the radian as a base unit rather than as a dimensionless unit.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torque#cite_note-16)

The traditional imperial units for torque are the [pound foot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pound-foot_(torque) "Pound-foot (torque)") (lbf-ft), or, for small values, the pound inch (lbf-in). In the US, torque is most commonly referred to as the **foot-pound** (denoted as either lb-ft or ft-lb) and the **inch-pound** (denoted as in-lb).[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torque#cite_note-GRAINGER-17)[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torque#cite_note-18) Practitioners depend on context and the hyphen in the abbreviation to know that these refer to torque and not to energy or moment of mass (as the symbolism ft-lb would properly imply).

### Conversion to other units

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Torque&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Conversion to other units")]

A conversion factor may be necessary when using different units of power or torque. For example, if [rotational speed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_speed "Rotational speed") (unit: revolution per minute or second) is used in place of angular speed (unit: radian per second), we must multiply by 2 π radians per revolution. In the following formulas, _P_ is power, _τ_ is torque, and _ν_ ([Greek letter nu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nu_(letter) "Nu (letter)")) is rotational speed.

![Image 45: {\displaystyle P=\tau \cdot 2\pi \cdot \nu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/99b5f82875cedc4eb4b6dc69b961318e5bc74368)

Showing units:

![Image 46: {\displaystyle P_{\rm {W}}=\tau _{\rm {N{\cdot }m}}\cdot 2\pi _{\rm {rad/rev}}\cdot \nu _{\rm {rev/s}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7a9e63a5781ac24119181d50279b26846bc291a5)

Dividing by 60 seconds per minute gives us the following.

![Image 47: {\displaystyle P_{\rm {W}}={\frac {\tau _{\rm {N{\cdot }m}}\cdot 2\pi _{\rm {rad/rev}}\cdot \nu _{\rm {rev/min}}}{\rm {60~s/min}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4682af3b5744161b1c9bd32bd3fbcd92207ef614)

where rotational speed is in revolutions per minute (rpm, rev/min).

Some people (e.g., American automotive engineers) use [horsepower](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horsepower "Horsepower") (mechanical) for power, foot-pounds (lbf⋅ft) for torque and rpm for rotational speed. This results in the formula changing to:

![Image 48: {\displaystyle P_{\rm {hp}}={\frac {\tau _{\rm {lbf{\cdot }ft}}\cdot 2\pi _{\rm {rad/rev}}\cdot \nu _{\rm {rev/min}}}{33,000}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/607c14a22825198bf33b7101d71f168e2ae76f8e)

The constant below (in foot-pounds per minute) changes with the definition of the horsepower; for example, using metric horsepower, it becomes approximately 32,550.

The use of other units (e.g., [BTU](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BTU "BTU") per hour for power) would require a different custom conversion factor.

For a rotating object, the _linear distance_ covered at the [circumference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumference "Circumference") of rotation is the product of the radius with the angle covered. That is: linear distance = radius × angular distance. And by definition, linear distance = linear speed × time = radius × angular speed × time.

By the definition of torque: torque = radius × force. We can rearrange this to determine force = torque ÷ radius. These two values can be substituted into the definition of [power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_(physics) "Power (physics)"):

![Image 49: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}{\text{power}}&={\frac {{\text{force}}\cdot {\text{linear distance}}}{\text{time}}}\\[6pt]&={\frac {\left({\dfrac {\text{torque}}{r}}\right)\cdot (r\cdot {\text{angular speed}}\cdot t)}{t}}\\[6pt]&={\text{torque}}\cdot {\text{angular speed}}.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4382d186e4085de735546ad46847d852af843fcb)

The radius _r_ and time _t_ have dropped out of the equation. However, angular speed must be in radians per unit of time, by the assumed direct relationship between linear speed and angular speed at the beginning of the derivation. If the rotational speed is measured in revolutions per unit of time, the linear speed and distance are increased proportionately by 2 π in the above derivation to give:

![Image 50: {\displaystyle {\text{power}}={\text{torque}}\cdot 2\pi \cdot {\text{rotational speed}}.\,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e34e09ce77bfd6e0195060380356aa914f39b0df)

If torque is in newton-metres and rotational speed in revolutions per second, the above equation gives power in newton-metres per second or watts. If Imperial units are used, and if torque is in pounds-force feet and rotational speed in revolutions per minute, the above equation gives power in foot pounds-force per minute. The horsepower form of the equation is then derived by applying the conversion factor 33,000 ft⋅lbf/min per horsepower:

![Image 51: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}{\text{power}}&={\text{torque}}\cdot 2\pi \cdot {\text{rotational speed}}\cdot {\frac {{\text{ft}}{\cdot }{\text{lbf}}}{\text{min}}}\cdot {\frac {\text{horsepower}}{33,000\cdot {\frac {{\text{ft}}\cdot {\text{lbf}}}{\text{min}}}}}\\[6pt]&\approx {\frac {{\text{torque}}\cdot {\text{RPM}}}{5,252}}\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/71f6394786e06bd8fb53a08ea0161897cf0ed9f9)

because ![Image 52: {\displaystyle 5252.113122\approx {\frac {33,000}{2\pi }}.\,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/abb756648093641b7d55acff6dde7a7fa4263b94)

Special cases and other facts
-----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Torque&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Special cases and other facts")]

[![Image 53](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Moment_arm.svg/250px-Moment_arm.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Moment_arm.svg)

Moment arm diagram

A very useful special case, often given as the definition of torque in fields other than physics, is as follows:

![Image 54: {\displaystyle \tau =({\text{moment arm}})({\text{force}}).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9085dc12739b4bb014c8c6ae32b28ce60ff50bbd)

The construction of the "moment arm" is shown in the figure to the right, along with the vectors **r** and **F** mentioned above. The problem with this definition is that it does not give the direction of the torque but only the magnitude, and hence it is difficult to use in three-dimensional cases. If the force is perpendicular to the displacement vector **r**, the moment arm will be equal to the distance to the centre, and torque will be a maximum for the given force. The equation for the magnitude of a torque, arising from a perpendicular force:

![Image 55: {\displaystyle \tau =({\text{distance to centre}})({\text{force}}).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c44a1c33b47ba8654a5ff431466eb8c41db7bdb7)

For example, if a person places a force of 10 N at the terminal end of a wrench that is 0.5 m long (or a force of 10 N acting 0.5 m from the twist point of a wrench of any length), the torque will be 5 N⋅m – assuming that the person moves the wrench by applying force in the plane of movement and perpendicular to the wrench.

[![Image 56](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/PrecessionOfATop.svg/250px-PrecessionOfATop.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:PrecessionOfATop.svg)

The torque caused by the two opposing forces **F**g and −**F**g causes a change in the angular momentum **L** in the direction of that torque. This causes the top to [precess](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Precess "Precess").

For an object to be in [static equilibrium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Static_equilibrium "Static equilibrium"), not only must the sum of the forces be zero, but also the sum of the torques (moments) about any point. For a two-dimensional situation with horizontal and vertical forces, the sum of the forces requirement is two equations: Σ _H_ = 0 and Σ _V_ = 0, and the torque a third equation: Σ _τ_ = 0. That is, to solve [statically determinate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statically_determinate "Statically determinate") equilibrium problems in two-dimensions, three equations are used.

### Net force versus torque

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Torque&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: Net force versus torque")]

When the net force on the system is zero, the torque measured from any point in space is the same. For example, the torque on a current-carrying loop in a uniform magnetic field is the same regardless of the point of reference. If the net force ![Image 57: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/da18bef8c979f3548bb0d8976f5844012d7b8256) is not zero, and ![Image 58: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {\tau }}_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b41f8bd78622bdf06a1e00063a8204e8b88c535c) is the torque measured from ![Image 59: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} _{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c197b71f97673f419ea1a520594f9f7b7c2fe0ad), then the torque measured from ![Image 60: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} _{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e31052adfa9397ad639d996f70a9877eac35eb7b) is ![Image 61: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {\tau }}_{2}={\boldsymbol {\tau }}_{1}+(\mathbf {r} _{2}-\mathbf {r} _{1})\times \mathbf {F} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4c675ab0e68364a604183783388e1c341930fded)

[![Image 62](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Torque_Curve.svg/250px-Torque_Curve.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Torque_Curve.svg)

Torque curve of a motorcycle ("BMW K 1200 R 2005"). The horizontal axis shows the [rotational speed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_speed "Rotational speed") (in [rpm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Revolutions_per_minute "Revolutions per minute")) that the [crankshaft](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crankshaft "Crankshaft") is turning, and the vertical axis is the torque (in [newton-metres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton-metre "Newton-metre")) that the engine is capable of providing at that speed.

Torque forms part of the basic specification of an [engine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engine "Engine"): the [power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_(physics) "Power (physics)") output of an engine is expressed as its torque multiplied by the angular speed of the drive shaft. [Internal-combustion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internal_combustion "Internal combustion") engines produce useful torque only over a limited range of [rotational speeds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_speed "Rotational speed") (typically from around 1,000–6,000[rpm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rpm "Rpm") for a small car). One can measure the varying torque output over that range with a [dynamometer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynamometer "Dynamometer"), and show it as a torque curve. [Steam engines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steam_engine "Steam engine") and [electric motors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_motor "Electric motor") tend to produce maximum torque close to zero rpm, with the torque diminishing as rotational speed rises (due to increasing friction and other constraints). Reciprocating steam-engines and electric motors can start heavy loads from zero rpm without a [clutch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clutch "Clutch").

In practice, the relationship between power and torque can be observed in [bicycles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bicycle "Bicycle"): Bicycles are typically composed of two road wheels, front and rear gears (referred to as [sprockets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sprockets "Sprockets")) meshing with a [chain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bicycle_chain "Bicycle chain"), and a [derailleur mechanism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derailleur_gears "Derailleur gears") if the bicycle's transmission system allows multiple gear ratios to be used (i.e. [multi-speed bicycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Single-speed_bicycle#Advantages_and_disadvantages_versus_multi-speed_bicycles "Single-speed bicycle")), all of which attached to the [frame](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bicycle_frame "Bicycle frame"). A [cyclist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclist "Cyclist"), the person who rides the bicycle, provides the input power by turning pedals, thereby [cranking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crank_(mechanism) "Crank (mechanism)") the front sprocket (commonly referred to as [chainring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crankset#Chainring "Crankset")). The input power provided by the cyclist is equal to the product of angular speed (i.e. the number of pedal revolutions per minute times 2 _π_) and the torque at the [spindle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axle "Axle") of the bicycle's [crankset](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crankset "Crankset"). The bicycle's [drivetrain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bicycle_drivetrain_systems "Bicycle drivetrain systems") transmits the input power to the road [wheel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wheel "Wheel"), which in turn conveys the received power to the road as the output power of the bicycle. Depending on the [gear ratio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gear_ratio "Gear ratio") of the bicycle, a (torque, angular speed)input pair is converted to a (torque, angular speed)output pair. By using a larger rear gear, or by switching to a lower gear in multi-speed bicycles, [angular speed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_frequency "Angular frequency") of the road wheels is decreased while the torque is increased, product of which (i.e. power) does not change.

Torque can be multiplied via three methods: by locating the fulcrum such that the length of a lever is increased; by using a longer lever; or by the use of a speed-reducing gearset or [gear box](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gear_box "Gear box"). Such a mechanism multiplies torque, as rotation rate is reduced.
