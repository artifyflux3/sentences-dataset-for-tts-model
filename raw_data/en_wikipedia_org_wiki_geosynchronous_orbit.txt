[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b0/Geosynchronous_orbit.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Geosynchronous_orbit.gif)

Animation (not to scale) showing geosynchronous satellite orbiting the Earth

A **geosynchronous orbit** (sometimes abbreviated **GSO**) is an Earth-centered [orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbit "Orbit") with an [orbital period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_period "Orbital period") that matches [Earth's rotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_rotation "Earth's rotation") on its axis, 23 hours, 56 minutes, and 4 seconds (one [sidereal day](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sidereal_day "Sidereal day")). The synchronization of rotation and orbital period means that, for an observer on Earth's surface, an object in geosynchronous orbit returns to exactly the same position in the sky after a period of one sidereal day. Over the course of a day, the object's position in the sky may remain still or trace out a path, [typically in a figure-8 form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analemma#Of_geosynchronous_satellites "Analemma"), whose precise characteristics depend on the orbit's [inclination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_inclination "Orbital inclination") and [eccentricity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity "Orbital eccentricity"). A circular geosynchronous orbit has a constant altitude of 35,786 km (22,236 mi).[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-sdc20150426-1)

A special case of geosynchronous orbit is the [geostationary orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geostationary_orbit "Geostationary orbit") (often abbreviated _GEO_), which is a circular geosynchronous orbit in Earth's [equatorial plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equatorial_plane "Equatorial plane") with both inclination and eccentricity equal to 0. A satellite in a geostationary orbit remains in the same position in the sky to observers on the surface.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-sdc20150426-1)

[Communications satellites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communications_satellite "Communications satellite") are often given geostationary or close-to-geostationary orbits, so that the [satellite antennas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satellite_antenna "Satellite antenna") that communicate with them do not have to move but can be pointed permanently at the fixed location in the sky where the satellite appears.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-sdc20150426-1)

[![Image 3](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Clarke_sm.jpg/250px-Clarke_sm.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Clarke_sm.jpg)

The geosynchronous orbit was popularised by the science fiction author [Arthur C. Clarke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arthur_C._Clarke "Arthur C. Clarke"), and is thus sometimes called the Clarke Orbit.

In 1929, [Herman Potočnik](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herman_Poto%C4%8Dnik "Herman Potočnik") described both geosynchronous orbits in general and the special case of the geostationary Earth orbit in particular as useful orbits for [space stations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_station "Space station").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-2) The first appearance of a geosynchronous [orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbit "Orbit") in popular literature was in October 1942, in the first [Venus Equilateral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Venus_Equilateral "Venus Equilateral") story by [George O. Smith](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_O._Smith "George O. Smith"),[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-VE-3) but Smith did not go into details. British [science fiction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Science_fiction "Science fiction") author [Arthur C. Clarke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arthur_C._Clarke "Arthur C. Clarke") popularised and expanded the concept in a 1945 paper entitled _Extra-Terrestrial Relays – Can Rocket Stations Give Worldwide Radio Coverage?_, published in _[Wireless World](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wireless\_World "Wireless World")_ magazine. Clarke acknowledged the connection in his introduction to _The Complete Venus Equilateral_.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-VEintro-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-clarke-5) The orbit, which Clarke first described as useful for broadcast and relay communications satellites,[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-clarke-5) is sometimes called the Clarke Orbit.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-6) Similarly, the collection of artificial satellites in this orbit is known as the Clarke Belt.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-7)

[![Image 4](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Syncom_2_side.jpg/250px-Syncom_2_side.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Syncom_2_side.jpg)

[Syncom 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syncom#Syncom_2 "Syncom"): The first functional geosynchronous satellite

In technical terminology, the geosynchronous orbits are often referred to as geostationary if they are roughly over the equator, but the terms are used somewhat interchangeably.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-8)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-9) Specifically, **geosynchronous Earth orbit** (**GEO**) may be a synonym for _geosynchronous [equatorial orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Near-equatorial\_orbit "Near-equatorial orbit")_,[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-10) or _geostationary Earth orbit_.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-NASA2001-11)

The first geosynchronous satellite was designed by [Harold Rosen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harold_Rosen_(electrical_engineer) "Harold Rosen (electrical engineer)") while he was working at [Hughes Aircraft](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hughes_Aircraft "Hughes Aircraft") in 1959. Inspired by [Sputnik 1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sputnik_1 "Sputnik 1"), he wanted to use a geostationary (geosynchronous equatorial) satellite to globalise communications. Telecommunications between the US and Europe was then possible between just 136 people at a time, and reliant on [high frequency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High_frequency "High frequency") radios and an [undersea cable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Submarine_communications_cable "Submarine communications cable").[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-dm-12)

Conventional wisdom at the time was that it would require too much [rocket](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rocket "Rocket") power to place a satellite in a geosynchronous orbit and it would not survive long enough to justify the expense,[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-13) so early efforts were put towards constellations of satellites in [low](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit "Low Earth orbit") or [medium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medium_Earth_Orbit "Medium Earth Orbit") Earth orbit.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-lat-14) The first of these were the passive [Echo balloon satellites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Project_Echo "Project Echo") in 1960, followed by [Telstar 1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Telstar_1 "Telstar 1") in 1962.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-15) Although these projects had difficulties with signal strength and tracking that could be solved through geosynchronous satellites, the concept was seen as impractical, so Hughes often withheld funds and support.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-lat-14)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-dm-12)

By 1961, Rosen and his team had produced a cylindrical prototype with a diameter of 76 centimetres (30 in), height of 38 centimetres (15 in), weighing 11.3 kilograms (25 lb); it was light, and small, enough to be placed into orbit by then-available rocketry, was [spin stabilised](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin-stabilisation "Spin-stabilisation") and used dipole antennas producing a pancake-shaped waveform. [[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-16) In August 1961, they were contracted to begin building the working satellite.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-dm-12) They lost [Syncom 1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syncom#Syncom_1 "Syncom") to electronics failure, but Syncom 2 was successfully placed into a geosynchronous orbit in 1963. Although its [inclined orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inclined_orbit "Inclined orbit") still required moving antennas, it was able to relay TV transmissions, and allowed for US President [John F. Kennedy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_F._Kennedy "John F. Kennedy") to phone Nigerian prime minister [Abubakar Tafawa Balewa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abubakar_Tafawa_Balewa "Abubakar Tafawa Balewa") from a ship on August 23, 1963.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-lat-14)[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-17)

Today there are hundreds of geosynchronous satellites providing [remote sensing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Remote_sensing "Remote sensing"), navigation and communications.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-dm-12)[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-sdc20150426-1)

Although most populated land locations on the planet now have terrestrial communications facilities ([microwave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microwave "Microwave"), [fiber-optic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiber-optic "Fiber-optic")), which often have latency and bandwidth advantages, and telephone access covering 96% of the population and internet access 90% as of 2018,[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-18) some rural and remote areas in developed countries are still reliant on satellite communications.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-19)[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-20)

### Geostationary orbit

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Geosynchronous_orbit&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Geostationary orbit")]

[![Image 5](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Geostat.gif/250px-Geostat.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Geostat.gif)

The geostationary satellite (green) always remains above the same marked spot on the equator (brown).

A geostationary equatorial orbit (GEO) is a circular geosynchronous orbit in the plane of the Earth's equator with a radius of approximately 42,164 km (26,199 mi) (measured from the center of the Earth).[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-smad-21): 156  A satellite in such an orbit is at an altitude of approximately 35,786 km (22,236 mi) above mean sea level. It maintains the same position relative to the Earth's surface. If one could see a satellite in geostationary orbit, it would appear to hover at the same point in the sky, i.e., not exhibit [diurnal motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diurnal_motion "Diurnal motion"), while the Sun, Moon, and stars would traverse the skies behind it. Such orbits are useful for [telecommunications satellites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Telecommunications_satellite "Telecommunications satellite").[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-22)

A perfectly stable geostationary orbit is an ideal that can only be approximated. In practice the satellite drifts out of this orbit because of perturbations such as the [solar wind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_wind "Solar wind"), [radiation pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiation_pressure "Radiation pressure"), variations in the Earth's gravitational field, and the [gravitational](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity "Gravity") effect of the [Moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moon "Moon") and [Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun "Sun"), and thrusters are used to maintain the orbit in a process known as [station-keeping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_station-keeping "Orbital station-keeping").[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-smad-21): 156

Eventually, without the use of thrusters, the orbit will become inclined, oscillating between 0° and 15° every 55 years. At the end of the satellite's lifetime, when fuel approaches depletion, satellite operators may decide to omit these expensive manoeuvres to correct inclination and only control eccentricity. This prolongs the life-time of the satellite as it consumes less fuel over time, but the satellite can then only be used by ground antennas capable of following the N-S movement.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-smad-21): 156

Geostationary satellites will also tend to drift around one of two stable longitudes of 75° and 255° without station keeping.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-smad-21): 157

### Elliptical and inclined geosynchronous orbits

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Geosynchronous_orbit&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Elliptical and inclined geosynchronous orbits")]

[![Image 6](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Qzss-45-0.09.jpg/250px-Qzss-45-0.09.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Qzss-45-0.09.jpg)

A quasi-[zenith](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zenith "Zenith") satellite orbit

Many objects in geosynchronous orbits have eccentric and/or inclined orbits. Eccentricity makes the orbit elliptical and appear to oscillate E-W in the sky from the viewpoint of a ground station, while inclination tilts the orbit compared to the equator and makes it appear to oscillate N-S from a groundstation. These effects combine to form an [analemma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analemma "Analemma") (figure-8).[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-smad-21): 122

Satellites in elliptical/eccentric orbits must be tracked by steerable [ground stations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ground_station "Ground station").[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-smad-21): 122

The Tundra orbit is an eccentric geosynchronous orbit, which allows the satellite to spend most of its time dwelling over one high latitude location. It sits at an inclination of 63.4°, which is a [frozen orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frozen_orbit "Frozen orbit"), which reduces the need for [stationkeeping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_station-keeping "Orbital station-keeping").[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-scs-23) At least two satellites are needed to provide continuous coverage over an area.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-jenkin-24) It was used by the [Sirius XM Satellite Radio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sirius_XM_Satellite_Radio "Sirius XM Satellite Radio") to improve signal strength in the northern US and Canada.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-Sirius_Launch-25)

The [Quasi-Zenith Satellite System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasi-Zenith_Satellite_System "Quasi-Zenith Satellite System") (QZSS) is a four-satellite system that operates in a geosynchronous orbit at an inclination of 42° and a 0.075 eccentricity.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-26) Each satellite dwells over [Japan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japan "Japan"), allowing signals to reach receivers in [urban canyons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urban_canyons "Urban canyons"), then passes quickly over Australia.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-27)

An example of a transition from Geostationary Transfer Orbit (GTO) to Geosynchronous Orbit (GSO):

[EchoStar XVII](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/EchoStar_XVII "EchoStar XVII")**·**

[Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth "Earth").

Geosynchronous satellites are launched to the east into a prograde orbit that matches the rotation rate of the equator. The smallest inclination that a satellite can be launched into is that of the launch site's latitude, so launching the satellite from close to the equator limits the amount of [inclination change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_inclination_change "Orbital inclination change") needed later.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-conf-28) Additionally, launching from close to the equator allows the speed of the Earth's rotation to give the satellite a boost. A launch site should have water or deserts to the east, so any failed rockets do not fall on a populated area.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-29)

Most [launch vehicles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Launch_vehicle "Launch vehicle") place geosynchronous satellites directly into a [geosynchronous transfer orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_transfer_orbit "Geosynchronous transfer orbit") (GTO), an elliptical orbit with an [apogee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apsis "Apsis") at GSO height and a low [perigee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apsis "Apsis"). On-board satellite propulsion is then used to raise the perigee, circularise and reach GSO.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-conf-28)[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-30)

Once in a viable geostationary orbit, spacecraft can change their longitudinal position by adjusting their semi-major axis such that the new period is shorter or longer than a sidereal day, in order to effect an apparent "drift" Eastward or Westward, respectively. Once at the desired longitude, the spacecraft's period is restored to geosynchronous.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-satsig-repo-gso-31)

A [statite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statite "Statite") is a hypothetical satellite that uses [radiation pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiation_pressure#Solar_radiation_pressure "Radiation pressure") from the Sun against a [solar sail](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_sail "Solar sail") to modify its orbit.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-st-32)

It would hold its location over the dark side of the Earth at a latitude of approximately 30 degrees. It would return to the same spot in the sky every 24 hours from an Earth-based viewer's perspective, so be functionally similar to a geosynchronous orbit.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-st-32)[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-33)

A further form of geosynchronous orbit is the theoretical [space elevator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_elevator "Space elevator"). If a mass orbiting above the geostationary belt is tethered to the earth’s surface, and the mass is accelerated to maintain an orbital period equal to one sidereal day, then since the orbit now requires more downward force than is supplied by gravity alone the tether will become tensioned by the extra centripetal force required, and this tension will keep the tether structure stable as a crawler carries objects up/down along it.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-34)

[![Image 9: Earth from space, surrounded by small white dots](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Debris-GEO1280.jpg/250px-Debris-GEO1280.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Debris-GEO1280.jpg)

A computer-generated image of space debris. Two debris fields are shown: around geosynchronous space and low Earth orbit.

Geosynchronous satellites require some [station-keeping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_station-keeping "Orbital station-keeping") in order to remain in position, and once they run out of thruster fuel and are no longer useful they are moved into a higher [graveyard orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graveyard_orbit "Graveyard orbit"). It is not feasible to deorbit geosynchronous satellites, for to do so would take far more fuel than would be used by slightly elevating the orbit; and atmospheric drag is negligible, giving GSOs lifetimes of thousands of years.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-35)

The retirement process is becoming increasingly regulated and satellites must have a 90% chance of moving over 200 km above the geostationary belt at end of life.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-36)

Space debris in geosynchronous orbits typically has a lower collision speed than at LEO since most GSO satellites orbit in the same plane, altitude and speed; however, the presence of satellites in [eccentric orbits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eccentric_orbit "Eccentric orbit") allows for collisions at up to 4 km/s. Although a collision is comparatively unlikely, GSO satellites have a limited ability to avoid any debris.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-37)

Debris less than 10 cm in diameter cannot be seen from the Earth, making it difficult to assess their prevalence.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-telk1-38)

Despite efforts to reduce risk, spacecraft collisions have occurred. The [European Space Agency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Space_Agency "European Space Agency") telecom satellite [Olympus-1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Olympus-1 "Olympus-1") was struck by a [meteoroid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteoroid "Meteoroid") on August 11, 1993, and eventually moved to a [graveyard orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graveyard_orbit "Graveyard orbit"),[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-The_Olympus_failure-39) and in 2006 the Russian [Express-AM11](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Express_(satellite) "Express (satellite)") communications satellite was struck by an unknown object and rendered inoperable,[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-srdc20060419-40) although its engineers had enough contact time with the satellite to send it into a graveyard orbit. In 2017 both [AMC-9](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AMC-9 "AMC-9") and [Telkom-1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Telkom-1 "Telkom-1") broke apart from an unknown cause.[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-41)[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-telk1-38)[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-42)

[![Image 10](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Geosynchronous_no_geostationary_orbit.gif/250px-Geosynchronous_no_geostationary_orbit.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Geosynchronous_no_geostationary_orbit.gif)

The orbit of a geosynchronous satellite at an inclination, from the perspective of an off-Earth observer ([ECI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth-centered_inertial "Earth-centered inertial")) and of an observer rotating around the Earth at its spin rate ([ECEF](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ECEF "ECEF")).

A geosynchronous orbit has the following properties:

*   Period: 1436 minutes (one [sidereal day](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sidereal_day "Sidereal day"))
*   [Semi-major axis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semi-major_and_semi-minor_axes#Astronomy "Semi-major and semi-minor axes"): 42,164 km[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-smad-21): 121 

All geosynchronous orbits have an orbital period equal to exactly one sidereal day.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-43) This means that the satellite will return to the same point above the Earth's surface every (sidereal) day, regardless of other orbital properties.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-44)[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-smad-21): 121  This orbital period, T, is directly related to the semi-major axis of the orbit through the formula:

![Image 11: {\displaystyle T=2\pi {\sqrt {a^{3} \over \mu }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1385eed5863f0e40f86905c95cd3d4af7788ce9e)
where:

a is the length of the orbit's semi-major axis![Image 12: {\displaystyle \mu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9fd47b2a39f7a7856952afec1f1db72c67af6161) is the [standard gravitational parameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_gravitational_parameter "Standard gravitational parameter") of the central body[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-smad-21): 137 
A geosynchronous orbit can have any inclination.

Satellites commonly have an inclination of zero, ensuring that the orbit remains over the equator at all times, making it stationary with respect to latitude from the point of view of a ground observer (and in the [ECEF](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ECEF "ECEF") reference frame).[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-smad-21): 122

Another popular inclinations is 63.4° for a Tundra orbit, which ensures that the orbit's [argument of perigee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Argument_of_perigee "Argument of perigee") does not change over time.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-scs-23)

In the special case of a geostationary orbit, the [ground track](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ground_track "Ground track") of a satellite is a single point on the [equator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equator "Equator"). In the general case of a geosynchronous orbit with a non-zero [inclination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inclination "Inclination") or [eccentricity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eccentricity_(orbit) "Eccentricity (orbit)"), the ground track is a more or less distorted figure-eight, returning to the same places once per sidereal day.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit#cite_note-smad-21): 122
