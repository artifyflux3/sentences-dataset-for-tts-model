[![Image 1](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Helium-II-creep.svg/250px-Helium-II-creep.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Helium-II-creep.svg)

[Helium II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#Helium_II "Helium") will "creep" along surfaces in order to find its own level—after a short while, the levels in the two containers will equalize. The [Rollin film](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rollin_film "Rollin film") also covers the interior of the larger container; if it were not sealed, the helium II would creep out and escape.

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Liquid_helium_Rollin_film.jpg/250px-Liquid_helium_Rollin_film.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Liquid_helium_Rollin_film.jpg)

The liquid helium is in the superfluid phase. A thin invisible film creeps up the inside wall of the bowl and down on the outside. A drop forms. It will fall off into the liquid helium below. This will repeat until the cup is empty—provided the liquid remains superfluid.

**Superfluidity** is the characteristic property of a [fluid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluid "Fluid") with zero [viscosity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viscosity "Viscosity") which therefore flows without any loss of [kinetic energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_energy "Kinetic energy"). When stirred, a superfluid forms [vortices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vortex "Vortex") that continue to rotate indefinitely. Superfluidity occurs in two [isotopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotope "Isotope") of [helium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium "Helium") ([helium-3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium-3 "Helium-3") and [helium-4](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium-4 "Helium-4")) when they are liquefied by cooling to [cryogenic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cryogenic "Cryogenic") temperatures. It is also a property of various other exotic [states of matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_of_matter "State of matter") theorized to exist in [astrophysics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrophysics "Astrophysics"), [high-energy physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-energy_physics "High-energy physics"), and theories of [quantum gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_gravity "Quantum gravity").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superfluidity#cite_note-:0-1) The semi-phenomenological theory of superfluidity was developed by Soviet theoretical physicists [Lev Landau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lev_Landau "Lev Landau") and [Isaak Khalatnikov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaak_Khalatnikov "Isaak Khalatnikov").

Superfluidity often co-occurs with [Bose–Einstein condensation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bose%E2%80%93Einstein_condensate "Bose–Einstein condensate"), but neither phenomenon is directly related to the other; not all Bose–Einstein condensates can be regarded as superfluids, and not all superfluids are Bose–Einstein condensates.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superfluidity#cite_note-:32-2) Even when superfluidity and condensation co-occur, their magnitudes are not linked: at low temperature, liquid helium has a large superfluid fraction but a low condensate fraction; while a weakly interacting BEC, with almost unity condensate fraction, can display a vanishing superfluid fraction.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superfluidity#cite_note-i043-3)

Superfluids have some potential practical uses, such as dissolving substances in a [quantum solvent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_solvent "Quantum solvent").

Superfluidity of liquid helium
------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Superfluidity&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Superfluidity of liquid helium")]

Superfluidity was discovered in [helium-4](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium-4 "Helium-4") by [Pyotr Kapitsa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyotr_Kapitsa "Pyotr Kapitsa")[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superfluidity#cite_note-4) and independently by [John F. Allen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_F._Allen_(physicist) "John F. Allen (physicist)") and [Don Misener](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Don_Misener "Don Misener")[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superfluidity#cite_note-5) in 1937. [Onnes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heike_Kamerlingh_Onnes "Heike Kamerlingh Onnes") possibly observed the superfluid [phase transition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phase_transition "Phase transition") on August 2 1911, the same day that he observed superconductivity in mercury.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superfluidity#cite_note-6) It has since been described through [phenomenology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phenomenology_(physics) "Phenomenology (physics)") and [microscopic theories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microscopic_theory "Microscopic theory").

In liquid helium-4, the superfluidity occurs at far higher temperatures than it does in [helium-3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium-3 "Helium-3"). Each atom of helium-4 is a [boson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boson "Boson") particle, by virtue of its [integer spin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer_spin "Integer spin"). A helium-3 atom is a [fermion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermion "Fermion") particle; it can form bosons only by pairing with another particle like itself, which occurs at much lower temperatures. The discovery of superfluidity in helium-3 was the basis for the award of the 1996 [Nobel Prize in Physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physics "Nobel Prize in Physics").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superfluidity#cite_note-:0-1) This process is similar to the [electron pairing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cooper_pair "Cooper pair") in [superconductivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superconductivity "Superconductivity").

Superfluidity in an [ultracold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultracold_neutrons "Ultracold neutrons")[fermionic gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermi_gas "Fermi gas") was experimentally proven by [Wolfgang Ketterle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Ketterle "Wolfgang Ketterle") and his team who observed [quantum vortices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_vortex "Quantum vortex") in [lithium-6](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lithium-6 "Lithium-6") at a temperature of 50 nK at [MIT](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology "Massachusetts Institute of Technology") in April 2005.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superfluidity#cite_note-7)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superfluidity#cite_note-8) Such vortices had previously been observed in an ultracold [bosonic gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boson "Boson") using [rubidium-87](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rubidium-87 "Rubidium-87") in 2000,[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superfluidity#cite_note-9) and more recently in [two-dimensional gases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two-dimensional_gas "Two-dimensional gas").[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superfluidity#cite_note-10) As early as 1999, [Lene Hau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lene_Hau "Lene Hau") created such a condensate using [sodium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium "Sodium") atoms[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superfluidity#cite_note-11) for the purpose of slowing light, and later stopping it completely.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superfluidity#cite_note-12) Her team subsequently used this system of compressed light[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superfluidity#cite_note-13) to generate the superfluid analogue of shock waves and tornadoes:[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superfluidity#cite_note-14)

> These dramatic excitations result in the formation of [solitons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soliton "Soliton") that in turn decay into [quantized vortices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_vortex "Quantum vortex")—created far out of equilibrium, in pairs of opposite circulation—revealing directly the process of superfluid breakdown in Bose–Einstein condensates. With a double light-roadblock setup, we can generate controlled collisions between shock waves resulting in completely unexpected, nonlinear excitations. We have observed hybrid structures consisting of vortex rings embedded in dark solitonic shells. The vortex rings act as 'phantom propellers' leading to very rich excitation dynamics.

— Lene Hau, SIAM Conference on Nonlinear Waves and Coherent Structures

Superfluids in astrophysics
---------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Superfluidity&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Superfluids in astrophysics")]

The idea that superfluidity exists inside [neutron stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron_star "Neutron star") was first proposed by [Arkady Migdal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arkady_Migdal "Arkady Migdal").[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superfluidity#cite_note-15)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superfluidity#cite_note-16) By analogy with electrons inside [superconductors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superconductor "Superconductor") forming [Cooper pairs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cooper_pair "Cooper pair") because of electron–lattice interaction, it is expected that [nucleons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleon "Nucleon") in a neutron star at sufficiently high density and low temperature can also form Cooper pairs because of the long-range attractive nuclear force and lead to superfluidity and superconductivity.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superfluidity#cite_note-17)

In high-energy physics and quantum gravity
------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Superfluidity&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: In high-energy physics and quantum gravity")]

[Superfluid vacuum theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superfluid_vacuum_theory "Superfluid vacuum theory") (SVT) is an approach in [theoretical physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theoretical_physics "Theoretical physics") and [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics") where the physical [vacuum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vacuum "Vacuum") is viewed as superfluid.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superfluidity#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVolovik2003-18)[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superfluidity#cite_note-Scott2023-19)

The ultimate goal of the approach is to develop scientific models that unify quantum mechanics (describing three of the four known fundamental interactions) with [gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity "Gravity"). This makes SVT a candidate for the theory of [quantum gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_gravity "Quantum gravity") and an extension of the [Standard Model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model "Standard Model").[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

It is hoped that development of such a theory would unify into a single consistent model of all fundamental interactions, and to describe all known interactions and elementary particles as different manifestations of the same entity, superfluid vacuum.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

On the macro-scale a larger similar phenomenon has been suggested as happening in the [murmurations](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/murmuration "wikt:murmuration") of [starlings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Starling "Starling"). The rapidity of change in flight patterns mimics the phase change leading to superfluidity in some liquid states.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superfluidity#cite_note-20)
