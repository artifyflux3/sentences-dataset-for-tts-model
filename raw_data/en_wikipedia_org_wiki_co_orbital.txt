In [astronomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy "Astronomy"), a **co-orbital configuration** is a configuration of two or more [astronomical objects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_object "Astronomical object") (such as [asteroids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asteroid "Asteroid"), [moons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_satellite "Natural satellite"), or [planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planet "Planet")) orbiting at the same, or very similar, distance from their primary; i.e., they are in a [1:1 mean-motion resonance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_resonance "Orbital resonance"). (or 1:-1 if orbiting in [opposite directions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Retrograde_and_prograde_motion "Retrograde and prograde motion")).[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Co-orbital#cite_note-1)

There are several classes of co-orbital objects, depending on their point of [libration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Libration "Libration"). The most common and best-known class is the [trojan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trojan_(astronomy) "Trojan (astronomy)"), which librates around one of the two stable [Lagrangian points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrangian_point "Lagrangian point") (Trojan points), L 4 and L 5, 60° ahead of and behind the larger body respectively. Another class is the [horseshoe orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horseshoe_orbit "Horseshoe orbit"), in which objects librate around 180° from the larger body. Objects librating around 0° are called [quasi-satellites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasi-satellite "Quasi-satellite").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Co-orbital#cite_note-coorbplanet-2)

An **exchange orbit** occurs when two co-orbital objects are of similar masses and thus exert a non-negligible influence on each other. The objects can exchange [semi-major axes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semi-major_axis "Semi-major axis") or [eccentricities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity "Orbital eccentricity") when they approach each other.

Orbital parameters that are used to describe the relation of co-orbital objects are the [longitude of the periapsis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Longitude_of_the_periapsis "Longitude of the periapsis") difference and the [mean longitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mean_longitude "Mean longitude") difference. The longitude of the periapsis is the sum of the mean longitude and the [mean anomaly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mean_anomaly "Mean anomaly")![Image 1: {\displaystyle ({\lambda }=\varpi +M)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6fcb83b226da38d2cc7f1b992c880f68e74c09ce) and the mean longitude is the sum of the [longitude of the ascending node](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Longitude_of_the_ascending_node "Longitude of the ascending node") and the [argument of periapsis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Argument_of_periapsis "Argument of periapsis")![Image 2: {\displaystyle (\varpi =\Omega +\omega )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/528957c36491161825dc831a9877af8e0070683c).

[![Image 3](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Lagrange_very_massive.svg/250px-Lagrange_very_massive.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Lagrange_very_massive.svg)

Trojan points are the points labelled [L 4](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrange_point#L4_and_L5 "Lagrange point") and [L 5](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrange_point#L4_and_L5 "Lagrange point"), highlighted in red, on the orbital path of the secondary object (blue), around the primary object (yellow).

Trojan objects orbit 60° ahead of (L 4) or behind (L 5) a more massive object, both in orbit around an even more massive central object. The best known examples are the large population of asteroids that orbit ahead of or behind [Jupiter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jupiter "Jupiter") around the [Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun "Sun"). Trojan objects do not orbit exactly at one of either [Lagrangian points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrangian_point "Lagrangian point"), but do remain relatively close to it, appearing to slowly orbit it. In technical terms, they librate around ![Image 4: {\displaystyle ({\Delta }{\lambda },{\Delta }\varpi )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e7c493c4643efcb5dbf615af06bd16ae2ed9587f) = (±60°, ±60°). The point around which they librate is the same, irrespective of their mass or orbital eccentricity.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Co-orbital#cite_note-coorbplanet-2)

### Trojan minor planets

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Co-orbital_configuration&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Trojan minor planets")]

There are several thousand known trojan minor planets orbiting the Sun. Most of these orbit near Jupiter's Lagrangian points, the traditional [Jupiter trojans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jupiter_trojan "Jupiter trojan"). As of 2015, there are also 13 [Neptune trojans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune_trojan "Neptune trojan"), 7 [Mars trojans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mars_trojan "Mars trojan"), 2 [Uranus trojans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uranus_trojan "Uranus trojan") ([(687170) 2011 QF 99](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/(687170)_2011_QF99 "(687170) 2011 QF99") and [(636872) 2014 YX 49](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/(636872)_2014_YX49 "(636872) 2014 YX49")), and 2 [Earth trojans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth_trojan "Earth trojan") ([2010 TK 7](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2010_TK7 "2010 TK7") and [(614689) 2020 XL 5](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/(614689)_2020_XL5 "(614689) 2020 XL5") ) that are known to exist. No Saturnian trojans have been observed until the discovery of [2019 UO14](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2019_UO14 "2019 UO14").

The Saturnian system contains two sets of trojan moons. Both [Tethys](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tethys_(moon) "Tethys (moon)") and [Dione](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dione_(moon) "Dione (moon)") have two trojan moons each, [Telesto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Telesto_(moon) "Telesto (moon)") and [Calypso](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calypso_(moon) "Calypso (moon)") in Tethys's L 4 and L 5 respectively, and [Helene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helene_(moon) "Helene (moon)") and [Polydeuces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polydeuces_(moon) "Polydeuces (moon)") in Dione's L 4 and L 5 respectively.

Polydeuces is noticeable for its wide [libration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Libration "Libration"): it wanders as far as ±30° from its Lagrangian point and ±2% from its mean orbital radius, along a [tadpole orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tadpole_orbit "Tadpole orbit") in 790 days (288 times its orbital period around Saturn, the same as Dione's).

A pair of co-orbital [exoplanets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exoplanet "Exoplanet") was proposed to be orbiting the star [Kepler-223](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler-223 "Kepler-223"), but this was later retracted.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Co-orbital#cite_note-3)

The possibility of a trojan planet to [Kepler-91b](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler-91b "Kepler-91b") was studied but the conclusion was that the transit-signal was a false-positive.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Co-orbital#cite_note-4)

In April 2023, a group of [amateur astronomers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amateur_astronomy "Amateur astronomy") reported two new exoplanet candidates co-orbiting, in a [horseshoe exchange orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horseshoe_orbit "Horseshoe orbit"), close to the star GJ 3470 (this star has a confirmed planet [GJ 3470 b](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GJ_3470_b "GJ 3470 b")). However, the mentioned study is only in preprint form on arXiv, and it has not yet been peer reviewed and published in a reputable scientific journal.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Co-orbital#cite_note-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Co-orbital#cite_note-6) Unrelated to the aforementioned claim, a strong candidate of a trojan planet in the GJ 3470 system was found by the TROY project and published in [Astronomy & Astrophysics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy_%26_Astrophysics "Astronomy & Astrophysics"), based on the radial velocity data.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Co-orbital#cite_note-Balsalobre-Ruza2024-7)

In July 2023, the possible detection of a cloud of debris co-orbital with the proto-planet [PDS 70 b](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PDS_70 "PDS 70") was announced. This debris cloud could be evidence of a Trojan [planetary-mass body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary-mass_object "Planetary-mass object") or one in the process of forming.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Co-orbital#cite_note-8)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Co-orbital#cite_note-eso2311-9)

One possibility for the [habitable zone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habitable_zone "Habitable zone") is a trojan planet of a [giant planet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giant_planet "Giant planet") close to its [star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star "Star").[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Co-orbital#cite_note-10)

The reason why no trojan planets have been definitively detected could be that tides destabilize their orbits.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Co-orbital#cite_note-11)

#### Formation of the Earth–Moon system

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Co-orbital_configuration&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Formation of the Earth–Moon system")]

According to the [giant impact hypothesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giant_impact_hypothesis "Giant impact hypothesis"), the [Moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moon "Moon") formed after a collision between two co-orbital objects: [Theia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theia_(planet) "Theia (planet)"), thought to have had about 10% of the mass of Earth (about as massive as [Mars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mars "Mars")), and the proto-Earth. Their orbits were perturbed by other planets, bringing Theia out of its trojan position and causing the collision.

 Saturn**·** Janus **·**Epimetheus

Objects in a horseshoe orbit librate around 180° from the primary. Their orbits encompass both equilateral Lagrangian points, i.e. L 4 and L 5.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Co-orbital#cite_note-coorbplanet-2)

The [Saturnian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saturn "Saturn") moons [Janus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Janus_(moon) "Janus (moon)") and [Epimetheus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epimetheus_(moon) "Epimetheus (moon)") share their orbits, the difference in semi-major axes being less than either's mean diameter. This means the moon with the smaller semi-major axis slowly catches up with the other. As it does this, the moons gravitationally tug at each other, increasing the semi-major axis of the moon that has caught up and decreasing that of the other. This reverses their relative positions proportionally to their masses and causes this process to begin anew with the moons' roles reversed. In other words, they effectively swap orbits, ultimately oscillating both about their mass-weighted mean orbit.

### Earth co-orbital asteroids

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Co-orbital_configuration&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Earth co-orbital asteroids")]

A small number of asteroids have been found which are co-orbital with Earth. The first of these to be discovered, asteroid [3753 Cruithne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3753_Cruithne "3753 Cruithne"), orbits the Sun with a period slightly less than one Earth year, resulting in an orbit that (from the point of view of Earth) appears as a bean-shaped orbit centered on a position ahead of the position of Earth. This orbit slowly moves further ahead of Earth's orbital position. When Cruithne's orbit moves to a position where it trails Earth's position, rather than leading it, the gravitational effect of Earth increases the orbital period, and hence the orbit then begins to lag, returning to the original location. The full cycle from leading to trailing Earth takes 770 years, leading to a horseshoe-shaped movement with respect to Earth.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Co-orbital#cite_note-12)

More resonant [near-Earth objects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Near-Earth_object "Near-Earth object") (NEOs) have since been discovered. These include [54509 YORP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/54509_YORP "54509 YORP"), [(85770) 1998 UP 1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/(85770)_1998_UP1 "(85770) 1998 UP1"), [2002 AA 29](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2002_AA29 "2002 AA29"), [(419624) 2010 SO 16](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/(419624)_2010_SO16 "(419624) 2010 SO16"), [2009 BD](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2009_BD "2009 BD"), and [2015 SO 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2015_SO2 "2015 SO2") which exist in resonant orbits similar to Cruithne's. [2010 TK 7](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2010_TK7 "2010 TK7") and [(614689) 2020 XL 5](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/(614689)_2020_XL5 "(614689) 2020 XL5") are the only two identified [Earth trojans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth_trojan "Earth trojan").

[Hungaria asteroids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hungaria_asteroid "Hungaria asteroid") were found to be one of the possible sources for co-orbital objects of the Earth with a lifetime up to ~58 [kyrs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kyr "Kyr").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Co-orbital#cite_note-13)

Quasi-satellites are co-orbital objects that librate around 0° from the primary. Low-eccentricity quasi-satellite orbits are highly unstable, but for moderate to high eccentricities such orbits can be stable.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Co-orbital#cite_note-coorbplanet-2) From a co-rotating perspective the quasi-satellite appears to orbit the primary like a [retrograde satellite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Retrograde_motion "Retrograde motion"), although at distances so large that it is not gravitationally bound to it.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Co-orbital#cite_note-coorbplanet-2) Two examples of [quasi-satellites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasi-satellite "Quasi-satellite") of the [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth "Earth") are [2014 OL 339](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2014_OL339 "2014 OL339")[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Co-orbital#cite_note-dynamics-14) and [469219 Kamoʻoalewa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/469219_Kamo%CA%BBoalewa "469219 Kamoʻoalewa").[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Co-orbital#cite_note-NASA-20160615-15)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Co-orbital#cite_note-2016HO3-16)

In addition to swapping semi-major axes like Saturn's moons Epimetheus and Janus, another possibility is to share the same axis, but swap eccentricities instead.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Co-orbital#cite_note-17)
