The tesseract can be unfolded into eight cubes into 3D space, just as the cube can be unfolded into six squares into 2D space.

In [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry"), a **tesseract** or **4-cube** is a [four-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Four-dimensional_space "Four-dimensional space")[hypercube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypercube "Hypercube"), analogous to a two-[dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension "Dimension")[square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_(geometry) "Square (geometry)") and a three-dimensional [cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube "Cube").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tesseract#cite_note-1) Just as the perimeter of the square consists of four edges and the surface of the cube consists of six square [faces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Face_(geometry) "Face (geometry)"), the [hypersurface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypersurface "Hypersurface") of the tesseract consists of eight cubical [cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_(geometry) "Cell (geometry)"), meeting at [right angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_angle "Right angle"). The tesseract is one of the six [convex regular 4-polytopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_regular_4-polytope "Convex regular 4-polytope").

The tesseract is also called an **8-cell**, **C 8**, (regular) **octachoron**, or **cubic prism**. It is the four-dimensional **measure polytope**, taken as a unit for hypervolume.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tesseract#cite_note-2)[Coxeter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harold_Scott_MacDonald_Coxeter "Harold Scott MacDonald Coxeter") labels it the _γ_ 4 polytope.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tesseract#cite_note-FOOTNOTECoxeter1973122%E2%80%93123%C2%A77.2._illustration_Fig_7.2%3Csmall%3EC%3C/small%3E-3) The term _hypercube_ without a dimension reference is frequently treated as a synonym for this specific [polytope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polytope "Polytope").

The _[Oxford English Dictionary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxford\_English\_Dictionary "Oxford English Dictionary")_ traces the word _tesseract_ to [Charles Howard Hinton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Howard_Hinton "Charles Howard Hinton")'s 1888 book _[A New Era of Thought](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A\_New\_Era\_of\_Thought "A New Era of Thought")_. The term derives from the [Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek "Ancient Greek")_téssara_ ([τέσσαρα](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CF%84%CE%AD%CF%83%CF%83%CE%B1%CF%81%CE%B1#Ancient_Greek "wikt:τέσσαρα") 'four') and _aktís_ ([ἀκτίς](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E1%BC%80%CE%BA%CF%84%CE%AF%CF%82#Ancient_Greek "wikt:ἀκτίς") 'ray'), referring to the four edges from each vertex to other vertices. Hinton originally spelled the word as _tessaract_.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tesseract#cite_note-4)

As a [regular polytope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polytope "Regular polytope") with three [cubes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube "Cube") folded together around every edge, it has [Schläfli symbol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schl%C3%A4fli_symbol "Schläfli symbol") {4,3,3} with [hyperoctahedral symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperoctahedral_group#By_dimension "Hyperoctahedral group") of order 384. Constructed as a 4D [hyperprism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperprism "Hyperprism") made of two parallel cubes, it can be named as a composite Schläfli symbol {4,3}×{}, with symmetry order 96. As a 4-4 [duoprism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duoprism "Duoprism"), a [Cartesian product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_product "Cartesian product") of two [squares](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_(geometry) "Square (geometry)"), it can be named by a composite Schläfli symbol {4}×{4}, with symmetry order 64. As an [orthotope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthotope "Orthotope") it can be represented by composite Schläfli symbol {}×{}×{}×{} or {}4, with symmetry order 16.

Since each vertex of a tesseract is adjacent to four edges, the [vertex figure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_figure "Vertex figure") of the tesseract is a regular [tetrahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron "Tetrahedron"). The [dual polytope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_polytope "Dual polytope") of the tesseract is the [16-cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/16-cell "16-cell") with Schläfli symbol {3,3,4}, with which it can be combined to form the compound of tesseract and 16-cell.

Each edge of a regular tesseract is of the same length. This is of interest when using tesseracts as the basis for a [network topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Network_topology "Network topology") to link multiple processors in [parallel computing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_computing "Parallel computing"): the distance between two nodes is at most 4 and there are many different paths to allow weight balancing.

A tesseract is bounded by eight three-dimensional [hyperplanes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperplane "Hyperplane"). Each pair of non-parallel hyperplanes intersects to form 24 square faces. Three cubes and three squares intersect at each edge. There are four cubes, six squares, and four edges meeting at every vertex. All in all, a tesseract consists of 8 cubes, 24 squares, 32 edges, and 16 vertices.

A _unit tesseract_ has side length 1, and is typically taken as the basic unit for [hypervolume](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypervolume "Hypervolume") in 4-dimensional space. _The_ unit tesseract in a [Cartesian coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system "Cartesian coordinate system") for 4-dimensional space has two opposite vertices at coordinates [0, 0, 0, 0] and [1, 1, 1, 1], and other vertices with coordinates at all possible combinations of 0 s and 1 s. It is the [Cartesian product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_product "Cartesian product") of the closed [unit interval](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_interval "Unit interval")[0, 1] in each axis.

Sometimes a unit tesseract is centered at the origin, so that its coordinates are the more symmetrical ![Image 42: {\displaystyle {\bigl (}{\pm {\tfrac {1}{2}}},\pm {\tfrac {1}{2}},\pm {\tfrac {1}{2}},\pm {\tfrac {1}{2}}{\bigr )}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ea30b0ff16c179c02023ef8e623326ef7637c3f0) This is the Cartesian product of the closed interval ![Image 43: {\displaystyle {\bigl [}{-{\tfrac {1}{2}}},{\tfrac {1}{2}}{\bigr ]}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/937dd70d7cd719d027e8e6f4d6f468ebe6e9dcb1) in each axis.

Another commonly convenient tesseract is the Cartesian product of the closed interval [−1, 1] in each axis, with vertices at coordinates (±1, ±1, ±1, ±1). This tesseract has side length 2 and hypervolume 2 4 = 16.

An unfolding of a [polytope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polytope "Polytope") is called a [net](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Net_(polyhedron) "Net (polyhedron)"). There are 261 distinct nets of the tesseract.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tesseract#cite_note-5) The unfoldings of the tesseract can be counted by mapping the nets to _paired trees_ (a [tree](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tree_(graph_theory) "Tree (graph theory)") together with a [perfect matching](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perfect_matching "Perfect matching") in its [complement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complement_graph "Complement graph")).

Each of the 261 nets can tile 3-space.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tesseract#cite_note-6)

The construction of [hypercubes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypercube "Hypercube") can be imagined the following way:

*   **1-dimensional:** Two points A and B can be connected to become a line, giving a new line segment AB.
*   **2-dimensional:** Two parallel line segments AB and CD separated by a distance of AB can be connected to become a square, with the corners marked as ABCD.
*   **3-dimensional:** Two parallel squares ABCD and EFGH separated by a distance of AB can be connected to become a cube, with the corners marked as ABCDEFGH.
*   **4-dimensional:** Two parallel cubes ABCDEFGH and IJKLMNOP separated by a distance of AB can be connected to become a tesseract, with the corners marked as ABCDEFGHIJKLMNOP. However, this parallel positioning of two cubes such that their 8 corresponding pairs of vertices are each separated by a distance of AB can only be achieved in a space of 4 or more dimensions.

[![Image 45: A diagram showing how to create a tesseract from a point](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Dimension_levels.svg/480px-Dimension_levels.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Dimension_levels.svg "A diagram showing how to create a tesseract from a point")

The 8 cells of the tesseract may be regarded (three different ways) as two interlocked rings of four cubes.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tesseract#cite_note-FOOTNOTECoxeter197018-7)

The tesseract can be decomposed into smaller 4-polytopes. It is the convex hull of the compound of two [demitesseracts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Demihypercube "Demihypercube") ([16-cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Demitesseract "Demitesseract")). It can also be [triangulated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point-set_triangulation "Point-set triangulation") into 4-dimensional [simplices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simplex "Simplex") ([irregular 5-cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/5-cell#Irregular_5-cells "5-cell")) that share their vertices with the tesseract. It is known that there are 92 487 256 such triangulations[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tesseract#cite_note-8) and that the fewest 4-dimensional simplices in any of them is 16.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tesseract#cite_note-9)

The dissection of the tesseract into instances of its [characteristic simplex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthoscheme#Characteristic_simplex_of_the_general_regular_polytope "Orthoscheme") (a particular [orthoscheme](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthoscheme "Orthoscheme") with Coxeter diagram ![Image 46](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/5e/CDel_node.png)![Image 47](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/8c/CDel_4.png)![Image 48](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/5e/CDel_node.png)![Image 49](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c3/CDel_3.png)![Image 50](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/5e/CDel_node.png)![Image 51](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c3/CDel_3.png)![Image 52](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/5e/CDel_node.png)) is the most basic direct construction of the tesseract possible. The **[characteristic 5-cell of the 4-cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/5-cell#Orthoschemes "5-cell")** is a [fundamental region](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_region "Fundamental region") of the tesseract's defining [symmetry group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coxeter_group "Coxeter group"), the group which generates the [B 4 polytopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/B4_polytope "B4 polytope"). The tesseract's characteristic simplex directly _generates_ the tesseract through the actions of the group, by reflecting itself in its own bounding facets (its _mirror walls_).

### Radial equilateral symmetry

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tesseract&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Radial equilateral symmetry")]

The radius of a [hypersphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypersphere "Hypersphere") circumscribed about a regular polytope is the distance from the polytope's center to one of the vertices, and for the tesseract this radius is equal to its edge length; the diameter of the sphere, the length of the diagonal between opposite vertices of the tesseract, is twice the edge length. Only a few uniform [polytopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polytopes "Polytopes") have this property, including the four-dimensional tesseract and [24-cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/24-cell#Radially_equilateral_honeycomb "24-cell"), the three-dimensional [cuboctahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cuboctahedron#Radial_equilateral_symmetry "Cuboctahedron"), and the two-dimensional [hexagon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hexagon "Hexagon"). In particular, the tesseract is the only hypercube (other than a zero-dimensional point) that is _radially equilateral_. The longest vertex-to-vertex diagonal of an ![Image 53: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)-dimensional hypercube of unit edge length is ![Image 54: {\displaystyle {\sqrt {n{\vphantom {t}}}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2f3bcdd520ac2e9bbafe000f27fbe789b54542b5) which for the square is ![Image 55: {\displaystyle {\sqrt {2}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/74847cf19189c725cee56e0e5b513d59f6eb9402) for the cube is ![Image 56: {\displaystyle {\sqrt {3}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/12cd13e3da52b93c66f838203a27e013f1070ab2) and only for the tesseract is ![Image 57: {\displaystyle {\sqrt {4}}=2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5d82f5e32bfe3dfb14b13ed6b632b48b063acd77) edge lengths.

This [configuration matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_4-polytope#As_configurations "Regular 4-polytope") represents the tesseract. The rows and columns correspond to vertices, edges, faces, and cells. The diagonal numbers say how many of each element occur in the whole tesseract. The diagonal reduces to the [f-vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/F-vector "F-vector") (16,32,24,8).

The nondiagonal numbers say how many of the column's element occur in or at the row's element.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tesseract#cite_note-FOOTNOTECoxeter197312%C2%A71.8_Configurations-10) For example, the 2 in the first column of the second row indicates that there are 2 vertices in (i.e., at the extremes of) each edge; the 4 in the second column of the first row indicates that 4 edges meet at each vertex.

The bottom row defines they facets, here cubes, have f-vector (8,12,6). The next row left of diagonal is ridge elements (facet of cube), here a square, (4,4).

The upper row is the f-vector of the [vertex figure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_figure "Vertex figure"), here tetrahedra, (4,6,4). The next row is vertex figure ridge, here a triangle, (3,3).

It is possible to project tesseracts into three- and two-dimensional spaces, similarly to projecting a cube into two-dimensional space.

[![Image 61](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Orthogonal_projection_envelopes_tesseract.png/250px-Orthogonal_projection_envelopes_tesseract.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Orthogonal_projection_envelopes_tesseract.png)

Parallel projection envelopes of the tesseract (each cell is drawn with different color faces, inverted cells are undrawn)

[![Image 62](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Hypercubeorder_binary.svg/250px-Hypercubeorder_binary.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Hypercubeorder_binary.svg)

The [rhombic dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhombic_dodecahedron "Rhombic dodecahedron") forms the convex hull of the tesseract's vertex-first parallel-projection. The number of vertices in the layers of this projection is 1 4 6 4 1—the fourth row in [Pascal's triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pascal%27s_triangle "Pascal's triangle").

The _cell-first_ parallel [projection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graphical_projection "Graphical projection") of the tesseract into three-dimensional space has a [cubical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube "Cube") envelope. The nearest and farthest cells are projected onto the cube, and the remaining six cells are projected onto the six square faces of the cube.

The _face-first_ parallel projection of the tesseract into three-dimensional space has a [cuboidal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cuboid "Cuboid") envelope. Two pairs of cells project to the upper and lower halves of this envelope, and the four remaining cells project to the side faces.

The _edge-first_ parallel projection of the tesseract into three-dimensional space has an envelope in the shape of a [hexagonal prism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hexagonal_prism "Hexagonal prism"). Six cells project onto rhombic prisms, which are laid out in the hexagonal prism in a way analogous to how the faces of the 3D cube project onto six rhombs in a hexagonal envelope under vertex-first projection. The two remaining cells project onto the prism bases.

The _vertex-first_ parallel projection of the tesseract into three-dimensional space has a [rhombic dodecahedral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhombic_dodecahedron "Rhombic dodecahedron") envelope. Two vertices of the tesseract are projected to the origin. There are exactly two ways of [dissecting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dissection_(geometry) "Dissection (geometry)") a rhombic dodecahedron into four congruent [rhombohedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhombohedron "Rhombohedron"), giving a total of eight possible rhombohedra, each a projected [cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube "Cube") of the tesseract. This projection is also the one with maximal volume. One set of projection vectors are _u_ = (1,1,−1,−1), _v_ = (−1,1,−1,1), _w_ = (1,−1,−1,1).

The tesseract, like all [hypercubes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypercubes "Hypercubes"), [tessellates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tessellation "Tessellation")[Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space"). The self-dual [tesseractic honeycomb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tesseractic_honeycomb "Tesseractic honeycomb") consisting of 4 tesseracts around each face has [Schläfli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ludwig_Schl%C3%A4fli "Ludwig Schläfli") symbol **{4,3,3,4}**. Hence, the tesseract has a [dihedral angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihedral_angle "Dihedral angle") of 90°.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tesseract#cite_note-FOOTNOTECoxeter1973293-11)

The tesseract's [radial equilateral symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tesseract#Radial_equilateral_symmetry) makes its tessellation the [unique regular body-centered cubic lattice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tesseractic_honeycomb#Sphere_packing "Tesseractic honeycomb") of equal-sized spheres, in any number of dimensions.

As a uniform [duoprism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duoprism "Duoprism"), the tesseract exists in a [sequence of uniform duoprisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uniform_4-polytope#Polygonal_prismatic_prisms:_.5Bp.5D_.C3.97_.5B_.5D_.C3.97_.5B_.5D "Uniform 4-polytope"): {_p_}×{4}.

The regular tesseract, along with the [16-cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/16-cell "16-cell"), exists in a set of 15 [uniform 4-polytopes with the same symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Truncated_tesseract#Related_uniform_polytopes_in_tesseract_symmetry "Truncated tesseract"). The tesseract {4,3,3} exists in a [sequence of regular 4-polytopes and honeycombs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hexagonal_tiling_honeycomb#Polytopes_and_honeycombs_with_tetrahedral_vertex_figures "Hexagonal tiling honeycomb"), {_p_,3,3} with [tetrahedral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron "Tetrahedron")[vertex figures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_figure "Vertex figure"), {3,3}. The tesseract is also in a [sequence of regular 4-polytope and honeycombs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Order-5_cubic_honeycomb#Related_polytopes_and_honeycombs_with_cubic_cells "Order-5 cubic honeycomb"), {4,3,_p_} with [cubic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube "Cube")[cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_(geometry) "Cell (geometry)").

Since their discovery, four-dimensional hypercubes have been a popular theme in art, architecture, and science fiction. Notable examples include:

*   "[And He Built a Crooked House](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/And_He_Built_a_Crooked_House "And He Built a Crooked House")", [Robert Heinlein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_A._Heinlein "Robert A. Heinlein")'s 1940 science fiction story featuring a building in the form of a four-dimensional hypercube.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tesseract#cite_note-13) This and [Martin Gardner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Martin_Gardner "Martin Gardner")'s "The No-Sided Professor", published in 1946, are among the first in science fiction to introduce readers to the [Moebius band](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moebius_band "Moebius band"), the [Klein bottle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Klein_bottle "Klein bottle"), and the hypercube (tesseract).
*   _[Crucifixion (Corpus Hypercubus)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crucifixion\_(Corpus\_Hypercubus) "Crucifixion (Corpus Hypercubus)")_, a 1954 oil painting by Salvador Dalí featuring a four-dimensional hypercube unfolded into a three-dimensional [Latin cross](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin_cross "Latin cross").[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tesseract#cite_note-14)
*   The [Grande Arche](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grande_Arche "Grande Arche"), a monument and building near Paris, France, completed in 1989. According to the monument's engineer, [Erik Reitzel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erik_Reitzel "Erik Reitzel"), the Grande Arche was designed to resemble the projection of a hypercube.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tesseract#cite_note-15)
*   _[Fez](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fez\_(video\_game) "Fez (video game)")_, a video game where one plays a character who can see beyond the two dimensions other characters can see, and must use this ability to solve platforming puzzles. Features "Dot", a tesseract who helps the player navigate the world and tells how to use abilities, fitting the theme of seeing beyond human perception of known dimensional space.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tesseract#cite_note-16)

The word _tesseract_ has been adopted for numerous other uses in popular culture, including as a plot device in works of science fiction, often with little or no connection to the four-dimensional hypercube; see [Tesseract (disambiguation)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tesseract_(disambiguation) "Tesseract (disambiguation)").
