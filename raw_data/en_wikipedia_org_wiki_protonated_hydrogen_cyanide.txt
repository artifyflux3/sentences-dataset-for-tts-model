**HCNH+**, also known as protonated [hydrogen cyanide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide "Hydrogen cyanide"), is a molecular ion of astrophysical interest. It also exists in the condensed state when formed by [superacids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superacid "Superacid").

In the [ground state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ground_state "Ground state"), HC+N H is a simple linear molecule, whereas its excited [triplet state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triplet_state "Triplet state") is expected to have [_cis_ and _trans_ isomeric forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cis%E2%80%93trans_isomerism "Cis–trans isomerism"). The higher-energy [structural isomers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Structural_isomer "Structural isomer") H 2 CN+ and C+N H 2 have also been studied theoretically.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protonated_hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-5)

As a relatively simple molecular ion, HCNH+ has been extensively studied in the laboratory. The very first spectrum taken at any wavelength focused on the _ν_ 2 (C−H stretch) ro-vibrational band in the [infrared](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infrared "Infrared"). [[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protonated_hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-6) Soon afterward, the same authors reported on their investigation of the _ν_ 1 (N−H stretch) band. [[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protonated_hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-7) Following these initial studies, several groups published manuscripts on the various ro-vibrational spectra of HCNH+, including studies of the _ν_ 3 band (C≡N stretch),[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protonated_hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-8) the _ν_ 4 band (H−C≡N bend),[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protonated_hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-9) and the _ν_ 5 band (H−N≡C bend) .[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protonated_hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-10)

While all of these studies focused on ro-vibrational spectra in the [infrared](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infrared "Infrared"), it was not until 1998 that technology advanced far enough for an investigation of the pure rotational spectrum of HCNH+ in the [microwave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microwave "Microwave") region to take place. At that time, microwave spectra for HCNH+ and its isotopomers HCND+ and DCND+ were published.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protonated_hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-11) Recently, the pure rotational spectrum of HCNH+ was measured again in order to more precisely determine the molecular rotational constants _B_ and _D_.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protonated_hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-amano-12)

Formation and destruction
-------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Protonated_hydrogen_cyanide&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Formation and destruction")]

According to the database at [astrochemistry.net](http://udfa.ajmarkwick.net/), the most advanced chemical models of HCNH+ include 71 total formation reactions and 21 total destruction reactions. Of these, however, only a handful dominate the overall formation and destruction.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protonated_hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-13) In the case of formation, the 7 dominant reactions are:

H+

3 + HCN → HCNH+ + H 2 H+

3 + HNC → HCNH+ + H 2 HCO+ + HCN → HCNH+ + CO HCO+ + HNC → HCNH+ + CO H 3 O+ + HCN → HCNH+ + H 2 O H 3 O+ + HNC → HCNH+ + H 2 O C+ + NH 3 → HCNH+ + H

Astronomical detections
-----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Protonated_hydrogen_cyanide&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Astronomical detections")]

### Initial interstellar detection

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Protonated_hydrogen_cyanide&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Initial interstellar detection")]

HCNH+ was first detected in [interstellar space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_molecules_in_interstellar_space "List of molecules in interstellar space") in 1986 toward the dense cloud [Sgr B2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sagittarius_B2 "Sagittarius B2") using the [NRAO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NRAO "NRAO") 12 m dish and the [Texas Millimeter Wave Observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Texas_Millimeter_Wave_Observatory&action=edit&redlink=1 "Texas Millimeter Wave Observatory (page does not exist)").[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protonated_hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-14) These observations utilized the _J_=1–0, 2–1, and 3–2 pure rotational transitions at 74, 148, and 222 GHz, respectively.

### Subsequent interstellar detections

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Protonated_hydrogen_cyanide&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Subsequent interstellar detections")]

Since the initial detection, HCNH+ has also been observed in TMC-1[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protonated_hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-schilke-15)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protonated_hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-16) as well as DR 21(OH)[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protonated_hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-schilke-15) .[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protonated_hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-17) The initial detection toward [Sgr B2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sagittarius_B2 "Sagittarius B2") has also been confirmed.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protonated_hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-schilke-15)[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protonated_hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-18) All 3 of these sources are dense molecular clouds, and to date HCNH+ has not been detected in diffuse interstellar material.

### Solar System bodies

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Protonated_hydrogen_cyanide&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Solar System bodies")]

While not directly detected via spectroscopy, the existence of HCNH+ has been inferred to exist in the atmosphere of [Saturn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saturn "Saturn")'s largest moon, [Titan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Titan_(moon) "Titan (moon)"),[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protonated_hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-19) based on data from the Ion and Neutral Mass Spectrometer (INMS) instrument aboard the [Cassini](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cassini-Huygens "Cassini-Huygens") space probe. Models of Titan's atmosphere had predicted that HCNH+ would be the dominant ion present, and a strong peak in the mass spectrum at ⁠_m_/_z_⁠=28 seems to support this theory.

In 1997, observations were made of the long-period comet [Hale–Bopp](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comet_Hale-Bopp "Comet Hale-Bopp") in an attempt to find HCNH+, [[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protonated_hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-20) but it was not detected. However, the upper limit derived from these observations, along with the detections of [HCN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide "Hydrogen cyanide"), [HNC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_isocyanide "Hydrogen isocyanide"), and [CN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyanide "Cyanide"), is important in understanding the chemistry associated with [comets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comet "Comet").
