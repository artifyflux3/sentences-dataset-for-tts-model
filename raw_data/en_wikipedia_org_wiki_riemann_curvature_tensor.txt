In the [mathematical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical "Mathematical") field of [differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry"), the **Riemann curvature tensor** or **Riemann–Christoffel tensor** (after [Bernhard Riemann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bernhard_Riemann "Bernhard Riemann") and [Elwin Bruno Christoffel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elwin_Bruno_Christoffel "Elwin Bruno Christoffel")) is the most common way used to express the [curvature of Riemannian manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvature_of_Riemannian_manifolds "Curvature of Riemannian manifolds"). It assigns a [tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor "Tensor") to each point of a [Riemannian manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_manifold "Riemannian manifold") (i.e., it is a [tensor field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_field "Tensor field")). It is a local invariant of Riemannian metrics that measures the failure of the second [covariant derivatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covariant_derivative "Covariant derivative") to commute. A Riemannian manifold has zero curvature if and only if it is _flat_, i.e. locally [isometric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isometry "Isometry") to the [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_curvature_tensor#cite_note-FOOTNOTELee2018193-1) The curvature tensor can also be defined for any [pseudo-Riemannian manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudo-Riemannian_manifold "Pseudo-Riemannian manifold"), or indeed any manifold equipped with an [affine connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_connection "Affine connection").

It is a central mathematical tool in the theory of [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity"), the modern theory of [gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity "Gravity"). The curvature of [spacetime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spacetime "Spacetime") is in principle observable via the [geodesic deviation equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic_deviation_equation "Geodesic deviation equation"). The curvature tensor represents the [tidal force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tidal_force "Tidal force") experienced by a rigid body moving along a [geodesic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic "Geodesic") in a sense made precise by the [Jacobi equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacobi_field "Jacobi field").

Occasionally, the curvature tensor is defined with the opposite sign.

The curvature tensor measures _noncommutativity of the covariant derivative_, and as such is the [integrability obstruction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integrability_condition "Integrability condition") for the existence of an isometry with Euclidean space (called, in this context, _flat_ space).

Figure showing the geometric meaning of the Riemann curvature tensor in a spherical curved manifold. The fact that this transfer can define two different arrows at the starting point gives rise to the Riemann curvature tensor. The orthogonal symbol indicates that the [dot product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dot_product "Dot product") (provided by the metric tensor) between the transmitted arrows (or the tangent arrows on the curve) is zero. The angle between the two arrows is zero when the space is flat and greater than zero when the space is curved. The more curved the space, the greater the angle.

One can see the effects of curved space by comparing a tennis court and the Earth. Start at the lower right corner of the tennis court, with a racket held out towards north. Then while walking around the outline of the court, at each step make sure the tennis racket is maintained in the same orientation, parallel to its previous positions. Once the loop is complete the tennis racket will be parallel to its initial starting position. This is because tennis courts are built so the surface is flat. On the other hand, the surface of the Earth is curved: we can complete a loop on the surface of the Earth. Starting at the equator, point a tennis racket north along the surface of the Earth. Once again the tennis racket should always remain parallel to its previous position, using the local plane of the horizon as a reference. For this path, first walk to the north pole, then walk sideways (i.e. without turning), then down to the equator, and finally walk backwards to your starting position. Now the tennis racket will be pointing towards the west, even though when you began your journey it pointed north and you never turned your body. This process is akin to [parallel transporting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_transport "Parallel transport") a vector along the path and the difference identifies how lines which appear "straight" are only "straight" locally. Each time a loop is completed the tennis racket will be deflected further from its initial position by an amount depending on the distance and the curvature of the surface. It is possible to identify paths along a curved surface where parallel transport works as it does on flat space. These are the [geodesics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesics "Geodesics") of the space, for example any segment of a great circle of a sphere.

The concept of a curved space in mathematics differs from conversational usage. For example, if the above process was completed on a cylinder one would find that it is not curved overall as the curvature around the cylinder cancels with the flatness along the cylinder, which is a consequence of [Gaussian curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaussian_curvature "Gaussian curvature") and Gauss's [Theorema Egregium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theorema_Egregium "Theorema Egregium"). A familiar example of this is a floppy pizza slice, which will remain rigid along its length if it is curved along its width.

The Riemann curvature tensor is a way to capture a measure of the intrinsic curvature. When you write it down in terms of its components (like writing down the components of a vector), it consists of a multi-dimensional array of sums and products of partial derivatives (some of those partial derivatives can be thought of as akin to capturing the curvature imposed upon someone walking in straight lines on a curved surface).

When a vector in a Euclidean space is [parallel transported](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_transport "Parallel transport") around a loop, it will again point in the initial direction after returning to its original position. However, this property does not hold in the general case. The Riemann curvature tensor directly measures the failure of this in a general [Riemannian manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_manifold "Riemannian manifold"). This failure is known as the non-[holonomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holonomy "Holonomy") of the manifold.

If there is nonzero [torsion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torsion_tensor "Torsion tensor"), the Bianchi identities involve the [torsion tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torsion_tensor "Torsion tensor").

The first (algebraic) Bianchi identity was discovered by [Ricci](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gregorio_Ricci-Curbastro "Gregorio Ricci-Curbastro"), but is often called the **first Bianchi identity** or **algebraic Bianchi identity**, because it looks similar to the differential [Bianchi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luigi_Bianchi "Luigi Bianchi") identity.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

The first three identities form a complete list of symmetries of the curvature tensor, i.e. given any tensor which satisfies the identities above, one can find a Riemannian manifold with such a curvature tensor at some point. Simple calculations show that such a tensor has ![Image 66: {\displaystyle n^{2}\left(n^{2}-1\right)/12}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f105296f0d0bc09c9bdfb4aa7ec73cb9254ba965) independent components.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_curvature_tensor#cite_note-9) Interchange symmetry follows from these. The algebraic symmetries are also equivalent to saying that _R_ belongs to the image of the [Young symmetrizer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Young_symmetrizer "Young symmetrizer") corresponding to the partition 2+2.

On a Riemannian manifold one has the covariant derivative ![Image 67: {\displaystyle \nabla _{u}R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/33f0f1975028ab91470cb70d18df8383804eee84) and the [Bianchi identity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bianchi_identity "Bianchi identity") (often called the second Bianchi identity or differential Bianchi identity) takes the form of the last identity in the table.

The [Ricci curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_curvature "Ricci curvature") tensor is the [contraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_contraction "Tensor contraction") of the first and third indices of the Riemann tensor.

![Image 68: {\displaystyle \underbrace {R_{ab}} _{\text{Ricci}}\equiv R^{c}{}_{acb}=g^{cd}\underbrace {R_{cadb}} _{\text{Riemann}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c6f57325acde2bab010c9726863d80e34684bfbe)
For a two-dimensional [surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_(topology) "Surface (topology)"), the Bianchi identities imply that the Riemann tensor has only one independent component, which means that the [Ricci scalar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_scalar "Ricci scalar") completely determines the Riemann tensor. There is only one valid expression for the Riemann tensor which fits the required symmetries:

and by contracting with the metric twice we find the explicit form:

![Image 70: {\displaystyle R_{abcd}=K\left(g_{ac}g_{db}-g_{ad}g_{cb}\right),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5eca86fc1a3e5ea151cf6e0b02190eac672992c0)
where ![Image 71: {\displaystyle g_{ab}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e69d450dd2c6434f120d72eba8865a4492121a2) is the [metric tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_tensor "Metric tensor") and ![Image 72: {\displaystyle K=R/2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/46380c338e4993d70a420e61939be16777142b51) is a function called the [Gaussian curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaussian_curvature "Gaussian curvature") and ![Image 73: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc), ![Image 74: {\displaystyle b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f11423fbb2e967f986e36804a8ae4271734917c3), ![Image 75: {\displaystyle c}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/86a67b81c2de995bd608d5b2df50cd8cd7d92455) and ![Image 76: {\displaystyle d}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e85ff03cbe0c7341af6b982e47e9f90d235c66ab) take values either 1 or 2. The Riemann tensor has only one functionally independent component. The Gaussian curvature coincides with the [sectional curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sectional_curvature "Sectional curvature") of the surface. It is also exactly half the [scalar curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_curvature "Scalar curvature") of the 2-manifold, while the [Ricci curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_curvature "Ricci curvature") tensor of the surface is simply given by

A Riemannian manifold is a [space form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_form "Space form") if its [sectional curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sectional_curvature "Sectional curvature") is equal to a constant ![Image 78: {\displaystyle K}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b76fce82a62ed5461908f0dc8f037de4e3686b0). The Riemann tensor of a space form is given by

![Image 79: {\displaystyle R_{abcd}=K\left(g_{ac}g_{db}-g_{ad}g_{cb}\right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/aacc7c91e743c91d5946713e4384da94a2abd688)
Conversely, except in dimension 2, if the curvature of a Riemannian manifold has this form for some function ![Image 80: {\displaystyle K}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b76fce82a62ed5461908f0dc8f037de4e3686b0), then the Bianchi identities imply that ![Image 81: {\displaystyle K}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b76fce82a62ed5461908f0dc8f037de4e3686b0) is constant and thus that the manifold is (locally) a space form.
