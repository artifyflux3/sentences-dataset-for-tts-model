A **sphere of influence** (**SOI**) in [astrodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrodynamics "Astrodynamics") and [astronomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy "Astronomy") is the [oblate spheroid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spheroid "Spheroid")-shaped region where a particular [celestial body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celestial_body "Celestial body") exerts the main [gravitational](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity "Gravity") influence on an [orbiting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbit "Orbit") object. This is usually used to describe the areas in the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System") where [planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planets "Planets") dominate the orbits of surrounding objects such as [moons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_satellite "Natural satellite"), despite the presence of the much more massive but distant [Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun "Sun").

In the [patched conic approximation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patched_conic_approximation "Patched conic approximation"), used in estimating the trajectories of bodies moving between the neighbourhoods of different bodies using a two-body approximation, ellipses and hyperbolae, the SOI is taken as the boundary where the trajectory switches which mass field it is influenced by. It is not to be confused with the [sphere of activity](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sphere_of_activity&action=edit&redlink=1 "Sphere of activity (page does not exist)") which extends well beyond the sphere of influence.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere_of_influence_(astrodynamics)#cite_note-Souami_Cresson_Biernacki_Pierret_p.-1)

The most common base models to calculate the sphere of influence are the [Hill sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hill_sphere "Hill sphere") and the [Laplace sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laplace_sphere "Laplace sphere"), but updated and particularly more dynamic ones have been described.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere_of_influence_(astrodynamics)#cite_note-Cavallari_Grassi_Gronchi_Ba%C3%B9_2023_p._107091-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere_of_influence_(astrodynamics)#cite_note-Araujo_Winter_Prado_Vieira_Martins_2008_pp._675%E2%80%93684-3) The general equation describing the [radius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radius "Radius") of the sphere ![Image 1: {\displaystyle r_{\text{SOI}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ee3bb5970544341e5d712a746e21b513994eef31) of a planet:[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere_of_influence_(astrodynamics)#cite_note-Seefelder-4)![Image 2: {\displaystyle r_{\text{SOI}}\approx a\left({\frac {m}{M}}\right)^{2/5}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b2714e6d7f0dad2482f560e0a341da91d7506b06) where

In the patched conic approximation, once an object leaves the planet's SOI, the primary/only gravitational influence is the Sun (until the object enters another body's SOI). Because the definition of _r_ SOI relies on the presence of the Sun and a planet, the term is only applicable in a [three-body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/N-body_problem "N-body problem") or greater system and requires the mass of the primary body to be much greater than the mass of the secondary body. This changes the three-body problem into a restricted two-body problem.

Increased accuracy on the SOI
-----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sphere_of_influence_(astrodynamics)&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Increased accuracy on the SOI")]

The Sphere of influence is, in fact, not quite a sphere. The distance to the SOI depends on the angular distance ![Image 4: {\displaystyle \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e5ab2664b422d53eb0c7df3b87e1360d75ad9af) from the massive body. Averaging over all possible directions we get: ![Image 6: {\displaystyle {\overline {r_{\text{SOI}}}}=0.9431a\left({\frac {m}{M}}\right)^{2/5}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cb366565bfa4c096cab14fbae7d19d05950b27a7)

Consider two point masses ![Image 7: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3) and ![Image 8: {\displaystyle B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47136aad860d145f75f3eed3022df827cee94d7a) at locations ![Image 9: {\displaystyle r_{A}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dbdfd7231b267d01d3319642a4ff3c1382981702) and ![Image 10: {\displaystyle r_{B}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c8c6c549d02366b12961786dfac0732e531da234), with mass ![Image 11: {\displaystyle m_{A}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b38fd8717ed74667ad31b45948df4058db8ddb35) and ![Image 12: {\displaystyle m_{B}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4d51a90fa9b077c0ac7ca77b8d68f09ea9efbf5c) respectively. Consider a frame centered on ![Image 20: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3). The gravity of ![Image 21: {\displaystyle B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47136aad860d145f75f3eed3022df827cee94d7a) is denoted as ![Image 22: {\displaystyle g_{B}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3c4b43aa900ad18b2b3712865fcfcb7d24534219) and will be treated as a perturbation to the dynamics of ![Image 23: {\displaystyle C}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4fc55753007cd3c18576f7933f6f089196732029) due to the gravity ![Image 24: {\displaystyle g_{A}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3c7ba6d327db9359457da13a61d21888cd7b0037) of body ![Image 25: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3). Hill sphere and Sphere Of Influence for Solar System bodies

The distance to the sphere of influence must thus satisfy ![Image 67: {\displaystyle {\frac {m_{B}}{m_{A}}}{\frac {r^{3}}{R^{3}}}={\frac {m_{A}}{m_{B}}}{\frac {R^{2}}{r^{2}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3c4d5d1e1e0b5ebe1156a3a875c65b5abb3d8c96) and so ![Image 68: {\displaystyle r=R\left({\frac {m_{A}}{m_{B}}}\right)^{2/5}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3f6ef20ada5e0aade319510569fbca45f834a226) is the radius of the sphere of influence of body ![Image 69: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3)

**Gravity well** (or funnel) is a metaphorical concept for a [gravitational field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_field "Gravitational field") of a mass, with the field being curved in a funnel-shaped well around the mass, illustrating the steep [gravitational potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_potential "Gravitational potential") and [its energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_potential_energy "Gravitational potential energy") that needs to be accounted for in order to escape or enter the main part of a sphere of influence.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere_of_influence_(astrodynamics)#cite_note-q926-11)

An example for this is the strong gravitational field of the [Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun "Sun") and [Mercury](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mercury_(planet) "Mercury (planet)") being deep within it.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere_of_influence_(astrodynamics)#cite_note-i513-12) At [perihelion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perihelion "Perihelion") Mercury goes even deeper into the Sun's gravity well, causing an anomalistic or perihelion [apsidal precession](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apsidal_precession#General_relativity "Apsidal precession") which is more recognizable than with other planets due to Mercury being deep in the gravity well. This characteristic of Mercury's orbit was famously calculated by [Albert Einstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Einstein "Albert Einstein") through his formulation of gravity with the [speed of light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light "Speed of light"), and the corresponding [general relativity theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity_theory "General relativity theory"), eventually being one of the first [cases proving the theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#Perihelion_precession_of_Mercury "Tests of general relativity").

[![Image 70](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Schwarzschild_effective_potential.svg/500px-Schwarzschild_effective_potential.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Schwarzschild_effective_potential.svg)

Gravity well illustrated with the [effective radial potentials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Effective_potential#Gravitational_potential "Effective potential") of [schwarzschild geodesics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schwarzschild_geodesics "Schwarzschild geodesics") for various angular momenta. Each point on the curves represent a radius or circular orbit and the curve represents their stability depending on the energy of their particle, with orbits therefore normally not remaining circular and migrating along the curve. At small radii, the energy drops precipitously, causing the particle to be pulled inexorably inwards to ![Image 71: {\textstyle r=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/60102717ce6167aad980e5ec2e8822c00bb63b66). However, when the normalized angular momentum ![Image 72: {\textstyle {\frac {a}{r_{\text{s}}}}={\frac {L}{mcr_{\text{s}}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/63ebe765f3c10b34e700fdf9ad12b3dcd2db8438) equals the square root of three, a metastable circular orbit is possible at the radius highlighted with a green circle. At higher angular momenta, there is a significant centrifugal barrier (orange curve) or _energy hill_[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere_of_influence_(astrodynamics)#cite_note-e554-13) and an unstable inner radius, highlighted in red.
