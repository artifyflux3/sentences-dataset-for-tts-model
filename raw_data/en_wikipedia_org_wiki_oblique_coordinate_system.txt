From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

A system of **skew coordinates**, sometimes called **oblique coordinates**, is a [curvilinear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvilinear_coordinates "Curvilinear coordinates")[coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_system "Coordinate system") where the [coordinate surfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_system#Coordinate_surface "Coordinate system") are not [orthogonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal "Orthogonal"),[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oblique_coordinate_system#cite_note-1) as in _[orthogonal coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal\_coordinates "Orthogonal coordinates")_.

Skew coordinates tend to be more complicated to work with compared to orthogonal coordinates since the [metric tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_tensor "Metric tensor") will have nonzero off-diagonal components, preventing many simplifications in formulas for [tensor algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_algebra "Tensor algebra") and [tensor calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_calculus "Tensor calculus"). The nonzero off-diagonal components of the metric tensor are a direct result of the non-orthogonality of the basis vectors of the coordinates, since by definition:[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oblique_coordinate_system#cite_note-p13-2)

![Image 1: {\displaystyle g_{ij}=\mathbf {e} _{i}\cdot \mathbf {e} _{j}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/509c54e78c5e35aab9c450571acd2bcba1040074)
where ![Image 2: {\displaystyle g_{ij}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e7c1130c3dec178129b287a3672c72f88e773832) is the metric tensor and ![Image 3: {\displaystyle \mathbf {e} _{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ba77c5a75ef9e230d1a36183785477a2eb3c5c1e) the (covariant) [basis vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basis_vector "Basis vector").

These coordinate systems can be useful if the geometry of a problem fits well into a skewed system. For example, solving [Laplace's equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laplace%27s_equation "Laplace's equation") in a [parallelogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram "Parallelogram") will be easiest when done in appropriately skewed coordinates.

Cartesian coordinates with one skewed axis
------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Skew_coordinates&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Cartesian coordinates with one skewed axis")]

[![Image 4](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/SkewCartesianSystem.svg/250px-SkewCartesianSystem.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:SkewCartesianSystem.svg)

A coordinate system where the _x_ axis has been bent toward the _z_ axis

The simplest 3D case of a skew coordinate system is a [Cartesian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinates "Cartesian coordinates") one where one of the axes (say the _x_ axis) has been bent by some angle ![Image 5: {\displaystyle \phi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/72b1f30316670aee6270a28334bdf4f5072cdde4), staying orthogonal to one of the remaining two axes. For this example, the _x_ axis of a Cartesian coordinate has been bent toward the _z_ axis by ![Image 6: {\displaystyle \phi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/72b1f30316670aee6270a28334bdf4f5072cdde4), remaining orthogonal to the _y_ axis.

### Algebra and useful quantities

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Skew_coordinates&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Algebra and useful quantities")]

Let ![Image 7: {\displaystyle \mathbf {e} _{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4aed8f473b59ea2b05d5720248b65333c35d3f0e), ![Image 8: {\displaystyle \mathbf {e} _{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5afa38f879249b10909c9dad8e9acf54dfa76286), and ![Image 9: {\displaystyle \mathbf {e} _{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/24cbe2f305c3398371155c651acadd5789addaf3) respectively be unit vectors along the ![Image 10: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4), ![Image 11: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d), and ![Image 12: {\displaystyle z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf368e72c009decd9b6686ee84a375632e11de98) axes. These represent the [covariant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covariance_and_contravariance_of_vectors "Covariance and contravariance of vectors") basis; computing their dot products gives the [metric tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_tensor "Metric tensor"):

![Image 13: {\displaystyle [g_{ij}]={\begin{pmatrix}1&0&\sin(\phi )\\0&1&0\\\sin(\phi )&0&1\end{pmatrix}},\qquad [g^{ij}]={\frac {1}{\cos ^{2}(\phi )}}{\begin{pmatrix}1&0&-\sin(\phi )\\0&\cos ^{2}(\phi )&0\\-\sin(\phi )&0&1\end{pmatrix}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bfa298130dc77c86691f753f20bd01e2825421e9)
where

![Image 14: {\displaystyle \quad g_{13}=\cos \left({\frac {\pi }{2}}-\phi \right)=\sin(\phi )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7f1168a86ccc4a38aababadbd9da2da5979c7c45)
and

![Image 15: {\displaystyle {\sqrt {g}}=\mathbf {e} _{1}\cdot (\mathbf {e} _{2}\times \mathbf {e} _{3})=\cos(\phi )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b82d2410a7fb617bb8953a5d86dd04341d99af69)
which are quantities that will be useful later on.

The contravariant basis is given by[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oblique_coordinate_system#cite_note-p13-2)

![Image 16: {\displaystyle \mathbf {e} ^{1}={\frac {\mathbf {e} _{2}\times \mathbf {e} _{3}}{\sqrt {g}}}={\frac {\mathbf {e} _{2}\times \mathbf {e} _{3}}{\cos(\phi )}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b13df9c85cf3cd61fc3942d5fe6d40245762a744)![Image 17: {\displaystyle \mathbf {e} ^{2}={\frac {\mathbf {e} _{3}\times \mathbf {e} _{1}}{\sqrt {g}}}=\mathbf {e} _{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8a23d75c56137d43c15622b83b8a069a067d19dc)![Image 18: {\displaystyle \mathbf {e} ^{3}={\frac {\mathbf {e} _{1}\times \mathbf {e} _{2}}{\sqrt {g}}}={\frac {\mathbf {e} _{1}\times \mathbf {e} _{2}}{\cos(\phi )}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/022001a074c79087f6088e405605abd10a3edf5e)
The contravariant basis isn't a very convenient one to use, however it shows up in definitions so must be considered. We'll favor writing quantities with respect to the covariant basis.

Since the basis vectors are all constant, vector addition and subtraction will simply be familiar component-wise adding and subtraction. Now, let

![Image 19: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} =\sum _{i}a^{i}\mathbf {e} _{i}\quad {\mbox{and}}\quad \mathbf {b} =\sum _{i}b^{i}\mathbf {e} _{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/44cc56ccf9db0cb4d6c654f1019c48e6d1d42514)
where the sums indicate summation over all values of the index (in this case, _i_ = 1, 2, 3). The [contravariant and covariant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covariance_and_contravariance_of_vectors "Covariance and contravariance of vectors") components of these vectors may be related by

![Image 20: {\displaystyle a^{i}=\sum _{j}a_{j}g^{ij}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81b9da14147d4dbdd6972f9e7f0516118c36373f)
so that, explicitly,

![Image 21: {\displaystyle a^{1}={\frac {a_{1}-\sin(\phi )a_{3}}{\cos ^{2}(\phi )}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d07200f2862248e6cadc152db8963c399c119559)![Image 22: {\displaystyle a^{2}=a_{2},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a330657d93eb23b0991550d3b1f6dc47925b6d32)![Image 23: {\displaystyle a^{3}={\frac {-\sin(\phi )a_{1}+a_{3}}{\cos ^{2}(\phi )}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/71fe000e143d4f20c6dbf2a03bd38e61c55baafb)
The [dot product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dot_product "Dot product") in terms of contravariant components is then

![Image 24: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} \cdot \mathbf {b} =\sum _{i}a^{i}b_{i}=a^{1}b^{1}+a^{2}b^{2}+a^{3}b^{3}+\sin(\phi )(a^{1}b^{3}+a^{3}b^{1})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/26bb1466e287042e2e2343964c17a0ee0a6ab2da)
and in terms of covariant components

![Image 25: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} \cdot \mathbf {b} ={\frac {1}{\cos ^{2}(\phi )}}[a_{1}b_{1}+a_{2}b_{2}\cos ^{2}(\phi )+a_{3}b_{3}-\sin(\phi )(a_{1}b_{3}+a_{3}b_{1})].}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f32fbf44972045564ba030979961b6c65fa5f8ca)
By definition,[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oblique_coordinate_system#cite_note-3) the [gradient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gradient "Gradient") of a scalar function _f_ is

![Image 26: {\displaystyle \nabla f=\sum _{i}\mathbf {e} ^{i}{\frac {\partial f}{\partial q^{i}}}={\frac {\partial f}{\partial x}}\mathbf {e} ^{1}+{\frac {\partial f}{\partial y}}\mathbf {e} ^{2}+{\frac {\partial f}{\partial z}}\mathbf {e} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bbce1b866460d2d7ebb45cd169069a6bf88fc2a9)
where ![Image 27: {\displaystyle q_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2752dcbff884354069fe332b8e51eb0a70a531b6) are the coordinates _x_, _y_, _z_ indexed. Recognizing this as a vector written in terms of the contravariant basis, it may be rewritten:

![Image 28: {\displaystyle \nabla f={\frac {{\frac {\partial f}{\partial x}}-\sin(\phi ){\frac {\partial f}{\partial z}}}{\cos(\phi )^{2}}}\mathbf {e} _{1}+{\frac {\partial f}{\partial y}}\mathbf {e} _{2}+{\frac {-\sin(\phi ){\frac {\partial f}{\partial x}}+{\frac {\partial f}{\partial z}}}{\cos(\phi )^{2}}}\mathbf {e} _{3}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/39a6e141c94f1623662e20ce0b269cf32c9d6f0d)
The [divergence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divergence "Divergence") of a vector ![Image 29: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1a957216653a9ee0d0133dcefd13fb75e36b8b9d) is

![Image 30: {\displaystyle \nabla \cdot \mathbf {a} ={\frac {1}{\sqrt {g}}}\sum _{i}{\frac {\partial }{\partial q^{i}}}\left({\sqrt {g}}a^{i}\right)={\frac {\partial a^{1}}{\partial x}}+{\frac {\partial a^{2}}{\partial y}}+{\frac {\partial a^{3}}{\partial z}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8a66b5b8a0e0c0bc517ca0966547e64e3dc1e424)
and of a tensor ![Image 31: {\displaystyle \mathbf {A} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0795cc96c75d81520a120482662b90f024c9a1a1)

![Image 32: {\displaystyle \nabla \cdot \mathbf {A} ={\frac {1}{\sqrt {g}}}\sum _{i,j}{\frac {\partial }{\partial q^{i}}}\left({\sqrt {g}}a^{ij}\mathbf {e} _{j}\right)=\sum _{i,j}\mathbf {e} _{j}{\frac {\partial a^{ij}}{\partial q^{i}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f27036c2c90539f56d915307b3e277ae4ff78c57)
The [Laplacian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laplacian "Laplacian") of _f_ is

![Image 33: {\displaystyle \nabla ^{2}f=\nabla \cdot \nabla f={\frac {1}{\cos(\phi )^{2}}}\left({\frac {\partial ^{2}f}{\partial x^{2}}}+{\frac {\partial ^{2}f}{\partial z^{2}}}-2\sin(\phi ){\frac {\partial ^{2}f}{\partial x\partial z}}\right)+{\frac {\partial ^{2}f}{\partial y^{2}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/08c58537c6617d022dbb0ea54a2c6b758ae22661)
and, since the covariant basis is normal and constant, the [vector Laplacian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_Laplacian "Vector Laplacian") is the same as the componentwise Laplacian of a vector written in terms of the covariant basis.

While both the dot product and gradient are somewhat messy in that they have extra terms (compared to a Cartesian system) the [advection operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Advection "Advection") which combines a dot product with a gradient turns out very simple:

![Image 34: {\displaystyle (\mathbf {a} \cdot \nabla )={\biggl (}\sum _{i}a^{i}e_{i}{\biggr )}\cdot {\biggl (}\sum _{i}{\frac {\partial }{\partial q^{i}}}\mathbf {e} ^{i}{\biggr )}={\biggl (}\sum _{i}a^{i}{\frac {\partial }{\partial q^{i}}}{\biggr )}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7ccd2b16314865db3523b58f228ac42d5bba2887)
which may be applied to both scalar functions and vector functions, componentwise when expressed in the covariant basis.

Finally, the [curl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curl_(mathematics) "Curl (mathematics)") of a vector is
