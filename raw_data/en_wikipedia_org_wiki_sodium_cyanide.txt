Chemical compound

**Sodium cyanide** is a [compound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_compound "Chemical compound") with the [formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_formula "Chemical formula")[Na](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium "Sodium")[C](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon "Carbon")[N](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen "Nitrogen") and the [structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_structure "Chemical structure")Na+−[C≡N](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyanide "Cyanide"). It is a white, [water-soluble](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solubility "Solubility") solid. [Cyanide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyanide "Cyanide") has a high affinity for metals, which leads to the high [toxicity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toxicity "Toxicity") of this [salt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salt_(chemistry) "Salt (chemistry)"). Its main application, in [gold mining](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gold_mining "Gold mining"), also exploits its high reactivity toward metals. It is a [moderately strong base](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strong_base "Strong base").

Production and chemical properties
----------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sodium_cyanide&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Production and chemical properties")]

Sodium cyanide is produced by treating [hydrogen cyanide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide "Hydrogen cyanide") with [sodium hydroxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide "Sodium hydroxide"):[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_cyanide#cite_note-Ullmann-4)

HCN + NaOH → NaCN + H 2 O
Worldwide production was estimated at 500,000 tons in the year 2006. Formerly it was prepared by the Castner process involving the reaction of [sodium amide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_amide "Sodium amide") with carbon at elevated temperatures.

NaNH 2 + C → NaCN + H 2
The structure of solid NaCN is related to that of [sodium chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride "Sodium chloride").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_cyanide#cite_note-5) The anions and cations are each six-coordinate. [Potassium cyanide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_cyanide "Potassium cyanide") (KCN) adopts a similar structure.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_cyanide#cite_note-6)

When treated with [acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acid "Acid"), it forms the toxic gas [hydrogen cyanide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide "Hydrogen cyanide"):

NaCN + H+ → HCN + Na+
Because the salt is derived from a weak acid, sodium cyanide readily reverts to HCN by [hydrolysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrolysis "Hydrolysis"); the moist solid emits small amounts of hydrogen cyanide, which is thought to smell like bitter [almonds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Almonds "Almonds") (not everyone can smell it—the ability thereof is due to a genetic trait[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_cyanide#cite_note-7)). Sodium cyanide reacts rapidly with strong acids to release hydrogen cyanide. This dangerous process represents a significant risk associated with cyanide salts. It is detoxified most efficiently with [hydrogen peroxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_peroxide "Hydrogen peroxide") (H 2 O 2) to produce [sodium cyanate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_cyanate "Sodium cyanate") (NaOCN) and water:[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_cyanide#cite_note-Ullmann-4)

NaCN + H 2 O 2 → NaOCN + H 2 O

Applications
------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sodium_cyanide&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Applications")]

### Cyanide mining

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sodium_cyanide&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Cyanide mining")]

[Gold cyanidation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gold_cyanidation "Gold cyanidation") (also known as the cyanide process) is the dominant technique for extracting [gold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gold "Gold"), much of which is obtained from low-grade [ore](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ore "Ore"). More than 70% of cyanide consumption globally is used for this purpose. The application exploits the high affinity of gold(I) for cyanide, which induces gold metal to oxidize and dissolve in the presence of air (oxygen) and water, producing the salt **sodium dicyanoaurate** (or sodium gold cyanide) (NaAu(CN)2):[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_cyanide#cite_note-Ullmann-4)

4 Au + 8 NaCN + O 2 + 2 H 2 O → 4 Na[Au(CN)2] + 4 NaOH
A similar process uses [potassium cyanide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_cyanide "Potassium cyanide") (KCN, a close relative of sodium cyanide) to produce [potassium dicyanoaurate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_dicyanoaurate "Potassium dicyanoaurate") (KAu(CN)2).

These soluble forms of gold (and silver) can be converted back to the metals by electrolysis.

### Chemical feedstock

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sodium_cyanide&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Chemical feedstock")]

Some commercially significant chemicals are derived from cyanide: [cyanuric chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyanuric_chloride "Cyanuric chloride"), [cyanogen chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyanogen_chloride "Cyanogen chloride"), and [cyanogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyanogen "Cyanogen").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_cyanide#cite_note-Ullmann-4)

Several specialty [nitriles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrile "Nitrile") may be produced by alkylation of cyanide, a strong [nucleophile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleophile "Nucleophile"). Illustrative is the synthesis of [benzyl cyanide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzyl_cyanide "Benzyl cyanide") by the reaction of [benzyl chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzyl_chloride "Benzyl chloride") and sodium cyanide:[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_cyanide#cite_note-8)

C 6 H 5 CH 2 Cl + NaCN → C 6 H 5 CH 2 CN + NaCl

### Electroplating

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sodium_cyanide&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Electroplating")]

Cyanide baths (solutions) of metal ions are used to [electroplate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electroplating "Electroplating") many metals, including [platinum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platinum "Platinum"), gold, and silver. [Cadmium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cadmium "Cadmium") plating gives excellent corrosion resistance and is achieved using [cadmium oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cadmium_oxide "Cadmium oxide") and sodium cyanide.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_cyanide#cite_note-9) Copper 'strikes' (thin high quality layers) are traditionally formed using cyanide electroplating, these thin interstitial layers allow adhesion between metals that are usually incompatible, such as chromium and aluminium.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

### Niche uses

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sodium_cyanide&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Niche uses")]

Being highly toxic, sodium cyanide is used to kill or stun rapidly such as in collecting jars used by [entomologists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entomologist "Entomologist") and in widely illegal [cyanide fishing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyanide_fishing "Cyanide fishing").

It was used as an [insecticide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Insecticide "Insecticide"), [rodenticide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rodenticide "Rodenticide") and [antibacterial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antibiotic "Antibiotic"), but these uses were cancelled by the [EPA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency "United States Environmental Protection Agency") in 1987.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_cyanide#cite_note-10)

Toxicity
--------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sodium_cyanide&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Toxicity")]

Main article: [Cyanide poisoning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyanide_poisoning "Cyanide poisoning")

Sodium cyanide, like other soluble cyanide salts, is among the most rapidly acting of all known poisons. NaCN is a potent inhibitor of [respiration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cellular_respiration "Cellular respiration"), acting on mitochondrial [cytochrome oxidase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cytochrome_oxidase "Cytochrome oxidase") and hence blocking electron transport. This results in decreased oxidative metabolism and oxygen utilization. [Lactic acidosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lactic_acidosis "Lactic acidosis") then occurs as a consequence of anaerobic metabolism. An oral dosage as small as 200–300 mg can be fatal.

Disposal
--------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sodium_cyanide&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Disposal")]

Due to toxicity considerations, the disposal of cyanide is subject to stringent regulations. Industrial cyanide effluent is typically destroyed by oxidation using [peroxysulfuric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peroxysulfuric_acid_(disambiguation) "Peroxysulfuric acid (disambiguation)"), [hydrogen peroxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_peroxide "Hydrogen peroxide"), [sulfur dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide "Sulfur dioxide")/copper salts ("Inco process") or all three ("Combiox Process"). Use of [sodium hypochlorite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_hypochlorite "Sodium hypochlorite"), traditional for laboratory-scale wastes, is impractical on a commercial scale. Hydrolysis at higher temperatures is highly effective, but requires specialized equipment. Lastly, cyanide wastes can be acidified for recovery of [hydrogen cyanide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide "Hydrogen cyanide").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_cyanide#cite_note-Ullmann-4)
