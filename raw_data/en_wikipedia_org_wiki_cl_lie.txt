In mathematics , a Clélie or Clelia curve is a curve on a sphere with the property: [ 1 ] If the surface of a sphere is described as usual by the longitude (angle φ φ {\displaystyle \varphi } ) and the colatitude (angle θ θ {\displaystyle \theta } ) then φ φ = c θ θ , c > 0 {\displaystyle \varphi =c\;\theta ,\quad c>0} .

The curve was named by Luigi Guido Grandi after Clelia Borromeo .

[ 2 ] [ 3 ] [ 4 ] Viviani's curve and spherical spirals are special cases of Clelia curves. In practice Clelia curves occur as the ground track of satellites in polar circular orbits , i.e., whose traces on the earth include the poles. If the orbit is a geosynchronous one, then c = 1 {\displaystyle c=1} and the trace is a Viviani's curve.

Parametric representation [ edit ] If the sphere of radius r {\displaystyle r} is parametrized in the spherical coordinate system by x = r ⋅ ⋅ cos ⁡ ⁡ θ θ ⋅ ⋅ cos ⁡ ⁡ φ φ y = r ⋅ ⋅ cos ⁡ ⁡ θ θ ⋅ ⋅ sin ⁡ ⁡ φ φ z = r ⋅ ⋅ sin ⁡ ⁡ θ θ {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}x&=r\cdot \cos \theta \cdot \cos \varphi \\y&=r\cdot \cos \theta \cdot \sin \varphi \\z&=r\cdot \sin \theta \end{aligned}}} where θ θ {\displaystyle \theta } and φ φ {\displaystyle \varphi } are angles, the longitude and latitude (respectively) of a point on the sphere
and these two angles are connected by a linear equation φ φ = c θ θ {\displaystyle \;\varphi =c\theta } , then using this equation to replace φ φ {\displaystyle \varphi } gives a parametric representation of a Clelia curve: x = r ⋅ ⋅ cos ⁡ ⁡ θ θ ⋅ ⋅ cos ⁡ ⁡ c θ θ y = r ⋅ ⋅ cos ⁡ ⁡ θ θ ⋅ ⋅ sin ⁡ ⁡ c θ θ z = r ⋅ ⋅ sin ⁡ ⁡ θ θ .

{\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}x&=r\cdot \cos \theta \cdot \cos c\theta \\y&=r\cdot \cos \theta \cdot \sin c\theta \\z&=r\cdot \sin \theta .\end{aligned}}} Examples [ edit ] Any Clelia curve meets the poles at least once.

For example: In case of c = 1 / n {\displaystyle \;c=1/n\;} the period is n ⋅ ⋅ 2 π π {\displaystyle \;n\cdot 2\pi \;} . If c {\displaystyle c} is a non rational number, the curve is not periodic.
