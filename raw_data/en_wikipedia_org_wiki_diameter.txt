In [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry"), a **diameter** of a [circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle "Circle") is any straight [line segment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_segment "Line segment") that passes through the centre of the circle and whose endpoints lie on the circle. It can also be defined as the longest [chord](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chord_(geometry) "Chord (geometry)") of the circle. Both definitions are also valid for the diameter of a [sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere "Sphere").

In more modern usage, the length d{\displaystyle d}![Image 16: {\displaystyle d}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e85ff03cbe0c7341af6b982e47e9f90d235c66ab) of a diameter is also called the diameter. In this sense one speaks of _the_ diameter rather than _a_ diameter (which refers to the line segment itself), because all diameters of a circle or sphere have the same length, this being twice the [radius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radius "Radius")r.{\displaystyle r.}![Image 17: {\displaystyle r.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/10110093812676dd04a92ce4c8b75940c366330a)

d=2 r or equivalently r=d 2.{\displaystyle d=2r\qquad {\text{or equivalently}}\qquad r={\frac {d}{2}}.}![Image 18: {\displaystyle d=2r\qquad {\text{or equivalently}}\qquad r={\frac {d}{2}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/be9b94eccd93d133fe5b031701c7eb68100b0ed5)
The word "diameter" is derived from [Ancient Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language "Ancient Greek language"): διάμετρος (_diametros_), "diameter of a circle", from διά (_dia_), "across, through" and μέτρον (_metron_), "measure".[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diameter#cite_note-1) It is often abbreviated DIA,dia,d,{\displaystyle {\text{DIA}},{\text{dia}},d,}![Image 19: {\displaystyle {\text{DIA}},{\text{dia}},d,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b6ddffcfdd51fedcf32cfba5b7c91feabc2b1263) or ∅.{\displaystyle \varnothing .}![Image 20: {\displaystyle \varnothing .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4a03ccd230ba31b26d48893930e4da31eaefeb05)

Constructions
-------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Diameter&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Constructions")]

With [straightedge and compass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Straightedge_and_compass "Straightedge and compass"), a diameter of a given circle can be constructed as the [perpendicular bisector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular_bisector "Perpendicular bisector") of an arbitrary chord. Drawing two diameters in this way can be used to locate the center of a circle, as their crossing point.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diameter#cite_note-2) To construct a diameter parallel to a given line, choose the chord to be perpendicular to the line.

The circle having a given line segment as its diameter can be constructed by straightedge and compass, by finding the [midpoint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Midpoint "Midpoint") of the segment and then drawing the circle centered at the midpoint through one of the ends of the line segment.

Generalizations
---------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Diameter&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Generalizations")]

The definitions given above are only valid for circles and spheres. However, they are special cases of a more general definition that is valid for any kind of n{\displaystyle n}![Image 23: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)-dimensional object, or a [set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_(mathematics) "Set (mathematics)") of scattered points. The _[diameter of a set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diameter\_of\_a\_set "Diameter of a set")_ is the [least upper bound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supremum "Supremum") of the set of all distances between pairs of points in the subset.

A different and incompatible definition is sometimes used for the diameter of a [conic section](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conic_section "Conic section"). In this context, a diameter is any [chord](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chord_(geometry) "Chord (geometry)") which passes through the [conic's centre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Center_(geometry)#Projective_conics "Center (geometry)"). A diameter of an [ellipse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse "Ellipse") is any line passing through the centre of the ellipse.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diameter#cite_note-7) Half of any such diameter may be called a _**semidiameter**_, although this term is most often a synonym for the [radius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radius "Radius") of a circle or sphere.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diameter#cite_note-8) The longest and shortest diameters are called the _[major axis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Major\_axis "Major axis")_ and _minor axis_, respectively. _[Conjugate diameters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conjugate\_diameters "Conjugate diameters")_ are a pair of diameters where one is parallel to a tangent to the ellipse at the endpoint of the other diameter.

Several kinds of object can be measured by _[equivalent diameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equivalent\_diameter "Equivalent diameter")_, the diameter of a circular or spherical approximation to the object. This includes [hydraulic diameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydraulic_diameter "Hydraulic diameter"), the equivalent diameter of a channel or pipe through which liquid flows, and the [Sauter mean diameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sauter_mean_diameter "Sauter mean diameter") of a collection of particles.

The diameter of a circle is exactly twice its radius. However, this is true only for a circle, and only in the [Euclidean metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_distance "Euclidean distance"). [Jung's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jung%27s_theorem "Jung's theorem") provides more general inequalities relating the diameter to the radius.

*   [Tangent lines to circles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_lines_to_circles "Tangent lines to circles")– Line which touches a circle at exactly one point
