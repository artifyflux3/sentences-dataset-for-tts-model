**Naphthalene** is an [organic compound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_compound "Organic compound") with formula [C](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon "Carbon")

10[H](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen")

8. It is the simplest [polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polycyclic_aromatic_hydrocarbon "Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbon"), and is a white [crystalline solid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystal "Crystal") with a characteristic odor that is detectable at concentrations as low as 0.08[ppm by mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parts-per_notation "Parts-per notation").[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naphthalene#cite_note-15) As an [aromatic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aromaticity "Aromaticity") hydrocarbon, naphthalene's structure consists of a fused pair of [benzene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene "Benzene") rings. It is the main ingredient of traditional [mothballs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mothball "Mothball").

In the early 1820s, two separate reports described a white solid with a pungent odor derived from the [distillation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distillation "Distillation") of [coal tar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coal_tar "Coal tar"). In 1821, [John Kidd](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Kidd_(chemist) "John Kidd (chemist)") cited these two disclosures and then described many of this substance's properties and the means of its production. He proposed the name _naphthaline_, as it had been derived from a kind of [naphtha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naphtha "Naphtha") (a broad term encompassing any volatile, flammable liquid hydrocarbon mixture, including coal tar).[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naphthalene#cite_note-16) Naphthalene's chemical formula was determined by [Michael Faraday](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michael_Faraday "Michael Faraday") in 1826. The structure of two fused [benzene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene "Benzene") rings was proposed by [Emil Erlenmeyer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emil_Erlenmeyer "Emil Erlenmeyer") in 1866,[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naphthalene#cite_note-17) and confirmed by [Carl Gräbe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Gr%C3%A4be "Carl Gräbe") three years later.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naphthalene#cite_note-18)

Physical properties
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Naphthalene&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Physical properties")]

A naphthalene molecule can be viewed as the fusion of a pair of [benzene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene "Benzene") rings. (In [organic chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_chemistry "Organic chemistry"), rings are _fused_ if they share two or more atoms.) As such, naphthalene is classified as a benzenoid [polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polycyclic_aromatic_hydrocarbon "Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbon") (PAH).[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naphthalene#cite_note-19)

The eight carbon atoms that are not shared by the two rings carry one hydrogen atom each. For purpose of the standard [IUPAC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IUPAC "IUPAC") nomenclature of derived compounds, those eight atoms are numbered 1 through 8 in sequence around the perimeter of the molecule, starting with a carbon atom adjacent to a shared one. The shared carbon atoms are labeled 4a (between 4 and 5) and 8a (between 8 and 1).[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naphthalene#cite_note-20)

The molecule is planar, like benzene. Unlike benzene, the carbon–carbon bonds in naphthalene are not of the same length. The bonds C1−C2, C3−C4, C5−C6 and C7−C8 are about 1.37 Å (137 pm) in length, whereas the other carbon–carbon bonds are about 1.42 Å (142 pm) long. This difference, established by [X-ray diffraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray_diffraction "X-ray diffraction"),[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naphthalene#cite_note-21) is consistent with the [valence bond](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Valence_bond_theory "Valence bond theory") model in naphthalene and in particular, with the theorem of [cross-conjugation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross-conjugation "Cross-conjugation"). This theorem would describe naphthalene as an [aromatic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aromaticity "Aromaticity") benzene unit bonded to a [diene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diene "Diene") but not extensively [conjugated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conjugated_system "Conjugated system") to it (at least in the [ground state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ground_state "Ground state")), which is consistent with two of its three [resonance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resonance_(chemistry) "Resonance (chemistry)") structures.

[![Image 23: Resonance structures of naphthalene](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Naphthalene_resonance_structure.svg/500px-Naphthalene_resonance_structure.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Naphthalene_resonance_structure.svg "Resonance structures of naphthalene")
Because of this resonance, the molecule has [bilateral symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bilateral_symmetry "Bilateral symmetry") across the plane of the shared carbon pair, as well as across the plane that bisects bonds C2-C3 and C6-C7, and across the plane of the carbon atoms. Thus there are two sets of equivalent hydrogen atoms: the _alpha_ positions, numbered 1, 4, 5, and 8, and the _beta_ positions, 2, 3, 6, and 7. Two [isomers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isomer "Isomer") are then possible for mono-substituted naphthalenes, corresponding to substitution at an alpha or beta position.

[![Image 24: Alpha vs beta symmetry-equivalent positions](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Naftalene_reattivita.PNG/180px-Naftalene_reattivita.PNG)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Naftalene_reattivita.PNG "Alpha vs beta symmetry-equivalent positions")[![Image 25](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Azulen.svg/120px-Azulen.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Azulen.svg)

Azulene

[Structural isomers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Structural_isomer "Structural isomer") of naphthalene that have two fused aromatic rings include [azulene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Azulene "Azulene"), which has a 5–7 fused ring system, and [Bicyclo[6.2.0]decapentaene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bicyclo(6.2.0)decapentaene "Bicyclo(6.2.0)decapentaene") which has a fused 4–8 ring system.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naphthalene#cite_note-22)

The point group symmetry of naphthalene is _D 2h_.

### Electrical conductivity

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Naphthalene&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Electrical conductivity")]

Pure crystalline naphthalene is a moderate insulator at room temperature, with [resistivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resistivity "Resistivity") of about 10 12[Ω](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ohm "Ohm")[m](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metre "Metre"). The resistivity drops more than a thousandfold on melting, to about 4 × 10 8 Ω m. Both in the liquid and in the solid, the resistivity depends on temperature as _ρ_ = _ρ_ 0 exp(_E_/(_kT_)), where _ρ_ 0 (Ω⋅m) and _E_ (eV) are constant parameters, _k_ is the Boltzmann constant (8.617 × 10−5 eV/[K](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvin "Kelvin")), and _T_ is absolute temperature (K). The parameter _E_ is 0.73 in the solid. However, the solid shows semiconducting character below 100 K.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naphthalene#cite_note-born1962-23)[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naphthalene#cite_note-sche1978-24)

### Reactions with electrophiles

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Naphthalene&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Reactions with electrophiles")]

In [electrophilic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrophile "Electrophile") aromatic [substitution reactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Substitution_reaction "Substitution reaction"), naphthalene reacts more readily than benzene. For example, chlorination and bromination of naphthalene proceeds without a [catalyst](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catalysis "Catalysis") to give [1-chloronaphthalene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1-chloronaphthalene "1-chloronaphthalene") and [1-bromonaphthalene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1-Bromonaphthalene "1-Bromonaphthalene"), respectively. Likewise, whereas both benzene and naphthalene can be [alkylated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkylated_naphthalene "Alkylated naphthalene") using [Friedel–Crafts reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedel%E2%80%93Crafts_reaction "Friedel–Crafts reaction") conditions, naphthalene can also be easily alkylated by reaction with [alkenes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkene "Alkene") or [alcohols](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alcohol_(chemistry) "Alcohol (chemistry)"), using [sulfuric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfuric_acid "Sulfuric acid") or [phosphoric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphoric_acid "Phosphoric acid") catalysts.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naphthalene#cite_note-Ullmann-25) Contrariwise, anhydrous [aluminium chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aluminium_chloride "Aluminium chloride") reacts with naphthalene to give a hexamer, in which one ring of each naphthalene monomer loses aromaticity, linking to the other monomers at the 1 and 4 positions.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naphthalene#cite_note-26)

In terms of [regiochemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regiochemistry "Regiochemistry"), electrophiles attack at the alpha position. The selectivity for alpha over beta substitution can be rationalized in terms of the resonance structures of the intermediate: for the alpha substitution intermediate, seven resonance structures can be drawn, of which four preserve an aromatic ring. For beta substitution, the intermediate has only six resonance structures, and only two of these are aromatic. [Sulfonation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedel-Crafts_reaction#Friedel%E2%80%93Crafts_dealkylation "Friedel-Crafts reaction") gives the "alpha" product [naphthalene-1-sulfonic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naphthalene-1-sulfonic_acid "Naphthalene-1-sulfonic acid") as the kinetic product but [naphthalene-2-sulfonic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naphthalene-2-sulfonic_acid "Naphthalene-2-sulfonic acid") as the thermodynamic product. The 1-isomer forms predominantly at 25°C, and the 2-isomer at 160°C. [Sulfonation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedel-Crafts_reaction#Friedel%E2%80%93Crafts_dealkylation "Friedel-Crafts reaction") to give the 1- and 2-sulfonic acid occurs readily:

H 2 SO 4 + C 10 H 8 → C 10 H 7 SO 3 H + H 2 O
Further sulfonation give di-, tri-, and tetrasulfonic acids.

Analogous to the synthesis of [phenyllithium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phenyllithium "Phenyllithium") is the conversion of 1-bromonaphthalene to 1-lithionaphthalene, by [lithium–halogen exchange](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lithium%E2%80%93halogen_exchange "Lithium–halogen exchange"):

C 10 H 7 Br + BuLi → C 10 H 7 Li + BuBr
The resulting lithionaphthalene undergoes a second lithiation, in contrast to the behavior of phenyllithium. These 1,8-dilithio derivatives are precursors to a host of [peri-naphthalene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peri-naphthalene "Peri-naphthalene") derivatives.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naphthalene#cite_note-27)

### Reduction and oxidation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Naphthalene&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Reduction and oxidation")]

With alkali metals, naphthalene forms the dark blue-green radical anion salts such as [sodium naphthalene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_naphthalene "Sodium naphthalene"), Na+C 10 H−

8. The naphthalene anions are strong reducing agents.

Naphthalene can be [hydrogenated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogenate "Hydrogenate") under high pressure in the presence of metal [catalysts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catalyst "Catalyst") to give 1,2,3,4-tetrahydronaphthalene(C

10 H

12), also known as [tetralin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetralin "Tetralin"). Further hydrogenation yields decahydronaphthalene or [decalin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decalin "Decalin") (C

10 H

18).

Oxidation with O

2 in the presence of [vanadium pentoxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vanadium_pentoxide "Vanadium pentoxide") as [catalyst](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catalyst "Catalyst") gives [phthalic anhydride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phthalic_anhydride "Phthalic anhydride"):

C 10 H 8 + 4.5 O 2 → C 6 H 4(CO)2 O + 2 CO 2 + 2 H 2 O
This reaction is the basis of the main use of naphthalene. [Oxidation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxidation "Oxidation") can also be effected using conventional stoichiometric [chromate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chromate_and_dichromate "Chromate and dichromate") or [permanganate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permanganate "Permanganate") reagents.

[![Image 26](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Naphthalene.jpg/250px-Naphthalene.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Naphthalene.jpg)

Naphthalene

From the 1960s until the 1990s, significant amounts of naphthalene were produced from heavy [petroleum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petroleum "Petroleum") fractions during [refining](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oil_refinery "Oil refinery"), but present-day production is mainly from [coal tar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coal_tar "Coal tar").[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naphthalene#cite_note-28) As of 2023, the global napthalene market was 2.25 million tons.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naphthalene#cite_note-29)

Naphthalene is the most abundant single component of coal tar.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naphthalene#cite_note-:0-30) The composition of coal tar varies with coal type and processing, but typical coal tar is about 10% naphthalene by weight.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naphthalene#cite_note-31) In industrial practice, [distillation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distillation "Distillation") of coal tar yields an oil containing about 50% naphthalene, along with twelve other [aromatic compounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aromatic_compound "Aromatic compound").[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naphthalene#cite_note-:0-30) This oil, after being washed with aqueous [sodium hydroxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide "Sodium hydroxide") to remove [acidic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acid "Acid") components (chiefly various [phenols](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phenol "Phenol")), and with sulfuric acid to remove [basic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Base_(chemistry) "Base (chemistry)") components, undergoes [fractional distillation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractional_distillation "Fractional distillation") to isolate naphthalene. The crude naphthalene resulting from this process is about 95% naphthalene by weight. The chief impurities are the sulfur-containing aromatic compound [benzothiophene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzothiophene "Benzothiophene") (<2%), [indane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indane "Indane") (0.2%), [indene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indene "Indene") (<2%), and [methylnaphthalene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methylnaphthalene "Methylnaphthalene") (<2%). Petroleum-derived naphthalene is usually purer than that derived from coal tar. Where required, crude naphthalene can be further purified by [recrystallization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recrystallization_(chemistry) "Recrystallization (chemistry)") from any of a variety of solvents, resulting in 99% naphthalene by weight, referred to as 80°C (melting point).[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naphthalene#cite_note-Ullmann-25)

In [North America](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_America "North America"), the coal tar producers are [Koppers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Koppers "Koppers") Inc., Ruetgers Canada Inc. and Recochem Inc., and the primary petroleum producer is Monument Chemical Inc. In Western Europe the well-known producers are Koppers, Ruetgers, and Deza. In [Eastern Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastern_Europe "Eastern Europe"), naphthalene is produced by a variety of integrated [metallurgy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metallurgy "Metallurgy") complexes (Severstal, Evraz, Mechel, MMK) in [Russia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russia "Russia"), dedicated naphthalene and phenol makers INKOR, Yenakievsky Metallurgy plant in [Ukraine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ukraine "Ukraine") and ArcelorMittal Temirtau in [Kazakhstan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kazakhstan "Kazakhstan").

### Other sources and occurrences

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Naphthalene&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Other sources and occurrences")]

Naphthalene and its alkyl [homologs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homologous_series "Homologous series") are the major constituents of [creosote](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Creosote "Creosote").

Trace amounts of naphthalene are produced by [magnolias](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnolia "Magnolia") and some species of [deer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deer "Deer"), as well as the [Formosan subterranean termite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formosan_subterranean_termite "Formosan subterranean termite"), possibly produced by the termite as a repellant against "ants, [poisonous fungi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poisonous_fungus "Poisonous fungus") and [nematode](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nematode "Nematode") worms".[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naphthalene#cite_note-32) Some strains of the [endophytic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endophytic "Endophytic") fungus _[Muscodor albus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muscodor\_albus "Muscodor albus")_ produce naphthalene among a range of volatile organic compounds, while _[Muscodor vitigenus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muscodor\_vitigenus "Muscodor vitigenus")_ produces naphthalene almost exclusively.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naphthalene#cite_note-33)

Naphthalene is used mainly as a precursor to derivative chemicals. The single largest use of naphthalene is the industrial production of [phthalic anhydride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phthalic_anhydride "Phthalic anhydride"), although more phthalic anhydride is made from [_o_-xylene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/O-Xylene "O-Xylene").

Naphthalene has been used as a [fumigant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fumigant "Fumigant"). It was once the primary ingredient in [mothballs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mothball "Mothball"), although its use has largely been replaced in favor of alternatives such as [1,4-dichlorobenzene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1,4-Dichlorobenzene "1,4-Dichlorobenzene"). In a sealed container containing naphthalene pellets, naphthalene vapors build up to levels toxic to both the adult and larval forms of many [moths](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moth "Moth") that attack textiles. Other [fumigant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fumigation "Fumigation") uses of naphthalene include use in soil as a fumigant [pesticide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pesticide "Pesticide"), in [attic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Attic "Attic") spaces to repel [insects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Insect "Insect") and animals such as [opossums](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Opossum "Opossum"),[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naphthalene#cite_note-34) and in museum storage-drawers and cupboards to protect the contents from attack by insect pests.

Molten naphthalene provides an excellent solubilizing medium for poorly soluble aromatic compounds. In many cases it is more efficient than other high-boiling solvents, such as [dichlorobenzene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dichlorobenzene "Dichlorobenzene"), [benzonitrile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzonitrile "Benzonitrile"), [nitrobenzene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrobenzene "Nitrobenzene") and [durene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Durene "Durene"). The reaction of [C 60](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene "Buckminsterfullerene") with [anthracene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anthracene "Anthracene") is conveniently conducted in refluxing naphthalene to give the 1:1 [Diels–Alder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diels%E2%80%93Alder_reaction "Diels–Alder reaction") adduct.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naphthalene#cite_note-35) The aromatization of hydroporphyrins has been achieved using a solution of [DDQ](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2,3-Dichloro-5,6-dicyano-1,4-benzoquinone "2,3-Dichloro-5,6-dicyano-1,4-benzoquinone") in naphthalene.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naphthalene#cite_note-36)

The single largest use of naphthalene is the production of [phthalic anhydride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phthalic_anhydride "Phthalic anhydride"), which is an intermediate used to make [plasticizers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasticizer "Plasticizer") for [polyvinyl chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyvinyl_chloride "Polyvinyl chloride"), and to make [alkyd resin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkyd_resin "Alkyd resin") polymers used in paints and varnishes.

#### Sulfonic acids and sulfonates

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Naphthalene&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: Sulfonic acids and sulfonates")]

Many [naphthalenesulfonic acids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naphthalene-2-sulfonic_acid "Naphthalene-2-sulfonic acid") and sulfonates are useful. Naphthalenesulfonic acids are used in the synthesis of [1-naphthol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1-naphthol "1-naphthol") and [2-naphthol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2-naphthol "2-naphthol"), precursors for various dyestuffs, pigments, rubber processing chemicals and other chemicals and pharmaceuticals.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naphthalene#cite_note-Ullmann-25) They are also used as dispersants in synthetic and natural rubbers, in agricultural [pesticides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pesticides "Pesticides"), in dyes, and in [lead–acid battery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lead%E2%80%93acid_battery "Lead–acid battery") plates. Naphthalenedisulfonic acids such as [Armstrong's acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Armstrong%27s_acid "Armstrong's acid") are used as precursors and to form pharmaceutical salts such as [CFT](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WIN-35428 "WIN-35428").

The [aminonaphthalenesulfonic acids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aminonaphthalenesulfonic_acids "Aminonaphthalenesulfonic acids") are precursors for synthesis of many synthetic [dyes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dye "Dye").

Alkyl [naphthalene sulfonates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naphthalenesulfonate "Naphthalenesulfonate") (ANS) are used in many industrial applications as nondetergent [surfactants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surfactant "Surfactant") (wetting agents) that effectively disperse colloidal systems in aqueous media. The major commercial applications are in the agricultural chemical industry, which uses ANS for wettable powder and wettable granular (dry-flowable) formulations, and in the textile and fabric industry, which uses the wetting and defoaming properties of ANS for bleaching and dyeing operations.

Some naphthalenesulfonate [polymers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polymer "Polymer") are [superplasticizers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superplasticizer "Superplasticizer") used for the production of high strength [concrete](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concrete "Concrete") as well as water reducers in the production of gypsum wallboard.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naphthalene#cite_note-37) They are produced by treating naphthalenesulfonic acid with [formaldehyde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde "Formaldehyde"), followed by neutralization with [sodium hydroxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide "Sodium hydroxide") or [calcium hydroxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calcium_hydroxide "Calcium hydroxide").

#### Other derivative uses

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Naphthalene&action=edit&section=16 "Edit section: Other derivative uses")]

[![Image 27](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Propranolol.svg/250px-Propranolol.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Propranolol.svg)

[Propranolol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propranolol "Propranolol") is a [beta blocker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beta_blocker "Beta blocker").

Many [azo dyes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Azo_dye "Azo dye") are produced from naphthalene. Useful [agrichemicals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agrichemical "Agrichemical") include naphthoxyacetic acids.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naphthalene#cite_note-Ullmann-25)

Hydrogenation of naphthalene gives tetrahydronaphthalene ([tetralin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetralin "Tetralin")) and decahydronaphthalene ([decalin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decalin "Decalin")), which are used as low-volatility [solvents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solvents "Solvents"). Tetralin is used as a hydrogen-donor solvent.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naphthalene#cite_note-Ullmann-25)

[Alkylation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkylation "Alkylation") of naphthalene with propylene gives a mixture of [diisopropylnaphthalenes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diisopropylnaphthalenes "Diisopropylnaphthalenes"), which are useful as nonvolatile liquids for inks.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naphthalene#cite_note-Ullmann-25)

Substituted naphthalenes serve as pharmaceuticals such as [propranolol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propranolol "Propranolol") (a [beta blocker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beta_blocker "Beta blocker")) and [nabumetone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nabumetone "Nabumetone") (a [nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drug](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nonsteroidal_anti-inflammatory_drug "Nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drug")).

Several uses stem from naphthalene's high volatility: it is used to create artificial pores in the manufacture of high-porosity [grinding wheels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grinding_wheel "Grinding wheel"); it is used in engineering studies of heat transfer using mass [sublimation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sublimation_(phase_transition) "Sublimation (phase transition)"); and it has been explored as a sublimable propellant for cold gas satellite thrusters.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naphthalene#cite_note-38)[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naphthalene#cite_note-39)

Exposure to large amounts of naphthalene may damage or destroy [red blood cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_blood_cell "Red blood cell"), most commonly in people with the inherited condition known as [glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase (G6PD) deficiency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glucose-6-phosphate_dehydrogenase_deficiency "Glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase deficiency"),[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naphthalene#cite_note-40) from which approximately 400 million people suffer.[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naphthalene#cite_note-GHR2017-41) Humans, in particular children, have developed the condition known as [hemolytic anemia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hemolytic_anemia "Hemolytic anemia"), after ingesting mothballs or deodorant blocks containing naphthalene. Symptoms include [fatigue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fatigue_(medical) "Fatigue (medical)"), lack of appetite, restlessness, and pale skin. Exposure to large amounts of naphthalene may cause [confusion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confusion "Confusion"), [nausea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nausea "Nausea"), [vomiting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vomiting "Vomiting"), [diarrhea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diarrhea "Diarrhea"), [blood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood "Blood") in the [urine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urine "Urine"), and [jaundice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jaundice "Jaundice") (yellow coloration of the skin due to dysfunction of the [liver](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liver "Liver")).[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naphthalene#cite_note-42)

The US [National Toxicology Program](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Toxicology_Program "National Toxicology Program") (NTP) held an experiment where male and female rats and mice were exposed to naphthalene vapors on weekdays for two years.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naphthalene#cite_note-43) Both male and female rats exhibited evidence of [carcinogenesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carcinogenic "Carcinogenic") with increased incidences of [adenoma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adenoma "Adenoma") and [neuroblastoma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neuroblastoma "Neuroblastoma") of the nose. Female mice exhibited some evidence of carcinogenesis based on increased incidences of [alveolar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulmonary_alveolus "Pulmonary alveolus") and [bronchiolar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bronchiole "Bronchiole")[adenomas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adenoma "Adenoma") of the [lung](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lung "Lung"), while male mice exhibited no evidence of carcinogenesis.

The [International Agency for Research on Cancer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Agency_for_Research_on_Cancer "International Agency for Research on Cancer") (IARC)[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naphthalene#cite_note-44) classifies naphthalene as possibly carcinogenic to humans and animals ([Group 2B](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_IARC_Group_2B_carcinogens "List of IARC Group 2B carcinogens")). The IARC also points out that acute exposure causes [cataracts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cataract "Cataract") in humans, [rats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rat "Rat"), [rabbits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rabbit "Rabbit"), and [mice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mouse "Mouse"); and that hemolytic anemia (described above) can occur in children and infants after oral or inhalation exposure or after maternal exposure during pregnancy. A probable mechanism for the carcinogenic effects of mothballs and some types of air fresheners containing naphthalene has been identified.[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naphthalene#cite_note-45)[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naphthalene#cite_note-EHANS-46)

[US government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federal_government_of_the_United_States "Federal government of the United States") agencies have set [occupational exposure limits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Occupational_exposure_limit "Occupational exposure limit") to naphthalene exposure. The [Occupational Safety and Health Administration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Occupational_Safety_and_Health_Administration "Occupational Safety and Health Administration") has set a [permissible exposure limit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permissible_exposure_limit "Permissible exposure limit") at 10 ppm (50 mg/m 3) over an eight-hour time-weighted average. The [National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Institute_for_Occupational_Safety_and_Health "National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health") has set a [recommended exposure limit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recommended_exposure_limit "Recommended exposure limit") at 10 ppm (50 mg/m 3) over an eight-hour time-weighted average, as well as a [short-term exposure limit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Short-term_exposure_limit "Short-term exposure limit") at 15 ppm (75 mg/m 3).[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naphthalene#cite_note-47) Naphthalene's minimum odor threshold is 0.084 ppm for humans.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naphthalene#cite_note-48)

Mothballs and other products containing naphthalene have been banned within the [EU](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Union "European Union") since 2008.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naphthalene#cite_note-Alderson-49)[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naphthalene#cite_note-Gray-50)

In [China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China "China"), the use of naphthalene in mothballs is forbidden.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naphthalene#cite_note-51) Danger to human health and the common use of natural [camphor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camphor "Camphor") are cited as reasons for the ban.
