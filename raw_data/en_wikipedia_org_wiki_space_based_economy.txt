From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Economic activity in space

Not to be confused with [Space industry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_industry "Space industry") or [Commercial use of space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commercial_use_of_space "Commercial use of space").

**Space-based economy** is economic activity in [outer space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_space "Outer space"), including [asteroid mining](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asteroid_mining "Asteroid mining"), [space manufacturing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_manufacturing "Space manufacturing"), [space trade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_trade "Space trade"), [space burial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_burial "Space burial"), [space advertising](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_advertising "Space advertising") and construction performed in space such as the building of [space stations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_station "Space station").

Space-based industrial efforts are presently in their infancy. Most such concepts would require a considerable long-term human presence in space and relatively low-cost access to space. The majority of proposals would also require technological or engineering developments in areas such as [robotics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robotics "Robotics"), [solar energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_energy "Solar energy"), and [life support systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life_support_system "Life support system").

A major catalyst in the development of a space economy has come from the rise of [reusable launch vehicles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reusable_launch_vehicle "Reusable launch vehicle") and [spacecraft](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reusable_spacecraft "Reusable spacecraft") in 2010s and the 2020s.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space-based_economy#cite_note-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space-based_economy#cite_note-2)

Some analysts have argued for creating an International Bank, to support deep space exploration.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space-based_economy#cite_note-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space-based_economy#cite_note-4)

Law
---

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Space-based_economy&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Law")]

The [1967 Outer Space Treaty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty "Outer Space Treaty") establishes that space, as the "province of all mankind," shall be freely explored and used by all nations, and that the use of the [moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moon "Moon") and all other [celestial bodies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_object "Astronomical object") shall be for peaceful purposes.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space-based_economy#cite_note-5)

In 2015, the U.S. Congress passed a law explicitly allowing American companies to use resources from the moon and asteroids, the [Commercial Space Launch Competitiveness Act of 2015](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commercial_Space_Launch_Competitiveness_Act_of_2015 "Commercial Space Launch Competitiveness Act of 2015").

In April 2020, the [U.S. Executive Order](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Executive_order "Executive order") 13914 clarified the position of the U.S. government on the use of space resources and how the United States will foster the commercial development of space resources.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space-based_economy#cite_note-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space-based_economy#cite_note-7) “Americans should have the right to engage in commercial exploration, recovery, and use of resources in outer space,” the order states.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space-based_economy#cite_note-8)
