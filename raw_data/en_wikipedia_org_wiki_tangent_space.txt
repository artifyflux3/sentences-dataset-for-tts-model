In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), the **tangent space** of a [manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manifold "Manifold") is a generalization of _[tangent lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent\_line "Tangent line")_ to curves in [two-dimensional space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_(mathematics) "Plane (mathematics)") and _[tangent planes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent#Tangent\_plane\_to\_a\_surface "Tangent")_ to surfaces in [three-dimensional space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_space "Three-dimensional space") in higher dimensions. In the context of physics, the tangent space to a manifold at a point can be viewed as the space of possible velocities for a particle moving on the manifold.

Informal description
--------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tangent_space&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Informal description")]

[![Image 1](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Tangent_Plane.svg/250px-Tangent_Plane.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Tangent_Plane.svg)

A pictorial representation of the tangent space of a single point ![Image 2: {\displaystyle P(r,\theta ,\phi )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4519fba54b04ec5f751f757c5fe8743dec1f3147) on a [sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere "Sphere"). A vector in this tangent space represents a possible velocity (of something moving on the sphere) at ![Image 3: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a). After moving in that direction to a nearby point, the velocity would then be given by a vector in the tangent space of that point—a different tangent space that is not shown.

In [differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry"), one can attach to every point ![Image 4: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) of a [differentiable manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differentiable_manifold "Differentiable manifold") a _tangent space_—a real [vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space") that intuitively contains the possible directions in which one can tangentially pass through ![Image 5: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4). The elements of the tangent space at ![Image 6: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) are called the _[tangent vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent\_vector "Tangent vector")_ at ![Image 7: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4). This is a generalization of the notion of a [vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_(mathematics_and_physics) "Vector (mathematics and physics)"), based at a given initial point, in a [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space"). The [dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension_of_a_vector_space "Dimension of a vector space") of the tangent space at every point of a [connected](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connected_space "Connected space") manifold is the same as that of the [manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manifold "Manifold") itself.

For example, if the given manifold is a ![Image 8: {\displaystyle 2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/901fc910c19990d0dbaaefe4726ceb1a4e217a0f)-[sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere "Sphere"), then one can picture the tangent space at a point as the plane that touches the sphere at that point and is [perpendicular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular "Perpendicular") to the sphere's radius through the point. More generally, if a given manifold is thought of as an [embedded](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Embedding "Embedding")[submanifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Submanifold "Submanifold") of [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space"), then one can picture a tangent space in this literal fashion. This was the traditional approach toward defining [parallel transport](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_transport "Parallel transport"). Many authors in [differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry") and [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity") use it.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_space#cite_note-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_space#cite_note-2) More strictly, this defines an affine tangent space, which is distinct from the space of tangent vectors described by modern terminology.

In [algebraic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_geometry "Algebraic geometry"), in contrast, there is an intrinsic definition of the _tangent space at a point_ of an [algebraic variety](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_variety "Algebraic variety")![Image 9: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) that gives a vector space with dimension at least that of ![Image 10: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) itself. The points ![Image 11: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) at which the dimension of the tangent space is exactly that of ![Image 12: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) are called _non-singular_ points; the others are called _singular_ points. For example, a curve that crosses itself does not have a unique tangent line at that point. The singular points of ![Image 13: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) are those where the "test to be a manifold" fails. See [Zariski tangent space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zariski_tangent_space "Zariski tangent space").

Once the tangent spaces of a manifold have been introduced, one can define [vector fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_field "Vector field"), which are abstractions of the velocity field of particles moving in space. A vector field attaches to every point of the manifold a vector from the tangent space at that point, in a smooth manner. Such a vector field serves to define a generalized [ordinary differential equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordinary_differential_equation "Ordinary differential equation") on a manifold: A solution to such a differential equation is a differentiable [curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curve "Curve") on the manifold whose derivative at any point is equal to the tangent vector attached to that point by the vector field.

All the tangent spaces of a manifold may be "glued together" to form a new differentiable manifold with twice the dimension of the original manifold, called the _[tangent bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent\_bundle "Tangent bundle")_ of the manifold.

The informal description above relies on a manifold's ability to be embedded into an ambient vector space ![Image 14: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{m}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6a87a024931038d1858dc22e8a194e5978c3412e) so that the tangent vectors can "stick out" of the manifold into the ambient space. However, it is more convenient to define the notion of a tangent space based solely on the manifold itself.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_space#cite_note-Isham2002-3)

There are various equivalent ways of defining the tangent spaces of a manifold. While the definition via the velocity of curves is intuitively the simplest, it is also the most cumbersome to work with. More elegant and abstract approaches are described below.

### Definition via tangent curves

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tangent_space&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Definition via tangent curves")]

In the embedded-manifold picture, a tangent vector at a point ![Image 15: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) is thought of as the _velocity_ of a [curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curve#Topology "Curve") passing through the point ![Image 16: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4). We can therefore define a tangent vector as an equivalence class of curves passing through ![Image 17: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) while being tangent to each other at ![Image 18: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4).

Suppose that ![Image 19: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) is a ![Image 20: {\displaystyle C^{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/167fdb0cfb5644c4623b5842e1a9141acd83b534)[differentiable manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differentiable_manifold "Differentiable manifold") (with [smoothness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smoothness "Smoothness")![Image 21: {\displaystyle k\geq 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d30d7dcf305b7bce39d36df72fe3985b47aa9961)) and that ![Image 22: {\displaystyle x\in M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9df57d73e9532bb93a1439890bcddbc2806f5859). Pick a [coordinate chart](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manifold#Charts,_atlases,_and_transition_maps "Manifold")![Image 23: {\displaystyle \varphi :U\to \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b0ee82f15526d60ae0cf3d5f1ba6a71f05463f9), where ![Image 24: {\displaystyle U}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/458a728f53b9a0274f059cd695e067c430956025) is an [open subset](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_set "Open set") of ![Image 25: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) containing ![Image 26: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4). Suppose further that two curves ![Image 27: {\displaystyle \gamma _{1},\gamma _{2}:(-1,1)\to M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7c933988e74346f1c4156a5ceeb64a21943cb916) with ![Image 28: {\displaystyle {\gamma _{1}}(0)=x={\gamma _{2}}(0)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1ce2d7ab5a8bf40be37c53018e610c666199055f) are given such that both ![Image 29: {\displaystyle \varphi \circ \gamma _{1},\varphi \circ \gamma _{2}:(-1,1)\to \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/34c8673ef266dcb4d5fe3fb3a2882c3789eaa175) are differentiable in the ordinary sense (we call these _differentiable curves initialized at ![Image 30: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest\_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4)_). Then ![Image 31: {\displaystyle \gamma _{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6c2d6da217f4d6e937887bad1c90b371314830f7) and ![Image 32: {\displaystyle \gamma _{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/482832093b568cdc09c3aeaa2585c5fc49100b63) are said to be _equivalent_ at ![Image 33: {\displaystyle 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2aae8864a3c1fec9585261791a809ddec1489950) if and only if the derivatives of ![Image 34: {\displaystyle \varphi \circ \gamma _{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4eda1f9c271264ec5cfb2a86e232946d17795938) and ![Image 35: {\displaystyle \varphi \circ \gamma _{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dec237f60e74ae1d86714541f971bcae0f718241) at ![Image 36: {\displaystyle 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2aae8864a3c1fec9585261791a809ddec1489950) coincide. This defines an [equivalence relation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equivalence_relation "Equivalence relation") on the set of all differentiable curves initialized at ![Image 37: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4), and [equivalence classes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equivalence_class "Equivalence class") of such curves are known as _tangent vectors_ of ![Image 38: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) at ![Image 39: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4). The equivalence class of any such curve ![Image 40: {\displaystyle \gamma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a223c880b0ce3da8f64ee33c4f0010beee400b1a) is denoted by ![Image 41: {\displaystyle \gamma '(0)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3db2fc37b07de2a9a0b049fd9444f571e11edc6f). The _tangent space_ of ![Image 42: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) at ![Image 43: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4), denoted by ![Image 44: {\displaystyle T_{x}M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0e9a02a3b6f9a6808be3b99d0b27d1b97b4bb025), is then defined as the set of all tangent vectors at ![Image 45: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4); it does not depend on the choice of coordinate chart ![Image 46: {\displaystyle \varphi :U\to \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b0ee82f15526d60ae0cf3d5f1ba6a71f05463f9).

[![Image 47](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Tangentialvektor.svg/250px-Tangentialvektor.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Tangentialvektor.svg)

The tangent space ![Image 48: {\displaystyle T_{x}M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0e9a02a3b6f9a6808be3b99d0b27d1b97b4bb025) and a tangent vector ![Image 49: {\displaystyle v\in T_{x}M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2f41985e80139b950c3dc0984e06bc9e36b6ef3c), along a curve traveling through ![Image 50: {\displaystyle x\in M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9df57d73e9532bb93a1439890bcddbc2806f5859).

To define vector-space operations on ![Image 51: {\displaystyle T_{x}M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0e9a02a3b6f9a6808be3b99d0b27d1b97b4bb025), we use a chart ![Image 52: {\displaystyle \varphi :U\to \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b0ee82f15526d60ae0cf3d5f1ba6a71f05463f9) and define a [map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Map_(mathematics) "Map (mathematics)")![Image 53: {\displaystyle \mathrm {d} {\varphi }_{x}:T_{x}M\to \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8a358e65195be95d639ab0cc58474033ae461f6a) by ![Image 54: {\textstyle {\mathrm {d} {\varphi }_{x}}(\gamma '(0)):={\frac {\mathrm {d} (\varphi \circ \gamma )}{\mathrm {d} {t}}}(0),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/12bce430e2657b5d15889ce4629d03ae412edcdd) where ![Image 55: {\displaystyle \gamma \in \gamma '(0)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c5ee640cf3e108d74e8d5838f552e02dcb3daff3). The map ![Image 56: {\displaystyle \mathrm {d} {\varphi }_{x}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/308f11a8722b8b6e8d81f1d453216411bb97f298) turns out to be [bijective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bijective "Bijective") and may be used to transfer the vector-space operations on ![Image 57: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d) over to ![Image 58: {\displaystyle T_{x}M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0e9a02a3b6f9a6808be3b99d0b27d1b97b4bb025), thus turning the latter set into an ![Image 59: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)-dimensional real vector space. Again, one needs to check that this construction does not depend on the particular chart ![Image 60: {\displaystyle \varphi :U\to \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b0ee82f15526d60ae0cf3d5f1ba6a71f05463f9) and the curve ![Image 61: {\displaystyle \gamma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a223c880b0ce3da8f64ee33c4f0010beee400b1a) being used, and in fact it does not.

### Definition via derivations

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tangent_space&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Definition via derivations")]

Suppose now that ![Image 62: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) is a ![Image 63: {\displaystyle C^{\infty }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/971ed05871d69309df32efdfd2020128c9cf69d8) manifold. A real-valued function ![Image 64: {\displaystyle f:M\to \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3f7c129dfb3446a2251aabc1cb3eba0b13ee7b21) is said to belong to ![Image 65: {\displaystyle {C^{\infty }}(M)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6a4f556427803c000d0ff61248491b81203842d2) if and only if for every coordinate chart ![Image 66: {\displaystyle \varphi :U\to \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b0ee82f15526d60ae0cf3d5f1ba6a71f05463f9), the map ![Image 67: {\displaystyle f\circ \varphi ^{-1}:\varphi [U]\subseteq \mathbb {R} ^{n}\to \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8aeb6f9eb7559fb915f3645b06bf2d1da494d814) is infinitely differentiable. Note that ![Image 68: {\displaystyle {C^{\infty }}(M)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6a4f556427803c000d0ff61248491b81203842d2) is a real [associative algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Associative_algebra "Associative algebra") with respect to the [pointwise product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pointwise_product "Pointwise product") and sum of functions and scalar multiplication.

A _[derivation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivation\_(abstract\_algebra) "Derivation (abstract algebra)")_ at ![Image 69: {\displaystyle x\in M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9df57d73e9532bb93a1439890bcddbc2806f5859) is defined as a [linear map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_map "Linear map")![Image 70: {\displaystyle D:{C^{\infty }}(M)\to \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f0fd5b183c9ce9f16453a4bcf43c95697e098fa0) that satisfies the Leibniz identity ![Image 71: {\displaystyle \forall f,g\in {C^{\infty }}(M):\qquad D(fg)=D(f)\cdot g(x)+f(x)\cdot D(g),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5f7d8691d46fa243b5d2dca29c913d66d43e6e3a) which is modeled on the [product rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Product_rule "Product rule") of calculus.

(For every identically constant function ![Image 72: {\displaystyle f={\text{const}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af5186528b4356957dc89445791dfe0cd5c8a843) it follows that ![Image 73: {\displaystyle D(f)=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ed5e2fc2c1dc63439f0f204f447fe79bf4d6bdde)).

*   ![Image 76: {\displaystyle (D_{1}+D_{2})(f):={D}_{1}(f)+{D}_{2}(f)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bd592ff7d4d9f5d13698193a28436049a92addfc) and
*   ![Image 77: {\displaystyle (\lambda \cdot D)(f):=\lambda \cdot D(f)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ce60e5b4400a9e497ebb08d663cc6662556b5f88)

turns ![Image 78: {\displaystyle T_{x}M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0e9a02a3b6f9a6808be3b99d0b27d1b97b4bb025) into a vector space.

Generalizations of this definition are possible, for instance, to [complex manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_manifold "Complex manifold") and [algebraic varieties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_variety "Algebraic variety"). However, instead of examining derivations ![Image 79: {\displaystyle D}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f34a0c600395e5d4345287e21fb26efd386990e6) from the full algebra of functions, one must instead work at the level of [germs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germ_(mathematics) "Germ (mathematics)") of functions. The reason for this is that the [structure sheaf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Structure_sheaf "Structure sheaf") may not be [fine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Injective_sheaf#Fine_sheaves "Injective sheaf") for such structures. For example, let ![Image 80: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) be an algebraic variety with [structure sheaf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Structure_sheaf "Structure sheaf")![Image 81: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {O}}_{X}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9fed6a46b79218af44f23e5d6f487fb7e0d6cd01). Then the [Zariski tangent space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zariski_tangent_space "Zariski tangent space") at a point ![Image 82: {\displaystyle p\in X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fae5d7ae3f7710fc989d0bd6dca038d74107f16d) is the collection of all ![Image 83: {\displaystyle \mathbb {k} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/21ff8b374f407dec6b1b2f35866c8e8cd0fb150d)-derivations ![Image 84: {\displaystyle D:{\mathcal {O}}_{X,p}\to \mathbb {k} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/98e9c835c10886dfe5f50e9fac60f34222d686a9), where ![Image 85: {\displaystyle \mathbb {k} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/21ff8b374f407dec6b1b2f35866c8e8cd0fb150d) is the [ground field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ground_field "Ground field") and ![Image 86: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {O}}_{X,p}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/20a040ebe65d99937e169707a887ab56b1ce6240) is the [stalk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stalk_(sheaf) "Stalk (sheaf)") of ![Image 87: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {O}}_{X}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9fed6a46b79218af44f23e5d6f487fb7e0d6cd01) at ![Image 88: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36).

### Equivalence of the definitions

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tangent_space&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Equivalence of the definitions")]

For ![Image 89: {\displaystyle x\in M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9df57d73e9532bb93a1439890bcddbc2806f5859) and a differentiable curve ![Image 90: {\displaystyle \gamma :(-1,1)\to M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3298d98305847aeb60fdc7cacdea38e827e0076a) such that ![Image 91: {\displaystyle \gamma (0)=x,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c8125125b13a215f25ca749bcca6bd65c2614904) define ![Image 92: {\displaystyle {D_{\gamma }}(f):=(f\circ \gamma )'(0)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/067a9225f3b3923ae3e16033b96fd05576d95e94) (where the derivative is taken in the ordinary sense because ![Image 93: {\displaystyle f\circ \gamma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7eaed1ad841924bc2c2ed3ecc68a03c97058ff25) is a function from ![Image 94: {\displaystyle (-1,1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e120a3bd60fc89b495dd7ef6039465b7e6a703b1) to ![Image 95: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc)). One can ascertain that ![Image 96: {\displaystyle D_{\gamma }(f)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/883781ac73fadb33a0cc440f1ae9377cb2ee47cf) is a derivation at the point ![Image 97: {\displaystyle x,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/feff4d40084c7351bf57b11ba2427f6331f5bdbe) and that equivalent curves yield the same derivation. Thus, for an equivalence class ![Image 98: {\displaystyle \gamma '(0),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ddc189af60079149d6c8580345121a18d3a290f0) we can define ![Image 99: {\displaystyle {D_{\gamma '(0)}}(f):=(f\circ \gamma )'(0),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/04827103d4b74d3a7bc7eb640428747a17376cdc) where the curve ![Image 100: {\displaystyle \gamma \in \gamma '(0)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c5ee640cf3e108d74e8d5838f552e02dcb3daff3) has been chosen arbitrarily. The map ![Image 101: {\displaystyle \gamma '(0)\mapsto D_{\gamma '(0)}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0cb322404a7d9485917791e22a7cad0133644b39) is a vector space isomorphism between the space of the equivalence classes ![Image 102: {\displaystyle \gamma '(0)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3db2fc37b07de2a9a0b049fd9444f571e11edc6f) and the space of derivations at the point ![Image 103: {\displaystyle x.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d07e9f568a88785ae48006ac3c4b951020f1699a)

### Definition via cotangent spaces

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tangent_space&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Definition via cotangent spaces")]

Again, we start with a ![Image 104: {\displaystyle C^{\infty }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/971ed05871d69309df32efdfd2020128c9cf69d8) manifold ![Image 105: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) and a point ![Image 106: {\displaystyle x\in M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9df57d73e9532bb93a1439890bcddbc2806f5859). Consider the [ideal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ideal_(ring_theory) "Ideal (ring theory)")![Image 107: {\displaystyle I}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/535ea7fc4134a31cbe2251d9d3511374bc41be9f) of ![Image 108: {\displaystyle C^{\infty }(M)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/48b543485314a4426ee2ff956f206cfd802d68d6) that consists of all smooth functions ![Image 109: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) vanishing at ![Image 110: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4), i.e., ![Image 111: {\displaystyle f(x)=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cf85883d74b75fe35ca8d3f2b44802df078e4fa1). Then ![Image 112: {\displaystyle I}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/535ea7fc4134a31cbe2251d9d3511374bc41be9f) and ![Image 113: {\displaystyle I^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1b35524ccbf943a202cfbb05ea9aaf6609a29ca0) are both real vector spaces, and the [quotient space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quotient_space_(linear_algebra) "Quotient space (linear algebra)")![Image 114: {\displaystyle I/I^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/19d4b3432c9980cba099ba225470ef439bd08262) can be shown to be [isomorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isomorphism "Isomorphism") to the [cotangent space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cotangent_space "Cotangent space")![Image 115: {\displaystyle T_{x}^{*}M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2f4c6a27dfd5f136186c72d25c457ceaba3f467d) through the use of [Taylor's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taylor%27s_theorem "Taylor's theorem"). The tangent space ![Image 116: {\displaystyle T_{x}M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0e9a02a3b6f9a6808be3b99d0b27d1b97b4bb025) may then be defined as the [dual space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_space "Dual space") of ![Image 117: {\displaystyle I/I^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/19d4b3432c9980cba099ba225470ef439bd08262).

While this definition is the most abstract, it is also the one that is most easily transferable to other settings, for instance, to the [varieties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_variety "Algebraic variety") considered in [algebraic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_geometry "Algebraic geometry").

If ![Image 118: {\displaystyle D}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f34a0c600395e5d4345287e21fb26efd386990e6) is a derivation at ![Image 119: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4), then ![Image 120: {\displaystyle D(f)=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ed5e2fc2c1dc63439f0f204f447fe79bf4d6bdde) for every ![Image 121: {\displaystyle f\in I^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5e04da54e71d3e8b076967e20c115b9ef1b0b55), which means that ![Image 122: {\displaystyle D}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f34a0c600395e5d4345287e21fb26efd386990e6) gives rise to a linear map ![Image 123: {\displaystyle I/I^{2}\to \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3b153da65a0f0686986963e09d62d948aa741a2e). Conversely, if ![Image 124: {\displaystyle r:I/I^{2}\to \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5cccc9b187f53851b58b6a6979605512c30c5879) is a linear map, then ![Image 125: {\displaystyle D(f):=r\left((f-f(x))+I^{2}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8943b3aabe66d48dea90fcd4b69a89597604c267) defines a derivation at ![Image 126: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4). This yields an equivalence between tangent spaces defined via derivations and tangent spaces defined via cotangent spaces.

If ![Image 127: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) is an open subset of ![Image 128: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d), then ![Image 129: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) is a ![Image 130: {\displaystyle C^{\infty }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/971ed05871d69309df32efdfd2020128c9cf69d8) manifold in a natural manner (take coordinate charts to be [identity maps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Identity_function "Identity function") on open subsets of ![Image 131: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d)), and the tangent spaces are all naturally identified with ![Image 132: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d).

### Tangent vectors as directional derivatives

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tangent_space&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Tangent vectors as directional derivatives")]

Another way to think about tangent vectors is as [directional derivatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Directional_derivative "Directional derivative"). Given a vector ![Image 133: {\displaystyle v}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e07b00e7fc0847fbd16391c778d65bc25c452597) in ![Image 134: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d), one defines the corresponding directional derivative at a point ![Image 135: {\displaystyle x\in \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c520ee2cb6ccf8a93c89a8c58a8378796bd52e53) by

![Image 136: {\displaystyle \forall f\in {C^{\infty }}(\mathbb {R} ^{n}):\qquad (D_{v}f)(x):=\left.{\frac {\mathrm {d} }{\mathrm {d} {t}}}[f(x+tv)]\right|_{t=0}=\sum _{i=1}^{n}v^{i}{\frac {\partial f}{\partial x^{i}}}(x).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/69094ef746485e599f1b39a5fbb70dd6730caa70)
This map is naturally a derivation at ![Image 137: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4). Furthermore, every derivation at a point in ![Image 138: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d) is of this form. Hence, there is a one-to-one correspondence between vectors (thought of as tangent vectors at a point) and derivations at a point.

As tangent vectors to a general manifold at a point can be defined as derivations at that point, it is natural to think of them as directional derivatives. Specifically, if ![Image 139: {\displaystyle v}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e07b00e7fc0847fbd16391c778d65bc25c452597) is a tangent vector to ![Image 140: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) at a point ![Image 141: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) (thought of as a derivation), then define the directional derivative ![Image 142: {\displaystyle D_{v}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6a7721f1f570d606715d4c046488254a537c85b1) in the direction ![Image 143: {\displaystyle v}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e07b00e7fc0847fbd16391c778d65bc25c452597) by

![Image 144: {\displaystyle \forall f\in {C^{\infty }}(M):\qquad {D_{v}}(f):=v(f).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e2716ebb9de7810a87ac633a6d5d177c5f60de07)
If we think of ![Image 145: {\displaystyle v}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e07b00e7fc0847fbd16391c778d65bc25c452597) as the initial velocity of a differentiable curve ![Image 146: {\displaystyle \gamma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a223c880b0ce3da8f64ee33c4f0010beee400b1a) initialized at ![Image 147: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4), i.e., ![Image 148: {\displaystyle v=\gamma '(0)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2720acd204fd0bf59df01609d5edc934709ba1c3), then instead, define ![Image 149: {\displaystyle D_{v}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6a7721f1f570d606715d4c046488254a537c85b1) by

![Image 150: {\displaystyle \forall f\in {C^{\infty }}(M):\qquad {D_{v}}(f):=(f\circ \gamma )'(0).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/02dace064db1c0bc5e794e7c37a3566fd0272ebe)

### Basis of the tangent space at a point

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tangent_space&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Basis of the tangent space at a point")]

For a ![Image 151: {\displaystyle C^{\infty }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/971ed05871d69309df32efdfd2020128c9cf69d8) manifold ![Image 152: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd), if a chart ![Image 153: {\displaystyle \varphi =(x^{1},\ldots ,x^{n}):U\to \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/def7fce9e71231721608f16bc357fdcf5ea4c6af) is given with ![Image 154: {\displaystyle p\in U}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/66f04c97c84237ad610995f15df5cd7fd0034d3e), then one can define an ordered basis ![Image 155: {\textstyle \left\{\left.{\frac {\partial }{\partial x^{1}}}\right|_{p},\dots ,\left.{\frac {\partial }{\partial x^{n}}}\right|_{p}\right\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3f00cd199876ae6361c97acd71f0f9ba024c8341) of ![Image 156: {\displaystyle T_{p}M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/71b20e0304a65ead2cafb33412a383b34fe527ca) by

![Image 157: {\displaystyle \forall i\in \{1,\ldots ,n\},~\forall f\in {C^{\infty }}(M):\qquad {\left.{\frac {\partial }{\partial x^{i}}}\right|_{p}}(f):=\left({\frac {\partial }{\partial x^{i}}}{\Big (}f\circ \varphi ^{-1}{\Big )}\right){\Big (}\varphi (p){\Big )}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f25c5a7752dc5deb9c27d8ac6f5a1b264ff9a962)
Then for every tangent vector ![Image 158: {\displaystyle v\in T_{p}M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8cde43d4dac32b2325e477be95a948eec8a77617), one has

![Image 159: {\displaystyle v=\sum _{i=1}^{n}v^{i}\left.{\frac {\partial }{\partial x^{i}}}\right|_{p}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2de78e563eb40ebf8c928f1e7a2826e0ce968ce6)
This formula therefore expresses ![Image 160: {\displaystyle v}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e07b00e7fc0847fbd16391c778d65bc25c452597) as a linear combination of the basis tangent vectors ![Image 161: {\textstyle \left.{\frac {\partial }{\partial x^{i}}}\right|_{p}\in T_{p}M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9427d424756292189d077bf1e9478397d5e91951) defined by the coordinate chart ![Image 162: {\displaystyle \varphi :U\to \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b0ee82f15526d60ae0cf3d5f1ba6a71f05463f9).[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_space#cite_note-4)

### The derivative of a map

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tangent_space&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: The derivative of a map")]

Every smooth (or differentiable) map ![Image 163: {\displaystyle \varphi :M\to N}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d7dd52f475d4d090b5f59f745e773f63d3176a01) between smooth (or differentiable) manifolds induces natural [linear maps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_map "Linear map") between their corresponding tangent spaces:

![Image 164: {\displaystyle \mathrm {d} {\varphi }_{x}:T_{x}M\to T_{\varphi (x)}N.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1f6fe130b2f7a984e87bdf278590b3edd1927282)
If the tangent space is defined via differentiable curves, then this map is defined by

![Image 165: {\displaystyle {\mathrm {d} {\varphi }_{x}}(\gamma '(0)):=(\varphi \circ \gamma )'(0).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2055e9a46b300448e527ec5d90e2d7ffcd81f5ab)
If, instead, the tangent space is defined via derivations, then this map is defined by

![Image 166: {\displaystyle [\mathrm {d} {\varphi }_{x}(D)](f):=D(f\circ \varphi ).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fbde4a6ca4ae02c2b63585e4f743d4de1eb0c8ad)
The linear map ![Image 167: {\displaystyle \mathrm {d} {\varphi }_{x}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/308f11a8722b8b6e8d81f1d453216411bb97f298) is called variously the _derivative_, _total derivative_, _differential_, or _pushforward_ of ![Image 168: {\displaystyle \varphi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/33ee699558d09cf9d653f6351f9fda0b2f4aaa3e) at ![Image 169: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4). It is frequently expressed using a variety of other notations:

![Image 170: {\displaystyle D\varphi _{x},\qquad (\varphi _{*})_{x},\qquad \varphi '(x).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5fdb85d6ce6f722d6d2f09cb5d3d92dfdd182df0)
In a sense, the derivative is the best linear approximation to ![Image 171: {\displaystyle \varphi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/33ee699558d09cf9d653f6351f9fda0b2f4aaa3e) near ![Image 172: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4). Note that when ![Image 173: {\displaystyle N=\mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/41482b35755b3f83a48d50500e2267e559787be5), then the map ![Image 174: {\displaystyle \mathrm {d} {\varphi }_{x}:T_{x}M\to \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7519a757d698d8e895c108ea58f2d83395d2f4c3) coincides with the usual notion of the [differential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_(calculus) "Differential (calculus)") of the function ![Image 175: {\displaystyle \varphi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/33ee699558d09cf9d653f6351f9fda0b2f4aaa3e). In [local coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_coordinates "Local coordinates") the derivative of ![Image 176: {\displaystyle \varphi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/33ee699558d09cf9d653f6351f9fda0b2f4aaa3e) is given by the [Jacobian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacobian_matrix_and_determinant "Jacobian matrix and determinant").

An important result regarding the derivative map is the following:

**Theorem**—If ![Image 177: {\displaystyle \varphi :M\to N}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d7dd52f475d4d090b5f59f745e773f63d3176a01) is a [local diffeomorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_diffeomorphism "Local diffeomorphism") at ![Image 178: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) in ![Image 179: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd), then ![Image 180: {\displaystyle \mathrm {d} {\varphi }_{x}:T_{x}M\to T_{\varphi (x)}N}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/101faed6458df5870e56dbd69a27f4611df49730) is a linear [isomorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isomorphism "Isomorphism"). Conversely, if ![Image 181: {\displaystyle \varphi :M\to N}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d7dd52f475d4d090b5f59f745e773f63d3176a01) is continuously differentiable and ![Image 182: {\displaystyle \mathrm {d} {\varphi }_{x}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/308f11a8722b8b6e8d81f1d453216411bb97f298) is an isomorphism, then there is an [open neighborhood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_set "Open set")![Image 183: {\displaystyle U}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/458a728f53b9a0274f059cd695e067c430956025) of ![Image 184: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) such that ![Image 185: {\displaystyle \varphi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/33ee699558d09cf9d653f6351f9fda0b2f4aaa3e) maps ![Image 186: {\displaystyle U}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/458a728f53b9a0274f059cd695e067c430956025) diffeomorphically onto its image.

This is a generalization of the [inverse function theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverse_function_theorem "Inverse function theorem") to maps between manifolds.
