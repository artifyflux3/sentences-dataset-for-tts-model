In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), the **Lebesgue covering dimension** or **topological dimension** of a [topological space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_space "Topological space") is one of several different ways of defining the [dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension "Dimension") of the space in a [topologically invariant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_invariant "Topological invariant") way.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebesgue_covering_dimension#cite_note-Lebesgue-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebesgue_covering_dimension#cite_note-Duda-2)

Informal discussion
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Lebesgue_covering_dimension&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Informal discussion")]

For ordinary [Euclidean spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space"), the Lebesgue covering dimension is just the ordinary Euclidean dimension: zero for points, one for lines, two for planes, and so on. However, not all topological spaces have this kind of "obvious" [dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension "Dimension"), and so a precise definition is needed in such cases. The definition proceeds by examining what happens when the space is covered by [open sets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_set "Open set").

In general, a topological space _X_ can be [covered by open sets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_cover "Open cover"), in that one can find a collection of open sets such that _X_ lies inside of their [union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Union_(set_theory) "Union (set theory)"). The covering dimension is the smallest number _n_ such that for every cover, there is a [refinement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Refinement_(topology) "Refinement (topology)") in which every point in _X_ lies in the [intersection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intersection_(set_theory) "Intersection (set theory)") of no more than _n_ + 1 covering sets. This is the gist of the formal definition below. The goal of the definition is to provide a number (an [integer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer")) that describes the space, and does not change as the space is continuously deformed; that is, a number that is invariant under [homeomorphisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homeomorphism "Homeomorphism").

The general idea is illustrated in the diagrams below, which show a cover and refinements of a circle and a square.

[![Image 1](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/8a/Radford-stretcher-bond.jpeg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Radford-stretcher-bond.jpeg)

[Henri Lebesgue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Lebesgue "Henri Lebesgue") used closed "bricks" to study covering dimension in 1921.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebesgue_covering_dimension#cite_note-FOOTNOTELebesgue1921-3)

The first formal definition of covering dimension was given by [Eduard Čech](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eduard_%C4%8Cech "Eduard Čech"), based on an earlier result of [Henri Lebesgue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Lebesgue "Henri Lebesgue").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebesgue_covering_dimension#cite_note-4)

A modern definition is as follows. An [open cover](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_cover "Open cover") of a topological space _X_ is a family of [open sets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_set "Open set")_U_ α such that their union is the whole space, ![Image 2: {\displaystyle \cup _{\alpha }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54f1141599c6bb73f2a6f38c32934e9ce6fc389b)_U_ α = _X_. The **order** or **ply** of an open cover ![Image 3: {\displaystyle {\mathfrak {A}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/34aa92fbdb716183c034a2cfc30dafbaa51cfcd6) = {_U_ α} is the smallest number _m_ (if it exists) for which each point of the space belongs to at most _m_ open sets in the cover: in other words U α 1 ∩ ⋅⋅⋅ ∩ U α _m_+1 = ![Image 4: {\displaystyle \emptyset }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6af50205f42bb2ec3c666b7b847d2c7f96e464c7) for α 1, ..., α _m_+1 distinct. A [refinement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Refinement_(topology) "Refinement (topology)") of an open cover ![Image 5: {\displaystyle {\mathfrak {A}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/34aa92fbdb716183c034a2cfc30dafbaa51cfcd6) = {_U_ α} is another open cover ![Image 6: {\displaystyle {\mathfrak {B}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f939c87a07b7af23e09792e9edb2c7caebb18864) = {_V_ β}, such that each _V_ β is contained in some _U_ α. The **covering dimension** of a topological space _X_ is defined to be the minimum value of _n_ such that every finite open cover ![Image 7: {\displaystyle {\mathfrak {A}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/34aa92fbdb716183c034a2cfc30dafbaa51cfcd6) of _X_ has an open refinement ![Image 8: {\displaystyle {\mathfrak {B}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f939c87a07b7af23e09792e9edb2c7caebb18864) with order _n_ + 1. The refinement ![Image 9: {\displaystyle {\mathfrak {B}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f939c87a07b7af23e09792e9edb2c7caebb18864) can always be chosen to be finite.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebesgue_covering_dimension#cite_note-5) Thus, if _n_ is finite, V β 1 ∩ ⋅⋅⋅ ∩ V β _n_+2 = ![Image 10: {\displaystyle \emptyset }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6af50205f42bb2ec3c666b7b847d2c7f96e464c7) for β 1, ..., β _n_+2 distinct. If no such minimal _n_ exists, the space is said to have infinite covering dimension.

As a special case, a non-empty topological space is [zero-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero-dimensional_space "Zero-dimensional space") with respect to the covering dimension if every open cover of the space has a refinement consisting of [disjoint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disjoint_set "Disjoint set") open sets, meaning any point in the space is contained in exactly one open set of this refinement.

The [empty set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empty_set "Empty set") has covering dimension −1: for any open cover of the empty set, each point of the empty set is not contained in any element of the cover, so the order of any open cover is 0.

Any given open cover of the [unit circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_circle "Unit circle") will have a refinement consisting of a collection of [open](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_(topology) "Open (topology)") arcs. The circle has dimension one, by this definition, because any such cover can be further refined to the stage where a given point _x_ of the circle is contained in _at most_ two open arcs. That is, whatever collection of arcs we begin with, some can be discarded or shrunk, such that the remainder still covers the circle but with simple overlaps.

Similarly, any open cover of the [unit disk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_disk "Unit disk") in the two-dimensional [plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_(mathematics) "Plane (mathematics)") can be refined so that any point of the disk is contained in no more than three open sets, while two are in general not sufficient. The covering dimension of the disk is thus two.

More generally, the _n_-dimensional [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space")![Image 11: {\displaystyle \mathbb {E} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7e8fa8586d428ff5706c6d0a00a7939950fad89b) has covering dimension _n_.

*   [Homeomorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homeomorphic "Homeomorphic") spaces have the same covering dimension. That is, the covering dimension is a [topological invariant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_invariant "Topological invariant").
*   The covering dimension of a normal space _X_ is ![Image 12: {\displaystyle \leq n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/61fbd99c9375a5553cc47f02b7da90cb8becd4ca)[if and only if](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/If_and_only_if "If and only if") for any [closed subset](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_subset "Closed subset")_A_ of _X_, if ![Image 13: {\displaystyle f:A\rightarrow S^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/49019e8db765c799c637da709ad79270723747ce) is continuous, then there is an extension of ![Image 14: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) to ![Image 15: {\displaystyle g:X\rightarrow S^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/faad65f06630bb6b2dd570accc64fb6110dde801). Here, ![Image 16: {\displaystyle S^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ee006452a59bf1eb29983b4412348b66517a2d23) is the [_n_-dimensional sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/N-sphere "N-sphere").
*   **Ostrand's theorem on covering dimension.** If X is a normal topological space and ![Image 17: {\displaystyle {\mathfrak {A}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/34aa92fbdb716183c034a2cfc30dafbaa51cfcd6) = {_U_ α} is a locally finite cover of _X_ of order ≤ _n_ + 1, then, for each 1 ≤ _i_ ≤ _n_ + 1, there exists a family of pairwise disjoint open sets ![Image 18: {\displaystyle {\mathfrak {B}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f939c87a07b7af23e09792e9edb2c7caebb18864)_i_ = {_V_ _i_,α} shrinking ![Image 19: {\displaystyle {\mathfrak {A}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/34aa92fbdb716183c034a2cfc30dafbaa51cfcd6), i.e. _V_ _i_,α ⊆ _U_ α, and together covering _X_.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebesgue_covering_dimension#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOstrand1971-6)
