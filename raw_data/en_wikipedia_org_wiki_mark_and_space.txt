From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

States of a communications signal

**Mark** and **space** are terms used in [telecommunications](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Telecommunications "Telecommunications") to describe two different signal states of a communications signal, generally at the [physical layer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_layer "Physical layer") of a communications system. The terms derive from the early days of the [electric telegraph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_telegraph "Electric telegraph") system, where the _marking_ state would cause a mark to be output on paper, and the _spacing_ state would create no mark.

[![Image 6](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/MIL-STD-188-100_char_struct_for_async_comms_1972-11-15.svg/250px-MIL-STD-188-100_char_struct_for_async_comms_1972-11-15.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:MIL-STD-188-100_char_struct_for_async_comms_1972-11-15.svg)

7-bit Asynchronous Data Communication surrounded by start, parity and end elements including one "0" (spacing) start element and one "1" (marking) stop element[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mark_and_space#cite_note-1)

[![Image 7](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Rs232_oscilloscope_trace.svg/250px-Rs232_oscilloscope_trace.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Rs232_oscilloscope_trace.svg)

Diagram of RS-232 signalling for an uppercase [ASCII](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ASCII "ASCII") "K" character (0x4b) with 1 start bit, 8 data bits, 1 stop bit. Mark and Space depict negative and positive voltage levels.

The terms would continue to be used in systems such as [RS-232](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RS-232 "RS-232"), with similar conventions, that "mark" would be encoded by a negative voltage (or current flow), and "space" by a positive voltage (or no current flow).[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mark_and_space#cite_note-Freeman-2) In such systems, the line is typically left in the "mark" state when idle.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mark_and_space#cite_note-3)

"Mark" is generally identified with the [binary digit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_digit "Binary digit") "1" and "space" with the binary digit "0".[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mark_and_space#cite_note-Freeman-2)
