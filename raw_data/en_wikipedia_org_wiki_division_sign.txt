The **division sign** (**÷**) is a [mathematical symbol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_mathematical_symbols "Glossary of mathematical symbols") consisting of a short horizontal line with a dot above and another dot below, used in [Anglophone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anglophone "Anglophone") countries to indicate the operation of [division](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_(mathematics) "Division (mathematics)"). This usage is not universal and the symbol has different meanings in other countries. Consequently, its use to denote division is deprecated in the [ISO 80000-2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO_80000-2 "ISO 80000-2") standard for notations used in mathematics, science and technology.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_sign#cite_note-ISO-1)

Plus and minuses. The obelus– or division sign– used as a variant of the minus sign in an excerpt from an official Norwegian trading statement form called «Næringsoppgave 1» for the taxation year 2010.

The [obelus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obelus "Obelus"), a historical [glyph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glyph "Glyph") consisting of a horizontal line with (or without) one or more dots, was first used as a symbol for division in 1659, in the [algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra "Algebra") book _\_Teutsche Algebra\__ by [Johann Rahn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johann_Rahn "Johann Rahn"), although previous writers had used the same symbol for subtraction.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_sign#cite_note-Cajori-2) Some near-contemporaries believed that [John Pell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Pell_(mathematician) "John Pell (mathematician)"), who edited the book, may have been responsible for this use of the symbol.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_sign#cite_note-Cajori-2) Other symbols for division include the [slash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slash_(punctuation) "Slash (punctuation)") or solidus /, the [colon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colon_(punctuation) "Colon (punctuation)"):, and the [fraction bar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fraction_(mathematics)#Vocabulary "Fraction (mathematics)") (the horizontal bar in a vertical fraction).[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_sign#cite_note-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_sign#cite_note-4) The ISO 80000-2 standard for mathematical notation in science and technology recommends only the solidus / or "fraction bar" for division, or the "colon" : for [ratios](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ratio "Ratio"); it says that the ÷ sign "should not be used" for division.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_sign#cite_note-ISO-1)

In [Italy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italy "Italy"), [Poland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poland "Poland") and [Russia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russia "Russia"), the ÷ sign was sometimes used to denote a [range of values](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interval_(mathematics) "Interval (mathematics)"), and in [Scandinavian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scandinavia "Scandinavia") countries it was, and sometimes still is, used as a negation sign:[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_sign#cite_note-5) the [Unicode Consortium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unicode_Consortium "Unicode Consortium") has allocated a separate [code point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Code_point "Code point"), U+2052⁒[COMMERCIAL MINUS SIGN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E2%81%92 "⁒") for this usage uniquely;[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_sign#cite_note-Silli1-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_sign#cite_note-Silli2-7) the exact form of the symbol displayed is typeface (font) dependent.

In computer systems
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Division_sign&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: In computer systems")]

The symbol was assigned to [code point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Code_point "Code point") 0xF7 in [ISO 8859-1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO_8859-1 "ISO 8859-1"), as the "division sign". This encoding was transferred to [Unicode](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unicode "Unicode") as U+00F7.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_sign#cite_note-8) In [HTML](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HTML "HTML"), it can be [encoded](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Character_encodings_in_HTML "Character encodings in HTML") as `&divide;` or `&#xF7;` (at [HTML level](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_XML_and_HTML_character_entity_references "List of XML and HTML character entity references") 3.2), or as `&#247;`.

[Unicode](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unicode "Unicode") provides various division symbols:[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_sign#cite_note-9)
