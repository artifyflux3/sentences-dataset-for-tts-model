**Harold Scott MacDonald** "**Donald**" **Coxeter**[CC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Order_of_Canada "Order of Canada")[FRS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Society "Fellow of the Royal Society")[FRSC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Society_of_Canada "Fellow of the Royal Society of Canada") (9 February 1907 – 31 March 2003)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harold_Scott_MacDonald_Coxeter#cite_note-Memoir-2) was a British-Canadian [geometer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometer "Geometer") and mathematician. He is regarded as one of the greatest geometers of the 20th century.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harold_Scott_MacDonald_Coxeter#cite_note-3)

Coxeter was born in England and educated at the [University of Cambridge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Cambridge "University of Cambridge"), with student visits to [Princeton University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Princeton_University "Princeton University"). He worked for 60 years at the [University of Toronto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Toronto "University of Toronto") in Canada, from 1936 until his retirement in 1996, becoming a full professor there in 1948. His many honours included membership in the [Royal Society of Canada](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Society_of_Canada "Royal Society of Canada"), the [Royal Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Society "Royal Society"), and the [Order of Canada](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Order_of_Canada "Order of Canada").

He was an author of 12 books, including _[The Fifty-Nine Icosahedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The\_Fifty-Nine\_Icosahedra "The Fifty-Nine Icosahedra")_ (1938) and _[Regular Polytopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular\_Polytopes\_(book) "Regular Polytopes (book)")_ (1947). Many concepts in geometry and [group theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_theory "Group theory") are named after him, including the [Coxeter graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coxeter_graph "Coxeter graph"), [Coxeter groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coxeter_group "Coxeter group"), [Coxeter's loxodromic sequence of tangent circles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coxeter%27s_loxodromic_sequence_of_tangent_circles "Coxeter's loxodromic sequence of tangent circles"), [Coxeter–Dynkin diagrams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coxeter%E2%80%93Dynkin_diagram "Coxeter–Dynkin diagram"), and the [Todd–Coxeter algorithm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Todd%E2%80%93Coxeter_algorithm "Todd–Coxeter algorithm").

Coxeter was born in [Kensington](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kensington "Kensington"), England, to Harold Samuel Coxeter and Lucy ([née](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Birth_name#Maiden_and_married_names "Birth name")Gee). His father had taken over the family business of Coxeter & Son, manufacturers of surgical instruments and compressed gases (including a mechanism for anaesthetising surgical patients with [nitrous oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrous_oxide "Nitrous oxide")), but was able to retire early and focus on sculpting and baritone singing; Lucy Coxeter was a portrait and landscape painter who had attended the [Royal Academy of Arts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Academy_of_Arts "Royal Academy of Arts"). A maternal cousin was the architect Sir [Giles Gilbert Scott](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giles_Gilbert_Scott "Giles Gilbert Scott").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harold_Scott_MacDonald_Coxeter#cite_note-ODNB-4)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harold_Scott_MacDonald_Coxeter#cite_note-Memoir-2)

In his youth, Coxeter composed music and was an accomplished pianist at the age of 10.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harold_Scott_MacDonald_Coxeter#cite_note-Roberts-5) He felt that [mathematics and music](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics_and_music "Mathematics and music") were intimately related, outlining his ideas in a 1962 article on "Music and Mathematics" in the _Canadian Music Journal_.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harold_Scott_MacDonald_Coxeter#cite_note-Roberts-5)

He was educated at [King Alfred School, London](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/King_Alfred_School,_London "King Alfred School, London"), and [St George's School, Harpenden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/St_George%27s_School,_Harpenden "St George's School, Harpenden"), where his best friend was John Flinders Petrie, later a mathematician for whom [Petrie polygons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petrie_polygon "Petrie polygon") were named. He was accepted at [King's College, Cambridge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/King%27s_College,_Cambridge "King's College, Cambridge"), in 1925, but decided to spend a year studying in hopes of gaining admittance to [Trinity College](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trinity_College,_Cambridge "Trinity College, Cambridge"), where the standard of mathematics was higher.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harold_Scott_MacDonald_Coxeter#cite_note-Memoir-2) Coxeter won an entrance scholarship and went to Trinity in 1926 to read mathematics. There he earned his BA (as [Senior Wrangler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Senior_Wrangler "Senior Wrangler")) in 1928, and his doctorate in 1931.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harold_Scott_MacDonald_Coxeter#cite_note-Roberts-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harold_Scott_MacDonald_Coxeter#cite_note-MacTutor-6) In 1932 he went to [Princeton University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Princeton_University "Princeton University") for a year as a [Rockefeller Fellow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rockefeller_Fellow "Rockefeller Fellow"), where he worked with [Hermann Weyl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Weyl "Hermann Weyl"), [Oswald Veblen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oswald_Veblen "Oswald Veblen"), and [Solomon Lefschetz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solomon_Lefschetz "Solomon Lefschetz").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harold_Scott_MacDonald_Coxeter#cite_note-MacTutor-6) Returning to Trinity for a year, he attended [Ludwig Wittgenstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ludwig_Wittgenstein "Ludwig Wittgenstein")'s seminars on the [philosophy of mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophy_of_mathematics "Philosophy of mathematics").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harold_Scott_MacDonald_Coxeter#cite_note-Roberts-5) Wittgenstein selected Coxeter and others to take notes of his lectures, the collection of which later became _[The Blue Book](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blue\_and\_Brown\_Books "Blue and Brown Books")_.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harold_Scott_MacDonald_Coxeter#cite_note-7) In 1934 he spent a further year at Princeton as a Procter Fellow.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harold_Scott_MacDonald_Coxeter#cite_note-MacTutor-6)

In 1936 Coxeter moved to the University of Toronto. In 1938 he and [P. Du Val](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/P._Du_Val "P. Du Val"), H. T. Flather, and [John Flinders Petrie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Flinders_Petrie "John Flinders Petrie") published _[The Fifty-Nine Icosahedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The\_Fifty-Nine\_Icosahedra "The Fifty-Nine Icosahedra")_ with [University of Toronto Press](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Toronto_Press "University of Toronto Press"). In 1940 Coxeter edited the eleventh edition of _Mathematical Recreations and Essays_,[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harold_Scott_MacDonald_Coxeter#cite_note-8) originally published by [W. W. Rouse Ball](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/W._W._Rouse_Ball "W. W. Rouse Ball") in 1892. He was elevated to [professor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Professor "Professor") in 1948. He was elected a Fellow of the [Royal Society of Canada](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Society_of_Canada "Royal Society of Canada") in 1948 and a [Fellow of the Royal Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Society "Fellow of the Royal Society") in 1950. He met [M. C. Escher](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M._C._Escher "M. C. Escher") in 1954 and the two became lifelong friends; his work on geometric figures helped inspire some of Escher's works, particularly the [_Circle Limit_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle_Limit_III "Circle Limit III") series based on [hyperbolic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_geometry "Hyperbolic geometry")[tessellations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tessellation "Tessellation"). He also inspired some of the innovations of [Buckminster Fuller](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminster_Fuller "Buckminster Fuller").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harold_Scott_MacDonald_Coxeter#cite_note-MacTutor-6) Coxeter, [M. S. Longuet-Higgins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M._S._Longuet-Higgins "M. S. Longuet-Higgins") and [J. C. P. Miller](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J._C._P._Miller "J. C. P. Miller") were the first to publish the full [list of uniform polyhedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_uniform_polyhedra "List of uniform polyhedra") (1954).[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harold_Scott_MacDonald_Coxeter#cite_note-9)

He worked for 60 years at the [University of Toronto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Toronto "University of Toronto") and published twelve books.

Coxeter was a [vegetarian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vegetarianism "Vegetarianism"). He attributed his longevity to his vegetarian diet, daily exercise such as fifty press-ups and standing on his head for fifteen minutes each morning, and consuming a nightly cocktail made from [Kahlúa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kahl%C3%BAa "Kahlúa") (a coffee liqueur), [peach schnapps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peach_schnapps "Peach schnapps"), and [soy milk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soy_milk "Soy milk").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harold_Scott_MacDonald_Coxeter#cite_note-ODNB-4)

Since 1978, the [Canadian Mathematical Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canadian_Mathematical_Society "Canadian Mathematical Society") have awarded the [Coxeter–James Prize](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coxeter%E2%80%93James_Prize "Coxeter–James Prize") in his honor.

He was made a [Fellow of the Royal Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Society "Fellow of the Royal Society") in 1950 and in 1997 he was awarded their [Sylvester Medal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sylvester_Medal "Sylvester Medal").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harold_Scott_MacDonald_Coxeter#cite_note-MacTutor-6) In 1990, he became a Foreign Member of the [American Academy of Arts and Sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Academy_of_Arts_and_Sciences "American Academy of Arts and Sciences")[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harold_Scott_MacDonald_Coxeter#cite_note-10) and in 1997 was made a [Companion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Order_of_Canada#Grades "Order of Canada") of the [Order of Canada](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Order_of_Canada "Order of Canada").[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harold_Scott_MacDonald_Coxeter#cite_note-11)

In 1973 he received the [Jeffery–Williams Prize](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jeffery%E2%80%93Williams_Prize "Jeffery–Williams Prize").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harold_Scott_MacDonald_Coxeter#cite_note-MacTutor-6)

A [festschrift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Festschrift "Festschrift") in his honour, _The Geometric Vein_, was published in 1982. It contained 41 essays on geometry, based on a symposium for Coxeter held at Toronto in 1979.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harold_Scott_MacDonald_Coxeter#cite_note-12) A second such volume, _The Coxeter Legacy_, was published in 2006 based on a Toronto Coxeter symposium held in 2004.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harold_Scott_MacDonald_Coxeter#cite_note-13)
