From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Unit of action or angular momentum

Not to be confused with [Joules per second](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joules_per_second "Joules per second").

The **joule-second** (symbol **J⋅s** or **J s**) is the unit of [action](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_(physics) "Action (physics)") and of [angular momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum "Angular momentum") in the [International System of Units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Units "International System of Units") (SI) equal to the product of an [SI derived unit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SI_derived_unit "SI derived unit"), the [joule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joule "Joule") (J), and an [SI base unit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SI_base_unit "SI base unit"), the [second](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second "Second") (s).[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joule-second#cite_note-1) The joule-second is a unit of [action](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_(physics) "Action (physics)") or of [angular momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum "Angular momentum"). The joule-second also appears in [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics") within the definition of the [Planck constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant "Planck constant").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joule-second#cite_note-2) Angular momentum is the product of an object's [moment of inertia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_of_inertia "Moment of inertia"), in units of kg⋅m 2 and its [angular velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_velocity "Angular velocity") in units of rad⋅s−1. This product of moment of inertia and angular velocity yields kg⋅m 2⋅s−1 or the joule-second. The Planck constant represents the energy of a wave, in units of joule, divided by the frequency of that wave, in units of s−1. This [quotient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quotient "Quotient") of energy and frequency also yields the joule-second (J⋅s).

Base units
----------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Joule-second&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Base units")]

In SI base units the joule-second becomes kilogram-meter squared-per second or kg⋅m 2⋅s−1. [Dimensional Analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimensional_analysis "Dimensional analysis") of the joule-second yields M L 2 T−1. Note the denominator of seconds (s) in the base units.

Confusion with joules per second
--------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Joule-second&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Confusion with joules per second")]

The joule-second (J⋅s) should not be confused with [joules _per_ second](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joules_per_second "Joules per second") (J/s) or watts (W). In physical processes, when the unit of time appears in the denominator of a ratio, the described process occurs at a [rate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rate_(mathematics) "Rate (mathematics)"). For example, in discussions about speed, an object like a car travels a known distance of kilometers spread over a known number of seconds, and the car's speed is measured in the unit kilometer _per_ hour (km/h). In physics, work _per_ time describes a system's [power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_(physics) "Power (physics)"), with the unit [watt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Watt "Watt") (W), which is equal to joules per second (J/s).
