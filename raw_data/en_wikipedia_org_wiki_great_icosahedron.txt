3D model of a great icosahedron

In [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry"), the **great icosahedron** is one of four [Kepler–Poinsot polyhedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler%E2%80%93Poinsot_polyhedra "Kepler–Poinsot polyhedra") ([nonconvex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nonconvex "Nonconvex")[regular polyhedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_regular_polytopes#Non-convex_2 "List of regular polytopes")), with [Schläfli symbol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schl%C3%A4fli_symbol "Schläfli symbol"){3,5⁄2}  and [Coxeter-Dynkin diagram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coxeter-Dynkin_diagram "Coxeter-Dynkin diagram") of ![Image 12](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/bd/CDel_node_1.png)![Image 13](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c3/CDel_3.png)![Image 14](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/5e/CDel_node.png)![Image 15](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/16/CDel_5.png)![Image 16](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/8f/CDel_rat.png)![Image 17](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d5/CDel_d2.png)![Image 18](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/5e/CDel_node.png). It is composed of 20 intersecting triangular faces, having five triangles meeting at each vertex in a [pentagrammic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentagram "Pentagram") sequence.

The great icosahedron can be constructed analogously to the pentagram, its two-dimensional analogue, via the extension of the (_n_–1)-dimensional [simplex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simplex "Simplex") faces of the core n-polytope (equilateral triangles for the great icosahedron, and [line segments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_segment "Line segment") for the pentagram) until the figure regains regular faces. The [grand 600-cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grand_600-cell "Grand 600-cell") can be seen as its four-dimensional analogue using the same process.

The edge length of a great icosahedron is ![Image 19: {\displaystyle {\frac {7+3{\sqrt {5}}}{2}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b279f11a6ebacc36bf7967c0db67cf98aefa2c87) times that of the original icosahedron.

Animated truncation sequence from {5/2, 3} to {3, 5/2}

It shares the same [vertex arrangement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_arrangement "Vertex arrangement") as the regular convex [icosahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Icosahedron "Icosahedron"). It also shares the same [edge arrangement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edge_arrangement "Edge arrangement") as the [small stellated dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Small_stellated_dodecahedron "Small stellated dodecahedron").

A truncation operation, repeatedly applied to the great icosahedron, produces a sequence of uniform polyhedra. Truncating edges down to points produces the [great icosidodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_icosidodecahedron "Great icosidodecahedron") as a rectified great icosahedron. The process completes as a birectification, reducing the original faces down to points, and producing the [great stellated dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_stellated_dodecahedron "Great stellated dodecahedron").

The [truncated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Truncation_(geometry) "Truncation (geometry)")_great stellated dodecahedron_ is a degenerate polyhedron, with 20 triangular faces from the truncated vertices, and 12 (hidden) doubled up pentagonal faces ({10/2}) as truncations of the original pentagram faces, the latter forming two [great dodecahedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_dodecahedron "Great dodecahedron") inscribed within and sharing the edges of the icosahedron.
