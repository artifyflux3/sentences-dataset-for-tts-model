**Glycine** (symbol **Gly** or **G**;[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-oxforddictionaries-7)) is an organic compound with the formula C 2 H 5 NO 2, and is the simplest stable [amino acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amino_acid "Amino acid"), distinguished by having a single hydrogen atom as its [side chain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Side_chain "Side chain"). As one of the 20 [proteinogenic amino acids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proteinogenic_amino_acid "Proteinogenic amino acid"), glycine is a fundamental building block of [proteins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proteins "Proteins") in all life and is encoded by all [codons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Codons "Codons") starting with GG (GGU, GGC, GGA, and GGG).[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-Berg2015-8)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-:3-9) Because of its minimal side chain, it is the only common amino acid that is not [chiral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chirality_(chemistry) "Chirality (chemistry)"), meaning it is superimposable on its mirror image.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-10)[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-iupac-chiral-11)

In the body, glycine plays several crucial roles. Its small and flexible structure is vital for the formation of certain protein structures, most notably in [collagen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collagen "Collagen"), where glycine makes up about 35% of the amino acid content and enables the tight coiling of the collagen triple helix.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-Berg2015-8)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-Nelson2021-12) Glycine disrupts the formation of [alpha-helices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_helix "Alpha helix") in [secondary protein structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Secondary_protein_structure "Secondary protein structure"), in favor instead of [random coils](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Random_coil "Random coil").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-pmid9649402-13) Beyond its structural role, glycine functions as an inhibitory neurotransmitter in the [central nervous system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_nervous_system "Central nervous system"),[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-14) particularly in the [spinal cord](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spinal_cord "Spinal cord") and [brainstem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brainstem "Brainstem"), where it helps regulate motor and sensory signals. Disruption of glycine signaling can lead to severe [neurological disorders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neurological_disorders "Neurological disorders") and [motor dysfunction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Motor_dysfunction "Motor dysfunction");[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-15) for example, the [tetanus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetanus "Tetanus") toxin causes spastic paralysis by blocking glycine release.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-Purves2018-16) It also serves as a key precursor for the synthesis of other important biomolecules, including the [porphyrins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Porphyrins "Porphyrins") that form [heme](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heme "Heme") in blood and the [purines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Purines "Purines") used to build [DNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNA "DNA") and [RNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RNA "RNA").[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-Berg2015-8)

Glycine is a white, sweet-tasting crystalline solid, leading to its name from Greek word glykys ([Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_language "Greek language"): γλυκύς) or "sweet".[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-kihara2004-17)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-oxforddictionaries-7) While [the body can synthesize](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Essential_amino_acid#Non-essential_amino_acid "Essential amino acid") it, it is also obtained from the diet and produced industrially by chemical synthesis for use as a [food additive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Food_additive "Food additive"), a [nutritional supplement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nutritional_supplement "Nutritional supplement"), and an intermediate in the manufacture of products such as the herbicide [glyphosate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glyphosate "Glyphosate").[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-Ullmanns-18) In aqueous solutions, glycine exists predominantly as a [zwitterion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zwitterion "Zwitterion") (H 3 N+CH 2 COO-), a [polar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_polarity "Chemical polarity") molecule with both a positive and negative charge, making it highly soluble in water.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-Haynes2017-19) It can also fit into [hydrophobic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrophobe "Hydrophobe") environment due to its minimal side chain.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-20)

History and etymology
---------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Glycine&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: History and etymology")]

Glycine was discovered in 1820 by French chemist [Henri Braconnot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Braconnot "Henri Braconnot") when he hydrolyzed [gelatin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gelatin "Gelatin") by boiling it with [sulfuric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfuric_acid "Sulfuric acid").[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-21) He originally called it "sugar of gelatin",[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-22)[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-23) but French chemist [Jean-Baptiste Boussingault](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_Boussingault "Jean-Baptiste Boussingault") showed in 1838 that it contained nitrogen.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-24) In 1847 American scientist [Eben Norton Horsford](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eben_Norton_Horsford "Eben Norton Horsford"), then a student of the German chemist [Justus von Liebig](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Justus_von_Liebig "Justus von Liebig"), proposed the name "glycocoll";[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-25)[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-26) however, the [Swedish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sweden "Sweden") chemist [Berzelius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J%C3%B6ns_Jacob_Berzelius "Jöns Jacob Berzelius") suggested the simpler current name a year later.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-27)[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-28) The name comes from the [Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek "Ancient Greek") word γλυκύς "sweet tasting"[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-29) (which is also related to the prefixes _[glyco-](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/glyco-#Prefix "wikt:glyco-")_ and _[gluco-](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/gluco-#Prefix "wikt:gluco-")_, as in _[glycoprotein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycoprotein "Glycoprotein")_ and _[glucose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glucose "Glucose")_). In 1858, the French chemist [Auguste Cahours](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Auguste_Andr%C3%A9_Thomas_Cahours "Auguste André Thomas Cahours") determined that glycine was an [amine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amine "Amine") of [acetic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid "Acetic acid").[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-30)

Although glycine can be isolated from [hydrolyzed proteins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrolyzed_protein "Hydrolyzed protein"), this route is not used for industrial production, as it can be manufactured more conveniently by chemical synthesis.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-31) The two main processes are [amination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amination "Amination") of [chloroacetic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chloroacetic_acid "Chloroacetic acid") with [ammonia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia "Ammonia"), giving glycine and [hydrochloric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid "Hydrochloric acid"),[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-32) and the [Strecker amino acid synthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strecker_amino_acid_synthesis "Strecker amino acid synthesis"),[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-33) which is the main synthetic method in the United States and Japan.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-usitc.gov-34) About 15 thousand [tonnes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tonne "Tonne") are produced annually in this way.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-Ull-35)

Glycine is also co-generated as an impurity in the synthesis of [EDTA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/EDTA "EDTA"), arising from reactions of the ammonia co-product.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-Ullmann/Roger-36)

Its acid–base properties are most important. In aqueous solution, glycine is [amphoteric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amphoteric "Amphoteric"): below pH = 2.4, it converts to the ammonium cation called glycinium. Above about pH 9.6, it converts to glycinate.

[![Image 24](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Glycine-protonation-states-2D-skeletal.png/960px-Glycine-protonation-states-2D-skeletal.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Glycine-protonation-states-2D-skeletal.png)
Glycine functions as a [bidentate ligand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bidentate_ligand "Bidentate ligand") for many metal ions, forming [amino acid complexes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amino_acid_complex "Amino acid complex").[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-37) A typical complex is Cu(glycinate)2, i.e. Cu(H 2 NCH 2 CO 2)2, which exists both in cis and trans isomers.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-38)[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-39)

With acid chlorides, glycine converts to the amidocarboxylic acid, such as [hippuric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hippuric_acid "Hippuric acid")[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-40) and [acetylglycine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylglycine "Acetylglycine").[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-41) With [nitrous acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrous_acid "Nitrous acid"), one obtains [glycolic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycolic_acid "Glycolic acid") ([van Slyke determination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Slyke_determination "Van Slyke determination")). With [methyl iodide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methyl_iodide "Methyl iodide"), the amine becomes [quaternized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quaternary_compound "Quaternary compound") to give [trimethylglycine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trimethylglycine "Trimethylglycine"), a natural product:

Pyrolysis of glycine or glycylglycine gives [2,5-diketopiperazine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2,5-diketopiperazine "2,5-diketopiperazine"), the cyclic diamide.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-43)

Glycine forms [esters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ester "Ester") with [alcohols](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alcohol_(chemistry) "Alcohol (chemistry)"). They are often isolated as their [hydrochloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrochloride "Hydrochloride"), such as [glycine methyl ester hydrochloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine_methyl_ester_hydrochloride "Glycine methyl ester hydrochloride"). Otherwise, the free ester tends to convert to [diketopiperazine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diketopiperazine "Diketopiperazine").

As a bifunctional molecule, glycine reacts with many reagents. These can be classified into N-centered and carboxylate-center reactions.

Glycine is not strictly [essential to the human diet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Essential_amino_acid#Essentiality_in_humans "Essential amino acid"), as it is biosynthesized in the body. However, it is considered semi-essential in that the amount that can be biosynthesized is insufficient for all metabolic uses.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-44)

It can be synthesized from the amino acid [serine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Serine "Serine"), which is in turn derived from [3-phosphoglycerate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3-phosphoglycerate "3-phosphoglycerate"). In most organisms, the enzyme [serine hydroxymethyltransferase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Serine_hydroxymethyltransferase "Serine hydroxymethyltransferase") catalyses this transformation via the cofactor [pyridoxal phosphate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyridoxal_phosphate "Pyridoxal phosphate"):[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-Lehninger-45)

serine + [tetrahydrofolate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahydrofolate "Tetrahydrofolate") → glycine + [_N 5_,_N 10_-methylene tetrahydrofolate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/5,10-Methylenetetrahydrofolate "5,10-Methylenetetrahydrofolate") + H 2 O
In _E. coli_, antibiotics that target folate depletes the supply of active tetrahydrofolates, halting glycine biosynthesis as a consequence.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-46)

In the liver of [vertebrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertebrate "Vertebrate"), glycine synthesis is catalyzed by [glycine synthase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine_synthase "Glycine synthase") (also called glycine cleavage enzyme). This conversion is readily [reversible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reversible_reaction "Reversible reaction"):[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-Lehninger-45)

CO 2 + NH+

4 + _N 5_,_N 10_-methylene tetrahydrofolate + [NADH](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nicotinamide_adenine_dinucleotide "Nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide") + H+ ⇌ Glycine + tetrahydrofolate + [NAD](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nicotinamide_adenine_dinucleotide "Nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide")+
In addition to being synthesized from serine, glycine can also be derived from [threonine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Threonine "Threonine"), [choline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Choline "Choline") or hydroxyproline via inter-organ metabolism of the liver and kidneys.[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-47)

Glycine is degraded via three pathways. The predominant pathway in animals and plants is the reverse of the glycine synthase pathway mentioned above. In this context, the enzyme system involved is usually called the [glycine cleavage system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine_cleavage_system "Glycine cleavage system"):[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-Lehninger-45)

Glycine + tetrahydrofolate + NAD+ ⇌ CO 2 + NH+

4 + _N 5_,_N 10_-methylene tetrahydrofolate + [NADH](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NADH "NADH") + H+
In the second pathway, glycine is degraded in two steps. The first step is the reverse of glycine biosynthesis from serine with serine hydroxymethyl transferase. Serine is then converted to [pyruvate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyruvate "Pyruvate") by [serine dehydratase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Serine_dehydratase "Serine dehydratase").[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-Lehninger-45)

In the third pathway of its degradation, glycine is converted to [glyoxylate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glyoxylate "Glyoxylate") by [D-amino acid oxidase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D-amino_acid_oxidase "D-amino acid oxidase"). Glyoxylate is then oxidized by hepatic [lactate dehydrogenase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lactate_dehydrogenase "Lactate dehydrogenase") to [oxalate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxalate "Oxalate") in an NAD+-dependent reaction.[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-Lehninger-45)

The half-life of glycine and its elimination from the body varies significantly based on dose.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-:0-48) In one study, the half-life varied between 0.5 and 4.0 hours.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-:0-48)

Physiological function
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Glycine&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Physiological function")]

The principal function of glycine is it acts as a [precursor to proteins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protein_precursor "Protein precursor"). Most proteins incorporate only small quantities of glycine, a notable exception being [collagen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collagen "Collagen"), which contains about 35% glycine due to its periodically repeated role in the formation of collagen's helix structure in conjunction with [hydroxyproline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroxyproline "Hydroxyproline").[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-Lehninger-45)[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-SzpakJAS-49) In the [genetic code](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genetic_code "Genetic code"), glycine is coded by all [codons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Codons "Codons") starting with GG, namely GGU, GGC, GGA and GGG.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-:3-9)

### As a biosynthetic intermediate

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Glycine&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: As a biosynthetic intermediate")]

In higher [eukaryotes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eukaryotes "Eukaryotes"), [δ-aminolevulinic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%CE%94-aminolevulinic_acid "Δ-aminolevulinic acid"), the key precursor to [porphyrins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Porphyrins "Porphyrins"), is biosynthesized from glycine and [succinyl-CoA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Succinyl-CoA "Succinyl-CoA") by the enzyme [ALA synthase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ALA_synthase "ALA synthase"). Glycine provides the central C 2 N subunit of all [purines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Purine "Purine").[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-Lehninger-45)

### As a neurotransmitter

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Glycine&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: As a neurotransmitter")]

Glycine is an inhibitory [neurotransmitter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neurotransmitter "Neurotransmitter") in the [central nervous system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_nervous_system "Central nervous system"), especially in the [spinal cord](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spinal_cord "Spinal cord"), [brainstem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brainstem "Brainstem"), and [retina](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Retina "Retina"). When [glycine receptors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine_receptors "Glycine receptors") are activated, [chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chloride "Chloride") enters the neuron via ionotropic receptors, causing an [inhibitory postsynaptic potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inhibitory_postsynaptic_potential "Inhibitory postsynaptic potential") (IPSP). [Strychnine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strychnine "Strychnine") is a strong antagonist at ionotropic glycine receptors, whereas [bicuculline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bicuculline "Bicuculline") is a weak one. Glycine is a required [co-agonist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Co-agonist "Co-agonist") along with [glutamate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glutamate "Glutamate") for [NMDA receptors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NMDA_receptor "NMDA receptor"). In contrast to the inhibitory role of glycine in the spinal cord, this behaviour is facilitated at the ([NMDA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NMDA "NMDA")) [glutamatergic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glutamatergic "Glutamatergic") receptors which are excitatory.[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-50) The [LD 50](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Median_lethal_dose "Median lethal dose") of glycine is 7930 mg/kg in rats (oral),[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-51) and it usually causes death by hyperexcitability.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

### As a toxin conjugation agent

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Glycine&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: As a toxin conjugation agent")]

Glycine [conjugation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drug_metabolism#Phase_II_%E2%80%93_conjugation "Drug metabolism") pathway has not been fully investigated.[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-52) Glycine is thought to be a hepatic detoxifier of a number endogenous and xenobiotic organic acids.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-53)[Bile acids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bile_acid "Bile acid") are normally conjugated to glycine in order to increase their solubility in water.[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-54)

The human body rapidly clears [sodium benzoate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_benzoate "Sodium benzoate") by combining it with glycine to form [hippuric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hippuric_acid "Hippuric acid") which is then excreted.[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-55) The metabolic pathway for this begins with the conversion of benzoate by [butyrate-CoA ligase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Butyrate-CoA_ligase "Butyrate-CoA ligase") into an intermediate product, [benzoyl-CoA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzoyl-CoA "Benzoyl-CoA"),[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-56) which is then metabolized by [glycine _N_-acyltransferase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine_N-acyltransferase "Glycine N-acyltransferase") into hippuric acid.[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-57)

In the US, glycine is typically sold in two grades: [United States Pharmacopeia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Pharmacopeia "United States Pharmacopeia") ("USP"), and technical grade. USP grade sales account for approximately 80 to 85 percent of the U.S. market for glycine. If purity greater than the USP standard is needed, for example for [intravenous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intravenous "Intravenous") injections, a more expensive pharmaceutical grade glycine can be used. Technical grade glycine, which may or may not meet USP grade standards, is sold at a lower price for use in industrial applications, e.g., as an agent in metal complexing and finishing.[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-58)

### Animal and human foods

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Glycine&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Animal and human foods")]

[![Image 25](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Cu%28gly%292%28OH2%29.png/250px-Cu%28gly%292%28OH2%29.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Cu(gly)2(OH2).png)

Structure of _cis_-Cu(glycinate)2(H 2 O)[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-59)

Glycine is not widely used in foods for its nutritional value, except in infusions. Instead, glycine's role in food chemistry is as a flavorant. It is mildly sweet, and it counters the aftertaste of [saccharine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saccharine "Saccharine"). It also has preservative properties, perhaps owing to its complexation to metal ions. Metal glycinate complexes, e.g. [copper(II) glycinate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Copper(II)_glycinate "Copper(II) glycinate") are used as supplements for animal feeds.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-Ull-35)

As of 1971, the U.S. [Food and Drug Administration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Food_and_Drug_Administration "Food and Drug Administration") "no longer regards glycine and its salts as [generally recognized as safe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generally_recognized_as_safe "Generally recognized as safe") for use in human food",[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-60) and only permits food uses of glycine under certain conditions.[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-61)

Glycine has been researched for its potential to [extend life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life_extension "Life extension").[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-:2-62)[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-63) The proposed mechanisms of this effect are its ability to clear [methionine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methionine "Methionine") from the body, and activating [autophagy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autophagy "Autophagy").[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-:2-62)

Glycine is an intermediate in the synthesis of a variety of chemical products. It is used in the manufacture of the [herbicides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herbicide "Herbicide")[glyphosate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glyphosate "Glyphosate"),[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-64)[iprodione](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iprodione "Iprodione"), glyphosine, [imiprothrin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imiprothrin "Imiprothrin"), and eglinazine.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-Ull-35) It is used as an intermediate of [antibiotics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antibiotic "Antibiotic") such as [thiamphenicol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thiamphenicol "Thiamphenicol").[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-65)

### Laboratory research

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Glycine&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Laboratory research")]

Glycine is a significant component of some solutions used in the [SDS-PAGE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyacrylamide_gel_electrophoresis "Polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis") method of protein analysis. It serves as a buffering agent, maintaining pH and preventing sample damage during electrophoresis.[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-66) Glycine is also used to remove protein-labeling antibodies from [Western blot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_blot "Western blot") membranes to enable the probing of numerous proteins of interest from SDS-PAGE gel. This allows more data to be drawn from the same specimen, increasing the reliability of the data, reducing the amount of sample processing, and number of samples required.[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-67) This process is known as stripping.

The presence of glycine outside the Earth was confirmed in 2009, based on the analysis of samples that had been taken in 2004 by the [NASA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NASA "NASA") spacecraft _[Stardust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stardust\_(spacecraft) "Stardust (spacecraft)")_ from comet [Wild 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wild_2 "Wild 2") and subsequently returned to Earth. Glycine had previously been identified in the [Murchison meteorite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Murchison_meteorite "Murchison meteorite") in 1970.[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-68) The discovery of glycine in outer space bolstered the hypothesis of so-called [soft-panspermia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudo-panspermia "Pseudo-panspermia"), which claims that the "building blocks" of life are widespread throughout the universe.[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-69) In 2016, detection of glycine within Comet [67P/Churyumov–Gerasimenko](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/67P/Churyumov%E2%80%93Gerasimenko "67P/Churyumov–Gerasimenko") by the [_Rosetta_ spacecraft](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rosetta_(spacecraft) "Rosetta (spacecraft)") was announced.[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-70)

The detection of glycine outside the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System") in the [interstellar medium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_medium "Interstellar medium") has been debated.[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-Snyder-71)

Glycine is proposed to be defined by early genetic codes.[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-72)[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-73)[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-74)[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-:1-75) For example, [low complexity regions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Low_complexity_regions_in_proteins "Low complexity regions in proteins") (in proteins), that may resemble the proto-peptides of the early [genetic code](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genetic_code "Genetic code") are highly enriched in glycine.[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycine#cite_note-:1-75)
