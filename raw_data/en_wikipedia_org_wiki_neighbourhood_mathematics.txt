In [topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology "Topology") and related areas of [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), a **neighbourhood** (or **neighborhood**) is one of the basic concepts in a [topological space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_space "Topological space"). It is closely related to the concepts of [open set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_set "Open set") and [interior](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interior_(topology) "Interior (topology)"). Intuitively speaking, a neighbourhood of a point is a [set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_(mathematics) "Set (mathematics)") of points containing that point where one can move some amount in any direction away from that point without leaving the set.

### Neighbourhood of a point

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Neighbourhood_(mathematics)&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Neighbourhood of a point")]

If ![Image 8: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) is a [topological space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_space "Topological space") and ![Image 9: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) is a point in ![Image 10: {\displaystyle X,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/09ba32eeb405f7f5f2bac1eb12987c47d2fd42df) then a **neighbourhood**[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neighbourhood_(mathematics)#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWillard2004Definition_4.1-1) of ![Image 11: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) is a subset ![Image 12: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) of ![Image 13: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) that includes an [open set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_set "Open set")![Image 14: {\displaystyle U}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/458a728f53b9a0274f059cd695e067c430956025) containing ![Image 15: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36), ![Image 16: {\displaystyle p\in U\subseteq V\subseteq X.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/28139ed07a231d3dcf33a3b393d005114e491b03)

This is equivalent to the point ![Image 17: {\displaystyle p\in X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fae5d7ae3f7710fc989d0bd6dca038d74107f16d) belonging to the [topological interior](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interior_(topology)#Interior_point "Interior (topology)") of ![Image 18: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) in ![Image 19: {\displaystyle X.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5ba76c5a460c4a0bb1639a193bc1830f0a773e03)

The neighbourhood ![Image 20: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) need not be an open subset of ![Image 21: {\displaystyle X.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5ba76c5a460c4a0bb1639a193bc1830f0a773e03) When ![Image 22: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) is open (resp. closed, compact, etc.) in ![Image 23: {\displaystyle X,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/09ba32eeb405f7f5f2bac1eb12987c47d2fd42df) it is called an **open neighbourhood**[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neighbourhood_(mathematics)#cite_note-2) (resp. closed neighbourhood, compact neighbourhood, etc.). Some authors[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neighbourhood_(mathematics)#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEngelking198912-3) require neighbourhoods to be open, so it is important to note their conventions.

[![Image 24](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Neighborhood_illust2.svg/250px-Neighborhood_illust2.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Neighborhood_illust2.svg)

A closed rectangle V is not a neighbourhood of any of its corners or its boundary since there is no open set in V containing any corner or edge point.

A set that is a neighbourhood of each of its points is open since it can be expressed as the union of open sets containing each of its points. A closed rectangle, as illustrated in the figure, is not a neighbourhood of all its points; points on the edges or corners of the rectangle are not contained in any open set that is contained within the rectangle.

The collection of all neighbourhoods of a point is called the [neighbourhood system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neighbourhood_system "Neighbourhood system") at the point.

Topology from neighbourhoods
----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Neighbourhood_(mathematics)&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Topology from neighbourhoods")]

The above definition is useful if the notion of [open set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_set "Open set") is already defined. There is an alternative way to define a topology, by first defining the [neighbourhood system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neighbourhood_system "Neighbourhood system"), and then open sets as those sets containing a neighbourhood of each of their points.

A neighbourhood system on ![Image 82: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) is the assignment of a [filter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Filter_(set_theory) "Filter (set theory)")![Image 83: {\displaystyle N(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ca1ee5a5ef6497108b2f6d7f1fc8a224a52062fa) of subsets of ![Image 84: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) to each ![Image 85: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) in ![Image 86: {\displaystyle X,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/09ba32eeb405f7f5f2bac1eb12987c47d2fd42df) such that

1.   the point ![Image 87: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) is an element of each ![Image 88: {\displaystyle U}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/458a728f53b9a0274f059cd695e067c430956025) in ![Image 89: {\displaystyle N(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ca1ee5a5ef6497108b2f6d7f1fc8a224a52062fa)
2.   each ![Image 90: {\displaystyle U}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/458a728f53b9a0274f059cd695e067c430956025) in ![Image 91: {\displaystyle N(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ca1ee5a5ef6497108b2f6d7f1fc8a224a52062fa) contains some ![Image 92: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) in ![Image 93: {\displaystyle N(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ca1ee5a5ef6497108b2f6d7f1fc8a224a52062fa) such that for each ![Image 94: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d) in ![Image 95: {\displaystyle V,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ace9595e3ce66fdec7e9d30202626accd676b11e)![Image 96: {\displaystyle U}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/458a728f53b9a0274f059cd695e067c430956025) is in ![Image 97: {\displaystyle N(y).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6ad921d32f05d009d7d4e49fc6ddc35d79e1ad68)

One can show that both definitions are compatible, that is, the topology obtained from the neighbourhood system defined using open sets is the original one, and vice versa when starting out from a neighbourhood system.

A **deleted neighbourhood** of a point ![Image 108: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) (sometimes called a **punctured neighbourhood**) is a neighbourhood of ![Image 109: {\displaystyle p,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/393fcf18074cb42eafb26b76c515a1e93e17512c) without ![Image 110: {\displaystyle \{p\}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4dc64a090e76279f4609c47c0bb3c54f255823ed) For instance, the [interval](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interval_(mathematics) "Interval (mathematics)")![Image 111: {\displaystyle (-1,1)=\{y:-1<y<1\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0f6213e6b36fed5da66ad8287877661259d11797) is a neighbourhood of ![Image 112: {\displaystyle p=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b3e6ac10fa45fb984d886065f959a6bdd467b5e8) in the [real line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_line "Real line"), so the set ![Image 113: {\displaystyle (-1,0)\cup (0,1)=(-1,1)\setminus \{0\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/179d91ec5a74fffa6c0b13b30976889c9a53c64c) is a deleted neighbourhood of ![Image 114: {\displaystyle 0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/916e773e0593223c306a3e6852348177d1934962) A deleted neighbourhood of a given point is not in fact a neighbourhood of the point. The concept of deleted neighbourhood occurs in the [definition of the limit of a function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limit_of_a_function#Functions_on_topological_spaces "Limit of a function") and in the definition of limit points (among other things).[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neighbourhood_(mathematics)#cite_note-4)
