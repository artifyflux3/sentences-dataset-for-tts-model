The **Maya calendar** is a system of [calendars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calendar "Calendar") used in [pre-Columbian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pre-Columbian_era "Pre-Columbian era")[Mesoamerica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mesoamerica "Mesoamerica") and in many modern communities in the [Guatemalan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guatemala "Guatemala") highlands,[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maya_calendar#cite_note-1)[Veracruz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Veracruz "Veracruz"), [Oaxaca](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oaxaca "Oaxaca") and [Chiapas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chiapas "Chiapas"), Mexico.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maya_calendar#cite_note-2)

The essentials of the Maya calendar are based upon a system which had been in common use throughout the region, dating back to at least the 5th century BC. It shares many aspects with calendars employed by other earlier Mesoamerican civilizations, such as the [Zapotec](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zapotec_civilization "Zapotec civilization") and [Olmec](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Olmec "Olmec") and contemporary or later ones such as the [Mixtec](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mixtec "Mixtec") and [Aztec calendars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aztec_calendar "Aztec calendar").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maya_calendar#cite_note-3)

By the [Maya mythological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maya_mythology "Maya mythology") tradition, as documented in Colonial Yucatec accounts and reconstructed from Late Classic and Postclassic inscriptions, the deity [Itzamna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Itzamna "Itzamna") is frequently credited with bringing the knowledge of the calendrical system to the ancestral Maya, along with [writing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Writing_system "Writing system") in general and other foundational aspects of Mayan culture.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maya_calendar#cite_note-4)

The Maya calendar consists of several cycles or _counts_ of different lengths. The 260-day count is known to scholars as the _[Tzolkin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tzolk%CA%BCin "Tzolk ºin")_, or _Tzolk ºin_.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maya_calendar#cite_note-Academia-5) The Tzolkin was combined with a 365-day vague solar year known as the [Haab º](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haab%CA%BC "Haab º") to form a synchronized cycle lasting for 52 Haab º called the [Calendar Round](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calendar_Round "Calendar Round"). The Calendar Round is still in use by many groups in the Guatemalan highlands.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maya_calendar#cite_note-6)

A different calendar was used to track longer periods of time and for the inscription of [calendar dates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calendar_date "Calendar date") (i.e., identifying when one event occurred in relation to others). This is the [Long Count](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mesoamerican_Long_Count_calendar "Mesoamerican Long Count calendar"). It is a count of days since a mythological starting-point.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maya_calendar#cite_note-7) According to the correlation between the Long Count and Western calendars accepted by the great majority of Maya researchers (known as the Goodman-Martinez-Thompson, or GMT, correlation), this starting-point is equivalent to August 11, 3114 BC in the [proleptic Gregorian calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proleptic_Gregorian_calendar "Proleptic Gregorian calendar") or September 6, in the [Julian calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Julian_calendar "Julian calendar") (‚àí3113 astronomical). The GMT correlation was chosen by [John Eric Sydney Thompson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J._Eric_S._Thompson "J. Eric S. Thompson") in 1935 on the basis of earlier correlations by [Joseph Goodman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_T._Goodman "Joseph T. Goodman") in 1905 (August 11), Juan Mart√≠nez Hern√°ndez in 1926 (August 12) and Thompson himself in 1927 (August 13).[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maya_calendar#cite_note-8) By its linear nature, the Long Count was capable of being extended to refer to any date far into the past or future. This calendar involved the use of a [positional notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positional_notation "Positional notation") system, in which each position signified an increasing [multiple](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiple_(mathematics) "Multiple (mathematics)") of the number of days. The [Maya numeral system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maya_numerals "Maya numerals") was essentially [vigesimal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vigesimal "Vigesimal") (i.e., [base](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Numeral_system "Numeral system")-20) and each unit of a given position represented 20 times the unit of the position which preceded it. An important exception was made for the second-order place value, which instead represented 18 √ó 20, or 360 days, more closely approximating the solar year than would 20 √ó 20 = 400 days. The cycles of the Long Count are independent of the solar year.

Many Maya Long Count inscriptions contain a [supplementary series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maya_calendar#Supplementary_Series), which provides information on the [lunar phase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunar_phase "Lunar phase"), number of the current [lunation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_moon "New moon") in a series of six and which of the nine [Lords of the Night](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lords_of_the_Night "Lords of the Night") rules.

Less-prevalent or poorly understood cycles, combinations and calendar progressions were also tracked. An _819-day Count_ is attested in a few inscriptions. Repeating sets of 9 days (see below "Nine lords of the night")[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maya_calendar#cite_note-9) associated with different groups of [deities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maya_mythology "Maya mythology"), animals and other significant concepts are also known.

The _[tzolk ºin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tzolk%CA%BCin "Tzolk ºin")_ (in modern Maya [orthography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthography "Orthography"); also commonly written _tzolkin_) is the name commonly employed by Mayanist researchers for the Maya Sacred Round or 260-day calendar. The word _tzolk ºin_ is a [neologism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neologism "Neologism") coined in [Yucatec Maya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yucatec_Maya_language "Yucatec Maya language"), to mean "count of days" (Coe 1992). The various names of this calendar as used by precolumbian Maya people are still debated by scholars. The [Aztec](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aztec "Aztec") calendar equivalent was called _[T≈çnalp≈çhualli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/T%C5%8Dnalp%C5%8Dhualli "T≈çnalp≈çhualli")_, in the [Nahuatl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nahuatl "Nahuatl") language.

The tzolk ºin calendar combines twenty day names with the thirteen day numbers to produce 260 unique days. It is used to determine the time of religious and ceremonial events and for divination. Each successive day is numbered from 1 up to 13 and then starting again at 1. Separately from this, every day is given a name in sequence from a list of 20 day names:

Some systems started the count with 1 Imix, followed by 2 Ik º, 3 Ak ºb ºal, etc. up to 13 B ºen. The day numbers then start again at 1 while the named-day sequence continues onwards, so the next days in the sequence are 1 Ix, 2 Men, 3 K ºib º, 4 Kab ºan, 5 Etz ºnab º, 6 Kawak and 7 Ajaw. With all twenty named days used, these now began to repeat the cycle while the number sequence continues, so the next day after 7 Ajaw is 8 Imix. The repetition of these interlocking 13- and 20-day cycles therefore takes 260 days to complete (that is, for every possible combination of number/named day to occur once).

The earliest known inscription with a Tzolk ºin is an Olmec earspool with 2 Ahau 3 Ceh - 6.3.10.9.0, September 2, -678 (Julian astronomical).[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maya_calendar#cite_note-12)

The Haab º was made up of eighteen months of twenty days each plus a period of five days ("nameless days") at the end of the year known as _Wayeb'_ (or _Uayeb_ in 16th-century orthography). The five days of Wayeb º were thought to be a dangerous time. Foster (2002) writes, "During Wayeb, portals between the mortal realm and the Underworld dissolved. No boundaries prevented the ill-intending deities from causing disasters." To ward off these evil spirits, the Maya had customs and rituals they practiced during Wayeb º. For example, people avoided leaving their houses and washing or combing their hair. Bricker (1982) estimates that the Haab º was first used around 550 BC with a starting point of the [winter solstice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Winter_solstice "Winter solstice").[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maya_calendar#cite_note-16)

The Haab º month names are known today by their corresponding names in colonial-era [Yukatek Maya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yucatec_Maya_language "Yucatec Maya language"), as transcribed by 16th-century sources (in particular, [Diego de Landa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diego_de_Landa "Diego de Landa") and books such as the _[Chilam Balam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chilam\_Balam "Chilam Balam")_ of Chumayel). Phonemic analyses of Haab º glyph names in pre-Columbian [Maya inscriptions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maya_script "Maya script") have demonstrated that the names for these twenty-day periods varied considerably from region to region and from period to period, reflecting differences in the base language(s) and usage in the Classic and Postclassic eras predating their recording by Spanish sources.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maya_calendar#cite_note-17)

Each day in the Haab º calendar was identified by a day number in the month followed by the name of the month. Day numbers began with a glyph translated as the "seating of" a named month, which is usually regarded as day 0 of that month, although a minority treat it as day 20 of the month preceding the named month. In the latter case, the seating of Pop is day 5 of Wayeb º. For the majority, the first day of the year was 0 Pop (the seating of Pop). This was followed by 1 Pop, 2 Pop as far as 19 Pop then 0 Wo, 1 Wo and so on.

Because the Haab º had 365 days and the [tropical year](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tropical_year "Tropical year") is 365.2422 days, the days of the Haab º did not coincide with the tropical year.

A Calendar Round date is a date that gives both the Tzolk ºin and Haab º. This date will repeat after 52 Haab º years or 18,980 days, a Calendar Round. For example, the current creation started on 4 Ahau 8 Kumk ºu. When this date recurs it is known as a Calendar Round completion.

Arithmetically, the duration of the Calendar Round is the [least common multiple](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Least_common_multiple "Least common multiple") of 260 and 365; 18,980 is 73 √ó 260 Tzolk ºin days and 52 √ó 365 Haab º days.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maya_calendar#cite_note-18)

Not every possible combination of Tzolk ºin and Haab º can occur. For Tzolk ºin days Imix, Kimi, Chuwen and Kib º, the Haab º day can only be 4, 9, 14 or 19; for Ik º, Manik º, Eb º and Kab ºan, the Haab º day can only be 0, 5, 10 or 15; for Akb ºal º, Lamat, B ºen and Etz ºnab º, the Haab º day can only be 1, 6, 11 or 16; for K ºan, Muluk, Ix and Kawak, the Haab º day can only be 2, 7, 12 or 17; and for Chikchan, Ok, Men and Ajaw, the Haab º day can only be 3, 8, 13 or 18.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maya_calendar#cite_note-19)

A "Year Bearer" is a [Tzolk ºin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tzolk%CA%BCin "Tzolk ºin") day name that occurs on 0 Pop, the first day of the [Haab º](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haab%CA%BC "Haab º"). Since there are 20 Tzolk ºin day names, 365 days in the Haab º, and the remainder of 365 divided by 20 is 5 (365 = 18√ó20 + 5), the Tzolk ºin day name for each successive 0 Pop will be 5 later in the cycle of Tzolk'in day names. Similarly, since there are 13 Tzolk'in day numbers, and the remainder of 365 divided by 13 is 1 (365 = 28√ó13 + 1), the Tzolk'in day number for each successive 0 Pop will be 1 greater than before. As such, the sequence of Tzolk'in dates corresponding to the Haab' date 0 Pop are as follows:

*   1 Ik º
*   2 Manik º
*   3 Eb º
*   4 Kab ºan
*   5 Ik º
*   ...
*   12 Kab'an
*   13 Ik'
*   1 Manik'
*   ...

Thus, the Year Bearers are the four Tzolk ºin day names that appear in this sequence: Ik', Manik', Eb', and Kab'an.

"Year Bearer" literally translates a Mayan concept.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maya_calendar#cite_note-20) Its importance resides in two facts. For one, the four years headed by the Year Bearers are named after them and share their characteristics; therefore, they also have their own prognostications and patron deities.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maya_calendar#cite_note-21) Moreover, since the Year Bearers are geographically identified with boundary markers or mountains, they help define the local community.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maya_calendar#cite_note-22)

The classic system of Year Bearers described above is found at Tikal and in the [Dresden Codex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dresden_Codex "Dresden Codex"). During the Late Classic period a different set of Year Bearers was in use in Campeche. In this system, the Year Bearers were the [Tzolk ºin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tzolk%CA%BCin "Tzolk ºin") that coincided with 1 Pop. These were Ak ºb ºal, Lamat, B ºen and Edznab. During the Post-Classic period in Yucat√°n a third system was in use. In this system the Year Bearers were the days that coincided with 2 Pop: K ºan, Muluc, Ix and Kawak. This system is found in the Chronicle of Oxkutzcab. In addition, just before the Spanish conquest in Mayapan the Maya began to number the days of the Haab º from 1 to 20. In this system the Year Bearers are the same as in the 1 Pop ‚Äì Campeche system. The Classic Year Bearer system is still in use in the Guatemalan highlands[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maya_calendar#cite_note-23) and in Veracruz, Oaxaca and Chiapas, Mexico.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maya_calendar#cite_note-Miles,_Susanna_W_pp._273-84-24)

[![Image 59](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/East_side_of_stela_C%2C_Quirigua.svg/250px-East_side_of_stela_C%2C_Quirigua.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:East_side_of_stela_C,_Quirigua.svg)

East side of Stela C, Quirigua with the mythical creation date of 13 baktuns, 0 katuns, 0 tuns, 0 uinals, 0 kins, 4 Ahau, 8 Cumku ‚Äì August 11, 3114 BC in the [proleptic Gregorian calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proleptic_Gregorian_calendar "Proleptic Gregorian calendar")

Since Calendar Round dates repeat every 18,980 days, approximately 52 solar years, the cycle repeats roughly once each lifetime, so a more refined method of dating was needed if history was to be recorded accurately. To specify dates over periods longer than 52 years, Mesoamericans used the Long Count calendar.

The Maya name for a day was _k ºin_. Twenty of these k ºins are known as a _winal_ or _uinal_. Eighteen winals make one _tun_. Twenty tuns are known as a _k ºatun_. Twenty k ºatuns make a _b ºak ºtun_.

The Long Count calendar identifies a date by counting the number of days from the Mayan creation date 4 Ahaw, 8 Kumk ºu (August 11, 3114 BC in the [proleptic Gregorian calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proleptic_Gregorian_calendar "Proleptic Gregorian calendar") or September 6 in the [Julian calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Julian_calendar "Julian calendar") -3113 astronomical dating). But instead of using a base-10 ([decimal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal "Decimal")) scheme, the Long Count days were tallied in a modified base-20 scheme. Thus 0.0.0.1.5 is equal to 25 and 0.0.0.2.0 is equal to 40. As the winal unit resets after only counting to 18, the Long Count consistently uses base-20 only if the tun is considered the primary unit of measurement, not the k ºin; with the k ºin and winal units being the number of days in the tun. The Long Count 0.0.1.0.0 represents 360 days, rather than the 400 in a purely base-20 ([vigesimal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vigesimal "Vigesimal")) count.

There are also four rarely used higher-order cycles: [piktun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Piktun "Piktun"), [kalabtun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Piktun "Piktun"), [k ºinchiltun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Piktun "Piktun"), and [alautun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Piktun "Piktun").

Since the Long Count dates are unambiguous, the Long Count was particularly well suited to use on monuments. The monumental inscriptions would not only include the 5 digits of the Long Count, but would also include the two tzolk ºin characters followed by the two haab º characters.

Misinterpretation of the [Mesoamerican Long Count calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mesoamerican_Long_Count_calendar "Mesoamerican Long Count calendar") was the basis for a popular belief that a [cataclysm would take place on December 21, 2012](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2012_phenomenon "2012 phenomenon"). December 21, 2012 was simply the day that the calendar went to the next [b ºak ºtun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baktun "Baktun"), at Long Count 13.0.0.0.0. The date of the start of the next b'ak'tun (Long Count 14.0.0.0.0) is March 26, 2407. The date of the start of the next [piktun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Piktun "Piktun") (a complete series of 20 b ºak ºtuns), at Long Count 1.0.0.0.0.0, is October 13, 4772.

Supplementary Series
--------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Maya_calendar&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Supplementary Series")]

Many Classic period inscriptions include a series of glyphs known as the Supplementary Series. The operation of this series was largely worked out by [John E. Teeple](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_E._Teeple "John E. Teeple"). The Supplementary Series most commonly consists of the following elements:

Each night was ruled by one of the nine lords of the underworld. This nine-day cycle was usually written as two glyphs: a glyph that referred to the Nine Lords as a group, followed by a glyph for the lord that would rule the next night.

A lunar series generally is written as five glyphs that provide information about the current [lunation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_moon "New moon"), the number of the lunation in a series of six, the current ruling lunar deity and the length of the current lunation.

The Maya counted the number of days in the current lunation. They used two systems for the zero date of the lunar cycle: either the first night they could see the thin crescent moon or the first morning when they could not see the waning moon.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maya_calendar#cite_note-25) The age of the moon was depicted by a set of glyphs that mayanists coined glyphs D and E:

*   A new moon glyph was used for day zero in the lunar cycle.
*   D glyphs were used for lunar ages for days 1 through 19, with the number of days that had passed from the new moon.
*   For lunar ages 20 to 30, an E glyph was used, with the number of days from 20.

The Maya counted the lunations. This cycle appears in the lunar series as two glyphs that modern scholars call the 'C' and 'X' glyphs. The C glyph could be prefixed with a number indicating the lunation. No prefixing number meant one, whereas the numbers two through six indicated the other lunations.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maya_calendar#cite_note-26)[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maya_calendar#cite_note-27) There was also a part of the C glyph that indicated where this fell in a larger cycle of 18 lunations. Accompanying the C glyph was the 'X' glyph that showed a similar pattern of 18 lunations.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maya_calendar#cite_note-28)[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maya_calendar#cite_note-29)

The present era lunar synodic period is about 29.5305877 [mean solar days](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mean_solar_time "Mean solar time") or about 29 days 12 hours 44 minutes and 2+7/9 seconds. As a whole number, the number of days per lunation will be either 29 or 30 days, with the 30-day intervals necessarily occurring slightly more frequently than the 29-day intervals. The Maya wrote whether the lunar month was 29 or 30 days as two glyphs: a glyph for lunation length followed by either a glyph made up of a moon glyph over a bundle with a suffix of 9 for a 29-day lunation or a moon glyph with a suffix of 10 for a 30-day lunation. Since the Maya didn't use fractions, lunations were approximated by using the formula that there were 149 lunations completed in 4400 days, which yielded a rather short mean month of exactly 4400/149 = 29+79/149 days = 29 days 12 hours 43 minutes and 29+59/149 seconds, or about 29.5302 days.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maya_calendar#cite_note-30)

Some Mayan monuments include glyphs that record an 819-day count[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maya_calendar#cite_note-31) in their Initial Series. These can also be found in the [Dresden codex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dresden_Codex "Dresden Codex").[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maya_calendar#cite_note-32) This is described in Thompson.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maya_calendar#cite_note-33) More examples of this can be found in Kelley.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maya_calendar#cite_note-34) Each group of 819 days was associated with one of four colors and the cardinal direction with which it was associated‚Äì black corresponded to west, red to east, white to north and yellow to south.

The 819-day count can be described several ways: Most of these are referred to using a "Y" glyph and a number. Many also have a glyph for K ºawill‚Äì the god with a smoking mirror in his head. K ºawill has been suggested as having a link to Jupiter.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maya_calendar#cite_note-35) In the [Dresden codex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dresden_Codex "Dresden Codex") almanac 59 there are [Chaacs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chaac "Chaac") of the four colors. The accompanying texts begin with a directional glyph and a verb for 819-day-count phrases. Anderson[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maya_calendar#cite_note-36) provides a detailed description of the 819-day count.

[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maya_calendar#cite_note-37)

During the late Classic period the Maya began to use an abbreviated short count instead of the Long Count. An example of this can be found on altar 14 at Tikal.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maya_calendar#cite_note-38) In the kingdoms of Postclassic Yucat√°n, the Short Count was used instead of the Long Count. The cyclical Short Count is a count of 13 k ºatuns (or 260 tuns), in which each k ºatun was named after its concluding day, Ahau ('Lord'). 1 Imix was selected as the recurrent 'first day' of the cycle, corresponding to 1 [Cipactli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cipactli "Cipactli") in the Aztec day count. The cycle was counted from katun 11 Ahau to katun 13 Ahau. Since a katun is 20 √ó 360 = 7200 days long, and the remainder of 7200 divided by 13 is 11 (7200 = 553√ó13 + 11), the day number of the concluding day of each successive katun is 9 greater than before (wrapping around at 13, since only 13 day numbers are used). That is, starting with the katun that begins with 1 Imix, the sequence of concluding day numbers is 11, 9, 7, 5, 3, 1, 12, 10, 8, 6, 4, 2, 13, 11, ..., all named Ahau. The concluding day 13 Ahau was followed by the re-entering first day 1 Imix. This is the system as found in the colonial Books of [Chilam Balam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chilam_Balam "Chilam Balam"). In characteristic Mesoamerican fashion, these books project the cycle onto the landscape, with 13 _Ahauob_ 'Lordships' dividing the land of Yucat√°n into 13 'kingdoms'.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maya_calendar#cite_note-39)
