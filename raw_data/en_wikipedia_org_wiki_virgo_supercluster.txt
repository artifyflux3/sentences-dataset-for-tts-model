The **Local Supercluster** (**LSC** or **LS**), or **Virgo Supercluster** is a formally defined supercluster of galaxies containing the [Virgo Cluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgo_Cluster "Virgo Cluster") and [Local Group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_Group "Local Group"). The latter contains the [Milky Way](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milky_Way "Milky Way") and [Andromeda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andromeda_Galaxy "Andromeda Galaxy") galaxies, among others. At least 100 [galaxy groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_group "Galaxy group") and [clusters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_cluster "Galaxy cluster") are located within the supercluster diameter of 33 [megaparsecs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parsec "Parsec") (110 million [light-years](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light-years "Light-years")). The Virgo Supercluster is one of about 10 million [superclusters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supercluster "Supercluster") in the [observable universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe "Observable universe"), with the main body of the supercluster, the Virgo Strand, connecting the [Hydra-Centaurus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydra-Centaurus "Hydra-Centaurus") and the [Perseus–Pisces Superclusters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perseus%E2%80%93Pisces_Supercluster "Perseus–Pisces Supercluster").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgo_Supercluster#cite_note-:1-2) The Virgo Supercluster is part of the [Pisces–Cetus Supercluster Complex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pisces%E2%80%93Cetus_Supercluster_Complex "Pisces–Cetus Supercluster Complex"), a [galaxy filament](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_filament "Galaxy filament").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgo_Supercluster#cite_note-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgo_Supercluster#cite_note-4)

A 2014 study indicates that the Local Supercluster is only a part of an even greater supercluster, [Laniakea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laniakea_Supercluster "Laniakea Supercluster"), a larger group centered on the [Great Attractor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Attractor "Great Attractor"),[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgo_Supercluster#cite_note-5) thus subsuming the former Virgo Supercluster under Laniakea.

Beginning with the first large sample of [nebulae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nebula "Nebula") published by [William](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Herschel "William Herschel") and [John Herschel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Herschel "John Herschel") in 1863, it was known that there is a marked excess of nebular fields in the constellation [Virgo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgo_(constellation) "Virgo (constellation)"), near the north [galactic pole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galactic_coordinate_system "Galactic coordinate system"). In the 1950s, French–American astronomer [Gérard de Vaucouleurs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G%C3%A9rard_de_Vaucouleurs "Gérard de Vaucouleurs") was the first to argue that this excess represented a large-scale galaxy-like structure, coining the term "Local Supergalaxy" in 1953, which he changed to "Local Supercluster" (LSC[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgo_Supercluster#cite_note-6)) in 1958. [Harlow Shapley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harlow_Shapley "Harlow Shapley"), in his 1959 book _Of Stars and Men_, suggested the term _Metagalaxy_.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgo_Supercluster#cite_note-7)

Debate went on during the 1960s and 1970s as to whether the Local Supercluster (LS) was actually a structure or a chance alignment of galaxies.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgo_Supercluster#cite_note-deV81-8) The issue was resolved with the large redshift surveys of the late 1970s and early 1980s, which convincingly showed the flattened concentration of galaxies along the supergalactic plane.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgo_Supercluster#cite_note-klypin03-9)

In 1982, [R. Brent Tully](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/R._Brent_Tully "R. Brent Tully") presented the conclusions of his research concerning the basic structure of the LS. It consists of two components: an appreciably flattened disk containing two thirds of the supercluster's luminous galaxies, and a roughly spherical halo containing the remaining third.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgo_Supercluster#cite_note-hu06-10) The disk itself is a thin (~1 [Mpc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parsec "Parsec")) ellipsoid with a long axis / short axis ratio of at least 6 to 1, and possibly as high as 9 to 1.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgo_Supercluster#cite_note-tully82-11) Data released in June 2003 from the 5-year [Two-degree-Field Galaxy Redshift Survey (2dF)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2dF_Galaxy_Redshift_Survey "2dF Galaxy Redshift Survey") has allowed astronomers to compare the LS to other superclusters. The LS represents a typical poor (that is, lacking a high density core) supercluster of rather small size. It has one rich galaxy cluster in the center, surrounded by filaments of galaxies and poor groups.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgo_Supercluster#cite_note-ein07-1)

The [Local Group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_Group "Local Group") is located on the outskirts of the LS in a small filament extending from the [Fornax Cluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fornax_Cluster "Fornax Cluster") to the [Virgo Cluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgo_Cluster "Virgo Cluster").[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgo_Supercluster#cite_note-klypin03-9) The Virgo Supercluster's volume is roughly 7,000 times that of the Local Group, or 100 billion times that of the Milky Way.

The main body of the Virgo Supercluster, the flattened disk of galaxies is simply part of a larger [galaxy filament](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_filament "Galaxy filament") known as the [Virgo Strand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgo_Strand "Virgo Strand") or [Centaurus–Virgo–PP Filament](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centaurus%E2%80%93Virgo%E2%80%93PP_Filament "Centaurus–Virgo–PP Filament").[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgo_Supercluster#cite_note-:0-12)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgo_Supercluster#cite_note-:1-2) This filament emanates from the [Centaurus Cluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centaurus_Cluster "Centaurus Cluster") through the [Virgo Cluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgo_Cluster "Virgo Cluster") and continues through the [Ursa Major Cluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ursa_Major_Cluster "Ursa Major Cluster") all the way to the [Perseus–Pisces Supercluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perseus%E2%80%93Pisces_Supercluster "Perseus–Pisces Supercluster").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgo_Supercluster#cite_note-:1-2) The [Virgo Strand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgo_Strand "Virgo Strand") consists of two branches, an upper branch consisting of the main body of the supercluster though the [Virgo Southern Extension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgo_Southern_Extension "Virgo Southern Extension"), the [Virgo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgo_Cluster "Virgo Cluster") and the [Ursa Major Cluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ursa_Major_Cluster "Ursa Major Cluster"), and a lower branch consisting of the [Crater](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Crater_Cloud&action=edit&redlink=1 "Crater Cloud (page does not exist)") and [Leo Clouds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leo_Cloud "Leo Cloud").[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgo_Supercluster#cite_note-:0-12) The rest of remaining third of the galaxies in Virgo Supercluster, including the Milky Way, lie outside the main body of the Supercluster which is the [Virgo Strand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgo_Strand "Virgo Strand").[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgo_Supercluster#cite_note-tully82-11)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgo_Supercluster#cite_note-:0-12)

Galaxy distribution
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Virgo_Supercluster&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Galaxy distribution")]

The [number density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_density "Number density") of galaxies in the LS falls off with the square of the distance from its center near the [Virgo Cluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgo_Cluster "Virgo Cluster"), suggesting that this cluster is not randomly located. Overall, the vast majority of the luminous galaxies (less than [absolute magnitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_magnitude "Absolute magnitude") −13) are concentrated in a small number of [clouds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_cloud "Galaxy cloud") (groups of [galaxy clusters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_cluster "Galaxy cluster")). Ninety-eight percent can be found in the following 11 clouds, given in decreasing order of number of luminous galaxies: [Canes Venatici](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M94_Group#Canes_Venatici_Cloud "M94 Group"), Virgo Cluster, [Virgo II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgo_II_Groups "Virgo II Groups") (southern extension), [Leo II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leo_II_Groups "Leo II Groups"), [Virgo III](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgo_III_Groups "Virgo III Groups"), Crater ([NGC 3672](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NGC_3672 "NGC 3672")), [Leo I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M96_Group "M96 Group"), Leo Minor ([NGC 2841](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NGC_2841 "NGC 2841")), Draco ([NGC 5907](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NGC_5907 "NGC 5907")), Antlia ([NGC 2997](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NGC_2997 "NGC 2997")), and [NGC 5643](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NGC_5643 "NGC 5643").[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgo_Supercluster#cite_note-tully82-11)

Of the luminous galaxies located in the disk, one third are in the Virgo Cluster, with the other two thirds located outside of the cluster.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgo_Supercluster#cite_note-tully82-11)

The luminous galaxies in the halo are concentrated in a small number of clouds (94% in 7 clouds). This distribution indicates that "most of the volume of the supergalactic plane is a great void."[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgo_Supercluster#cite_note-tully82-11) A helpful analogy that matches the observed distribution is that of soap bubbles. Flattish clusters and [superclusters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superclusters "Superclusters") are found at the intersection of bubbles, which are large, roughly spherical (on the order of 20–60 [Mpc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parsec "Parsec") in diameter) voids in space.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgo_Supercluster#cite_note-ma96-13) Long filamentary structures seem to predominate. An example of this is the [Hydra–Centaurus Supercluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydra%E2%80%93Centaurus_Supercluster "Hydra–Centaurus Supercluster"), the nearest supercluster to the Virgo Supercluster, which starts at a distance of roughly 30 Mpc and extends to 60 Mpc.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgo_Supercluster#cite_note-fairall89-14)

### Large-scale dynamics

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Virgo_Supercluster&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Large-scale dynamics")]

Since the late 1980s it has been apparent that not only the [Local Group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_Group "Local Group"), but all matter out to a distance of at least 50 [Mpc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parsec "Parsec") is experiencing a bulk flow on the order of 600 km/s in the direction of the [Norma Cluster (Abell 3627)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norma_Cluster "Norma Cluster").[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgo_Supercluster#cite_note-plionis91-15) Lynden-Bell et al. (1988) dubbed the cause of this the "[Great Attractor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Attractor "Great Attractor")". The Great Attractor is now understood to be the center of mass of an even larger structure of galaxy clusters, dubbed "[Laniakea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laniakea_Supercluster "Laniakea Supercluster")", which includes the Virgo Supercluster (including the Local Group) as well as the Hydra-Centaurus Supercluster, the Pavo-Indus Supercluster, and the Fornax Group.

The Great Attractor, together with the entire supercluster, is found to be moving toward [Shapley Supercluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shapley_Supercluster "Shapley Supercluster"), with center of [Shapley Attractor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shapley_Attractor "Shapley Attractor").[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgo_Supercluster#cite_note-16)

The LS has a total mass _M_ ≈ 10 15[M☉](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_mass "Solar mass") and a total optical luminosity _L_ ≈ 3×10 12[L☉](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_luminosity "Solar luminosity").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgo_Supercluster#cite_note-ein07-1) This yields a [mass-to-light ratio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass-to-light_ratio "Mass-to-light ratio") of about 300 times that of the solar ratio (M☉/L☉ = 1), a figure that is consistent with results obtained for other superclusters.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgo_Supercluster#cite_note-todd98-17)[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgo_Supercluster#cite_note-heymans08-18) By comparison, the [mass-to-light ratio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass-to-light_ratio "Mass-to-light ratio") for the [Milky Way](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milky_Way "Milky Way") is 63.8 assuming a solar [absolute magnitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_magnitude "Absolute magnitude") of 4.83,[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgo_Supercluster#cite_note-nssdc-19) a Milky Way absolute magnitude of −20.9,[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgo_Supercluster#cite_note-20) and a Milky Way mass of 1.25×10 12 M☉.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgo_Supercluster#cite_note-McMillan2011-21) These ratios are one of the main arguments in favor of the presence of large amounts of [dark matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter "Dark matter") in the universe; if dark matter did not exist, much smaller mass-to-light ratios would be expected.
