**Sulfur mononitride** is an [inorganic compound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inorganic_compound "Inorganic compound") with the [molecular formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_formula "Molecular formula") SN. It is the sulfur analogue of and [isoelectronic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isoelectronicity "Isoelectronicity") to the radical [nitric oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitric_oxide "Nitric oxide"), NO. It was initially detected in 1975, in outer space in giant molecular clouds and later the coma of comets.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_mononitride#cite_note-emp90_1_335-1) This spurred further laboratory studies of the compound. Synthetically, it is produced by electric discharge in mixtures of nitrogen and sulfur compounds, or combustion in the gas phase and by photolysis in solution.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_mononitride#cite_note-burr1985-2)

The NS [radical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radical_(chemistry) "Radical (chemistry)") is a highly transient species, with a lifetime on the order of milliseconds, but it can be observed spectroscopically over short periods of time through several methods of generation. NS is too reactive to isolate as a solid or liquid, and has only been prepared as a vapor in low pressure or low-temperature matrices due to its tendency to rapidly oligomerize to more stable, diamagnetic species.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_mononitride#cite_note-:4-3)

### Discharge of nitrogen and sulfur vapor

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_mononitride&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Discharge of nitrogen and sulfur vapor")]

Transmission of electric discharge through a glass tube with quartz windows containing a mixture of nitrogen and sulfur vapor (rigorously free of oxygen) results in the spectrum of emitted light gaining bands consistent with the formation of NS.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_mononitride#cite_note-4)

Passing a mixture of gaseous N 2 and S 2 Cl 2 through the side arm of an absorption cell undergoing microwave discharge produces NS. Infrared diode laser spectroscopy taken using this method allowed for derivation of the equilibrium rotational constant, and therefore calculation of the equilibrium bond length as 1.4940 Å.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_mononitride#cite_note-5)

With low pressure microwave discharge of elemental nitrogen and sulfur, followed by low temperature trapping in argon matrices, one obtains a mixture of products including NS, NNS, SNS, and NSS. By adding excess sulfur, SSNS is also produced.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_mononitride#cite_note-6)

### Burning of sulfur and nitrogen doped flames

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_mononitride&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Burning of sulfur and nitrogen doped flames")]

[Methane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane "Methane") was premixed with fuel in the form of either O 2, N 2 O, or air and burned at ambient pressure. The source of nitrogen was introduced by addition of 1–5 mol% NH 3 gas and sulfur by 0.01–0.5 mol% H 2 S or SF 6 gas. A steady state concentration of NS within the flame front is observed by [laser-induced fluorescence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laser-induced_fluorescence "Laser-induced fluorescence") (LIF) spectrum.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_mononitride#cite_note-:2-7)

### Flash laser photolysis of tetranitrogen tetrasulfide

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_mononitride&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Flash laser photolysis of tetranitrogen tetrasulfide")]

The NS radical was detected by LIF spectrum as the product of [photolysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photodissociation "Photodissociation") of tetranitrogen tetrasulfide (N 4 S 4) gas by a 248 nm laser.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_mononitride#cite_note-:0-8)

### Flash and continuous photolysis of Cr(CH 3 CN)5(NS)2+

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_mononitride&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Flash and continuous photolysis of Cr(CH3CN)5(NS)2+")]

Aerated solutions of Cr(CH 3 CN)5(NS)2+ are highly photoactive and prone to rapid decomposition. Deaerated solutions of Cr(CH 3 CN)5(NS)2+ in [acetonitrile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetonitrile "Acetonitrile") are stable as long as they are kept in the dark. Continuous photolysis using 366 nm light is slow, while using a 355 nm pulsed laser results in faster labilization of NS.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_mononitride#cite_note-:1-9)

Evidence suggests that NS can react with itself to reach N 2 S 2, N 4 S 4, and polymers of the form (NS)x. (NS)x forms from polymerization of cyclo-N 2 S 2.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_mononitride#cite_note-:4-3)

N 3 S 3.

The radical decay time of NS alone is on the order of 1-3 ms. As evident by no change to this decay time upon addition of NO or O 2 at ambient temperatures, the NS radical is unreactive with NO and O 2. However, rapid, first-order decay is observed with the addition of NO 2. This reaction is proposed to proceed through various intermediates, ultimately reaching final products of N 2 and SO 2.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_mononitride#cite_note-:0-8) This rapid reaction occurs with a [rate constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reaction_rate_constant "Reaction rate constant") of k = (2.54 ± 0.12) × 10−11 cm 3 molecules−1 s−1 at 295 K. By use of [Density Functional Theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Density_functional_theory "Density functional theory") based computational calculations, the minima and transition states of the potential energy surface of this reaction have been predicted.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_mononitride#cite_note-:0-8)

### Astronomical reactivity

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_mononitride&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Astronomical reactivity")]

Within the inner coma of comets, many reactions are theorized to be relevant to the formation and reactivity of the NS radical.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_mononitride#cite_note-:3-11)

### Metal-thionitrosyl complexes

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_mononitride&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Metal-thionitrosyl complexes")]

As a ligand, NS acts as a σ-donor and π-acceptor, forming metal-thionitrosyl complexes. Transition-metal thionitrosyl complexes have been prepared by the following procedures:[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_mononitride#cite_note-:5-12)

*   Sulfur transfer to metal nitrido complexes 
    *   Example: Reflux of (Ph 4 P)[OsNCl 4] and (Ph 4 P)NCS yields green-brown solid [Ph 4 P]2[Os(NS)(NCS)5] [[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_mononitride#cite_note-13)

*   Reaction of trithiazyltrichloride with transition metal complexes 
    *   NSCl 3 + OsCl 3> [Os(NS)Cl 3]

*   Halide abstraction from coordinated thiazyl complexes 
    *   Abstraction of sulfur-bonded fluorine from [(η 5−C 5 H 5)Cr(NO)2(NSF)]-[AsF 6] by AsF 5> [(η 5-C 5 H 5)Cr(NO)2(NS)]-[AsF 6]2

*   Reaction of NS+ salts with transition metal complexes 
    *   NS+SbF 6− + [M(CO)5 Br] > [M(CO)5(NS)]2+, M=Mn, Re [[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_mononitride#cite_note-14)
    *   NS+AsF 6− + [(η 5-C 5 H 5)Fe(CO)2(SO 2)]+> [(η 5-C 5 H 5)Fe(CO)2(NS)] [AsF 6]2

*   Reaction of tetrasulfur tetranitride with metal halides or nitrides

From [X-ray crystallography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray_crystallography "X-ray crystallography") of many of such metal-thionitrosyl complexes, one can observe that the M-N-S bond angle is nearly linear, suggesting sp [hybridization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hybridization_(chemistry) "Hybridization (chemistry)") about N. Short M-N distances and long N-S distances reflect the resonance structure of M=N=S having greater contribution than M-N≡S.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_mononitride#cite_note-:5-12)

Typical _v_(NS) IR stretching frequencies are approximately 1065 cm−1 for low-valent transition metal complexes and around 1390 cm−1 in the high valent cases, whereas the free gas-phase radical exhibits a 1204 cm−1 signal.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_mononitride#cite_note-:4-3)

### Electronic structure of Fe(S 2 CNMe 2)2(NS)

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_mononitride&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Electronic structure of Fe(S2CNMe2)2(NS)")]

The electronic structures of Fe(S 2 CNMe 2)2(NE), where E=O, S, or Se were calculated using [Density Functional Theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Density_functional_theory "Density functional theory") methods. It was found that the large [Mulliken](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mulliken_population_analysis "Mulliken population analysis") spin density remained concentrated on the Fe(NE) core and Fe-N distances experienced little change from the [chalcogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chalcogen "Chalcogen") atom used. The [HOMO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HOMO_and_LUMO "HOMO and LUMO") of both nitrosyl and thionitrosyl complexes retained 1a 1 (d z 2) character. The small changes in the energies of the spin orbitals of the complexes, particularly the decreased energetic gap between 2b 2 and 1b 1 and 2b 1 and 1b 1 orbitals is attributed to NS being a weaker π-acceptor than NO.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_mononitride#cite_note-:1-9)

### Photoinduced NS transfer from chromium to iron

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_mononitride&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Photoinduced NS transfer from chromium to iron")]

When a spin-trapping agent, such as Fe(S 2 CNEt 2)2 is present during the photolysis of Cr(CH 3 CN)5(NS)2+, new S=1/2 EPR bands are observed, attributed to the formation of Fe(S 2 CNEt 2)2(NS), and the signal from Cr(CH 3 CN)5(NS)2+ disappears. This suggests that the NS radical has transferred from the chromium complex to the iron complex.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_mononitride#cite_note-:1-9)

An example of an NS in situ transfer is the following reaction, which needs light to occur:

(Cr(CH 3 CN)5(NS)2+ + Fe(S 2 CNEt 2) + CH 3 CN → Cr(CH 3 CN)2+6 + Fe(S 2 CNEt 2)2(NS)
This was particularly significant as it was the first controlled and well-characterized reactivity of NS in solution. Further, it showed the potential for similar reactivity in known reactions with NO, such as use of this iron dithiocarbamate complex.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_mononitride#cite_note-:4-3)

The valence electrons of this compound match those of [nitric oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitric_oxide "Nitric oxide"). Sulfur mononitride can be described as some average of a set of [resonance structures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resonance_structure "Resonance structure"). The singly bonded structure (first resonance structure shown) has little contribution. The formal bond order is considered to be 2.5.

The decreasing [electronegativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronegativity "Electronegativity") with increasingly heavy chalcogenides leads to a reversal of the dipole. In NO, oxygen is the more electronegative element. In NS, nitrogen is more electronegative. The NS radical is significantly more unstable and prone to catenation than NO.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_mononitride#cite_note-:4-3)

Molecules in distant astronomical regions can be identified based on their unique rotational transitions, of which the corresponding microwave frequencies are detectable by antennae on Earth. The presence of interstellar sulfur mononitride was first reported in 1975 by back to back letters published in the Astrophysical Journal.

Interstellar NS was first identified in the giant molecular cloud [Sagittarius B2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sagittarius_B2 "Sagittarius B2") (Sgr B2). Its presence was reported in two concurrent articles. Measurements conducted with the [National Radio Astronomy Observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Radio_Astronomy_Observatory "National Radio Astronomy Observatory") telescope at Kitt Peak, Arizona, picked up millimeter-wavelength radiation in Sgr B2 attributed to _c-_ state transitions of NS in the 2 Π 1/2 state from J=5/2 to J=3/2 at 115.16 GHz.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_mononitride#cite_note-15) This assignment was confirmed by measurements conducted at University of Texas Millimeter Wave Observatory on Mount Locke as well, demonstrating J=5/2 to J=3/2 _c_-state and _d_-state transitions at 115.16 GHz and 115.6 GHz, respectively. Hyperfine interactions arise from 14 N magnetic and electric-quadrupole moments.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_mononitride#cite_note-16)

NS has been detected in regions responsible for forming massive stars, such as [giant molecular clouds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giant_molecular_clouds "Giant molecular clouds") like Sg B2 and cold, dark clouds such as L134N and TMC-1. One survey found NS in 12 out of 14 GMC studied, additionally observing the J=7/2 to J=5/2 and J=3/2 to J=1/2 transitions at 161 and 69 GHz, respectively. The abundance of NS in these regions was approximated based on the ratio of observed to intrinsic hyperfine line strengths as well as modeling using a statistical equilibrium program, finding low abundance in all except the Orion molecular cloud.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_mononitride#cite_note-17)

NS was also observed in the coma of the comets [Hyakutake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comet_Hyakutake "Comet Hyakutake") and [Hale-Bopp](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comet_Hale%E2%80%93Bopp "Comet Hale–Bopp"). It is believed that the observed abundance is higher than gas-phase, ion-molecule models due to an unidentified species X-NS photo-dissociating to release NS.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_mononitride#cite_note-:3-11)

Industrial applications
-----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_mononitride&action=edit&section=16 "Edit section: Industrial applications")]

Detection of NS at steady state concentration in the reaction zone of the combustion of methane doped with ammonia and a fuel sulfur such as H 2 S suggests that NS may be an important reactive intermediate in burning of hydrocarbon flames in a reducing atmosphere, which is relevant to coal pyrolysis and combustion.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_mononitride#cite_note-:2-7)

Fossil fuels contain bound nitrogen, which releases elevated levels of [nitric oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitric_oxide "Nitric oxide") emissions during combustion. NO x emissions can be controlled by denitrification of the fuel source, combustion chamber modification, or both. One developing technique is the reburning of NO x, which is reduced to N 2. These fuels also contain variable amounts of sulfur, which is oxidized to SO 2. Therefore, understanding the reactivity of NO and SO 2 is crucial to the process of reburning. The experimental apparatus to test this involved a primary flame for producing combustion products, which were mixed with NO and SO 2 to mimic coal burning byproducts. This mixture was fed into the burner at atmospheric pressure. 1–2% decrease in NO x concentration is observed at various percentages of total fuel inlet (reburn ratio) in the presence of 0.1% SO 2, which is attributed to the formation of H 2 S, HS, and the resulting reaction with NO, giving rise to NS. The reaction is:[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_mononitride#cite_note-18)

HS + NO → NS + OH
