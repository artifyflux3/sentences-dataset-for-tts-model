Title: Quadric

URL Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadric_surface

Published Time: 2002-11-09T22:02:47Z

Markdown Content:
In mathematics, a **quadric** or **quadric surface** is a [generalization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generalization "Generalization") of [conic sections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conic_section "Conic section") ([ellipses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse "Ellipse"), [parabolas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parabola "Parabola"), and [hyperbolas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbola "Hyperbola")). In [three-dimensional space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_space "Three-dimensional space"), quadrics include [ellipsoids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipsoid "Ellipsoid"), [paraboloids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paraboloid "Paraboloid"), and [hyperboloids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperboloid "Hyperboloid").

More generally, a quadric [hypersurface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypersurface "Hypersurface") (of dimension _D_) embedded in a [higher dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Higher_dimensional "Higher dimensional") space (of dimension _D_ + 1) is defined as the [zero set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero_set "Zero set") of an [irreducible polynomial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irreducible_polynomial "Irreducible polynomial") of [degree](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_of_a_polynomial "Degree of a polynomial") two in _D_ + 1 variables; for example, _D_=1 is the case of conic sections ([plane curves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_curve "Plane curve")). When the defining polynomial is not [absolutely irreducible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolutely_irreducible "Absolutely irreducible"), the zero set is generally not considered a quadric, although it is often called a _degenerate quadric_ or a _reducible quadric_.

A quadric is an [affine algebraic variety](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_algebraic_variety "Affine algebraic variety"), or, if it is reducible, an [affine algebraic set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_algebraic_set "Affine algebraic set"). Quadrics may also be defined in [projective spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_space "Projective space"); see [§Normal form of projective quadrics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadric_surface#Normal_form_of_projective_quadrics), below.

In coordinates _x_ 1, _x_ 2, ..., _x_ _D_+1, the general quadric is thus defined by the [algebraic equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_equation "Algebraic equation")[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadric_surface#cite_note-geom-1)

![Image 1: {\displaystyle \sum _{i,j=1}^{D+1}x_{i}Q_{ij}x_{j}+\sum _{i=1}^{D+1}P_{i}x_{i}+R=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ef3f36aed09d5d9f3eb0b33f1f40f92d7b8be960)
which may be compactly written in vector and matrix notation as:

![Image 2: {\displaystyle xQx^{\mathrm {T} }+Px^{\mathrm {T} }+R=0\,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/97e50091c2b80ed2c217246ca08ee90bf676eb82)
where _x_ = (_x_ 1, _x_ 2, ..., _x_ _D_+1) is a row [vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_(geometry) "Vector (geometry)"), _x_ T is the [transpose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transpose "Transpose") of _x_ (a column vector), _Q_ is a (_D_ + 1) × (_D_ + 1)[matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_(mathematics) "Matrix (mathematics)") and _P_ is a (_D_ + 1)-dimensional row vector and _R_ a scalar constant. The values _Q_, _P_ and _R_ are often taken to be over [real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number") or [complex numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number"), but a quadric may be defined over any [field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)").

As the dimension of a [Euclidean plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_plane "Euclidean plane") is two, quadrics in a Euclidean plane have dimension one and are thus [plane curves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_curve "Plane curve"). They are called _conic sections_, or _conics_.

[![Image 3](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Eccentricity.svg/330px-Eccentricity.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Eccentricity.svg)

Circle (_e_=0), ellipse (_e_=0.5), parabola (_e_=1), and hyperbola (_e_=2) with fixed focus _F_ and directrix.

In three-dimensional [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space"), quadrics have dimension two, and are known as **quadric surfaces**. Their [quadratic equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadratic_equation "Quadratic equation") have the form

![Image 4: {\displaystyle Ax^{2}+By^{2}+Cz^{2}+Dxy+Eyz+Fxz+Gx+Hy+Iz+J=0,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a626ab8c882082de8ed895fa67d1cc9412c24817)
where ![Image 5: {\displaystyle A,B,\ldots ,J}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a60f0b320fea81fb9c280bacb0a97e29dc8f10cf) are real numbers, and at least one of A, B, and C is nonzero.

The quadric surfaces are classified and named by their shape, which corresponds to the [orbits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbit_(group_theory) "Orbit (group theory)") under [affine transformations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_transformation "Affine transformation"). That is, if an affine transformation maps a quadric onto another one, they belong to the same class, and share the same name and many properties.

The [principal axis theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principal_axis_theorem "Principal axis theorem") shows that for any (possibly reducible) quadric, a suitable change of [Cartesian coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinates "Cartesian coordinates") or, equivalently, a [Euclidean transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_transformation "Euclidean transformation") allows putting the equation of the quadric into a unique simple form on which the class of the quadric is immediately visible. This form is called the [normal form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canonical_form "Canonical form") of the equation, since two quadrics have the same normal form [if and only if](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/If_and_only_if "If and only if") there is a Euclidean transformation that maps one quadric to the other. The normal forms are as follows:

![Image 6: {\displaystyle {x^{2} \over a^{2}}+{y^{2} \over b^{2}}+\varepsilon _{1}{z^{2} \over c^{2}}+\varepsilon _{2}=0,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a7abec6c8359e3aa211ed22f701cbae423d2a75b)![Image 7: {\displaystyle {x^{2} \over a^{2}}-{y^{2} \over b^{2}}+\varepsilon _{3}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9d7cabf96759f291f9714a3d76eb7f31c67f515c)![Image 8: {\displaystyle {x^{2} \over a^{2}}+\varepsilon _{4}=0,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6b5500711cf8ff2cfd4ce95e26fc4cdcb0c97c2f)![Image 9: {\displaystyle z={x^{2} \over a^{2}}+\varepsilon _{5}{y^{2} \over b^{2}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af6bdd69316e7f3599acfe8cbe7618a3f2d88207)
where the ![Image 10: {\displaystyle \varepsilon _{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/00e1b6ad3cbad4af49bf21a3ad2dc379ff045079) are either 1, −1 or 0, except ![Image 11: {\displaystyle \varepsilon _{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2953d4625ffb2e717e1ffc32198ed56fa40ea54a) which takes only the value 0 or 1.

Each of these 17 normal forms[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadric_surface#cite_note-ela-2) corresponds to a single orbit under affine transformations. In three cases there are no real points: ![Image 12: {\displaystyle \varepsilon _{1}=\varepsilon _{2}=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dfe376bc3efd9bb443bb497f5c2f4b4615390088) (_imaginary ellipsoid_), ![Image 13: {\displaystyle \varepsilon _{1}=0,\varepsilon _{2}=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9c5c00a51574f1b0dcffe09dedf29b62564ad6ea) (_imaginary elliptic cylinder_), and ![Image 14: {\displaystyle \varepsilon _{4}=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/838628ab85748aaab8ed2a80abc261e2f24d65b6) (pair of [complex conjugate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_conjugate "Complex conjugate") parallel planes, a reducible quadric). In one case, the _imaginary cone_, there is a single point (![Image 15: {\displaystyle \varepsilon _{1}=1,\varepsilon _{2}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/594afc0ae5588708ad23f7a4e6cd6dc39f739040)). If ![Image 16: {\displaystyle \varepsilon _{1}=\varepsilon _{2}=0,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2a53c3292cbd8737d42812beabf0654f77c35560) one has a line (in fact two complex conjugate intersecting planes). For ![Image 17: {\displaystyle \varepsilon _{3}=0,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f9276c8e18ce4aa80a8b87b912016aef9094a121) one has two intersecting planes (reducible quadric). For ![Image 18: {\displaystyle \varepsilon _{4}=0,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7e7d8ce5e341591d29094bea69b9e433826bd315) one has a double plane. For ![Image 19: {\displaystyle \varepsilon _{4}=-1,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cb5489ba808309ac7e26cf5f3d8636504b0729f2) one has two parallel planes (reducible quadric).

Thus, among the 17 normal forms, there are nine true quadrics: a cone, three cylinders (often called degenerate quadrics) and five non-degenerate quadrics ([ellipsoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipsoid "Ellipsoid"), [paraboloids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paraboloid "Paraboloid") and [hyperboloids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperboloid "Hyperboloid")), which are detailed in the following tables. The eight remaining quadrics are the imaginary ellipsoid (no real point), the imaginary cylinder (no real point), the imaginary cone (a single real point), and the reducible quadrics, which are decomposed in two planes; there are five such decomposed quadrics, depending whether the planes are distinct or not, parallel or not, real or complex conjugate.

Definition and basic properties
-------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Quadric&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Definition and basic properties")]

An _affine quadric_ is the set of [zeros](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero_of_a_function "Zero of a function") of a polynomial of degree two. When not specified otherwise, the polynomial is supposed to have [real](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number") coefficients, and the zeros are points in a [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space"). However, most properties remain true when the coefficients belong to any [field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)") and the points belong in an [affine space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_space "Affine space"). As usual in [algebraic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_geometry "Algebraic geometry"), it is often useful to consider points over an [algebraically closed field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraically_closed_field "Algebraically closed field") containing the polynomial coefficients, generally the [complex numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number"), when the coefficients are real.

Many properties becomes easier to state (and to prove) by extending the quadric to the [projective space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_space "Projective space") by [projective completion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_completion "Projective completion"), consisting of adding [points at infinity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Points_at_infinity "Points at infinity"). Technically, if

![Image 53: {\displaystyle p(x_{1},\ldots ,x_{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f7a35f7b13095c259c4a2735680899f941190c4e)
is a polynomial of degree two that defines an affine quadric, then its projective completion is defined by [homogenizing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homogenization_of_a_polynomial "Homogenization of a polynomial")p into

![Image 54: {\displaystyle P(X_{0},\ldots ,X_{n})=X_{0}^{2}\,p\left({\frac {X_{1}}{X_{0}}},\ldots ,{\frac {X_{n}}{X_{0}}}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1fa56d186a3acf4ae301264e318bedc8aac12ac4)
(this is a polynomial, because the degree of p is two). The points of the projective completion are the points of the projective space whose [projective coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_coordinates "Projective coordinates") are zeros of P.

So, a _projective quadric_ is the set of zeros in a projective space of a [homogeneous polynomial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homogeneous_polynomial "Homogeneous polynomial") of degree two.

As the above process of homogenization can be reverted by setting _X_ 0 = 1:

![Image 55: {\displaystyle p(x_{1},\ldots ,x_{n})=P(1,x_{1},\ldots ,x_{n})\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3f533f4803b5d04d1eb35198e5a140a123c92a8)
it is often useful to not distinguish an affine quadric from its projective completion, and to talk of the _affine equation_ or the _projective equation_ of a quadric. However, this is not a perfect equivalence; it is generally the case that ![Image 56: {\displaystyle P(\mathbf {X} )=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/517cb0b4a5d8a4b8dd50d2baed229b2b81c062f4) will include points with ![Image 57: {\displaystyle X_{0}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a341262173c747b2994e894e171b7c9b2a718c53), which are not also solutions of ![Image 58: {\displaystyle p(\mathbf {x} )=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bea1b0b17ecb4ae2948231bbe9acaaffac968397) because these points in projective space correspond to points "at infinity" in affine space.

A quadric in an [affine space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_space "Affine space") of dimension n is the set of zeros of a polynomial of degree 2. That is, it is the set of the points whose coordinates satisfy an equation

![Image 59: {\displaystyle p(x_{1},\ldots ,x_{n})=0,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0002029058375328a08119596ffd6642dc0e3f90)
where the polynomial p has the form

![Image 60: {\displaystyle p(x_{1},\ldots ,x_{n})=\sum _{i=1}^{n}\sum _{j=1}^{n}a_{i,j}x_{i}x_{j}+\sum _{i=1}^{n}(a_{i,0}+a_{0,i})x_{i}+a_{0,0}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/223ce4cd066d4ab8a8f98f2ee60ddc47d7443ee4)
for a matrix ![Image 61: {\displaystyle A=(a_{i,j})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e5aa0abeb651c26c54d0c0bbd78a88202843fd7d) with ![Image 62: {\displaystyle i}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/add78d8608ad86e54951b8c8bd6c8d8416533d20) and ![Image 63: {\displaystyle j}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2f461e54f5c093e92a55547b9764291390f0b5d0) running from 0 to ![Image 64: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b). When the [characteristic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Characteristic_(algebra) "Characteristic (algebra)") of the [field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)") of the coefficients is not two, generally ![Image 65: {\displaystyle a_{i,j}=a_{j,i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b2192031af3e1a9031b874fcb05bfe302a6b12a2) is assumed; equivalently ![Image 66: {\displaystyle A=A^{\mathsf {T}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a40817313212c3351965305c8a294321e97604e0). When the characteristic of the field of the coefficients is two, generally ![Image 67: {\displaystyle a_{i,j}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6f78a20885449906d149153b13189700e8a9c744) is assumed when ![Image 68: {\displaystyle j<i}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/49a18ef9f535c9ff19683360978ab4550c92d4d1); equivalently ![Image 69: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3) is [upper triangular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Upper_triangular "Upper triangular").

The equation may be shortened, as the matrix equation

![Image 70: {\displaystyle \mathbf {x} ^{\mathsf {T}}A\mathbf {x} =0\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8736344d8ff0089af560c39f8784ebbcabd2deab)
with

![Image 71: {\displaystyle \mathbf {x} ={\begin{pmatrix}1&x_{1}&\cdots &x_{n}\end{pmatrix}}^{\mathsf {T}}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3b6829ec6c7619c9c3c0cab2c40994937b17f2c5)
The equation of the projective completion is almost identical:

![Image 72: {\displaystyle \mathbf {X} ^{\mathsf {T}}A\mathbf {X} =0,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0cf0d522626a6563d840b12fc9b2d8cda3b37480)
with

![Image 73: {\displaystyle \mathbf {X} ={\begin{pmatrix}X_{0}&X_{1}&\cdots &X_{n}\end{pmatrix}}^{\mathsf {T}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6261cc54ca6672381778246dae8b70ca0d568d58)
These equations define a quadric as an [algebraic hypersurface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypersurface "Hypersurface") of [dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension "Dimension")_n_ − 1 and degree two in a space of dimension n.

A quadric is said to be **non-degenerate** if the matrix ![Image 74: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3) is [invertible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invertible_matrix "Invertible matrix").

A non-degenerate quadric is non-singular in the sense that its projective completion has no [singular point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Singular_point_of_an_algebraic_variety "Singular point of an algebraic variety") (a cylinder is non-singular in the affine space, but it is a degenerate quadric that has a singular point at infinity).

The singular points of a degenerate quadric are the points whose projective coordinates belong to the [null space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Null_space "Null space") of the matrix A.

A quadric is reducible if and only if the [rank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rank_(linear_algebra) "Rank (linear algebra)") of A is one (case of a double hyperplane) or two (case of two hyperplanes).

Normal form of projective quadrics
----------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Quadric&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Normal form of projective quadrics")]

In [real projective space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_projective_space "Real projective space"), by [Sylvester's law of inertia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sylvester%27s_law_of_inertia "Sylvester's law of inertia"), a non-singular [quadratic form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadratic_form "Quadratic form")_P_(_X_) may be put into the normal form

![Image 75: {\displaystyle P(X)=\pm X_{0}^{2}\pm X_{1}^{2}\pm \cdots \pm X_{D+1}^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/03215ce862b27f9b0703aa826a1f8e25229e59de)
by means of a suitable [projective transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_transformation "Projective transformation") (normal forms for singular quadrics can have zeros as well as ±1 as coefficients). For two-dimensional surfaces (dimension _D_=2) in three-dimensional space, there are exactly three non-degenerate cases:

![Image 76: {\displaystyle P(X)={\begin{cases}X_{0}^{2}+X_{1}^{2}+X_{2}^{2}+X_{3}^{2}\\X_{0}^{2}+X_{1}^{2}+X_{2}^{2}-X_{3}^{2}\\X_{0}^{2}+X_{1}^{2}-X_{2}^{2}-X_{3}^{2}\end{cases}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3a94bd95c9964560742a87e772557a4ec59d198c)
The first case is the [empty set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empty_set "Empty set").

The second case generates the ellipsoid, the elliptic paraboloid or the hyperboloid of two sheets, depending on whether the chosen plane at infinity cuts the quadric in the empty set, in a point, or in a nondegenerate conic respectively. These all have positive [Gaussian curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaussian_curvature "Gaussian curvature").

The third case generates the hyperbolic paraboloid or the hyperboloid of one sheet, depending on whether the plane at infinity cuts it in two lines, or in a nondegenerate conic respectively. These are doubly [ruled surfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ruled_surface "Ruled surface") of negative Gaussian curvature.

The degenerate form

![Image 77: {\displaystyle X_{0}^{2}-X_{1}^{2}-X_{2}^{2}=0.\,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ce265390f3956fee36089055a7e36d48c1a7f193)
generates the elliptic cylinder, the parabolic cylinder, the hyperbolic cylinder, or the cone, depending on whether the plane at infinity cuts it in a point, a line, two lines, or a nondegenerate conic respectively. These are singly ruled surfaces of zero Gaussian curvature.

We see that projective transformations don't mix Gaussian curvatures of different sign. This is true for general surfaces.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadric_surface#cite_note-3)

In [complex projective space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_projective_space "Complex projective space") all of the nondegenerate quadrics become indistinguishable from each other.

Rational parametrization
------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Quadric&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Rational parametrization")]

Given a non-singular point A of a quadric, a line passing through A is either tangent to the quadric, or intersects the quadric in exactly one other point (as usual, a line contained in the quadric is considered as a tangent, since it is contained in the [tangent hyperplane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_space "Tangent space")). This means that the lines passing through A and not tangent to the quadric are in [one to one correspondence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One_to_one_correspondence "One to one correspondence") with the points of the quadric that do not belong to the tangent hyperplane at A. Expressing the points of the quadric in terms of the direction of the corresponding line provides [parametric equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parametric_equation "Parametric equation") of the following forms.

In the case of conic sections (quadric curves), this parametrization establishes a [bijection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bijection "Bijection") between a projective conic section and a [projective line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_line "Projective line"); this bijection is an [isomorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isomorphism "Isomorphism") of [algebraic curves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_curve "Algebraic curve"). In higher dimensions, the parametrization defines a [birational map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Birational_map "Birational map"), which is a bijection between [dense](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dense_set "Dense set")[open](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_set "Open set") subsets of the quadric and a projective space of the same dimension (the topology that is considered is the usual one in the case of a real or complex quadric, or the [Zariski topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zariski_topology "Zariski topology") in all cases). The points of the quadric that are not in the image of this bijection are the points of intersection of the quadric and its tangent hyperplane at A.

In the affine case, the parametrization is a [rational parametrization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_parametrization "Rational parametrization") of the form

![Image 78: {\displaystyle x_{i}={\frac {f_{i}(t_{1},\ldots ,t_{n-1})}{f_{0}(t_{1},\ldots ,t_{n-1})}}\quad {\text{for }}i=1,\ldots ,n,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0ccfe582c1639ad6a61275efea196d1352f444cb)
where ![Image 79: {\displaystyle x_{1},\ldots ,x_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/737e02a5fbf8bc31d443c91025339f9fd1de1065) are the coordinates of a point of the quadric, ![Image 80: {\displaystyle t_{1},\ldots ,t_{n-1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5e630a779335ae235a05bd2da9ca69dd556b1b3f) are parameters, and ![Image 81: {\displaystyle f_{0},f_{1},\ldots ,f_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d89293b0eec1e6c44f0ea47dd5cd3a15122d96cb) are polynomials of degree at most two.

In the projective case, the parametrization has the form

![Image 82: {\displaystyle X_{i}=F_{i}(T_{1},\ldots ,T_{n})\quad {\text{for }}i=0,\ldots ,n,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f7c0e2cdc54ee366bb85575d14c174ca70be305d)
where ![Image 83: {\displaystyle X_{0},\ldots ,X_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3cbae4eeff4d0d41ad6815ec9a6888b473f1535b) are the projective coordinates of a point of the quadric, ![Image 84: {\displaystyle T_{1},\ldots ,T_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3a1155de788385b27629939fbfc2c8a1ae1a5dbe) are parameters, and ![Image 85: {\displaystyle F_{0},\ldots ,F_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/963e886cbea24a3c03678e109313e4beed98fa1d) are homogeneous polynomials of degree two.

One passes from one parametrization to the other by putting ![Image 86: {\displaystyle x_{i}=X_{i}/X_{0},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a197202a252375ba344dd9429e3cec27c08cd624) and ![Image 87: {\displaystyle t_{i}=T_{i}/T_{n}\,:}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/44bbf546be2fa968a4baf114b1f1098b29f325d5)

![Image 88: {\displaystyle F_{i}(T_{1},\ldots ,T_{n})=T_{n}^{2}\,f_{i}\!{\left({\frac {T_{1}}{T_{n}}},\ldots ,{\frac {T_{n-1}}{T_{n}}}\right)}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eed60359204cfd9c693cd7d0f1ec084c287aa47f)
For computing the parametrization and proving that the degrees are as asserted, one may proceed as follows in the affine case. One can proceed similarly in the projective case.

Let q be the quadratic polynomial that defines the quadric, and ![Image 89: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} =(a_{1},\ldots a_{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8078489527ed1119359b1ab78eeacd6cf91317f2) be the [coordinate vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_vector "Coordinate vector") of the given point of the quadric (so, ![Image 90: {\displaystyle q(\mathbf {a} )=0).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7509ae6cb0cc90e11edeb2887cec1ce5d79cb6bb) Let ![Image 91: {\displaystyle \mathbf {x} =(x_{1},\ldots x_{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/62fdc0059f465c22d52715b674adb90c204977da) be the coordinate vector of the point of the quadric to be parametrized, and ![Image 92: {\displaystyle \mathbf {t} =(t_{1},\ldots ,t_{n-1},1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/be120ccc69a2d1e12e13666d0a1348b85d868535) be a vector defining the direction used for the parametrization (directions whose last coordinate is zero are not taken into account here; this means that some points of the affine quadric are not parametrized; one says often that they are parametrized by [points at infinity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Points_at_infinity "Points at infinity") in the space of parameters) . The points of the intersection of the quadric and the line of direction ![Image 93: {\displaystyle \mathbf {t} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/16ff63cc74a931900e79b3caacbae3fa8cc66845) passing through ![Image 94: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1a957216653a9ee0d0133dcefd13fb75e36b8b9d) are the points ![Image 95: {\displaystyle \mathbf {x} =\mathbf {a} +\lambda \mathbf {t} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/78251db46fc98427d99cd7f78baa8f85d5c77f7c) such that

![Image 96: {\displaystyle q(\mathbf {a} +\lambda \mathbf {t} )=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/233efe001363dd94cf78cd4c5d32cef0e50a0f23)
for some value of the scalar ![Image 97: {\displaystyle \lambda .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4bb9c58e3f6b2de892e10ef516f96f07da0423e0) This is an equation of degree two in ![Image 98: {\displaystyle \lambda ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/00aebb041f4a569408e310294efcc29e0eded7dc) except for the values of ![Image 99: {\displaystyle \mathbf {t} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/16ff63cc74a931900e79b3caacbae3fa8cc66845) such that the line is tangent to the quadric (in this case, the degree is one if the line is not included in the quadric, or the equation becomes ![Image 100: {\displaystyle 0=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1b40dbc191d233d6ee82fed12d752b6495dbdecb) otherwise). The coefficients of ![Image 101: {\displaystyle \lambda }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b43d0ea3c9c025af1be9128e62a18fa74bedda2a) and ![Image 102: {\displaystyle \lambda ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/852130fa360f0bd8dda19f023a90f16293611563) are respectively of degree at most one and two in ![Image 103: {\displaystyle \mathbf {t} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5a8968c3a263ba0c901a7755827de9221d3a0412) As the constant coefficient is ![Image 104: {\displaystyle q(\mathbf {a} )=0,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8547b027b2fbf306b69d0f2be890f3ab891b98b8) the equation becomes linear by dividing by ![Image 105: {\displaystyle \lambda ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/00aebb041f4a569408e310294efcc29e0eded7dc) and its unique solution is the quotient of a polynomial of degree at most one by a polynomial of degree at most two. Substituting this solution into the expression of ![Image 106: {\displaystyle \mathbf {x} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5626dc6e8b297f96a453bae14a3d29e4b2979934) one obtains the desired parametrization as fractions of polynomials of degree at most two.

### Example: circle and spheres

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Quadric&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Example: circle and spheres")]

Let consider the quadric of equation

![Image 107: {\displaystyle x_{1}^{2}+x_{2}^{2}+\cdots x_{n}^{2}-1=0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0228fdc412cc8a40f17ee99eb2c91771ef82ca63)
For ![Image 108: {\displaystyle n=2,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4014eb4f6564eccaacc683fc9ff882da482c5268) this is the [unit circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_circle "Unit circle"); for ![Image 109: {\displaystyle n=3}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1c5a5a42ced00df920fad4ab2d4acdb960a4105b) this is the [unit sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_sphere "Unit sphere"); in higher dimensions, this is the [unit hypersphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_hypersphere "Unit hypersphere").

The point ![Image 110: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} =(0,\ldots ,0,-1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/de34f65e2200d4d5a7755433b130b5e25391238d) belongs to the quadric (the choice of this point among other similar points is only a question of convenience). So, the equation ![Image 111: {\displaystyle q(\mathbf {a} +\lambda \mathbf {t} )=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/233efe001363dd94cf78cd4c5d32cef0e50a0f23) of the preceding section becomes

![Image 112: {\displaystyle (\lambda t_{1}^{2})+\cdots +(\lambda t_{n-1})^{2}+(1-\lambda )^{2}-1=0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3d364a86d0ec1bc05ff53841191171599091b478)
By expanding the squares, simplifying the constant terms, dividing by ![Image 113: {\displaystyle \lambda ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/00aebb041f4a569408e310294efcc29e0eded7dc) and solving in ![Image 114: {\displaystyle \lambda ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/00aebb041f4a569408e310294efcc29e0eded7dc) one obtains

![Image 115: {\displaystyle \lambda ={\frac {2}{1+t_{1}^{2}+\cdots +t_{n-1}^{2}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7c2c8b35713534cffb7a7c17d357576d0cd52b22)
Substituting this into ![Image 116: {\displaystyle \mathbf {x} =\mathbf {a} +\lambda \mathbf {t} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/78251db46fc98427d99cd7f78baa8f85d5c77f7c) and simplifying the expression of the last coordinate, one obtains the parametric equation

![Image 117: {\displaystyle {\begin{cases}x_{1}={\frac {2t_{1}}{1+t_{1}^{2}+\cdots +t_{n-1}^{2}}}\\\vdots \\x_{n-1}={\frac {2t_{n-1}}{1+t_{1}^{2}+\cdots +t_{n-1}^{2}}}\\x_{n}={\frac {1-t_{1}^{2}-\cdots -t_{n-1}^{2}}{1+t_{1}^{2}+\cdots +t_{n-1}^{2}}}.\end{cases}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d01b84edaf89e4d2762a85e8c1c5b74cc27846b3)
By homogenizing, one obtains the projective parametrization

![Image 118: {\displaystyle {\begin{cases}X_{0}=T_{1}^{2}+\cdots +T_{n}^{2}\\X_{1}=2T_{1}T_{n}\\\vdots \\X_{n-1}=2T_{n-1}T_{n}\\X_{n}=T_{n}^{2}-T_{1}^{2}-\cdots -T_{n-1}^{2}.\end{cases}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4fff4a26d280fd41c27465c68c568d0f53147c3b)
A straightforward verification shows that this induces a bijection between the points of the quadric such that ![Image 119: {\displaystyle X_{n}\neq -X_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/04632b5e2e3e8999731c51b4762fae40dd206107) and the points such that ![Image 120: {\displaystyle T_{n}\neq 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3edc9e7fefa58f3332f07bbe5d832b9f83c92a46) in the projective space of the parameters. On the other hand, all values of ![Image 121: {\displaystyle (T_{1},\ldots ,T_{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/51b31d0b9514b93764413e9594c63d1c74076d71) such that ![Image 122: {\displaystyle T_{n}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/17e1b58c03a49a265dd6c3758fb30ac8c5277058) and ![Image 123: {\displaystyle T_{1}^{2}+\cdots +T_{n-1}^{2}\neq 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7a423728f84e3cbb1bd33c4c481ea7dc170b4588) give the point ![Image 124: {\displaystyle A.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8a71bf21ad35b8fe05555041d54d1e17eeb0f490)

In the case of conic sections (![Image 125: {\displaystyle n=2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a02c8bd752d2cc859747ca1f3a508281bdbc3b34)), there is exactly one point with ![Image 126: {\displaystyle T_{n}=0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/55734e9b38b5bcda4c71e4c7f994557c091addd8) and one has a bijection between the circle and the projective line.

For ![Image 127: {\displaystyle n>2,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1b21deacd0235492da634f2c63e9e705c4bab7fb) there are many points with ![Image 128: {\displaystyle T_{n}=0,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3c0ec51c50ffe5ece6f211abaf0c7d9ae3914205) and thus many parameter values for the point ![Image 129: {\displaystyle A.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8a71bf21ad35b8fe05555041d54d1e17eeb0f490) On the other hand, the other points of the quadric for which ![Image 130: {\displaystyle X_{n}=-X_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d5725249dc235acc0f490c8bc207df7adc1b2564) (and thus ![Image 131: {\displaystyle x_{n}=-1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a87066607d8f9c960d7b64df41ce7026dde4190c)) cannot be obtained for any value of the parameters. These points are the points of the intersection of the quadric and its tangent plane at ![Image 132: {\displaystyle A.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8a71bf21ad35b8fe05555041d54d1e17eeb0f490) In this specific case, these points have nonreal complex coordinates, but it suffices to change one sign in the equation of the quadric for producing real points that are not obtained with the resulting parametrization.

A quadric is _defined over_ a [field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)")![Image 133: {\displaystyle F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/545fd099af8541605f7ee55f08225526be88ce57) if the coefficients of its equation belong to ![Image 134: {\displaystyle F.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a6b34655c2ef19b56c81af0e6d1f2f6df0d3ed33) When ![Image 135: {\displaystyle F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/545fd099af8541605f7ee55f08225526be88ce57) is the field ![Image 136: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Q} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c5909f0b54e4718fa24d5fd34d54189d24a66e9a) of the [rational numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_number "Rational number"), one can suppose that the coefficients are [integers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer") by [clearing denominators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clearing_denominators "Clearing denominators").

A point of a quadric defined over a field ![Image 137: {\displaystyle F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/545fd099af8541605f7ee55f08225526be88ce57) is said [rational](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_point "Rational point") over ![Image 138: {\displaystyle F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/545fd099af8541605f7ee55f08225526be88ce57) if its coordinates belong to ![Image 139: {\displaystyle F.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a6b34655c2ef19b56c81af0e6d1f2f6df0d3ed33) A rational point over the field ![Image 140: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc) of the real numbers, is called a real point.

A rational point over ![Image 141: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Q} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c5909f0b54e4718fa24d5fd34d54189d24a66e9a) is called simply a _rational point_. By clearing denominators, one can suppose and one supposes generally that the [projective coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_coordinates "Projective coordinates") of a rational point (in a quadric defined over ![Image 142: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Q} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c5909f0b54e4718fa24d5fd34d54189d24a66e9a)) are integers. Also, by clearing denominators of the coefficients, one supposes generally that all the coefficients of the equation of the quadric and the polynomials occurring in the parametrization are integers.

Finding the rational points of a projective quadric amounts thus to solving a [Diophantine equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diophantine_equation "Diophantine equation").

Given a rational point A over a quadric over a field F, the parametrization described in the preceding section provides rational points when the parameters are in F, and, conversely, every rational point of the quadric can be obtained from parameters in F, if the point is not in the tangent hyperplane at A.

It follows that, if a quadric has a rational point, it has many other rational points (infinitely many if F is infinite), and these points can be algorithmically generated as soon one knows one of them.

As said above, in the case of projective quadrics defined over ![Image 143: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Q} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/91185244fbdded6ea99a5e9e6603299128b10928) the parametrization takes the form

![Image 144: {\displaystyle X_{i}=F_{i}(T_{1},\ldots ,T_{n})\quad {\text{for }}i=0,\ldots ,n,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f7c0e2cdc54ee366bb85575d14c174ca70be305d)
where the ![Image 145: {\displaystyle F_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/18f9a5ed62f131c8ad9acdf7f8539f103f1e0ec9) are homogeneous polynomials of degree two with integer coefficients. Because of the homogeneity, one can consider only parameters that are [setwise coprime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Setwise_coprime "Setwise coprime") integers. If ![Image 146: {\displaystyle Q(X_{0},\ldots ,X_{n})=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9572b95f3824d7bf0df21c8dce9c4067a5aadc60) is the equation of the quadric, a solution of this equation is said _primitive_ if its components are setwise coprime integers. The primitive solutions are in one to one correspondence with the rational points of the quadric ([up to](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Up_to "Up to") a change of sign of all components of the solution). The non-primitive integer solutions are obtained by multiplying primitive solutions by arbitrary integers; so they do not deserve a specific study. However, setwise coprime parameters can produce non-primitive solutions, and one may have to divide by a [greatest common divisor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greatest_common_divisor "Greatest common divisor") to arrive at the associated primitive solution.

### Pythagorean triples

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Quadric&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Pythagorean triples")]

This is well illustrated by [Pythagorean triples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagorean_triple "Pythagorean triple"). A Pythagorean triple is a [triple](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triple_(mathematics) "Triple (mathematics)")![Image 147: {\displaystyle (a,b,c)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ae973a762a92b9cd3eafe7f283890ccfa9b887e8) of positive integers such that ![Image 148: {\displaystyle a^{2}+b^{2}=c^{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/90b56b985c78deb115014efe90ce634d73dd51fa) A Pythagorean triple is _primitive_ if ![Image 149: {\displaystyle a,b,c}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f13f068df656c1b1911ae9f81628c49a6181194d) are setwise coprime, or, equivalently, if any of the three pairs ![Image 150: {\displaystyle (a,b),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0066e8fc90702a659ee69bff970050aba59ee02c)![Image 151: {\displaystyle (b,c)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f05051750d2a528c99f12dc06dc047c251e30043) and ![Image 152: {\displaystyle (a,c)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/da1c8a41db4f0379b698642858ee2fa64b42533a) is coprime.

By choosing ![Image 153: {\displaystyle A=(-1,0,1),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/107fc2b1e6acb2fc4915c09dced1080d08d10774) the above method provides the parametrization

![Image 154: {\displaystyle {\begin{cases}a=m^{2}-n^{2}\\b=2mn\\c=m^{2}+n^{2}\end{cases}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6a7d652f403a8f96c1d98ca06e0d2c4db72ba8ee)
for the quadric of equation ![Image 155: {\displaystyle a^{2}+b^{2}-c^{2}=0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/46cc38d8956a2a2ab0624fadf9ad92bed6bfc12d) (The names of variables and parameters are being changed from the above ones to those that are common when considering Pythagorean triples).

If m and n are coprime integers such that ![Image 156: {\displaystyle m>n>0,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5b51616fbead63c0820310534bcde6e75ebbf47f) the resulting triple is a Pythagorean triple. If one of m and n is even and the other is odd, this resulting triple is primitive; otherwise, m and n are both odd, and one obtains a primitive triple by dividing by 2.

In summary, the primitive Pythagorean triples with ![Image 157: {\displaystyle b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f11423fbb2e967f986e36804a8ae4271734917c3) even are obtained as

![Image 158: {\displaystyle a=m^{2}-n^{2},\quad b=2mn,\quad c=m^{2}+n^{2},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b7e4e6a8be65c41bb67d8a1071ea180fc0e6c655)
with m and n coprime integers such that one is even and ![Image 159: {\displaystyle m>n>0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a80d02f2a5ba7e75d5878972baa2e8b4359e73c0) (this is [Euclid's formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid%27s_formula "Euclid's formula")). The primitive Pythagorean triples with ![Image 160: {\displaystyle b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f11423fbb2e967f986e36804a8ae4271734917c3) odd are obtained as

![Image 161: {\displaystyle a={\frac {m^{2}-n^{2}}{2}},\quad b=mn,\quad c={\frac {m^{2}+n^{2}}{2}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2e93f9c06fa9537cfd583c573e78c71d374850cd)
with m and n coprime odd integers such that ![Image 162: {\displaystyle m>n>0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a41214fc160145e84fe6750b3a1de0a84d7024fe)

As the exchange of a and b transforms a Pythagorean triple into another Pythagorean triple, only one of the two cases is sufficient for producing all primitive Pythagorean triples [up to](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Up_to "Up to") the order of a and b.

### Intersection with a line

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Quadric&action=edit&section=16 "Edit section: Intersection with a line")]

For the intersection of an arbitrary line ![Image 232: {\displaystyle g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3556280e66fe2c0d0140df20935a6f057381d77) with a real quadric ![Image 233: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {Q}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/64114d2e2b6847f9e57c33e8f4a5c6d08e40d482), the following cases may occur:

a) ![Image 234: {\displaystyle g\cap {\mathcal {Q}}=\emptyset \;}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/86733152d307d70425032edd409ebbe6d7a04668) and ![Image 235: {\displaystyle g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3556280e66fe2c0d0140df20935a6f057381d77) is called _exterior line_ b) ![Image 236: {\displaystyle g\subset {\mathcal {Q}}\;}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b3a4c97b50e4b9ecd7aceb2ddaf8c82f95e51705) and ![Image 237: {\displaystyle g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3556280e66fe2c0d0140df20935a6f057381d77) is called a _line in the quadric_ c) ![Image 238: {\displaystyle |g\cap {\mathcal {Q}}|=1\;}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/12cefece27a2b5e270d4537d0875ae21468164ad) and ![Image 239: {\displaystyle g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3556280e66fe2c0d0140df20935a6f057381d77) is called _tangent line_ d) ![Image 240: {\displaystyle |g\cap {\mathcal {Q}}|=2\;}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/49de36724d11e7fe8c3f1027b97dacc8a7a2632b) and ![Image 241: {\displaystyle g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3556280e66fe2c0d0140df20935a6f057381d77) is called _secant line_.
**Proof:** Let ![Image 242: {\displaystyle g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3556280e66fe2c0d0140df20935a6f057381d77) be a line, which intersects ![Image 243: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {Q}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/64114d2e2b6847f9e57c33e8f4a5c6d08e40d482) at point ![Image 244: {\displaystyle \;U=\langle {\vec {u}}\rangle \;}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cd4ca073cc0326c997a2cef6684238b0637943de) and ![Image 245: {\displaystyle \;V=\langle {\vec {v}}\rangle \;}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6ac67d2ce8c42b3723a5bf357f80c194681b967b) is a second point on ![Image 246: {\displaystyle g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3556280e66fe2c0d0140df20935a6f057381d77). From ![Image 247: {\displaystyle \;q({\vec {u}})=0\;}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/395c0cbd1fd4b7f3735059feaeaebbd2a5088041) one obtains

 I) In case of ![Image 249: {\displaystyle g\subset U^{\perp }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3de4ee1d7714d8c6783e8f7491c4ae4e3a977fe9) the equation ![Image 250: {\displaystyle f({\vec {u}},{\vec {v}})=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d9fcb448330c1ae13ee0ec8b48144958dc34d7a4) holds and it is ![Image 251: {\displaystyle \;q(x{\vec {u}}+{\vec {v}})=q({\vec {v}})\;}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/874ae2b6da826fc2c413d41957bf23df306d44f8) for any ![Image 252: {\displaystyle x\in K}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f9075ef2df706bf2764f76182a1ddf55ff231a92). Hence either ![Image 253: {\displaystyle \;q(x{\vec {u}}+{\vec {v}})=0\;}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/75916f4552170af9d98030c1628d54cc09ffb832) for _any_![Image 254: {\displaystyle x\in K}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f9075ef2df706bf2764f76182a1ddf55ff231a92) or ![Image 255: {\displaystyle \;q(x{\vec {u}}+{\vec {v}})\neq 0\;}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c7048b285502f58244a8215696fb5f6f4e85fe66) for _any_![Image 256: {\displaystyle x\in K}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f9075ef2df706bf2764f76182a1ddf55ff231a92), which proves b) and b').

 II) In case of ![Image 257: {\displaystyle g\not \subset U^{\perp }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/90354f3e06c8f0ae3c76265aa8a4c347e42a8461) one obtains ![Image 258: {\displaystyle f({\vec {u}},{\vec {v}})\neq 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5d2a4b78ec4c8043f8c0c75171c59d53da18b7f5) and the equation ![Image 259: {\displaystyle \;q(x{\vec {u}}+{\vec {v}})=q({\vec {v}})+xf({\vec {u}},{\vec {v}})=0\;}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b766cf9cfaf72d63f1a317f43122bb133f08947) has exactly one solution ![Image 260: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4). Hence: ![Image 261: {\displaystyle |g\cap {\mathcal {Q}}|=2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6abf31c00d1f7925fe5d9f5980485c992015860f), which proves c).

### _f_-radical, _q_-radical

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Quadric&action=edit&section=17 "Edit section: f-radical, q-radical")]

In the classical cases ![Image 265: {\displaystyle K=\mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a6419d3aa99701ca996737b17a5e1174d53e6c9e) or ![Image 266: {\displaystyle \mathbb {C} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f9add4085095b9b6d28d045fd9c92c2c09f549a7) there exists only one radical, because of ![Image 267: {\displaystyle \operatorname {char} K\neq 2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/df0922306e6d099b5dbf51f3970f95bf35bb8e79) and ![Image 268: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) and ![Image 269: {\displaystyle q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/06809d64fa7c817ffc7e323f85997f783dbdf71d) are closely connected. In case of ![Image 270: {\displaystyle \operatorname {char} K=2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8092c871388215096a9ba1159960975b006df598) the quadric ![Image 271: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {Q}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/64114d2e2b6847f9e57c33e8f4a5c6d08e40d482) is not determined by ![Image 272: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) (see above) and so one has to deal with two radicals:

a) ![Image 273: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {R}}:=\{P\in {\mathcal {P}}\mid P^{\perp }={\mathcal {P}}\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/087f41bb878fd170758d8e9ba8dc270c3b33ac7e) is a projective subspace. ![Image 274: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {R}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/74532dc308c806964b832df0d0d73352195c2f2f) is called **_f_-radical** of quadric ![Image 275: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {Q}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/64114d2e2b6847f9e57c33e8f4a5c6d08e40d482).b) ![Image 276: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {S}}:={\mathcal {R}}\cap {\mathcal {Q}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1ec71329537f043106b575a0ff5eb426430f89be) is called **singular radical** or _![Image 277: {\displaystyle q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest\_v1/media/math/render/svg/06809d64fa7c817ffc7e323f85997f783dbdf71d)-radical_ of ![Image 278: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {Q}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/64114d2e2b6847f9e57c33e8f4a5c6d08e40d482).c) In case of ![Image 279: {\displaystyle \operatorname {char} K\neq 2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/df0922306e6d099b5dbf51f3970f95bf35bb8e79) one has ![Image 280: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {R}}={\mathcal {S}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b15ba2f43e3c740af4997c40d2b8571a87bc8c3f).
A quadric is called **non-degenerate** if ![Image 281: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {S}}=\emptyset }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/78d20694c4143ba097defdf8f2c43ef88d796182).

**Examples in ![Image 282: {\displaystyle P_{2}(K)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4c3599b89232abeb5ee516d0a7733a115a2fd0f)** (see above):

**(E1):** For ![Image 283: {\displaystyle \;q({\vec {x}})=x_{1}x_{2}-x_{3}^{2}\;}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ea7438737386c494132fc5e6d123c548a3e3500c) (conic) the bilinear form is ![Image 284: {\displaystyle f({\vec {x}},{\vec {y}})=x_{1}y_{2}+x_{2}y_{1}-2x_{3}y_{3}\;.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5b4ffc9661c8a9120983bb1fd9d273ec67f0d907)

 In case of ![Image 285: {\displaystyle \operatorname {char} K\neq 2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/df0922306e6d099b5dbf51f3970f95bf35bb8e79) the polar spaces are never ![Image 286: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {P}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/10d6ec962de5797ba4f161c40e66dca74ae95cc6). Hence ![Image 287: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {R}}={\mathcal {S}}=\emptyset }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6d9b557fc8ee1e20726135e9a9c1c709c6b0eb75).

 In case of ![Image 288: {\displaystyle \operatorname {char} K=2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8092c871388215096a9ba1159960975b006df598) the bilinear form is reduced to ![Image 289: {\displaystyle f({\vec {x}},{\vec {y}})=x_{1}y_{2}+x_{2}y_{1}\;}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c9cff5a426cb1dea0250ac2fd047735105e75863) and ![Image 290: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {R}}=\langle (0,0,1)^{\text{T}}\rangle \notin {\mathcal {Q}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3825752df96b8888e66aa25ac361c65c18abbd7). Hence ![Image 291: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {R}}\neq {\mathcal {S}}=\emptyset \;.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f9002c8afc4a19be7d92074cac1c7bb185af3921) In this case the _f_-radical is the common point of all tangents, the so called _knot_.

 In both cases ![Image 292: {\displaystyle S=\emptyset }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6a7c38be46a9b3fe15e5893edc24def5ba893e5e) and the quadric (conic) ist _non-degenerate_.

**(E2):** For ![Image 293: {\displaystyle \;q({\vec {x}})=x_{1}x_{2}\;}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/77181359305beb17fe0fe713e6309ae92ed63a3b) (pair of lines) the bilinear form is ![Image 294: {\displaystyle f({\vec {x}},{\vec {y}})=x_{1}y_{2}+x_{2}y_{1}\;}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c9cff5a426cb1dea0250ac2fd047735105e75863) and ![Image 295: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {R}}=\langle (0,0,1)^{\text{T}}\rangle ={\mathcal {S}}\;,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/62ec5b4c9b25fad04c2d9c882ec8cc79da95e0d1) the intersection point. 

 In this example the quadric is _degenerate_.

A quadric is a rather homogeneous object:

### _q_-subspaces and index of a quadric

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Quadric&action=edit&section=19 "Edit section: q-subspaces and index of a quadric")]

A subspace ![Image 316: {\displaystyle \;{\mathcal {U}}\;}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/173471b1d0322f5c1fabc84aec37428f30f873d4) of ![Image 317: {\displaystyle P_{n}(K)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1349e79d97463253b416afd078fa9eb8646e7b08) is called ![Image 318: {\displaystyle q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/06809d64fa7c817ffc7e323f85997f783dbdf71d)-subspace if ![Image 319: {\displaystyle \;{\mathcal {U}}\subset {\mathcal {Q}}\;}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0c79f7c10124d029dd6d42f9a8330f1487398f7c)

For example: points on a sphere or [lines on a hyperboloid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ruled_surface "Ruled surface") (s. below).

Any two _maximal_![Image 320: {\displaystyle q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/06809d64fa7c817ffc7e323f85997f783dbdf71d)-subspaces have the same dimension ![Image 321: {\displaystyle m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a07d98bb302f3856cbabc47b2b9016692e3f7bc).[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadric_surface#cite_note-5)
Let be ![Image 322: {\displaystyle m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a07d98bb302f3856cbabc47b2b9016692e3f7bc) the dimension of the maximal ![Image 323: {\displaystyle q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/06809d64fa7c817ffc7e323f85997f783dbdf71d)-subspaces of ![Image 324: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {Q}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/64114d2e2b6847f9e57c33e8f4a5c6d08e40d482) then

The integer ![Image 325: {\displaystyle \;i:=m+1\;}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/66108b0598ee02f7784880fe47b41b1bdb818036) is called **index** of ![Image 326: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {Q}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/64114d2e2b6847f9e57c33e8f4a5c6d08e40d482).
**Theorem:[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadric_surface#cite_note-6)**

For the index ![Image 327: {\displaystyle i}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/add78d8608ad86e54951b8c8bd6c8d8416533d20) of a non-degenerate quadric ![Image 328: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {Q}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/64114d2e2b6847f9e57c33e8f4a5c6d08e40d482) in ![Image 329: {\displaystyle P_{n}(K)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1349e79d97463253b416afd078fa9eb8646e7b08) the following is true: ![Image 330: {\displaystyle i\leq {\frac {n+1}{2}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e2fd21b3249f90eddcb2fb584fc736f29b853522).
Let be ![Image 331: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {Q}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/64114d2e2b6847f9e57c33e8f4a5c6d08e40d482) a non-degenerate quadric in ![Image 332: {\displaystyle P_{n}(K),n\geq 2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8d5766e4206ddcb971cd2f37b7650a2f1f851856), and ![Image 333: {\displaystyle i}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/add78d8608ad86e54951b8c8bd6c8d8416533d20) its index.

In case of ![Image 334: {\displaystyle i=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9fb857369fbc28dab4b5d76bb835e6f196b1d1cf) quadric ![Image 335: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {Q}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/64114d2e2b6847f9e57c33e8f4a5c6d08e40d482) is called _sphere_ (or [oval](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oval_(projective_plane) "Oval (projective plane)") conic if ![Image 336: {\displaystyle n=2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a02c8bd752d2cc859747ca1f3a508281bdbc3b34)).In case of ![Image 337: {\displaystyle i=2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fd4e89f84cbdca577f73238abfb2fa18119c6eca) quadric ![Image 338: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {Q}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/64114d2e2b6847f9e57c33e8f4a5c6d08e40d482) is called _hyperboloid_ (of one sheet).
**Examples:**

a) Quadric ![Image 339: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {Q}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/64114d2e2b6847f9e57c33e8f4a5c6d08e40d482) in ![Image 340: {\displaystyle P_{2}(K)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4c3599b89232abeb5ee516d0a7733a115a2fd0f) with form ![Image 341: {\displaystyle \;q({\vec {x}})=x_{1}x_{2}-x_{3}^{2}\;}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ea7438737386c494132fc5e6d123c548a3e3500c) is non-degenerate with index 1.b) If polynomial ![Image 342: {\displaystyle \;p(\xi )=\xi ^{2}+a_{0}\xi +b_{0}\;}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/72bb78e9791212972d5f11790b6f76eef74768c0) is [irreducible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irreducible_polynomial "Irreducible polynomial") over ![Image 343: {\displaystyle K}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b76fce82a62ed5461908f0dc8f037de4e3686b0) the quadratic form ![Image 344: {\displaystyle \;q({\vec {x}})=x_{1}^{2}+a_{0}x_{1}x_{2}+b_{0}x_{2}^{2}-x_{3}x_{4}\;}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/efebd6c6d355a4a6923a930262c9ffc908e74fab) gives rise to a non-degenerate quadric ![Image 345: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {Q}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/64114d2e2b6847f9e57c33e8f4a5c6d08e40d482) in ![Image 346: {\displaystyle P_{3}(K)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6fad7b3b4d0be56fbe718e59d416b2eed9da7bd3) of index 1 (sphere). For example: ![Image 347: {\displaystyle \;p(\xi )=\xi ^{2}+1\;}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e7cd4acea14c447265dbf1b05515684f8197b6f5) is irreducible over ![Image 348: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc) (but not over ![Image 349: {\displaystyle \mathbb {C} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f9add4085095b9b6d28d045fd9c92c2c09f549a7)!).c) In ![Image 350: {\displaystyle P_{3}(K)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6fad7b3b4d0be56fbe718e59d416b2eed9da7bd3) the quadratic form ![Image 351: {\displaystyle \;q({\vec {x}})=x_{1}x_{2}+x_{3}x_{4}\;}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/80527d0a720e41aeb9d2ebb85f4bfbc7df326820) generates a _hyperboloid_.

### Generalization of quadrics: quadratic sets

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Quadric&action=edit&section=20 "Edit section: Generalization of quadrics: quadratic sets")]

It is not reasonable to formally extend the definition of quadrics to spaces over genuine skew fields (division rings). Because one would obtain secants bearing more than 2 points of the quadric which is totally different from _usual_ quadrics.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadric_surface#cite_note-7)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadric_surface#cite_note-8)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadric_surface#cite_note-9) The reason is the following statement.

A [division ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_ring "Division ring")![Image 352: {\displaystyle K}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b76fce82a62ed5461908f0dc8f037de4e3686b0) is [commutative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_ring "Commutative ring") if and only if any [equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadratic_equation "Quadratic equation")![Image 353: {\displaystyle x^{2}+ax+b=0,\ a,b\in K}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9a6d2deb2fbb384e111728b43445fce9e6cdecdf), has at most two solutions.
There are _generalizations_ of quadrics: [quadratic sets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadratic_set "Quadratic set").[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadric_surface#cite_note-10) A quadratic set is a set of points of a projective space with the same geometric properties as a quadric: every line intersects a quadratic set in at most two points or is contained in the set.
