**Angular momentum** (sometimes called **moment of momentum** or **rotational momentum**) is the [rotational](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational "Rotational") analog of [linear momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum "Momentum"). It is an important [physical quantity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_quantity "Physical quantity") because it is a [conserved quantity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservation_law "Conservation law")– the total angular momentum of a [closed system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_system "Closed system") remains constant. Angular momentum has both a [direction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direction_(geometry) "Direction (geometry)") and a magnitude, and both are conserved. [Bicycles and motorcycles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bicycle_and_motorcycle_dynamics "Bicycle and motorcycle dynamics"), [flying discs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flying_disc "Flying disc"),[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#cite_note-1)[rifled bullets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rifling "Rifling"), and [gyroscopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gyroscope "Gyroscope") owe their useful properties to conservation of angular momentum. Conservation of angular momentum is also why [hurricanes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hurricane "Hurricane")[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#cite_note-Tropical_Cyclone_Structure-2) form spirals and [neutron stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron_star "Neutron star") have high rotational rates. In general, conservation limits the possible motion of a system, but it does not uniquely determine it.

The three-dimensional angular momentum for a [point particle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_particle "Point particle") is classically represented as a [pseudovector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudovector "Pseudovector")**r** × **p**, the [cross product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product "Cross product") of the particle's [position vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Position_vector "Position vector")**r** (relative to some origin) and its [momentum vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum_vector "Momentum vector"); the latter is **p** = _m_**v** in [Newtonian mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newtonian_mechanics "Newtonian mechanics"). Unlike linear momentum, angular momentum depends on where this origin is chosen, since the particle's position is measured from it.

Angular momentum is an [extensive quantity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intensive_and_extensive_properties "Intensive and extensive properties"); that is, the total angular momentum of any composite system is the sum of the angular momenta of its constituent parts. For a [continuous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuum_mechanics "Continuum mechanics")[rigid body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rigid_body "Rigid body") or a [fluid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluid "Fluid"), the total angular momentum is the [volume integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume_integral "Volume integral") of angular momentum density (angular momentum per unit volume in the limit as volume shrinks to zero) over the entire body.

Similar to conservation of linear momentum, where it is conserved if there is no external force, angular momentum is conserved if there is no external [torque](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torque "Torque"). Torque can be defined as the rate of change of angular momentum, analogous to [force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Force "Force"). The net _external_ torque on any system is always equal to the _total_ torque on the system; the sum of all internal torques of any system is always 0 (this is the rotational analogue of [Newton's third law of motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_third_law_of_motion "Newton's third law of motion")). Therefore, for a [closed system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_system "Closed system") (where there is no net external torque), the _total_ torque on the system must be 0, which means that the total angular momentum of the system is constant.

The change in angular momentum for a particular interaction is called **angular impulse**, sometimes **twirl**.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#cite_note-3) Angular impulse is the angular analog of (linear) [impulse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Impulse_(physics) "Impulse (physics)").

Definition in classical mechanics
---------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Angular_momentum&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Definition in classical mechanics")]

Just as for [angular velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_velocity "Angular velocity"), there are two special types of angular [momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum "Momentum") of an object: the **spin angular momentum** is the angular momentum about the object's [center of mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Center_of_mass "Center of mass"), while the **orbital angular momentum** is the angular momentum about a chosen center of rotation. The [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth "Earth") has an orbital angular momentum by nature of revolving around the [Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun "Sun"), and a spin angular momentum by nature of its daily rotation around the polar axis. The total angular momentum is the sum of the spin and orbital angular momenta. In the case of the Earth the primary conserved quantity is the total angular momentum of the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System") because angular momentum is exchanged to a small but important extent among the planets and the Sun. The orbital angular momentum vector of a point particle is always parallel and directly proportional to its orbital angular [velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity "Velocity") vector **ω**, where the constant of proportionality depends on both the mass of the particle and its distance from origin. The spin angular momentum vector of a rigid body is proportional but not always parallel to the spin angular velocity vector **Ω**, making the constant of proportionality a second-rank [tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor "Tensor") rather than a scalar.

### Orbital angular momentum in two dimensions

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Angular_momentum&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Orbital angular momentum in two dimensions")]

[![Image 25](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Ang_mom_2d.png/250px-Ang_mom_2d.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Ang_mom_2d.png)

[Velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity "Velocity") of the [particle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle "Particle")_m_ with respect to the origin _O_ can be resolved into components parallel to (_v_∥) and perpendicular to (_v_⊥) the radius vector _r_. The **angular momentum** of _m_ is proportional to the [perpendicular component](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular_component "Perpendicular component")_v_⊥ of the velocity, or equivalently, to the perpendicular distance _r_⊥ from the origin.

Angular momentum is a [vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_vector "Euclidean vector") quantity (more precisely, a [pseudovector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudovector "Pseudovector")) that represents the product of a body's [rotational inertia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_of_inertia "Moment of inertia") and [rotational velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_velocity "Angular velocity") (in radians/sec) about a particular axis. However, if the particle's trajectory lies in a single [plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_(geometry) "Plane (geometry)"), it is sufficient to discard the vector nature of angular momentum, and treat it as a [scalar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_(mathematics) "Scalar (mathematics)") (more precisely, a [pseudoscalar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudoscalar "Pseudoscalar")).[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#cite_note-Wilson-5) Angular momentum can be considered a rotational analog of linear momentum. Thus, where linear momentum p is proportional to [mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass "Mass")m and [linear speed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed "Speed")v,[![Image 26: {\displaystyle p=mv,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eb4b93eb594373b6fa496b126057996eefce3364)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:MathWikibase&qid=Q41273) angular momentum L is proportional to [moment of inertia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_of_inertia "Moment of inertia")I and [angular speed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_frequency "Angular frequency")ω measured in radians per second.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#cite_note-Worthington-6)[![Image 27: {\displaystyle L=I\omega .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d1ce1597dcef54047a562d9108835bcbd8358d32)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:MathWikibase&qid=Q161254)

Unlike mass, which depends only on amount of matter, moment of inertia depends also on the position of the axis of rotation and the distribution of the matter. Unlike linear velocity, which does not depend upon the choice of origin, orbital angular velocity is always measured with respect to a fixed origin. Therefore, strictly speaking, L should be referred to as the angular momentum _relative to that center_.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#cite_note-Taylor90-7)

In the case of circular motion of a single particle, we can use ![Image 28: {\displaystyle I=r^{2}m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2ce8ff961fab0cdfe7ac50e8bee6d6ef1ee62347) and ![Image 29: {\displaystyle \omega ={v}/{r}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2d74ee5180d273d318e51935cdcf89fc155de092) to expand angular momentum as ![Image 30: {\displaystyle L=r^{2}m\cdot {v}/{r},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1a5d9ca58b6c02f3910eb59baaa6467d2b40fe45) reducing to: ![Image 31: {\displaystyle L=rmv,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3ce9be937fd731eccda69830a7cf2128deecb0ac)

the product of the [radius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radius "Radius") of rotation r and the linear momentum of the particle ![Image 32: {\displaystyle p=mv}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2acbe7154884d4dbe30b9a0b399e43cefd8654c7), where ![Image 33: {\displaystyle v=r\omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/23b5b5c066f5be8dee84c4cb33dea2383de8b012) is the [linear (tangential) speed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed#Tangential_speed "Speed").

This simple analysis can also apply to non-circular motion if one uses the component of the motion [perpendicular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular "Perpendicular") to the [radius vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radius_vector "Radius vector"): ![Image 34: {\displaystyle L=rmv_{\perp },}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54cba40e6e82b32083735501d13f41e68cccf06d) where ![Image 35: {\displaystyle v_{\perp }=v\sin(\theta )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/163f5f644793a0f6eb62286bb838848317b0a8cb) is the perpendicular component of the motion. Expanding, ![Image 36: {\displaystyle L=rmv\sin(\theta ),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f093a6fdb253e9d684af3f225239b92697cba364) rearranging, ![Image 37: {\displaystyle L=r\sin(\theta )mv,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e0e238548108c2e9e0c3ea696da8500c9885015c) and reducing, angular momentum can also be expressed, ![Image 38: {\displaystyle L=r_{\perp }mv,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eb8528a226fa2915919bed41269ad720065d1352) where ![Image 39: {\displaystyle r_{\perp }=r\sin(\theta )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9e429dae54c4064967213f2be1cf6170f814e664) is the length of the [_moment arm_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torque#Moment_arm_formula "Torque"), a line dropped perpendicularly from the origin onto the path of the particle. It is this definition, (length of moment arm) × (linear momentum), to which the term _moment of momentum_ refers.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#cite_note-Dadourian-8)

### Orbital angular momentum in three dimensions

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Angular_momentum&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Orbital angular momentum in three dimensions")]

[![Image 48](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/09/Torque_animation.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Torque_animation.gif)

Relationship between [force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Force "Force") (**F**), [torque](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torque "Torque") (**τ**), [momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum "Momentum") (**p**), and angular momentum (**L**) vectors in a rotating system. **r** is the [position vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Position_(vector) "Position (vector)").

To completely define orbital angular momentum in [three dimensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_space "Three-dimensional space"), it is required to know the rate at which the position vector sweeps out an angle, the direction perpendicular to the instantaneous plane of [angular displacement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_displacement "Angular displacement"), and the [mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass "Mass") involved, as well as how this mass is distributed in space.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#cite_note-9) By retaining this [vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_vector "Euclidean vector") nature of angular momentum, the general nature of the equations is also retained, and can describe any sort of three-dimensional [motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Motion_(physics) "Motion (physics)") about the center of rotation – [circular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circular_motion "Circular motion"), [linear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_motion "Linear motion"), or otherwise. In [vector notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_notation "Vector notation"), the orbital angular momentum of a [point particle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_particle "Point particle") in motion about the origin can be expressed as: ![Image 49: {\displaystyle \mathbf {L} =I{\boldsymbol {\omega }},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/afb18c91ec4284e8e4142e3b7fea1b3cb54baf40) where

This can be expanded, reduced, and by the rules of [vector algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_calculus "Vector calculus"), rearranged: ![Image 50: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\mathbf {L} &=\left(r^{2}m\right)\left({\frac {\mathbf {r} \times \mathbf {v} }{r^{2}}}\right)\\&=m\left(\mathbf {r} \times \mathbf {v} \right)\\&=\mathbf {r} \times m\mathbf {v} \\&=\mathbf {r} \times \mathbf {p} ,\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a06879332a77f41eab2433dce133a996b34e0716) which is the [cross product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product "Cross product") of the position vector ![Image 51: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eca0f46511c4c986c48b254073732c0bd98ae0c1) and the linear momentum ![Image 52: {\displaystyle \mathbf {p} =m\mathbf {v} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a271a96e7b925fd39686375167c76d406e87c813) of the particle. By the definition of the cross product, the ![Image 53: {\displaystyle \mathbf {L} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6f5f750865376a1a4ae2b15a00b4ff9c75a66630) vector is [perpendicular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular "Perpendicular") to both ![Image 54: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eca0f46511c4c986c48b254073732c0bd98ae0c1) and ![Image 55: {\displaystyle \mathbf {p} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dd73e3862cb92b016721b8c492eadb4e8a577527). It is directed perpendicular to the plane of angular displacement, as indicated by the [right-hand rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right-hand_rule "Right-hand rule") – so that the angular velocity is seen as [counter-clockwise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clockwise "Clockwise") from the head of the vector. Conversely, the ![Image 56: {\displaystyle \mathbf {L} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6f5f750865376a1a4ae2b15a00b4ff9c75a66630) vector defines the [plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_(geometry) "Plane (geometry)") in which ![Image 57: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eca0f46511c4c986c48b254073732c0bd98ae0c1) and ![Image 58: {\displaystyle \mathbf {p} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dd73e3862cb92b016721b8c492eadb4e8a577527) lie.

By defining a [unit vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_vector "Unit vector")![Image 59: {\displaystyle \mathbf {\hat {u}} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1adccb18b3b18c193af9f9ca2b0c0c500103e3ea) perpendicular to the plane of angular displacement, a [scalar angular speed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_frequency "Angular frequency")![Image 60: {\displaystyle \omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/48eff443f9de7a985bb94ca3bde20813ea737be8) results, where ![Image 61: {\displaystyle \omega \mathbf {\hat {u}} ={\boldsymbol {\omega }},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dc410d6366c23541fe0100e618a54d8f293a6b55) and ![Image 62: {\displaystyle \omega ={\frac {v_{\perp }}{r}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/95c29a5398be29c77b66df211e844987301487fe) where ![Image 63: {\displaystyle v_{\perp }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f80a8cf80254aa3ef2640555e94986487d5cba0b) is the perpendicular component of the motion, as above.

The two-dimensional scalar equations of the previous section can thus be given direction: ![Image 64: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\mathbf {L} &=I{\boldsymbol {\omega }}\\&=I\omega \mathbf {\hat {u}} \\&=\left(r^{2}m\right)\omega \mathbf {\hat {u}} \\&=rmv_{\perp }\mathbf {\hat {u}} \\&=r_{\perp }mv\mathbf {\hat {u}} ,\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/138200e265424b4778f846322c7d77ae28571d5e) and ![Image 65: {\displaystyle \mathbf {L} =rmv\mathbf {\hat {u}} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a761fad402cde98517baffffd37adae166f765d) for circular motion, where all of the motion is perpendicular to the radius ![Image 66: {\displaystyle r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d1ecb613aa2984f0576f70f86650b7c2a132538).

In the [spherical coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_coordinate_system "Spherical coordinate system") the angular momentum vector expresses as

Analogy to linear momentum
--------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Angular_momentum&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Analogy to linear momentum")]

Angular momentum can be described as the rotational analog of [linear momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_momentum "Linear momentum"). Like linear momentum it involves elements of [mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass "Mass") and [displacement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Displacement_(vector) "Displacement (vector)"). Unlike linear momentum it also involves elements of [position](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Position_(vector) "Position (vector)") and [shape](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shape "Shape").

Many problems in physics involve matter in motion about some certain point in space, be it in actual rotation about it, or simply moving past it, where it is desired to know what effect the moving matter has on the point—can it exert energy upon it or perform work about it? [Energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy "Energy"), the ability to do [work](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Work_(physics) "Work (physics)"), can be stored in matter by setting it in motion—a combination of its [inertia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inertia "Inertia") and its displacement. Inertia is measured by its [mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass "Mass"), and displacement by its [velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity "Velocity"). Their product, ![Image 68: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}({\text{amount of inertia}})\times ({\text{amount of displacement}})&={\text{amount of (inertia⋅displacement)}}\\{\text{mass}}\times {\text{velocity}}&={\text{momentum}}\\m\times v&=p\\\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7b0cb2452f95a0f974572985f205329a1d55bc71) is the matter's [momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum "Momentum").[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#cite_note-10) Referring this momentum to a central point introduces a complication: the momentum is not applied to the point directly. For instance, a particle of matter at the outer edge of a wheel is, in effect, at the end of a [lever](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lever "Lever") of the same length as the wheel's radius, its momentum turning the lever about the center point. This imaginary lever is known as the _moment arm_. It has the effect of multiplying the momentum's effort in proportion to its length, an effect known as a [_moment_.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_(physics) "Moment (physics)") Hence, the particle's momentum referred to a particular point, ![Image 69: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}({\text{moment arm}})\times ({\text{amount of inertia}})\times ({\text{amount of displacement}})&={\text{moment of (inertia⋅displacement)}}\\{\text{length}}\times {\text{mass}}\times {\text{velocity}}&={\text{moment of momentum}}\\r\times m\times v&=L\\\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4002dc08fbcabcd33066cf654e7fd49e1adc402e) is the _angular momentum_, sometimes called, as here, the _moment of momentum_ of the particle versus that particular center point. The equation ![Image 70: {\displaystyle L=rmv}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cae824c40f5d6282d0a4d286c1bdb7a7382c925e) combines a moment (a mass ![Image 71: {\displaystyle m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a07d98bb302f3856cbabc47b2b9016692e3f7bc) turning moment arm ![Image 72: {\displaystyle r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d1ecb613aa2984f0576f70f86650b7c2a132538)) with a linear (straight-line equivalent) speed ![Image 73: {\displaystyle v}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e07b00e7fc0847fbd16391c778d65bc25c452597). Linear speed referred to the central point is simply the product of the distance ![Image 74: {\displaystyle r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d1ecb613aa2984f0576f70f86650b7c2a132538) and the angular speed ![Image 75: {\displaystyle \omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/48eff443f9de7a985bb94ca3bde20813ea737be8) versus the point: ![Image 76: {\displaystyle v=r\omega ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/574eabd58d8470d1f1eded75c6b545fb35f48d5c) another moment. Hence, angular momentum contains a double moment: ![Image 77: {\displaystyle L=rmr\omega .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e34e11145331181be5f931f586322cd5b0a81aee) Simplifying slightly, ![Image 78: {\displaystyle L=r^{2}m\omega ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d901cacb8e9e59ef9ac4d17913b6d7922c47808f) the quantity ![Image 79: {\displaystyle r^{2}m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1bbbb15eaf46c7e295eefc1ceb32d90161f7bac4) is the particle's [moment of inertia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_of_inertia "Moment of inertia"), sometimes called the second moment of mass. It is a measure of rotational inertia.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#cite_note-11)

[![Image 80](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Moment_of_inertia_examples.gif/250px-Moment_of_inertia_examples.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Moment_of_inertia_examples.gif)

[Moment of inertia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_of_inertia "Moment of inertia") (shown here), and therefore angular momentum, is different for each shown configuration of [mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass "Mass") and [axis of rotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation_around_a_fixed_axis "Rotation around a fixed axis").

The above analogy of the translational momentum and rotational momentum can be expressed in vector form:[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

*   ![Image 81: {\textstyle \mathbf {p} =m\mathbf {v} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fc2e572b5c0a5b7cd86beb2652f17d86bfb79a7d) for linear motion
*   ![Image 82: {\displaystyle \mathbf {L} =I{\boldsymbol {\omega }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/794f5746b0089a4b7de0494195195a6291eda0aa) for rotation

The direction of momentum is related to the direction of the velocity for linear movement. The direction of angular momentum is related to the angular velocity of the rotation.

Because [moment of inertia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_of_inertia "Moment of inertia") is a crucial part of the spin angular momentum, the latter necessarily includes all of the complications of the former, which is calculated by multiplying elementary bits of the mass by the [squares](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_(algebra) "Square (algebra)") of their [distances](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distances "Distances") from the center of rotation.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#cite_note-Oberg-12) Therefore, the total moment of inertia, and the angular momentum, is a complex function of the configuration of the [matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matter "Matter") about the center of rotation and the orientation of the rotation for the various bits.

For a [rigid body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rigid_body "Rigid body"), for instance a wheel or an asteroid, the orientation of rotation is simply the position of the [rotation axis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation_around_a_fixed_axis "Rotation around a fixed axis") versus the matter of the body. It may or may not pass through the [center of mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Center_of_mass "Center of mass"), or it may lie completely outside of the body. For the same body, angular momentum may take a different value for every possible axis about which rotation may take place.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#cite_note-13) It reaches a minimum when the axis passes through the center of mass.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#cite_note-14)

For a collection of objects revolving about a center, for instance all of the bodies of the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System"), the orientations may be somewhat organized, as is the Solar System, with most of the bodies' axes lying close to the system's axis. Their orientations may also be completely random.

In brief, the more mass and the farther it is from the center of rotation (the longer the [moment arm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torque#Moment_arm_formula "Torque")), the greater the moment of inertia, and therefore the greater the angular momentum for a given [angular velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_velocity "Angular velocity"). In many cases the [moment of inertia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_of_inertia "Moment of inertia"), and hence the angular momentum, can be simplified by,[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#cite_note-15)[![Image 83: {\displaystyle I=k^{2}m,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9262e5be6a71693fea700ba53795be70fde6291c)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:MathWikibase&qid=Q165618)where ![Image 84: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40) is the [radius of gyration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radius_of_gyration "Radius of gyration"), the distance from the axis at which the entire mass ![Image 85: {\displaystyle m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a07d98bb302f3856cbabc47b2b9016692e3f7bc) may be considered as concentrated.

Similarly, for a [point mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_particle "Point particle")![Image 86: {\displaystyle m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a07d98bb302f3856cbabc47b2b9016692e3f7bc) the [moment of inertia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_of_inertia "Moment of inertia") is defined as, ![Image 87: {\displaystyle I=r^{2}m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2ce8ff961fab0cdfe7ac50e8bee6d6ef1ee62347)where ![Image 88: {\displaystyle r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d1ecb613aa2984f0576f70f86650b7c2a132538) is the [radius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radius "Radius") of the point mass from the center of rotation, and for any collection of particles ![Image 89: {\displaystyle m_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/95ec8e804f69706d3f5ad235f4f983220c8df7c2) as the sum, ![Image 90: {\displaystyle \sum _{i}I_{i}=\sum _{i}r_{i}^{2}m_{i}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0f3dfef75a36a8270f6aca1157b1721c696550b0)

Angular momentum's dependence on position and shape is reflected in its [units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Units_of_measurement "Units of measurement") versus linear momentum: kg⋅m 2/s or N⋅m⋅s for angular momentum versus [kg⋅m/s](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SI_derived_unit "SI derived unit") or [N⋅s](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SI_derived_unit "SI derived unit") for linear momentum. When calculating angular momentum as the product of the moment of inertia times the angular velocity, the angular velocity must be expressed in radians per second, where the radian assumes the dimensionless value of unity. (When performing dimensional analysis, it may be productive to use [orientational analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimensional_analysis#Siano's_extension:_orientational_analysis "Dimensional analysis") which treats radians as a base unit, but this is not done in the [International system of units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_system_of_units "International system of units")). The units if angular momentum can be interpreted as [torque](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torque "Torque")⋅time. An object with angular momentum of _L_ N⋅m⋅s can be reduced to zero angular velocity by an angular [impulse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Impulse_(physics) "Impulse (physics)") of _L_ N⋅m⋅s.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#cite_note-16)[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#cite_note-17)

The [plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_(geometry) "Plane (geometry)")[perpendicular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular "Perpendicular") to the axis of angular momentum and passing through the center of mass[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#cite_note-18) is sometimes called the _invariable plane_, because the direction of the axis remains fixed if only the interactions of the bodies within the system, free from outside influences, are considered.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#cite_note-Rankine-19) One such plane is the [invariable plane of the Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invariable_plane "Invariable plane").

### Angular momentum and torque

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Angular_momentum&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Angular momentum and torque")]

[Newton's second law of motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_laws_of_motion#Newton's_second_law "Newton's laws of motion") can be expressed mathematically, ![Image 91: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} =m\mathbf {a} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/93aa54e6c7e8df66d85d06b6eb0b0a2d3ec4ce20) or [force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Force "Force") = [mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass "Mass") × [acceleration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acceleration "Acceleration"). The rotational equivalent for point particles may be derived as follows: ![Image 92: {\displaystyle \mathbf {L} =I{\boldsymbol {\omega }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/794f5746b0089a4b7de0494195195a6291eda0aa) which means that the torque (i.e. the time [derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative "Derivative") of the angular momentum) is [![Image 93: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {\tau }}={\frac {dI}{dt}}{\boldsymbol {\omega }}+I{\frac {d{\boldsymbol {\omega }}}{dt}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d2c66790aae79872ce38c8f57bb7666a5e5bf05b)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:MathWikibase&qid=Q48103)

Because the moment of inertia is ![Image 94: {\displaystyle mr^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ddd9d0ea2911509b014b72a7b536acb7376cb455), it follows that ![Image 95: {\displaystyle {\frac {dI}{dt}}=2mr{\frac {dr}{dt}}=2rp_{||}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7e7393f1bd48479c3e727ed9bd2a44ba8959f28e), and ![Image 96: {\displaystyle {\frac {d\mathbf {L} }{dt}}=I{\frac {d{\boldsymbol {\omega }}}{dt}}+2rp_{||}{\boldsymbol {\omega }},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3fbdc5103e70d211066c7dc4abe45e04df273953) which, reduces to ![Image 97: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {\tau }}=I{\boldsymbol {\alpha }}+2rp_{||}{\boldsymbol {\omega }}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7bf36dafd07a96cefd7b472c30ea9b6e1dd4d3bf) This is the rotational analog of Newton's second law. Note that the torque is not necessarily proportional or parallel to the [angular acceleration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_acceleration "Angular acceleration") (as one might expect). The reason for this is that the moment of inertia of a particle can change with time, something that cannot occur for ordinary mass.

Conservation of angular momentum
--------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Angular_momentum&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Conservation of angular momentum")]

[![Image 98](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Cup_of_Russia_2010_-_Yuko_Kawaguti_%282%29.jpg/250px-Cup_of_Russia_2010_-_Yuko_Kawaguti_%282%29.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Cup_of_Russia_2010_-_Yuko_Kawaguti_(2).jpg)

A [figure skater](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Figure_skating "Figure skating") in a spin uses conservation of angular momentum – decreasing her [moment of inertia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_of_inertia "Moment of inertia") by drawing in her arms and legs increases her [rotational speed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_velocity "Angular velocity").

### General considerations

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Angular_momentum&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: General considerations")]

A rotational analog of [Newton's third law of motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_laws_of_motion#Newton's_third_law "Newton's laws of motion") might be written, "In a [closed system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_system "Closed system"), no torque can be exerted on any matter without the exertion on some other matter of an equal and opposite torque about the same axis."[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#cite_note-Crew-20) Hence, _angular momentum can be exchanged between objects in a closed system, but total angular momentum before and after an exchange remains constant (is conserved)_.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#cite_note-21)

Seen another way, a rotational analogue of [Newton's first law of motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_laws_of_motion#Newton's_first_law "Newton's laws of motion") might be written, "A rigid body continues in a state of uniform rotation unless acted upon by an external influence."[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#cite_note-Crew-20) Thus _with no external influence to act upon it, the original angular momentum of the system remains constant_.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#cite_note-22)

The conservation of angular momentum is used in analyzing [_central force motion_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_central-force_problem "Classical central-force problem"). If the net force on some body is directed always toward some point, the _center_, then there is no torque on the body with respect to the center, as all of the force is directed along the [radius vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Position_(vector) "Position (vector)"), and none is [perpendicular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular "Perpendicular") to the radius. Mathematically, torque ![Image 99: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {\tau }}=\mathbf {r} \times \mathbf {F} =\mathbf {0} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bce23213f7078a404609088e6b314db820290527) because in this case ![Image 100: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eca0f46511c4c986c48b254073732c0bd98ae0c1) and ![Image 101: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/da18bef8c979f3548bb0d8976f5844012d7b8256) are parallel vectors. Therefore, the angular momentum of the body about the center is constant. This is the case with [gravitational attraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity "Gravity") in the [orbits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbit "Orbit") of [planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planet "Planet") and [satellites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satellite "Satellite"), where the gravitational force is always directed toward the primary body and orbiting bodies conserve angular momentum by exchanging distance and velocity as they move about the primary. Central force motion is also used in the analysis of the [Bohr model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bohr_model "Bohr model") of the [atom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atom "Atom").

For a planet, angular momentum is distributed between the spin of the planet and its revolution in its orbit, and these are often exchanged by various mechanisms. The conservation of angular momentum in the [Earth–Moon system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunar_theory "Lunar theory") results in the transfer of angular momentum from Earth to Moon, due to [tidal torque](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tidal_acceleration "Tidal acceleration") the Moon exerts on the Earth. This in turn results in the slowing down of the rotation rate of Earth, at about 65.7 nanoseconds per day,[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#cite_note-23) and in gradual increase of the radius of Moon's orbit, at about 3.82 centimeters per year.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#cite_note-24)

[![Image 102](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/PrecessionOfATop.svg/250px-PrecessionOfATop.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:PrecessionOfATop.svg)

The [torque](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torque "Torque") caused by the two opposing forces **F**g and −**F**g causes a change in the angular momentum **L** in the direction of that torque (since torque is the time derivative of angular momentum). This causes the [top](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spinning_top "Spinning top") to [precess](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Precess "Precess").

The conservation of angular momentum explains the angular acceleration of an [ice skater](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ice_skating "Ice skating") as they bring their arms and legs close to the vertical axis of rotation. By bringing part of the mass of their body closer to the axis, they decrease their body's moment of inertia. Because angular momentum is the product of [moment of inertia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_of_inertia "Moment of inertia") and [angular velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_velocity "Angular velocity"), if the angular momentum remains constant (is conserved), then the angular velocity (rotational speed) of the skater must increase.

The same phenomenon results in extremely fast spin of compact stars (like [white dwarfs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_dwarf "White dwarf"), [neutron stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron_star "Neutron star") and [black holes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_hole "Black hole")) when they are formed out of much larger and slower rotating stars.

Conservation is not always a full explanation for the dynamics of a system but is a key constraint. For example, a [spinning top](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spinning_top "Spinning top") is subject to gravitational torque making it lean over and change the angular momentum about the [nutation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nutation "Nutation") axis, but neglecting friction at the point of spinning contact, it has a conserved angular momentum about its spinning axis, and another about its [precession](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Precession "Precession") axis. Also, in any [planetary system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_system "Planetary system"), the planets, star(s), comets, and asteroids can all move in numerous complicated ways, but only so that the angular momentum of the system is conserved.

[Noether's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noether%27s_theorem "Noether's theorem") states that every [conservation law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservation_law "Conservation law") is associated with a [symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry "Symmetry") (invariant) of the underlying physics. The symmetry associated with conservation of angular momentum is [rotational invariance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_invariance "Rotational invariance"). The fact that the physics of a system is unchanged if it is rotated by any angle about an axis implies that angular momentum is conserved.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#cite_note-25)

### Relation to Newton's second law of motion

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Angular_momentum&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Relation to Newton's second law of motion")]

While angular momentum total conservation can be understood separately from [Newton's laws of motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_laws_of_motion "Newton's laws of motion") as stemming from [Noether's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noether%27s_theorem "Noether's theorem") in systems symmetric under rotations, it can also be understood simply as an efficient method of calculation of results that can also be otherwise arrived at directly from Newton's second law, together with laws governing the forces of nature (such as Newton's third law, [Maxwell's equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxwell%27s_equations "Maxwell's equations") and [Lorentz force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentz_force "Lorentz force")). Indeed, given initial conditions of position and velocity for every point, and the forces at such a condition, one may use Newton's second law to calculate the second derivative of position, and solving for this gives full information on the development of the physical system with time.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#cite_note-26) Note, however, that this is no longer true in [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics"), due to the existence of [particle spin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_(physics) "Spin (physics)"), which is an angular momentum that cannot be described by the cumulative effect of point-like motions in space.

As an example, consider decreasing of the [moment of inertia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_of_inertia "Moment of inertia"), e.g. when a [figure skater](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Figure_skating "Figure skating") is pulling in their hands, speeding up the circular motion. In terms of angular momentum conservation, we have, for angular momentum _L_, moment of inertia _I_ and angular velocity _ω_: ![Image 103: {\displaystyle 0=dL=d(I\cdot \omega )=dI\cdot \omega +I\cdot d\omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d15093607ae440e94bbf63e759eb62b687ab1110)

Using this, we see that the change requires an energy of: ![Image 104: {\displaystyle dE=d\left({\tfrac {1}{2}}I\cdot \omega ^{2}\right)={\tfrac {1}{2}}dI\cdot \omega ^{2}+I\cdot \omega \cdot d\omega =-{\tfrac {1}{2}}dI\cdot \omega ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/10b138f0d46df7e9572073f9ffbaf30371999a41) so that a decrease in the moment of inertia requires investing energy.

This can be compared to the work done as calculated using Newton's laws. Each point in the rotating body is accelerating, at each point of time, with radial acceleration of: ![Image 105: {\displaystyle -r\cdot \omega ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/988f476c4a01afb3d9d6b6d6aba262632eeebea9)

Let us observe a point of mass _m_, whose position vector relative to the center of motion is perpendicular to the z-axis at a given point of time, and is at a distance _z_. The [centripetal force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_force "Centripetal force") on this point, keeping the circular motion, is: ![Image 106: {\displaystyle -m\cdot z\cdot \omega ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e5b5d5e9aee971b197b897a9c6f8c1e41968260d)

Thus the work required for moving this point to a distance _dz_ farther from the center of motion is: ![Image 107: {\displaystyle dW=-m\cdot z\cdot \omega ^{2}\cdot dz=-m\cdot \omega ^{2}\cdot d\left({\tfrac {1}{2}}z^{2}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e4cb725b4a5fa01176b14940347a963885953f73)

For a non-pointlike body one must integrate over this, with _m_ replaced by the mass density per unit _z_. This gives: ![Image 108: {\displaystyle dW=-{\tfrac {1}{2}}dI\cdot \omega ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/56874f3287508ee23fb1f2afb9d94eef5c4b2eea) which is exactly the energy required for keeping the angular momentum conserved.

Note, that the above calculation can also be performed per mass, using [kinematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematics "Kinematics") only. Thus the phenomena of figure skater accelerating tangential velocity while pulling their hands in, can be understood as follows in layman's language: The skater's palms are not moving in a straight line, so they are constantly accelerating inwards, but do not gain additional speed because the accelerating is always done when their motion inwards is zero. However, this is different when pulling the palms closer to the body: The acceleration due to rotation now increases the speed; but because of the rotation, the increase in speed does not translate to a significant speed inwards, but to an increase of the rotation speed.

### Stationary-action principle

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Angular_momentum&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Stationary-action principle")]

In classical mechanics it can be shown that the rotational invariance of action functionals implies conservation of angular momentum. The action is defined in classical physics as a functional of positions, ![Image 109: {\displaystyle x_{i}(t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/644229f2cfb7cba7444d056ec5176b0d1ab7fc46) often represented by the use of square brackets, and the final and initial times. It assumes the following form in cartesian coordinates:![Image 110: {\displaystyle S\left([x_{i}];t_{1},t_{2}\right)\equiv \int _{t_{1}}^{t_{2}}dt\left({\frac {1}{2}}m{\frac {dx_{i}}{dt}}\ {\frac {dx_{i}}{dt}}-V(x_{i})\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a1a0a77886ae741be2679b9b7123e90de2e3ae9)where the repeated indices indicate summation over the index. If the action is invariant of an infinitesimal transformation, it can be mathematically stated as: ![Image 111: {\textstyle \delta S=S\left([x_{i}+\delta x_{i}];t_{1},t_{2}\right)-S\left([x_{i}];t_{1},t_{2}\right)=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/560e494d7450ac05174984f59efb1377447616e5).

Under the transformation, ![Image 112: {\displaystyle x_{i}\rightarrow x_{i}+\delta x_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/10388124f5221ee2ff96225668b932b77a5f13fa), the action becomes: ![Image 113: {\displaystyle S\left([x_{i}+\delta x_{i}];t_{1},t_{2}\right)=\!\int _{t_{1}}^{t_{2}}dt\left({\frac {1}{2}}m{\frac {d(x_{i}+\delta x_{i})}{dt}}{\frac {d(x_{i}+\delta x_{i})}{dt}}-V(x_{i}+\delta x_{i})\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b6cea9bc1b2c5c3fe5c29911ab7cedd3b088354b) where we can employ the expansion of the terms up-to first order in ![Image 114: {\textstyle \delta x_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5b0c1719d121852a8fd2910c6849d3c6c4339801): ![Image 115: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}{\frac {d(x_{i}+\delta x_{i})}{dt}}{\frac {d(x_{i}+\delta x_{i})}{dt}}&\simeq {\frac {dx_{i}}{dt}}{\frac {dx_{i}}{dt}}-2{\frac {d^{2}x_{i}}{dt^{2}}}\delta x_{i}+2{\frac {d}{dt}}\left(\delta x_{i}{\frac {dx_{i}}{dt}}\right)\\V(x_{i}+\delta x_{i})&\simeq V(x_{i})+\delta x_{i}{\frac {\partial V}{\partial x_{i}}}\\\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/07663115c3170bd454b07a6aa838567be76673ff)giving the following change in action: ![Image 116: {\displaystyle S[x_{i}+\delta x_{i}]\simeq S[x_{i}]+\int _{t_{1}}^{t_{2}}dt\,\delta x_{i}\left(-{\frac {\partial V}{\partial x_{i}}}-m{\frac {d^{2}x_{i}}{dt^{2}}}\right)+m\int _{t_{1}}^{t_{2}}dt{\frac {d}{dt}}\left(\delta x_{i}{\frac {dx_{i}}{dt}}\right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3d0a75604f66354be4b20123019ee46516b215e)

Since all rotations can be expressed as [matrix exponential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3D_rotation_group#Exponential_map "3D rotation group") of skew-symmetric matrices, i.e. as ![Image 117: {\displaystyle R({\hat {n}},\theta )=e^{M\theta }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2603ebefa9ea9e46f70503e6d179f5fc0902ccb7) where ![Image 118: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) is a skew-symmetric matrix and ![Image 119: {\displaystyle \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e5ab2664b422d53eb0c7df3b87e1360d75ad9af) is angle of rotation, we can express the change of coordinates due to the rotation ![Image 120: {\displaystyle R({\hat {n}},\delta \theta )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bca338321baae64860660f620bb99d48e3678310), up-to first order of infinitesimal angle of rotation, ![Image 121: {\displaystyle \delta \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2bab72879c043fc87fd27b8b7098be098849695a) as: ![Image 122: {\displaystyle \delta x_{i}=M_{ij}x_{j}\delta \theta .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dedeaa16d342d961468d421d2d38b4e7b4277b34)

Combining the equation of motion and **rotational invariance of action**, we get from the above equations that:![Image 123: {\displaystyle 0=\delta S=\int _{t_{1}}^{t_{2}}dt{\frac {d}{dt}}\left(m{\frac {dx_{i}}{dt}}\delta x_{i}\right)=M_{ij}\,\delta \theta \,m\,x_{j}{\frac {dx_{i}}{dt}}{\Bigg \vert }_{t_{1}}^{t_{2}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/90376b22e55f2e7f198cea413901d1baac1faab3)Since this is true for any matrix ![Image 124: {\displaystyle M_{ij}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6136d919b3f10cc58cc023e05a5c36b2f44e111f) that satisfies ![Image 125: {\displaystyle M_{ij}=-M_{ji},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f3aaca2ed0e81e7e02541decbecba9a17c70cadc) it results in the conservation of the following quantity: ![Image 126: {\displaystyle \ell _{ij}(t):=m\left(x_{i}{\frac {dx_{j}}{dt}}-x_{j}{\frac {dx_{i}}{dt}}\right),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/df700b150e2984a7eb4b75dac1e4a9f19497df1b) as ![Image 127: {\displaystyle \ell _{ij}(t_{1})=\ell _{ij}(t_{2})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a49159037ee5d793aaff4612513371486a410b7e). This corresponds to the conservation of angular momentum throughout the motion.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#cite_note-27)

### Lagrangian formalism

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Angular_momentum&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Lagrangian formalism")]

In [Lagrangian mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrangian_mechanics "Lagrangian mechanics"), angular momentum for rotation around a given axis, is the [conjugate momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conjugate_momentum "Conjugate momentum") of the [generalized coordinate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generalized_coordinate "Generalized coordinate") of the angle around the same axis. For example, ![Image 128: {\displaystyle L_{z}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/77a5b940110c5e1fe03782a31c5e700939ae20e6), the angular momentum around the z axis, is: ![Image 129: {\displaystyle L_{z}={\frac {\partial {\cal {L}}}{\partial {\dot {\theta }}_{z}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/46ec2a2d5043dc0be22545107c74c2a5d9e703a3) where ![Image 130: {\displaystyle {\cal {L}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cddbb21ad79aa4e70f27927e433fd985873a3b6a) is the Lagrangian and ![Image 131: {\displaystyle \theta _{z}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/043cbe96ac7ea3ea664c287abee4ea1819373353) is the angle around the z axis.

Note that ![Image 132: {\displaystyle {\dot {\theta }}_{z}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ebb1c138bd0e051666aae97e19177d0f45976d30), the time derivative of the angle, is the [angular velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_velocity "Angular velocity")![Image 133: {\displaystyle \omega _{z}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e6664a519a737b4e06b4668df3e837d4e4986db1). Ordinarily, the Lagrangian depends on the angular velocity through the kinetic energy: The latter can be written by separating the velocity to its radial and tangential part, with the tangential part at the x-y plane, around the z-axis, being equal to: ![Image 134: {\displaystyle \sum _{i}{\tfrac {1}{2}}m_{i}{v_{T}}_{i}^{2}=\sum _{i}{\tfrac {1}{2}}m_{i}\left(x_{i}^{2}+y_{i}^{2}\right){{\omega _{z}}_{i}}^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/540dac61440dd8da1145e3c998d2f9562d2213f2) where the subscript i stands for the i-th body, and _m_, _v_ _T_ and _ω_ _z_ stand for mass, tangential velocity around the z-axis and angular velocity around that axis, respectively.

For a body that is not point-like, with density _ρ_, we have instead: ![Image 135: {\displaystyle {\frac {1}{2}}\int \rho (x,y,z)\left(x_{i}^{2}+y_{i}^{2}\right){{\omega _{z}}_{i}}^{2}\,dx\,dy={\frac {1}{2}}{I_{z}}_{i}{{\omega _{z}}_{i}}^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/352ad9f064c3713ba6d5a5584eb634b562149b80) where integration runs over the area of the body,[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#cite_note-28) and _I_ z is the moment of inertia around the z-axis.

Thus, assuming the potential energy does not depend on _ω_ _z_ (this assumption may fail for electromagnetic systems), we have the angular momentum of the _i_ th object: ![Image 136: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}{L_{z}}_{i}&={\frac {\partial {\cal {L}}}{\partial {{\omega _{z}}_{i}}}}={\frac {\partial E_{k}}{\partial {{\omega _{z}}_{i}}}}\\&={I_{z}}_{i}\cdot {\omega _{z}}_{i}\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ced48449127d30dc5fcde4c5eab09328b68c93b9)

We have thus far rotated each object by a separate angle; we may also define an overall angle _θ_ z by which we rotate the whole system, thus rotating also each object around the z-axis, and have the overall angular momentum: ![Image 137: {\displaystyle L_{z}=\sum _{i}{I_{z}}_{i}\cdot {\omega _{z}}_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/15e4a01264119c9ff46f2e4099dcc92eec496a41)

From [Euler–Lagrange equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler%E2%80%93Lagrange_equation "Euler–Lagrange equation") it then follows that: ![Image 138: {\displaystyle 0={\frac {\partial {\cal {L}}}{\partial {{\theta _{z}}_{i}}}}-{\frac {d}{dt}}\left({\frac {\partial {\cal {L}}}{\partial {{{\dot {\theta }}_{z}}_{i}}}}\right)={\frac {\partial {\cal {L}}}{\partial {{\theta _{z}}_{i}}}}-{\frac {d{L_{z}}_{i}}{dt}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e4eca9ec00bbbf83969b41733aaa004b16f23484)

Since the lagrangian is dependent upon the angles of the object only through the potential, we have: ![Image 139: {\displaystyle {\frac {d{L_{z}}_{i}}{dt}}={\frac {\partial {\cal {L}}}{\partial {{\theta _{z}}_{i}}}}=-{\frac {\partial V}{\partial {{\theta _{z}}_{i}}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e200f643c16a72c101334aea3c7b2fcb031059e1) which is the torque on the _i_ th object.

Suppose the system is invariant to rotations, so that the potential is independent of an overall rotation by the angle _θ_ z (thus it may depend on the angles of objects only through their differences, in the form ![Image 140: {\displaystyle V({\theta _{z}}_{i},{\theta _{z}}_{j})=V({\theta _{z}}_{i}-{\theta _{z}}_{j})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bdf948ed90f5ec985945fc05ff41552e955811fb)). We therefore get for the total angular momentum: ![Image 141: {\displaystyle {\frac {dL_{z}}{dt}}=-{\frac {\partial V}{\partial {\theta _{z}}}}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/aadf954d3dc9b7f87b433a5420606bc0e0fc27b9) And thus the angular momentum around the z-axis is conserved.

This analysis can be repeated separately for each axis, giving conversation of the angular momentum vector. However, the angles around the three axes cannot be treated simultaneously as generalized coordinates, since they are not independent; in particular, two angles per point suffice to determine its position. While it is true that in the case of a rigid body, fully describing it requires, in addition to three [translational](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Translational_symmetry "Translational symmetry") degrees of freedom, also specification of three rotational degrees of freedom; however these cannot be defined as rotations around the [Cartesian axes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system "Cartesian coordinate system") (see [Euler angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler_angles "Euler angles")). This caveat is reflected in quantum mechanics in the non-trivial [commutation relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutation_relation "Commutation relation") of the different components of the [angular momentum operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum_operator "Angular momentum operator").

### Hamiltonian formalism

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Angular_momentum&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Hamiltonian formalism")]

Equivalently, in [Hamiltonian mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamiltonian_mechanics "Hamiltonian mechanics") the Hamiltonian can be described as a function of the angular momentum. As before, the part of the kinetic energy related to rotation around the z-axis for the _i_ th object is: ![Image 142: {\displaystyle {\frac {1}{2}}{I_{z}}_{i}{{\omega _{z}}_{i}}^{2}={\frac {{{L_{z}}_{i}}^{2}}{2{I_{z}}_{i}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9fea4670d6d67a9c53d3b14e287afe4b053b0de0) which is analogous to the energy dependence upon momentum along the z-axis, ![Image 143: {\displaystyle {\frac {{{p_{z}}_{i}}^{2}}{{2m}_{i}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a130e7f0e2bb096205ed92293b706638b0422cb2).

Hamilton's equations relate the angle around the z-axis to its conjugate momentum, the angular momentum around the same axis: ![Image 144: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}{\frac {d{\theta _{z}}_{i}}{dt}}&={\frac {\partial {\mathcal {H}}}{\partial {L_{z}}_{i}}}={\frac {{L_{z}}_{i}}{{I_{z}}_{i}}}\\{\frac {d{L_{z}}_{i}}{dt}}&=-{\frac {\partial {\mathcal {H}}}{\partial {\theta _{z}}_{i}}}=-{\frac {\partial V}{\partial {\theta _{z}}_{i}}}\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fffb1fe3d60ae0a0ab90e9f3b2556e026b5053f0)

The first equation gives ![Image 145: {\displaystyle {L_{z}}_{i}={I_{z}}_{i}\cdot {{{\dot {\theta }}_{z}}_{i}}={I_{z}}_{i}\cdot {\omega _{z}}_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8fff0339cc32d6f14853026e32bfeaa291018302)

And so we get the same results as in the Lagrangian formalism.

Note, that for combining all axes together, we write the kinetic energy as: ![Image 146: {\displaystyle E_{k}={\frac {1}{2}}\sum _{i}{\frac {|\mathbf {p} _{i}|^{2}}{2m_{i}}}=\sum _{i}\left({\frac {{p_{r}}_{i}^{2}}{2m_{i}}}+{\frac {1}{2}}{\mathbf {L} _{i}}^{\textsf {T}}{I_{i}}^{-1}\mathbf {L} _{i}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b779494825d16a88de6fd83809bfa0e685cfbec9) where _p_ r is the momentum in the radial direction, and the [moment of inertia is a 3-dimensional matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_of_inertia#Inertia_matrix_in_different_reference_frames "Moment of inertia"); bold letters stand for 3-dimensional vectors.

For point-like bodies we have: ![Image 147: {\displaystyle E_{k}=\sum _{i}\left({\frac {{p_{r}}_{i}^{2}}{2m_{i}}}+{\frac {|{\mathbf {L} _{i}}|^{2}}{2m_{i}{r_{i}}^{2}}}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7380e3e3a705ca0b46a1b795e69371969da9c357)

This form of the kinetic energy part of the Hamiltonian is useful in analyzing [central potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_potential "Central potential") problems, and is easily transformed to a [quantum mechanical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanical "Quantum mechanical") work frame (e.g. in the [hydrogen atom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_atom "Hydrogen atom") problem).

Angular momentum in orbital mechanics
-------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Angular_momentum&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Angular momentum in orbital mechanics")]

While in classical mechanics the language of angular momentum can be replaced by Newton's laws of motion, it is particularly useful for motion in [central potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_potential "Central potential") such as planetary motion in the solar system. Thus, the orbit of a planet in the solar system is defined by its energy, angular momentum and angles of the orbit major axis relative to a coordinate frame.

In astrodynamics and [celestial mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celestial_mechanics "Celestial mechanics"), a quantity closely related to angular momentum is defined as[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#cite_note-29)![Image 148: {\displaystyle \mathbf {h} =\mathbf {r} \times \mathbf {v} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6d21943fd68a6ac77006c0d7b71cedd7b3639b10) called _[specific angular momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Specific\_angular\_momentum "Specific angular momentum")_. Note that ![Image 149: {\displaystyle \mathbf {L} =m\mathbf {h} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/451a7af5aa41456418306ab6657c780e020a4855)[Mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass "Mass") is often unimportant in orbital mechanics calculations, because motion of a body is determined by [gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity "Gravity"). The primary body of the system is often so much larger than any bodies in motion about it that the gravitational effect of the smaller bodies on it can be neglected; it maintains, in effect, constant velocity. The motion of all bodies is affected by its gravity in the same way, regardless of mass, and therefore all move approximately the same way under the same conditions.

Angular momentum is also an extremely useful concept for describing rotating rigid bodies such as a [gyroscope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gyroscope "Gyroscope") or a rocky planet. For a continuous mass distribution with [density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Density "Density") function _ρ_(**r**), a differential [volume element](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume_element "Volume element")_dV_ with [position vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Position_vector "Position vector")**r** within the mass has a mass element _dm_ = _ρ_(**r**)_dV_. Therefore, the [infinitesimal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinitesimal "Infinitesimal") angular momentum of this element is: ![Image 150: {\displaystyle d\mathbf {L} =\mathbf {r} \times dm\mathbf {v} =\mathbf {r} \times \rho (\mathbf {r} )dV\mathbf {v} =dV\mathbf {r} \times \rho (\mathbf {r} )\mathbf {v} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/745bcb263750bb4b6df11130e916204d27d891f0) and [integrating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume_integral "Volume integral") this [differential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_(infinitesimal) "Differential (infinitesimal)") over the volume of the entire mass gives its total angular momentum: ![Image 151: {\displaystyle \mathbf {L} =\int _{V}dV\mathbf {r} \times \rho (\mathbf {r} )\mathbf {v} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0dfcf143a296d05052ee9f5612c8c78977806eea)

In the derivation which follows, integrals similar to this can replace the sums for the case of continuous mass.

### Collection of particles

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Angular_momentum&action=edit&section=16 "Edit section: Collection of particles")]

[![Image 152](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Ang_mom_vector_diagram.png/250px-Ang_mom_vector_diagram.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Ang_mom_vector_diagram.png)

The angular momentum of the particles _i_ is the sum of the cross products **R** × _M_**V** + Σ**r**_i_ × _m i_**v**_i_.

For a collection of particles in motion about an arbitrary origin, it is informative to develop the equation of angular momentum by resolving their motion into components about their own center of mass and about the origin. Given,

The total mass of the particles is simply their sum, ![Image 153: {\displaystyle M=\sum _{i}m_{i}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1c71e78a6457b75b0af823f3989eabc616b624b6)

The position vector of the center of mass is defined by,[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#cite_note-30)![Image 154: {\displaystyle M\mathbf {R} =\sum _{i}m_{i}\mathbf {R} _{i}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b0f133d75bb6cbe0958d66acc95e5e186655942e)

By inspection,

Angular momentum in general relativity
--------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Angular_momentum&action=edit&section=19 "Edit section: Angular momentum in general relativity")]

[![Image 179](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Angular_momentum_bivector_and_pseudovector.svg/250px-Angular_momentum_bivector_and_pseudovector.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Angular_momentum_bivector_and_pseudovector.svg)

The 3-angular momentum as a [bivector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bivector "Bivector") (plane element) and [axial vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axial_vector "Axial vector"), of a particle of mass _m_ with instantaneous 3-position **x** and 3-momentum **p**.

In modern (20th century) theoretical physics, angular momentum (not including any intrinsic angular momentum – see [below](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#Angular_momentum_in_quantum_mechanics)) is described using a different formalism, instead of a classical [pseudovector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudovector "Pseudovector"). In this formalism, angular momentum is the [2-form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2-form "2-form")[Noether charge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noether_charge "Noether charge") associated with rotational invariance. As a result, angular momentum is generally not conserved locally for general [curved spacetimes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curved_space "Curved space"), unless they have rotational symmetry;[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#cite_note-Hawking-32) whereas globally the notion of angular momentum itself only makes sense if the spacetime is asymptotically flat.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#cite_note-Misner-33) If the spacetime is only axially symmetric like for the [Kerr metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kerr_metric "Kerr metric"), the total angular momentum is not conserved but ![Image 180: {\displaystyle p_{\phi }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b374d416af2eff24ab4f701fc6549a10dc0e4783) is conserved which is related to the invariance of rotating around the symmetry-axis, where note that ![Image 181: {\displaystyle p_{\phi }=g_{\phi \mu }p^{\mu }=mg_{\mu \phi }dX^{\mu }/d\tau }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5408970a789c69430a87dc4d8d4066265820e97c) where ![Image 182: {\displaystyle g_{\mu \nu }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e5bf4140993a891f5782167dc8a0c236dc7667b8) is the [metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_tensor_(general_relativity) "Metric tensor (general relativity)"), ![Image 183: {\displaystyle m={\sqrt {|p_{\mu }p^{\mu }|}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1e615963330fa3441e5322dd47103b9c314770d6) is the [rest mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rest_mass "Rest mass"), ![Image 184: {\displaystyle dX^{\mu }/d\tau }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/28e567d77bea2220c7a9d674dfb3a9670ed25948) is the [four-velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Four-velocity "Four-velocity"), and ![Image 185: {\displaystyle X^{\mu }=(t,r,\theta ,\phi )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d87dfa4faef9e85803b21313cde12ac73f8acfd) is the four-position in spherical coordinates.

In classical mechanics, the angular momentum of a particle can be reinterpreted as a plane element: ![Image 186: {\displaystyle \mathbf {L} =\mathbf {r} \wedge \mathbf {p} \,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a728ebb909aecdf46e42d9d76e7ef344fa0bf0ba) in which the [exterior product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_product "Exterior product") (∧) replaces the [cross product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product "Cross product") (×) (these products have similar characteristics but are nonequivalent). This has the advantage of a clearer geometric interpretation as a plane element, defined using the vectors **x** and **p**, and the expression is true in any number of dimensions. In Cartesian coordinates: ![Image 187: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\mathbf {L} &=\left(xp_{y}-yp_{x}\right)\mathbf {e} _{x}\wedge \mathbf {e} _{y}+\left(yp_{z}-zp_{y}\right)\mathbf {e} _{y}\wedge \mathbf {e} _{z}+\left(zp_{x}-xp_{z}\right)\mathbf {e} _{z}\wedge \mathbf {e} _{x}\\&=L_{xy}\mathbf {e} _{x}\wedge \mathbf {e} _{y}+L_{yz}\mathbf {e} _{y}\wedge \mathbf {e} _{z}+L_{zx}\mathbf {e} _{z}\wedge \mathbf {e} _{x}\,,\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/771af9385db3ca15515c2538fa62846f3fe6194e) or more compactly in index notation: ![Image 188: {\displaystyle L_{ij}=x_{i}p_{j}-x_{j}p_{i}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/32e4099346e97f23ae25a0c5424c77bfeaa02cce)

The angular velocity can also be defined as an anti-symmetric second order tensor, with components _ω ij_. The relation between the two anti-symmetric tensors is given by the moment of inertia which must now be a fourth order tensor:[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#cite_note-34)![Image 189: {\displaystyle L_{ij}=I_{ijk\ell }\omega _{k\ell }\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/34c1df7926b52a248cb20e1277546a850aec2045)

Again, this equation in **L** and _**ω**_ as tensors is true in any number of dimensions. This equation also appears in the [geometric algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_algebra "Geometric algebra") formalism, in which **L** and _**ω**_ are bivectors, and the moment of inertia is a mapping between them.

In [relativistic mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_mechanics "Relativistic mechanics"), the [relativistic angular momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_angular_momentum "Relativistic angular momentum") of a particle is expressed as an [anti-symmetric tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antisymmetric_tensor "Antisymmetric tensor") of second order: ![Image 190: {\displaystyle M_{\alpha \beta }=X_{\alpha }P_{\beta }-X_{\beta }P_{\alpha }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e0444666934b68983d172799a6719a3b12eb2f31) in terms of [four-vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Four-vector "Four-vector"), namely the [four-position](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Four-position "Four-position")_X_ and the [four-momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Four-momentum "Four-momentum")_P_, and absorbs the above **L** together with the [moment of mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_of_mass "Moment of mass"), i.e., the product of the relativistic mass of the particle and its [center of mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Center_of_mass "Center of mass"), which can be thought of as describing the motion of its center of mass, since mass–energy is conserved.

In each of the above cases, for a system of particles the total angular momentum is just the sum of the individual particle angular momenta, and the center of mass is for the system.

Angular momentum in quantum mechanics
-------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Angular_momentum&action=edit&section=20 "Edit section: Angular momentum in quantum mechanics")]

In [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics"), angular momentum (like other quantities) is expressed as an [operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operator_(physics) "Operator (physics)"), and its one-dimensional projections have [quantized eigenvalues](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_spectrum "Point spectrum"). Angular momentum is subject to the [Heisenberg uncertainty principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heisenberg_uncertainty_principle "Heisenberg uncertainty principle"), implying that at any time, only one [projection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_projection "Vector projection") (also called "component") can be measured with definite precision; the other two then remain uncertain. Because of this, the axis of rotation of a quantum particle is undefined. Quantum particles _do_ possess a type of non-orbital angular momentum called "spin", but this angular momentum does not correspond to a spinning motion.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#cite_note-35) In [relativistic quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics#Relativistic_quantum_angular_momentum "Relativistic quantum mechanics") the above relativistic definition becomes a tensorial operator.

### Spin, orbital, and total angular momentum

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Angular_momentum&action=edit&section=21 "Edit section: Spin, orbital, and total angular momentum")]

[![Image 191](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Classical_angular_momentum.svg/330px-Classical_angular_momentum.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Classical_angular_momentum.svg)

Angular momenta of a _classical_ object.

*   **Left:** "spin" angular momentum **S** is really orbital angular momentum of the object at every point.
*   **Right:** extrinsic orbital angular momentum **L** about an axis.
*   **Top:** the [moment of inertia tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_of_inertia_tensor "Moment of inertia tensor")**I** and [angular velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_velocity "Angular velocity")**ω** (**L** is not always parallel to **ω**).[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#cite_note-36)
*   **Bottom:** momentum **p** and its radial position **r** from the axis. The total angular momentum (spin plus orbital) is **J**. For a _quantum_ particle the interpretations are different; [particle spin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_(physics) "Spin (physics)") does _not_ have the above interpretation.

The classical definition of angular momentum as ![Image 192: {\displaystyle \mathbf {L} =\mathbf {r} \times \mathbf {p} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/22179a9b81408e19de312b5fbfec30ff62cefa4a) can be carried over to quantum mechanics, by reinterpreting **r** as the quantum [position operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Position_operator "Position operator") and **p** as the quantum [momentum operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum_operator "Momentum operator"). **L** is then an [operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operator_(physics) "Operator (physics)"), specifically called the _[orbital angular momentum operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular\_momentum\_operator "Angular momentum operator")_. The components of the angular momentum operator satisfy the commutation relations of the [Lie algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_algebra "Lie algebra")[SO(3)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3D_rotation_group "3D rotation group").[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#cite_note-37) Indeed, these operators are precisely the infinitesimal action of the rotation group on the quantum [Hilbert space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space "Hilbert space").[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#cite_note-38) (See also the discussion below of the angular momentum operators as the generators of rotations.)

However, in quantum physics, there is another type of angular momentum, called _spin angular momentum_, represented by the spin operator **S**. Spin is often depicted as a particle literally spinning around an axis, but this is a misleading and inaccurate picture: spin is an intrinsic property of a particle, unrelated to any sort of motion in space and fundamentally different from orbital angular momentum. All [elementary particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementary_particle "Elementary particle") have a characteristic spin (possibly zero),[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#cite_note-39) and almost all [elementary particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementary_particle "Elementary particle") have nonzero spin.[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#cite_note-40) For example [electrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron "Electron") have "spin 1/2" (this actually means "spin [ħ](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reduced_Planck_constant "Reduced Planck constant")/2"), [photons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon "Photon") have "spin 1" (this actually means "spin ħ"), and [pi-mesons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pion "Pion") have spin 0.[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#cite_note-41)

Finally, there is [total angular momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Total_angular_momentum "Total angular momentum")**J**, which combines both the spin and orbital angular momentum of all particles and fields. (For one particle, **J** = **L** + **S**.) [Conservation of angular momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservation_of_angular_momentum "Conservation of angular momentum") applies to **J**, but not to **L** or **S**; for example, the [spin–orbit interaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin%E2%80%93orbit_interaction "Spin–orbit interaction") allows angular momentum to transfer back and forth between **L** and **S**, with the total remaining constant. Electrons and photons need not have integer-based values for total angular momentum, but can also have half-integer values.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#cite_note-42)

In molecules the total angular momentum **F** is the sum of the rovibronic (orbital) angular momentum **N**, the electron spin angular momentum **S**, and the nuclear spin angular momentum **I**. For electronic singlet states the rovibronic angular momentum is denoted **J** rather than **N**. As explained by Van Vleck,[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#cite_note-43) the components of the molecular rovibronic angular momentum referred to molecule-fixed axes have different commutation relations from those for the components about space-fixed axes.

In [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics"), angular momentum is [quantized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum_quantization "Angular momentum quantization") – that is, it cannot vary continuously, but only in "[quantum leaps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_number "Quantum number")" between certain allowed values. For any system, the following restrictions on measurement results apply, where ![Image 193: {\displaystyle \hbar }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/de68de3a92517953436c93b5a76461d49160cc41) is the [reduced Planck constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reduced_Planck_constant "Reduced Planck constant") and ![Image 194: {\displaystyle {\hat {n}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d125dccc556f5c8b0bf98a4f3847590b3f353bd4) is any [Euclidean vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_vector "Euclidean vector") such as x, y, or z:

[![Image 195](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Circular_Standing_Wave.gif/250px-Circular_Standing_Wave.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Circular_Standing_Wave.gif)

In this [standing wave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standing_wave "Standing wave") on a circular string, the circle is broken into exactly 8 [wavelengths](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wavelength "Wavelength"). A standing wave like this can have 0, 1, 2, or any integer number of wavelengths around the circle, but it _cannot_ have a non-integer number of wavelengths like 8.3. In quantum mechanics, angular momentum is quantized for a similar reason.

The [reduced Planck constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reduced_Planck_constant "Reduced Planck constant")![Image 196: {\displaystyle \hbar }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/de68de3a92517953436c93b5a76461d49160cc41) is tiny by everyday standards, about 10−34[J s](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joule-second "Joule-second"), and therefore this quantization does not noticeably affect the angular momentum of macroscopic objects. However, it is very important in the microscopic world. For example, the structure of [electron shells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_shell "Electron shell") and subshells in chemistry is significantly affected by the quantization of angular momentum.

Quantization of angular momentum was first postulated by [Niels Bohr](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Niels_Bohr "Niels Bohr") in [his model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bohr_model "Bohr model") of the atom and was later predicted by [Erwin Schrödinger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erwin_Schr%C3%B6dinger "Erwin Schrödinger") in his [Schrödinger equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schr%C3%B6dinger_equation#Quantization "Schrödinger equation").

In the definition ![Image 197: {\displaystyle \mathbf {L} =\mathbf {r} \times \mathbf {p} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/22179a9b81408e19de312b5fbfec30ff62cefa4a), six operators are involved: The [position operators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Position_operator "Position operator")![Image 198: {\displaystyle r_{x}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3705946c668b0bd4b21e7c19b95bb6ed05ea4ed), ![Image 199: {\displaystyle r_{y}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/49e52760272851bdfd60c01877ee11bfc1857a60), ![Image 200: {\displaystyle r_{z}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e07006192648caeccd5ad603f234b1f11e2e40b9), and the [momentum operators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum_operator "Momentum operator")![Image 201: {\displaystyle p_{x}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5a5055ed65713825b48aa6ee05118c072e6f026a), ![Image 202: {\displaystyle p_{y}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1108b3eff778ca6f026e3e28c26cb093174cc2d0), ![Image 203: {\displaystyle p_{z}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1dbd3c1a6173a7974e0095301da94447c5f67657). However, the [Heisenberg uncertainty principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uncertainty_principle "Uncertainty principle") tells us that it is not possible for all six of these quantities to be known simultaneously with arbitrary precision. Therefore, there are limits to what can be known or measured about a particle's angular momentum. It turns out that the best that one can do is to simultaneously measure both the angular momentum vector's [magnitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnitude_(vector) "Magnitude (vector)") and its component along one axis.

The uncertainty is closely related to the fact that different components of an angular momentum operator do not [commute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutator "Commutator"), for example ![Image 204: {\displaystyle L_{x}L_{y}\neq L_{y}L_{x}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/14b5e35b0c4b63f2a2bd24c39f08e6e1c86f9528). (For the precise [commutation relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutation_relation "Commutation relation"), see [angular momentum operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum_operator "Angular momentum operator").)

### Total angular momentum as generator of rotations

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Angular_momentum&action=edit&section=24 "Edit section: Total angular momentum as generator of rotations")]

As mentioned above, orbital angular momentum **L** is defined as in classical mechanics: ![Image 205: {\displaystyle \mathbf {L} =\mathbf {r} \times \mathbf {p} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/22179a9b81408e19de312b5fbfec30ff62cefa4a), but _total_ angular momentum **J** is defined in a different, more basic way: **J** is defined as the "generator of rotations".[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#cite_note-littlejohn-44) More specifically, **J** is defined so that the operator ![Image 206: {\displaystyle R({\hat {n}},\phi )\equiv \exp \left(-{\frac {i}{\hbar }}\phi \,\mathbf {J} \cdot {\hat {\mathbf {n} }}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/05d8a19780700ce3d93b241a525c4441f6479e34) is the [rotation operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation_operator_(quantum_mechanics) "Rotation operator (quantum mechanics)") that takes any system and rotates it by angle ![Image 207: {\displaystyle \phi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/72b1f30316670aee6270a28334bdf4f5072cdde4) about the axis ![Image 208: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathbf {n} }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/aae87b164ba005e99b51066c46d1eacc7f56564a). (The "exp" in the formula refers to [operator exponential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_exponential "Matrix exponential").) To put this the other way around, whatever our quantum Hilbert space is, we expect that the [rotation group SO(3)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation_group_SO(3) "Rotation group SO(3)") will act on it. There is then an associated action of the Lie algebra so(3) of SO(3); the operators describing the action of so(3) on our Hilbert space are the (total) angular momentum operators.

The relationship between the angular momentum operator and the rotation operators is the same as the relationship between [Lie algebras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_algebra "Lie algebra") and [Lie groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_group "Lie group") in mathematics. The close relationship between angular momentum and rotations is reflected in [Noether's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noether%27s_theorem "Noether's theorem") that proves that angular momentum is conserved whenever the laws of physics are rotationally invariant.

Angular momentum in electrodynamics
-----------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Angular_momentum&action=edit&section=25 "Edit section: Angular momentum in electrodynamics")]

When describing the motion of a [charged particle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charged_particle "Charged particle") in an [electromagnetic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_field "Electromagnetic field"), the [canonical momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canonical_momentum "Canonical momentum")**P** (derived from the [Lagrangian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrangian_mechanics "Lagrangian mechanics") for this system) is not [gauge invariant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauge_invariant "Gauge invariant"). As a consequence, the canonical angular momentum **L** = **r** × **P** is not gauge invariant either. Instead, the momentum that is physical, the so-called _kinetic momentum_ (used throughout this article), is (in [SI units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SI_units "SI units")) ![Image 209: {\displaystyle \mathbf {p} =m\mathbf {v} =\mathbf {P} -e\mathbf {A} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b13cdc37559fc7edc45f19ca88d0763bb4e5000) where _e_ is the [electric charge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_charge "Electric charge") of the particle and **A** the [magnetic vector potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_vector_potential "Magnetic vector potential") of the electromagnetic field. The gauge-invariant angular momentum, that is _kinetic angular momentum_, is given by ![Image 210: {\displaystyle \mathbf {K} =\mathbf {r} \times (\mathbf {P} -e\mathbf {A} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0ea5e7e0c75ea3090123ba971034a6cd72db6b79)

The interplay with quantum mechanics is discussed further in the article on [canonical commutation relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canonical_commutation_relation "Canonical commutation relation").

Angular momentum in nature and the cosmos
-----------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Angular_momentum&action=edit&section=27 "Edit section: Angular momentum in nature and the cosmos")]

[Tropical cyclones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tropical_cyclones "Tropical cyclones") and other related weather phenomena involve conservation of angular momentum in order to explain the dynamics. Winds revolve slowly around low pressure systems, mainly due to the [coriolis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coriolis_force#Meteorology "Coriolis force") effect. If the low pressure intensifies and the slowly circulating air is drawn toward the center, the molecules must speed up in order to conserve angular momentum. By the time they reach the center, the speeds become destructive.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#cite_note-Tropical_Cyclone_Structure-2)

[Johannes Kepler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johannes_Kepler "Johannes Kepler") determined the laws of planetary motion without knowledge of conservation of momentum. However, not long after his discovery their derivation was determined from conservation of angular momentum. Planets move more slowly the further they are out in their elliptical orbits, which is explained intuitively by the fact that orbital angular momentum is proportional to the radius of the orbit. Since the mass does not change and the angular momentum is conserved, the velocity drops.

[Tidal acceleration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tidal_acceleration "Tidal acceleration") is an effect of the tidal forces between an orbiting natural satellite (e.g. the [Moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moon "Moon")) and the primary planet that it orbits (e.g. Earth). The gravitational torque between the Moon and the tidal bulge of Earth causes the Moon to be constantly promoted to a slightly higher orbit (~3.8 cm per year) and Earth to be [decelerated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%CE%94T_(timekeeping)#Values_prior_to_1955 "ΔT (timekeeping)") (by −25.858 ± 0.003″/cy²) in its rotation (the [length of the day increases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%CE%94T_(timekeeping)#Universal_time "ΔT (timekeeping)") by ~1.7 ms per century, +2.3 ms from tidal effect and −0.6 ms from post-glacial rebound). The Earth loses angular momentum which is transferred to the Moon such that the overall angular momentum is conserved.

Angular momentum in engineering and technology
----------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Angular_momentum&action=edit&section=28 "Edit section: Angular momentum in engineering and technology")]

Video: A [gyroscopic exercise tool](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gyroscopic_exercise_tool "Gyroscopic exercise tool") is an application of the conservation of angular momentum for muscle strengthening. A mass quickly rotating about its axis in a ball-shaped device defines an angular momentum. When the person exercising tilts the ball, a force results which even increases the rotational speed when reacted to specifically by the user.

Examples of using conservation of angular momentum for practical advantage are abundant. In engines such as [steam engines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steam_engines "Steam engines") or [internal combustion engines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internal_combustion_engines "Internal combustion engines"), a [flywheel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flywheel "Flywheel") is needed to efficiently convert the lateral motion of the pistons to rotational motion.

[Inertial navigation systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inertial_navigation_system "Inertial navigation system") explicitly use the fact that angular momentum is conserved with respect to the [inertial frame](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inertial_frame "Inertial frame") of space. Inertial navigation is what enables submarine trips under the polar ice cap, but are also crucial to all forms of modern navigation.

[Rifled bullets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rifle "Rifle") use the stability provided by conservation of angular momentum to be more true in their trajectory. The invention of rifled firearms and cannons gave their users significant strategic advantage in battle, and thus were a technological turning point in history.

[Isaac Newton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaac_Newton "Isaac Newton"), in the [_Principia_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophiae_Naturalis_Principia_Mathematica "Philosophiae Naturalis Principia Mathematica"), hinted at angular momentum in his examples of the [first law of motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_law_of_motion "First law of motion"),

> A top, whose parts by their cohesion are perpetually drawn aside from rectilinear motions, does not cease its rotation, otherwise than as it is retarded by the air. The greater bodies of the planets and comets, meeting with less resistance in more free spaces, preserve their motions both progressive and circular for a much longer time.[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#cite_note-47)

He did not further investigate angular momentum directly in the _Principia_, saying:

> From such kind of reflexions also sometimes arise the circular motions of bodies about their own centers. But these are cases which I do not consider in what follows; and it would be too tedious to demonstrate every particular that relates to this subject.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#cite_note-48)

However, his geometric proof of the [law of areas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler%27s_laws_of_planetary_motion#Second_law "Kepler's laws of planetary motion") is an outstanding example of Newton's genius, and indirectly proves angular momentum conservation in the case of a [central force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_force "Central force").

#### Newton's derivation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Angular_momentum&action=edit&section=31 "Edit section: Newton's derivation")]

[![Image 216](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/73/Newton_area_law_derivation.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Newton_area_law_derivation.gif)

Newton's derivation of the area law using geometric means

As a [planet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planet "Planet") orbits the [Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun "Sun"), the line between the Sun and the planet sweeps out equal areas in equal intervals of time. This had been known since Kepler expounded his [second law of planetary motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler%27s_laws_of_planetary_motion "Kepler's laws of planetary motion"). Newton derived a unique geometric proof, and went on to show that the attractive force of the Sun's [gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity "Gravity") was the cause of all of Kepler's laws.

During the first interval of time, an object is in motion from point **A** to point **B**. Undisturbed, it would continue to point **c** during the second interval. When the object arrives at **B**, it receives an impulse directed toward point **S**. The impulse gives it a small added velocity toward **S**, such that if this were its only velocity, it would move from **B** to **V** during the second interval. By the [rules of velocity composition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram_of_force "Parallelogram of force"), these two velocities add, and point **C** is found by construction of parallelogram **BcCV**. Thus the object's path is deflected by the impulse so that it arrives at point **C** at the end of the second interval. Because the triangles **SBc** and **SBC** have the same base **SB** and the same height **Bc** or **VC**, they have the same area. By symmetry, triangle **SBc** also has the same area as triangle **SAB**, therefore the object has swept out equal areas **SAB** and **SBC** in equal times.

At point **C**, the object receives another impulse toward **S**, again deflecting its path during the third interval from **d** to **D**. Thus it continues to **E** and beyond, the triangles **SAB**, **SBc**, **SBC**, **SCd**, **SCD**, **SDe**, **SDE** all having the same area. Allowing the time intervals to become ever smaller, the path **ABCDE** approaches indefinitely close to a continuous curve.

Note that because this derivation is geometric, and no specific force is applied, it proves a more general law than Kepler's second law of planetary motion. It shows that the Law of Areas applies to any central force, attractive or repulsive, continuous or non-continuous, or zero.

#### Conservation of angular momentum in the law of areas

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Angular_momentum&action=edit&section=32 "Edit section: Conservation of angular momentum in the law of areas")]

The proportionality of angular momentum to the area swept out by a moving object can be understood by realizing that the bases of the triangles, that is, the lines from **S** to the object, are equivalent to the [radius r](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#Scalar_%E2%80%93_angular_momentum_in_two_dimensions), and that the heights of the triangles are proportional to the perpendicular component of [velocity v⊥](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#Scalar_%E2%80%93_angular_momentum_in_two_dimensions). Hence, if the area swept per unit time is constant, then by the triangular area formula ⁠1/2⁠(base)(height), the product (base)(height) and therefore the product rv⊥ are constant: if r and the base length are decreased, v⊥ and height must increase proportionally. Mass is constant, therefore [angular momentum rmv⊥](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#Scalar_%E2%80%93_angular_momentum_in_two_dimensions) is conserved by this exchange of distance and velocity.

In the case of triangle **SBC**, area is equal to ⁠1/2⁠(**SB**)(**VC**). Wherever **C** is eventually located due to the impulse applied at **B**, the product (**SB**)(**VC**), and therefore rmv⊥ remain constant. Similarly so for each of the triangles.

Another areal proof of conservation of angular momentum for any central force uses Mamikon's sweeping tangents theorem.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#cite_note-49)[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#cite_note-50)

[Leonhard Euler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leonhard_Euler "Leonhard Euler"), [Daniel Bernoulli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Daniel_Bernoulli "Daniel Bernoulli"), and [Patrick d'Arcy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patrick_d%27Arcy "Patrick d'Arcy") all understood angular momentum in terms of conservation of [areal velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Areal_velocity "Areal velocity"), a result of their analysis of Kepler's second law of planetary motion. It is unlikely that they realized the implications for ordinary rotating matter.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#cite_note-51)

In 1736 Euler, like Newton, touched on some of the equations of angular momentum in his _[Mechanica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanica "Mechanica")_ without further developing them.[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#cite_note-52)

Bernoulli wrote in a 1744 letter of a "moment of rotational motion", possibly the first conception of angular momentum as we now understand it.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#cite_note-53)

In 1799, [Pierre-Simon Laplace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre-Simon_Laplace "Pierre-Simon Laplace") first realized that a fixed plane was associated with rotation—his _[invariable plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invariable\_plane "Invariable plane")_.

[Louis Poinsot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis_Poinsot "Louis Poinsot") in 1803 began representing rotations as a line segment perpendicular to the rotation, and elaborated on the "conservation of moments".

In 1852 [Léon Foucault](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L%C3%A9on_Foucault "Léon Foucault") used a [gyroscope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gyroscope "Gyroscope") in an experiment to display the Earth's rotation.

[William J. M. Rankine's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_John_Macquorn_Rankine "William John Macquorn Rankine") 1858 _Manual of Applied Mechanics_ defined angular momentum in the modern sense for the first time:

> ...a line whose length is proportional to the magnitude of the angular momentum, and whose direction is perpendicular to the plane of motion of the body and of the fixed point, and such, that when the motion of the body is viewed from the extremity of the line, the radius-vector of the body seems to have right-handed rotation.

In an 1872 edition of the same book, Rankine stated that "The term _angular momentum_ was introduced by Mr. Hayward,"[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#cite_note-54) probably referring to R.B. Hayward's article _On a Direct Method of estimating Velocities, Accelerations, and all similar Quantities with respect to Axes moveable in any manner in Space with Applications,_[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#cite_note-Hayward-55) which was introduced in 1856, and published in 1864. Rankine was mistaken, as numerous publications feature the term starting in the late 18th to early 19th centuries.[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#cite_note-56) However, Hayward's article apparently was the first use of the term and the concept seen by much of the English-speaking world. Before this, angular momentum was typically referred to as "momentum of rotation" in English.[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum#cite_note-57)
