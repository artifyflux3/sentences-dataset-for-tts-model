**Taurus** (Latin, '[Bull](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bull "Bull")') is one of the [constellations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constellation "Constellation") of the [zodiac](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zodiac "Zodiac") and is located in the [northern celestial hemisphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northern_celestial_hemisphere "Northern celestial hemisphere"). Taurus is a large and prominent constellation in the [Northern Hemisphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere "Northern Hemisphere")'s winter sky. It is one of the oldest constellations, dating back to the [Early Bronze Age](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Early_Bronze_Age "Early Bronze Age") at least, when it marked the location of the Sun during the spring [equinox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equinox "Equinox"). Its importance to the agricultural calendar influenced [various bull figures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sacred_bull "Sacred bull") in the mythologies of Ancient [Sumer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sumerian_religion "Sumerian religion"), [Akkad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Akkadian_religion "Akkadian religion"), [Assyria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Assyrian_religion "Assyrian religion"), [Babylon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Babylonian_religion "Babylonian religion"), [Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Egyptian_religion "Ancient Egyptian religion"), [Greece](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek_religion "Ancient Greek religion"), and [Rome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Religion_in_ancient_Rome "Religion in ancient Rome"). Its traditional astrological symbol is [![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Taurus_symbol_%28fixed_width%29.svg/20px-Taurus_symbol_%28fixed_width%29.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Taurus_symbol_(fixed_width).svg) (♉︎), which resembles a bull's head.

A number of features exist that are of interest to astronomers. Taurus hosts two of the nearest [open clusters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_cluster "Open cluster") to Earth, the [Pleiades](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pleiades "Pleiades") and the [Hyades](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyades_(star_cluster) "Hyades (star cluster)"), both of which are visible to the naked eye. At first magnitude, the [red giant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_giant "Red giant")[Aldebaran](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aldebaran "Aldebaran") is the brightest star in the constellation. In the northeast part of Taurus is [Messier 1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Messier_1 "Messier 1"), more commonly known as the Crab Nebula, a [supernova remnant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supernova_remnant "Supernova remnant") containing the [Crab Pulsar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crab_Pulsar "Crab Pulsar"). One of the closest regions of active star formation, the Taurus-Auriga complex, crosses into the northern part of the constellation. The [variable star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variable_star "Variable star")[T Tauri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/T_Tauri "T Tauri") is the prototype of a class of [pre-main-sequence stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pre-main-sequence_star "Pre-main-sequence star").

Taurus is a large and prominent constellation in the [northern hemisphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northern_hemisphere "Northern hemisphere")'s winter sky, between [Aries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aries_(constellation) "Aries (constellation)") to the west and [Gemini](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gemini_(constellation) "Gemini (constellation)") to the east; to the north lies [Perseus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perseus_(constellation) "Perseus (constellation)") and [Auriga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Auriga_(constellation) "Auriga (constellation)"), to the southeast [Orion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(constellation) "Orion (constellation)"), to the south [Eridanus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eridanus_(constellation) "Eridanus (constellation)"), and to the southwest [Cetus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cetus "Cetus"). In late November-early December, Taurus reaches opposition (furthest point from the Sun) and is visible the entire night. By late March, it is setting at sunset and completely disappears behind the Sun's glare from May to July.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-Garfinkle_1997-5)

This constellation forms part of the zodiac and hence is intersected by the [ecliptic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecliptic "Ecliptic"). This circle across the [celestial sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celestial_sphere "Celestial sphere") forms the apparent path of the Sun as the Earth completes its annual orbit. As the orbital plane of the Moon and the planets lie near the ecliptic, they can usually be found in the constellation Taurus during some part of each year.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-Garfinkle_1997-5) The [galactic plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galactic_plane "Galactic plane") of the [Milky Way](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milky_Way "Milky Way") intersects the northeast corner of the constellation and the [galactic anticenter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galactic_anticenter "Galactic anticenter") is located near the border between Taurus and Auriga. Taurus is the only constellation crossed by all three of the galactic equator, celestial equator, and ecliptic. A ring-like galactic structure known as [Gould's Belt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gould%27s_Belt "Gould's Belt") passes through the constellation.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-crossen_rhemann2004-6)

The recommended three-letter abbreviation for the constellation, as adopted by the [International Astronomical Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union "International Astronomical Union") in 1922, is "Tau".[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-pa30_469-7) The official constellation boundaries, as set by Belgian astronomer [Eugène Delporte](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eug%C3%A8ne_Joseph_Delporte "Eugène Joseph Delporte") in 1930, are defined by a polygon of 26 segments. In the [equatorial coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equatorial_coordinate_system "Equatorial coordinate system"), the [right ascension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_ascension "Right ascension") coordinates of these borders lie between 03 h 23.4 m and 05 h 53.3 m, while the [declination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Declination "Declination") coordinates are between 31.10° and −1.35°.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-boundary-8) Because a small part of the constellation lies to the south of the [celestial equator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celestial_equator "Celestial equator"), this can not be a completely [circumpolar constellation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumpolar_constellation "Circumpolar constellation") at any latitude.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-seeds2007-9)

There are four stars above [magnitude 3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apparent_magnitude "Apparent magnitude") in Taurus.

The brightest member of this constellation is Aldebaran, an orange-hued, [spectral class](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectral_class "Spectral class") K5 III [giant star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giant_star "Giant star").[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-Burnham-11) Its name derives from **[الدبران](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D8%A7%D9%84%D8%AF%D8%A8%D8%B1%D8%A7%D9%86 "wikt:الدبران")**_\_al-dabarān\__, [Arabic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabic_language "Arabic language") for "the follower", probably from the fact that it follows the [Pleiades](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pleiades "Pleiades") during the nightly motion of the [celestial sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celestial_sphere "Celestial sphere") across the sky.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChartrand1983188-12)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-schaff-13)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAllen1963383-14) Forming the profile of a Bull's face is a _V_ or _K_-shaped [asterism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asterism_(astronomy) "Asterism (astronomy)") of stars. This outline is created by prominent members of the [Hyades](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyades_(star_cluster) "Hyades (star cluster)"),[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-Olcott-15) the nearest distinct open [star cluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_cluster "Star cluster") after the [Ursa Major Moving Group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ursa_Major_Moving_Group "Ursa Major Moving Group").[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-inglis2004-16) In this profile, Aldebaran forms the bull's bloodshot eye, which has been described as "glaring menacingly at the hunter Orion",[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-saski_boddy2003-17) a constellation that lies just to the southeast. Aldebaran has around 116% the mass of the Sun.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-Farr2018-18) It also hosts a candidate exoplanet.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-Farr2018-18)

The Hyades span about 5° of the sky, so that they can only be viewed in their entirety with binoculars or the unaided eye.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-FOOTNOTERidpathTirion200355-19) It includes a naked eye double star, [Theta Tauri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theta_Tauri "Theta Tauri") (the proper name of Theta 2 Tauri is _Chamukuy_),[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-IAU-LSN-20) with a separation of 5.6[arcminutes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arcminutes "Arcminutes").[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-kaler_theta_tauri-21)

In the northwestern quadrant of the Taurus constellation lie the Pleiades ([M45](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Messier_object "Messier object")), one of the best known open clusters, easily visible to the naked eye. The seven most prominent stars in this cluster are at least visual [magnitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apparent_magnitude "Apparent magnitude") six, and so the cluster is also named the "Seven Sisters". However, many more stars are visible with even a modest telescope.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-marx_pfau_lamble1992-22) Astronomers estimate that the cluster has approximately 500–1,000 stars, all of which are around 100 million years old. However, they vary considerably in type. The Pleiades themselves are represented by large, bright stars; also many small [brown dwarfs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brown_dwarf "Brown dwarf") and [white dwarfs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_dwarf "White dwarf") exist. The cluster is estimated to dissipate in another 250 million years.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-objects-23) The Pleiades cluster is classified as a Shapley class c and Trumpler class I 3 r n cluster, indicating that it is irregularly shaped and loose, though concentrated at its center and detached from the star-field.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-FOOTNOTELevy200594-24)

To the east, the two horns of the bull are formed by [Beta (β) Tauri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beta_Tauri "Beta Tauri") and [Zeta (ζ) Tauri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zeta_Tauri "Zeta Tauri"); two star systems that are separated by 8°. Beta is a white, spectral class B7 III giant star known as _El Nath_, which comes from the Arabic phrase "the butting", as in butting by the horns of the bull.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChartrand1983184-25) At magnitude 1.65, it is the second brightest star in the constellation, and shares the border with the neighboring constellation of Auriga. As a result, it also bears the designation Gamma Aurigae. Zeta Tauri (the proper name is _Tianguan_[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-IAU-LSN-20)) is an [eclipsing binary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eclipsing_binary "Eclipsing binary") star that completes an orbit every 133 days.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-Burnham-11)

The star [Lambda (λ) Tauri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lambda_Tauri "Lambda Tauri") is an eclipsing binary star. This system consists of a spectral class B3 star being orbited by a less massive class A4 star. The [plane of their orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_plane_(astronomy) "Orbital plane (astronomy)") lies almost along the line of sight to the Earth. Every 3.953 days the system temporarily decreases in brightness by 1.1 magnitudes as the brighter star is partially eclipsed by the dimmer companion. The two stars are separated by only 0.1 [astronomical units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_unit "Astronomical unit"), so their shapes are modified by mutual tidal interaction. This results in a variation of their net magnitude throughout each orbit.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-apj263_289-26)

Located about 1.8° west of [Epsilon (ε) Tauri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epsilon_Tauri "Epsilon Tauri") is [T Tauri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/T_Tauri "T Tauri"), the prototype of a class of [variable stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variable_star "Variable star") called [T Tauri stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/T_Tauri_star "T Tauri star"). This star undergoes erratic changes in luminosity, varying between [magnitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visual_magnitude "Visual magnitude") 9 to 13 over a period of weeks or months.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-Garfinkle_1997-5) This is a newly formed stellar object that is just emerging from its envelope of gas and dust, but has not yet become a [main sequence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Main_sequence "Main sequence") star.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-araa27_1_351-27) The surrounding [reflection nebula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflection_nebula "Reflection nebula")[NGC 1555](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NGC_1555 "NGC 1555") is illuminated by T Tauri, and thus is also variable in luminosity.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-noao_1555-28) To the north lies [Kappa Tauri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kappa_Tauri "Kappa Tauri"), a visual [double star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double_star "Double star") consisting of two A7-type components. The pair have a separation of just 5.6 arc minutes, making them a challenge to split with the naked eye.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-FOOTNOTEO'Meara2011475-29)

In the northern part of the constellation to the northeast of the Pleiades lies the Crystal Ball Nebula, known by its catalogue designation of [NGC 1514](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NGC_1514 "NGC 1514"). This [planetary nebula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula "Planetary nebula") is of historical interest following its discovery by German-born English astronomer [William Herschel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Herschel "William Herschel") in 1790. Prior to that time, astronomers had assumed that [nebulae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nebula "Nebula") were simply unresolved groups of stars. However, Herschel could clearly resolve a star at the center of the nebula that was surrounded by a nebulous cloud of some type. In 1864, English astronomer [William Huggins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Huggins "William Huggins") used the spectrum of this nebula to deduce that the nebula is a luminous gas, rather than stars.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-FOOTNOTEO'Meara201174%E2%80%9377-30)

North-west of ζ Tauri by 1.15 degrees is the [Crab Nebula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crab_Nebula "Crab Nebula") (M1), a supernova remnant. This expanding [nebula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nebula "Nebula") was created by a [Type II supernova](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Type_II_supernova "Type II supernova") explosion, which was seen from Earth on July 4, 1054. It was bright enough to be observed during the day and is mentioned in Chinese historical texts. At its peak, the supernova reached magnitude −4, but the nebula is currently magnitude 8.4 and requires a telescope to observe.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-hawkins2002-33)[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-covington2002-34)[North American peoples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas "Indigenous peoples of the Americas") also observed the supernova, as evidenced from a painting on a New Mexican canyon and various pieces of pottery that depict the event. However, the remnant itself was not discovered until 1731, when [John Bevis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Bevis "John Bevis") found it.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-objects-23)

[IRAS 05437+2502](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IRAS_05437%2B2502 "IRAS 05437+2502"), a nebula

This constellation includes part of the Taurus-Auriga complex, or Taurus dark clouds, a star-forming region containing sparse, filamentary clouds of gas and dust. This spans a diameter of 98 [light-years](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light-year "Light-year") (30 [parsecs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parsec "Parsec")) and contains 35,000[solar masses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_mass "Solar mass") of material, which is both larger and less massive than the [Orion Nebula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_Nebula "Orion Nebula").[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-schulz2005-35) At a distance of 490 light-years (150 parsecs), this is one of the nearest active star forming regions.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-babu_feigelson1996-36) Located in this region, about 10° to the northeast of Aldebaran, is an asterism [NGC 1746](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NGC_1746 "NGC 1746") spanning a width of 45 [arcminutes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arcminute "Arcminute").[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-FOOTNOTEO'Meara201184-32)

During November, the [Taurid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurid "Taurid")[meteor shower](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor_shower "Meteor shower") appears to radiate from the general direction of this constellation. The [Beta Taurid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beta_Taurid "Beta Taurid") meteor shower occurs during the months of June and July in the daytime, and is normally observed using radio techniques.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-lewis1997-37) Between 18 and 29 October, both the [Northern Taurids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northern_Taurids "Northern Taurids") and the [Southern Taurids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southern_Taurids "Southern Taurids") are active; though the latter stream is stronger.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-jenniskens2012-38) However, between November 1 and 10, the two streams equalize.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-jenniskens2012-38)

History and mythology
---------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Taurus_(constellation)&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: History and mythology")]

[![Image 7](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e5/Constellation_Taureau_-_al-Sufi.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Constellation_Taureau_-_al-Sufi.jpg)

[![Image 8](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Sidney_Hall_-_Urania%27s_Mirror_-_Taurus.jpg/250px-Sidney_Hall_-_Urania%27s_Mirror_-_Taurus.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Sidney_Hall_-_Urania%27s_Mirror_-_Taurus.jpg)

Taurus as depicted in _[Urania's Mirror](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urania%27s\_Mirror "Urania's Mirror")_, a set of constellation cards published in London c.1825.

The identification of the [constellation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constellation "Constellation") of Taurus with a bull is very old, certainly dating to the [Chalcolithic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chalcolithic "Chalcolithic"), and perhaps even to the [Upper Paleolithic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Upper_Paleolithic "Upper Paleolithic"). Michael Rappenglück of the [University of Munich](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Munich "University of Munich") believes that Taurus is represented in a [cave painting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cave_painting "Cave painting") at the Hall of the Bulls in the caves at [Lascaux](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lascaux "Lascaux") (dated to roughly 15,000 BC), which he believes is accompanied by a depiction of the Pleiades.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-arxiv0810.1592-39)[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-whitehouse20000809-40) The name "seven sisters" has been used for the Pleiades in the languages of many cultures, including indigenous groups of [Australia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Australia "Australia"), [North America](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_America "North America") and [Siberia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siberia "Siberia"). This suggests that the name may have a common ancient origin.[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-brown2008-41)

Taurus marked the point of [vernal (spring) equinox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/March_equinox "March equinox") in the [Chalcolithic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chalcolithic "Chalcolithic") and the [Early Bronze Age](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Early_Bronze_Age "Early Bronze Age"), from about 4000 BC to 1700 BC, after which it moved into the neighboring constellation Aries.[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-noonan05-42) The Pleiades were closest to the Sun at vernal equinox around the [23rd century BC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/23rd_century_BC "23rd century BC"). In [Babylonian astronomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Babylonian_astronomy "Babylonian astronomy"), the constellation was listed in the [MUL.APIN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MUL.APIN "MUL.APIN") as _GU 4.AN.NA_, "The [Bull of Heaven](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bull_of_Heaven "Bull of Heaven")".[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-rogers1998-43) Although it has been claimed that "when the Babylonians first set up their zodiac, the vernal equinox lay in Taurus,"[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-wilson1997-44) there is a claim that the MUL.APIN tablets indicate[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-rogers1998-43) that the vernal equinox was marked by the Babylonian constellation known as "the hired man" (the modern Aries).[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-hartner1965-45)

In the [Old Babylonian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Akkadian_language "Akkadian language")_[Epic of Gilgamesh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epic\_of\_Gilgamesh "Epic of Gilgamesh")_, the goddess [Ishtar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ishtar "Ishtar") sends Taurus, the [Bull of Heaven](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bull_of_Heaven "Bull of Heaven"), to kill Gilgamesh for spurning her advances.[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-hines2002-46) Enkidu tears off the bull's hind part and hurls the quarters into the sky where they become the stars we know as Ursa Major and Ursa Minor. Some locate Gilgamesh as the neighboring constellation of Orion, facing Taurus as if in combat,[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-ns120_36-47) while others identify him with the sun whose rising on the equinox vanquishes the constellation. In early Mesopotamian art, the Bull of Heaven was closely associated with [Inanna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inanna "Inanna"), the [Sumerian goddess](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mesopotamian_religion "Mesopotamian religion") of sexual love, fertility, and warfare. One of the oldest depictions shows the bull standing before the goddess' standard; since it has 3 stars depicted on its back (the cuneiform sign for "star-constellation"), there is good reason to regard this as the constellation later known as Taurus.[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-white2008-48)

The same iconic representation of the Heavenly Bull was depicted in the [Dendera zodiac](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dendera_zodiac "Dendera zodiac"), an [Egyptian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egypt "Egypt") bas-relief carving in a ceiling that depicted the [celestial hemisphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celestial_hemisphere "Celestial hemisphere") using a [planisphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planisphere "Planisphere"). In these ancient cultures, the orientation of the horns was portrayed as upward or backward. This differed from the later Greek depiction where the horns pointed forward.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-jbaa108_1_9-49) To the Egyptians, the constellation Taurus was a sacred bull that was associated with the renewal of life in spring. When the spring equinox entered Taurus, the constellation would become covered by the Sun in the western sky as spring began. This "sacrifice" led to the renewal of the land.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-ptak1998-50) To the early [Hebrews](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hebrews "Hebrews"), Taurus was the first constellation in their zodiac and consequently it was represented by the first letter in their alphabet, [Aleph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aleph "Aleph").[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAllen1963381-51)

In [Greek mythology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_mythology "Greek mythology"), Taurus was identified with [Zeus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zeus "Zeus"), who assumed the form of a magnificent white bull to abduct [Europa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Europa_(consort_of_Zeus) "Europa (consort of Zeus)"), a legendary Phoenician princess. In illustrations of Greek mythology, only the front portion of this constellation is depicted; this was sometimes explained as Taurus being partly submerged as he carried Europa out to sea. A second Greek myth portrays Taurus as [Io](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Io_(mythology) "Io (mythology)"), a mistress of Zeus. To hide his lover from his wife [Hera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hera "Hera"), Zeus changed Io into the form of a heifer.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-ridpath2018-52) Greek mythographer [Acusilaus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acusilaus "Acusilaus") marks the bull Taurus as the same that formed the myth of the [Cretan Bull](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cretan_Bull "Cretan Bull"), one of [The Twelve Labors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Labors_of_Hercules "Labors of Hercules") of [Heracles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heracles "Heracles").[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-palaephatus_stern1996-53)

Taurus became an important object of worship among the [Druids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Druid "Druid"). Their Tauric religious festival was held while the Sun passed through the constellation.[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-noonan05-42) Among the arctic people known as the [Inuit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inuit "Inuit"), the constellation is called Sakiattiat and the Hyades is Nanurjuk, with the latter representing the spirit of the [polar bear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_bear "Polar bear"). Aldebaran represents the bear, with the remainder of the stars in the Hyades being dogs that are holding the beast at bay.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-penprase2010-54)

In [Buddhism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buddhism "Buddhism"), legends hold that [Gautama Buddha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gautama_Buddha "Gautama Buddha") was born when the [full moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Full_moon "Full moon") was in [Vaisakha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vaisakha "Vaisakha"), or Taurus.[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-grundwedel1901-55) Buddha's birthday is celebrated with the Wesak Festival, or [Vesākha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ves%C4%81kha "Vesākha"), which occurs on the first or second full moon when the Sun is in Taurus.[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-mr2007-56)

In 1990, due to the [precession of the equinoxes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Precession_of_the_equinoxes "Precession of the equinoxes"), the position of the Sun on the first day of summer (June 21) crossed the IAU boundary of Gemini into Taurus.[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-griffith-57) The Sun will slowly move through Taurus at a rate of 1° east every 72 years until approximately 2600 AD, at which point it will be in Aries on the first day of summer[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_].

As of 2008, the Sun appears in the constellation Taurus from May 13 to June 21.[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-comins_kaufman2008-58) In [tropical astrology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tropical_astrology "Tropical astrology"), the Sun is considered to be in the sign [Taurus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(astrology) "Taurus (astrology)") from April 20 to May 20.[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-sharp2005-59)

The [space probe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_probe "Space probe")_[Pioneer 10](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pioneer\_10 "Pioneer 10")_ is moving in the direction of this constellation, though it will not be nearing any of the stars in this constellation for many thousands of years, by which time its batteries will be long dead.[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-NASA20030225-60)

### Solar eclipse of May 29, 1919

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Taurus_(constellation)&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Solar eclipse of May 29, 1919")]

Several stars in the Hyades star cluster, including [Kappa Tauri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kappa_Tauri "Kappa Tauri"), were photographed during the total [solar eclipse of May 29, 1919](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_eclipse_of_May_29,_1919 "Solar eclipse of May 29, 1919"), by the expedition of [Arthur Eddington](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arthur_Eddington "Arthur Eddington") in [Príncipe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pr%C3%ADncipe "Príncipe") and others in [Sobral, Brazil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sobral,_Cear%C3%A1 "Sobral, Ceará"), that [confirmed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity "Tests of general relativity")[Albert Einstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Einstein "Albert Einstein")'s prediction of the [bending of light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_lens "Gravitational lens") around the [Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun "Sun") according to his [general theory of relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_theory_of_relativity "General theory of relativity") which he published in 1915.[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)#cite_note-Eddington1920-61)
