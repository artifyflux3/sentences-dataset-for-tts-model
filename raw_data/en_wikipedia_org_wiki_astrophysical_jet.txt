[![Image 1](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/ESO_Centaurus_A_LABOCA.jpg/250px-ESO_Centaurus_A_LABOCA.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:ESO_Centaurus_A_LABOCA.jpg)

The [starburst galaxy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Starburst_galaxy "Starburst galaxy")[Centaurus A](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centaurus_A "Centaurus A"), with its plasma jets extending over a million [light years](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light-year "Light-year"), is considered as the closest [active](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Active_galactic_nucleus "Active galactic nucleus")[radio galaxy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radio_galaxy "Radio galaxy") to [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth "Earth"). The 870-micron [submillimetre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Submillimetre_astronomy "Submillimetre astronomy") data, from [LABOCA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atacama_Pathfinder_Experiment "Atacama Pathfinder Experiment") on APEX, are shown in orange. [X-ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray "X-ray") data from the [Chandra X-ray Observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chandra_X-ray_Observatory "Chandra X-ray Observatory") are shown in [blue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blue "Blue"). [Visible light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light "Light") data from the [Wide Field Imager](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MPG/ESO_telescope "MPG/ESO telescope") (WFI) on the MPG/ESO 2.2 m telescope located at La Silla, [Chile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chile "Chile"), show the background [stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star "Star") and the galaxy's characteristic [dust lane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dust_lane "Dust lane") in close to "true colour".

An **astrophysical jet** is an [astronomical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy "Astronomy") phenomenon where outflows of [ionised](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ionization "Ionization") matter are emitted as extended beams along the [axis of rotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation "Rotation").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrophysical_jet#cite_note-1) When this greatly accelerated matter in the beam approaches the [speed of light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light "Speed of light"), astrophysical jets become **relativistic jets** as they show effects from [special relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_relativity "Special relativity").

The formation and powering of astrophysical jets are highly complex phenomena that are associated with many types of [high-energy astronomical sources](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-energy_astronomy "High-energy astronomy"). They likely arise from dynamic interactions within [accretion disks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accretion_disk "Accretion disk"), whose active processes are commonly connected with compact central objects such as [black holes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_hole "Black hole"), [neutron stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron_star "Neutron star") or [pulsars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulsar "Pulsar"). One explanation is that tangled [magnetic fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_field "Magnetic field") are organised to aim two diametrically opposing beams away from the central source by angles only several degrees wide (c. > 1%).[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrophysical_jet#cite_note-Kundt-2) Jets may also be influenced by a [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity") effect known as [frame-dragging](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frame-dragging "Frame-dragging").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrophysical_jet#cite_note-3)

Most of the largest and most active jets are created by [supermassive black holes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supermassive_black_hole "Supermassive black hole") (SMBH) in the centre of [active galaxies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Active_galactic_nucleus "Active galactic nucleus") such as [quasars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasar "Quasar") and radio galaxies or within galaxy clusters.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrophysical_jet#cite_note-4) Such jets can exceed millions of [parsecs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parsec "Parsec") in length.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrophysical_jet#cite_note-Kundt-2) Other astronomical objects that contain jets include [cataclysmic variable stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cataclysmic_variable_star "Cataclysmic variable star"), [X-ray binaries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray_binary "X-ray binary") and [gamma-ray bursts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamma-ray_burst "Gamma-ray burst") (GRB). Jets on a much smaller scale (~parsecs) may be found in star forming regions, including [T Tauri stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/T_Tauri_star "T Tauri star") and [Herbig–Haro objects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herbig%E2%80%93Haro_object "Herbig–Haro object"); these objects are partially formed by the interaction of jets with the [interstellar medium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_medium "Interstellar medium"). [Bipolar outflows](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bipolar_outflow "Bipolar outflow") may also be associated with [protostars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protostar "Protostar"),[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrophysical_jet#cite_note-5) or with evolved [post-AGB](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Post-AGB "Post-AGB") stars, [planetary nebulae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula "Planetary nebula") and [bipolar nebulae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bipolar_nebula "Bipolar nebula").

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/M87_jet.jpg/250px-M87_jet.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:M87_jet.jpg)

[Elliptical galaxy M87](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptical_galaxy_M87 "Elliptical galaxy M87") emitting a relativistic jet, as seen by the [Hubble Space Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope "Hubble Space Telescope")

Relativistic jets are beams of ionised matter accelerated close to the speed of light. Most have been observationally associated with central black holes of some [active galaxies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Active_galactic_nucleus "Active galactic nucleus"), [radio galaxies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radio_galaxy "Radio galaxy") or [quasars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasar "Quasar"), and also by galactic [stellar black holes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_black_hole "Stellar black hole"), [neutron stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron_star "Neutron star") or [pulsars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulsar "Pulsar"). Beam lengths may extend between several thousand,[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrophysical_jet#cite_note-6) hundreds of thousands[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrophysical_jet#cite_note-7) or millions of parsecs.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrophysical_jet#cite_note-Kundt-2) Jet velocities when approaching the speed of light show significant effects of the [special theory of relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_relativity "Special relativity"); for example, [relativistic beaming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_beaming "Relativistic beaming") that changes the apparent beam brightness.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrophysical_jet#cite_note-8)

Massive central black holes in galaxies have the most powerful jets, but their structure and behaviours are similar to those of smaller galactic [neutron stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron_stars "Neutron stars") and black holes. These SMBH systems are often called [microquasars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microquasar "Microquasar") and show a large range of velocities. [SS 433](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SS_433 "SS 433") jet, for example, has a mean velocity of 0.26[c](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light "Speed of light").[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrophysical_jet#cite_note-9) Relativistic jet formation may also explain observed gamma-ray bursts, which have the most relativistic jets known, being [ultrarelativistic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultrarelativistic_limit "Ultrarelativistic limit").[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrophysical_jet#cite_note-10)

Mechanisms behind the composition of jets remain uncertain,[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrophysical_jet#cite_note-11) though some studies favour models where jets are composed of an electrically neutral mixture of [nuclei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_nucleus "Atomic nucleus"), [electrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron "Electron"), and [positrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positron "Positron"), while others are consistent with jets composed of positron–electron plasma.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrophysical_jet#cite_note-12)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrophysical_jet#cite_note-brandeis.edu-13)[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrophysical_jet#cite_note-auto-14) Trace nuclei swept up in a relativistic positron–electron jet would be expected to have extremely high energy, as these heavier nuclei should attain velocity equal to the positron and electron velocity.

Rotation as possible energy source
----------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Astrophysical_jet&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Rotation as possible energy source")]

Because of the enormous amount of energy needed to launch a relativistic jet, some jets are possibly powered by spinning [black holes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_hole "Black hole"). However, the frequency of high-energy astrophysical sources with jets suggests combinations of different mechanisms indirectly identified with the energy within the associated accretion disk and X-ray emissions from the generating source. Two early theories have been used to explain how energy can be transferred from a black hole into an astrophysical jet:

*   [Blandford–Znajek process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blandford%E2%80%93Znajek_process "Blandford–Znajek process").[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrophysical_jet#cite_note-15) This theory explains the extraction of energy from magnetic fields around an accretion disk, which are dragged and twisted by the spin of the black hole. Relativistic material is then feasibly launched by the tightening of the field lines.
*   [Penrose mechanism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Penrose_process "Penrose process").[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrophysical_jet#cite_note-16) Here energy is extracted from a [rotating black hole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotating_black_hole "Rotating black hole") by [frame dragging](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frame_dragging "Frame dragging"), which was later theoretically proven by [Reva Kay Williams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reva_Kay_Williams "Reva Kay Williams") to be able to extract [relativistic particle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_particle "Relativistic particle") energy and momentum,[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrophysical_jet#cite_note-17) and subsequently shown to be a possible mechanism for jet formation.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrophysical_jet#cite_note-18) This effect includes using general relativistic [gravitomagnetism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitomagnetism "Gravitomagnetism").

Relativistic jets from neutron stars
------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Astrophysical_jet&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Relativistic jets from neutron stars")]

[![Image 3](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Lighthouse_nebula.jpg/250px-Lighthouse_nebula.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Lighthouse_nebula.jpg)

The pulsar IGR J11014-6103 with supernova remnant origin, nebula and jet

Jets may also be observed from spinning neutron stars. An example is pulsar [IGR J11014-6103](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IGR_J11014-6103 "IGR J11014-6103"), which has the largest jet so far observed in the [Milky Way](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milky_Way "Milky Way"), and whose velocity is estimated at 80% the speed of light (0.8 _c_). X-ray observations have been obtained, but there is no detected radio signature nor accretion disk.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrophysical_jet#cite_note-19)[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrophysical_jet#cite_note-20) Initially, this pulsar was presumed to be rapidly spinning, but later measurements indicate the spin rate is only 15.9 Hz.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrophysical_jet#cite_note-21)[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrophysical_jet#cite_note-22) Such a slow spin rate and lack of accretion material suggest the jet is neither rotation nor accretion powered, though it appears aligned with the pulsar rotation axis and perpendicular to the pulsar's true motion.
