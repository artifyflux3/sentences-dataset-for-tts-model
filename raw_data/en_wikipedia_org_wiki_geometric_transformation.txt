From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), a **geometric transformation** is any [bijection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bijection "Bijection") of a [set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_(mathematics) "Set (mathematics)") to itself (or to another such set) with some salient [geometrical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry") underpinning, such as preserving distances, [angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle "Angle"), or ratios (scale). More specifically, it is a [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)") whose [domain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_of_a_function "Domain of a function") and [range](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Range_of_a_function "Range of a function") are sets of [points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_(geometry) "Point (geometry)") – most often a [real coordinate space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_coordinate_space "Real coordinate space"), ![Image 1: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e150115ab9f63023215109595b76686a1ff890fd) or ![Image 2: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5) – such that the function is bijective so that its [inverse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverse_function "Inverse function") exists.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_transformation#cite_note-Usiskin_2003_p._84-1) The study of geometry may be approached by the study of these transformations, such as in [transformation geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transformation_geometry "Transformation geometry").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_transformation#cite_note-2)

Geometric transformations can be classified by the dimension of their operand sets (thus distinguishing between, say, planar transformations and spatial transformations). They can also be classified according to the properties they preserve:

*   [Displacements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Displacement_(vector) "Displacement (vector)") preserve [distances](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distance "Distance") and [oriented angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle "Angle") (e.g., [translations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Translation_(geometry) "Translation (geometry)"));[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_transformation#cite_note-3)
*   [Isometries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isometry "Isometry") preserve angles and distances (e.g., [Euclidean transformations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_transformation "Euclidean transformation"));[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_transformation#cite_note-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_transformation#cite_note-Berger-5)
*   [Similarities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Similarity_(geometry) "Similarity (geometry)") preserve angles and ratios between distances (e.g., resizing);[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_transformation#cite_note-6)
*   [Affine transformations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_transformation "Affine transformation") preserve [parallelism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_(geometry) "Parallel (geometry)") (e.g., [scaling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scaling_(geometry) "Scaling (geometry)"), [shear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shear_mapping "Shear mapping"));[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_transformation#cite_note-Berger-5)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_transformation#cite_note-7)
*   [Projective transformations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_transformation "Projective transformation") preserve [collinearity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collinearity "Collinearity");[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_transformation#cite_note-Wilkinson-8)

Each of these classes contains the previous one.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_transformation#cite_note-Wilkinson-8)

*   [Möbius transformations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M%C3%B6bius_transformation "Möbius transformation") using complex coordinates on the plane (as well as [circle inversion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle_inversion "Circle inversion")) preserve the set of all lines and circles, but may interchange lines and circles.

*   [Diffeomorphisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffeomorphism "Diffeomorphism") (bidifferentiable transformations) are the transformations that are affine in the first order; they contain the preceding ones as special cases, and can be further refined.

Transformations of the same type form [groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_(mathematics) "Group (mathematics)") that may be sub-groups of other transformation groups.

Opposite group actions
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Geometric_transformation&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Opposite group actions")]

Many geometric transformations are expressed with linear algebra. The bijective linear transformations are elements of a [general linear group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_linear_group "General linear group"). The [linear transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_transformation "Linear transformation")_A_ is non-singular. For a [row vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Row_vector "Row vector")_v_, the [matrix product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_product "Matrix product")_vA_ gives another row vector _w_ = _vA_.

The [transpose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transpose "Transpose") of a row vector _v_ is a column vector _v_ T, and the transpose of the above equality is ![Image 13: {\displaystyle w^{T}=(vA)^{T}=A^{T}v^{T}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c6dacece82e5ce232dee41305165d709f8b09d3d) Here _A_ T provides a left action on column vectors.

In transformation geometry there are [compositions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Composition_of_relations "Composition of relations")_AB_. Starting with a row vector _v_, the right action of the composed transformation is _w_ = _vAB_. After transposition,

![Image 14: {\displaystyle w^{T}=(vAB)^{T}=(AB)^{T}v^{T}=B^{T}A^{T}v^{T}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4ec14a0f8b37041706737aaa9646badfd7414a14)
Thus for _AB_ the associated left [group action](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_action "Group action") is ![Image 15: {\displaystyle B^{T}A^{T}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c5517ef36ce6a5fd93190d40c31690b92c2367f8) In the study of [opposite groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Opposite_group "Opposite group"), the distinction is made between opposite group actions because [commutative groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_group "Commutative group") are the only groups for which these opposites are equal.

Active and passive transformations
----------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Geometric_transformation&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Active and passive transformations")]

[![Image 16](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/PassiveActive.JPG/330px-PassiveActive.JPG)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:PassiveActive.JPG)

In the active transformation (left), a point P is transformed to point P′ by rotating clockwise by [angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle "Angle")θ about the [origin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Origin_(mathematics) "Origin (mathematics)") of a fixed coordinate system. In the passive transformation (right), point P stays fixed, while the coordinate system rotates counterclockwise by an angle θ about its origin. The coordinates of P′ after the active transformation relative to the original coordinate system are the same as the coordinates of P relative to the rotated coordinate system.

Geometric transformations can be distinguished into two types: [active](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Active_and_passive_transformation "Active and passive transformation") or alibi transformations which change the physical position of a set of [points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_(geometry) "Point (geometry)") relative to a fixed [frame of reference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frame_of_reference "Frame of reference") or [coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_system "Coordinate system") (_[alibi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alibi "Alibi")_ meaning "being somewhere else at the same time"); and passive or alias transformations which leave points fixed but change the frame of reference or coordinate system relative to which they are described (_[alias](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudonym "Pseudonym")_ meaning "going under a different name").[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_transformation#cite_note-10)[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_transformation#cite_note-Active_and_passive_transformation_Davidson-11) By _transformation_, [mathematicians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematician "Mathematician") usually refer to active transformations, while [physicists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physicist "Physicist") and [engineers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineer "Engineer") could mean either.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

For instance, active transformations are useful to describe successive positions of a [rigid body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rigid_body "Rigid body"). On the other hand, passive transformations may be useful in human motion analysis to observe the motion of the [tibia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tibia "Tibia") relative to the [femur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Femur "Femur"), that is, its motion relative to a (_local_) coordinate system which moves together with the femur, rather than a (_global_) coordinate system which is fixed to the floor.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_transformation#cite_note-Active_and_passive_transformation_Davidson-11)

In [three-dimensional Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_Euclidean_space "Three-dimensional Euclidean space"), any [proper rigid transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rigid_transformation "Rigid transformation"), whether active or passive, can be represented as a [screw displacement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Screw_displacement "Screw displacement"), the composition of a [translation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Translation_(geometry) "Translation (geometry)") along an axis and a [rotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation_(mathematics) "Rotation (mathematics)") about that axis.

The terms _active transformation_ and _passive transformation_ were first introduced in 1957 by [Valentine Bargmann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Valentine_Bargmann "Valentine Bargmann") for describing [Lorentz transformations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentz_transformations "Lorentz transformations") in [special relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_relativity "Special relativity").[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_transformation#cite_note-12)
