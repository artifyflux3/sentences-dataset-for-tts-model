In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), a **Coxeter group**, named after [H. S. M. Coxeter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harold_Scott_MacDonald_Coxeter "Harold Scott MacDonald Coxeter"), is an [abstract group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_(mathematics) "Group (mathematics)") that admits a [formal description](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Presentation_of_a_group "Presentation of a group") in terms of [reflections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflection_(mathematics) "Reflection (mathematics)") (or [kaleidoscopic mirrors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaleidoscope "Kaleidoscope")). Indeed, the finite Coxeter groups are precisely the finite Euclidean [reflection groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflection_group "Reflection group"); for example, the [symmetry group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry_group "Symmetry group") of each [regular polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polyhedron "Regular polyhedron") is a finite Coxeter group. However, not all Coxeter groups are finite, and not all can be described in terms of [symmetries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry_in_mathematics "Symmetry in mathematics") and Euclidean reflections. Coxeter groups were introduced in 1934 as abstractions of reflection groups,[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coxeter_group#cite_note-Coxeter1934-1) and finite Coxeter groups were classified in 1935.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coxeter_group#cite_note-Coxeter1935-2)

Coxeter groups find applications in many areas of mathematics. Examples of finite Coxeter groups include the symmetry groups of [regular polytopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polytope "Regular polytope"), and the [Weyl groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weyl_group "Weyl group") of [simple Lie algebras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simple_Lie_algebra "Simple Lie algebra"). Examples of infinite Coxeter groups include the [triangle groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle_group "Triangle group") corresponding to [regular tessellations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tessellation#Overview "Tessellation") of the [Euclidean plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_plane "Euclidean plane") and the [hyperbolic plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_space "Hyperbolic space"), and the Weyl groups of infinite-dimensional [Kac–Moody algebras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kac%E2%80%93Moody_algebra "Kac–Moody algebra").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coxeter_group#cite_note-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coxeter_group#cite_note-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coxeter_group#cite_note-5)

Formally, a **Coxeter group** can be defined as a group with the [presentation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Presentation_of_a_group "Presentation of a group")

![Image 1: {\displaystyle \left\langle r_{1},r_{2},\ldots ,r_{n}\mid (r_{i}r_{j})^{m_{ij}}=1\right\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d351823dfc3cc3241b16731504526e98d62bea2c)
where ![Image 2: {\displaystyle m_{ii}=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a25045ba17b4faf1148da03d8ee08c1ef73f8c67) and ![Image 3: {\displaystyle m_{ij}=m_{ji}\geq 2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b6922e122a27a1b1c376b77ad274fe5e2f8eadb4) is either an integer or ![Image 4: {\displaystyle \infty }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c26c105004f30c27aa7c2a9c601550a4183b1f21) for ![Image 5: {\displaystyle i\neq j}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d95aeb406bb427ac96806bc00c30c91d31b858be). Here, the condition ![Image 6: {\displaystyle m_{ij}=\infty }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8f2915283230fb7eb2940f0a8edf234e95fbb322) means that no relation of the form ![Image 7: {\displaystyle (r_{i}r_{j})^{m}=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4fa9dd12bc61d3c999854fa4f228c73aaf95eb5e) for any integer ![Image 8: {\displaystyle m\geq 2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eca2e437e89ef4565a87f1a6d90ed37eef1d8ce3) should be imposed.

The pair ![Image 9: {\displaystyle (W,S)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/97d1926ec014fa8130b3e3a3b99fe6c8467db607) where ![Image 10: {\displaystyle W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54a9c4c547f4d6111f81946cad242b18298d70b7) is a Coxeter group with generators ![Image 11: {\displaystyle S=\{r_{1},\dots ,r_{n}\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7f50983dbed58bf9635fb28f5993f4e6abbdc6a9) is called a **Coxeter system**. Note that in general ![Image 12: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2) is _not_ uniquely determined by ![Image 13: {\displaystyle W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54a9c4c547f4d6111f81946cad242b18298d70b7). For example, the Coxeter groups of type ![Image 14: {\displaystyle B_{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/db17e43bc3f2416503df52a301a7d2779ab3a8ac) and ![Image 15: {\displaystyle A_{1}\times A_{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cf688bcc32a590bb92c042bee7ee3ee21dde40ca) are isomorphic but the Coxeter systems are not equivalent, since the former has 3 generators and the latter has 1 + 3 = 4 generators (see below for an explanation of this notation).

A number of conclusions can be drawn immediately from the above definition.

![Image 16: {\displaystyle xx=yy=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0b7eced76e52f13b2d79992fe49082ce9a1fd920),together with ![Image 17: {\displaystyle xyxy=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/48b8a623f3b4a4431edb5f5c217a668241cca542)implies that ![Image 18: {\displaystyle xy=x(xyxy)y=(xx)yx(yy)=yx}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0c963df6ac501fb497499c400f88c7a43153d28e).Alternatively, since the generators are involutions, ![Image 19: {\displaystyle r_{i}=r_{i}^{-1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/049942b982083832d6dcd8c743893573b1d63bd4), so ![Image 20: {\displaystyle 1=(r_{i}r_{j})^{2}=r_{i}r_{j}r_{i}r_{j}=r_{i}r_{j}r_{i}^{-1}r_{j}^{-1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6c73c3e7319062411f71986b7163726199262e41). That is to say, the [commutator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutator "Commutator") of ![Image 21: {\displaystyle r_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a0b6d651eaf432dbf1f106021c8bb499ae83fd1f) and ![Image 22: {\displaystyle r_{j}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a7d3b724fc249d56f0d550b92f6891380467e350) is equal to 1, or equivalently that ![Image 23: {\displaystyle r_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a0b6d651eaf432dbf1f106021c8bb499ae83fd1f) and ![Image 24: {\displaystyle r_{j}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a7d3b724fc249d56f0d550b92f6891380467e350) commute.
The reason that ![Image 25: {\displaystyle m_{ij}=m_{ji}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/540501dde83cd4b74d24f29e8c0a5e614a63e794) for ![Image 26: {\displaystyle i\neq j}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d95aeb406bb427ac96806bc00c30c91d31b858be) is stipulated in the definition is that

together with

![Image 28: {\displaystyle (xy)^{m}=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/11b20220745f82cafc5040e2532b93651b7db2a6)
already implies that

![Image 29: {\displaystyle (yx)^{m}=(yx)^{m}yy=y(xy)^{m}y=yy=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a4a8d21e5fc15a8947eb08f413f0b287cecb6dec).
An alternative proof of this implication is the observation that ![Image 30: {\displaystyle (xy)^{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6a65f085c84cfc0f8b26fccc79f1875024c2e5c4) and ![Image 31: {\displaystyle (yx)^{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/091007b75a680b807e3dc58ad18f24f5d3459fb3) are [conjugates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conjugate_elements "Conjugate elements"): indeed ![Image 32: {\displaystyle y(xy)^{k}y^{-1}=(yx)^{k}yy^{-1}=(yx)^{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e491950e90644a9d89f6dd2c5c8b057c8ca3d5b2).

### Coxeter matrix and Schläfli matrix

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Coxeter_group&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Coxeter matrix and Schläfli matrix")]

The **Coxeter matrix** is the ![Image 33: {\displaystyle n\times n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/59d2b4cb72e304526cf5b5887147729ea259da78)[symmetric matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetric_matrix "Symmetric matrix") with entries ![Image 34: {\displaystyle m_{ij}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cd6f1bb2d6548dca472922bcbcb77e7ad3a5b4df). Indeed, every symmetric matrix with diagonal entries exclusively 1 and nondiagonal entries in the set ![Image 35: {\displaystyle \{2,3,\ldots \}\cup \{\infty \}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/219da687d3bf4a006ef041a596e058f3183fd0be) is a Coxeter matrix.

The Coxeter matrix can be conveniently encoded by a **[Coxeter diagram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coxeter%E2%80%93Dynkin_diagram "Coxeter–Dynkin diagram")**, as per the following rules.

In particular, two generators [commute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_operation "Commutative operation") if and only if they are not joined by an edge. Furthermore, if a Coxeter graph has two or more [connected components](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connected_component_(graph_theory) "Connected component (graph theory)"), the associated group is the [direct product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direct_product_of_groups "Direct product of groups") of the groups associated to the individual components. Thus the [disjoint union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disjoint_union "Disjoint union") of Coxeter graphs yields a [direct product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direct_product_of_groups "Direct product of groups") of Coxeter groups.

The Coxeter matrix, ![Image 36: {\displaystyle M_{ij}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6136d919b3f10cc58cc023e05a5c36b2f44e111f), is related to the ![Image 37: {\displaystyle n\times n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/59d2b4cb72e304526cf5b5887147729ea259da78)[Schläfli matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schl%C3%A4fli_matrix "Schläfli matrix")![Image 38: {\displaystyle C}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4fc55753007cd3c18576f7933f6f089196732029) with entries ![Image 39: {\displaystyle C_{ij}=-2\cos(\pi /M_{ij})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cad7d9e15ba154522066dae8fc2a7673ea436643), but the elements are modified, being proportional to the [dot product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dot_product "Dot product") of the pairwise generators. The Schläfli matrix is useful because its [eigenvalues](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eigenvalues "Eigenvalues") determine whether the Coxeter group is of _finite type_ (all positive), _affine type_ (all non-negative, at least one zero), or _indefinite type_ (otherwise). The indefinite type is sometimes further subdivided, e.g. into hyperbolic and other Coxeter groups. However, there are multiple non-equivalent definitions for hyperbolic Coxeter groups.

Abstraction of reflection groups
--------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Coxeter_group&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Abstraction of reflection groups")]

Coxeter groups are an abstraction of reflection groups. Coxeter groups are _abstract_ groups, in the sense of being given via a presentation. On the other hand, reflection groups are _concrete_, in the sense that each of its elements is the composite of finitely many geometric reflections about linear hyperplanes in some euclidean space. Technically, a reflection group is a subgroup of a [linear group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_group "Linear group") (or various generalizations) generated by orthogonal matrices of determinant -1. Each generator of a Coxeter group has order 2, which abstracts the geometric fact that performing a reflection twice is the identity. Each relation of the form ![Image 108: {\displaystyle (r_{i}r_{j})^{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/99e1635073d97a8251a726413226e5fb13b9af2b), corresponding to the geometric fact that, given two [hyperplanes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperplane "Hyperplane") meeting at an angle of ![Image 109: {\displaystyle \pi /k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf1e285b3086bae7c756f1d2a950dd26c2e607aa), the composite of the two reflections about these hyperplanes is a rotation by ![Image 110: {\displaystyle 2\pi /k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6f5d6e857431016a18f54a242f5e20b080f86835), which has order _k_.

In this way, every reflection group may be presented as a Coxeter group.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coxeter_group#cite_note-Coxeter1934-1) The converse is partially true: every finite Coxeter group admits a faithful [representation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_representation "Linear representation") as a finite reflection group of some Euclidean space.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coxeter_group#cite_note-Coxeter1935-2) However, not every infinite Coxeter group admits a representation as a reflection group.

Finite Coxeter groups have been classified.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coxeter_group#cite_note-Coxeter1935-2)

Finite Coxeter groups
---------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Coxeter_group&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Finite Coxeter groups")]

[![Image 111](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Finite_coxeter.svg/500px-Finite_coxeter.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Finite_coxeter.svg)

Coxeter graphs of the irreducible finite Coxeter groups

Finite Coxeter groups are classified in terms of their [Coxeter diagrams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coxeter%E2%80%93Dynkin_diagram "Coxeter–Dynkin diagram").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coxeter_group#cite_note-Coxeter1935-2)

The finite Coxeter groups with connected Coxeter diagrams consist of three one-parameter families of increasing dimension (![Image 112: {\displaystyle A_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/730f6906700685b6d52f3958b1c2ae659d2d97d2) for ![Image 113: {\displaystyle n\geq 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d8ce9ce38d06f6bf5a3fe063118c09c2b6202bfe), ![Image 114: {\displaystyle B_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2f568bf6d34e97b9fdda0dc7e276d6c4501d2045) for ![Image 115: {\displaystyle n\geq 2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e6bf67f9d06ca3af619657f8d20ee1322da77174), and ![Image 116: {\displaystyle D_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0fe03857347bf517e7fbda4085b0dafd6018cf18) for ![Image 117: {\displaystyle n\geq 4}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/25010fec4b0f68f1b46f49d14917d962acca0b16)), a one-parameter family of dimension two (![Image 118: {\displaystyle I_{2}(p)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4e74418e1e0e3e1820b6e2a62ac3893905016e76) for ![Image 119: {\displaystyle p\geq 5}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0ea5cb36d99c0607a6bd621df42ac05d9e44a0f7)), and six [exceptional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exceptional_object "Exceptional object") groups (![Image 120: {\displaystyle E_{6},E_{7},E_{8},F_{4},H_{3},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b14b7a7aaa5a61b10920edef783a9bfefda19b5) and ![Image 121: {\displaystyle H_{4}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf998dd6a6fe8dd490c0e468d118be09b8a11995)). Every finite Coxeter group is the [direct product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direct_product "Direct product") of finitely many of these irreducible groups.[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coxeter_group#cite_note-6)

Many, but not all of these, are Weyl groups, and every Weyl group can be realized as a Coxeter group. The Weyl groups are the families ![Image 122: {\displaystyle A_{n},B_{n},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/491da91dc427d12cd7c6b352755413608cc9a267) and ![Image 123: {\displaystyle D_{n},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f791ccfe467d310fef894c66d25763afacfb438e) and the exceptions ![Image 124: {\displaystyle E_{6},E_{7},E_{8},F_{4},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f4babce22c2055adce853e1d8713eb3776ba9845) and ![Image 125: {\displaystyle I_{2}(6),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a32b6507d87ed7095fd9400bc35ca600f4b2280c) denoted in Weyl group notation as ![Image 126: {\displaystyle G_{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b20fd4e1b6ab2dfc3d03cfde2300e597cf552271)

The non-Weyl ones are the exceptions ![Image 127: {\displaystyle H_{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f4bc9dc729e1d53cb3651e618e87861c26656f6d) and ![Image 128: {\displaystyle H_{4},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bedba3ce0a79b34d419d8ccab22336849e91ba38) and those members of the family ![Image 129: {\displaystyle I_{2}(p)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4e74418e1e0e3e1820b6e2a62ac3893905016e76) that are not [exceptionally isomorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exceptional_isomorphism "Exceptional isomorphism") to a Weyl group (namely ![Image 130: {\displaystyle I_{2}(3)\cong A_{2},I_{2}(4)\cong B_{2},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8c7069a0d6ad660b4e7b1da4726064ccea792636) and ![Image 131: {\displaystyle I_{2}(6)\cong G_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3fc57d7b6bc000be45f2f4c07161faaa4e14824e)).

This can be proven by comparing the restrictions on (undirected) [Dynkin diagrams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynkin_diagram "Dynkin diagram") with the restrictions on Coxeter diagrams of finite groups: formally, the Coxeter graph can be obtained from the Dynkin diagram by discarding the direction of the edges, and replacing every double edge with an edge labelled 4 and every triple edge by an edge labelled 6. Also note that every finitely generated Coxeter group is an [automatic group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Automatic_group "Automatic group").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coxeter_group#cite_note-BrinkAndHowlett-7) Dynkin diagrams have the additional restriction that the only permitted edge labels are 2, 3, 4, and 6, which yields the above. Geometrically, this corresponds to the [crystallographic restriction theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystallographic_restriction_theorem "Crystallographic restriction theorem"), and the fact that excluded polytopes do not fill space or tile the plane – for ![Image 132: {\displaystyle H_{3},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/829c6f0972c011657221215fe4e1ab24e25564a1) the dodecahedron (dually, icosahedron) does not fill space; for ![Image 133: {\displaystyle H_{4},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bedba3ce0a79b34d419d8ccab22336849e91ba38) the 120-cell (dually, 600-cell) does not fill space; for ![Image 134: {\displaystyle I_{2}(p)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4e74418e1e0e3e1820b6e2a62ac3893905016e76) a _p_-gon does not tile the plane except for ![Image 135: {\displaystyle p=3,4,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/01ffa5c4c4d19c96ee72227f60bfc60931a852ee) or ![Image 136: {\displaystyle 6}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/39d81124420a058a7474dfeda48228fb6ee1e253) (the triangular, square, and hexagonal tilings, respectively).

Note further that the (directed) Dynkin diagrams _B n_ and _C n_ give rise to the same Weyl group (hence Coxeter group), because they differ as _directed_ graphs, but agree as _undirected_ graphs – direction matters for root systems but not for the Weyl group; this corresponds to the [hypercube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypercube "Hypercube") and [cross-polytope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross-polytope "Cross-polytope") being different regular polytopes but having the same symmetry group.

### Symmetry groups of regular polytopes

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Coxeter_group&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Symmetry groups of regular polytopes")]

The symmetry group of every regular polytope is a finite Coxeter group. Note that [dual polytopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_polytope "Dual polytope") have the same symmetry group.

There are three series of regular polytopes in all dimensions. The symmetry group of a regular _n_-simplex is the symmetric group _S_ _n_+1, also known as the Coxeter group of type _A n_. The symmetry group of the _n_-[cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube "Cube") and its dual, the _n_-cross-polytope, is _B n_, and is known as the [hyperoctahedral group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperoctahedral_group "Hyperoctahedral group").

The exceptional regular polytopes in dimensions two, three, and four, correspond to other Coxeter groups. In two dimensions, the [dihedral groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihedral_group "Dihedral group"), which are the symmetry groups of [regular polygons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polygon "Regular polygon"), form the series _I_ 2(_p_), for _p_ ≥ 3. In three dimensions, the symmetry group of the regular [dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dodecahedron "Dodecahedron") and its dual, the regular [icosahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Icosahedron "Icosahedron"), is _H_ 3, known as the [full icosahedral group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Full_icosahedral_group "Full icosahedral group"). In four dimensions, there are three exceptional regular polytopes, the [24-cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/24-cell "24-cell"), the [120-cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/120-cell "120-cell"), and the [600-cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/600-cell "600-cell"). The first has symmetry group _F_ 4, while the other two are dual and have symmetry group _H_ 4.

The Coxeter groups of type _D_ _n_, _E_ 6, _E_ 7, and _E_ 8 are the symmetry groups of certain [semiregular polytopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semiregular_polytope "Semiregular polytope").

Affine Coxeter groups
---------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Coxeter_group&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Affine Coxeter groups")]

Coxeter diagrams for the affine Coxeter groups

[![Image 309](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Stiefel_diagram_for_G2.png/250px-Stiefel_diagram_for_G2.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Stiefel_diagram_for_G2.png)

Stiefel diagram for the ![Image 310: {\displaystyle G_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/645011b0c6933a02f5f7d84624f78220d747427e) root system

The **affine Coxeter groups** form a second important series of Coxeter groups. These are not finite themselves, but each contains a [normal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_subgroup "Normal subgroup")[abelian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abelian_group "Abelian group")[subgroup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subgroup "Subgroup") such that the corresponding quotient group is finite. In each case, the quotient group is itself a Coxeter group, and the Coxeter graph of the affine Coxeter group is obtained from the Coxeter graph of the quotient group by adding another vertex and one or two additional edges. For example, for _n_≥2, the graph consisting of _n_+1 vertices in a circle is obtained from _A n_ in this way, and the corresponding Coxeter group is the affine Weyl group of _A n_ (the [affine symmetric group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_symmetric_group "Affine symmetric group")). For _n_=2, this can be pictured as a subgroup of the symmetry group of the standard tiling of the plane by equilateral triangles.

In general, given a root system, one can construct the associated _[Stiefel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eduard\_Stiefel "Eduard Stiefel") diagram_, consisting of the hyperplanes orthogonal to the roots along with certain translates of these hyperplanes. The affine Coxeter group (or affine Weyl group) is then the group generated by the (affine) reflections about all the hyperplanes in the diagram.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coxeter_group#cite_note-11) The Stiefel diagram divides the plane into infinitely many connected components called _alcoves_, and the affine Coxeter group acts freely and transitively on the alcoves, just as the ordinary Weyl group acts freely and transitively on the Weyl chambers. The figure at right illustrates the Stiefel diagram for the ![Image 311: {\displaystyle G_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/645011b0c6933a02f5f7d84624f78220d747427e) root system.

Suppose ![Image 312: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) is an irreducible root system of rank ![Image 313: {\displaystyle r>1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7ea016da928cdc74478976d798c3d307d64cc2fb) and let ![Image 314: {\displaystyle \alpha _{1},\ldots ,\alpha _{r}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/489735de4b4e5d6112555f7a4cba9eef11838b66) be a collection of simple roots. Let, also, ![Image 315: {\displaystyle \alpha _{r+1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c84b9e7fcb8188d4ae1c150d4aaa1f8085c1fcb9) denote the highest root. Then the affine Coxeter group is generated by the ordinary (linear) reflections about the hyperplanes perpendicular to ![Image 316: {\displaystyle \alpha _{1},\ldots ,\alpha _{r}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/489735de4b4e5d6112555f7a4cba9eef11838b66), together with an affine reflection about a translate of the hyperplane perpendicular to ![Image 317: {\displaystyle \alpha _{r+1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c84b9e7fcb8188d4ae1c150d4aaa1f8085c1fcb9). The Coxeter graph for the affine Weyl group is the Coxeter–Dynkin diagram for ![Image 318: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33), together with one additional node associated to ![Image 319: {\displaystyle \alpha _{r+1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c84b9e7fcb8188d4ae1c150d4aaa1f8085c1fcb9). In this case, one alcove of the Stiefel diagram may be obtained by taking the fundamental Weyl chamber and cutting it by a translate of the hyperplane perpendicular to ![Image 320: {\displaystyle \alpha _{r+1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c84b9e7fcb8188d4ae1c150d4aaa1f8085c1fcb9).[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coxeter_group#cite_note-12)

Hyperbolic Coxeter groups
-------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Coxeter_group&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Hyperbolic Coxeter groups")]

There are infinitely many [hyperbolic Coxeter groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coxeter%E2%80%93Dynkin_diagram#Hyperbolic_Coxeter_groups "Coxeter–Dynkin diagram") describing reflection groups in [hyperbolic space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_geometry "Hyperbolic geometry"), notably including the hyperbolic triangle groups.

Irreducible Coxeter groups
--------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Coxeter_group&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Irreducible Coxeter groups")]

A Coxeter group is said to be _irreducible_ if its Coxeter–Dynkin diagram is connected. Every Coxeter group is the [direct product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direct_product_of_groups "Direct product of groups") of the irreducible groups that correspond to the [components](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Component_(graph_theory) "Component (graph theory)") of its Coxeter–Dynkin diagram.

A choice of reflection generators gives rise to a [length function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Length_function "Length function")_ℓ_ on a Coxeter group, namely the minimum number of uses of generators required to express a group element; this is precisely the length in the [word metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Word_metric "Word metric") in the [Cayley graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cayley_graph "Cayley graph"). An expression for _v_ using _ℓ_(_v_) generators is a _reduced word_. For example, the permutation (13) in _S_ 3 has two reduced words, (12)(23)(12) and (23)(12)(23). The function ![Image 459: {\displaystyle v\to (-1)^{\ell (v)}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/335177d12d0d154e8ba9d15b5d2583b0c4b62d73) defines a map ![Image 460: {\displaystyle G\to \{\pm 1\},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e2a65d7fe0f20aca672f4cc8d160886960597fe1) generalizing the [sign map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sign_map "Sign map") for the symmetric group.

Using reduced words one may define three [partial orders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_order "Partial order") on the Coxeter group, the (right) **[weak order](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weak_Bruhat_order "Weak Bruhat order")**, the **absolute order** and the **[Bruhat order](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bruhat_order "Bruhat order")** (named for [François Bruhat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Bruhat "François Bruhat")). An element _v_ exceeds an element _u_ in the Bruhat order if some (or equivalently, any) reduced word for _v_ contains a reduced word for _u_ as a substring, where some letters (in any position) are dropped. In the weak order, _v_≥_u_ if some reduced word for _v_ contains a reduced word for _u_ as an initial segment. Indeed, the word length makes this into a [graded poset](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graded_poset "Graded poset"). The [Hasse diagrams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hasse_diagram "Hasse diagram") corresponding to these orders are objects of study, and are related to the [Cayley graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cayley_graph "Cayley graph") determined by the generators. The absolute order is defined analogously to the weak order, but with generating set/alphabet consisting of all conjugates of the Coxeter generators.

For example, the permutation (1 2 3) in _S_ 3 has only one reduced word, (12)(23), so covers (12) and (23) in the Bruhat order but only covers (12) in the weak order.

Since a Coxeter group ![Image 461: {\displaystyle W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54a9c4c547f4d6111f81946cad242b18298d70b7) is generated by finitely many elements of order 2, its [abelianization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abelianization "Abelianization") is an [elementary abelian 2-group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementary_abelian_group "Elementary abelian group"), i.e., it is isomorphic to the direct sum of several copies of the [cyclic group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclic_group "Cyclic group")![Image 462: {\displaystyle Z_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c98d433ae289ecb2b88f895b407538b0e4183b28). This may be restated in terms of the first [homology group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_cohomology#Group_homology "Group cohomology") of ![Image 463: {\displaystyle W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54a9c4c547f4d6111f81946cad242b18298d70b7).

The [Schur multiplier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schur_multiplier "Schur multiplier")![Image 464: {\displaystyle M(W)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/34cf26a09862e77ca6c3a08ca8096266cef58089), equal to the second homology group of ![Image 465: {\displaystyle W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54a9c4c547f4d6111f81946cad242b18298d70b7), was computed in ([Ihara & Yokonuma 1965](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coxeter_group#CITEREFIharaYokonuma1965)) for finite reflection groups and in ([Yokonuma 1965](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coxeter_group#CITEREFYokonuma1965)) for affine reflection groups, with a more unified account given in ([Howlett 1988](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coxeter_group#CITEREFHowlett1988)). In all cases, the Schur multiplier is also an elementary abelian 2-group. For each infinite family ![Image 466: {\displaystyle \{W_{n}\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a6532c1ebae06f6b252fb513d03f83cf5b3a8022) of finite or affine Weyl groups, the rank of ![Image 467: {\displaystyle M(W_{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2f36973203943ad0a290ba740168b2b37e6f2b62) stabilizes as ![Image 468: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) goes to infinity.
