A reflection through an axis

In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), a **reflection** (also spelled **reflexion**)[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflection_(mathematics)#cite_note-1) is a [mapping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)") from a [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space") to itself that is an [isometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isometry "Isometry") with a [hyperplane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperplane "Hyperplane") as the set of [fixed points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fixed_point_(mathematics) "Fixed point (mathematics)"); this set is called the [axis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axis_of_symmetry "Axis of symmetry") (in dimension 2) or [plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_(mathematics) "Plane (mathematics)") (in dimension 3) of reflection. The image of a figure by a reflection is its [mirror image](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mirror_image "Mirror image") in the axis or plane of reflection. For example the mirror image of the small Latin letter **p** for a reflection with respect to a [vertical axis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertical_axis "Vertical axis") (a _vertical reflection_) would look like **q**. Its image by reflection in a [horizontal axis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horizontal_axis "Horizontal axis") (a _horizontal reflection_) would look like **b**. A reflection is an [involution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Involution_(mathematics) "Involution (mathematics)"): when applied twice in succession, every point returns to its original location, and every geometrical object is restored to its original state.

The term _reflection_ is sometimes used for a larger class of mappings from a Euclidean space to itself, namely the non-identity isometries that are involutions. The set of fixed points (the "mirror") of such an isometry is an [affine subspace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_subspace "Affine subspace"), but is possibly smaller than a hyperplane. For instance a [reflection through a point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_reflection "Point reflection") is an involutive isometry with just one fixed point; the image of the letter **p** under it would look like a **d**. This operation is also known as a [central inversion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_reflection "Point reflection") ([Coxeter 1969](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflection_(mathematics)#CITEREFCoxeter1969), §7.2), and exhibits Euclidean space as a [symmetric space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetric_space "Symmetric space"). In a [Euclidean vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_vector_space "Euclidean vector space"), the reflection in the point situated at the origin is the same as vector negation. Other examples include reflections in a line in three-dimensional space. Typically, however, unqualified use of the term "reflection" means reflection in a [hyperplane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperplane "Hyperplane").

Some mathematicians use "**flip**" as a synonym for "reflection".[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflection_(mathematics)#cite_note-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflection_(mathematics)#cite_note-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflection_(mathematics)#cite_note-4)

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Perpendicular-construction.svg/250px-Perpendicular-construction.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Perpendicular-construction.svg)

Point Q is the reflection of point P through the line AB.

In a plane (or, respectively, 3-dimensional) geometry, to find the reflection of a point drop a [perpendicular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular "Perpendicular") from the point to the line (plane) used for reflection, and extend it the same distance on the other side. To find the reflection of a figure, reflect each point in the figure.

To reflect point P through the line AB using [compass and straightedge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compass_and_straightedge "Compass and straightedge"), proceed as follows (see figure):

*   Step 1 (red): construct a [circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle "Circle") with center at P and some fixed radius _r_ to create points A′ and B′ on the line AB, which will be [equidistant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equidistant "Equidistant") from P.
*   Step 2 (green): construct circles centered at A′ and B′ having radius _r_. P and Q will be the points of intersection of these two circles.

Point Q is then the reflection of point P through line AB.

The [matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_(mathematics) "Matrix (mathematics)") for a reflection is [orthogonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_matrix "Orthogonal matrix") with [determinant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Determinant "Determinant") −1 and [eigenvalues](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eigenvalue "Eigenvalue") −1, 1, 1, ..., 1. The product of two such matrices is a special orthogonal matrix that represents a rotation. Every [rotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation_(mathematics) "Rotation (mathematics)") is the result of reflecting in an even number of reflections in hyperplanes through the origin, and every [improper rotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Improper_rotation "Improper rotation") is the result of reflecting in an odd number. Thus reflections generate the [orthogonal group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_group "Orthogonal group"), and this result is known as the [Cartan–Dieudonné theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartan%E2%80%93Dieudonn%C3%A9_theorem "Cartan–Dieudonné theorem").

Similarly the [Euclidean group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_group "Euclidean group"), which consists of all isometries of Euclidean space, is generated by reflections in affine hyperplanes. In general, a [group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_(mathematics) "Group (mathematics)") generated by reflections in affine hyperplanes is known as a [reflection group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflection_group "Reflection group"). The [finite groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_group "Finite group") generated in this way are examples of [Coxeter groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coxeter_group "Coxeter group").

Reflection across a line in the plane
-------------------------------------
