In the [mathematical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics") field of [differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry"), the **exterior covariant derivative** is an extension of the notion of [exterior derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_derivative "Exterior derivative") to the setting of a differentiable [principal bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principal_bundle "Principal bundle") or [vector bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_bundle "Vector bundle") with a [connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connection_(principal_bundle) "Connection (principal bundle)").

Let _G_ be a [Lie group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_group "Lie group") and _P_ → _M_ be a [principal _G_-bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principal_bundle "Principal bundle") on a [smooth manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smooth_manifold "Smooth manifold")_M_. Suppose there is a [connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connection_(principal_bundle) "Connection (principal bundle)") on _P_; this yields a natural direct sum decomposition ![Image 1: {\displaystyle T_{u}P=H_{u}\oplus V_{u}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7a0755fd440cb02665049d437abdcd5bd89ff18b) of each tangent space into the [horizontal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horizontal_subspace "Horizontal subspace") and [vertical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertical_subspace "Vertical subspace") subspaces. Let ![Image 2: {\displaystyle h:T_{u}P\to H_{u}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/70e817cd8f352cc282749587673bfafc2e1b7ef2) be the projection to the horizontal subspace.

If _ϕ_ is a [_k_-form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector-valued_form "Vector-valued form") on _P_ with values in a vector space _V_, then its exterior covariant derivative _Dϕ_ is a form defined by

![Image 3: {\displaystyle D\phi (v_{0},v_{1},\dots ,v_{k})=d\phi (hv_{0},hv_{1},\dots ,hv_{k})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/51782987376021d6a8b96018eb80e3de0d13184a)
where _v_ _i_ are tangent vectors to _P_ at _u_.

Suppose that _ρ_: _G_ → GL(_V_) is a [representation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Representation_of_a_Lie_group "Representation of a Lie group") of _G_ on a vector space _V_. If _ϕ_ is [equivariant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equivariant "Equivariant") in the sense that

![Image 4: {\displaystyle R_{g}^{*}\phi =\rho (g)^{-1}\phi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/83517ca3012f9ea7ed8fca106b86254ac28c9d30)
where ![Image 5: {\displaystyle R_{g}(u)=ug}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8956c55dd14edf2ef61c89d2fec55b24f946ffe3), then _Dϕ_ is a [tensorial (_k_ + 1)-form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensorial_form "Tensorial form") on _P_ of the type _ρ_: it is equivariant and horizontal (a form _ψ_ is horizontal if _ψ_(_v_ 0, ..., _v_ k) = _ψ_(_hv_ 0, ..., _hv_ _k_).)

By [abuse of notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abuse_of_notation "Abuse of notation"), the differential of _ρ_ at the identity element may again be denoted by _ρ_:

![Image 6: {\displaystyle \rho :{\mathfrak {g}}\to {\mathfrak {gl}}(V).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf3ce6caf6fef15531d6fdb15f0102eeb0bc7ca2)
Let ![Image 7: {\displaystyle \omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/48eff443f9de7a985bb94ca3bde20813ea737be8) be the [connection one-form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connection_one-form "Connection one-form") and ![Image 8: {\displaystyle \rho (\omega )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2ec4310990330c3bc93887ae6dc648d917f36b87) the representation of the connection in ![Image 9: {\displaystyle {\mathfrak {gl}}(V).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/52ebef9855c163cc8abc33481c21a7f860e29ffc) That is, ![Image 10: {\displaystyle \rho (\omega )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2ec4310990330c3bc93887ae6dc648d917f36b87) is a [![Image 11: {\displaystyle {\mathfrak {gl}}(V)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9ff2fd2cad20f84f28573b770667f3475816df68)-valued form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_algebra-valued_differential_form#operations "Lie algebra-valued differential form"), vanishing on the horizontal subspace. If _ϕ_ is a tensorial _k_-form of type _ρ_, then

![Image 12: {\displaystyle D\phi =d\phi +\rho (\omega )\cdot \phi ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c104dd0440ec4dcca02370f423a8fc86ae71b24f)[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_covariant_derivative#cite_note-1)
where, following the notation in _[Lie algebra-valued differential form §Operations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie\_algebra-valued\_differential\_form#Operations "Lie algebra-valued differential form")_, we wrote

![Image 13: {\displaystyle (\rho (\omega )\cdot \phi )(v_{1},\dots ,v_{k+1})={1 \over (1+k)!}\sum _{\sigma }\operatorname {sgn} (\sigma )\rho (\omega (v_{\sigma (1)}))\phi (v_{\sigma (2)},\dots ,v_{\sigma (k+1)}).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8a198b1251ba6b38520e1bd8cd323f3f38ebfc4a)
Unlike the usual [exterior derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_derivative "Exterior derivative"), which squares to 0, the exterior covariant derivative does not. In general, one has, for a tensorial zero-form _ϕ_,

![Image 14: {\displaystyle D^{2}\phi =F\cdot \phi .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/40185b924fe1bf6f7779066f0e441f5c67f11d7e)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_covariant_derivative#cite_note-2)
where _F_ = _ρ_(Ω) is the representation[_[clarification needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Please\_clarify "Wikipedia:Please clarify")_] in ![Image 15: {\displaystyle {\mathfrak {gl}}(V)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9ff2fd2cad20f84f28573b770667f3475816df68) of the [curvature two-form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvature_form "Curvature form") Ω. The form F is sometimes referred to as the [field strength tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_strength_tensor "Field strength tensor"), in analogy to the role it plays in [electromagnetism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetism "Electromagnetism"). Note that _D_ 2 vanishes for a [flat connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flat_connection "Flat connection") (i.e. when Ω = 0).

If _ρ_: _G_ → GL(_**R**_ _n_), then one can write

![Image 16: {\displaystyle \rho (\Omega )=F=\sum {F^{i}}_{j}{e^{j}}_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6736f6fc9e3a43da34e7dc5456d489b7bbf0a5d3)
where ![Image 17: {\displaystyle {e^{i}}_{j}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ad71b803cc76ca74525475ef5f952e97c74aa6b9) is the matrix with 1 at the (_i_, _j_)-th entry and zero on the other entries. The matrix ![Image 18: {\displaystyle {F^{i}}_{j}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/813cd5c39ab75ed201300dfe76125718a16b4128) whose entries are 2-forms on _P_ is called the **curvature matrix**.

Given a smooth real vector bundle _E_ → _M_ with a [connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connection_(vector_bundle) "Connection (vector bundle)")∇ and rank r, the **exterior covariant derivative** is a real-linear map on the [vector-valued differential forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector-valued_differential_forms "Vector-valued differential forms") that are valued in E:

![Image 19: {\displaystyle d^{\nabla }:\Omega ^{k}(M,E)\to \Omega ^{k+1}(M,E).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1e42fefa676b46e68c3784a4c515b5e93c40fc69)
The covariant derivative is such a map for _k_ = 0. The exterior covariant derivatives extends this map to general k. There are several equivalent ways to define this object:

*   [[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_covariant_derivative#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBesse1987Section_1.12Kol%C3%A1%C5%99MichorSlov%C3%A1k1993Section_11.13-3) Suppose that a vector-valued differential 2-form is regarded as assigning to each p a multilinear map _s_ _p_: _T_ _p_ _M_ × _T_ _p_ _M_ → _E_ _p_ which is completely anti-symmetric. Then the exterior covariant derivative d∇_s_ assigns to each p a multilinear map _T_ _p_ _M_ × _T_ _p_ _M_ × _T_ _p_ _M_ → _E_ _p_ given by the formula

![Image 20: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\nabla _{x_{1}}(s(X_{2},X_{3}))&-\nabla _{x_{2}}(s(X_{1},X_{3}))+\nabla _{x_{3}}(s(X_{1},X_{2}))\\&-s([X_{1},X_{2}],x_{3})+s([X_{1},X_{3}],x_{2})-s([X_{2},X_{3}],x_{1}).\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9c32bee7d4a68b6e472eb8d319782038d54ea230)where _x_ 1, _x_ 2, _x_ 3 are arbitrary tangent vectors at p which are extended to smooth locally-defined vector fields _X_ 1, _X_ 2 _X_ 3. The legitimacy of this definition depends on the fact that the above expression depends only on _x_ 1, _x_ 2, _x_ 3, and not on the choice of extension. This can be verified by the Leibniz rule for covariant differentiation and for the [Lie bracket of vector fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_bracket_of_vector_fields "Lie bracket of vector fields"). The pattern established in the above formula in the case _k_ = 2 can be directly extended to define the exterior covariant derivative for arbitrary k.
*   [[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_covariant_derivative#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDonaldsonKronheimer199035EguchiGilkeyHanson1980281Jost2017169Taylor2011547-4) The exterior covariant derivative may be characterized by the axiomatic property of defining for each k a real-linear map Ω _k_(_M_, _E_) → Ω _k_ + 1(_M_, _E_) which for _k_ = 0 is the covariant derivative and in general satisfies the Leibniz rule

![Image 21: {\displaystyle d^{\nabla }(\omega \wedge s)=(d\omega )\wedge s+(-1)^{k}\omega \wedge (d^{\nabla }s)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bc217ac85efc16587a45d79ae2cc2d19b2498153)for any differential k-form ω and any vector-valued form s. This may also be viewed as a direct inductive definition. For instance, for any vector-valued differential 1-form s and any local frame _e_ 1, ..., _e_ _r_ of the vector bundle, the coordinates of s are locally-defined differential 1-forms _ω_ 1, ..., _ω_ _r_. The above inductive formula then says that[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_covariant_derivative#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMilnorStasheff1974292%E2%80%93293-5)![Image 22: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}d^{\nabla }s&=d^{\nabla }(\omega ^{1}e_{1}+\cdots +\omega ^{r}e_{r})\\&=(d\omega ^{1})e_{1}+\cdots +(d\omega ^{r})e_{r}-\omega ^{1}\nabla e_{1}-\cdots -\omega ^{r}\nabla e_{r}.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7a61bcebc71eb21059cedf9e65f6723ad94fc8bf)In order for this to be a legitimate definition of _d_∇_s_, it must be verified that the choice of local frame is irrelevant. This can be checked by considering a second local frame obtained by an arbitrary change-of-basis matrix; the [inverse matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverse_matrix "Inverse matrix") provides the change-of-basis matrix for the 1-forms _ω_ 1, ..., _ω_ _r_. When substituted into the above formula, the Leibniz rule as applied for the standard exterior derivative and for the covariant derivative ∇ cancel out the arbitrary choice.
*   [[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_covariant_derivative#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEellsSampson1964Section_3.A.3PenroseRindler1987263-6) A vector-valued differential 2-form s may be regarded as a certain collection of functions _s_ α _ij_ assigned to an arbitrary local frame of E over a local coordinate chart of M. The exterior covariant derivative is then defined as being given by the functions

![Image 23: {\displaystyle (d^{\nabla }s)^{\alpha }{}_{ijk}=\nabla _{i}s^{\alpha }{}_{jk}-\nabla _{j}s^{\alpha }{}_{ik}+\nabla _{k}s^{\alpha }{}_{ij}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fe6c73e462540d40ea2b3c26199122fc75eb7893)The fact that this defines a tensor field valued in E is a direct consequence of the same fact for the covariant derivative. The further fact that it is a differential 3-form valued in E asserts the full anti-symmetry in _i_, _j_, _k_ and is directly verified from the above formula and the contextual assumption that s is a vector-valued differential 2-form, so that _s_ α _ij_ = −_s_ α _ji_. The pattern in this definition of the exterior covariant derivative for _k_ = 2 can be directly extended to larger values of k.

This definition may alternatively be expressed in terms of an arbitrary local frame of E but without considering coordinates on M. Then a vector-valued differential 2-form is expressed by differential 2-forms _s_ 1, ..., _s_ _r_ and the connection is expressed by the connection 1-forms, a skew-symmetric _r_ × _r_ matrix of differential 1-forms θ α β. The exterior covariant derivative of s, as a vector-valued differential 3-form, is expressed relative to the local frame by r many differential 3-forms, defined by ![Image 24: {\displaystyle (d^{\nabla }s)^{\alpha }=d(s^{\alpha })+\theta _{\beta }{}^{\alpha }\wedge s^{\beta }.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e8266f073b2f5c53d6000d2791dd9fb3924854eb)
In the case of the trivial real line bundle ℝ × _M_ → _M_ with its standard connection, vector-valued differential forms and differential forms can be naturally identified with one another, and each of the above definitions coincides with the standard [exterior derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_derivative "Exterior derivative").

Given a principal bundle, any [linear representation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Representation_of_a_Lie_group "Representation of a Lie group") of the structure group defines an [associated bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Associated_bundle "Associated bundle"), and any connection on the principal bundle induces a connection on the associated vector bundle. Differential forms valued in the vector bundle may be naturally identified with fully anti-symmetric [tensorial forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector-valued_differential_forms#Basic_or_tensorial_forms_on_principal_bundles "Vector-valued differential forms") on the total space of the principal bundle. Under this identification, the notions of exterior covariant derivative for the principal bundle and for the vector bundle coincide with one another.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_covariant_derivative#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKol%C3%A1%C5%99MichorSlov%C3%A1k1993112%E2%80%93114-7)

The [curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvature_form "Curvature form") of a connection on a vector bundle may be defined as the composition of the two exterior covariant derivatives Ω 0(_M_, _E_) → Ω 1(_M_, _E_) and Ω 1(_M_, _E_) → Ω 2(_M_, _E_), so that it is defined as a real-linear map _F_: Ω 0(_M_, _E_) → Ω 2(_M_, _E_). It is a fundamental but not immediately apparent fact that _F_(_s_)_p_: _T_ _p_ _M_ × _T_ _p_ _M_ → _E_ _p_ only depends on _s_(_p_), and does so linearly. As such, the curvature may be regarded as an element of Ω 2(_M_, End(_E_)). Depending on how the exterior covariant derivative is formulated, various alternative but equivalent definitions of curvature (some without the language of exterior differentiation) can be obtained.

It is a well-known fact that the composition of the standard exterior derivative with itself is zero: _d_(_d_ ω) = 0. In the present context, this can be regarded as saying that the standard connection on the trivial line bundle ℝ × _M_ → _M_ has zero curvature.

*   [Bianchi's second identity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bianchi%27s_second_identity "Bianchi's second identity"), which says that the exterior covariant derivative of Ω is zero (that is, _D_ Ω = 0) can be stated as: ![Image 25: {\displaystyle d\Omega +\operatorname {ad} (\omega )\cdot \Omega =d\Omega +[\omega \wedge \Omega ]=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bb556a6d0ee8a8351893b21eeb849e74500df86b).

On the other hand, _Dϕ_(_X_#) = 0. If _X_ is a horizontal tangent vector, then ![Image 29: {\displaystyle D\phi (X)=d\phi (X)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/466f5095a21f38c34a6192bca27b43e418f7dccb) and ![Image 30: {\displaystyle \omega (X)=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ba548cb04df17ea2dba65b7b5f66d57c8f5e0c45). For the general case, let _X_ _i_'s be tangent vectors to _P_ at some point such that some of _X_ _i_'s are horizontal and the rest vertical. If _X_ _i_ is vertical, we think of it as a Lie algebra element and then identify it with the fundamental vector field generated by it. If _X_ _i_ is horizontal, we replace it with the [horizontal lift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horizontal_lift "Horizontal lift") of the vector field extending the pushforward π _X_ _i_. This way, we have extended _X_ _i_'s to vector fields. Note the extension is such that we have: [_X_ _i_, _X_ _j_] = 0 if _X_ _i_ is horizontal and _X_ _j_ is vertical.
