A 2D slice of a 6D Calabi–Yau quintic manifold.

In [algebraic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_geometry "Algebraic geometry") and [differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry"), a **Calabi–Yau manifold**, also known as a **Calabi–Yau space**, is a particular type of [manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manifold "Manifold") which has certain properties, such as [Ricci flatness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_flat "Ricci flat"), yielding applications in [theoretical physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theoretical_physics "Theoretical physics"). Particularly in [superstring theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superstring_theory "Superstring theory"), the extra dimensions of [spacetime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spacetime "Spacetime") are sometimes conjectured to take the form of a 6-dimensional Calabi–Yau manifold, which led to the idea of [mirror symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mirror_symmetry_(string_theory) "Mirror symmetry (string theory)"). Their name was coined by [Candelas et al. (1985)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calabi%E2%80%93Yau_manifold#CITEREFCandelasHorowitzStromingerWitten1985), after [Eugenio Calabi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eugenio_Calabi "Eugenio Calabi")([1954](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calabi%E2%80%93Yau_manifold#CITEREFCalabi1954), [1957](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calabi%E2%80%93Yau_manifold#CITEREFCalabi1957)), who first conjectured that compact complex manifolds of Kähler type with vanishing first [Chern class](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chern_class "Chern class") always admit Ricci-flat Kähler metrics, and [Shing-Tung Yau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shing-Tung_Yau "Shing-Tung Yau")([1978](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calabi%E2%80%93Yau_manifold#CITEREFYau1978)), who proved the [Calabi conjecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calabi_conjecture "Calabi conjecture").

Calabi–Yau manifolds are [complex manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_manifold "Complex manifold") that are generalizations of [K3 surfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K3_surface "K3 surface") in any number of [complex dimensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_dimension "Complex dimension") (i.e. any even number of real [dimensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension "Dimension")). They were originally defined as compact [Kähler manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K%C3%A4hler_manifold "Kähler manifold") with a vanishing first [Chern class](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chern_class "Chern class") and a Ricci-flat metric, though many other similar but inequivalent definitions are sometimes used.

The definition was motivated by the work of [Shing-Tung Yau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shing-Tung_Yau "Shing-Tung Yau"), who proved [Eugenio Calabi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eugenio_Calabi "Eugenio Calabi")'s conjecture that any compact [Kähler manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K%C3%A4hler_manifold "Kähler manifold") with vanishing first Chern class also admits a (typically different) Kähler metric with vanishing Ricci tensor.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calabi%E2%80%93Yau_manifold#cite_note-FOOTNOTEYauNadis2010-1)

There are many other definitions of a Calabi–Yau manifold used by different authors, some inequivalent. This section summarizes some of the more common definitions and the relations between them.

If a compact Kähler manifold is simply connected, then the weak definition above is equivalent to the stronger definition. [Enriques surfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enriques_surface "Enriques surface") give examples of complex manifolds that have Ricci-flat metrics, but their canonical bundles are not trivial, so they are Calabi–Yau manifolds according to the second but not the first definition above. On the other hand, their double covers are Calabi–Yau manifolds for both definitions (in fact, K3 surfaces).

By far the hardest part of proving the equivalences between the various properties above is proving the existence of Ricci-flat metrics. This follows from Yau's proof of the [Calabi conjecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calabi_conjecture "Calabi conjecture"), which implies that a compact Kähler manifold with a vanishing first real Chern class has a Kähler metric in the same class with vanishing Ricci curvature. (The class of a Kähler metric is the cohomology class of its associated 2-form.) Calabi showed such a metric is unique.

There are many other inequivalent definitions of Calabi–Yau manifolds that are sometimes used, which differ in the following ways (among others):

*   The first Chern class may vanish as an integral class or as a real class.
*   Most definitions assert that Calabi–Yau manifolds are compact, but some allow them to be non-compact. In the generalization to non-compact manifolds, the difference ![Image 10: {\displaystyle (\Omega \wedge {\bar {\Omega }}-\omega ^{n}/n!)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/64ec0b632e81965b9f7ad7e446061efde4a044d6) must vanish asymptotically. Here, ![Image 11: {\displaystyle \omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/48eff443f9de7a985bb94ca3bde20813ea737be8) is the Kähler form associated with the Kähler metric, ![Image 12: {\displaystyle g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3556280e66fe2c0d0140df20935a6f057381d77).[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calabi%E2%80%93Yau_manifold#cite_note-FOOTNOTETianYau1991-2)
*   Some definitions put restrictions on the [fundamental group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_group "Fundamental group") of a Calabi–Yau manifold, such as demanding that it be finite or trivial. Any Calabi–Yau manifold has a finite cover that is the product of a torus and a simply-connected Calabi–Yau manifold.
*   Some definitions require that the holonomy be exactly equal to ![Image 13: {\displaystyle SU(n)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a32de6314d41905468b6149bf865f35186b06f56) rather than a subgroup of it, which implies that the [Hodge numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hodge_number "Hodge number")![Image 14: {\displaystyle h^{i,0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/590c9ea7954b7b3b52133e1f83f057b35e0f510b) vanish for ![Image 15: {\displaystyle 0<i<\dim(M)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/19393997b2d9e6300d4b6e2197ba4072386e4bb0). Abelian surfaces have a Ricci flat metric with holonomy strictly smaller than ![Image 16: {\displaystyle SU(2)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/27f8cd5de228a45abf34210c1666cd46dd87bc12) (in fact trivial) so are not Calabi–Yau manifolds according to such definitions.
*   Most definitions assume that a Calabi–Yau manifold has a Riemannian metric, but some treat them as complex manifolds without a metric.
*   Most definitions assume the manifold is non-singular, but some allow mild singularities. While the Chern class fails to be well-defined for singular Calabi–Yau's, the canonical bundle and canonical class may still be defined if all the singularities are [Gorenstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gorenstein_scheme "Gorenstein scheme"), and so may be used to extend the definition of a smooth Calabi–Yau manifold to a possibly singular Calabi–Yau variety.

The fundamental fact is that any smooth [algebraic variety](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_variety "Algebraic variety") embedded in a [projective space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_space "Projective space") is a Kähler manifold, because there is a natural [Fubini–Study metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fubini%E2%80%93Study_metric "Fubini–Study metric") on a projective space which one can restrict to the algebraic variety. By definition, if ![Image 17: {\displaystyle \omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/48eff443f9de7a985bb94ca3bde20813ea737be8) is the Kähler metric on the algebraic variety ![Image 18: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) and the canonical bundle ![Image 19: {\displaystyle K_{X}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3485fd0e87deb55b2544efb4320a21496f22bcea) is trivial, then ![Image 20: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) is Calabi–Yau. Moreover, there is unique Kähler metric ![Image 21: {\displaystyle \omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/48eff443f9de7a985bb94ca3bde20813ea737be8) on ![Image 22: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) such that ![Image 23: {\displaystyle [\omega _{0}]=[\omega ]\in H^{2}(X,\mathbb {R} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7d1195af4ab2801a1b6a4fbf969946b63a3ab819), a fact which was conjectured by [Eugenio Calabi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eugenio_Calabi "Eugenio Calabi") and proved by [Shing-Tung Yau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shing-Tung_Yau "Shing-Tung Yau") (see [Calabi conjecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calabi_conjecture "Calabi conjecture")).

### Calabi–Yau algebraic curves

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Calabi%E2%80%93Yau_manifold&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Calabi–Yau algebraic curves")]

In one complex dimension, the only compact examples are [tori](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torus "Torus"), which form a one-parameter family. The Ricci-flat metric on a torus is actually a [flat metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flat_metric "Flat metric"), so that the [holonomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holonomy "Holonomy") is the trivial group SU(1). A one-dimensional Calabi–Yau manifold is a complex [elliptic curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptic_curve "Elliptic curve"), and in particular, [algebraic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_variety "Algebraic variety").

### CY algebraic surfaces

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Calabi%E2%80%93Yau_manifold&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: CY algebraic surfaces")]

In two complex dimensions, the [K3 surfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K3_surface "K3 surface") furnish the only compact simply connected Calabi–Yau manifolds. These can be constructed as quartic surfaces in ![Image 24: {\displaystyle \mathbb {P} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7fb80175bc622b3936c7a0438fc690b2ec410b4f), such as the complex algebraic variety defined by the vanishing locus of

> ![Image 25: {\displaystyle x_{0}^{4}+x_{1}^{4}+x_{2}^{4}+x_{3}^{4}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/86a8c7a9447cf7e4464a2bf332d5e9cf15dd02c4) for ![Image 26: {\displaystyle [x_{0}:x_{1}:x_{2}:x_{3}]\in \mathbb {P} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4c78a65509aba1ef885e2bcf7706b08a07fe21af)

Other examples can be constructed as elliptic fibrations,[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calabi%E2%80%93Yau_manifold#cite_note-3) as quotients of abelian surfaces,[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calabi%E2%80%93Yau_manifold#cite_note-4) or as [complete intersections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complete_intersection "Complete intersection").

Non simply-connected examples are given by [abelian surfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abelian_surface "Abelian surface"), which are real four tori ![Image 27: {\displaystyle \mathbb {T} ^{4}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0de8b87b6b97be4a88afab0c49f0205fb9359ba0) equipped with a complex manifold structure. [Enriques surfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enriques_surface "Enriques surface") and [hyperelliptic surfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperelliptic_surface "Hyperelliptic surface") have first Chern class that vanishes as an element of the real cohomology group, but not as an element of the integral cohomology group, so Yau's theorem about the existence of a Ricci-flat metric still applies to them but they are sometimes not considered to be Calabi–Yau manifolds. Abelian surfaces are sometimes excluded from the classification of being Calabi–Yau, as their holonomy (again the trivial group) is a [proper subgroup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subgroup "Subgroup") of SU(2), instead of being isomorphic to SU(2). However, the [Enriques surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enriques_surface "Enriques surface") subset do not conform entirely to the SU(2) subgroup in the [String theory landscape](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/String_theory_landscape "String theory landscape").

In three complex dimensions, classification of the possible Calabi–Yau manifolds is an open problem, although Yau suspects that there is a finite number of families (albeit a much bigger number than his estimate from 20 years ago). In turn, it has also been conjectured by [Miles Reid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Miles_Reid "Miles Reid") that the number of topological types of Calabi–Yau 3-folds is infinite, and that they can all be transformed continuously ( through certain mild singularizations such as [conifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conifold "Conifold")) one into another—much as [Riemann surfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_surface "Riemann surface") can.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calabi%E2%80%93Yau_manifold#cite_note-5) One example of a three-dimensional Calabi–Yau manifold is a non-singular [quintic threefold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quintic_threefold "Quintic threefold") in [**CP**4](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_projective_space "Complex projective space"), which is the [algebraic variety](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_variety "Algebraic variety") consisting of all of the zeros of a homogeneous quintic [polynomial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial "Polynomial") in the homogeneous coordinates of the **CP**4. Another example is a smooth model of the [Barth–Nieto quintic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barth%E2%80%93Nieto_quintic "Barth–Nieto quintic"). Some discrete quotients of the quintic by various ![Image 28: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} _{5}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/20e64a4b3b4926087c2691c2db517c2d9ba465ea) actions are also Calabi–Yau and have received a lot of attention in the literature. One of these is related to the original quintic by [mirror symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mirror_symmetry_(string_theory) "Mirror symmetry (string theory)").

For every positive integer ![Image 29: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b), the [zero set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero_set "Zero set"), in the homogeneous coordinates of the complex projective space **CP**_n_+1, of a non-singular homogeneous degree _![Image 30: {\displaystyle n+2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest\_v1/media/math/render/svg/aaf4207e44c00a9312fe3e8710efbefcd6eafdc0)_ polynomial in _![Image 31: {\displaystyle n+2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest\_v1/media/math/render/svg/aaf4207e44c00a9312fe3e8710efbefcd6eafdc0)_ variables is a compact Calabi–Yau _![Image 32: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest\_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)_-fold. The case _![Image 33: {\displaystyle n=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest\_v1/media/math/render/svg/d9ec7e1edc2e6d98f5aec2a39ae5f1c99d1e1425)_ describes an elliptic curve, while for _![Image 34: {\displaystyle n=2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest\_v1/media/math/render/svg/a02c8bd752d2cc859747ca1f3a508281bdbc3b34)_ one obtains a K3 surface.

More generally, Calabi–Yau varieties/orbifolds can be found as weighted complete intersections in a [weighted projective space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weighted_projective_space "Weighted projective space"). The main tool for finding such spaces is the [adjunction formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adjunction_formula "Adjunction formula").

All [hyper-Kähler manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyper-K%C3%A4hler_manifold "Hyper-Kähler manifold") are Calabi–Yau manifolds.

Applications in superstring theory
----------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Calabi%E2%80%93Yau_manifold&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Applications in superstring theory")]

Calabi–Yau manifolds are important in [superstring theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superstring_theory "Superstring theory"). Essentially, Calabi–Yau manifolds are shapes that satisfy the requirement of space for the six "unseen" spatial dimensions of string theory, which may be smaller than our currently observable lengths as they have not yet been detected. A popular alternative known as [large extra dimensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Large_extra_dimension "Large extra dimension"), which often occurs in [braneworld](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Braneworld "Braneworld") models, is that the Calabi–Yau is large but we are confined to a small subset on which it intersects a [D-brane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D-brane "D-brane"). Further extensions into higher dimensions are currently being explored with additional ramifications for [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity").

In the most conventional superstring models, ten conjectural dimensions in [string theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/String_theory "String theory") are supposed to come as four of which we are aware, carrying some kind of [fibration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fibration "Fibration") with fiber dimension six. [Compactification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compactification_(physics) "Compactification (physics)") on Calabi–Yau _n_-folds are important because they leave some of the original [supersymmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supersymmetry "Supersymmetry") unbroken. More precisely, in the absence of [fluxes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ramond%E2%80%93Ramond_field "Ramond–Ramond field"), compactification on a Calabi–Yau 3-fold (real dimension 6) leaves one quarter of the original supersymmetry unbroken if the [holonomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holonomy "Holonomy") is the full SU(3).

More generally, a flux-free compactification on an _n_-manifold with holonomy SU(_n_) leaves 2 1−_n_ of the original supersymmetry unbroken, corresponding to 2 6−_n_ supercharges in a compactification of [type IIA supergravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Type_IIA_supergravity "Type IIA supergravity") or 2 5−_n_ supercharges in a compactification of type I. When fluxes are included the supersymmetry condition instead implies that the compactification manifold be a [generalized Calabi–Yau](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Generalized_Calabi%E2%80%93Yau&action=edit&redlink=1 "Generalized Calabi–Yau (page does not exist)"), a notion introduced by [Hitchin (2003)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calabi%E2%80%93Yau_manifold#CITEREFHitchin2003). These models are known as [flux compactifications](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compactification_(physics)#Flux_compactification "Compactification (physics)").

[F-theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/F-theory "F-theory") compactifications on various Calabi–Yau four-folds provide physicists with a method to find a large number of classical solutions in the so-called [string theory landscape](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/String_theory_landscape "String theory landscape").

Connected with each hole in the Calabi–Yau space is a group of low-energy string vibrational patterns. Since string theory states that our familiar elementary particles correspond to low-energy string vibrations, the presence of multiple holes causes the string patterns to fall into multiple groups, or [families](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generation_(particle_physics) "Generation (particle physics)"). Although the following statement has been simplified, it conveys the logic of the argument: if the Calabi–Yau has three holes, then three families of vibrational patterns and thus three families of particles will be observed experimentally.

Logically, since strings vibrate through all the dimensions, the shape of the curled-up ones will affect their vibrations and thus the properties of the elementary particles observed. For example, [Andrew Strominger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andrew_Strominger "Andrew Strominger") and [Edward Witten](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edward_Witten "Edward Witten") have shown that the masses of particles depend on the manner of the intersection of the various holes in a Calabi–Yau. In other words, the positions of the holes relative to one another and to the substance of the Calabi–Yau space was found by Strominger and Witten to affect the masses of particles in a certain way. This is true of all particle properties.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calabi%E2%80%93Yau_manifold#cite_note-7)

A **Calabi–Yau algebra** was introduced by [Victor Ginzburg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Victor_Ginzburg "Victor Ginzburg") to transport the geometry of a Calabi–Yau manifold to [noncommutative algebraic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noncommutative_algebraic_geometry "Noncommutative algebraic geometry").[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calabi%E2%80%93Yau_manifold#cite_note-8)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calabi%E2%80%93Yau_manifold#cite_note-9)
