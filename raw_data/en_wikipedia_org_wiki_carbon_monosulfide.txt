Chemical compound

**Carbon monosulfide** is a [chemical compound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_compound "Chemical compound") with the [formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_formula "Chemical formula") CS. This [diatomic molecule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diatomic_molecule "Diatomic molecule") is the sulfur analogue of [carbon monoxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide "Carbon monoxide"), and is unstable as a solid or a liquid, but it has been observed as a gas both in the laboratory and in the [interstellar medium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_medium "Interstellar medium").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monosulfide#cite_note-1) The molecule resembles carbon monoxide with a triple bond between carbon and sulfur. The molecule is not intrinsically unstable, but it tends to polymerize in sunlight to a brown mass, as first discovered in 1868 and 1872.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monosulfide#cite_note-2) The polymer is quite stable, decomposing a little at 360°C to carbon disulfide. This tendency towards polymerization reflects the greater stability of C–S single bonds.

[Polymers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polymer "Polymer") with the formula (CS)_n_ have been reported,[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monosulfide#cite_note-3) and the formal dimer is [ethenedithione](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethenedithione "Ethenedithione"). Also, CS has been observed as a ligand in some [transition metal complexes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transition_metal_complex "Transition metal complex").[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

The simplest carbon monosulfide synthesis decomposes [carbon disulfide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_disulfide "Carbon disulfide") in a high-voltage AC arc.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monosulfide#cite_note-4)
