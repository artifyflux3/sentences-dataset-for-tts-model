[![Image 1](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Force_acting_as_centripetal_force.svg/250px-Force_acting_as_centripetal_force.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Force_acting_as_centripetal_force.svg)

A particle is disturbed from its uniform linear motion by a series of short kicks (1, 2, ...), giving its trajectory a nearly circular shape. The force is referred to as a centripetal force in the limit of a continuously acting force directed towards the center of curvature of the path.

**Centripetal force** (from [Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin "Latin")_centrum_, "center" and _petere_, "to seek"[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_force#cite_note-1)) is the [force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Force "Force") that makes a body follow a curved [path](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trajectory "Trajectory"). The direction of the centripetal force is always [orthogonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonality "Orthogonality") to the motion of the body and towards the fixed point of the instantaneous [center of curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osculating_circle "Osculating circle") of the path. [Isaac Newton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaac_Newton "Isaac Newton") coined the term,[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_force#cite_note-2) describing it as "a force by which bodies are drawn or impelled, or in any way tend, towards a point as to a centre".[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_force#cite_note-3) In [Newtonian mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newtonian_mechanics "Newtonian mechanics"), gravity provides the centripetal force causing astronomical [orbits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbit "Orbit").

One common example involving centripetal force is the case in which a body moves with uniform speed along a circular path. The centripetal force is directed at right angles to the motion and also along the radius towards the centre of the circular path.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_force#cite_note-Hibbeler-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_force#cite_note-Tipler0-5) The mathematical description was derived in 1659 by the Dutch physicist [Christiaan Huygens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens "Christiaan Huygens").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_force#cite_note-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_force#cite_note-:0-7)

From the [kinematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematics "Kinematics") of curved motion it is known that an object moving at [tangential speed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangential_speed "Tangential speed")_v_ along a path with [radius of curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radius_of_curvature "Radius of curvature")_r_ accelerates toward the center of curvature at a rate [![Image 2: {\displaystyle {\textbf {a}}_{c}=\lim _{\Delta t\to 0}{\frac {\Delta {\textbf {v}}}{\Delta t}},\quad a_{c}={\frac {v^{2}}{r}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6c8c326d297987a6a53890b5bd1684a282fdd34d)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:MathWikibase&qid=Q2248131) Here, ![Image 3: {\displaystyle a_{c}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81c3b8855d2273be442a8cdddc9943fd2af67bab) is the [centripetal acceleration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_acceleration "Centripetal acceleration") and ![Image 4: {\displaystyle \Delta {\textbf {v}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/83f053f35d0ade0ce20d3a46ff9276323c30ae6f) is the [difference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_vector#Addition_and_subtraction "Euclidean vector") between the velocity vectors at ![Image 5: {\displaystyle t+\Delta {t}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/043b4fa2662efb142c2b656772b10ab75b33a5da) and ![Image 6: {\displaystyle t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/65658b7b223af9e1acc877d848888ecdb4466560).

By [Newton's second law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_second_law "Newton's second law"), the cause of acceleration is a net force acting on the object, which is proportional to its mass _m_ and its acceleration. The force, usually referred to as a _centripetal force_, has a magnitude[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_force#cite_note-8)[![Image 7: {\displaystyle F_{c}=ma_{c}=m{\frac {v^{2}}{r}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/664bfb5dc870da1f7cb752d1ad881c573973ec19)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:MathWikibase&qid=Q172881) and is, like centripetal acceleration, directed toward the center of curvature of the object's trajectory.

This force is also sometimes written in terms of the [angular velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_velocity "Angular velocity")_ω_ of the object about the center of the circle, related to the tangential velocity by the formula ![Image 18: {\displaystyle v=\omega r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b5edc54db08559dc3e33cb3cc568126e12b46521) so that ![Image 19: {\displaystyle F_{c}=mr\omega ^{2}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2d42a452955897071a31ca54f9e2ce0458f9b9b7)

Expressed using the [orbital period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_period "Orbital period")_T_ for one revolution of the circle, [![Image 20: {\displaystyle \omega ={\frac {2\pi }{T}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/689caf6d9f39dbc57a3d4ee99c82c98e31193e72)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:MathWikibase&qid=Q161635) the equation becomes[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_force#cite_note-11)![Image 21: {\displaystyle F_{c}=mr\left({\frac {2\pi }{T}}\right)^{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/20601dc151382a1704910eab7e27eaacd187f395)

In particle accelerators, velocity can be very high (close to the [speed of light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light "Speed of light") in vacuum) so the same rest mass now exerts greater inertia (relativistic mass) thereby requiring greater force for the same centripetal acceleration, so the equation becomes:[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_force#cite_note-12)![Image 22: {\displaystyle F_{c}={\frac {\gamma mv^{2}}{r}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/275f0e8ba8a59da88fed203479193d7b0b0d8d5b) where [![Image 23: {\displaystyle \gamma ={\frac {1}{\sqrt {1-{\frac {v^{2}}{c^{2}}}}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/abacf009fa4db767f015172584c8fc89e7725745)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:MathWikibase&qid=Q599404) is the [Lorentz factor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentz_factor "Lorentz factor").

Thus the centripetal force is given by: ![Image 24: {\displaystyle F_{c}=\gamma mv\omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d95c324af1820b836762625a06ad6e94a18075cc) which is the rate of change of [relativistic momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_momentum "Relativistic momentum")![Image 25: {\displaystyle \gamma mv}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5296be0d6efbb223f5a2fbef8e674a5228fcaaf4).

[![Image 26](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Centripetal_force_diagram.svg/250px-Centripetal_force_diagram.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Centripetal_force_diagram.svg)

A body experiencing [uniform circular motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uniform_circular_motion "Uniform circular motion") requires a centripetal force, towards the axis as shown, to maintain its circular path.

In the case of an object that is swinging around on the end of a rope in a horizontal plane, the centripetal force on the object is supplied by the tension of the rope. The rope example is an example involving a 'pull' force. The centripetal force can also be supplied as a 'push' force, such as in the case where the normal reaction of a wall supplies the centripetal force for a [wall of death](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wall_of_death_(motorcycle_act) "Wall of death (motorcycle act)") or a [Rotor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotor_(ride) "Rotor (ride)") rider.

[Newton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaac_Newton "Isaac Newton")'s idea of a centripetal force corresponds to what is nowadays referred to as a [central force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_force "Central force"). When a [satellite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satellite "Satellite") is in [orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbit "Orbit") around a [planet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planet "Planet"), gravity is considered to be a centripetal force even though in the case of eccentric orbits, the gravitational force is directed towards the focus, and not towards the instantaneous center of curvature.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_force#cite_note-13)

Another example of centripetal force arises in the helix that is traced out when a charged particle moves in a uniform [magnetic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_field "Magnetic field") in the absence of other external forces. In this case, the magnetic force is the centripetal force that acts towards the helix axis.

Analysis of several cases
-------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Centripetal_force&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Analysis of several cases")]

Below are three examples of increasing complexity, with derivations of the formulas governing velocity and acceleration.

### Uniform circular motion

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Centripetal_force&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Uniform circular motion")]

Uniform circular motion refers to the case of constant rate of rotation. Here are two approaches to describing this case.

#### Calculus derivation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Centripetal_force&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Calculus derivation")]

In two dimensions, the position vector ![Image 27: {\displaystyle {\textbf {r}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8a907369fc0aa51241fcd0620f96478563a6c810), which has magnitude (length) ![Image 28: {\displaystyle r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d1ecb613aa2984f0576f70f86650b7c2a132538) and directed at an angle ![Image 29: {\displaystyle \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e5ab2664b422d53eb0c7df3b87e1360d75ad9af) above the x-axis, can be expressed in [Cartesian coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinates "Cartesian coordinates") using the [unit vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_vectors "Unit vectors")![Image 30: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathbf {x} }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8a5451354f837d5d89774e1de386b44a903d929d) and ![Image 31: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathbf {y} }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3826fc591cfe42348f58b15e4c23152f30931ef):[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_force#cite_note-14)![Image 32: {\displaystyle {\textbf {r}}=r\cos(\theta ){\hat {\mathbf {x} }}+r\sin(\theta ){\hat {\mathbf {y} }}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e531c82c842971aeecbffeba3a3812efb38bb06b)

The assumption of [uniform circular motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uniform_circular_motion "Uniform circular motion") requires three things:

1.   The object moves only on a circle.
2.   The radius of the circle ![Image 33: {\displaystyle r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d1ecb613aa2984f0576f70f86650b7c2a132538) does not change in time.
3.   The object moves with constant [angular velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_velocity "Angular velocity")![Image 34: {\displaystyle \omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/48eff443f9de7a985bb94ca3bde20813ea737be8) around the circle. Therefore, ![Image 35: {\displaystyle \theta =\omega t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a312c1c22a5255e85c1372abd0d55b7768c6b1cf) where ![Image 36: {\displaystyle t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/65658b7b223af9e1acc877d848888ecdb4466560) is time.

The [velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity "Velocity")![Image 37: {\displaystyle {\textbf {v}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ea31d06d1f25599e56d852db92f3ebc85ac75a8d) and [acceleration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acceleration "Acceleration")![Image 38: {\displaystyle {\textbf {a}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af9f1604cec45bc8d60e31610f9ec1b7c6599b68) of the motion are the first and second derivatives of position with respect to time:

![Image 39: {\displaystyle {\textbf {r}}=r\cos(\omega t){\hat {\mathbf {x} }}+r\sin(\omega t){\hat {\mathbf {y} }},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5ea79c3ea661a6fa816ac2b68eda9e20186d8d1e)[![Image 40: {\displaystyle {\textbf {v}}={\dot {\textbf {r}}}=-r\omega \sin(\omega t){\hat {\mathbf {x} }}+r\omega \cos(\omega t){\hat {\mathbf {y} }},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/551201f0131462bc7f97db84de151e235e4b2611)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:MathWikibase&qid=Q11465)[![Image 41: {\displaystyle {\textbf {a}}={\ddot {\textbf {r}}}=-\omega ^{2}(r\cos(\omega t){\hat {\mathbf {x} }}+r\sin(\omega t){\hat {\mathbf {y} }}).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/90e24fe09932bc763f48b25940a6cf432623c283)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:MathWikibase&qid=Q11376)

The term in parentheses is the original expression of ![Image 42: {\displaystyle {\textbf {r}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8a907369fc0aa51241fcd0620f96478563a6c810) in [Cartesian coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinates "Cartesian coordinates"). Consequently, ![Image 43: {\displaystyle {\textbf {a}}=-\omega ^{2}{\textbf {r}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/741274c060a908cfd95284007943293cc09370bd) The negative sign shows that the acceleration is pointed towards the center of the circle (opposite the radius), hence it is called "centripetal" (i.e. "center-seeking"). While objects naturally follow a straight path (due to [inertia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inertia "Inertia")), this centripetal acceleration describes the circular motion path caused by a centripetal force.

#### Derivation using vectors

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Centripetal_force&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Derivation using vectors")]

[![Image 44](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Circular_motion_vectors.svg/250px-Circular_motion_vectors.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Circular_motion_vectors.svg)

Vector relationships for uniform circular motion; vector **Ω** representing the rotation is normal to the plane of the orbit with polarity determined by the [right-hand rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right-hand_rule "Right-hand rule") and magnitude _dθ_ /_dt_.

The image at right shows the vector relationships for uniform circular motion. The rotation itself is represented by the angular velocity vector **Ω**, which is normal to the plane of the orbit (using the [right-hand rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right-hand_rule "Right-hand rule")) and has magnitude given by:

![Image 45: {\displaystyle |\mathbf {\Omega } |={\frac {\mathrm {d} \theta }{\mathrm {d} t}}=\omega \ ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a0f2f065452864d2f700d78aa215398852221bb9)
with _θ_ the angular position at time _t_. In this subsection, d _θ_/d _t_ is assumed constant, independent of time. The distance traveled **dℓ** of the particle in time d _t_ along the circular path is

![Image 46: {\displaystyle \mathrm {d} {\boldsymbol {\ell }}=\mathbf {\Omega } \times \mathbf {r} (t)\mathrm {d} t\ ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/024c9d9b5f05e177fbf44e936f89c481b875d250)
which, by properties of the [vector cross product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_cross_product "Vector cross product"), has magnitude _r_ d _θ_ and is in the direction tangent to the circular path.

Consequently,

![Image 47: {\displaystyle {\frac {\mathrm {d} \mathbf {r} }{\mathrm {d} t}}=\lim _{{\Delta }t\to 0}{\frac {\mathbf {r} (t+{\Delta }t)-\mathbf {r} (t)}{{\Delta }t}}={\frac {\mathrm {d} {\boldsymbol {\ell }}}{\mathrm {d} t}}\ .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5c69c2ef8d6d0f6c2e1026c55a602ef68ff7406d)
In other words,

![Image 48: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} \ {\stackrel {\mathrm {def} }{=}}\ {\frac {\mathrm {d} \mathbf {r} }{\mathrm {d} t}}={\frac {\mathrm {d} \mathbf {\boldsymbol {\ell }} }{\mathrm {d} t}}=\mathbf {\Omega } \times \mathbf {r} (t)\ .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/43d9d833abd7d6fb831f21ae904656bc6d506d74)
Differentiating with respect to time, ![Image 49: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} \ {\stackrel {\mathrm {def} }{=}}\ {\frac {\mathrm {d} \mathbf {v} }{d\mathrm {t} }}=\mathbf {\Omega } \times {\frac {\mathrm {d} \mathbf {r} (t)}{\mathrm {d} t}}=\mathbf {\Omega } \times \left[\mathbf {\Omega } \times \mathbf {r} (t)\right]\ .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/55bdc59db211dba1e224034884d244146c97c2ba)

[Lagrange's formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triple_product#Vector_triple_product "Triple product") states: ![Image 50: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} \times \left(\mathbf {b} \times \mathbf {c} \right)=\mathbf {b} \left(\mathbf {a} \cdot \mathbf {c} \right)-\mathbf {c} \left(\mathbf {a} \cdot \mathbf {b} \right)\ .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6dbba230699959cb2ee5c62c68ed727233cac5bf)

Applying Lagrange's formula with the observation that **Ω • r**(_t_) = 0 at all times, ![Image 51: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} =-{|\mathbf {\Omega |} }^{2}\mathbf {r} (t)\ .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1fe0a21658fde14fe1ca11a3a356d74e405c8a84)

In words, the acceleration is pointing directly opposite to the radial displacement **r** at all times, and has a magnitude: ![Image 52: {\displaystyle |\mathbf {a} |=|\mathbf {r} (t)|\left({\frac {\mathrm {d} \theta }{\mathrm {d} t}}\right)^{2}=r{\omega }^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/83d35bc7eafb0075290a96932d7f265f9e8eb7ad) where vertical bars |...| denote the vector magnitude, which in the case of **r**(_t_) is simply the radius _r_ of the path. This result agrees with the previous section, though the notation is slightly different.

When the rate of rotation is made constant in the analysis of [nonuniform circular motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_force#Nonuniform_circular_motion), that analysis agrees with this one.

A merit of the vector approach is that it is manifestly independent of any coordinate system.

#### Example: The banked turn

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Centripetal_force&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Example: The banked turn")]

[![Image 53](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Banked_turn.svg/250px-Banked_turn.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Banked_turn.svg)

Upper panel: Ball on a banked circular track moving with constant speed _v_; Lower panel: Forces on the ball

The upper panel in the image at right shows a ball in circular motion on a banked curve. The curve is banked at an angle _θ_ from the horizontal, and the surface of the road is considered to be slippery. The objective is to find what angle the bank must have so the ball does not slide off the road.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_force#cite_note-Lerner-15) Intuition tells us that, on a flat curve with no banking at all, the ball will simply slide off the road; while with a very steep banking, the ball will slide to the center unless it travels the curve rapidly.

Apart from any acceleration that might occur in the direction of the path, the lower panel of the image above indicates the forces on the ball. There are _two_ forces; one is the force of gravity vertically downward through the [center of mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Center_of_mass "Center of mass") of the ball _m_**g**, where _m_ is the mass of the ball and **g** is the [gravitational acceleration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_acceleration "Gravitational acceleration"); the second is the upward [normal force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_force "Normal force") exerted by the road at a right angle to the road surface _m_**a**n. The centripetal force demanded by the curved motion is also shown above. This centripetal force is not a third force applied to the ball, but rather must be provided by the [net force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Net_force "Net force") on the ball resulting from [vector addition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_addition "Vector addition") of the [normal force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_force "Normal force") and the [force of gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Force_of_gravity "Force of gravity"). The resultant or [net force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Net_force "Net force") on the ball found by [vector addition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_addition "Vector addition") of the [normal force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_force "Normal force") exerted by the road and vertical force due to [gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity "Gravity") must equal the centripetal force dictated by the need to travel a circular path. The curved motion is maintained so long as this net force provides the centripetal force requisite to the motion.

The horizontal net force on the ball is the horizontal component of the force from the road, which has magnitude |**F**h| = _m_|**a**n| sin _θ_. The vertical component of the force from the road must counteract the gravitational force: |**F**v| = _m_|**a**n| cos _θ_ = _m_|**g**|, which implies |**a**n| = |**g**| / cos _θ_. Substituting into the above formula for |**F**h| yields a horizontal force to be: [![Image 54: {\displaystyle |\mathbf {F} _{\mathrm {h} }|=m|\mathbf {g} |{\frac {\sin \theta }{\cos \theta }}=m|\mathbf {g} |\tan \theta \,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2f4b26b5ee1fe387edea1e8d6a45ffeba7ab0fd0)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:MathWikibase&qid=Q11402)

On the other hand, at velocity |**v**| on a circular path of radius _r_, kinematics says that the force needed to turn the ball continuously into the turn is the radially inward centripetal force **F**c of magnitude: ![Image 55: {\displaystyle |\mathbf {F} _{\mathrm {c} }|=m|\mathbf {a} _{\mathrm {c} }|={\frac {m|\mathbf {v} |^{2}}{r}}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/816b0c7872050439f2f82cc402296a0738f8a7df)

Consequently, the ball is in a stable path when the angle of the road is set to satisfy the condition: ![Image 56: {\displaystyle m|\mathbf {g} |\tan \theta ={\frac {m|\mathbf {v} |^{2}}{r}}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/79926b6d805c72f071bddbddfd3b6dba00b6d63f) or, ![Image 57: {\displaystyle \tan \theta ={\frac {|\mathbf {v} |^{2}}{|\mathbf {g} |r}}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/79fdd6580f0b1baff9cb5a64ac5c0b1dcc36bfe7)

As the angle of bank _θ_ approaches 90°, the [tangent function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_function "Tangent function") approaches infinity, allowing larger values for |**v**|2/_r_. In words, this equation states that for greater speeds (bigger |**v**|) the road must be banked more steeply (a larger value for _θ_), and for sharper turns (smaller _r_) the road also must be banked more steeply, which accords with intuition. When the angle _θ_ does not satisfy the above condition, the horizontal component of force exerted by the road does not provide the correct centripetal force, and an additional frictional force tangential to the road surface is called upon to provide the difference. If [friction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friction "Friction") cannot do this (that is, the [coefficient of friction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coefficient_of_friction "Coefficient of friction") is exceeded), the ball slides to a different radius where the balance can be realized.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_force#cite_note-Schaum-16)[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_force#cite_note-Darbyshire-17)

These ideas apply to air flight as well. See the FAA pilot's manual.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_force#cite_note-FAA-18)

### Nonuniform circular motion

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Centripetal_force&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Nonuniform circular motion")]

[![Image 58](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Nonuniform_circular_motion.svg/250px-Nonuniform_circular_motion.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Nonuniform_circular_motion.svg)

 / _R_.[_[clarify](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Please\_clarify "Wikipedia:Please clarify")_]

As a generalization of the uniform circular motion case, suppose the angular rate of rotation is not constant. The acceleration now has a tangential component, as shown the image at right. This case is used to demonstrate a derivation strategy based on a [polar coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_coordinate_system "Polar coordinate system").

Let **r**(_t_) be a vector that describes the position of a [point mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_mass "Point mass") as a function of time. Since we are assuming [circular motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circular_motion "Circular motion"), let **r**(_t_) = _R_·**u**_r_, where _R_ is a constant (the radius of the circle) and **u**r is the [unit vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_vector "Unit vector") pointing from the origin to the point mass. The direction of **u**_r_ is described by _θ_, the angle between the x-axis and the unit vector, measured counterclockwise from the x-axis. The other unit vector for polar coordinates, **u**θ is perpendicular to **u**_r_ and points in the direction of increasing _θ_. These polar unit vectors can be expressed in terms of [Cartesian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system "Cartesian coordinate system") unit vectors in the _x_ and _y_ directions, denoted ![Image 59: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathbf {i} }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c67b997d3ff51d850c47e3172ceabe2402937bff) and ![Image 60: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathbf {j} }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/84570c224d310d5347cf16692ded2ff4def37387) respectively:[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_force#cite_note-19)![Image 61: {\displaystyle \mathbf {u} _{r}=\cos \theta \ {\hat {\mathbf {i} }}+\sin \theta \ {\hat {\mathbf {j} }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9dc9165af30c29ebe22daf83486950518b6054c3) and ![Image 62: {\displaystyle \mathbf {u} _{\theta }=-\sin \theta \ {\hat {\mathbf {i} }}+\cos \theta \ {\hat {\mathbf {j} }}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9200bbe5c7c711384fb082ed5cfd32273569040d)

One can differentiate to find velocity: ![Image 63: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\mathbf {v} &=r{\frac {d\mathbf {u} _{r}}{dt}}\\&=r{\frac {d}{dt}}\left(\cos \theta \ {\hat {\mathbf {i} }}+\sin \theta \ {\hat {\mathbf {j} }}\right)\\&=r{\frac {d\theta }{dt}}{\frac {d}{d\theta }}\left(\cos \theta \ {\hat {\mathbf {i} }}+\sin \theta \ {\hat {\mathbf {j} }}\right)\\&=r{\frac {d\theta }{dt}}\left(-\sin \theta \ {\hat {\mathbf {i} }}+\cos \theta \ {\hat {\mathbf {j} }}\right)\\&=r{\frac {d\theta }{dt}}\mathbf {u} _{\theta }\\&=\omega r\mathbf {u} _{\theta }\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/57e2760833c14841f4d41ad2eb5d6c2a92e9450a) where ω is the angular velocity _dθ_/_dt_.

This result for the velocity matches expectations that the velocity should be directed tangentially to the circle, and that the magnitude of the velocity should be _rω_. Differentiating again, and noting that ![Image 64: {\displaystyle {\frac {d\mathbf {u} _{\theta }}{dt}}=-{\frac {d\theta }{dt}}\mathbf {u} _{r}=-\omega \mathbf {u} _{r}\ ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3813bb3771d24c7ae6409a30fcb4aa23e1559bf6) we find that the acceleration, **a** is: ![Image 65: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} =r\left({\frac {d\omega }{dt}}\mathbf {u} _{\theta }-\omega ^{2}\mathbf {u} _{r}\right)\ .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/735759c09db3cb9e60ba87dd11be840738dce341)

Thus, the radial and tangential components of the acceleration are: ![Image 66: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} _{r}=-\omega ^{2}r\ \mathbf {u} _{r}=-{\frac {|\mathbf {v} |^{2}}{r}}\ \mathbf {u} _{r}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b0bcaff0dac1f7c0698a0fd8731024b5a537f30b) and ![Image 67: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} _{\theta }=r\ {\frac {d\omega }{dt}}\ \mathbf {u} _{\theta }={\frac {d|\mathbf {v} |}{dt}}\ \mathbf {u} _{\theta }\ ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/95c760530df6bc953880c551ab982bee827d24f2) where |**v**| = _r_ _ω_ is the magnitude of the velocity (the speed).

These equations express mathematically that, in the case of an object that moves along a circular path with a changing speed, the acceleration of the body may be decomposed into a [perpendicular component](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular_component "Perpendicular component") that changes the direction of motion (the centripetal acceleration), and a parallel, or [tangential component](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangential_component "Tangential component"), that changes the speed.

Position vector **r**, always points radially from the origin.

[![Image 69](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Velocity_vector_plane_polar_coords.svg/250px-Velocity_vector_plane_polar_coords.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Velocity_vector_plane_polar_coords.svg)

Velocity vector **v**, always tangent to the path of motion.

[![Image 70](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Acceleration_vector_plane_polar_coords.svg/250px-Acceleration_vector_plane_polar_coords.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Acceleration_vector_plane_polar_coords.svg)

Acceleration vector **a**, not parallel to the radial motion but offset by the angular and Coriolis accelerations, nor tangent to the path but offset by the centripetal and radial accelerations.

Kinematic vectors in plane polar coordinates. Notice the setup is not restricted to 2d space, but a plane in any higher dimension.

[![Image 71](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Polar_unit_vectors.PNG/250px-Polar_unit_vectors.PNG)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Polar_unit_vectors.PNG)

Polar unit vectors at two times _t_ and _t_ + _dt_ for a particle with trajectory **r** ( _t_ ); on the left the unit vectors **u**ρ and **u**θ at the two times are moved so their tails all meet, and are shown to trace an arc of a unit radius circle. Their rotation in time _dt_ is _d_ θ, just the same angle as the rotation of the trajectory **r** ( _t_ ).

The above results can be derived perhaps more simply in [polar coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_coordinate_system "Polar coordinate system"), and at the same time extended to general motion within a plane, as shown next. Polar coordinates in the plane employ a radial unit vector **u**ρ and an angular unit vector **u**θ, as shown above.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_force#cite_note-polar-20) A particle at position **r** is described by:

![Image 72: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} =\rho \mathbf {u} _{\rho }\ ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4dcf99817c25534e0e6bd8f8116b332d902fa70a)

where the notation _ρ_ is used to describe the distance of the path from the origin instead of _R_ to emphasize that this distance is not fixed, but varies with time. The unit vector **u**ρ travels with the particle and always points in the same direction as **r**(_t_). Unit vector **u**θ also travels with the particle and stays orthogonal to **u**ρ. Thus, **u**ρ and **u**θ form a local Cartesian coordinate system attached to the particle, and tied to the path travelled by the particle.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_force#cite_note-21) By moving the unit vectors so their tails coincide, as seen in the circle at the left of the image above, it is seen that **u**ρ and **u**θ form a right-angled pair with tips on the unit circle that trace back and forth on the perimeter of this circle with the same angle _θ_(_t_) as **r**(_t_).

When the particle moves, its velocity is

![Image 73: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} ={\frac {\mathrm {d} \rho }{\mathrm {d} t}}\mathbf {u} _{\rho }+\rho {\frac {\mathrm {d} \mathbf {u} _{\rho }}{\mathrm {d} t}}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f58ed3c5198c095dfefdc851d4d58e4448dfc607)
To evaluate the velocity, the derivative of the unit vector **u**ρ is needed. Because **u**ρ is a unit vector, its magnitude is fixed, and it can change only in direction, that is, its change d**u**ρ has a component only perpendicular to **u**ρ. When the trajectory **r**(_t_) rotates an amount d _θ_, **u**ρ, which points in the same direction as **r**(_t_), also rotates by d _θ_. See image above. Therefore, the change in **u**ρ is

![Image 74: {\displaystyle \mathrm {d} \mathbf {u} _{\rho }=\mathbf {u} _{\theta }\mathrm {d} \theta \,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4df69f4ae872fbde9c7a5be1220396356993e14e)
or

![Image 75: {\displaystyle {\frac {\mathrm {d} \mathbf {u} _{\rho }}{\mathrm {d} t}}=\mathbf {u} _{\theta }{\frac {\mathrm {d} \theta }{\mathrm {d} t}}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/542f5893e46c4542a0b9c219f0ee133aa33a3196)
In a similar fashion, the rate of change of **u**θ is found. As with **u**ρ, **u**θ is a unit vector and can only rotate without changing size. To remain orthogonal to **u**ρ while the trajectory **r**(_t_) rotates an amount d _θ_, **u**θ, which is orthogonal to **r**(_t_), also rotates by d _θ_. See image above. Therefore, the change d**u**θ is orthogonal to **u**θ and proportional to d _θ_ (see image above):

![Image 76: {\displaystyle {\frac {\mathrm {d} \mathbf {u} _{\theta }}{\mathrm {d} t}}=-{\frac {\mathrm {d} \theta }{\mathrm {d} t}}\mathbf {u} _{\rho }\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5c11a9eb76f23c2c0a054237add2838fd47c7675)
The equation above shows the sign to be negative: to maintain orthogonality, if d**u**ρ is positive with d _θ_, then d**u**θ must decrease.

Substituting the derivative of **u**ρ into the expression for velocity:

![Image 77: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} ={\frac {\mathrm {d} \rho }{\mathrm {d} t}}\mathbf {u} _{\rho }+\rho \mathbf {u} _{\theta }{\frac {\mathrm {d} \theta }{\mathrm {d} t}}=v_{\rho }\mathbf {u} _{\rho }+v_{\theta }\mathbf {u} _{\theta }=\mathbf {v} _{\rho }+\mathbf {v} _{\theta }\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/557a56367e0707ddf3205444d10fac47708215c6)
To obtain the acceleration, another time differentiation is done:

![Image 78: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} ={\frac {\mathrm {d} ^{2}\rho }{\mathrm {d} t^{2}}}\mathbf {u} _{\rho }+{\frac {\mathrm {d} \rho }{\mathrm {d} t}}{\frac {\mathrm {d} \mathbf {u} _{\rho }}{\mathrm {d} t}}+{\frac {\mathrm {d} \rho }{\mathrm {d} t}}\mathbf {u} _{\theta }{\frac {\mathrm {d} \theta }{\mathrm {d} t}}+\rho {\frac {\mathrm {d} \mathbf {u} _{\theta }}{\mathrm {d} t}}{\frac {\mathrm {d} \theta }{\mathrm {d} t}}+\rho \mathbf {u} _{\theta }{\frac {\mathrm {d} ^{2}\theta }{\mathrm {d} t^{2}}}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f0725be8fded9493f3527fc4706f30cde5773119)
Substituting the derivatives of **u**ρ and **u**θ, the acceleration of the particle is:[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_force#cite_note-Taylor-22)

![Image 79: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\mathbf {a} &={\frac {\mathrm {d} ^{2}\rho }{\mathrm {d} t^{2}}}\mathbf {u} _{\rho }+2{\frac {\mathrm {d} \rho }{\mathrm {d} t}}\mathbf {u} _{\theta }{\frac {\mathrm {d} \theta }{\mathrm {d} t}}-\rho \mathbf {u} _{\rho }\left({\frac {\mathrm {d} \theta }{\mathrm {d} t}}\right)^{2}+\rho \mathbf {u} _{\theta }{\frac {\mathrm {d} ^{2}\theta }{\mathrm {d} t^{2}}}\ ,\\&=\mathbf {u} _{\rho }\left[{\frac {\mathrm {d} ^{2}\rho }{\mathrm {d} t^{2}}}-\rho \left({\frac {\mathrm {d} \theta }{\mathrm {d} t}}\right)^{2}\right]+\mathbf {u} _{\theta }\left[2{\frac {\mathrm {d} \rho }{\mathrm {d} t}}{\frac {\mathrm {d} \theta }{\mathrm {d} t}}+\rho {\frac {\mathrm {d} ^{2}\theta }{\mathrm {d} t^{2}}}\right]\\&=\mathbf {u} _{\rho }\left[{\frac {\mathrm {d} v_{\rho }}{\mathrm {d} t}}-{\frac {v_{\theta }^{2}}{\rho }}\right]+\mathbf {u} _{\theta }\left[{\frac {2}{\rho }}v_{\rho }v_{\theta }+\rho {\frac {\mathrm {d} }{\mathrm {d} t}}{\frac {v_{\theta }}{\rho }}\right]\,.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4bd23fad91b9a145bc62e918b5124ee62c4a537b)
As a particular example, if the particle moves in a circle of constant radius _R_, then d _ρ_/d _t_ = 0, **v** = **v**θ, and:

![Image 80: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} =\mathbf {u} _{\rho }\left[-\rho \left({\frac {\mathrm {d} \theta }{\mathrm {d} t}}\right)^{2}\right]+\mathbf {u} _{\theta }\left[\rho {\frac {\mathrm {d} ^{2}\theta }{\mathrm {d} t^{2}}}\right]=\mathbf {u} _{\rho }\left[-{\frac {v^{2}}{r}}\right]+\mathbf {u} _{\theta }\left[{\frac {\mathrm {d} v}{\mathrm {d} t}}\right]\ }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/df2f314be17d32d8d204bef757376219863181c7)

where ![Image 81: {\displaystyle v=v_{\theta }.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/85d717e45833e0c830ca46adc991305360232362)

These results agree with those above for [nonuniform circular motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_force#Nonuniform_circular_motion). See also the article on [non-uniform circular motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-uniform_circular_motion "Non-uniform circular motion"). If this acceleration is multiplied by the particle mass, the leading term is the centripetal force and the negative of the second term related to [angular acceleration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_acceleration "Angular acceleration") is sometimes called the [Euler force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler_force "Euler force").[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_force#cite_note-Lanczos-23)

For trajectories other than circular motion, for example, the more general trajectory envisioned in the image above, the instantaneous center of rotation and radius of curvature of the trajectory are related only indirectly to the coordinate system defined by **u ρ** and **u θ** and to the length |**r**(_t_)| = _ρ_. Consequently, in the general case, it is not straightforward to disentangle the centripetal and Euler terms from the above general acceleration equation.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_force#cite_note-Curtis-24)[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_force#cite_note-Lee-25) To deal directly with this issue, local coordinates are preferable, as discussed next.

[![Image 82](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Local_unit_vectors.PNG/250px-Local_unit_vectors.PNG)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Local_unit_vectors.PNG)

Local coordinate system for planar motion on a curve. Two different positions are shown for distances _s_ and _s_ + _ds_ along the curve. At each position _s_, unit vector **u**n points along the outward normal to the curve and unit vector **u**t is tangential to the path. The radius of curvature of the path is ρ as found from the rate of rotation of the tangent to the curve with respect to arc length, and is the radius of the [osculating circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osculating_circle "Osculating circle") at position _s_. The unit circle on the left shows the rotation of the unit vectors with _s_.

Local coordinates mean a set of coordinates that travel with the particle,[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_force#cite_note-observer-26) and have orientation determined by the path of the particle.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_force#cite_note-Ito-27) Unit vectors are formed as shown in the image at right, both tangential and normal to the path. This coordinate system sometimes is referred to as _intrinsic_ or _path coordinates_[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_force#cite_note-Kumar-28)[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_force#cite_note-Rao-29) or _nt-coordinates_, for _normal-tangential_, referring to these unit vectors. These coordinates are a very special example of a more general concept of local coordinates from the theory of differential forms.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_force#cite_note-Morita-30)

Distance along the path of the particle is the arc length _s_, considered to be a known function of time.

![Image 83: {\displaystyle s=s(t)\ .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bcea5a3bc0a52f17e27709828077122b5fffa3c2)
A center of curvature is defined at each position _s_ located a distance _ρ_ (the [radius of curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osculating_circle#Mathematical_description "Osculating circle")) from the curve on a line along the normal **u**n (_s_). The required distance _ρ_(_s_) at arc length _s_ is defined in terms of the rate of rotation of the tangent to the curve, which in turn is determined by the path itself. If the orientation of the tangent relative to some starting position is _θ_(_s_), then _ρ_(_s_) is defined by the derivative d _θ_/d _s_:

![Image 84: {\displaystyle {\frac {1}{\rho (s)}}=\kappa (s)={\frac {\mathrm {d} \theta }{\mathrm {d} s}}\ .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b5c4902ca10a174327f5c1a33ed2ff61dc95cb99)
The radius of curvature usually is taken as positive (that is, as an absolute value), while the _[curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvature#In\_terms\_of\_a\_general\_parametrization "Curvature")_ _κ_ is a signed quantity.

A geometric approach to finding the center of curvature and the radius of curvature uses a limiting process leading to the [osculating circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osculating_circle "Osculating circle").[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_force#cite_note-osculating-31)[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_force#cite_note-Chen0-32) See image above.

Using these coordinates, the motion along the path is viewed as a succession of circular paths of ever-changing center, and at each position _s_ constitutes [non-uniform circular motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-uniform_circular_motion "Non-uniform circular motion") at that position with radius _ρ_. The local value of the angular rate of rotation then is given by:

![Image 85: {\displaystyle \omega (s)={\frac {\mathrm {d} \theta }{\mathrm {d} t}}={\frac {\mathrm {d} \theta }{\mathrm {d} s}}{\frac {\mathrm {d} s}{\mathrm {d} t}}={\frac {1}{\rho (s)}}\ {\frac {\mathrm {d} s}{\mathrm {d} t}}={\frac {v(s)}{\rho (s)}}\ ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/295650c9f5fbe7feac6a57fbfd570c4d8288f643)
with the local speed _v_ given by:

![Image 86: {\displaystyle v(s)={\frac {\mathrm {d} s}{\mathrm {d} t}}\ .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5856d69514b863338c16d254d95c578dcb3ade0d)
As for the other examples above, because unit vectors cannot change magnitude, their rate of change is always perpendicular to their direction (see the left-hand insert in the image above):[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_force#cite_note-Gregory-33)

![Image 87: {\displaystyle {\frac {d\mathbf {u} _{\mathrm {n} }(s)}{ds}}=\mathbf {u} _{\mathrm {t} }(s){\frac {d\theta }{ds}}=\mathbf {u} _{\mathrm {t} }(s){\frac {1}{\rho }}\ ;}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d79d48df994af08d8e66797d894c14f8d548a239)![Image 88: {\displaystyle {\frac {d\mathbf {u} _{\mathrm {t} }(s)}{\mathrm {d} s}}=-\mathbf {u} _{\mathrm {n} }(s){\frac {\mathrm {d} \theta }{\mathrm {d} s}}=-\mathbf {u} _{\mathrm {n} }(s){\frac {1}{\rho }}\ .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a4f0615e9d4a6b1b07dcb977a337627c04c0df98)
Consequently, the velocity and acceleration are:[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_force#cite_note-Chen0-32)[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_force#cite_note-Whittaker-34)[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_force#cite_note-Ginsberg-35)

![Image 89: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} (t)=v\mathbf {u} _{\mathrm {t} }(s)\ ;}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/64d9c82b704ad8ae363013c85603eaa1aa5afa87)
and using the [chain-rule of differentiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chain_rule "Chain rule"):

![Image 90: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} (t)={\frac {\mathrm {d} v}{\mathrm {d} t}}\mathbf {u} _{\mathrm {t} }(s)-{\frac {v^{2}}{\rho }}\mathbf {u} _{\mathrm {n} }(s)\ ;}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f0b2c18a7bca7308c6ce83d36f1eb6c4919877c6) with the tangential acceleration ![Image 91: {\displaystyle {\frac {\mathrm {\mathrm {d} } v}{\mathrm {\mathrm {d} } t}}={\frac {\mathrm {d} v}{\mathrm {d} s}}\ {\frac {\mathrm {d} s}{\mathrm {d} t}}={\frac {\mathrm {d} v}{\mathrm {d} s}}\ v\ .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a6dbb08ccbabac54ebc6ee7b5ff3dbaa85a16abd)
In this local coordinate system, the acceleration resembles the expression for [nonuniform circular motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_force#Nonuniform_circular_motion) with the local radius _ρ_(_s_), and the centripetal acceleration is identified as the second term.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_force#cite_note-Shelley-36)

Extending this approach to three dimensional space curves leads to the [Frenet–Serret formulas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frenet%E2%80%93Serret_formulas "Frenet–Serret formulas").[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_force#cite_note-Andrews-37)[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_force#cite_note-Chand-38)

##### Alternative approach

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Centripetal_force&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Alternative approach")]

Looking at the image above, one might wonder whether adequate account has been taken of the difference in curvature between _ρ_(_s_) and _ρ_(_s_ + d _s_) in computing the arc length as d _s_ = _ρ_(_s_)d _θ_. Reassurance on this point can be found using a more formal approach outlined below. This approach also makes connection with the article on [curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvature#In_terms_of_a_general_parametrization "Curvature").

To introduce the unit vectors of the local coordinate system, one approach is to begin in Cartesian coordinates and describe the local coordinates in terms of these Cartesian coordinates. In terms of arc length _s_, let the path be described as:[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_force#cite_note-curvature-39)![Image 92: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} (s)=\left[x(s),\ y(s)\right].}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ea9cb4aedaa6696cd195a0329b20a3a6d4c1f0d0)

Then an incremental displacement along the path d _s_ is described by: ![Image 93: {\displaystyle \mathrm {d} \mathbf {r} (s)=\left[\mathrm {d} x(s),\ \mathrm {d} y(s)\right]=\left[x'(s),\ y'(s)\right]\mathrm {d} s\ ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e41305b24920b77bceb3d266edba4ab08249618)

where primes are introduced to denote derivatives with respect to _s_. The magnitude of this displacement is d _s_, showing that:[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_force#cite_note-Shabana-40)

![Image 94: {\displaystyle \left[x'(s)^{2}+y'(s)^{2}\right]=1\ .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/50c42e82eda34ce15d98149c930dfb7ba7cec77d)(Eq. 1)
This displacement is necessarily a tangent to the curve at _s_, showing that the unit vector tangent to the curve is: ![Image 95: {\displaystyle \mathbf {u} _{\mathrm {t} }(s)=\left[x'(s),\ y'(s)\right],}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/52a7fe41893b23da5d10fa21095cbf823374b339) while the outward unit vector normal to the curve is ![Image 96: {\displaystyle \mathbf {u} _{\mathrm {n} }(s)=\left[y'(s),\ -x'(s)\right],}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/aefd42926b0e6c5fd1cdd0b91ae514b65fd3dfc0)

[Orthogonality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonality "Orthogonality") can be verified by showing that the vector [dot product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dot_product "Dot product") is zero. The unit magnitude of these vectors is a consequence of [Eq. 1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_force#Eq._1). Using the tangent vector, the angle _θ_ of the tangent to the curve is given by: ![Image 97: {\displaystyle \sin \theta ={\frac {y'(s)}{\sqrt {x'(s)^{2}+y'(s)^{2}}}}=y'(s)\ ;}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bd7c00c7d6c6f5ed00b25ad04bef30f29b8cbc22) and ![Image 98: {\displaystyle \cos \theta ={\frac {x'(s)}{\sqrt {x'(s)^{2}+y'(s)^{2}}}}=x'(s)\ .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/de7930a20d70205920ac9b7e9ad6d9d467682eb2)

The radius of curvature is introduced completely formally (without need for geometric interpretation) as: ![Image 99: {\displaystyle {\frac {1}{\rho }}={\frac {\mathrm {d} \theta }{\mathrm {d} s}}\ .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/137dd26ad43c87013d83c53cba9fcba336c2459d)

The derivative of _θ_ can be found from that for sin _θ_: ![Image 100: {\displaystyle {\frac {\mathrm {d} \sin \theta }{\mathrm {d} s}}=\cos \theta {\frac {\mathrm {d} \theta }{\mathrm {d} s}}={\frac {1}{\rho }}\cos \theta \ ={\frac {1}{\rho }}x'(s)\ .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/91110a23d691fca3bd55353899e9a10adab89e4f)

Now: ![Image 101: {\displaystyle {\frac {\mathrm {d} \sin \theta }{\mathrm {d} s}}={\frac {\mathrm {d} }{\mathrm {d} s}}{\frac {y'(s)}{\sqrt {x'(s)^{2}+y'(s)^{2}}}}={\frac {y''(s)x'(s)^{2}-y'(s)x'(s)x''(s)}{\left(x'(s)^{2}+y'(s)^{2}\right)^{3/2}}}\ ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d4f81066685aa3b57bd6eafeb263e4bf1d17ba9a) in which the denominator is unity. With this formula for the derivative of the sine, the radius of curvature becomes: ![Image 102: {\displaystyle {\frac {\mathrm {d} \theta }{\mathrm {d} s}}={\frac {1}{\rho }}=y''(s)x'(s)-y'(s)x''(s)={\frac {y''(s)}{x'(s)}}=-{\frac {x''(s)}{y'(s)}}\ ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c93b53fdc4e13101bf399161832a12ee1ece7a87) where the equivalence of the forms stems from differentiation of [Eq. 1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_force#Eq._1): ![Image 103: {\displaystyle x'(s)x''(s)+y'(s)y''(s)=0\ .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1aeecb586cba1a3fb2f636dcfe24e5d7c3464310) With these results, the acceleration can be found: ![Image 104: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\mathbf {a} (s)&={\frac {\mathrm {d} }{\mathrm {d} t}}\mathbf {v} (s)={\frac {\mathrm {d} }{\mathrm {d} t}}\left[{\frac {\mathrm {d} s}{\mathrm {d} t}}\left(x'(s),\ y'(s)\right)\right]\\&=\left({\frac {\mathrm {d} ^{2}s}{\mathrm {d} t^{2}}}\right)\mathbf {u} _{\mathrm {t} }(s)+\left({\frac {\mathrm {d} s}{\mathrm {d} t}}\right)^{2}\left(x''(s),\ y''(s)\right)\\&=\left({\frac {\mathrm {d} ^{2}s}{\mathrm {d} t^{2}}}\right)\mathbf {u} _{\mathrm {t} }(s)-\left({\frac {\mathrm {d} s}{\mathrm {d} t}}\right)^{2}{\frac {1}{\rho }}\mathbf {u} _{\mathrm {n} }(s)\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c5b63aca630aacb31a8ef0fc21bcf2f8e86af3ca) as can be verified by taking the dot product with the unit vectors **u**t(_s_) and **u**n(_s_). This result for acceleration is the same as that for circular motion based on the radius _ρ_. Using this coordinate system in the inertial frame, it is easy to identify the force normal to the trajectory as the centripetal force and that parallel to the trajectory as the tangential force. From a qualitative standpoint, the path can be approximated by an arc of a circle for a limited time, and for the limited time a particular radius of curvature applies, the centrifugal and Euler forces can be analyzed on the basis of circular motion with that radius.

This result for acceleration agrees with that found earlier. However, in this approach, the question of the change in radius of curvature with _s_ is handled completely formally, consistent with a geometric interpretation, but not relying upon it, thereby avoiding any questions the image above might suggest about neglecting the variation in _ρ_.

##### Example: circular motion

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Centripetal_force&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Example: circular motion")]

To illustrate the above formulas, let _x_, _y_ be given as:

![Image 105: {\displaystyle x=\alpha \cos {\frac {s}{\alpha }}\ ;\ y=\alpha \sin {\frac {s}{\alpha }}\ .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/39eaa7fcfb52c46abe01e9f7743bca42e344eed5)
Then:

![Image 106: {\displaystyle x^{2}+y^{2}=\alpha ^{2}\ ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/915842721edb933505dea460a2cec21380d37266)
which can be recognized as a circular path around the origin with radius _α_. The position _s_ = 0 corresponds to [_α_, 0], or 3 o'clock. To use the above formalism, the derivatives are needed:

![Image 107: {\displaystyle y^{\prime }(s)=\cos {\frac {s}{\alpha }}\ ;\ x^{\prime }(s)=-\sin {\frac {s}{\alpha }}\ ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/27032bdc0367e46e4335cea9ca3ea7c0279c4895)![Image 108: {\displaystyle y^{\prime \prime }(s)=-{\frac {1}{\alpha }}\sin {\frac {s}{\alpha }}\ ;\ x^{\prime \prime }(s)=-{\frac {1}{\alpha }}\cos {\frac {s}{\alpha }}\ .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ee8d839265dd78afdcf8e292d55e33eb55754e06)
With these results, one can verify that:

![Image 109: {\displaystyle x^{\prime }(s)^{2}+y^{\prime }(s)^{2}=1\ ;\ {\frac {1}{\rho }}=y^{\prime \prime }(s)x^{\prime }(s)-y^{\prime }(s)x^{\prime \prime }(s)={\frac {1}{\alpha }}\ .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0b9008692daa44a406963fb44c3adde6238f1081)
The unit vectors can also be found:

![Image 110: {\displaystyle \mathbf {u} _{\mathrm {t} }(s)=\left[-\sin {\frac {s}{\alpha }}\ ,\ \cos {\frac {s}{\alpha }}\right]\ ;\ \mathbf {u} _{\mathrm {n} }(s)=\left[\cos {\frac {s}{\alpha }}\ ,\ \sin {\frac {s}{\alpha }}\right]\ ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/72b1a6646bfe5026f496df581576f09251db1a9c)
which serve to show that _s_ = 0 is located at position [_ρ_, 0] and _s_ = _ρ_ π/2 at [0, _ρ_], which agrees with the original expressions for _x_ and _y_. In other words, _s_ is measured counterclockwise around the circle from 3 o'clock. Also, the derivatives of these vectors can be found:

![Image 111: {\displaystyle {\frac {\mathrm {d} }{\mathrm {d} s}}\mathbf {u} _{\mathrm {t} }(s)=-{\frac {1}{\alpha }}\left[\cos {\frac {s}{\alpha }}\ ,\ \sin {\frac {s}{\alpha }}\right]=-{\frac {1}{\alpha }}\mathbf {u} _{\mathrm {n} }(s)\ ;}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d6d3efccbed76c27507654645897ccc0b587b08)![Image 112: {\displaystyle \ {\frac {\mathrm {d} }{\mathrm {d} s}}\mathbf {u} _{\mathrm {n} }(s)={\frac {1}{\alpha }}\left[-\sin {\frac {s}{\alpha }}\ ,\ \cos {\frac {s}{\alpha }}\right]={\frac {1}{\alpha }}\mathbf {u} _{\mathrm {t} }(s)\ .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/29f755d9d1bb8f6f884a3bdc8ae8d1be5147674a)
To obtain velocity and acceleration, a time-dependence for _s_ is necessary. For counterclockwise motion at variable speed _v_(_t_):

![Image 113: {\displaystyle s(t)=\int _{0}^{t}\ dt^{\prime }\ v(t^{\prime })\ ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0c0792f3dd6b60d2b5e4ce98e467196a60f3bc28)
where _v_(_t_) is the speed and _t_ is time, and _s_(_t_ = 0) = 0. Then:

![Image 114: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} =v(t)\mathbf {u} _{\mathrm {t} }(s)\ ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fdbbee158fa77e3f1fbc2764e3be65e188bee722)![Image 115: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} ={\frac {\mathrm {d} v}{\mathrm {d} t}}\mathbf {u} _{\mathrm {t} }(s)+v{\frac {\mathrm {d} }{\mathrm {d} t}}\mathbf {u} _{\mathrm {t} }(s)={\frac {\mathrm {d} v}{\mathrm {d} t}}\mathbf {u} _{\mathrm {t} }(s)-v{\frac {1}{\alpha }}\mathbf {u} _{\mathrm {n} }(s){\frac {\mathrm {d} s}{\mathrm {d} t}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/89a511eb623e641c0a4e545b20428138a9c028cc)![Image 116: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} ={\frac {\mathrm {d} v}{\mathrm {d} t}}\mathbf {u} _{\mathrm {t} }(s)-{\frac {v^{2}}{\alpha }}\mathbf {u} _{\mathrm {n} }(s)\ ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/96632d0fb8d74d1ad8e364c91e9615b1a680f20a)
where it already is established that α = ρ. This acceleration is the standard result for [non-uniform circular motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-uniform_circular_motion "Non-uniform circular motion").
