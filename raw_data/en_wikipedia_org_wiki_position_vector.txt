In [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry"), a **position** or **position vector**, also known as **location vector** or **radius vector**, is a [Euclidean vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_vector "Euclidean vector") that represents a [point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_(geometry) "Point (geometry)")_P_ in [space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space "Space"). Its length represents the distance in relation to an arbitrary reference [origin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Origin_(mathematics) "Origin (mathematics)")_O_, and its [direction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direction_(geometry) "Direction (geometry)") represents the angular orientation with respect to given reference axes. Usually denoted **x**, **r**, or **s**, it corresponds to the straight line segment from _O_ to _P_. In other words, it is the [displacement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Displacement_(vector) "Displacement (vector)") or [translation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Translation_(geometry) "Translation (geometry)") that maps the origin to _P_:[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Position_vector#cite_note-1)

![Image 5: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} ={\overrightarrow {OP}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b01ba8e32362a89d298cf8d9e3e82ec5a8647ab3)
The term **position vector** is used mostly in the fields of [differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry"), [mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanics "Mechanics") and occasionally [vector calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_calculus "Vector calculus"). Frequently this is used in [two-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two-dimensional "Two-dimensional") or [three-dimensional space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_space "Three-dimensional space"), but can be easily generalized to [Euclidean spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space") and [affine spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_space "Affine space") of any [dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension "Dimension").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Position_vector#cite_note-phys_keller-2)

The **relative position** of a point _Q_ with respect to point _P_ is the Euclidean vector resulting from the subtraction of the two absolute position vectors (each with respect to the origin):

![Image 6: {\displaystyle \Delta \mathbf {r} =\mathbf {s} -\mathbf {r} ={\overrightarrow {PQ}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9d645e8fc2d31704b1d65596a1cdf28739c0d9c6)
where ![Image 7: {\displaystyle \mathbf {s} ={\overrightarrow {OQ}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47d87beb28b75f18d034389dc119ee3715c39629). The _[relative direction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relative\_direction "Relative direction")_ between two points is their relative position normalized as a [unit vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_vector "Unit vector").

Definition and representation
-----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Position_(geometry)&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Definition and representation")]

[![Image 8](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Space_curve.svg/250px-Space_curve.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Space_curve.svg)

[Space curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_curve "Space curve") in 3D. The [position vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Position_vector "Position vector")**r** is parameterized by a scalar _t_. At **r** = **a** the red line is the tangent to the curve, and the blue plane is normal to the curve.

In [three dimensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three_dimensions "Three dimensions"), any set of three-dimensional coordinates and their corresponding basis vectors can be used to define the location of a point in space—whichever is the simplest for the task at hand may be used.

Commonly, one uses the familiar [Cartesian coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system "Cartesian coordinate system"), or sometimes [spherical polar coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_polar_coordinates "Spherical polar coordinates"), or [cylindrical coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylindrical_coordinate_system "Cylindrical coordinate system"):

![Image 9: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\mathbf {r} (t)&\equiv \mathbf {r} (x,y,z)\equiv x(t)\mathbf {\hat {e}} _{x}+y(t)\mathbf {\hat {e}} _{y}+z(t)\mathbf {\hat {e}} _{z}\\&\equiv \mathbf {r} (r,\theta ,\phi )\equiv r(t)\mathbf {\hat {e}} _{r}{\big (}\theta (t),\phi (t){\big )}\\&\equiv \mathbf {r} (r,\phi ,z)\equiv r(t)\mathbf {\hat {e}} _{r}{\big (}\phi (t){\big )}+z(t)\mathbf {\hat {e}} _{z},\\\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/34f304ee5df8065c0b69fa0167ec278591008da2)
where _t_ is a [parameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parametric_equation "Parametric equation"), owing to their rectangular or circular symmetry. These different coordinates and corresponding basis vectors represent the same position vector. More general [curvilinear coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvilinear_coordinates "Curvilinear coordinates") could be used instead and are in contexts like [continuum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuum_mechanics "Continuum mechanics") and [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity") (in the latter case one needs an additional time coordinate).

[Linear algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_algebra "Linear algebra") allows for the abstraction of an _n_-dimensional position vector. A position vector can be expressed as a linear combination of [basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basis_(linear_algebra) "Basis (linear algebra)") vectors:[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Position_vector#cite_note-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Position_vector#cite_note-4)

![Image 10: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} =\sum _{i=1}^{n}x_{i}\mathbf {e} _{i}=x_{1}\mathbf {e} _{1}+x_{2}\mathbf {e} _{2}+\dotsb +x_{n}\mathbf {e} _{n}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2952a63a3c83901733de83bebddd836525330829)
The [set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_(mathematics) "Set (mathematics)") of all position vectors forms [position space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Position_space "Position space") (a [vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space") whose elements are the position vectors), since positions can be added ([vector addition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_addition "Vector addition")) and scaled in length ([scalar multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_multiplication "Scalar multiplication")) to obtain another position vector in the space. The notion of "space" is intuitive, since each _x i_ (_i_ = 1, 2, …, _n_) can have any value, the collection of values defines a point in space.

The _[dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension\_(linear\_algebra) "Dimension (linear algebra)")_ of the position space is _n_ (also denoted dim(_R_) = _n_). The _[coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinates "Coordinates")_ of the vector **r** with respect to the basis vectors **e**_i_ are _x_ _i_. The vector of coordinates forms the [coordinate vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_vector "Coordinate vector") or _n_-[tuple](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tuple "Tuple") (_x_ 1, _x_ 2, …, _x n_).

Each coordinate _x i_ may be parameterized a number of [parameters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parameter "Parameter")_t_. One parameter _x i_(_t_) would describe a curved 1D path, two parameters _x i_(_t_ 1, _t_ 2) describes a curved 2D surface, three _x i_(_t_ 1, _t_ 2, _t_ 3) describes a curved 3D volume of space, and so on.

The [linear span](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_span "Linear span") of a basis set _B_ = {**e**1, **e**2, …, **e**_n_} equals the position space _R_, denoted span(_B_) = _R_.

### Differential geometry

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Position_(geometry)&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Differential geometry")]

Position vector fields are used to describe continuous and differentiable space curves, in which case the independent parameter needs not be time, but can be (e.g.) arc length of the curve.

In any [equation of motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equation_of_motion "Equation of motion"), the position vector **r**(_t_) is usually the most sought-after quantity because this function defines the motion of a particle (i.e. a [point mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_mass "Point mass")) – its location relative to a given coordinate system at some time _t_.

To define motion in terms of position, each coordinate may be parametrized by time; since each successive value of time corresponds to a sequence of successive spatial locations given by the coordinates, the [continuum limit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuum_limit "Continuum limit") of many successive locations is a path the particle traces.

In the case of one dimension, the position has only one component, so it effectively degenerates to a scalar coordinate. It could be, say, a vector in the _x_ direction, or the radial _r_ direction. Equivalent notations include

![Image 11: {\displaystyle \mathbf {x} \equiv x\equiv x(t),\quad r\equiv r(t),\quad s\equiv s(t).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7e9fc20a8b12f60a2264124ffbaac9efa2f367c9)[![Image 12](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Kinematics.svg/250px-Kinematics.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Kinematics.svg)

Kinematic quantities of a classical particle: mass _m_, position**r**, velocity**v**, acceleration**a**

For a position vector **r** that is a function of time _t_, the [time derivatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_derivative "Time derivative") can be computed with respect to _t_. These derivatives have common utility in the study of [kinematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematics "Kinematics"), [control theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Control_theory "Control theory"), [engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineering "Engineering") and other sciences.

[Velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity "Velocity")![Image 13: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} ={\frac {\mathrm {d} \mathbf {r} }{\mathrm {d} t}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f8949645bb6e9b7a0283512bfabcbfe5bb0e3fce)where d**r** is an [infinitesimally](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_(infinitesimal) "Differential (infinitesimal)") small [displacement (vector)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Displacement_(vector) "Displacement (vector)").[Acceleration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acceleration "Acceleration")![Image 14: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} ={\frac {\mathrm {d} \mathbf {v} }{\mathrm {d} t}}={\frac {\mathrm {d} ^{2}\mathbf {r} }{\mathrm {d} t^{2}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/701050f989b0820b4ac0cc2c721b120092aa50c5)[Jerk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerk_(physics) "Jerk (physics)")![Image 15: {\displaystyle \mathbf {j} ={\frac {\mathrm {d} \mathbf {a} }{\mathrm {d} t}}={\frac {\mathrm {d} ^{2}\mathbf {v} }{\mathrm {d} t^{2}}}={\frac {\mathrm {d} ^{3}\mathbf {r} }{\mathrm {d} t^{3}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7eb92243c452ce20a45a921d3a6a4afd5386e8f5)
These names for the first, second and third derivative of position are commonly used in basic kinematics.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Position_vector#cite_note-stewart-5) By extension, the higher-order derivatives can be computed in a similar fashion. Study of these higher-order derivatives can improve approximations of the original displacement function. Such higher-order terms are required in order to accurately represent the displacement function as [a sum of an infinite sequence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taylor_series "Taylor series"), enabling several analytical techniques in engineering and physics.
