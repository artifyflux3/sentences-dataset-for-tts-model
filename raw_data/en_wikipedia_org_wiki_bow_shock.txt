In [astrophysics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrophysics "Astrophysics"), **bow shocks** are shock waves in regions where the conditions of density and pressure change dramatically due to blowing [stellar wind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_wind "Stellar wind").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bow_shock#cite_note-:0-1) Bow shock occurs when the [magnetosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetosphere "Magnetosphere") of an astrophysical object interacts with the nearby flowing ambient [plasma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasma_(physics) "Plasma (physics)") such as the [solar wind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_wind "Solar wind"). For Earth and other magnetized planets, it is the boundary at which the speed of the stellar wind abruptly drops as a result of its approach to the [magnetopause](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetopause "Magnetopause"). For stars, this boundary is typically the edge of the [astrosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrosphere "Astrosphere"), where the stellar wind meets the [interstellar medium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_medium "Interstellar medium").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bow_shock#cite_note-:0-1)

The defining criterion of a [shock wave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shock_wave "Shock wave") is that the bulk velocity of the [plasma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasma_(physics) "Plasma (physics)") drops from "[supersonic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supersonic_speed "Supersonic speed")" to "subsonic", where [the speed of sound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_sound#Speed_of_sound_in_plasma "Speed of sound") c s is defined by ![Image 2: {\displaystyle c_{s}^{2}=\gamma p/\rho }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ca85af2824055b58f82e773aa787c55d15016100) where ![Image 3: {\displaystyle \gamma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a223c880b0ce3da8f64ee33c4f0010beee400b1a) is the [ratio of specific heats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat_capacity_ratio "Heat capacity ratio"), ![Image 4: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) is the [pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pressure "Pressure"), and ![Image 5: {\displaystyle \rho }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1f7d439671d1289b6a816e6af7a304be40608d64) is the density of the plasma.

A common complication in astrophysics is the presence of a magnetic field. For instance, the charged particles making up the solar wind follow spiral paths along magnetic field lines. The velocity of each particle as it gyrates around a field line can be treated similarly to a thermal velocity in an ordinary gas, and in an ordinary gas the mean thermal velocity is roughly the speed of sound. At the bow shock, the bulk forward velocity of the wind (which is the component of the velocity parallel to the field lines about which the particles gyrate) drops below the speed at which the particles are gyrating.

The best-studied example of a bow shock is that occurring where the Sun's wind encounters [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth "Earth")'s magnetopause, although bow shocks occur around all planets, both unmagnetized, such as [Mars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mars "Mars")[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bow_shock#cite_note-2) and [Venus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Venus "Venus")[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bow_shock#cite_note-3) and magnetized, such as [Jupiter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jupiter#Magnetosphere "Jupiter")[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bow_shock#cite_note-4) or [Saturn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saturn#Magnetosphere "Saturn").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bow_shock#cite_note-5) Earth's bow shock is about 17 kilometres (11 mi) thick[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bow_shock#cite_note-6) and located about 90,000 kilometres (56,000 mi) from the planet.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bow_shock#cite_note-7)

Bow shocks form at [comets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comet "Comet") as a result of the interaction between the solar wind and the cometary ionosphere. Far away from the Sun, a comet is an icy boulder without an atmosphere. As it approaches the Sun, the heat of the sunlight causes gas to be released from the [cometary nucleus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cometary_nucleus "Cometary nucleus"), creating an atmosphere called a [coma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coma_(cometary) "Coma (cometary)"). The coma is partially ionized by the sunlight, and when the solar wind passes through this ion coma, the bow shock appears.

The first observations were made in the 1980s and 90s as several spacecraft flew by comets [21P/Giacobini–Zinner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/21P/Giacobini%E2%80%93Zinner "21P/Giacobini–Zinner"),[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bow_shock#cite_note-8)[1P/Halley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halley%27s_Comet "Halley's Comet"),[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bow_shock#cite_note-9) and [26P/Grigg–Skjellerup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/26P/Grigg%E2%80%93Skjellerup "26P/Grigg–Skjellerup").[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bow_shock#cite_note-10) It was then found that the bow shocks at comets are wider and more gradual than the sharp planetary bow shocks seen at Earth, for example. These observations were all made near [perihelion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perihelion_and_aphelion "Perihelion and aphelion") when the bow shocks already were fully developed.

The _[Rosetta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rosetta\_(spacecraft) "Rosetta (spacecraft)")_ spacecraft followed comet [67P/Churyumov–Gerasimenko](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/67P/Churyumov%E2%80%93Gerasimenko "67P/Churyumov–Gerasimenko") from far out in the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System"), at a heliocentric distance of 3.6 [AU](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_unit "Astronomical unit"), in toward perihelion at 1.24 AU, and back out again. This allowed _Rosetta_ to observe the bow shock as it formed when the outgassing increased during the comet's journey toward the Sun. In this early state of development the shock was called the "infant bow shock".[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bow_shock#cite_note-11) The infant bow shock is asymmetric and, relative to the distance to the nucleus, wider than fully developed bow shocks.

[![Image 6](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Voyager_1_entering_heliosheath_region.jpg/250px-Voyager_1_entering_heliosheath_region.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Voyager_1_entering_heliosheath_region.jpg)

The bubble-like heliosphere moving through the interstellar medium and its different structures.

For several decades, the solar wind has been thought to form a bow shock at the edge of the [heliosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heliosphere "Heliosphere"), where it collides with the surrounding interstellar medium. Moving away from the Sun, the point where the solar wind flow becomes subsonic is the [termination shock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Termination_shock "Termination shock"), the point where the interstellar medium and solar wind pressures balance is the [heliopause](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heliopause_(astronomy) "Heliopause (astronomy)"), and the point where the flow of the interstellar medium becomes subsonic would be the bow shock. This solar bow shock was thought to lie at a distance around 230 AU[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bow_shock#cite_note-12) from the Sun – more than twice the distance of the termination shock as encountered by the Voyager spacecraft.

However, data obtained in 2012 from NASA's [Interstellar Boundary Explorer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_Boundary_Explorer "Interstellar Boundary Explorer") (IBEX) indicates the lack of any solar bow shock.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bow_shock#cite_note-bow-13) Along with corroborating results from the [Voyager spacecraft](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voyager_program "Voyager program"), these findings have motivated some theoretical refinements; current thinking is that formation of a bow shock is prevented, at least in the galactic region through which the Sun is passing, by a combination of the strength of the local interstellar magnetic-field and of the relative velocity of the heliosphere.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bow_shock#cite_note-14)

In 2006, a far infrared bow shock was detected near the [AGB star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asymptotic_giant_branch "Asymptotic giant branch")[R Hydrae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/R_Hydrae "R Hydrae").[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bow_shock#cite_note-15)

[![Image 7](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Red_Giant_Plunging_Through_Space.jpg/500px-Red_Giant_Plunging_Through_Space.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Red_Giant_Plunging_Through_Space.jpg)

The bow shock around R Hydrae[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bow_shock#cite_note-16)

Bow shocks are also a common feature in [Herbig Haro objects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herbig_Haro_objects "Herbig Haro objects"), in which a much stronger [collimated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collimated_light "Collimated light") outflow of gas and dust from the star interacts with the interstellar medium, producing bright bow shocks that are visible at optical wavelengths.

Around massive stars
--------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bow_shock&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Around massive stars")]

[![Image 12: Beta Cru](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Bet_cru_WISE.png/250px-Bet_cru_WISE.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Bet_cru_WISE.png)

The bow shock around Beta Crucis (Mimosa), which is the less bright blue star on the middle left. [unWISE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wide-field_Infrared_Survey_Explorer#unWISE_and_CatWISE "Wide-field Infrared Survey Explorer") image

[![Image 13: Zeta Oph](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Sig12-014.png/250px-Sig12-014.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Sig12-014.png)

Zeta Ophiuchi is the most famous bowshock of a massive star. Image is from the Spitzer Space Telescope.

If a massive star is a [runaway star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_kinematics#Runaway_stars "Stellar kinematics"), or if the [interstellar medium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_medium "Interstellar medium") moves relative to the star, it can form an [infrared](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infrared "Infrared") bow-shock that is detectable in 24 μm and sometimes in 8μm of the [Spitzer Space Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spitzer_Space_Telescope "Spitzer Space Telescope") or the W3/W4-channels of [WISE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wide-field_Infrared_Survey_Explorer "Wide-field Infrared Survey Explorer"). In 2016 Kobulnicky et al. created the largest spitzer/WISE bow-shock catalog to date with 709 bow-shock candidates.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bow_shock#cite_note-17)[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bow_shock#cite_note-:1-18) To get a larger bow-shock catalog [The Milky Way Project](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Milky_Way_Project "The Milky Way Project") (a [Citizen Science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Citizen_Science "Citizen Science") project) did map infrared bow-shocks in the galactic plane. The search identified 311 new bow shock candidates.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bow_shock#cite_note-19) This larger catalog will help to understand the stellar wind of massive stars.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bow_shock#cite_note-20)

Magnetic draping effect
-----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bow_shock&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Magnetic draping effect")]

A similar effect, known as the magnetic draping effect, occurs when a super-Alfvénic plasma flow impacts an unmagnetized object such as what happens when the solar wind reaches the ionosphere of Venus:[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bow_shock#cite_note-21) the flow deflects around the object draping the [magnetic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_field "Magnetic field") along the wake flow.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bow_shock#cite_note-22)

The condition for the flow to be super-Alfvénic means that the relative velocity between the flow and object, ![Image 14: {\displaystyle v}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e07b00e7fc0847fbd16391c778d65bc25c452597), is larger than the local [Alfvén velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alfv%C3%A9n_velocity "Alfvén velocity")![Image 15: {\displaystyle V_{A}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6f799f117ef739f7d3645a44d17e2c9f4245ab52) which means a large [Alfvénic Mach number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alfv%C3%A9nic_Mach_number "Alfvénic Mach number"): ![Image 16: {\displaystyle M_{A}\gg 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3a702bf671fdc8e8698f5d3ba79721d301a6f483). For unmagnetized and [electrically conductive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrically_conductive "Electrically conductive") objects, the ambient field creates [electric currents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_currents "Electric currents") inside the object, and into the surrounding plasma, such that the flow is deflected and slowed as the time scale of magnetic [dissipation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dissipation "Dissipation") is much longer than the time scale of magnetic field [advection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Advection "Advection"). The induced currents in turn generate magnetic fields that deflect the flow creating a bow shock. For example, the [ionospheres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ionosphere "Ionosphere") of Mars and Venus provide the conductive environments for the interaction with the solar wind. Without an ionosphere, the flowing magnetized plasma is absorbed by the non-conductive body. The latter occurs, for example, when the solar wind interacts with the [Moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moon "Moon") which has no ionosphere. In magnetic draping, the field lines are wrapped and draped around the leading side of the object creating a narrow sheath which is similar to the bow shocks in the planetary magnetospheres. The concentrated magnetic field increases until the [ram pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ram_pressure "Ram pressure") becomes comparable to the [magnetic pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_pressure "Magnetic pressure") in the sheath:

![Image 17: {\displaystyle \rho _{0}v^{2}={\frac {B_{0}^{2}}{2\mu _{0}}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b8eaddc6bae6b1d9ae58e4e941c452fe7670eda)
where ![Image 18: {\displaystyle \rho _{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d9c04a9d26b86af8c6205ba2a6287fd655b6b714) is the density of the plasma, ![Image 19: {\displaystyle B_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/03a3f39a56ba486e7c6ec89b99f5ae2a21fa75b6) is the draped magnetic field near the object, and ![Image 20: {\displaystyle v}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e07b00e7fc0847fbd16391c778d65bc25c452597) is the relative speed between the plasma and the object. Magnetic draping has been detected around planets, moons, solar coronal mass ejections, and galaxies.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bow_shock#cite_note-23)
