The **Big Bang** is a [physical theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_theory "Physical theory") that describes how the [universe expanded](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expansion_of_the_universe "Expansion of the universe") from an initial state of high [density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_density "Energy density") and [temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperature "Temperature").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-HTUW-1) Various [cosmological models](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_cosmology "Physical cosmology") based on the Big Bang concept explain a broad range of phenomena,[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-FOOTNOTESilk2009208-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-FOOTNOTESingh2004560-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-4) including the abundance of [light elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_element "Light element"), the [cosmic microwave background](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background "Cosmic microwave background") (CMB) [radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation "Electromagnetic radiation"), and [large-scale structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Large-scale_structure_of_the_Universe "Large-scale structure of the Universe"). The uniformity of the [universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe "Universe"), known as the [horizon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horizon_problem "Horizon problem") and [flatness problems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flatness_problem "Flatness problem"), is explained through [cosmic inflation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_inflation "Cosmic inflation"): a phase of accelerated expansion during the earliest stages. Detailed measurements of the expansion rate of the universe place the Big Bang singularity at an estimated 13.787±0.02 billion years ago, which is considered the [age of the universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Age_of_the_universe "Age of the universe"). A wide range of empirical evidence strongly favors the Big Bang event, which is now widely accepted.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-Kragh_1996-5)

Extrapolating this cosmic expansion backward in time using the known [laws of physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_law#Laws_of_physics "Scientific law"), the models describe an extraordinarily hot and dense primordial universe. Physics lacks a widely accepted theory that can model the earliest conditions of the Big Bang.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-Chow-2008-6) As the universe expanded, it cooled sufficiently to allow the formation of [subatomic particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subatomic_particle "Subatomic particle"), and later [atoms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atom "Atom"). These primordial elements—mostly [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen"), with some [helium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium "Helium") and [lithium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lithium "Lithium")—then coalesced under the force of [gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity "Gravity") aided by [dark matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter "Dark matter"), forming early [stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star "Star") and galaxies. Measurements of the redshifts of [supernovae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Type_Ia_supernova "Type Ia supernova") indicate that the [expansion of the universe is accelerating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe "Accelerating expansion of the universe"), an observation attributed to a concept called [dark energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_energy "Dark energy").

The concept of an expanding universe was introduced by the [physicist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physicist "Physicist")[Alexander Friedmann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_Friedmann "Alexander Friedmann") in 1922 with the mathematical derivation of the [Friedmann equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedmann_equations "Friedmann equations").[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-m853-7)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-z155-8)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-c686-9)[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-c625-10) The earliest empirical observation of an expanding universe is known as [Hubble's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble%27s_law "Hubble's law"), published in work by physicist [Edwin Hubble](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edwin_Hubble "Edwin Hubble") in 1929, which discerned that galaxies are moving away from Earth at a rate that accelerates proportionally with distance. [Independent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_multiple_discoveries#20th_century "List of multiple discoveries") of Friedmann's work, and independent of Hubble's observations, in 1931 physicist [Georges Lemaître](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georges_Lema%C3%AEtre "Georges Lemaître") proposed that the universe emerged from a "primeval [atom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atom "Atom")," introducing the modern notion of the Big Bang. In 1964, the CMB was discovered. Over the next few years measurements showed this radiation to be uniform over directions in the sky and the shape of the energy versus intensity curve, both consistent with the Big Bang models of high temperatures and densities in the distant past. By the late 1960s most cosmologists were convinced that competing [steady-state model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steady-state_model "Steady-state model") of cosmic evolution was incorrect.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-11)

There remain aspects of the observed universe that are not yet adequately explained by the Big Bang models. These include the unequal abundances of matter and [antimatter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antimatter "Antimatter") known as [baryon asymmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baryon_asymmetry "Baryon asymmetry"), the detailed nature of [dark matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter "Dark matter") surrounding galaxies, and the origin of [dark energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_energy "Dark energy").[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-peebles-12)

Features of the models
----------------------

### Assumptions

Big Bang cosmology models depend on three major assumptions: the universality of physical laws, the [cosmological principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmological_principle "Cosmological principle"), and that the matter content can be modeled as a [perfect fluid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perfect_fluid "Perfect fluid").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-PDG-2024-13) The universality of physical laws is one of the underlying principles of the [theory of relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theory_of_relativity "Theory of relativity"). The cosmological principle states that on large scales the [universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe "Universe") is [homogeneous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homogeneous_space "Homogeneous space") and [isotropic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotropy "Isotropy")—appearing the same in all directions regardless of location.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-Francis2018-14) A perfect fluid has no viscosity; the pressure of a perfect fluid is proportional to its density.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-KolbTurner2018-15): 49

These ideas were initially taken as postulates, but later efforts were made to test each of them. For example, the first assumption has been tested by observations showing that the largest possible deviation of the [fine-structure constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fine-structure_constant "Fine-structure constant") over much of the age of the universe is of order 10−5.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-16) The key physical law behind these models, [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity") has passed stringent [tests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity "Tests of general relativity") on the scale of the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System") and [binary stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_star "Binary star").[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-17)[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-18) The cosmological principle has been confirmed to a level of 10−5 via observations of the temperature of the CMB. At the scale of the CMB horizon, the universe has been measured to be homogeneous with an upper bound [on the order of](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/On_the_order_of "On the order of") 10% inhomogeneity, as of 1995.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-19)

### Expansion prediction

The cosmological principle dramatically simplifies the equations of general relativity, giving the [Friedmann–Lemaître–Robertson–Walker metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedmann%E2%80%93Lema%C3%AEtre%E2%80%93Robertson%E2%80%93Walker_metric "Friedmann–Lemaître–Robertson–Walker metric") to describe the geometry of the universe and, with the assumption of a perfect fluid, the [Friedmann equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedmann_equations "Friedmann equations") giving the time dependence of that geometry. The only parameter at this level of description is the mass-energy density: the [geometry of the universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shape_of_the_universe "Shape of the universe") and its [expansion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expansion_of_the_universe "Expansion of the universe") is a direct consequence of its density.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-Peacock-1998-20): 73  All of the major features of Big Bang cosmology are related to these results.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-KolbTurner2018-15): 49

### Mass-energy density

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/UniverseComposition.svg/375px-UniverseComposition.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:UniverseComposition.svg)

Estimated relative distribution for components of the energy density of the universe. (In February 2015, the European-led research team behind the [Planck cosmology probe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_(spacecraft) "Planck (spacecraft)") released new data refining these values to 4.9% ordinary matter, 25.9% dark matter and 69.1% dark energy.)

In Big Bang cosmology, the [mass–energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass%E2%80%93energy_equivalence "Mass–energy equivalence") density controls the shape and evolution of the universe. By combining astronomical observations with known laws of [thermodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamics "Thermodynamics") and [particle physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_physics "Particle physics"), cosmologists have worked out the components of the density over the lifespan of the universe. In the current universe, luminous [matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matter "Matter"), the stars, planets, and so on makes up less than 5% of the density. [Dark matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter "Dark matter") accounts for 27% and [dark energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_energy "Dark energy") the remaining 68%.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-NASA_Planck_Mission-21)

### Horizons

An important feature of the Big Bang spacetime is the presence of [particle horizons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_horizon "Particle horizon"). Since the universe has a finite age, and [light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light "Light") travels at a finite speed, there may be events in the past whose light has not yet had time to reach earth. This places a limit or a _past horizon_ on the most distant objects that can be observed. Conversely, because space is expanding, and more distant objects are receding ever more quickly, light emitted by us today may never "catch up" to very distant objects. This defines a _future horizon_, which limits the events in the future that we will be able to influence. The presence of either type of horizon depends on the details of the [Friedmann–Lemaître–Robertson–Walker (FLRW) metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedmann%E2%80%93Lema%C3%AEtre%E2%80%93Robertson%E2%80%93Walker_metric "Friedmann–Lemaître–Robertson–Walker metric") that describes the expansion of the universe.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-kolb_c3-22)

Our understanding of the universe back to very early times suggests that there is a past horizon, though in practice our view is also limited by the opacity of the universe at early times. So our view cannot extend further backward in time, though the horizon recedes in space. If the expansion of the universe continues to accelerate, there is a future horizon as well.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-kolb_c3-22)

### Thermalization

Some processes in the early universe occurred too slowly, compared to the expansion rate of the universe, to reach approximate [thermodynamic equilibrium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamic_equilibrium "Thermodynamic equilibrium"). Others were fast enough to reach [thermalization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermalization "Thermalization"). The parameter usually used to find out whether a process in the very early universe has reached thermal equilibrium is the ratio between the rate of the process (usually rate of collisions between particles) and the [Hubble parameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble_parameter "Hubble parameter"). The larger the ratio, the more time particles had to thermalize before they were too far away from each other.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-23)

Timeline
--------

According to the Big Bang models, the universe at the beginning was very hot and very compact, and since then it has been expanding and cooling.

### Singularity

Existing theories of physics cannot tell us about the moment of the Big Bang.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-Chow-2008-6) Extrapolation of the expansion of the universe backwards in time using only [classical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_field_theory "Classical field theory") general relativity yields a [gravitational singularity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_singularity "Gravitational singularity") with [infinite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinity#Physics "Infinity")[density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Density "Density") and [temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperature "Temperature") at a finite time in the past.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-Hawking_Ellis_1973-24) However this classical gravitational theory is expected to be inadequate to describe physics under these conditions.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-Peacock-1998-20): 275  Thus the meaning of this singularity in the context of the Big Bang is unclear.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-25)

The earliest time that general relativity can be applied is called the [Planck time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_time "Planck time").[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-Peacock-1998-20): 274  Earlier, during the [Planck epoch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_epoch "Planck epoch"), when the temperature of the universe was close to the [Planck scale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_scale "Planck scale") (around 10 32 K or 10 28 eV) [quantum gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_gravity "Quantum gravity") effects are expected to be dominant. To date there is no accepted theory of quantum gravity; above the Planck energy scale, undiscovered physics could influence the expansion history of the universe.

### Inflation and baryogenesis

The earliest phases of the Big Bang are subject to much speculation, given the lack of available data. In the most common models the universe was filled homogeneously and isotropically with a very high [energy density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_density "Energy density") and huge temperatures and [pressures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pressure "Pressure"), and was very rapidly expanding and cooling. The period up to 10−43 seconds into the expansion, the [Planck epoch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_epoch "Planck epoch"), was a phase in which the four [fundamental forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_force "Fundamental force")—the [electromagnetic force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_force "Electromagnetic force"), the [strong nuclear force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strong_nuclear_force "Strong nuclear force"), the [weak nuclear force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weak-Nuclear_Force "Weak-Nuclear Force"), and the [gravitational force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_force "Gravitational force"), were unified as one.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-:0-26) In this stage, the [characteristic scale length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Characteristic_length "Characteristic length") of the universe was the [Planck length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_length "Planck length"), 1.6×10−35 m, and consequently had a temperature of approximately 10 32 degrees Celsius. Even the very concept of a particle breaks down in these conditions. A proper understanding of this period awaits the development of a theory of [quantum gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_gravity "Quantum gravity").[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-Hawley_Holcomb_2005-27)[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-28) The Planck epoch was succeeded by the [grand unification epoch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grand_unification_epoch "Grand unification epoch") beginning at 10−43 seconds, where gravitation separated from the other forces as the universe's temperature fell.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-:0-26)

At approximately 10−37 seconds into the expansion, a [phase transition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phase_transition "Phase transition") caused a [cosmic inflation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inflation_(cosmology) "Inflation (cosmology)"), during which the universe grew [exponentially](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponential_growth "Exponential growth"), unconstrained by the [light speed invariance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light "Speed of light"), and temperatures dropped by a factor of 100,000. This concept is motivated by the [flatness problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flatness_problem "Flatness problem"), where the [density of matter and energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Density_of_the_universe "Density of the universe") is very close to the critical density needed to produce a [flat universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flat_universe "Flat universe"). That is, the [shape of the universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shape_of_the_universe "Shape of the universe") has no overall [geometric curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spacetime_curvature "Spacetime curvature") due to gravitational influence. Microscopic [quantum fluctuations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_fluctuation "Quantum fluctuation") that occurred because of [Heisenberg's uncertainty principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uncertainty_principle "Uncertainty principle") were "frozen in" by inflation, becoming amplified into the seeds that would later form the large-scale structure of the universe.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-Guth1998-29) At a time around 10−36 seconds, the [electroweak epoch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electroweak_epoch "Electroweak epoch") begins when the strong nuclear force separates from the other forces, with only the electromagnetic force and weak nuclear force remaining unified.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-:1-30)

All of the mass-energy in all of the galaxies currently visible started in a sphere with a radius around 4 x 10-29 m then grew to a sphere with a radius around 0.9 m by the end of inflation.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-Ryden-2016-31): 202  Reheating followed as the [inflaton field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inflaton_field "Inflaton field") decayed, until the universe obtained the temperatures required for the [production](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pair_production "Pair production") of a [quark–gluon plasma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quark%E2%80%93gluon_plasma "Quark–gluon plasma") as well as all other [elementary particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementary_particle "Elementary particle").[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-32)[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-33) Temperatures were so high that the random motions of particles were at [relativistic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_relativity "Special relativity")[speeds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_speed "Relativistic speed"), and [particle–antiparticle pairs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antimatter "Antimatter") of all kinds were being continuously created and destroyed in collisions.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-HTUW-1) At some point, an unknown reaction called [baryogenesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baryogenesis "Baryogenesis") violated the conservation of [baryon number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baryon_number "Baryon number"), leading to a very small excess of [quarks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quark "Quark") and [leptons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lepton "Lepton") over antiquarks and antileptons—of the order of one part in 30 million. This resulted in the predominance of matter over antimatter in the present universe.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-kolb_c6-34)

### Cooling

[![Image 3: A map of the universe, with specks and strands of light of different colors.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/2MASS_LSS_chart-NEW_Nasa.jpg/960px-2MASS_LSS_chart-NEW_Nasa.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:2MASS_LSS_chart-NEW_Nasa.jpg)

Panoramic view of the entire [near-infrared](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infrared#Regions_within_the_infrared "Infrared") sky reveals the distribution of galaxies beyond the [Milky Way](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milky_Way "Milky Way"). Galaxies are color-coded by [redshift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift "Redshift").

The universe continued to decrease in density and fall in temperature, hence the typical energy of each particle was decreasing. [Symmetry-breaking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Explicit_symmetry_breaking "Explicit symmetry breaking") phase transitions put the [fundamental forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_force "Fundamental force") of physics and the parameters of elementary particles into their present form, with the electromagnetic force and weak nuclear force separating at about 10−12 seconds.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-:1-30)[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-kolb_c7-35)

After about 10−11 seconds, the picture becomes less speculative, since particle energies drop to values that can be attained in [particle accelerators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_accelerator "Particle accelerator"). At about 10−6 seconds, [quarks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quark "Quark") and [gluons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gluon "Gluon") combined to form [baryons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baryon "Baryon") such as [protons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton "Proton") and [neutrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron "Neutron"). The small excess of quarks over antiquarks led to a small excess of baryons over antibaryons. The temperature was no longer high enough to create either new proton–antiproton or neutron–antineutron pairs. A mass [annihilation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Annihilation "Annihilation") immediately followed, leaving just one in 10 8 of the original matter particles and none of their [antiparticles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiparticle "Antiparticle").[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-36) A similar process happened at about 1 second for electrons and positrons. After these annihilations, the remaining protons, neutrons and electrons were no longer moving relativistically and the energy density of the universe was dominated by [photons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon "Photon") (with a minor contribution from [neutrinos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutrino "Neutrino")).

A few minutes into the expansion, when the temperature was about a billion [kelvin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvin "Kelvin") and the density of matter in the universe was comparable to the current density of Earth's atmosphere, neutrons combined with protons to form the universe's [deuterium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deuterium "Deuterium") and [helium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium "Helium")[nuclei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_nucleus "Atomic nucleus") in a process called [Big Bang nucleosynthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_nucleosynthesis "Big Bang nucleosynthesis") (BBN).[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-kolb_c4-37) Most protons remained uncombined as hydrogen nuclei.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-peacock_c9-38)

As the universe cooled, the [rest energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rest_mass "Rest mass") density of matter came to gravitationally dominate over that of the photon and neutrino radiation at a time of about 50,000 years. At a time of about 380,000 years, the universe cooled enough that electrons and nuclei combined into neutral [atoms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atom "Atom") (mostly [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen")) in an event called [recombination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recombination_(cosmology) "Recombination (cosmology)"). This process made the previously opaque universe transparent, and the photons that last scattered during this epoch comprise the cosmic microwave background.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-peacock_c9-38)

### Structure formation

[![Image 4](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Heic1401a-Abell2744-20140107.jpg/500px-Heic1401a-Abell2744-20140107.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Heic1401a-Abell2744-20140107.jpg)

[Abell 2744](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abell_2744 "Abell 2744")[galaxy cluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_cluster "Galaxy cluster") – [Hubble Frontier Fields view](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope#Frontier_Fields_program "Hubble Space Telescope")[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-NASA-20140107-39)

After the recombination epoch, the slightly denser regions of the uniformly distributed matter gravitationally attracted nearby matter and thus grew even denser, forming gas clouds, stars, galaxies, and the other astronomical structures observable today.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-HTUW-1) The details of this process depend on the amount and type of matter in the universe. The four possible types of matter are known as [cold dark matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cold_dark_matter "Cold dark matter") (CDM), [warm dark matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Warm_dark_matter "Warm dark matter"), [hot dark matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hot_dark_matter "Hot dark matter"), and [baryonic matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baryon#Baryonic_matter "Baryon"). The best measurements available, from the [Wilkinson Microwave Anisotropy Probe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe "Wilkinson Microwave Anisotropy Probe") (WMAP), show that the data is well-fit by a [Lambda-CDM model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lambda-CDM_model "Lambda-CDM model") in which dark matter is assumed to be cold. This CDM is estimated to make up about 23% of the matter/energy of the universe, while baryonic matter makes up about 4.6%.[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-wmap7year-40)

### Cosmic acceleration

Independent lines of evidence from Type Ia supernovae and the CMB imply that the universe today is dominated by a mysterious form of energy known as [dark energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_energy "Dark energy"), which appears to homogeneously permeate all of space. Observations suggest that 73% of the total energy density of the present day universe is in this form. When the universe was very young it was likely infused with dark energy, but with everything closer together, [gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity "Gravity") predominated, braking the expansion. Eventually, after billions of years of expansion, the declining density of matter relative to the density of dark energy allowed the expansion of the universe to begin to accelerate.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-peebles-12)

Dark energy in its simplest formulation is modeled by a [cosmological constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmological_constant "Cosmological constant") term in [Einstein field equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_field_equations "Einstein field equations") of general relativity, but its composition and mechanism are unknown. More generally, the details of its equation of state and relationship with the Standard Model of particle physics continue to be investigated both through observation and theory.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-peebles-12)

All of this cosmic evolution after the [inflationary epoch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inflationary_epoch "Inflationary epoch") can be rigorously described and modeled by the lambda-CDM model of cosmology, which uses the independent frameworks of [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics") and general relativity. There are no easily testable models that would describe the situation prior to approximately 10−15 seconds.[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-FOOTNOTEManly2011chpt._7:_%22The_Ultimate_Free_Lunch%22[[Category:Wikipedia_articles_needing_page_number_citations_from_January_2020]]%3Csup_class=%22noprint_Inline-Template_%22_style=%22white-space:nowrap;%22%3E&#91;%3Ci%3E[[Wikipedia:Citing_sources|%3Cspan_title=%22This_citation_requires_a_reference_to_the_specific_page_or_range_of_pages_in_which_the_material_appears.&#32;(January_2020)%22%3Epage&nbsp;needed%3C/span%3E]]%3C/i%3E&#93;%3C/sup%3E-41) Understanding this earliest of eras in the history of the universe is one of the greatest [unsolved problems in physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_unsolved_problems_in_physics "List of unsolved problems in physics").

Concept history
---------------

### Etymology

[English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English_people "English people")[astronomer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomer "Astronomer")[Fred Hoyle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fred_Hoyle "Fred Hoyle") is credited with coining the term "Big Bang" during a talk for a March 1949 [BBC Radio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BBC_Radio "BBC Radio") broadcast,[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-42) saying: "These theories were based on the hypothesis that all the matter in the universe was created in one big bang at a particular time in the remote past."[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-43)[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-Kragh2013-44) However, it did not catch on until the 1970s.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-Kragh2013-44)

It is popularly reported that Hoyle, who favored an alternative "[steady-state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steady-state_model "Steady-state model")" cosmological model, intended this to be pejorative,[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-45)[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-Mathew2013-46)[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-Ferris-47) but Hoyle explicitly denied this and said it was just a striking image meant to highlight the difference between the two models.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-Croswell1995_chpt9-48)[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-Mitton2011_p129-49)[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-51)[Helge Kragh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helge_Kragh "Helge Kragh") writes that the evidence for the claim that it was meant as a pejorative is "unconvincing", and mentions a number of indications that it was not a pejorative.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-Kragh2013-44)

A primordial singularity is sometimes called "the Big Bang",[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-52) but the term can also refer to a more generic early hot, dense phase.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-53) The term itself has been argued to be a misnomer because it evokes an explosion.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-Kragh2013-44)[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-54) The argument is that whereas an explosion suggests expansion into a surrounding space, the Big Bang only describes the intrinsic expansion of the contents of the universe.[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-55)[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-56) Another issue pointed out by Santhosh Mathew is that bang implies sound, which is not an important feature of the model.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-Mathew2013-46) However, an attempt to find a more suitable alternative was not successful.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-Kragh2013-44) According to [Timothy Ferris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timothy_Ferris "Timothy Ferris"):[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-Ferris-47)[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-57)

> The term 'big bang' was coined with derisive intent by Fred Hoyle, and its endurance testifies to Sir Fred's creativity and wit. Indeed, the term survived an international competition in which three judges — the television science reporter [Hugh Downs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hugh_Downs "Hugh Downs"), the astronomer [Carl Sagan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Sagan "Carl Sagan"), and myself — sifted through 13,099 entries from 41 countries and concluded that none was apt enough to replace it. No winner was declared, and like it or not, we are stuck with 'big bang'.

### Before the name

[![Image 5](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/XDF-scale.jpg/500px-XDF-scale.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:XDF-scale.jpg)

_[XDF](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble\_Ultra-Deep\_Field#Hubble\_eXtreme\_Deep\_Field "Hubble Ultra-Deep Field")_ size compared to the size of the [Moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moon "Moon") (_XDF_ is the small box to the left of, and nearly below, the Moon) – several thousand galaxies, each consisting of billions of stars, are in this small view.

[![Image 6](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/The_Hubble_eXtreme_Deep_Field.jpg/500px-The_Hubble_eXtreme_Deep_Field.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:The_Hubble_eXtreme_Deep_Field.jpg)

_[XDF](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble\_Ultra-Deep\_Field#Hubble\_eXtreme\_Deep\_Field "Hubble Ultra-Deep Field")_ (2012) view – each light speck is a galaxy – some of these are as old as 13.2 billion years[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-Space-20120925-58) – the universe is estimated to contain 200 billion galaxies.

[![Image 7](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/XDF-separated.jpg/500px-XDF-separated.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:XDF-separated.jpg)

_[XDF](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble\_Ultra-Deep\_Field#Hubble\_eXtreme\_Deep\_Field "Hubble Ultra-Deep Field")_ image shows fully mature galaxies in the foreground plane – nearly mature galaxies from 5 to 9 billion years ago – [protogalaxies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protogalaxy "Protogalaxy"), blazing with [young stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Young_star "Young star"), beyond 9 billion years.

Early cosmological models developed from observations of the structure of the universe and from theoretical considerations. In 1912, [Vesto Slipher](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vesto_Slipher "Vesto Slipher") measured the first [Doppler shift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doppler_effect "Doppler effect") of a "[spiral nebula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spiral_galaxy#Spiral_nebula "Spiral galaxy")" (spiral nebula is the obsolete term for spiral galaxies), and soon discovered that almost all such nebulae were receding from Earth. He did not grasp the cosmological implications of this fact, and indeed at the time it was [highly controversial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Debate_(astronomy) "Great Debate (astronomy)") whether or not these nebulae were "island universes" outside our [Milky Way](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milky_Way "Milky Way").[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-59)[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-60) Ten years later, [Alexander Friedmann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_Friedmann "Alexander Friedmann"), a [Russian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russia "Russia")[cosmologist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_cosmology "Physical cosmology") and [mathematician](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematician "Mathematician"), derived the [Friedmann equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedmann_equations "Friedmann equations") from the Einstein field equations, showing that the universe might be expanding in contrast to the [static universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Static_universe "Static universe") model advocated by [Albert Einstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Einstein "Albert Einstein") at that time.[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-af1922-61)[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-62)

In 1924, [American](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Americans "Americans") astronomer [Edwin Hubble](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edwin_Hubble "Edwin Hubble")'s measurement of the great distance to the nearest spiral nebulae showed that these systems were indeed other galaxies. Starting that same year, Hubble painstakingly developed a series of distance indicators, the forerunner of the [cosmic distance ladder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_distance_ladder "Cosmic distance ladder"), using the 100-inch (2.5 m) [Hooker telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/100-inch_Hooker_telescope "100-inch Hooker telescope") at [Mount Wilson Observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mount_Wilson_Observatory "Mount Wilson Observatory"). This allowed him to estimate distances to galaxies whose [redshifts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift "Redshift") had already been measured, mostly by Slipher. In 1929, Hubble discovered a correlation between distance and [recessional velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recessional_velocity "Recessional velocity")—now known as Hubble's law.[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-hubble-63)[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-christianson-64)

Independently deriving Friedmann's equations in 1927, [Georges Lemaître](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georges_Lema%C3%AEtre "Georges Lemaître"), a [Belgian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belgium "Belgium")[physicist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physicist "Physicist") and [Roman Catholic priest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Catholic_priest "Roman Catholic priest"), proposed that the recession of the nebulae was due to the expansion of the universe.[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-gl1927-65)[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-66) He inferred the relation that Hubble would later observe, given the cosmological principle.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-peebles-12) In 1931, Lemaître went further and suggested that the evident expansion of the universe, if projected back in time, meant that the further in the past the smaller the universe was, until at some finite time in the past all the mass of the universe was concentrated into a single point, a "primeval atom" where and when the fabric of time and space came into existence.[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-67)

In the 1920s and 1930s, almost every major cosmologist preferred an eternal steady-state universe, and several complained that the beginning of time implied by an expanding universe imported religious concepts into physics; this objection was later repeated by supporters of the steady-state theory.[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-68) This perception was enhanced by the fact that the originator of the expanding universe concept, Lemaître, was a Roman Catholic priest.[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-WGBH1927-69)[Arthur Eddington](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arthur_Eddington "Arthur Eddington") agreed with [Aristotle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotle "Aristotle") that the universe did not have a beginning in time, _viz_., that [matter is eternal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eternity_of_the_world "Eternity of the world"). A beginning in time was "repugnant" to him.[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-70)[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-71) Lemaître, however, disagreed:

> If the world has begun with a single [quantum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum "Quantum"), the notions of space and time would altogether fail to have any meaning at the beginning; they would only begin to have a sensible meaning when the original quantum had been divided into a sufficient number of quanta. If this suggestion is correct, the beginning of the world happened a little before the beginning of space and time.[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-72)

During the 1930s, other ideas were proposed as [non-standard cosmologies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-standard_cosmology "Non-standard cosmology") to explain Hubble's observations, including the [Milne model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milne_model "Milne model"),[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-73) the [oscillatory universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclic_model "Cyclic model") (originally suggested by Friedmann, but advocated by Albert Einstein and [Richard C. Tolman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_C._Tolman "Richard C. Tolman"))[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-74) and [Fritz Zwicky](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fritz_Zwicky "Fritz Zwicky")'s [tired light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tired_light "Tired light") hypothesis.[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-75)

After [World War II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II "World War II"), two distinct possibilities emerged. One was Fred Hoyle's steady-state model, whereby new matter would be created as the universe seemed to expand. In this model the universe is roughly the same at any point in time.[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-76) The other was Lemaître's expanding universe theory, advocated and developed by [George Gamow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_Gamow "George Gamow"), who used it to develop a theory for the abundance of chemical elements in the universe.[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-77) and whose associates, [Ralph Alpher](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ralph_Alpher "Ralph Alpher") and [Robert Herman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Herman "Robert Herman"), predicted the cosmic background radiation.[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-78)

### As a named model

Ironically, it was Hoyle who coined the phrase that came to be applied to Lemaître's theory, referring to it as "this _big bang_ idea" during a BBC Radio broadcast in March 1949.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-Mitton2011_p129-49)[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-Kragh2013-44)[[notes 1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-79) For a while, support was split between these two theories. Eventually, the observational evidence, most notably from radio [source counts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Source_counts "Source counts"), began to favor Big Bang over steady state. The discovery and confirmation of the CMB in 1964 secured the Big Bang as the best theory of the origin and evolution of the universe.[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-penzias-80)

In 1968 and 1970, [Roger Penrose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roger_Penrose "Roger Penrose"), [Stephen Hawking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stephen_Hawking "Stephen Hawking"), and [George F. R. Ellis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_F._R._Ellis "George F. R. Ellis") published papers where they showed that [mathematical singularities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Singularity_(mathematics) "Singularity (mathematics)") were an inevitable initial condition of relativistic models of the Big Bang.[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-81)[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-82) Then, from the 1970s to the 1990s, cosmologists worked on characterizing the features of the Big Bang universe and resolving outstanding problems. In 1981, [Alan Guth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alan_Guth "Alan Guth") made a breakthrough in theoretical work on resolving certain outstanding theoretical problems in the Big Bang models with the introduction of an epoch of rapid expansion in the early universe he called "inflation".[[82]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-83) Meanwhile, during these decades, two questions in [observational cosmology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observational_cosmology "Observational cosmology") that generated much discussion and disagreement were over the precise values of the Hubble Constant[[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-84) and the matter-density of the universe (before the discovery of dark energy, thought to be the key predictor for the eventual [fate of the universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultimate_fate_of_the_universe "Ultimate fate of the universe")).[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-85)

Significant progress in Big Bang cosmology has been made since the late 1990s as a result of advances in [telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Telescope "Telescope") technology as well as the analysis of data from satellites such as the [Cosmic Background Explorer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_Background_Explorer "Cosmic Background Explorer") (COBE),[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-cobe-86) the [Hubble Space Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope "Hubble Space Telescope") and WMAP.[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-wmap1year-87) Cosmologists now have fairly precise and accurate measurements of many of the parameters of the Big Bang model, and have made the unexpected discovery that the expansion of the universe appears to be accelerating.[[87]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-88)[[88]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-89)

Observational evidence
----------------------

> "[The] big bang picture is too firmly grounded in data from every area to be proved invalid in its general features."

The Big Bang models offer a comprehensive explanation for a broad range of observed phenomena, including the abundances of the [light elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_element "Chemical element"), the [cosmic microwave background](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background "Cosmic microwave background"), [large-scale structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#Large-scale_structure "Observable universe"), and [Hubble's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble%27s_law "Hubble's law").[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-Wright2009-91) The earliest and most direct observational evidence of the validity of the theory are the expansion of the universe according to Hubble's law (as indicated by the redshifts of galaxies), discovery and measurement of the cosmic microwave background and the relative abundances of light elements produced by [Big Bang nucleosynthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_nucleosynthesis "Big Bang nucleosynthesis") (BBN). More recent evidence includes observations of [galaxy formation and evolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_formation_and_evolution "Galaxy formation and evolution"), and the distribution of [large-scale cosmic structures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Large-scale_structure_of_the_cosmos "Large-scale structure of the cosmos").[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-92) These are sometimes called the "four pillars" of the Big Bang models.[[92]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-93)[[93]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-94)

Precise modern models of the Big Bang appeal to various exotic physical phenomena that have not been observed in terrestrial laboratory experiments or incorporated into the Standard Model of particle physics. Of these features, [dark matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter "Dark matter") is currently the subject of most active laboratory investigations.[[94]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-95) Remaining issues include the [cuspy halo problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cuspy_halo_problem "Cuspy halo problem")[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-Diemand2005-96) and the [dwarf galaxy problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dwarf_galaxy_problem "Dwarf galaxy problem")[[96]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-Mart%C3%ADnez-Delgado-97) of cold dark matter. Dark energy is also an area of intense interest for scientists, but it is not clear whether direct detection of dark energy will be possible.[[97]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-98) Inflation and baryogenesis remain more speculative features of current Big Bang models. Viable, quantitative explanations for such phenomena are still being sought. These are unsolved problems in physics.

### Hubble's law and the expansion of the universe

[![Image 8](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Redshifted.png/250px-Redshifted.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Redshifted.png)

Redshift of absorption lines due to recessional velocity

Observations of distant galaxies and [quasars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasar "Quasar") show that these objects are redshifted: the light emitted from them has been shifted to longer wavelengths. This can be seen by taking a [frequency spectrum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectral_density "Spectral density") of an object and matching the [spectroscopic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopy "Spectroscopy") pattern of [emission or absorption lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectral_line "Spectral line") corresponding to atoms of the chemical elements interacting with the light. These redshifts are [uniformly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homogeneity_(physics) "Homogeneity (physics)") isotropic, distributed evenly among the observed objects in all directions. If the redshift is interpreted as a Doppler shift, the recessional velocity of the object can be calculated. For some galaxies, it is possible to estimate distances via the [cosmic distance ladder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_distance_ladder "Cosmic distance ladder"). When the recessional velocities are plotted against these distances, a linear relationship known as [Hubble's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble%27s_law "Hubble's law") is observed:[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-hubble-63)![Image 9: {\displaystyle v=H_{0}D}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f54dd8bb79c20c2f3240c51cee81c418ea0f9d83) where

Hubble's law implies that the universe is uniformly expanding everywhere. This cosmic expansion was predicted from general relativity by Friedmann in 1922[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-af1922-61) and Lemaître in 1927,[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-gl1927-65) well before Hubble made his 1929 analysis and observations, and it remains the cornerstone of the Big Bang model as developed by Friedmann, Lemaître, Robertson, and Walker.

The theory requires the relation ![Image 10: {\displaystyle v=HD}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cf4c255ecc475a7ce9c30bbc9dde534d48f88892) to hold at all times, where ![Image 11: {\displaystyle D}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f34a0c600395e5d4345287e21fb26efd386990e6) is the proper distance, ![Image 12: {\displaystyle v}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e07b00e7fc0847fbd16391c778d65bc25c452597) is the recessional velocity, and ![Image 13: {\displaystyle v}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e07b00e7fc0847fbd16391c778d65bc25c452597), ![Image 14: {\displaystyle H}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/75a9edddcca2f782014371f75dca39d7e13a9c1b), and ![Image 15: {\displaystyle D}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f34a0c600395e5d4345287e21fb26efd386990e6) vary as the universe expands (hence we write ![Image 16: {\displaystyle H_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/43910602a221b7a4c373791f94793e3008622070) to denote the present-day Hubble "constant"). For distances much smaller than the size of the [observable universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe "Observable universe"), the Hubble redshift can be thought of as the Doppler shift corresponding to the recession velocity ![Image 17: {\displaystyle v}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e07b00e7fc0847fbd16391c778d65bc25c452597). For distances comparable to the size of the observable universe, the attribution of the cosmological redshift becomes more ambiguous, although its interpretation as a kinematic Doppler shift remains the most natural one.[[98]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-Hogg-99)

An unexplained discrepancy with the determination of the Hubble constant is known as [Hubble tension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble_tension "Hubble tension"). Techniques based on observation of the CMB suggest a lower value of this constant compared to the quantity derived from measurements based on the cosmic distance ladder.[[99]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-di_Valentino_2021_153001-100)

### Cosmic microwave background radiation

[![Image 18](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Cmbr.svg/250px-Cmbr.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Cmbr.svg)

The [cosmic microwave background](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background "Cosmic microwave background") spectrum measured by the FIRAS instrument on the [COBE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_Background_Explorer "Cosmic Background Explorer") satellite is the most-precisely measured [blackbody](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_body "Black body") spectrum in nature.[[100]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-dpf99-101) The [data points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_point "Data point") and [error bars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_error_of_estimation "Standard error of estimation") on this graph are obscured by the theoretical curve.

In 1964, [Arno Penzias](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arno_Allan_Penzias "Arno Allan Penzias") and [Robert Wilson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Woodrow_Wilson "Robert Woodrow Wilson") serendipitously discovered the cosmic background radiation, an omnidirectional signal in the [microwave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microwave "Microwave") band.[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-penzias-80) Their discovery provided substantial confirmation of the big-bang predictions by Alpher, Herman and Gamow around 1950. Through the 1970s, the radiation was found to be approximately consistent with a [blackbody](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_body "Black body") spectrum in all directions; this spectrum has been redshifted by the expansion of the universe, and today corresponds to approximately 2.725 K. This tipped the balance of evidence in favor of the Big Bang model, and Penzias and Wilson were awarded the 1978 [Nobel Prize in Physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physics "Nobel Prize in Physics").

The _surface of last scattering_ corresponding to emission of the CMB occurs shortly after _[recombination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recombination\_(cosmology) "Recombination (cosmology)")_, the epoch when neutral hydrogen becomes stable. Prior to this, the universe comprised a hot dense photon-baryon plasma sea where photons were quickly [scattered](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomson_scattering "Thomson scattering") from free charged particles. Peaking at around 372±14[kyr](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kyr "Kyr"),[[101]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-WMAP2003Spergel-102) the mean free path for a photon becomes long enough to reach the present day and the universe becomes transparent.

[![Image 19](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/WMAP_2012.png/330px-WMAP_2012.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:WMAP_2012.png)

9 year WMAP image of the cosmic microwave background radiation (2012).[[102]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-arXiv-20121220-103)[[103]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-Space-20121221-104) The radiation is [isotropic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotropy "Isotropy") to roughly one part in 100,000.[[104]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-105)

In 1989, [NASA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NASA "NASA") launched COBE, which made two major advances: in 1990, high-precision spectrum measurements showed that the CMB frequency spectrum is an almost perfect blackbody with no deviations at a level of 1 part in 10 4, and measured a residual temperature of 2.726 K (more recent measurements have revised this figure down slightly to 2.7255 K); then in 1992, further COBE measurements discovered tiny fluctuations ([anisotropies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anisotropy "Anisotropy")) in the CMB temperature across the sky, at a level of about one part in 10 5.[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-cobe-86)[John C. Mather](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_C._Mather "John C. Mather") and [George Smoot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_Smoot "George Smoot") were awarded the 2006 Nobel Prize in Physics for their leadership in these results.

During the following decade, CMB anisotropies were further investigated by a large number of ground-based and balloon experiments. In 2000–2001, several experiments, most notably [BOOMERanG](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BOOMERanG_experiment "BOOMERanG experiment"), found the [shape of the universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shape_of_the_universe "Shape of the universe") to be spatially almost flat by measuring the typical angular size (the size on the sky) of the anisotropies.[[105]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-106)[[106]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-107)[[107]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-108)

In early 2003, the first results of the Wilkinson Microwave Anisotropy Probe were released, yielding what were at the time the most accurate values for some of the cosmological parameters. The results disproved several specific cosmic inflation models, but are consistent with the inflation theory in general.[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-wmap1year-87) The _[Planck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck\_(spacecraft) "Planck (spacecraft)")_ space probe was launched in May 2009. Other ground and balloon-based [cosmic microwave background experiments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_cosmic_microwave_background_experiments "List of cosmic microwave background experiments") are ongoing.

### Abundance of primordial elements

[![Image 20](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Universe-09-00183-g004.png/500px-Universe-09-00183-g004.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Universe-09-00183-g004.png)

Time evolution of light element abundances during Big Bang nucleosynthesis

Using Big Bang models, it is possible to calculate the expected concentration of the isotopes [helium-4](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium-4 "Helium-4") (4 He), [helium-3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium-3 "Helium-3") (3 He), deuterium (2 H), and [lithium-7](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotopes_of_lithium#Lithium-7 "Isotopes of lithium") (7 Li) in the universe as ratios to the amount of ordinary hydrogen.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-kolb_c4-37) The relative abundances depend on a single parameter, the ratio of photons to baryons. This value can be calculated independently from the detailed structure of CMB fluctuations. The ratios predicted (by mass, not by abundance) are about 0.25 for 4 He:H, about 10−3 for 2 H:H, about 10−4 for 3 He:H, and about 10−9 for 7 Li:H.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-kolb_c4-37)

The measured abundances all agree at least roughly with those predicted from a single value of the baryon-to-photon ratio. The agreement is excellent for deuterium, close but formally discrepant for 4 He, and off by a factor of two for 7 Li (this anomaly is known as the [cosmological lithium problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmological_lithium_problem "Cosmological lithium problem")); in the latter two cases, there are substantial [systematic uncertainties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observational_error#Random_errors_versus_systematic_errors "Observational error"). Nonetheless, the general consistency with abundances predicted by BBN is strong evidence for the Big Bang, as the theory is the only known explanation for the relative abundances of light elements, and it is virtually impossible to "tune" the Big Bang to produce much more or less than 20–30% helium.[[108]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-109) Indeed, there is no obvious reason outside of the Big Bang that, for example, the young universe before [star formation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_formation "Star formation"), as determined by studying matter supposedly free of [stellar nucleosynthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis "Stellar nucleosynthesis") products, should have more helium than deuterium or more deuterium than 3 He, and in constant ratios, too.[[109]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-Ryden2003-110): 182–185

### Galactic evolution and distribution

Detailed observations of the [morphology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_morphological_classification "Galaxy morphological classification") and distribution of galaxies and [quasars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasar "Quasar") are in agreement with the current Big Bang models. A combination of observations and theory suggest that the first quasars and galaxies formed within a billion years after the Big Bang,[[110]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-111) and since then, larger structures have been forming, such as [galaxy clusters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_cluster "Galaxy cluster") and [superclusters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supercluster "Supercluster").[[111]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-Bertschinger-112)

Populations of stars have been aging and evolving, so that distant galaxies (which are observed as they were in the early universe) appear very different from nearby galaxies (observed in a more recent state). Moreover, galaxies that formed relatively recently appear markedly different from galaxies formed at similar distances but shortly after the Big Bang. These observations are strong arguments against the steady-state model. Observations of star formation, galaxy and quasar distributions and larger structures, agree well with Big Bang simulations of the formation of structure in the universe, and are helping to complete details of the theory.[[111]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-Bertschinger-112)[[112]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-113)

### Primordial gas clouds

[![Image 21](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/PIA17993-DetectorsForInfantUniverseStudies-20140317.jpg/250px-PIA17993-DetectorsForInfantUniverseStudies-20140317.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:PIA17993-DetectorsForInfantUniverseStudies-20140317.jpg)

[Focal plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Focal_plane "Focal plane") of [BICEP2 telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BICEP_and_Keck_Array "BICEP and Keck Array") under a microscope – used to search for polarization in the CMB[[113]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-BICEP2-2014-114)[[114]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-NASA-20140317-115)[[115]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-NYT-20140317-116)[[116]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-NYT-20140324-117)

In 2011, astronomers found what they believe to be pristine clouds of primordial gas by analyzing absorption lines in the spectra of distant quasars. Before this discovery, all other astronomical objects have been observed to contain heavy elements that are formed in stars. Despite being sensitive to carbon, oxygen, and silicon, these three elements were not detected in these two clouds.[[117]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-118)[[118]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-119) Since the clouds of gas have no detectable levels of heavy elements, they likely formed in the first few minutes after the Big Bang, during BBN.

### Other lines of evidence

The age of the universe as estimated from the Hubble expansion and the CMB is now in agreement with other estimates using the ages of the oldest stars, both as measured by applying the theory of [stellar evolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_evolution "Stellar evolution") to globular clusters and through [radiometric dating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiometric_dating "Radiometric dating") of individual [Population II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_population#Population_II_stars "Stellar population") stars.[[119]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-120) It is also in agreement with age estimates based on measurements of the expansion using [Type Ia supernovae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Type_Ia_supernova "Type Ia supernova") and measurements of temperature fluctuations in the cosmic microwave background.[[120]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-Planck_2015-121) The agreement of independent measurements of this age supports the [Lambda-CDM](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lambda-CDM_model "Lambda-CDM model") (ΛCDM) model, since the model is used to relate some of the measurements to an age estimate, and all estimates turn agree. Still, some observations of objects from the relatively early universe (in particular quasar [APM 08279+5255](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/APM_08279%2B5255 "APM 08279+5255")) raise concern as to whether these objects had enough time to form so early in the ΛCDM model.[[121]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-122)[[122]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-123)

The prediction that the CMB temperature was higher in the past has been experimentally supported by observations of very low temperature absorption lines in gas clouds at high redshift.[[123]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-124) This prediction also implies that the amplitude of the [Sunyaev–Zel'dovich effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sunyaev%E2%80%93Zeldovich_effect "Sunyaev–Zeldovich effect") in clusters of galaxies does not depend directly on redshift. Observations have found this to be roughly true, but this effect depends on cluster properties that do change with cosmic time, making precise measurements difficult.[[124]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-125)[[125]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-126)

### Future observations

Future [gravitational-wave observatories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational-wave_observatory "Gravitational-wave observatory") might be able to detect primordial [gravitational waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_wave "Gravitational wave"), relics of the early universe, up to less than a second after the Big Bang.[[126]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-Ghosh-127)[[127]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-Billings-128)

As with any theory, a number of mysteries and problems have arisen as a result of the development of the Big Bang models. Some of these mysteries and problems have been resolved while others are still outstanding. Proposed solutions to some of the problems in the Big Bang model have revealed new mysteries of their own. For example, the [horizon problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horizon_problem "Horizon problem"), the [magnetic monopole problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inflation_(cosmology)#Magnetic-monopole_problem "Inflation (cosmology)"), and the [flatness problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flatness_problem "Flatness problem") are most commonly resolved with inflation theory, but the details of the inflationary universe are still left unresolved and many, including some founders of the theory, say it has been disproven.[[128]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-129)[[129]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-130)[[130]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-131)[[131]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-132) What follows are a list of the mysterious aspects of the Big Bang concept still under intense investigation by cosmologists and [astrophysicists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrophysics "Astrophysics").

### Baryon asymmetry

It is not yet understood why the universe has more matter than antimatter.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-kolb_c6-34) It is generally assumed that when the universe was young and very hot it was in [statistical equilibrium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistical_physics "Statistical physics") and contained equal numbers of baryons and antibaryons. Both matter and antimatter were much more abundant than today, with a tiny asymmetry of only one part in 10 billion. The matter and antimatter collided and annihilated, leaving only the residual amount of matter. Today observations suggest that the universe, including its most distant parts, is made almost entirely of normal matter with very little antimatter.[[132]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-133)

If matter and antimatter were in complete symmetry, then annihilation would result in only photons and virtually no matter at all, which is obviously not what is observed. A process called baryogenesis was hypothesized to account for the asymmetry. For baryogenesis to occur, the [Sakharov conditions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sakharov_conditions "Sakharov conditions") must be satisfied. These require that baryon number is not conserved, that [C-symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C-symmetry "C-symmetry") and [CP-symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CP_violation "CP violation") are violated and that the universe depart from [thermodynamic equilibrium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamic_equilibrium "Thermodynamic equilibrium").[[133]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-sakharov-134)[[134]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-135) All these conditions occur in the Standard Model, but the effects are not strong enough to explain the present baryon asymmetry.

### Dark energy

Measurements of the redshift–[magnitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apparent_magnitude "Apparent magnitude") relation for [type Ia supernovae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Type_Ia_supernova "Type Ia supernova") indicate that the expansion of the universe has been accelerating since the universe was about half its present age. To explain this acceleration, cosmological models require that much of the energy in the universe consists of a component with large negative pressure, dubbed "dark energy".[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-peebles-12)

Dark energy, though speculative, solves numerous problems. Measurements of the cosmic microwave background indicate that the universe is very nearly spatially flat, and therefore according to general relativity the universe must have almost exactly the [critical density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedmann_equations#Density_parameter "Friedmann equations") of mass/energy. But the mass density of the universe can be measured from its gravitational clustering, and is found to have only about 30% of the critical density.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-peebles-12) Since theory suggests that dark energy does not cluster in the usual way it is the best explanation for the "missing" energy density. Dark energy also helps to explain two geometrical measures of the overall curvature of the universe, one using the frequency of [gravitational lenses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_lens "Gravitational lens"),[[135]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-136) and the other using the characteristic pattern of the large-scale structure--[baryon acoustic oscillations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baryon_acoustic_oscillations "Baryon acoustic oscillations")--as a cosmic ruler.[[136]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-137)[[137]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-138)

Negative pressure is believed to be a property of [vacuum energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vacuum_energy "Vacuum energy"), but the exact nature and existence of dark energy remains one of the great mysteries of the Big Bang. Results from the WMAP team in 2008 are in accordance with a universe that consists of 73% dark energy, 23% dark matter, 4.6% regular matter and less than 1% neutrinos.[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-wmap7year-40) According to theory, the energy density in matter decreases with the expansion of the universe, but the dark energy density remains constant (or nearly so) as the universe expands. Therefore, matter made up a larger fraction of the total energy of the universe in the past than it does today, but its fractional contribution will fall in the [far future](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Far_future "Far future") as dark energy becomes even more dominant.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

The dark energy component of the universe has been explained by theorists using a variety of competing theories including Einstein's cosmological constant but also extending to more exotic forms of [quintessence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quintessence_(physics) "Quintessence (physics)") or other modified gravity schemes.[[138]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-139) A [cosmological constant problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmological_constant_problem "Cosmological constant problem"), sometimes called the "most embarrassing problem in physics", results from the apparent discrepancy between the measured energy density of dark energy, and the one naively predicted from [Planck units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_units "Planck units").[[139]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-140)

### Dark matter

[![Image 22](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Cosmological_Composition_%E2%80%93_Pie_Chart.svg/500px-Cosmological_Composition_%E2%80%93_Pie_Chart.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Cosmological_Composition_%E2%80%93_Pie_Chart.svg)

[Chart](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pie_chart "Pie chart") shows the proportion of different components of the universe – about 95% is [dark matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter "Dark matter") and [dark energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_energy "Dark energy").

During the 1970s and the 1980s, various observations showed that there is not sufficient visible matter in the universe to account for the apparent strength of gravitational forces within and between galaxies. This led to the idea that up to 90% of the matter in the universe is dark matter that does not emit light or interact with normal baryonic matter. In addition, the assumption that the universe is mostly normal matter led to predictions that were strongly inconsistent with observations. In particular, the universe today is far more lumpy and contains far less deuterium than can be accounted for without dark matter. While dark matter has always been controversial, it is inferred by various observations: the anisotropies in the CMB, the [galaxy rotation problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_rotation_problem "Galaxy rotation problem"), [galaxy cluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_cluster "Galaxy cluster")[velocity dispersions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity_dispersion "Velocity dispersion"), large-scale structure distributions, [gravitational lensing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_lens "Gravitational lens") studies, and [X-ray measurements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray_astronomy "X-ray astronomy") of galaxy clusters.[[140]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-141)

Indirect evidence for dark matter comes from its gravitational influence on other matter, as no dark matter particles have been observed in laboratories. Many particle physics candidates for dark matter have been proposed, and several projects to detect them directly are underway.[[141]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-pdg-142)

Additionally, there are outstanding problems associated with the currently favored cold dark matter model which include the [dwarf galaxy problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dwarf_galaxy_problem "Dwarf galaxy problem")[[96]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-Mart%C3%ADnez-Delgado-97) and the [cuspy halo problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cuspy_halo_problem "Cuspy halo problem").[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-Diemand2005-96) Alternative theories have been proposed that do not require a large amount of undetected matter, but instead modify the laws of gravity established by Newton and Einstein; yet no alternative theory has been as successful as the cold dark matter proposal in explaining all extant observations.[[142]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-143)

### Horizon problem

The horizon problem results from the premise that information cannot travel [faster than light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Faster-than-light "Faster-than-light"). In a universe of finite age this sets a limit—the particle horizon—on the separation of any two regions of space that are in [causal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Causality_(physics) "Causality (physics)") contact.[[143]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-kolb_c8-144) The observed isotropy of the CMB is problematic in this regard: if the universe had been dominated by radiation or matter at all times up to the epoch of last scattering, the particle horizon at that time would correspond to about 2 degrees on the sky. There would then be no mechanism to cause wider regions to have the same temperature.[[109]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-Ryden2003-110): 191

A resolution to this apparent inconsistency is offered by inflation theory in which a homogeneous and isotropic [scalar energy field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_field "Scalar field") dominates the universe at some very early period (before baryogenesis). During inflation, the universe undergoes [exponential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponential_growth "Exponential growth") expansion, and the particle horizon expands much more rapidly than previously assumed, so that regions presently on opposite sides of the observable universe are well inside each other's particle horizon. The observed isotropy of the CMB then follows from the fact that this larger region was in causal contact before the beginning of inflation.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-Guth1998-29): 180

Heisenberg's uncertainty principle predicts that during the inflationary phase there would be [quantum thermal fluctuations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primordial_fluctuations "Primordial fluctuations"), which would be magnified to a cosmic scale. These fluctuations served as the seeds for all the current structures in the universe.[[109]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-Ryden2003-110): 207  Inflation predicts that the primordial fluctuations are nearly [scale invariant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scale_invariance "Scale invariance") and [Gaussian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_distribution "Normal distribution"), which has been confirmed by measurements of the CMB.[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-wmap1year-87): sec 6

A related issue to the classic horizon problem arises because in most standard cosmological inflation models, inflation ceases well before [electroweak symmetry breaking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Higgs_mechanism "Higgs mechanism") occurs, so inflation should not be able to prevent large-scale discontinuities in the [electroweak vacuum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/False_vacuum "False vacuum") since distant parts of the observable universe were causally separate when the [electroweak epoch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electroweak_epoch "Electroweak epoch") ended.[[144]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-145)

### Magnetic monopoles

The magnetic monopole objection was raised in the late 1970s. [Grand unified theories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grand_Unified_Theory "Grand Unified Theory") (GUTs) predicted [topological defects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_defect "Topological defect") in space that would manifest as [magnetic monopoles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_monopole "Magnetic monopole"). These objects would be produced efficiently in the hot early universe, resulting in a density much higher than is consistent with observations, given that no monopoles have been found. This problem is resolved by cosmic inflation, which removes all point defects from the observable universe, in the same way that it drives the geometry to flatness.[[143]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-kolb_c8-144)

### Flatness problem

[![Image 23](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/End_of_universe.jpg/500px-End_of_universe.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:End_of_universe.jpg)

The overall [geometry of the universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shape_of_the_universe "Shape of the universe") is determined by whether the [Omega cosmological parameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedmann_equations#Density_parameter "Friedmann equations") is less than, equal to or greater than 1. Shown from top to bottom are a [closed universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shape_of_the_universe#Universe_with_positive_curvature "Shape of the universe") with positive curvature, a [hyperbolic universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shape_of_the_universe#Universe_with_negative_curvature "Shape of the universe") with negative curvature and a [flat universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shape_of_the_universe#Universe_with_zero_curvature "Shape of the universe") with zero curvature.

The flatness problem (also known as the oldness problem) is an observational problem associated with a FLRW.[[143]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-kolb_c8-144) The universe may have positive, negative, or zero spatial [curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvature "Curvature") depending on its total energy density. Curvature is negative if its density is less than the critical density; positive if greater; and zero at the critical density, in which case space is said to be _flat_. Observations indicate the universe is consistent with being flat.[[145]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-Filippenko2002-146)[[146]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-Krauss2009-147)

The problem is that any small departure from the critical density grows with time, and yet the universe today remains very close to flat.[[notes 2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-148) Given that a natural timescale for departure from flatness might be the [Planck time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_time "Planck time"), 10−43 seconds,[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-HTUW-1) the fact that the universe has reached neither a [heat death](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat_death_of_the_universe "Heat death of the universe") nor a [Big Crunch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Crunch "Big Crunch") after billions of years requires an explanation. For instance, even at the relatively late age of a few minutes (the time of nucleosynthesis), the density of the universe must have been within one part in 10 14 of its critical value, or it would not exist as it does today.[[147]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-149)

Misconceptions
--------------

In addition to [confusion about the nature of cosmic expansion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expansion_of_the_universe#Confusions_about_cosmic_expansion "Expansion of the universe"), the Big Bang model itself is sometimes misunderstood.

One of the common misconceptions about the Big Bang model is that it fully explains the [origin of the universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmogony "Cosmogony"). However, the Big Bang model does not describe how energy, time, and space were caused, but rather it describes the emergence of the present universe from an ultra-dense and high-temperature initial state.[[148]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-150)

Another common misconception relates to the recession speeds associated with Hubble's law. These are not velocities in a relativistic sense (for example, they are not related to the spatial components of [4-velocities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/4-velocity "4-velocity")). Therefore, it is not remarkable that according to Hubble's law, galaxies farther than the [Hubble distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble_distance "Hubble distance") recede faster than the speed of light. Such recession speeds do not correspond to [faster-than-light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Faster-than-light "Faster-than-light") travel.[[149]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-Davis_Lineweaver2004-151)

Implications
------------

Given current understanding, scientific extrapolations about the future of the universe are only possible for finite durations, albeit for much longer periods than the current age of the universe. Anything beyond that becomes increasingly speculative. Likewise, at present, a proper understanding of the origin of the universe can only be subject to conjecture.[[150]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-Starobinsky_2000-152)

### Pre–Big Bang cosmology

The Big Bang explains the evolution of the universe from a starting density and temperature that is well beyond humanity's capability to replicate, so extrapolations to the most extreme conditions and earliest times are necessarily more speculative. Lemaître called this initial state the "_primeval atom_" while Gamow called the material "_[ylem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ylem "Ylem")_". How the initial state of the universe originated is still an open question, but the Big Bang model does constrain some of its characteristics. For example, if specific [laws of nature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_law "Scientific law") were to come to existence in a random way, inflation models show, some combinations of these are far more probable,[[151]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHawking198869-153) partly explaining why our Universe is rather stable. Another possible explanation for the stability of the Universe could be a hypothetical multiverse, which assumes every possible universe to exist, and thinking species could only emerge in those stable enough.[[152]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-154) A flat universe implies a balance between [gravitational potential energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_energy "Gravitational energy") and other energy forms, requiring no additional energy to be created.[[145]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-Filippenko2002-146)[[146]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-Krauss2009-147)

The Big Bang theory is built upon the equations of classical general relativity, which are not expected to be valid at the origin of cosmic time, as the temperature of the universe approaches the Planck scale. Correcting this will require the development of a correct treatment of quantum gravity.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-Hawking_Ellis_1973-24) Certain quantum gravity treatments, such as the [Wheeler–DeWitt equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wheeler%E2%80%93DeWitt_equation "Wheeler–DeWitt equation"), imply that time itself could be an [emergent property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emergence#Nonliving,_physical_systems "Emergence").[[153]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-155) As such, physics may conclude that [time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time "Time") did not exist before the Big Bang.[[154]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-156)[[155]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-157)[[156]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-158)[[157]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-159)[[158]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-160)

While it is not known what could have preceded the hot dense state of the early universe or how and why it originated, or even whether such questions are sensible, speculation abounds on the subject of "[cosmogony](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmogony "Cosmogony")".

Some speculative proposals in this regard, each of which entails untested hypotheses, are:

*   The simplest models, in which the Big Bang was caused by [quantum fluctuations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_fluctuation "Quantum fluctuation"). That scenario had very little chance of happening, but, according to the [totalitarian principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Totalitarian_principle "Totalitarian principle"), even the most improbable event will eventually happen. It took place instantly, in our perspective, due to the absence of perceived time before the Big Bang.[[159]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-161)[[160]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-162)[[161]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-163)[[162]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-164)
*   [Emergent Universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emergent_Universe "Emergent Universe") models, which feature a low-activity past-eternal era before the Big Bang, resembling ancient ideas of a [cosmic egg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_egg "Cosmic egg") and birth of the world out of [primordial chaos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chaos_(cosmogony) "Chaos (cosmogony)").
*   Models in which the whole of spacetime is finite, including the [Hartle–Hawking no-boundary condition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hartle%E2%80%93Hawking_state "Hartle–Hawking state"). For these cases, the Big Bang does represent the limit of time but without a singularity.[[163]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-165) In such a case, the universe is self-sufficient.[[164]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHawking198871-166)
*   [Brane cosmology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brane_cosmology "Brane cosmology") models, in which inflation is due to the movement of [branes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brane "Brane") in [string theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/String_theory "String theory"); the pre-Big Bang model; the [ekpyrotic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ekpyrotic_universe "Ekpyrotic universe") model, in which the Big Bang is the result of a collision between branes; and the [cyclic model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclic_model "Cyclic model"), a variant of the ekpyrotic model in which collisions occur periodically. In the latter model the Big Bang was preceded by a Big Crunch and the universe cycles from one process to the other.[[165]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-167)[[166]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-168)[[167]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-rebirth-169)[[168]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-rebirth2-170)[[169]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-171)
*   [Eternal inflation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eternal_inflation "Eternal inflation"), in which universal inflation ends locally here and there in a random fashion, each end-point leading to a _bubble universe_, expanding from its own big bang.[[170]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-172)[[171]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-173) This is sometimes referred to as pre-big bang inflation.[[172]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-174)

Proposals in the last two categories see the Big Bang as an event in either a much larger and [older universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roger_Penrose#An_earlier_universe "Roger Penrose") or in a [multiverse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiverse "Multiverse").

### Ultimate fate of the universe

Before observations of dark energy, cosmologists considered two scenarios for the future of the universe. If the mass density of the universe were greater than the critical density, then the universe would reach a maximum size and then begin to collapse. It would become denser and hotter again, ending with a state similar to that in which it started—a [Big Crunch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Crunch "Big Crunch").[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-kolb_c3-22)

Alternatively, if the density in the universe were equal to or below the critical density, the expansion would slow down but never stop. Star formation would cease with the consumption of interstellar gas in each galaxy; stars would burn out, leaving [white dwarfs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_dwarf "White dwarf"), [neutron stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron_star "Neutron star"), and black holes. Collisions between these would result in mass accumulating into larger and larger black holes. The average temperature of the universe would very gradually asymptotically approach [absolute zero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_zero "Absolute zero")—a [Big Freeze](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Future_of_an_expanding_universe "Future of an expanding universe").[[173]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-175) Moreover, if protons are [unstable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton_decay "Proton decay"), then baryonic matter would disappear, leaving only radiation and black holes. Eventually, black holes would evaporate by emitting [Hawking radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hawking_radiation "Hawking radiation"). The [entropy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy "Entropy") of the universe would increase to the point where no organized form of energy could be extracted from it, a scenario known as heat death.[[174]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-dying-176)

Modern observations of accelerating expansion imply that more and more of the currently visible universe will pass beyond our [event horizon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Event_horizon "Event horizon") and out of contact with us. The eventual result is not known. The Lambda-CDM model of the universe contains dark energy in the form of a cosmological constant. This theory suggests that only gravitationally bound systems, such as galaxies, will remain together, and they too will be subject to heat death as the universe expands and cools. Other explanations of dark energy, called [phantom dark energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phantom_dark_energy "Phantom dark energy") theories, suggest that ultimately galaxy clusters, stars, planets, atoms, nuclei, and matter itself will be torn apart by the ever-increasing expansion in a so-called [Big Rip](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Rip "Big Rip").[[175]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-177)

### Religious and philosophical interpretations

As a description of the origin of the universe, the Big Bang has significant bearing on religion and philosophy.[[176]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-Harris2002-178)[[177]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-Frame2009-179) As a result, it has become one of the liveliest areas in the discourse between [science and religion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relationship_between_religion_and_science "Relationship between religion and science").[[178]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-Harrison2010-180) Some believe the Big Bang implies a creator,[[179]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-181)[[180]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-182)[[181]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-183)[[182]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-184) while others argue that Big Bang cosmology makes the notion of a creator superfluous.[[177]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-Frame2009-179)[[183]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_cosmology#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHawking1988Introduction:_%22...&nbsp;a_universe_with_no_edge_in_space,_no_beginning_or_end_in_time,_and_nothing_for_a_Creator_to_do.%22_%E2%80%94_[[Carl_Sagan]]-185)

The term "Big Bang" is an unfortunate misnomer. It implies an "explosion," and explosions are events that happen _in_ space.
