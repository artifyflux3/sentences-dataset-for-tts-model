**Pythagoras of Samos**[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-spelling-2) ([Ancient Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language "Ancient Greek language"): Πυθαγόρας; [c.](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/circa#English "wikt:circa") 570– c. 495 BC)[[b]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-date-3) was an ancient [Ionian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ionians "Ionians")[Greek philosopher](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek_philosophy "Ancient Greek philosophy"), [polymath](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polymath "Polymath"), and the eponymous founder of [Pythagoreanism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoreanism "Pythagoreanism"). His political and religious teachings were well known in [Magna Graecia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magna_Graecia "Magna Graecia") and influenced the philosophies of [Plato](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plato "Plato"), [Aristotle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotle "Aristotle"), and, through them, [Western philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_philosophy "Western philosophy"). Modern scholars disagree regarding Pythagoras's education and influences, but most agree that he travelled to [Croton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crotone "Crotone") in southern Italy around 530 BC, where he founded a school in which initiates were allegedly sworn to secrecy and lived a communal, [ascetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asceticism "Asceticism") lifestyle.

In antiquity, Pythagoras was credited with [mathematical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_mathematics "Greek mathematics") and scientific discoveries, such as the [Pythagorean theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagorean_theorem "Pythagorean theorem"), [Pythagorean tuning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagorean_tuning "Pythagorean tuning"), the [five regular solids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platonic_solids "Platonic solids"), the [theory of proportions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proportionality_(mathematics) "Proportionality (mathematics)"), the [sphericity of the Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_Earth "Spherical Earth"), the identity of the [morning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphorus_(morning_star) "Phosphorus (morning star)") and [evening stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hesperus "Hesperus") as the planet [Venus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Venus "Venus"), and the division of the globe into [five climatic zones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geographical_zone "Geographical zone"). He was reputedly the first man to call himself a philosopher ("lover of wisdom").[[c]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-philosopher-4) Historians debate whether Pythagoras made these discoveries and pronouncements, as some of the accomplishments credited to him likely originated earlier or were made by his colleagues or successors, such as [Hippasus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hippasus "Hippasus") and [Philolaus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philolaus "Philolaus").

The teaching most securely identified with Pythagoras is the "transmigration of souls" or _[metempsychosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metempsychosis "Metempsychosis")_, which holds that every [soul](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soul "Soul") is [immortal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immortality "Immortality") and, upon death, [enters into a new body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reincarnation "Reincarnation"). He may have also devised the doctrine of _[musica universalis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Musica\_universalis "Musica universalis")_, which holds that the [planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_planet "Classical planet") move according to [mathematical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics") ratios and thus resonate to produce an inaudible symphony of music. Following Croton's decisive victory over [Sybaris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sybaris "Sybaris") in around 510 BC, Pythagoras's followers came into conflict with supporters of [democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_democracy "Greek democracy"), and their meeting houses were burned. Pythagoras may have been killed during this persecution, or he may have escaped to [Metapontum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metapontum "Metapontum") and died there.

Pythagoras influenced Plato whose [dialogues](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socratic_dialogue "Socratic dialogue") (especially _[Timaeus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timaeus\_(dialogue) "Timaeus (dialogue)")_) exhibit Pythagorean ideas. A major revival of his teachings occurred in the first century BC among [Middle Platonists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_Platonism "Middle Platonism"), coinciding with the rise of [Neopythagoreanism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neopythagoreanism "Neopythagoreanism"). Pythagoras continued to be regarded as a great philosopher throughout the [Middle Ages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_Ages "Middle Ages") and Pythagoreanism had an influence on scientists such as [Nicolaus Copernicus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nicolaus_Copernicus "Nicolaus Copernicus"), [Johannes Kepler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johannes_Kepler "Johannes Kepler"), and [Isaac Newton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaac_Newton "Isaac Newton"). Pythagorean symbolism was also used throughout early modern [European esotericism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_esotericism "Western esotericism"), and his teachings as portrayed in [Ovid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ovid "Ovid")'s _[Metamorphoses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metamorphoses "Metamorphoses")_ would later influence the modern vegetarian movement.

Life
----

No authentic writings of Pythagoras have survived,[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJoost-Gaugier200611-5)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTECelenza2010796-6) and almost nothing is known for certain about his life.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGregory201521%E2%80%9323-7)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTECopleston200329-8) The earliest sources on Pythagoras's life, from [Xenophanes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xenophanes "Xenophanes"), [Heraclitus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heraclitus "Heraclitus"), [Empedocles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empedocles "Empedocles"), [Ion of Chios](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ion_of_Chios "Ion of Chios"), and [Herodotus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herodotus "Herodotus")[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTELloyd201428-9) are brief, ambiguous, and often [satirical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satire "Satire").[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn20012-10)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurkert1985299-11) The major sources on Pythagoras's life are three biographies from late antiquity written by [Diogenes Laërtius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diogenes_La%C3%ABrtius "Diogenes Laërtius"), [Porphyry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Porphyry_(philosopher) "Porphyry (philosopher)"), and [Iamblichus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iamblichus "Iamblichus"), all of which are filled primarily with myths and legends[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTECopleston200329-8)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn20015-12)[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEZhmud20129-13) and which become longer and more fantastic in their descriptions of Pythagoras's achievements the more removed they are from Pythagoras's times.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn20015-12)[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEZhmud20129-13) However, Porphyry and Iamblichus also used some material taken from earlier writings in the 4th century BC by [Aristotle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotle "Aristotle")'s students [Dicaearchus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dicaearchus "Dicaearchus"), [Aristoxenus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristoxenus "Aristoxenus"), and [Heraclides Ponticus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heraclides_Ponticus "Heraclides Ponticus"),[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurkert1972109-14) which, when it can be identified, is generally considered to be the most reliable.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurkert1972109-14)

### Early life

> There is not a single detail in the life of Pythagoras that stands uncontradicted. But it is possible, from a more or less critical selection of the data, to construct a plausible account.

[Herodotus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herodotus "Herodotus")[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHdt._4297%C2%A795-16) and [Isocrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isocrates "Isocrates") agree that Pythagoras was the son of Mnesarchus,[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJoost-Gaugier200616-17)[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn20016-18) and that he was born on the Greek island of [Samos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samos "Samos") in the eastern [Aegean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aegean_Sea "Aegean Sea").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJoost-Gaugier200611-5)[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn20016-18)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKenny20049-19) Mnesarchus is said to have been a gem-engraver or a wealthy merchant,[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPorphyry,_Vit._Pyth%C2%A71,_%C2%A710-20)[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-21)[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJoost-Gaugier200621-22) but his ancestry is disputed and unclear.[[d]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-ancestry-23)[Apollonius of Tyana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apollonius_of_Tyana "Apollonius of Tyana") writes that Pythagoras's mother was Pythaïs, a woman who was said to be a descendant of [Ancaeus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancaeus_(son_of_Poseidon) "Ancaeus (son of Poseidon)"), the mythical founder of Samos.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTETaub2017[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidodm7DgAAQBAJpgPA122_122]-24)[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-25)[Iamblichus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iamblichus "Iamblichus") tells the story that the Pythia prophesied to her while she was pregnant with him that she would give birth to a man supremely beautiful, wise, and beneficial to humankind.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200559-26) As to the date of his birth, [Aristoxenus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristoxenus "Aristoxenus") stated that Pythagoras left Samos in the reign of [Polycrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polycrates "Polycrates"), at the age of 40, which would give a date of birth around 570 BC.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPorphyry,_Vit._Pyth%C2%A79-27) Pythagoras's name led him to be associated with [Pythian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythia "Pythia")[Apollo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apollo "Apollo") (_Pūthíā_); [Aristippus of Cyrene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristippus_of_Cyrene "Aristippus of Cyrene") in the 4th century BC explained his name by saying, "He spoke [[ἀγορεύω](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E1%BC%80%CE%B3%CE%BF%CF%81%CE%B5%CF%8D%CF%89 "wikt:ἀγορεύω"), _agoreúō_] the truth no less than did the Pythian [[πυθικός](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%A0%CF%85%CE%B8%CE%AF%CE%B1 "wikt:Πυθία")_puthikós_]".[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200559-26)

During Pythagoras's formative years, Samos was a thriving cultural hub known for its feats of advanced architectural engineering, including the building of the [Tunnel of Eupalinos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tunnel_of_Eupalinos "Tunnel of Eupalinos"), and for its riotous festival culture.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200545%E2%80%9347-28) It was a major center of trade in the Aegean where traders brought goods from the [Near East](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Near_East "Near East").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJoost-Gaugier200611-5) According to Christiane L. Joost-Gaugier, these traders almost certainly brought with them Near Eastern ideas and traditions.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJoost-Gaugier200611-5) Pythagoras's early life also coincided with the flowering of early Ionian [natural philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_philosophy "Natural philosophy").[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn20016-18)[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200544%E2%80%9345-29) He was a contemporary of the philosophers [Anaximander](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anaximander "Anaximander"), [Anaximenes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anaximenes_of_Miletus "Anaximenes of Miletus"), and the historian [Hecataeus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hecataeus_of_Miletus "Hecataeus of Miletus"), all of whom lived in [Miletus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Miletus "Miletus"), across the sea from Samos.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200544%E2%80%9345-29)

### Reputed travels

Modern scholarship has shown that the culture of [Archaic Greece](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archaic_Greece "Archaic Greece") was heavily influenced by those of [Levantine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levant "Levant") and [Mesopotamian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mesopotamia "Mesopotamia") cultures, which appears to have been recognized by authors later in the Classical and Hellenistic periods, who attributed many of Pythagoras' unusual and unconventional beliefs to invented travels to far off lands, where he learned from those people himself.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg20057%E2%80%938-30) The doctrine of [metempsychosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metempsychosis "Metempsychosis"), or reincarnation of the soul after death, which Herodotus had mistakenly attributed to the Egyptians, led to an elaborate tale[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPorphyry,_Vit._Pyth%C2%A76-31) where Pythagoras learned the [Egyptian language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egyptian_language "Egyptian language") from the [Pharaoh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pharaoh "Pharaoh")[Amasis II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amasis_II "Amasis II") himself, and then traveled to study with the Egyptian priests at [Diospolis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thebes,_Egypt "Thebes, Egypt") (Thebes), where he was the only foreigner ever to be granted the privilege of taking part in their worship.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg20057%E2%80%938-30) Other ancient writers, however, claimed that Pythagoras had learned these teachings from the [Magi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magi "Magi") in [Persia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persia "Persia") or even from [Zoroaster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zoroaster "Zoroaster") himself.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDiog_VIII%C2%A71.1,_%C2%A71.3-32)[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg20057%E2%80%938-30) The [Phoenicians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phoenicia "Phoenicia") are also reputed to have taught Pythagoras [arithmetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic "Arithmetic") and the [Chaldeans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chaldea "Chaldea") to have taught him astronomy.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg20057%E2%80%938-30) By the third century BC, Pythagoras was already reported to have studied under the [Jews](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jews "Jews") as well.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg20057%E2%80%938-30) By the third century AD, Pythagoras was also reported by [Philostratus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philostratus "Philostratus") to have studied under sages or [gymnosophists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gymnosophist "Gymnosophist") in [India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/India "India"), and, according to [Iamblichus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iamblichus "Iamblichus"), also with the [Celts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celts "Celts") and [Iberians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iberians "Iberians").[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg20057%E2%80%938-30)

### Alleged Greek teachers

Ancient sources also record Pythagoras having studied under a variety of native Greek thinkers.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg20058-33) Diogenes Laërtius asserts that Pythagoras later visited [Crete](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crete "Crete"), where he went to the [Cave of Ida](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mount_Ida "Mount Ida") with [Epimenides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epimenides "Epimenides").[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDiog_VIII1.1,_1.3-34) Some identify Hermodamas of Samos as a possible tutor.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg20058-33)[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-35) Hermodamas represented the indigenous Samian [rhapsodic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhapsode "Rhapsode") tradition and his father Creophylos was said to have been the host of his rival poet [Homer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homer "Homer").[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg20058-33) Others credit [Bias of Priene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bias_of_Priene "Bias of Priene"), Thales,[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIamblichus,_Vit._Pyth%C2%A79-36) or [Anaximander](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anaximander "Anaximander") (a pupil of Thales).[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg20058-33)[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIamblichus,_Vit._Pyth%C2%A79-36)[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPorphyry,_Vit._Pyth%C2%A72-37) Other traditions claim the mythic bard [Orpheus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orpheus "Orpheus") as Pythagoras's teacher, thus representing the [Orphic Mysteries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orphism_(religion) "Orphism (religion)").[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg20058-33) The Neoplatonists wrote of a "sacred discourse" Pythagoras had written on the gods in the [Doric Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doric_Greek "Doric Greek") dialect, which they believed had been dictated to Pythagoras by the Orphic priest Aglaophamus upon his initiation to the orphic Mysteries at [Leibethra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leibethra "Leibethra").[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg20058-33) Iamblichus credited Orpheus with having been the model for Pythagoras's manner of speech, his spiritual attitude, and his manner of worship.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg20058%E2%80%939-38) Iamblichus describes Pythagoreanism as a synthesis of everything Pythagoras had learned from Orpheus, from the Egyptian priests, from the [Eleusinian Mysteries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eleusinian_Mysteries "Eleusinian Mysteries"), and from other religious and philosophical traditions.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg20058%E2%80%939-38) Contradicting all these reports, the novelist [Antonius Diogenes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antonius_Diogenes "Antonius Diogenes"), writing in the second century BC, reports that Pythagoras discovered all his doctrines himself by [interpreting dreams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dream_interpretation "Dream interpretation").[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg20058-33) Riedweg states that, although these stories are fanciful, Pythagoras's teachings were definitely influenced by Orphism to a noteworthy extent.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg20059-39)

Of the various Greek sages claimed to have taught Pythagoras, [Pherecydes of Syros](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pherecydes_of_Syros "Pherecydes of Syros") is mentioned most often.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg20059-39)[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-40) Similar miracle stories were told about both Pythagoras and Pherecydes, including one in which the hero predicts a shipwreck, one in which he predicts the conquest of [Messina](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Messina "Messina"), and one in which he drinks from a well and predicts an earthquake.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg20059-39)[Apollonius Paradoxographus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apollonius_Paradoxographus "Apollonius Paradoxographus"), a paradoxographer who may have lived in the second century BC, identified Pythagoras's [thaumaturgic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thaumaturgy "Thaumaturgy") ideas as a result of Pherecydes's influence.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg20059-39) Another story, which may be traced to the Neopythagorean philosopher [Nicomachus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nicomachus "Nicomachus"), tells that, when Pherecydes was old and dying on the island of [Delos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Delos "Delos"), Pythagoras returned to care for him and pay his respects.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg20059-39)[Duris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duris_of_Samos "Duris of Samos"), the historian and [tyrant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tyrant "Tyrant") of Samos, is reported to have patriotically boasted of an epitaph supposedly penned by Pherecydes which declared that Pythagoras's wisdom exceeded his own.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg20059-39) On the grounds of all these references connecting Pythagoras with Pherecydes, Riedweg concludes that there may well be some historical foundation to the tradition that Pherecydes was Pythagoras's teacher.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg20059-39) Pythagoras and Pherecydes also appear to have shared similar views on the soul and the teaching of metempsychosis.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg20059-39)

Map of Italy showing locations associated with Pythagoras

Porphyry repeats an account from [Antiphon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiphon_(writer) "Antiphon (writer)"), who reported that, while he was still on Samos, Pythagoras founded a school known as the "semicircle".[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200510-41)[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTECornelliMcKirahan201364-42) Here, Samians debated matters of public concern.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200510-41)[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTECornelliMcKirahan201364-42) Supposedly, the school became so renowned that the brightest minds in all of Greece came to Samos to hear Pythagoras teach.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200510-41) Pythagoras himself dwelled in a secret cave, where he studied in private and occasionally held discourses with a few of his close friends.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200510-41)[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTECornelliMcKirahan201364-42) Christoph Riedweg, a German scholar of early Pythagoreanism, states that it is entirely possible Pythagoras may have taught on Samos,[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200510-41) but cautions that Antiphon's account, which makes reference to a specific building that was still in use during his own time, appears to be motivated by Samian patriotic interest.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200510-41)

Around 530 BC, when Pythagoras was about forty years old, he left Samos.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJoost-Gaugier200611-5)[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn20016-18)[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200511-43)[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGregory201522-44) His later admirers claimed that he left because he disagreed with the [tyranny](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tyrant "Tyrant") of [Polycrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polycrates "Polycrates") in Samos;[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200511-43) Riedweg notes that this explanation closely aligns with Nicomachus's emphasis on Pythagoras's purported love of freedom, but that Pythagoras's enemies portrayed him as having a proclivity towards tyranny.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200511-43) Other accounts claim that Pythagoras left Samos because he was so overburdened with public duties in Samos, because of the high estimation in which he was held by his fellow-citizens.[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-45) He arrived in the Greek colony of Croton (today's [Crotone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crotone "Crotone"), in [Calabria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calabria "Calabria")) in what was then [Magna Graecia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magna_Graecia "Magna Graecia").[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn20016-18)[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGregory201522-44)[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDe_Vogel196621ff-46)[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-47)[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200511%E2%80%9312-48) All sources agree that Pythagoras was charismatic and quickly acquired great political influence in his new environment.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn20016-18)[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDe_Vogel1966148%E2%80%93150-49)[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200512%E2%80%9313-50) He served as an advisor to the elites in Croton and gave them frequent advice.[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200512%E2%80%9318-51) Later biographers tell fantastical stories of the effects of his eloquent speeches in leading the people of Croton to abandon their luxurious and corrupt way of life and devote themselves to the purer system which he came to introduce.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-52)[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200513%E2%80%9318-53)

### Family and friends

[Suda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suda "Suda") writes that Pythagoras had 4 children (Telauges, Mnesarchus, Myia and Arignote).[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-54) The wrestler [Milo of Croton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milo_of_Croton "Milo of Croton") was said to have been a close associate of Pythagoras[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg20055%E2%80%936,_59,_73-55) and was credited with having saved the philosopher's life when a roof was about to collapse.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg20055%E2%80%936,_59,_73-55) This association may have been the result of confusion with a different man named Pythagoras, who was an athletics trainer.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200510-41)

### Death

Pythagoras's emphasis on dedication and asceticism are credited with aiding in Croton's decisive victory over the neighboring colony of [Sybaris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sybaris "Sybaris") in 510 BC.[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn20016%E2%80%937-56) After the victory, some prominent citizens of Croton proposed a [democratic constitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_democracy "Greek democracy"), which the Pythagoreans rejected.[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn20016%E2%80%937-56) The supporters of democracy, headed by [Cylon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylon_of_Croton "Cylon of Croton") and Ninon, the former of whom is said to have been irritated by his exclusion from Pythagoras's brotherhood, roused the populace against them.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200519-57) Followers of Cylon and Ninon attacked the Pythagoreans during one of their meetings, either in the house of Milo or in some other meeting-place.[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn20017-58)[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200519%E2%80%9320-59) Accounts of the attack are often contradictory and many probably confused it with the later anti-Pythagorean rebellions, such as the one in Metapontum in 454 BC.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200519-57)[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPlutarch,%C2%A0de_Gen._Socr419%C2%A7583a-60) The building was apparently set on fire,[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn20017-58) and many of the assembled members perished;[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn20017-58) only the younger and more active members managed to escape.[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-61)

Sources disagree regarding whether Pythagoras was present when the attack occurred and, if he was, whether or not he managed to escape.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurkert1972106-15)[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200519%E2%80%9320-59) In some accounts, Pythagoras was not at the meeting when the Pythagoreans were attacked because he was on Delos tending to the dying Pherecydes.[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200519%E2%80%9320-59) According to another account from Dicaearchus, Pythagoras was at the meeting and managed to escape,[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200520-62) leading a small group of followers to the nearby city of [Locris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Locris "Locris"), where they pleaded for sanctuary, but were denied.[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200520-62) They reached the city of [Metapontum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metapontum "Metapontum"), where they took shelter in the temple of the [Muses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muses "Muses") and died there of starvation after forty days without food.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurkert1972106-15)[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn20017-58)[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200520-62)[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGrant1989278-63) Another tale recorded by Porphyry claims that, as Pythagoras's enemies were burning the house, his devoted students laid down on the ground to make a path for him to escape by walking over their bodies across the flames like a bridge.[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200520-62) Pythagoras managed to escape, but was so despondent at the deaths of his beloved students that he committed suicide.[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200520-62) A different legend reported by both Diogenes Laërtius and Iamblichus states that Pythagoras almost managed to escape, but that he came to a fava bean field and refused to run through it, since doing so would violate his teachings, so he stopped instead and was killed.[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200520-62) This story seems to have originated from the writer Neanthes, who told it about later Pythagoreans, not about Pythagoras himself.[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200520-62)

Teachings
---------

### Metempsychosis

Although the exact details of Pythagoras's teachings are uncertain,[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurkert1972106%E2%80%93109-64)[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn20015%E2%80%936-65) it is possible to reconstruct a general outline of his main ideas.[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurkert1972106%E2%80%93109-64)[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn20019%E2%80%9311-66) Aristotle writes at length about the teachings of the Pythagoreans,[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTECopleston200331-67)[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurkert197229%E2%80%9330-68) but without mentioning Pythagoras directly.[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTECopleston200331-67)[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurkert197229%E2%80%9330-68) One of Pythagoras's main doctrines appears to have been _[metempsychosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metempsychosis "Metempsychosis")_,[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn200111-69)[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEZhmud2012232-70)[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurkert1985300%E2%80%93301-71)[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGregory201524%E2%80%9325-72)[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTECopleston200330%E2%80%9331-73) the belief that all [souls](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soul "Soul") are immortal and that, after death, a soul is transferred into a new body.[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn200111-69)[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGregory201524%E2%80%9325-72) This teaching is referenced by Xenophanes, Ion of Chios, and Herodotus.[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn200111-69)[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-74) The earliest source on Pythagoras's metempsychosis is a satirical poem probably written after his death by the Greek philosopher [Xenophanes of Colophon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xenophanes_of_Colophon "Xenophanes of Colophon") (c. 570– c. 478 BC), who had been one of his contemporaries,[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJoost-Gaugier200612-75)[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200562-76) in which Xenophanes describes Pythagoras interceding on behalf of a [dog](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dog "Dog") that is being beaten, professing to recognize in its cries the voice of a departed friend.[[e]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-Xenophanes-77)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurkert1985299-11)[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJoost-Gaugier200612-75)[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTECopleston200331-67) Nothing whatsoever, however, is known about the nature or mechanism by which Pythagoras believed metempsychosis to occur.[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGregory201525-78)

[Empedocles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empedocles "Empedocles") alludes in one of his poems that Pythagoras may have claimed to possess the ability to recall his former incarnations.[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn200112-79) Diogenes Laërtius reports an account from [Heraclides Ponticus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heraclides_Ponticus "Heraclides Ponticus") that Pythagoras told people that he had lived four previous lives that he could remember in detail.[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDiog_VIII%C2%A71.3%E2%80%93%C2%A71.5-80)[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTECornelliMcKirahan2013164%E2%80%93167-81)[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-82) The first of these lives was as [Aethalides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aethalides "Aethalides") the son of [Hermes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermes "Hermes"), who granted him the ability to remember all his past incarnations.[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTECornelliMcKirahan2013164%E2%80%93165-83) Next, he was incarnated as [Euphorbus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euphorbus "Euphorbus"), a minor hero from the [Trojan War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trojan_War "Trojan War") briefly mentioned in the _[Iliad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iliad "Iliad")_.[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTECornelliMcKirahan2013165%E2%80%93166-84) He then became the philosopher [Hermotimus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermotimus_of_Clazomenae "Hermotimus of Clazomenae"),[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTECornelliMcKirahan2013167-85) who recognized the shield of Euphorbus in the temple of Apollo.[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTECornelliMcKirahan2013167-85) His final incarnation was as Pyrrhus, a fisherman from [Delos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Delos "Delos").[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTECornelliMcKirahan2013167-85) One of his past lives, as reported by [Dicaearchus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dicaearchus "Dicaearchus"), was as a beautiful courtesan.[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEZhmud2012232-70)[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-86)

### Numerology

[![Image 8: Diagram showing the tetractys, an equilateral triangle made up of ten dots, with one dot in the top row, two in the second, three in the third, and four in the bottom.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Tetractys.svg/250px-Tetractys.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Tetractys.svg)

Pythagoras is credited with having devised the [tetractys](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetractys "Tetractys"),[[82]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGregory201528%E2%80%9329-87) an important [sacred symbol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Religious_symbol "Religious symbol") in later Pythagoreanism.[[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200529-88)[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn20011%E2%80%932-89)

Another belief attributed to Pythagoras was that of the "[harmony of the spheres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Musica_universalis "Musica universalis")",[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200529%E2%80%9330-90)[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGregory201538%E2%80%9339-91) which maintained that the planets and stars move according to mathematical equations, which correspond to musical notes and thus produce an inaudible symphony.[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200529%E2%80%9330-90)[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGregory201538%E2%80%9339-91) According to Porphyry, Pythagoras taught that the seven [Muses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muse "Muse") were actually the [seven planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_planet "Classical planet") singing together.[[87]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200530-92)

> The so-called Pythagoreans applied themselves to mathematics, and were the first to develop this science; and through studying it they came to believe that its principles are the principles of everything.

— [Aristot. Met. 1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#CITEREFAristot._Met._1), 985b

Modern scholars typically ascribe these discoveries to the later Pythagorean philosopher [Philolaus of Croton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philolaus_of_Croton "Philolaus of Croton") (c. 470– c. 385 BC), whose extant fragments are the earliest texts to describe the numerological and musical theories that were later ascribed to Pythagoras.[[88]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJoost-Gaugier200687%E2%80%9388-93) In his landmark study _Lore and Science in Ancient Pythagoreanism_, Walter Burkert argues that Pythagoras was a charismatic political and religious teacher,[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn20012%E2%80%933-94) but that the number philosophy attributed to him was really an innovation by Philolaus.[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn20013-95) According to Burkert, Pythagoras never dealt with numbers at all, let alone made any noteworthy contribution to mathematics.[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn20012%E2%80%933-94) Burkert argues that the only mathematics the Pythagoreans ever actually engaged in was simple, [proofless](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_proof "Mathematical proof")[arithmetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic "Arithmetic"),[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurkert1972428%E2%80%93433-96) but that these arithmetic discoveries did contribute significantly to the beginnings of mathematics.[[92]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurkert1972465-97) For the later Pythagoreans, Pythagoras was credited with devising the [tetractys](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetractys "Tetractys"), the triangular figure of four rows which add up to the "perfect" number, ten.[[82]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGregory201528%E2%80%9329-87)[[93]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurkert1972467%E2%80%93468-98) The Pythagoreans regarded the tetractys as a symbol of utmost mystical importance.[[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200529-88) Iamblichus, in his _Life of Pythagoras_, states that the tetractys was "so admirable, and so divinised by those who understood [it]," that Pythagoras's students would swear oaths by it.[[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200529-88)[[94]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIamblichus,_Vit._Pyth%C2%A729-99)

This shouldn't be confused with a simplified version known today as "[Pythagorean numerology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Numerology#Pythagorean_method "Numerology")", involving a variant of an isopsephic technique known – among other names – as _pythmenes_'roots'[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-pythmenes1-100) or 'base numbers',[[96]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-101) by means of which the base values of letters in a word were mathematically reduced by addition or division, in order to obtain a single value from one to nine for the whole name or word.[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-pythmenes1-100)

Pythagoreanism
--------------

### Communal lifestyle

[![Image 9: Painting showing a group of people dressed in white classical garb standing at the edge of a cliff overlooking the sea watching as the sun rises. The central figure, probably Pythagoras himself, is turned away from our view towards the sunrise. He has long braids and his long beard is partially visible from the side. Both of his arms are raised into the air. The three men closest to him, two on his left and one on his right, are kneeling and making frantic gestures, possibly weeping. Behind them, an older man plays a harp and two women play lyres. A young man without a beard and an middle-aged man with a beard play lyres as well, while another young man plays the aulos. A man in the foreground at the back of the group kneels prayerfully towards the sunrise. In the background, at the far left side of the painting, a woman, a girl, a boy, and a young, naked child watch the Pythagoreans. The woman and the girl are carrying pots, indicating they have been fetching water.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Bronnikov_gimnpifagoreizev.jpg/500px-Bronnikov_gimnpifagoreizev.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Bronnikov_gimnpifagoreizev.jpg)

_Pythagoreans Celebrate the Sunrise_ (1869) by [Fyodor Bronnikov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fyodor_Bronnikov "Fyodor Bronnikov")

Both [Plato](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plato "Plato") and [Isocrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isocrates "Isocrates") state that, above all else, Pythagoras was known as the founder of a new way of life.[[97]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-102)[[98]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTECornelliMcKirahan2013168-103)[[99]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGrant1989277-104) The organization Pythagoras founded at Croton was called a "school",[[100]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPorphyry,_Vit._Pyth%C2%A719-105)[[101]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-106) but, in many ways, resembled a [monastery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monastery "Monastery").[[102]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200531-107) The adherents were bound by a [vow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vow "Vow") to Pythagoras and each other, for the purpose of pursuing the [religious](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Religious "Religious") and [ascetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ascetic "Ascetic") observances, and of studying his religious and [philosophical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophical "Philosophical") theories.[[103]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-108) The members of the sect [shared all their possessions in common](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Community_ownership "Community ownership")[[104]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTECornelliMcKirahan201365-109) and were devoted to each other to the exclusion of outsiders.[[105]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-110)[[106]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTECornelliMcKirahan201368%E2%80%9369-111) Ancient sources record that the Pythagoreans ate meals in common after the manner of the [Spartans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sparta "Sparta").[[107]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-112)[[108]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKenny200410-113) One Pythagorean [maxim](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxim_(literature) "Maxim (literature)") was "_koinà tà phílōn_" ("All things in common among friends").[[104]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTECornelliMcKirahan201365-109) Both Iamblichus and Porphyry provide detailed accounts of the organization of the school, although the primary interest of both writers is not historical accuracy, but rather to present Pythagoras as a divine figure, sent by the [gods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_gods "Greek gods") to benefit mankind.[[109]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-114) Iamblichus, in particular, presents the "Pythagorean Way of Life" as a pagan alternative to the Christian monastic communities of his own time.[[102]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200531-107) For Pythagoreans, the highest reward humans could attain was for their soul to join in the life of the gods and thus escape the cycle of reincarnation.[[110]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn200152-115) Two groups existed within early Pythagoreanism: the _mathematikoi_ ("learners") and the _akousmatikoi_ ("listeners").[[111]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEZhmud20122,_16-116)[[112]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGregory201531-117) The _akousmatikoi_ are traditionally identified by scholars as "old believers" in mysticism, numerology, and religious teachings;[[112]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGregory201531-117) whereas the _mathematikoi_ are traditionally identified as a more intellectual, modernist faction who were more rationalist and scientific.[[112]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGregory201531-117) Gregory cautions that there was probably not a sharp distinction between them and that many Pythagoreans probably believed the two approaches were compatible.[[112]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGregory201531-117) The study of mathematics and music may have been connected to the worship of Apollo.[[113]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-118) The Pythagoreans believed that music was a purification for the soul, just as medicine was a purification for the body.[[87]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200530-92) One anecdote of Pythagoras reports that when he encountered some drunken youths trying to break into the home of a virtuous woman, he sang a solemn tune with long [spondees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spondee "Spondee") and the boys' "raging willfulness" was quelled.[[87]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200530-92) The Pythagoreans also placed particular emphasis on the importance of [physical exercise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_exercise "Physical exercise");[[102]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200531-107) therapeutic [dancing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dance "Dance"), daily morning walks along [scenic routes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scenic_route "Scenic route"), and [athletics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sport_of_athletics "Sport of athletics") were major components of the Pythagorean lifestyle.[[102]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200531-107) Moments of contemplation at the beginning and end of each day were also advised.[[114]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200533%E2%80%9334-119)

### Prohibitions and regulations

[![Image 10: Old manuscript illustration showing a cloaked and hooded man labelled "Pythagoras" raising his arms and turning his face away from a fava bean plant, labelled "Fabe."](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Do_Not_Eat_Beans.jpg/250px-Do_Not_Eat_Beans.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Do_Not_Eat_Beans.jpg)

French manuscript from 1512/1514, showing Pythagoras turning his face away from [fava beans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vicia_faba "Vicia faba") in revulsion

Pythagorean teachings were known as "symbols" (_symbola_)[[115]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn20018-120) and members took a vow of silence that they would not reveal these symbols to non-members.[[115]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn20018-120)[[98]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTECornelliMcKirahan2013168-103)[[116]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-121) Those who did not obey the laws of the community were expelled[[117]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTECornelliMcKirahan201369-122) and the remaining members would erect [tombstones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tombstone "Tombstone") for them as though they had died.[[117]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTECornelliMcKirahan201369-122) A number of "oral sayings" (_akoúsmata_) attributed to Pythagoras have survived,[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTECopleston200331-67)[[118]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200564%E2%80%9367-123) dealing with how members of the Pythagorean community should perform sacrifices, how they should honor the gods, how they should "move from here", and how they should be buried.[[119]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200564-124) Many of these sayings emphasize the importance of ritual purity and avoiding defilement.[[120]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200565-125)[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTECopleston200330%E2%80%9331-73) Other extant oral sayings forbid Pythagoreans from breaking bread, poking fires with swords, or picking up crumbs[[108]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKenny200410-113) and teach that a person should always put the right sandal on before the left.[[108]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKenny200410-113) The exact meanings of these sayings, however, are frequently obscure.[[121]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200565%E2%80%9367-126) Iamblichus preserves Aristotle's descriptions of the original, ritualistic intentions behind a few of these sayings,[[122]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200565%E2%80%9366-127) but these apparently later fell out of fashion, because Porphyry provides markedly different ethical-philosophical interpretations of them:[[123]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200566%E2%80%9367-128)

| Pythagorean saying | Original ritual purpose according to Aristotle/Iamblichus | Porphyry's philosophical interpretation |
| --- | --- | --- |
| "Do not take roads traveled by the public."[[124]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200566-129)[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTECopleston200331-67) | "Fear of being defiled by the impure"[[124]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200566-129) | "with this he forbade following the opinions of the masses, yet to follow the ones of the few and the educated".[[124]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200566-129) |
| "and [do] not wear images of the gods on rings"[[124]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200566-129) | "Fear of defiling them by wearing them."[[124]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200566-129) | "One should not have the teaching and knowledge of the gods quickly at hand and visible [for everyone], nor communicate them to the masses."[[124]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200566-129) |
| "and pour libations for the gods from a drinking cup's handle [the 'ear']"[[124]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200566-129) | "Efforts to keep the divine and the human strictly separate"[[124]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200566-129) | "thereby he enigmatically hints that the gods should be honored and praised with music; for it goes through the ears".[[124]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200566-129) |

New initiates were allegedly not permitted to meet Pythagoras until after they had completed a five-year initiation period,[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTECornelliMcKirahan201364-42) during which they were required to remain silent.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTECornelliMcKirahan201364-42) Sources indicate that Pythagoras himself was unusually progressive in his attitudes towards women[[125]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPomeroy2013xvi-130) and female members of Pythagoras's school appear to have played an active role in its operations.[[115]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn20018-120)[[125]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPomeroy2013xvi-130) Iamblichus provides a list of 235 famous Pythagoreans,[[126]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPomeroy20131-131) seventeen of whom are women.[[126]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPomeroy20131-131) In later times, many prominent female philosophers contributed to the development of [Neopythagoreanism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neopythagoreanism "Neopythagoreanism").[[127]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPomeroy2013xvi%E2%80%93xvii-132)

Pythagoreanism also entailed a number of dietary prohibitions.[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTECopleston200330%E2%80%9331-73)[[108]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKenny200410-113)[[128]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-133) It is more or less agreed that Pythagoras issued a prohibition against the consumption of [fava beans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vicia_faba "Vicia faba")[[129]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEZhmud2012137,_200-134)[[108]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKenny200410-113) and the meat of non-sacrificial animals such as fish and poultry.[[130]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEZhmud2012200-135)[[108]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKenny200410-113) Both of these assumptions, however, have been contradicted.[[131]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTECopleston200330-136)[[132]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-137) Pythagorean dietary restrictions may have been motivated by belief in the doctrine of [metempsychosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metempsychosis "Metempsychosis");[[98]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTECornelliMcKirahan2013168-103)[[133]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPlutarch,_de_Esu_Carn540%E2%80%93545,_557%E2%80%93571%C2%A7993,_%C2%A7996,_%C2%A7997-138)[[134]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn20019-139)[[135]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKenny200410%E2%80%9311-140) alternatively, they may be based on the genetic prevalence of [favism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Favism "Favism"), a type of enzyme deficiency anemia, in the Mediterranean.[[136]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-141) Some ancient writers present Pythagoras as enforcing a strictly [vegetarian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vegetarianism "Vegetarianism") diet.[[f]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-vegetarianism-142)[[98]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTECornelliMcKirahan2013168-103)[[134]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn20019-139)[Eudoxus of Cnidus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eudoxus_of_Cnidus "Eudoxus of Cnidus"), a student of Archytas, writes, "Pythagoras was distinguished by such purity and so avoided killing and killers that he not only abstained from animal foods, but even kept his distance from cooks and hunters."[[137]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-143)[[138]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEZhmud2012235-144) Other authorities contradict this statement.[[139]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-145) According to [Aristoxenus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristoxenus "Aristoxenus"),[[140]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-146) Pythagoras allowed the use of all kinds of animal food except the flesh of [oxen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxen "Oxen") used for [ploughing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plough "Plough"), and [rams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sheep "Sheep").[[138]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEZhmud2012235-144)[[141]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-147) According to Heraclides Ponticus, Pythagoras ate the meat from sacrifices[[138]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEZhmud2012235-144) and established a diet for athletes dependent on meat.[[138]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEZhmud2012235-144)

Within his own lifetime, Pythagoras was already the subject of elaborate [hagiographic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hagiography "Hagiography") legends.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn20015-12)[[142]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg20051-148) Aristotle described Pythagoras as a [wonder-worker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wonder-worker "Wonder-worker") and somewhat of a supernatural figure.[[143]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg20052-149)[[144]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGregory201530%E2%80%9331-150) In a fragment, Aristotle writes that Pythagoras had a golden thigh,[[143]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg20052-149)[[145]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGregory201530-151)[[146]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKenny200411-152) which he publicly exhibited at the [Olympic Games](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Olympic_Games "Ancient Olympic Games")[[143]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg20052-149) and showed to [Abaris the Hyperborean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abaris_the_Hyperborean "Abaris the Hyperborean") as proof of his identity as the "Hyperborean Apollo".[[143]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg20052-149)[[147]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-153) Supposedly, the priest of Apollo gave Pythagoras a magic arrow, which he used to fly over long distances and perform ritual purifications.[[148]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcKeown2013155-154) He was supposedly once seen at both Metapontum and Croton [at the same time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bilocation "Bilocation").[[149]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-155)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn20015-12)[[145]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGregory201530-151)[[146]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKenny200411-152) When Pythagoras crossed the river Kosas (the modern-day [Basento](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basento "Basento")), "several witnesses" reported that they heard it greet him by name.[[150]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurkert1972144-156)[[145]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGregory201530-151) In Roman times, a legend claimed that Pythagoras was the son of Apollo.[[146]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKenny200411-152)

Pythagoras was said to have dressed all in white.[[143]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg20052-149)[[151]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJoost-Gaugier200647-157) He is also said to have borne a golden [wreath](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wreath "Wreath") atop his head[[143]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg20052-149) and to have worn [trousers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trousers "Trousers") after the fashion of the [Thracians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thrace "Thrace").[[143]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg20052-149) Pythagoras was said to have had extraordinary success in dealing with animals.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn20015-12)[[152]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTECornelliMcKirahan2013160-158) A fragment from Aristotle records that, when a deadly snake bit Pythagoras, he bit it back and killed it.[[148]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcKeown2013155-154)[[145]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGregory201530-151) Both Porphyry and Iamblichus report that Pythagoras once persuaded a bull not to eat fava beans[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn20015-12)[[152]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTECornelliMcKirahan2013160-158) and that he once convinced a notoriously destructive bear to swear that it would never harm a living thing again, and that the bear kept its word.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn20015-12)[[152]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTECornelliMcKirahan2013160-158) Riedweg suggests that Pythagoras may have personally encouraged these legends,[[142]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg20051-148) but Gregory states that there is no direct evidence of this.[[112]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGregory201531-117)

Attributed discoveries
----------------------

### In mathematics

[![Image 11: Diagram illustrating the Pythagorean theorem](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Pythagorean.svg/330px-Pythagorean.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Pythagorean.svg)

**[The Pythagorean theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagorean_theorem "Pythagorean theorem")**: The sum of the areas of the two squares on the legs (_a_ and _b_) equals the area of the square on the hypotenuse (_c_).

Although Pythagoras is most famous today for his alleged mathematical discoveries,[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn20011%E2%80%932-89)[[153]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGregory201521%E2%80%9322-159) classical historians dispute whether he himself ever actually made any significant contributions to the field.[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurkert1972428%E2%80%93433-96)[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn20012%E2%80%933-94) Many mathematical and scientific discoveries were attributed to Pythagoras, including [his famous theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagorean_theorem "Pythagorean theorem"),[[154]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-160) as well as discoveries in the fields of [music](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Music_of_ancient_Greece "Music of ancient Greece"),[[155]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-161)[astronomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_astronomy "Greek astronomy"),[[156]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-162) and [medicine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek_medicine "Ancient Greek medicine").[[157]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-163) Since at least the first century BC, Pythagoras has commonly been given credit for discovering the Pythagorean theorem,[[158]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn200132%E2%80%9333-164)[[159]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200526%E2%80%9327-165) a theorem in geometry that states that "in a right-angled triangle the square of the hypotenuse is equal [to the sum of] the squares of the two other sides"[[160]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200527-166)—that is, ![Image 12: {\displaystyle a^{2}+b^{2}=c^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7ef0a5a4b8ab98870ae5d6d7c7b4dfe3fb6612e2). According to a popular legend, after he discovered this theorem, Pythagoras sacrificed an ox, or possibly even a whole _[hecatomb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hecatomb "Hecatomb")_, to the gods.[[160]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200527-166)[[161]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurkert1972428-167) Cicero rejected this story as spurious[[160]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200527-166) because of the much more widely held belief that Pythagoras forbade blood sacrifices.[[160]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200527-166) Porphyry attempted to explain the story by asserting that the ox was actually made of [dough](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dough "Dough").[[160]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200527-166)

The Pythagorean theorem was known and used by the [Babylonians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Babylonian_mathematics "Babylonian mathematics") and [Indians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_mathematics "Indian mathematics") centuries before Pythagoras,[[162]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurkert1972429,_462-168)[[160]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200527-166)[[163]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn200132-169) and Burkert rejects the suggestion that Pythagoras had anything to do with it,[[164]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurkert1972429-170) noting that Pythagoras was never credited with having proved any theorem in antiquity.[[164]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurkert1972429-170) Furthermore, the manner in which the Babylonians employed Pythagorean numbers implies that they knew that the principle was generally applicable, and knew some kind of proof, which has not yet been found in the (still largely unpublished) [cuneiform](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cuneiform "Cuneiform") sources.[[g]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-babylon-171)

### In music

[![Image 13: Woodcut showing four scenes. In the upper right scene, blacksmiths are pounding with hammers. In the upper left scene, a man labelled "Pitagora" is shown playing different-sized bells and glasses with different amounts of liquid in them. Both the bells and glasses are labelled. In the bottom left scene, "Pitagora" is striking chords of different length laid out across a table, once again, all of which have numbers labels. In the bottom right scene, "Pitagora" and another man labeled "Phylolavs" are shown playing auloi.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Gaffurio_Pythagoras.png/250px-Gaffurio_Pythagoras.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Gaffurio_Pythagoras.png)

Late medieval woodcut from [Franchino Gafurio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Franchinus_Gaffurius "Franchinus Gaffurius")'s _Theoria musice_ (1492), showing Pythagoras with bells and other instruments in Pythagorean tuning[[165]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGregory201528-172)

According to legend, Pythagoras discovered that musical notes could be translated into mathematical equations when he passed blacksmiths at work one day and heard the sound of their [hammers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagorean_hammers "Pythagorean hammers") clanging against the anvils.[[166]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200527%E2%80%9328-173)[[167]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGregory201527-174) Thinking that the sounds of the hammers were beautiful and harmonious, except for one,[[168]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200528-175) he rushed into the [blacksmith](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blacksmith "Blacksmith") shop and began testing the hammers.[[168]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200528-175) He then realized that the tune played when the hammer struck was directly proportional to the size of the hammer and therefore concluded that music was mathematical.[[167]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGregory201527-174)[[168]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg200528-175)

### In astronomy

In ancient times, Pythagoras and his contemporary [Parmenides of Elea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parmenides "Parmenides") were both credited with having been the first to teach that the [Earth was spherical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_Earth "Spherical Earth"),[[169]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurkert1972306-176) the first to divide the globe into [five climatic zones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geographical_zone "Geographical zone"),[[169]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurkert1972306-176) and the first to identify the [morning star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphorus_(morning_star) "Phosphorus (morning star)") and the [evening star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hesperus "Hesperus") as the same celestial object (now known as [Venus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Venus "Venus")).[[170]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurkert1972307%E2%80%93308-177) Of the two philosophers, Parmenides has a much stronger claim to having been the first[[171]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurkert1972306%E2%80%93308-178) and the attribution of these discoveries to Pythagoras seems to have possibly originated from a [pseudepigraphal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudepigrapha "Pseudepigrapha") poem.[[170]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurkert1972307%E2%80%93308-177)[Empedocles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empedocles "Empedocles"), who lived in Magna Graecia shortly after Pythagoras and Parmenides, knew that the earth was spherical.[[172]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn200153-179) By the end of the fifth century BC, this fact was universally accepted among Greek intellectuals.[[173]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDicks197068-180)

Later influence in antiquity
----------------------------

### On Greek philosophy

Sizeable Pythagorean communities existed in Magna Graecia, [Phlius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phlius "Phlius"), and [Thebes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thebes,_Greece "Thebes, Greece") during the early fourth century BC.[[174]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn200148%E2%80%9349-181) Around the same time, the Pythagorean philosopher [Archytas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archytas "Archytas") was highly influential on the politics of the city of [Tarentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taranto "Taranto") in Magna Graecia.[[175]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn200139-182) According to later tradition, Archytas was elected as _[strategos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strategos "Strategos")_ ("general") seven times, even though others were prohibited from serving more than a year.[[175]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn200139-182) Archytas was also a renowned mathematician and musician.[[176]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn200139%E2%80%9343-183) He was a close friend of Plato[[177]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn200139%E2%80%9340-184) and he is quoted in Plato's _Republic_.[[178]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn200140,_44%E2%80%9345-185)[[179]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPlato,_RepublicVII,_530d-186) Aristotle states that the philosophy of Plato was heavily dependent on the teachings of the Pythagoreans.[[180]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAristot._Met._1987a-187)[[181]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn20011-188)[[182]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-189) Cicero repeats this statement, remarking that _Platonem ferunt didicisse Pythagorea omnia_ ("They say Plato learned all things Pythagorean").[[183]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-190) According to [Charles H. Kahn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_H._Kahn "Charles H. Kahn"), Plato's middle dialogues, including _[Meno](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meno "Meno")_, _[Phaedo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phaedo "Phaedo")_, and _[The Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic\_(Plato) "Republic (Plato)")_, have a strong "Pythagorean coloring",[[184]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn200155-191) and his last few dialogues (particularly _[Philebus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philebus "Philebus")_ and _[Timaeus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timaeus\_(dialogue) "Timaeus (dialogue)")_)[[185]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn200155%E2%80%9362-192) are extremely Pythagorean in character.[[185]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn200155%E2%80%9362-192)

The poet [Heraclitus of Ephesus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heraclitus_of_Ephesus "Heraclitus of Ephesus") (fl.c. 500 BC), who was born a few miles across the sea from Samos and may have lived within Pythagoras's lifetime,[[186]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJoost-Gaugier200613-193) mocked Pythagoras as a clever charlatan,[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn20012-10)[[186]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJoost-Gaugier200613-193) remarking that "Pythagoras, son of Mnesarchus, practiced inquiry more than any other man, and selecting from these writings he manufactured a wisdom for himself—much learning, artful knavery."[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn20012-10)[[186]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJoost-Gaugier200613-193)[Alcmaeon of Croton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alcmaeon_of_Croton "Alcmaeon of Croton") ([fl.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Floruit "Floruit")c. 450 BC), a doctor who lived in Croton at around the same time Pythagoras lived there,[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJoost-Gaugier200612-75) incorporates many Pythagorean teachings into his writings[[187]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJoost-Gaugier200612%E2%80%9313-194) and alludes to having possibly known Pythagoras personally.[[187]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJoost-Gaugier200612%E2%80%9313-194) The Greek poets [Ion of Chios](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ion_of_Chios "Ion of Chios") (c. 480– c. 421 BC) and [Empedocles of Acragas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empedocles_of_Acragas "Empedocles of Acragas") (c. 493– c. 432 BC) both express admiration for Pythagoras in their poems.[[188]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJoost-Gaugier200614%E2%80%9315-195)

According to [R. M. Hare](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/R._M._Hare "R. M. Hare"), Plato's _[Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic\_(Plato) "Republic (Plato)")_ may be partially based on the "tightly organised community of like-minded thinkers" established by Pythagoras at Croton.[[189]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHare1999117%E2%80%93119-196) Additionally, Plato may have borrowed from Pythagoras the idea that mathematics and abstract thought are a secure basis for philosophy, science, and morality.[[189]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHare1999117%E2%80%93119-196) Plato and Pythagoras shared a "mystical approach to the [soul](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soul_(spirit) "Soul (spirit)") and its place in the material world"[[189]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHare1999117%E2%80%93119-196) and both were probably influenced by [Orphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orphicism "Orphicism").[[189]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHare1999117%E2%80%93119-196) The historian of philosophy [Frederick Copleston](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frederick_Copleston "Frederick Copleston") states that Plato probably borrowed [his tripartite theory of the soul](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plato%27s_tripartite_theory_of_soul "Plato's tripartite theory of soul") from the Pythagoreans.[[190]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTECopleston200337-197)

A revival of Pythagorean teachings occurred in the first century BC[[191]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg2005123%E2%80%93124-198) when [Middle Platonist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_Platonism "Middle Platonism") philosophers such as [Eudorus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eudorus_of_Alexandria "Eudorus of Alexandria") and [Philo of Alexandria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philo_of_Alexandria "Philo of Alexandria") hailed the rise of a "new" Pythagoreanism in [Alexandria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexandria "Alexandria").[[192]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg2005124-199) At around the same time, [Neopythagoreanism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neopythagoreanism "Neopythagoreanism") became prominent.[[193]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg2005125%E2%80%93126-200) The first-century AD philosopher [Apollonius of Tyana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apollonius_of_Tyana "Apollonius of Tyana") sought to emulate Pythagoras and live by Pythagorean teachings.[[194]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg2005125-201) The later first-century Neopythagorean philosopher [Moderatus of Gades](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moderatus_of_Gades "Moderatus of Gades") expanded on Pythagorean number philosophy[[194]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg2005125-201) and probably understood the soul as a "kind of mathematical harmony".[[194]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg2005125-201) The Neopythagorean mathematician and musicologist [Nicomachus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nicomachus "Nicomachus") likewise expanded on Pythagorean numerology and music theory.[[193]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg2005125%E2%80%93126-200)[Numenius of Apamea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Numenius_of_Apamea "Numenius of Apamea") interpreted Plato's teachings in light of Pythagorean doctrines.[[195]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg2005126%E2%80%93127-202)

### On art and architecture

[![Image 14: Painting showing a massive room with a high, domed ceiling. A hole is open at the top of the dome. Columns and statues line the walls.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Giovanni_Paolo_Panini_-_Interior_of_the_Pantheon%2C_Rome_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/250px-Giovanni_Paolo_Panini_-_Interior_of_the_Pantheon%2C_Rome_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Giovanni_Paolo_Panini_-_Interior_of_the_Pantheon,_Rome_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg)

[Hadrian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hadrian "Hadrian")'s [Pantheon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pantheon,_Rome "Pantheon, Rome") in [Rome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rome "Rome"), depicted in this eighteenth-century painting by [Giovanni Paolo Panini](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giovanni_Paolo_Panini "Giovanni Paolo Panini"), was built according to Pythagorean teachings.[[196]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJoost-Gaugier2006166%E2%80%93181-203)

The oldest known building designed according to Pythagorean teachings is the [Porta Maggiore Basilica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Porta_Maggiore_Basilica "Porta Maggiore Basilica"),[[197]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJoost-Gaugier2006154-204) a subterranean basilica which was built during the reign of the Roman emperor [Nero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nero "Nero") as a secret place of worship for Pythagoreans.[[198]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJoost-Gaugier2006154%E2%80%93156-205) The basilica was built underground because of the Pythagorean emphasis on secrecy[[199]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJoost-Gaugier2006157%E2%80%93158-206) and also because of the legend that Pythagoras had sequestered himself in a cave on Samos.[[200]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJoost-Gaugier2006158-207) The basilica's apse is in the east and its atrium in the west out of respect for the rising sun.[[201]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJoost-Gaugier2006158%E2%80%93159-208) It has a narrow entrance leading to a small pool where the initiates could purify themselves.[[202]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJoost-Gaugier2006159-209) The building is also designed according to Pythagorean numerology,[[203]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJoost-Gaugier2006159%E2%80%93161-210) with each table in the sanctuary providing seats for seven people.[[204]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJoost-Gaugier2006161-211) Three aisles lead to a single altar, symbolizing the three parts of the soul approaching the unity of Apollo.[[204]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJoost-Gaugier2006161-211) The apse depicts a scene of the poet [Sappho](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sappho "Sappho") leaping off the [Leucadian cliffs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lefkada "Lefkada"), clutching her lyre to her breast, while Apollo stands beneath her, extending his right hand in a gesture of protection,[[205]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJoost-Gaugier2006162-212) symbolizing Pythagorean teachings about the immortality of the soul.[[205]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJoost-Gaugier2006162-212) The interior of the sanctuary is almost entirely white because the color white was regarded by Pythagoreans as sacred.[[206]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJoost-Gaugier2006162%E2%80%93164-213)

The emperor [Hadrian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hadrian "Hadrian")'s [Pantheon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pantheon,_Rome "Pantheon, Rome") in [Rome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rome "Rome") was also built based on Pythagorean numerology.[[196]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJoost-Gaugier2006166%E2%80%93181-203) The temple's circular plan, central axis, hemispherical [dome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dome "Dome"), and alignment with the four cardinal directions symbolize Pythagorean views on the order of the universe.[[207]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJoost-Gaugier2006167%E2%80%93168-214) The single [oculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oculus_(architecture) "Oculus (architecture)") at the top of the dome symbolizes the monad and the sun-god Apollo.[[208]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJoost-Gaugier2006168-215) The twenty-eight ribs extending from the oculus symbolize the moon, because twenty-eight was the same number of months on the Pythagorean lunar calendar.[[209]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJoost-Gaugier2006169%E2%80%93170-216) The five coffered rings beneath the ribs represent the marriage of the sun and moon.[[210]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJoost-Gaugier2006170%E2%80%93172-217)

### In early Christianity

Many early Christians had a deep respect for Pythagoras.[[211]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJoost-Gaugier200657%E2%80%9365-218)[Eusebius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eusebius "Eusebius") (c. 260– c. 340 AD), bishop of [Caesarea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesarea_Maritima "Caesarea Maritima"), praises Pythagoras in his _Against Hierokles_ for his rule of silence, his frugality, his "extraordinary" morality, and his wise teachings.[[212]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJoost-Gaugier200657-219) In another work, Eusebius compares Pythagoras to [Moses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moses "Moses").[[212]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJoost-Gaugier200657-219) In one of his letters, the [Church Father](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Church_Fathers "Church Fathers")[Jerome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerome "Jerome") (c. 347– c. 420 AD) praises Pythagoras for his wisdom[[212]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJoost-Gaugier200657-219) and, in another letter, he credits Pythagoras for his belief in the immortality of the soul, which he suggests Christians inherited from him.[[213]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJoost-Gaugier200657%E2%80%9358-220)[Augustine of Hippo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Augustine_of_Hippo "Augustine of Hippo") (354–430 AD) rejected Pythagoras's teaching of metempsychosis without explicitly naming him, but otherwise expressed admiration for him.[[214]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJoost-Gaugier200658%E2%80%9359-221) In _[On the Trinity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/On\_the\_Trinity "On the Trinity")_, Augustine lauds the fact that Pythagoras was humble enough to call himself a _philosophos_ or "lover of wisdom" rather than a "sage".[[215]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJoost-Gaugier200659-222) In another passage, Augustine defends Pythagoras's reputation, arguing that Pythagoras certainly never taught the doctrine of metempsychosis.[[215]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJoost-Gaugier200659-222)

Influence after antiquity
-------------------------

### In the Middle Ages

[![Image 15: Medieval carving of a man with long hair and a long beard hunched over a musical instrument he is working on](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Pythagore-chartres.jpg/250px-Pythagore-chartres.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Pythagore-chartres.jpg)

Pythagoras appears in a relief sculpture on one of the [archivolts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archivolts "Archivolts") over the right door of the west portal at [Chartres Cathedral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chartres_Cathedral "Chartres Cathedral").[[216]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTECelenza2010798-223)

During the [Middle Ages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_Ages "Middle Ages"), Pythagoras was revered as the founder of mathematics and music, two of the [Seven Liberal Arts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seven_Liberal_Arts "Seven Liberal Arts").[[216]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTECelenza2010798-223) He appears in numerous medieval depictions, in illuminated manuscripts and in the relief sculptures on the portal of the [Cathedral of Chartres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cathedral_of_Chartres "Cathedral of Chartres").[[216]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTECelenza2010798-223) The _Timaeus_ was the only dialogue of Plato to survive in Latin translation in western Europe,[[216]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTECelenza2010798-223) which led [William of Conches](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_of_Conches "William of Conches") (c. 1080–1160) to declare that Plato was Pythagorean.[[216]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTECelenza2010798-223) A large-scale translation movement emerged during the Abbasid Caliphate, translating many Greek texts into Arabic. Works ascribed to Pythagoras included the "Golden Verses" and snippets of his scientific and mathematical theories.[[217]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTELindberg1978-224)[_[page needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing\_sources "Wikipedia:Citing sources")_] By translating and disseminating Pythagorean texts, Islamic scholars ensured their survival and wider accessibility. This preserved knowledge that might have otherwise been lost through the decline of the Roman Empire and the neglect of classical learning in Europe.[[218]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTELindberg2013-225)[_[page needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing\_sources "Wikipedia:Citing sources")_] In the 1430s, the Camaldolese friar [Ambrose Traversari](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ambrose_Traversari "Ambrose Traversari") translated Diogenes Laërtius's _Lives and Opinions of Eminent Philosophers_ from Greek into Latin[[216]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTECelenza2010798-223) and, in the 1460s, the philosopher [Marsilio Ficino](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marsilio_Ficino "Marsilio Ficino") translated Porphyry and Iamblichus's _Lives of Pythagoras_ into Latin as well,[[216]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTECelenza2010798-223) thereby allowing them to be read and studied by western scholars.[[216]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTECelenza2010798-223) In 1494, the Greek Neopythagorean scholar [Constantine Lascaris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constantine_Lascaris "Constantine Lascaris") published _[The Golden Verses of Pythagoras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The\_Golden\_Verses\_of\_Pythagoras "The Golden Verses of Pythagoras")_, translated into Latin, with a printed edition of his _Grammatica_,[[219]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERusso20045%E2%80%9387,_especially_51%E2%80%9353-226) thereby bringing them to a widespread audience.[[219]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERusso20045%E2%80%9387,_especially_51%E2%80%9353-226) In 1499, he published the first Renaissance biography of Pythagoras in his work _Vitae illustrium philosophorum siculorum et calabrorum_, issued in [Messina](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Messina "Messina").[[219]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERusso20045%E2%80%9387,_especially_51%E2%80%9353-226)

### On modern science

In his preface to his book _[On the Revolution of the Heavenly Spheres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/On\_the\_Revolution\_of\_the\_Heavenly\_Spheres "On the Revolution of the Heavenly Spheres")_ (1543), [Nicolaus Copernicus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nicolaus_Copernicus "Nicolaus Copernicus") cites various Pythagoreans as the most important influences on the development of his [heliocentric model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heliocentrism "Heliocentrism") of the universe,[[216]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTECelenza2010798-223)[[220]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn2001160-227) deliberately omitting mention of [Aristarchus of Samos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristarchus_of_Samos "Aristarchus of Samos"), a non-Pythagorean astronomer who had developed a fully heliocentric model in the fourth century BC, in effort to portray his model as fundamentally Pythagorean.[[220]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn2001160-227)[Johannes Kepler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johannes_Kepler "Johannes Kepler") considered himself to be a Pythagorean.[[216]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTECelenza2010798-223)[[221]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn2001161%E2%80%93171-228) He believed in the Pythagorean doctrine of _musica universalis_ and it was his search for the mathematical equations behind this doctrine that led to his discovery of the [laws of planetary motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler%27s_laws_of_planetary_motion "Kepler's laws of planetary motion"). Kepler titled his book on the subject _[Harmonices Mundi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harmonices\_Mundi "Harmonices Mundi")_ (_Harmonics of the World_), after the Pythagorean teaching that had inspired him.[[216]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTECelenza2010798-223)[[222]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn2001162-229) He also called Pythagoras the "grandfather" of all Copernicans.[[223]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJames1993142-230)

[Albert Einstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Einstein "Albert Einstein") believed that a scientist may also be "a Platonist or a Pythagorean insofar as he considers the viewpoint of logical simplicity as an indispensable and effective tool of his research."[[224]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn2001172-231) The English philosopher [Alfred North Whitehead](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alfred_North_Whitehead "Alfred North Whitehead") argued that "In a sense, Plato and Pythagoras stand nearer to modern physical science than does Aristotle. The two former were mathematicians, whereas Aristotle was the son of a doctor".[[225]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWhitehead195336%E2%80%9337-232) By this measure, Whitehead declared that Einstein and other modern scientists like him are "following the pure Pythagorean tradition."[[224]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKahn2001172-231)[[226]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWhitehead195336-233)

### On vegetarianism

[![Image 16: Painting showing Pythagoras on the far left quizzically stroking his beard as he gazes upon a massive pile of fruits and vegetables. Two followers stand behind him, fully clothed. A man with a greying beard sits at the base of a tree gesturing to the pile of produce. Next to him, a fleshy, nude woman with blonde hair plucks fruits from it. Slightly behind her, two other women, one partially clothed and the other nude but obscured by the tree branch, are also plucking fruits. At the far right end of the painting, two nude, faun-like men with beards and pointed ears hurl more fruits upon the pile.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Pythagoras_advocating_vegetarianism_%281618-20%29%3B_Peter_Paul_Rubens.jpg/500px-Pythagoras_advocating_vegetarianism_%281618-20%29%3B_Peter_Paul_Rubens.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Pythagoras_advocating_vegetarianism_(1618-20);_Peter_Paul_Rubens.jpg)

_Pythagoras Advocating Vegetarianism_ (1618–1630) by [Peter Paul Rubens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Paul_Rubens "Peter Paul Rubens") was inspired by Pythagoras's speech in Ovid's _Metamorphoses_.[[227]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBorlik2011192-234) The painting portrays the Pythagoreans with corpulent bodies, indicating a belief that vegetarianism was healthful and nutritious.[[227]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBorlik2011192-234)

A fictionalized portrayal of Pythagoras appears in Book XV of [Ovid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ovid "Ovid")'s _[Metamorphoses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metamorphoses "Metamorphoses")_,[[228]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBorlik2011189-235) in which he delivers a speech imploring his followers to adhere to a strictly vegetarian diet.[[229]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBorlik2011189%E2%80%93190-236) It was through [Arthur Golding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arthur_Golding "Arthur Golding")'s 1567 English translation of Ovid's _Metamorphoses_ that Pythagoras was best known to English-speakers throughout the early modern period.[[229]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBorlik2011189%E2%80%93190-236)[John Donne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Donne "John Donne")'s _Progress of the Soul_ discusses the implications of the doctrines expounded in the speech,[[230]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBorlik2011190-237) and [Michel de Montaigne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michel_de_Montaigne "Michel de Montaigne") quoted the speech no less than three times in his treatise "Of Cruelty" to voice his moral objections against the mistreatment of animals.[[230]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBorlik2011190-237)[John Dryden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Dryden "John Dryden") included a translation of the scene with Pythagoras in his 1700 work _[Fables, Ancient and Modern](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fables,\_Ancient\_and\_Modern "Fables, Ancient and Modern")_,[[230]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBorlik2011190-237) and [John Gay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Gay "John Gay")'s 1726 fable "Pythagoras and the Countryman" reiterates its major themes, linking carnivorism with tyranny.[[230]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBorlik2011190-237)[Lord Chesterfield](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lord_Chesterfield "Lord Chesterfield") records that his conversion to vegetarianism had been motivated by reading Pythagoras's speech in Ovid's _Metamorphoses_.[[230]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBorlik2011190-237) Until the word _vegetarianism_ was coined in the 1840s, vegetarians were referred to in English as "Pythagoreans".[[230]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBorlik2011190-237)

### On Western esotericism

Early modern [European esotericism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_esotericism "Western esotericism") drew heavily on the teachings of Pythagoras.[[216]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTECelenza2010798-223) The German [humanist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Renaissance_humanism "Renaissance humanism") scholar [Johannes Reuchlin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johannes_Reuchlin "Johannes Reuchlin") (1455–1522) synthesized Pythagoreanism with [Christian theology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christian_theology "Christian theology") and Jewish [Kabbalah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kabbalah "Kabbalah"),[[231]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg2005127%E2%80%93128-238) arguing that Kabbalah and Pythagoreanism were both inspired by [Mosaic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moses "Moses") tradition[[232]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg2005128-239) and that Pythagoras was therefore a kabbalist.[[232]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg2005128-239) In his dialogue _De verbo mirifico_ (1494), Reuchlin compared the Pythagorean tetractys to the [ineffable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ineffability "Ineffability") divine name [YHWH](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetragrammaton "Tetragrammaton"),[[231]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg2005127%E2%80%93128-238) ascribing each of the four letters of the tetragrammaton a symbolic meaning according to Pythagorean mystical teachings.[[232]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg2005128-239)

[Heinrich Cornelius Agrippa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heinrich_Cornelius_Agrippa "Heinrich Cornelius Agrippa")'s popular and influential three-volume treatise _[De Occulta Philosophia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three\_Books\_of\_Occult\_Philosophy "Three Books of Occult Philosophy")_ cites Pythagoras as a "religious magi"[[233]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFrench200230-240) and advances the idea that Pythagoras's mystical numerology operates on a [supercelestial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allegory_in_Renaissance_literature#Three_world_theory "Allegory in Renaissance literature") level,[[233]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFrench200230-240) a religious term used to describe a high heavenly realm used during his time. The [freemasons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freemasonry "Freemasonry") deliberately modeled their society on the community founded by Pythagoras at Croton.[[234]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiedweg2005133-241)[Rosicrucianism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rosicrucianism "Rosicrucianism") used Pythagorean symbolism,[[216]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTECelenza2010798-223) as did [Robert Fludd](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Fludd "Robert Fludd") (1574–1637),[[216]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTECelenza2010798-223) who believed his own musical writings to have been inspired by Pythagoras.[[216]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTECelenza2010798-223)[John Dee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Dee "John Dee") was heavily influenced by Pythagorean ideology,[[235]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTESherman199515-242)[[233]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFrench200230-240) particularly the teaching that all things are made of numbers.[[235]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTESherman199515-242)[[233]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFrench200230-240)

### On literature

The [Transcendentalists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transcendentalism "Transcendentalism") read the ancient _Lives of Pythagoras_ as guides on how to live a model life.[[236]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBregman2002186-243)[Henry David Thoreau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry_David_Thoreau "Henry David Thoreau") was impacted by [Thomas Taylor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Taylor_(neoplatonist) "Thomas Taylor (neoplatonist)")'s translations of Iamblichus's _Life of Pythagoras_ and [Stobaeus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stobaeus "Stobaeus")'s _Pythagoric Sayings_[[236]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBregman2002186-243) and his views on nature may have been influenced by the Pythagorean idea of images corresponding to archetypes.[[236]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBregman2002186-243) The Pythagorean teaching of _musica universalis_ is a recurring theme throughout Thoreau's _[magnum opus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnum\_opus "Magnum opus")_, _[Walden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Walden "Walden")_.[[236]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBregman2002186-243)
