Title: Metric space

URL Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space

Published Time: 2001-10-01T14:26:33Z

Markdown Content:
[![Image 1](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Manhattan_distance.svg/250px-Manhattan_distance.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Manhattan_distance.svg)

The [plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two-dimensional_Euclidean_space "Two-dimensional Euclidean space") (a set of points) can be equipped with different metrics. In the [taxicab metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taxicab_geometry "Taxicab geometry") the red, yellow and blue paths have the same [length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arc_length "Arc length") (12), and are all shortest paths. In the [Euclidean metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_metric "Euclidean metric"), the green path has length ![Image 2: {\displaystyle 6{\sqrt {2}}\approx 8.49}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6d4363249f648e42df783c885be8eb92338f2af2), and is the unique shortest path, whereas the red, yellow, and blue paths still have length 12.

In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), a **metric space** is a [set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_(mathematics) "Set (mathematics)") together with a notion of _[distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distance "Distance")_ between its [elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Element_(mathematics) "Element (mathematics)"), usually called [points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_(geometry) "Point (geometry)"). The distance is measured by a [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)") called a **metric** or **distance function**.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space#cite_note-FOOTNOTE%C4%8Cech196942-1) Metric spaces are a general setting for studying many of the concepts of [mathematical analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_analysis "Mathematical analysis") and [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry").

The most familiar example of a metric space is [3-dimensional Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3-dimensional_Euclidean_space "3-dimensional Euclidean space") with its usual notion of distance. Other well-known examples are a [sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere "Sphere") equipped with the [angular distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_distance "Angular distance") and the [hyperbolic plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_plane "Hyperbolic plane"). A metric may correspond to a [metaphorical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conceptual_metaphor "Conceptual metaphor"), rather than physical, notion of distance: for example, the set of 100-character Unicode strings can be equipped with the [Hamming distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamming_distance "Hamming distance"), which measures the number of characters that need to be changed to get from one string to another.

Since they are very general, metric spaces are a tool used in many different branches of mathematics. Many types of mathematical objects have a natural notion of distance and therefore admit the structure of a metric space, including [Riemannian manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_manifold "Riemannian manifold"), [normed vector spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normed_vector_space "Normed vector space"), and [graphs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_(discrete_mathematics) "Graph (discrete mathematics)"). In [abstract algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abstract_algebra "Abstract algebra"), the [_p_-adic numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/P-adic_numbers "P-adic numbers") arise as elements of the [completion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Completion_(metric_space) "Completion (metric space)") of a metric structure on the [rational numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_numbers "Rational numbers"). Metric spaces are also studied in their own right in **metric geometry**[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBuragoBuragoIvanov2001-2) and **analysis on metric spaces**.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHeinonen2001-3)

Many of the basic notions of [mathematical analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_analysis "Mathematical analysis"), including [balls](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ball_(mathematics) "Ball (mathematics)"), [completeness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complete_metric_space "Complete metric space"), as well as [uniform](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uniform_continuity "Uniform continuity"), [Lipschitz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lipschitz_continuity "Lipschitz continuity"), and [Hölder continuity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/H%C3%B6lder_continuity "Hölder continuity"), can be defined in the setting of metric spaces. Other notions, such as [continuity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_function "Continuous function"), [compactness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compactness "Compactness"), and [open](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_set "Open set") and [closed sets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_set "Closed set"), can be defined for metric spaces, but also in the even more general setting of [topological spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_space "Topological space").

Definition and illustration
---------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Metric_space&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Definition and illustration")]

[![Image 3](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Great-circle_distance_vs_straight_line_distance.svg/250px-Great-circle_distance_vs_straight_line_distance.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Great-circle_distance_vs_straight_line_distance.svg)

A diagram illustrating the great-circle distance (in cyan) and the straight-line distance (in red) between two points P and Q on a sphere.

To see the utility of different notions of distance, consider the [surface of the Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_of_the_Earth "Surface of the Earth") as a set of points. We can measure the distance between two such points by the length of the [shortest path along the surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great-circle_distance "Great-circle distance"), "[as the crow flies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/As_the_crow_flies "As the crow flies")"; this is particularly useful for shipping and aviation. We can also measure the straight-line distance between two points through the Earth's interior; this notion is, for example, natural in [seismology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seismology "Seismology"), since it roughly corresponds to the length of time it takes for seismic waves to travel between those two points.

The notion of distance encoded by the metric space axioms has relatively few requirements. This generality gives metric spaces a lot of flexibility. At the same time, the notion is strong enough to encode many intuitive facts about what distance means. This means that general results about metric spaces can be applied in many different contexts.

Like many fundamental mathematical concepts, the metric on a metric space can be interpreted in many different ways. A particular metric may not be best thought of as measuring physical distance, but, instead, as the cost of changing from one state to another (as with [Wasserstein metrics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wasserstein_metric "Wasserstein metric") on spaces of [measures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Measure_(mathematics) "Measure (mathematics)")) or the degree of difference between two objects (for example, the [Hamming distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamming_distance "Hamming distance") between two strings of characters, or the [Gromov–Hausdorff distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gromov%E2%80%93Hausdorff_convergence "Gromov–Hausdorff convergence") between metric spaces themselves).

Formally, a **metric space** is an [ordered pair](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordered_pair "Ordered pair")(_M_, _d_) where M is a set and d is a **metric** on M, i.e., a [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)")![Image 4: {\displaystyle d\,\colon M\times M\to \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fa7a2e6b602e9a9325d834170bf5b841bf3bf9cf)satisfying the following axioms for all points ![Image 5: {\displaystyle x,y,z\in M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ba283a127121ad64c98d3f69ced0ac4a86ec6414):[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBuragoBuragoIvanov20011-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGromov2007xv-5)

1.   The distance from a point to itself is zero: ![Image 6: {\displaystyle d(x,x)=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f8b0b392db0a4abf6c47dcd5b35f90592401e756)
2.   (Positivity) The distance between two distinct points is always positive: ![Image 7: {\displaystyle {\text{If }}x\neq y{\text{, then }}d(x,y)>0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/14c273c8a68c3cbc06a20d236f26fb83043fd941)
3.   ([Symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetric_function "Symmetric function")) The distance from x to y is always the same as the distance from y to x: ![Image 8: {\displaystyle d(x,y)=d(y,x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c7fea33d0e60116abd16287351eb6bf142a61fdd)
4.   The [triangle inequality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle_inequality "Triangle inequality") holds: ![Image 9: {\displaystyle d(x,z)\leq d(x,y)+d(y,z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/43ae751284c2944886e1effbfe4e0c1293f98419)This is a natural property of both physical and metaphorical notions of distance: you can arrive at z from x by taking a detour through y, but this will not make your journey any shorter than the direct path.

If the metric d is unambiguous, one often refers by [abuse of notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abuse_of_notation "Abuse of notation") to "the metric space M".

By taking all axioms except the second, one can show that distance is always non-negative:![Image 10: {\displaystyle 0=d(x,x)\leq d(x,y)+d(y,x)=2d(x,y)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dbb5c81eebd08b8155f33c70761a4f619b436e6d)Therefore the second axiom can be weakened to ![Image 11: {\textstyle {\text{If }}x\neq y{\text{, then }}d(x,y)\neq 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a711db1f66ee741f7620e40bafcf4c905c6d7f21) and combined with the first to make ![Image 12: {\textstyle d(x,y)=0\iff x=y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8f45cfc9536a8e2fc8a1a5a7128fc148c4fbb125).[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space#cite_note-6)

The [real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number") with the distance function ![Image 13: {\displaystyle d(x,y)=|y-x|}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d4ccdaa16fa9887b70273ee18e3b1b2f20d61d3f) given by the [absolute difference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_difference "Absolute difference") form a metric space. Many properties of metric spaces and functions between them are generalizations of concepts in [real analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_analysis "Real analysis") and coincide with those concepts when applied to the real line.

#### Metrics on Euclidean spaces

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Metric_space&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Metrics on Euclidean spaces")]

[![Image 14](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Minkowski_distance_examples.svg/250px-Minkowski_distance_examples.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Minkowski_distance_examples.svg)

Comparison of Chebyshev, Euclidean and taxicab distances for the hypotenuse of a 3-4-5 triangle on a chessboard

The Euclidean plane ![Image 15: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e150115ab9f63023215109595b76686a1ff890fd) can be equipped with many different metrics. The [Euclidean distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_distance "Euclidean distance") familiar from school mathematics can be defined by ![Image 16: {\displaystyle d_{2}((x_{1},y_{1}),(x_{2},y_{2}))={\sqrt {(x_{2}-x_{1})^{2}+(y_{2}-y_{1})^{2}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ea0ba1812589f13bde6abe0610dd32061edb0f1d)

The [_taxicab_ or _Manhattan_ distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taxicab_geometry "Taxicab geometry") is defined by ![Image 17: {\displaystyle d_{1}((x_{1},y_{1}),(x_{2},y_{2}))=|x_{2}-x_{1}|+|y_{2}-y_{1}|}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e7b3acf7666b59a8c0c458360ec598ea129f1777) and can be thought of as the distance you need to travel along horizontal and vertical lines to get from one point to the other, as illustrated at the top of the article.

The _maximum_, ![Image 18: {\displaystyle L^{\infty }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b9ab400cc4dfd865180cd84c72dc894ca457671f), or _[Chebyshev distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chebyshev\_distance "Chebyshev distance")_ is defined by ![Image 19: {\displaystyle d_{\infty }((x_{1},y_{1}),(x_{2},y_{2}))=\max\{|x_{2}-x_{1}|,|y_{2}-y_{1}|\}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7af57ea864a570149bc3a0482a3a7111812c7905) This distance does not have an easy explanation in terms of paths in the plane, but it still satisfies the metric space axioms. It can be thought of similarly to the number of moves a [king](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/King_(chess) "King (chess)") would have to make on a [chess](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chess "Chess")[board](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Board_game "Board game") to travel from one point to another on the given space.

In fact, these three distances, while they have distinct properties, are similar in some ways. Informally, points that are close in one are close in the others, too. This observation can be quantified with the formula ![Image 20: {\displaystyle d_{\infty }(p,q)\leq d_{2}(p,q)\leq d_{1}(p,q)\leq 2d_{\infty }(p,q),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c5ef8a8fb50768d565fbf6cec4727b4fda84fc56) which holds for every pair of points ![Image 21: {\displaystyle p,q\in \mathbb {R} ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1ee72fcef6aa20ae237e8d7242fef4eb83de566e).

A radically different distance can be defined by setting ![Image 22: {\displaystyle d(p,q)={\begin{cases}0,&{\text{if }}p=q,\\1,&{\text{otherwise.}}\end{cases}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/765a68868cb7a61922dced5212dc20949d561cb1) Using [Iverson brackets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iverson_bracket "Iverson bracket"), ![Image 23: {\displaystyle d(p,q)=[p\neq q]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e025bbb02a33d44a0c2373007bd8e878240f2dc4) In this _discrete metric_, all distinct points are 1 unit apart: none of them are close to each other, and none of them are very far away from each other either. Intuitively, the discrete metric no longer remembers that the set is a plane, but treats it just as an undifferentiated set of points.

All of these metrics make sense on ![Image 24: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d) as well as ![Image 25: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e150115ab9f63023215109595b76686a1ff890fd).

Given a metric space (_M_, _d_) and a [subset](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subset "Subset")![Image 26: {\displaystyle A\subseteq M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3817146332479574af6e80d38b1247eada04ae83), we can consider A to be a metric space by measuring distances the same way we would in M. Formally, the _induced metric_ on A is a function ![Image 27: {\displaystyle d_{A}:A\times A\to \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d1d52f04717ef0fb3463da72303831852a125990) defined by ![Image 28: {\displaystyle d_{A}(x,y)=d(x,y).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/382fe6588674cd4853889d6b327c321d3fd01039) For example, if we take the two-dimensional sphere S 2 as a subset of ![Image 29: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5), the Euclidean metric on ![Image 30: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5) induces the straight-line metric on S 2 described above. Two more useful examples are the open interval (0, 1) and the closed interval [0, 1] thought of as subspaces of the real line.

[Arthur Cayley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arthur_Cayley "Arthur Cayley"), in his article "On Distance", extended metric concepts beyond Euclidean geometry into domains bounded by a conic in a projective space. His [distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distance "Distance") was given by logarithm of a [cross ratio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_ratio "Cross ratio"). Any projectivity leaving the conic stable also leaves the cross ratio constant, so isometries are implicit. This method provides models for [elliptic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptic_geometry "Elliptic geometry") and [hyperbolic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_geometry "Hyperbolic geometry"), and [Felix Klein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Felix_Klein "Felix Klein"), in several publications, established the field of [non-euclidean geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-euclidean_geometry "Non-euclidean geometry") through the use of the [Cayley-Klein metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cayley-Klein_metric "Cayley-Klein metric").

The idea of an abstract space with metric properties was addressed in 1906 by [René Maurice Fréchet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Maurice_Fr%C3%A9chet "René Maurice Fréchet")[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space#cite_note-7) and the term _metric space_ was coined by [Felix Hausdorff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Felix_Hausdorff "Felix Hausdorff") in 1914.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space#cite_note-8)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space#cite_note-9)[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space#cite_note-10)

Fréchet's work laid the foundation for understanding [convergence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convergence_of_random_variables "Convergence of random variables"), [continuity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuity_equation "Continuity equation"), and other key concepts in non-geometric spaces. This allowed mathematicians to study functions and sequences in a broader and more flexible way. This was important for the growing field of functional analysis. Mathematicians like Hausdorff and [Stefan Banach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stefan_Banach "Stefan Banach") further refined and expanded the framework of metric spaces. Hausdorff introduced [topological spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_space "Topological space") as a generalization of metric spaces. Banach's work in [functional analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functional_analysis "Functional analysis") heavily relied on the metric structure. Over time, metric spaces became a central part of [modern mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_mathematics "History of mathematics"). They have influenced various fields including [topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology "Topology"), [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry"), and [applied mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Applied_mathematics "Applied mathematics"). Metric spaces continue to play a crucial role in the study of abstract mathematical concepts.

A distance function is enough to define notions of closeness and convergence that were first developed in [real analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_analysis "Real analysis"). Properties that depend on the structure of a metric space are referred to as _metric properties_. Every metric space is also a [topological space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_space "Topological space"), and some metric properties can also be rephrased without reference to distance in the language of topology; that is, they are really [topological properties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_property "Topological property").

### The topology of a metric space

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Metric_space&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: The topology of a metric space")]

For any point x in a metric space M and any real number _r_> 0, the [_open ball_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ball_(mathematics) "Ball (mathematics)") of radius r around x is defined to be the set of points that are strictly less than distance r from x: ![Image 31: {\displaystyle B_{r}(x)=\{y\in M:d(x,y)<r\}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1c23ac48e61a11d09e030c3fbbee290b9eaea2c8) This is a natural way to define a set of points that are relatively close to x. Therefore, a set ![Image 32: {\displaystyle N\subseteq M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cfe9ab1adee54a74e4a9595b31e0f3302b9e4c2e) is a [_neighborhood_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neighborhood_(mathematics) "Neighborhood (mathematics)") of x (informally, it contains all points "close enough" to x) if it contains an open ball of radius r around x for some _r_> 0.

An _open set_ is a set which is a neighborhood of all its points. It follows that the open balls form a [base](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Base_(topology) "Base (topology)") for a topology on M. In other words, the open sets of M are exactly the unions of open balls. As in any topology, [closed sets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_set "Closed set") are the complements of open sets. Sets may be both open and closed as well as neither open nor closed.

This topology does not carry all the information about the metric space. For example, the distances _d_ 1, _d_ 2, and _d_∞ defined above all induce the same topology on ![Image 33: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e150115ab9f63023215109595b76686a1ff890fd), although they behave differently in many respects. Similarly, ![Image 34: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc) with the Euclidean metric and its subspace the interval (0, 1) with the induced metric are [homeomorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homeomorphism "Homeomorphism") but have very different metric properties.

Conversely, not every topological space can be given a metric. Topological spaces which are compatible with a metric are called [_metrizable_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metrizable_space "Metrizable space") and are particularly well-behaved in many ways: in particular, they are [paracompact](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paracompact_space "Paracompact space")[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space#cite_note-11)[Hausdorff spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hausdorff_space "Hausdorff space") (hence [normal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_space "Normal space")) and [first-countable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First-countable_space "First-countable space").[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space#cite_note-12) The [Nagata–Smirnov metrization theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nagata%E2%80%93Smirnov_metrization_theorem "Nagata–Smirnov metrization theorem") gives a characterization of metrizability in terms of other topological properties, without reference to metrics.

[Convergence of sequences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limit_of_a_sequence "Limit of a sequence") in Euclidean space is defined as follows:

A sequence (_x n_) converges to a point x if for every ε > 0 there is an integer N such that for all _n_>_N_, _d_(_x n_, _x_) < ε.
Convergence of sequences in a topological space is defined as follows:

A sequence (_x n_) converges to a point x if for every open set U containing x there is an integer N such that for all _n_>_N_, ![Image 35: {\displaystyle x_{n}\in U}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9552d871d5e5c3d4f0cdee55ea24cf6253ed5e85).
In metric spaces, both of these definitions make sense and they are equivalent. This is a general pattern for [topological properties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_property "Topological property") of metric spaces: while they can be defined in a purely topological way, there is often a way that uses the metric which is easier to state or more familiar from real analysis.

Informally, a metric space is _complete_ if it has no "missing points": every sequence that looks like it should converge to something actually converges.

To make this precise: a sequence (_x n_) in a metric space M is [_Cauchy_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cauchy_sequence "Cauchy sequence") if for every ε > 0 there is an integer N such that for all _m_, _n_>_N_, _d_(_x m_, _x n_) < ε. By the triangle inequality, any convergent sequence is Cauchy: if x m and x n are both less than ε away from the limit, then they are less than 2ε away from each other. If the converse is true—every Cauchy sequence in M converges—then M is complete.

Euclidean spaces are complete, as is ![Image 36: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e150115ab9f63023215109595b76686a1ff890fd) with the other metrics described above. Two examples of spaces which are not complete are (0, 1) and the rationals, each with the metric induced from ![Image 37: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc). One can think of (0, 1) as "missing" its endpoints 0 and 1. The rationals are missing all the irrationals, since any irrational has a sequence of rationals converging to it in ![Image 38: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc) (for example, its successive decimal approximations). These examples show that completeness is _not_ a topological property, since ![Image 39: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc) is complete but the homeomorphic space (0, 1) is not.

This notion of "missing points" can be made precise. In fact, every metric space has a unique [_completion_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Completion_(metric_space) "Completion (metric space)"), which is a complete space that contains the given space as a [dense](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dense_set "Dense set") subset. For example, [0, 1] is the completion of (0, 1), and the real numbers are the completion of the rationals.

Since complete spaces are generally easier to work with, completions are important throughout mathematics. For example, in abstract algebra, the [_p_-adic numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/P-adic_numbers "P-adic numbers") are defined as the completion of the rationals under a different metric. Completion is particularly common as a tool in [functional analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functional_analysis "Functional analysis"). Often one has a set of nice functions and a way of measuring distances between them. Taking the completion of this metric space gives a new set of functions which may be less nice, but nevertheless useful because they behave similarly to the original nice functions in important ways. For example, [weak solutions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weak_solution "Weak solution") to [differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_equation "Differential equation") typically live in a completion (a [Sobolev space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sobolev_space "Sobolev space")) rather than the original space of nice functions for which the differential equation actually makes sense.

### Bounded and totally bounded spaces

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Metric_space&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Bounded and totally bounded spaces")]

[![Image 40](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Diameter_of_a_Set.svg/250px-Diameter_of_a_Set.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Diameter_of_a_Set.svg)

Diameter of a set.

A metric space M is _bounded_ if there is an r such that no pair of points in M is more than distance r apart.[[b]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space#cite_note-13) The least such r is called the [diameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diameter_of_a_set "Diameter of a set") of M.

The space M is called _precompact_ or _[totally bounded](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Totally\_bounded "Totally bounded")_ if for every _r_> 0 there is a finite [cover](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cover_(topology) "Cover (topology)") of M by open balls of radius r. Every totally bounded space is bounded. To see this, start with a finite cover by r-balls for some arbitrary r. Since the subset of M consisting of the centers of these balls is finite, it has finite diameter, say D. By the triangle inequality, the diameter of the whole space is at most _D_ + 2 _r_. The converse does not hold: an example of a metric space that is bounded but not totally bounded is ![Image 41: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e150115ab9f63023215109595b76686a1ff890fd) (or any other infinite set) with the discrete metric.

Compactness is a topological property which generalizes the properties of a closed and bounded subset of Euclidean space. There are several equivalent definitions of compactness in metric spaces:

1.   A metric space M is compact if every open cover has a finite subcover (the usual topological definition).
2.   A metric space M is compact if every sequence has a convergent subsequence. (For general topological spaces this is called [sequential compactness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sequentially_compact_space "Sequentially compact space") and is not equivalent to compactness.)
3.   A metric space M is compact if it is complete and totally bounded. (This definition is written in terms of metric properties and does not make sense for a general topological space, but it is nevertheless topologically invariant since it is equivalent to compactness.)

One example of a compact space is the closed interval [0, 1].

Compactness is important for similar reasons to completeness: it makes it easy to find limits. Another important tool is [Lebesgue's number lemma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebesgue%27s_number_lemma "Lebesgue's number lemma"), which shows that for any open cover of a compact space, every point is relatively deep inside one of the sets of the cover.

Functions between metric spaces
-------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Metric_space&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: Functions between metric spaces")]

[![Image 42](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Functions_between_metric_spaces.svg/330px-Functions_between_metric_spaces.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Functions_between_metric_spaces.svg)

[Euler diagram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler_diagram "Euler diagram") of types of functions between metric spaces.

Unlike in the case of topological spaces or algebraic structures such as [groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_(mathematics) "Group (mathematics)") or [rings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ring_(mathematics) "Ring (mathematics)"), there is no single "right" type of [structure-preserving function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morphism "Morphism") between metric spaces. Instead, one works with different types of functions depending on one's goals. Throughout this section, suppose that ![Image 43: {\displaystyle (M_{1},d_{1})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/94d3c0aca0772489db301d6c287dc52c5545bc75) and ![Image 44: {\displaystyle (M_{2},d_{2})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b05a4c68a67ff10c60270286f08ee4917de29200) are two metric spaces. The words "function" and "map" are used interchangeably.

One interpretation of a "structure-preserving" map is one that fully preserves the distance function:

A function ![Image 45: {\displaystyle f:M_{1}\to M_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/46f53db9ee82416bb7380eee882afeb7533bf639) is _distance-preserving_[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBuragoBuragoIvanov20012-14) if for every pair of points x and y in _M_ 1, ![Image 46: {\displaystyle d_{2}(f(x),f(y))=d_{1}(x,y).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bab26e06458a11228b6022d106fe307a480a0d66)
It follows from the metric space axioms that a distance-preserving function is injective. A bijective distance-preserving function is called an _isometry_.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space#cite_note-15) One perhaps non-obvious example of an isometry between spaces described in this article is the map ![Image 47: {\displaystyle f:(\mathbb {R} ^{2},d_{1})\to (\mathbb {R} ^{2},d_{\infty })}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d98ff99747477211f9aa50fc39961e47c4b9613a) defined by ![Image 48: {\displaystyle f(x,y)=(x+y,x-y).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b5a6fc13924420432747020d500f1f2aefbad8dd)

If there is an isometry between the spaces _M_ 1 and _M_ 2, they are said to be _isometric_. Metric spaces that are isometric are [essentially identical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isomorphism "Isomorphism").

On the other end of the spectrum, one can forget entirely about the metric structure and study [continuous maps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_function_(topology) "Continuous function (topology)"), which only preserve topological structure. There are several equivalent definitions of continuity for metric spaces. The most important are:

(These first two definitions are _not_ equivalent for all topological spaces.)
A _[homeomorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homeomorphism "Homeomorphism")_ is a continuous bijection whose inverse is also continuous; if there is a homeomorphism between _M_ 1 and _M_ 2, they are said to be _homeomorphic_. Homeomorphic spaces are the same from the point of view of topology, but may have very different metric properties. For example, ![Image 49: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc) is unbounded and complete, while (0, 1) is bounded but not complete.

### Uniformly continuous maps

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Metric_space&action=edit&section=18 "Edit section: Uniformly continuous maps")]

A function ![Image 50: {\displaystyle f\,\colon M_{1}\to M_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6aa1f72d391488d26c0f48c7c0ace1aa256daa7e) is _[uniformly continuous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uniform\_continuity "Uniform continuity")_ if for every real number ε > 0 there exists δ > 0 such that for all points x and y in _M_ 1 such that ![Image 51: {\displaystyle d(x,y)<\delta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/95e7bd68b6630a4968db4b0e2152b76a75056a23), we have ![Image 52: {\displaystyle d_{2}(f(x),f(y))<\varepsilon .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8140d93a391f5eb8705fd6ad6322faa406771ae7)

The only difference between this definition and the ε–δ definition of continuity is the order of quantifiers: the choice of δ must depend only on ε and not on the point x. However, this subtle change makes a big difference. For example, uniformly continuous maps take Cauchy sequences in _M_ 1 to Cauchy sequences in _M_ 2. In other words, uniform continuity preserves some metric properties which are not purely topological.

On the other hand, the [Heine–Cantor theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heine%E2%80%93Cantor_theorem "Heine–Cantor theorem") states that if _M_ 1 is compact, then every continuous map is uniformly continuous. In other words, uniform continuity cannot distinguish any non-topological features of compact metric spaces.

### Lipschitz maps and contractions

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Metric_space&action=edit&section=19 "Edit section: Lipschitz maps and contractions")]

A [Lipschitz map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lipschitz_continuity "Lipschitz continuity") is one that stretches distances by at most a bounded factor. Formally, given a real number _K_> 0, the map ![Image 53: {\displaystyle f\,\colon M_{1}\to M_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6aa1f72d391488d26c0f48c7c0ace1aa256daa7e) is K-_Lipschitz_ if ![Image 54: {\displaystyle d_{2}(f(x),f(y))\leq Kd_{1}(x,y)\quad {\text{for all}}\quad x,y\in M_{1}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a8dd7e0a4bcf513c8571bcb3fae31fcc35f7a8cd) Lipschitz maps are particularly important in metric geometry, since they provide more flexibility than distance-preserving maps, but still make essential use of the metric.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGromov2007xvii-16) For example, a curve in a metric space is [rectifiable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arc_length "Arc length") (has finite length) if and only if it has a Lipschitz reparametrization.

A 1-Lipschitz map is sometimes called a _nonexpanding_ or _[metric map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric\_map "Metric map")_. Metric maps are commonly taken to be the morphisms of the [category of metric spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category_of_metric_spaces "Category of metric spaces").

A K-Lipschitz map for _K_< 1 is called a _[contraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contraction\_mapping "Contraction mapping")_. The [Banach fixed-point theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Banach_fixed-point_theorem "Banach fixed-point theorem") states that if M is a complete metric space, then every contraction ![Image 55: {\displaystyle f:M\to M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9887c379b4c7403189b8bd4c2eae331f47d33717) admits a unique [fixed point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fixed_point_(mathematics) "Fixed point (mathematics)"). If the metric space M is compact, the result holds for a slightly weaker condition on f: a map ![Image 56: {\displaystyle f:M\to M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9887c379b4c7403189b8bd4c2eae331f47d33717) admits a unique fixed point if ![Image 57: {\displaystyle d(f(x),f(y))<d(x,y)\quad {\mbox{for all}}\quad x\neq y\in M_{1}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fc66e3706c76037136fec484554f245e2ed76ea2)

A [quasi-isometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasi-isometry "Quasi-isometry") is a map that preserves the "large-scale structure" of a metric space. Quasi-isometries need not be continuous. For example, ![Image 58: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e150115ab9f63023215109595b76686a1ff890fd) and its subspace ![Image 59: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a807ab4cb3de13a66771b5a303aca31e0391e6aa) are quasi-isometric, even though one is connected and the other is discrete. The equivalence relation of quasi-isometry is important in [geometric group theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_group_theory "Geometric group theory"): the [Švarc–Milnor lemma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C5%A0varc%E2%80%93Milnor_lemma "Švarc–Milnor lemma") states that all spaces on which a group [acts geometrically](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_group_action "Geometric group action") are quasi-isometric.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMargalitThomas2017-17)

Formally, the map ![Image 60: {\displaystyle f\,\colon M_{1}\to M_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6aa1f72d391488d26c0f48c7c0ace1aa256daa7e) is a _quasi-isometric embedding_ if there exist constants _A_ ≥ 1 and _B_ ≥ 0 such that ![Image 61: {\displaystyle {\frac {1}{A}}d_{2}(f(x),f(y))-B\leq d_{1}(x,y)\leq Ad_{2}(f(x),f(y))+B\quad {\text{ for all }}\quad x,y\in M_{1}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/86d80138a711860401a8e82fedc649d7d086de93) It is a _quasi-isometry_ if in addition it is _quasi-surjective_, i.e. there is a constant _C_ ≥ 0 such that every point in ![Image 62: {\displaystyle M_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d5d4dffae5ee0db4cc433e252ee9ed7530e5cf0) is at distance at most C from some point in the image ![Image 63: {\displaystyle f(M_{1})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1c16e62abf9983afb653dbdfc69e9da5319b27f9).

### Normed vector spaces

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Metric_space&action=edit&section=23 "Edit section: Normed vector spaces")]

A [normed vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normed_vector_space "Normed vector space") is a vector space equipped with a _[norm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norm\_(mathematics) "Norm (mathematics)")_, which is a function that measures the length of vectors. The norm of a vector v is typically denoted by ![Image 66: {\displaystyle \lVert v\rVert }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e373cfcd31951e395710bc6b431120c3c3931745). Any normed vector space can be equipped with a metric in which the distance between two vectors x and y is given by ![Image 67: {\displaystyle d(x,y):=\lVert x-y\rVert .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fa80d85ddbb90dda9fde16b8d5e4058857c1d2a2) The metric d is said to be _induced_ by the norm ![Image 68: {\displaystyle \lVert {\cdot }\rVert }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7d4e4508f912075db0aca27e0e828fbcadd9e067). Conversely,[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space#cite_note-FOOTNOTENariciBeckenstein201147%E2%80%9366-18) if a metric d on a [vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space")X is

then it is the metric induced by the norm ![Image 69: {\displaystyle \lVert x\rVert :=d(x,0).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dc798453419e3222a827999903ce8bc240459937) A similar relationship holds between [seminorms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seminorm "Seminorm") and [pseudometrics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudometric_space "Pseudometric space").

Among examples of metrics induced by a norm are the metrics _d_ 1, _d_ 2, and _d_∞ on ![Image 70: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e150115ab9f63023215109595b76686a1ff890fd), which are induced by the [Manhattan norm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manhattan_norm "Manhattan norm"), the [Euclidean norm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_norm "Euclidean norm"), and the [maximum norm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maximum_norm "Maximum norm"), respectively. More generally, the [Kuratowski embedding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kuratowski_embedding "Kuratowski embedding") allows one to see any metric space as a subspace of a normed vector space.

Infinite-dimensional normed vector spaces, particularly spaces of functions, are studied in [functional analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functional_analysis "Functional analysis"). Completeness is particularly important in this context: a complete normed vector space is known as a [Banach space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Banach_space "Banach space"). An unusual property of normed vector spaces is that [linear transformations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_transformation "Linear transformation") between them are continuous if and only if they are Lipschitz. Such transformations are known as [bounded operators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bounded_operator "Bounded operator").

[![Image 71](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Approximate_arc_length.svg/250px-Approximate_arc_length.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Approximate_arc_length.svg)

One possible approximation for the arc length of a curve. The approximation is never longer than the arc length, justifying the definition of arc length as a [supremum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supremum "Supremum").

A [curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curve "Curve") in a metric space (_M_, _d_) is a continuous function ![Image 72: {\displaystyle \gamma :[0,T]\to M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f51598fa2d3e48c963d4e56c0af969afaa51f549). The [length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arc_length "Arc length") of γ is measured by ![Image 73: {\displaystyle L(\gamma )=\sup _{0=x_{0}<x_{1}<\cdots <x_{n}=T}\left\{\sum _{k=1}^{n}d(\gamma (x_{k-1}),\gamma (x_{k}))\right\}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/57fa5c5287e046af0e8484e05fa038b82aae6d36) In general, this supremum may be infinite; a curve of finite length is called _rectifiable_.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBuragoBuragoIvanov2001Definition_2.3.1-19) Suppose that the length of the curve γ is equal to the distance between its endpoints—that is, it is the shortest possible path between its endpoints. After reparametrization by arc length, γ becomes a _[geodesic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic "Geodesic")_: a curve which is a distance-preserving function.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMargalitThomas2017-17) A geodesic is a shortest possible path between any two of its points.[[c]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space#cite_note-22)

A _geodesic metric space_ is a metric space which admits a geodesic between any two of its points. The spaces ![Image 74: {\displaystyle (\mathbb {R} ^{2},d_{1})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2362b4a4d7e9b64ee7e2262dee7de30d2403e299) and ![Image 75: {\displaystyle (\mathbb {R} ^{2},d_{2})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/14ffad81c43f03ddc159b7d4a7b620dc195aafce) are both geodesic metric spaces. In ![Image 76: {\displaystyle (\mathbb {R} ^{2},d_{2})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/14ffad81c43f03ddc159b7d4a7b620dc195aafce), geodesics are unique, but in ![Image 77: {\displaystyle (\mathbb {R} ^{2},d_{1})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2362b4a4d7e9b64ee7e2262dee7de30d2403e299), there are often infinitely many geodesics between two points, as shown in the figure at the top of the article.

The space M is a _[length space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Length\_space "Length space")_ (or the metric d is _intrinsic_) if the distance between any two points x and y is the infimum of lengths of paths between them. Unlike in a geodesic metric space, the infimum does not have to be attained. An example of a length space which is not geodesic is the Euclidean plane minus the origin: the points (1, 0) and (-1, 0) can be joined by paths of length arbitrarily close to 2, but not by a path of length 2. An example of a metric space which is not a length space is given by the straight-line metric on the sphere: the straight line between two points through the center of the Earth is shorter than any path along the surface.

Given any metric space (_M_, _d_), one can define a new, intrinsic distance function _d_ intrinsic on M by setting the distance between points x and y to be the infimum of the d-lengths of paths between them. For instance, if d is the straight-line distance on the sphere, then _d_ intrinsic is the great-circle distance. However, in some cases _d_ intrinsic may have infinite values. For example, if M is the [Koch snowflake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Koch_snowflake "Koch snowflake") with the subspace metric d induced from ![Image 78: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e150115ab9f63023215109595b76686a1ff890fd), then the resulting intrinsic distance is infinite for any pair of distinct points.

### Riemannian manifolds

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Metric_space&action=edit&section=25 "Edit section: Riemannian manifolds")]

A [Riemannian manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_manifold "Riemannian manifold") is a space equipped with a Riemannian [metric tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_tensor "Metric tensor"), which determines lengths of [tangent vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_space "Tangent space") at every point. This can be thought of defining a notion of distance infinitesimally. In particular, a differentiable path ![Image 79: {\displaystyle \gamma :[0,T]\to M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f51598fa2d3e48c963d4e56c0af969afaa51f549) in a Riemannian manifold M has length defined as the integral of the length of the tangent vector to the path: ![Image 80: {\displaystyle L(\gamma )=\int _{0}^{T}|{\dot {\gamma }}(t)|dt.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/78f26517d50ba283d6685065612192c5f2f10d11) On a connected Riemannian manifold, one then defines the distance between two points as the infimum of lengths of smooth paths between them. This construction generalizes to other kinds of infinitesimal metrics on manifolds, such as [sub-Riemannian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sub-Riemannian_manifold "Sub-Riemannian manifold") and [Finsler metrics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finsler_manifold "Finsler manifold").

The Riemannian metric is uniquely determined by the distance function; this means that in principle, all information about a Riemannian manifold can be recovered from its distance function. One direction in metric geometry is finding purely metric (["synthetic"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synthetic_geometry "Synthetic geometry")) formulations of properties of Riemannian manifolds. For example, a Riemannian manifold is a [CAT(_k_) space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CAT(k)_space "CAT(k) space") (a synthetic condition which depends purely on the metric) if and only if its [sectional curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sectional_curvature "Sectional curvature") is bounded above by k.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBuragoBuragoIvanov2001127-23) Thus CAT(_k_) spaces generalize upper curvature bounds to general metric spaces.

### Metric measure spaces

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Metric_space&action=edit&section=26 "Edit section: Metric measure spaces")]

Real analysis makes use of both the metric on ![Image 81: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d) and the [Lebesgue measure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebesgue_measure "Lebesgue measure"). Therefore, generalizations of many ideas from analysis naturally reside in [metric measure spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Metric_measure_space&action=edit&redlink=1 "Metric measure space (page does not exist)"): spaces that have both a [measure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Measure_(mathematics) "Measure (mathematics)") and a metric which are compatible with each other. Formally, a _metric measure space_ is a metric space equipped with a [Borel regular measure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Borel_regular_measure "Borel regular measure") such that every ball has positive measure.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHeinonen2007191-24) For example Euclidean spaces of dimension n, and more generally n-dimensional Riemannian manifolds, naturally have the structure of a metric measure space, equipped with the [Lebesgue measure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebesgue_measure "Lebesgue measure"). Certain [fractal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal "Fractal") metric spaces such as the [Sierpiński gasket](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sierpi%C5%84ski_gasket "Sierpiński gasket") can be equipped with the α-dimensional [Hausdorff measure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hausdorff_measure "Hausdorff measure") where α is the [Hausdorff dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hausdorff_dimension "Hausdorff dimension"). In general, however, a metric space may not have an "obvious" choice of measure.

One application of metric measure spaces is generalizing the notion of [Ricci curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_curvature "Ricci curvature") beyond Riemannian manifolds. Just as CAT(_k_) and [Alexandrov spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexandrov_space "Alexandrov space") generalize sectional curvature bounds, [RCD spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=RCD_space&action=edit&redlink=1 "RCD space (page does not exist)") are a class of metric measure spaces which generalize lower bounds on Ricci curvature.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space#cite_note-25)

### Graphs and finite metric spaces

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Metric_space&action=edit&section=28 "Edit section: Graphs and finite metric spaces")]

A metric space is _discrete_ if its induced topology is the [discrete topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discrete_topology "Discrete topology"). Although many concepts, such as completeness and compactness, are not interesting for such spaces, they are nevertheless an object of study in several branches of mathematics. In particular, finite metric spaces (those having a [finite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_set "Finite set") number of points) are studied in [combinatorics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Combinatorics "Combinatorics") and [theoretical computer science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theoretical_computer_science "Theoretical computer science").[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space#cite_note-26) Embeddings in other metric spaces are particularly well-studied. For example, not every finite metric space can be [isometrically embedded](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isometry "Isometry") in a Euclidean space or in [Hilbert space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space "Hilbert space"). On the other hand, in the worst case the required distortion (bilipschitz constant) is only logarithmic in the number of points.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space#cite_note-27)[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space#cite_note-28)

For any [undirected connected graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_(discrete_mathematics) "Graph (discrete mathematics)")G, the set V of vertices of G can be turned into a metric space by defining the [distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distance_(graph_theory) "Distance (graph theory)") between vertices x and y to be the length of the shortest edge path connecting them. In graph theory, this is also called the [shortest-path distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distance_(graph_theory) "Distance (graph theory)") or geodesic distance. In [geometric group theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_group_theory "Geometric group theory") this construction is applied to the [Cayley graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cayley_graph "Cayley graph") of a (typically infinite) [finitely-generated group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finitely-generated_group "Finitely-generated group"), yielding the [word metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Word_metric "Word metric"). Up to a bilipschitz homeomorphism, the word metric depends only on the group and not on the chosen finite generating set.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMargalitThomas2017-17)

### Metric embeddings and approximations

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Metric_space&action=edit&section=29 "Edit section: Metric embeddings and approximations")]

An important area of study in finite metric spaces is the embedding of complex metric spaces into simpler ones while controlling the distortion of distances. This is particularly useful in computer science and discrete mathematics, where algorithms often perform more efficiently on simpler structures like tree metrics.

A significant result in this area is that any finite metric space can be probabilistically embedded into a _tree metric_ with an expected distortion of ![Image 82: {\displaystyle O(logn)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3d667abec28e6f6ced3d873ef6ed57887b7983ef), where ![Image 83: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) is the number of points in the metric space.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space#cite_note-29)

This embedding is notable because it achieves the best possible asymptotic bound on distortion, matching the lower bound of ![Image 84: {\displaystyle \Omega (logn)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3c2c169279bffdcddc4477df352804519f3b05eb). The tree metrics produced in this embedding _dominate_ the original metrics, meaning that distances in the tree are greater than or equal to those in the original space. This property is particularly useful for designing approximation algorithms, as it allows for the preservation of distance-related properties while simplifying the underlying structure.

The result has significant implications for various computational problems:

*   **Network design**: Improves approximation algorithms for problems like the _Group Steiner tree problem_ (a generalization of the _[Steiner tree problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steiner\_tree\_problem "Steiner tree problem")_) and _Buy-at-bulk network design_ (a problem in _[Network planning and design](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Network\_planning\_and\_design "Network planning and design")_) by simplifying the metric space to a tree metric.
*   **Clustering**: Enhances algorithms for clustering problems where hierarchical clustering can be performed more efficiently on tree metrics.
*   **Online algorithms**: Benefits problems like the _[k-server problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K-server\_problem "K-server problem")_ and _[metrical task system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metrical\_task\_system "Metrical task system")_ by providing better competitive ratios through simplified metrics.

The technique involves constructing a hierarchical decomposition of the original metric space and converting it into a tree metric via a randomized algorithm. The ![Image 85: {\displaystyle O(logn)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3d667abec28e6f6ced3d873ef6ed57887b7983ef) distortion bound has led to improved [approximation ratios](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Approximation_ratio "Approximation ratio") in several algorithmic problems, demonstrating the practical significance of this theoretical result.

### Distances between mathematical objects

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Metric_space&action=edit&section=30 "Edit section: Distances between mathematical objects")]

In modern mathematics, one often studies spaces whose points are themselves mathematical objects. A distance function on such a space generally aims to measure the dissimilarity between two objects. Here are some examples:

*   **Functions to a metric space.** If X is any set and M is a metric space, then the set of all [bounded functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bounded_function "Bounded function")![Image 86: {\displaystyle f\colon X\to M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/01174240f796f3f567c3a311ff2fddc1e05edd9f) (i.e. those functions whose image is a [bounded subset](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bounded_subset "Bounded subset") of ![Image 87: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd)) can be turned into a metric space by defining the distance between two bounded functions f and g to be ![Image 88: {\displaystyle d(f,g)=\sup _{x\in X}d(f(x),g(x)).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d88e98e11dc5c2deab1273d66dc5596b19d21aea) This metric is called the [uniform metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uniform_metric "Uniform metric") or supremum metric.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space#cite_note-FOOTNOTE%C3%93_Searc%C3%B3id2006107-30) If M is complete, then this [function space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_space "Function space") is complete as well; moreover, if X is also a topological space, then the subspace consisting of all bounded [continuous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_function_(topology) "Continuous function (topology)") functions from X to M is also complete. When X is a subspace of ![Image 89: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d), this function space is known as a [classical Wiener space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_Wiener_space "Classical Wiener space").
*   **[String metrics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/String_metric "String metric") and [edit distances](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edit_distance "Edit distance").** There are many ways of measuring distances between [strings of characters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/String_(computer_science) "String (computer science)"), which may represent [sentences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sentence_(linguistics) "Sentence (linguistics)") in [computational linguistics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computational_linguistics "Computational linguistics") or [code words](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Code_word_(communication) "Code word (communication)") in [coding theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coding_theory "Coding theory"). _Edit distances_ attempt to measure the number of changes necessary to get from one string to another. For example, the [Hamming distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamming_distance "Hamming distance") measures the minimal number of substitutions needed, while the [Levenshtein distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levenshtein_distance "Levenshtein distance") measures the minimal number of deletions, insertions, and substitutions; both of these can be thought of as distances in an appropriate graph.
*   [Graph edit distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_edit_distance "Graph edit distance") is a measure of dissimilarity between two [graphs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_(discrete_mathematics) "Graph (discrete mathematics)"), defined as the minimal number of [graph edit operations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_operations "Graph operations") required to transform one graph into another.
*   [Wasserstein metrics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wasserstein_metric "Wasserstein metric") measure the distance between two [measures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Measure_(mathematics) "Measure (mathematics)") on the same metric space. The Wasserstein distance between two measures is, roughly speaking, the [cost of transporting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optimal_transport "Optimal transport") one to the other.
*   The set of all m by n[matrices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_(mathematics) "Matrix (mathematics)") over some [field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)") is a metric space with respect to the [rank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rank_(linear_algebra) "Rank (linear algebra)") distance ![Image 90: {\displaystyle d(A,B)=\mathrm {rank} (B-A)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cb439fd4e5e09a4306d1c8840918ba4e0cc19175).
*   The [Helly metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helly_metric "Helly metric") in [game theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Game_theory "Game theory") measures the difference between [strategies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strategy_(game_theory) "Strategy (game theory)") in a game.

### Hausdorff and Gromov–Hausdorff distance

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Metric_space&action=edit&section=31 "Edit section: Hausdorff and Gromov–Hausdorff distance")]

The idea of spaces of mathematical objects can also be applied to subsets of a metric space, as well as metric spaces themselves. [Hausdorff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hausdorff_distance "Hausdorff distance") and [Gromov–Hausdorff distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gromov%E2%80%93Hausdorff_convergence "Gromov–Hausdorff convergence") define metrics on the set of compact subsets of a metric space and the set of compact metric spaces, respectively.

Suppose (_M_, _d_) is a metric space, and let S be a subset of M. The _distance from S to a point x of M_ is, informally, the distance from x to the closest point of S. However, since there may not be a single closest point, it is defined via an [infimum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infimum "Infimum"): ![Image 91: {\displaystyle d(x,S)=\inf\{d(x,s):s\in S\}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c7c78d84b87b3cf79cd3426d990c4e625822f42e) In particular, ![Image 92: {\displaystyle d(x,S)=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7357226d0d09b20f4b272e471a5ea3399b2603ef) if and only if x belongs to the [closure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closure_(topology) "Closure (topology)") of S. Furthermore, distances between points and sets satisfy a version of the triangle inequality: ![Image 93: {\displaystyle d(x,S)\leq d(x,y)+d(y,S),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8ba425307ff730dd52b36f126ee6676d41d026ad) and therefore the map ![Image 94: {\displaystyle d_{S}:M\to \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a8df407542bad947427e70703c6028999759187) defined by ![Image 95: {\displaystyle d_{S}(x)=d(x,S)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e00ad46069facc42cde980592537671262b7dd65) is continuous. Incidentally, this shows that metric spaces are [completely regular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Completely_regular "Completely regular").

Given two subsets S and T of M, their _Hausdorff distance_ is ![Image 96: {\displaystyle d_{H}(S,T)=\max\{\sup\{d(s,T):s\in S\},\sup\{d(t,S):t\in T\}\}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2eb121a92a05c5a675982e5faf4a8fe14ba42f8d) Informally, two sets S and T are close to each other in the Hausdorff distance if no element of S is too far from T and vice versa. For example, if S is an open set in Euclidean space T is an [ε-net](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Delone_set "Delone set") inside S, then ![Image 97: {\displaystyle d_{H}(S,T)<\varepsilon }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b65dfd5e4eff2be6eb7c2beec013433876581277). In general, the Hausdorff distance ![Image 98: {\displaystyle d_{H}(S,T)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/29ec1b199a5dd18b8b7e005111a5ab00583a899e) can be infinite or zero. However, the Hausdorff distance between two distinct compact sets is always positive and finite. Thus the Hausdorff distance defines a metric on the set of compact subsets of M.

The Gromov–Hausdorff metric defines a distance between (isometry classes of) compact metric spaces. The _Gromov–Hausdorff distance_ between compact spaces X and Y is the infimum of the Hausdorff distance over all metric spaces Z that contain X and Y as subspaces. While the exact value of the Gromov–Hausdorff distance is rarely useful to know, the resulting topology has found many applications.

### Product metric spaces

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Metric_space&action=edit&section=34 "Edit section: Product metric spaces")]

If ![Image 99: {\displaystyle (M_{1},d_{1}),\ldots ,(M_{n},d_{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0dc0196b8ce0cbd450496c2ae526bfb1dbec9932) are metric spaces, and N is the [Euclidean norm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_norm "Euclidean norm") on ![Image 100: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d), then ![Image 101: {\displaystyle {\bigl (}M_{1}\times \cdots \times M_{n},d_{\times }{\bigr )}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/581f2973a4e207cf31674934f26a9d61dd4f569f) is a metric space, where the [product metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Product_metric "Product metric") is defined by ![Image 102: {\displaystyle d_{\times }{\bigl (}(x_{1},\ldots ,x_{n}),(y_{1},\ldots ,y_{n}){\bigr )}=N{\bigl (}d_{1}(x_{1},y_{1}),\ldots ,d_{n}(x_{n},y_{n}){\bigr )},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/185b237e4eae21a0037bfa59b3deddef95f0d915) and the induced topology agrees with the [product topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Product_topology "Product topology"). By the equivalence of norms in finite dimensions, a topologically equivalent metric is obtained if N is the [taxicab norm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taxicab_norm "Taxicab norm"), a [p-norm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norm_(mathematics)#p-norm "Norm (mathematics)"), the [maximum norm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maximum_norm "Maximum norm"), or any other norm which is non-decreasing as the coordinates of a positive n-tuple increase (yielding the triangle inequality).

Similarly, a metric on the topological product of countably many metric spaces can be obtained using the metric ![Image 103: {\displaystyle d(x,y)=\sum _{i=1}^{\infty }{\frac {1}{2^{i}}}{\frac {d_{i}(x_{i},y_{i})}{1+d_{i}(x_{i},y_{i})}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/56a5f182da6193316b6f30d0a233e308e75b77ae)

The topological product of uncountably many metric spaces need not be metrizable. For example, an uncountable product of copies of ![Image 104: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc) is not [first-countable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First-countable_space "First-countable space") and thus is not metrizable.

### Quotient metric spaces

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Metric_space&action=edit&section=35 "Edit section: Quotient metric spaces")]

If M is a metric space with metric d, and ![Image 105: {\displaystyle \sim }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/afcc42adfcfdc24d5c4c474869e5d8eaa78d1173) is an [equivalence relation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equivalence_relation "Equivalence relation") on M, then we can endow the quotient set ![Image 106: {\displaystyle M/\!\sim }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ff61e9b8e0301cd76db0a7491c2ef7ce60377d26) with a pseudometric. The distance between two equivalence classes ![Image 107: {\displaystyle [x]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/07548563c21e128890501e14eb7c80ee2d6fda4d) and ![Image 108: {\displaystyle [y]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f637a17dac262ee0fc8d58e31d08ca3ebe5a0fed) is defined as ![Image 109: {\displaystyle d'([x],[y])=\inf\{d(p_{1},q_{1})+d(p_{2},q_{2})+\dotsb +d(p_{n},q_{n})\},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c12948e3aa74a8510139fedcafd35cc2844a73d4) where the [infimum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infimum "Infimum") is taken over all finite sequences ![Image 110: {\displaystyle (p_{1},p_{2},\dots ,p_{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ad0bb27c095bc998293f9ef21c05a574c16b7100) and ![Image 111: {\displaystyle (q_{1},q_{2},\dots ,q_{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/18cf72f1465acdf17cda70842b2b98f6e2a586b7) with ![Image 112: {\displaystyle p_{1}\sim x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5d74c5d4ca8283557ba3b1d7a1148249f74342fc), ![Image 113: {\displaystyle q_{n}\sim y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c64a72e4d52f27cb0394f85c8640e129130ba8a2), ![Image 114: {\displaystyle q_{i}\sim p_{i+1},i=1,2,\dots ,n-1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8b0d2b1e0e9a088d4bdea5565a8d3d4b2d348c1).[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBuragoBuragoIvanov2001Definition_3.1.12-33) In general this will only define a [pseudometric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudometric_space "Pseudometric space"), i.e. ![Image 115: {\displaystyle d'([x],[y])=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4f0686af91a21fdf1ed41059fa39ffbbe883bd4f) does not necessarily imply that ![Image 116: {\displaystyle [x]=[y]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9c34866e2a1216182b168dc1e272b0b02f1aab3a). However, for some equivalence relations (e.g., those given by gluing together polyhedra along faces), ![Image 117: {\displaystyle d'}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1f310a68106a9e308bdaf887ff8f7171c4cb9d96) is a metric.

The quotient metric ![Image 118: {\displaystyle d'}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1f310a68106a9e308bdaf887ff8f7171c4cb9d96) is characterized by the following [universal property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universal_property "Universal property"). If ![Image 119: {\displaystyle f\,\colon (M,d)\to (X,\delta )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/482f907df9256fc335a774b302ca80749a5acbc3) is a metric (i.e. 1-Lipschitz) map between metric spaces satisfying _f_(_x_) = _f_(_y_) whenever ![Image 120: {\displaystyle x\sim y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bbd1014d850b7c883eb76301dd58c643e3c7e4eb), then the induced function ![Image 121: {\displaystyle {\overline {f}}\,\colon {M/\sim }\to X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4d963c71b05c8393d1d331379f1811830d39748b), given by ![Image 122: {\displaystyle {\overline {f}}([x])=f(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/aa40e344823d6033e5b82eaf6f31f3a562c6c9c6), is a metric map ![Image 123: {\displaystyle {\overline {f}}\,\colon (M/\sim ,d')\to (X,\delta ).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bea19e71e4a1f532560303795db7d528f88fd24b)

The quotient metric does not always induce the [quotient topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quotient_topology "Quotient topology"). For example, the topological quotient of the metric space ![Image 124: {\displaystyle \mathbb {N} \times [0,1]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0c5ec3356c30166cf0b83c98cb406ee5517cb93c) identifying all points of the form ![Image 125: {\displaystyle (n,0)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5fd8a7c3a302914ba5ae7cac4d8df11b59943934) is not metrizable since it is not [first-countable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First-countable_space "First-countable space"), but the quotient metric is a well-defined metric on the same set which induces a [coarser topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comparison_of_topologies "Comparison of topologies"). Moreover, different metrics on the original topological space (a disjoint union of countably many intervals) lead to different topologies on the quotient.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space#cite_note-34)

A topological space is [sequential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sequential_space "Sequential space") if and only if it is a (topological) quotient of a metric space.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space#cite_note-35)

Generalizations of metric spaces
--------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Metric_space&action=edit&section=36 "Edit section: Generalizations of metric spaces")]

There are several notions of spaces which have less structure than a metric space, but more than a topological space.

*   [Uniform spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uniform_space "Uniform space") are spaces in which distances are not defined, but uniform continuity is.
*   [Approach spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Approach_space "Approach space") are spaces in which point-to-set distances are defined, instead of point-to-point distances. They have particularly good properties from the point of view of [category theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category_theory "Category theory").
*   [Continuity spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuity_space "Continuity space") are a generalization of metric spaces and [posets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poset "Poset") that can be used to unify the notions of metric spaces and [domains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_theory "Domain theory").

There are also numerous ways of relaxing the axioms for a metric, giving rise to various notions of generalized metric spaces. These generalizations can also be combined. The terminology used to describe them is not completely standardized. Most notably, in [functional analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functional_analysis "Functional analysis") pseudometrics often come from [seminorms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seminorm "Seminorm") on vector spaces, and so it is natural to call them "semimetrics". This conflicts with the use of the term in [topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology "Topology").

Some authors define metrics so as to allow the distance function d to attain the value ∞, i.e. distances are non-negative numbers on the [extended real number line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extended_real_number_line "Extended real number line").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBuragoBuragoIvanov20011-4) Such a function is also called an _extended metric_ or "∞-metric". Every extended metric can be replaced by a real-valued metric that is topologically equivalent. This can be done using a [subadditive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subadditive_function "Subadditive function") monotonically increasing bounded function which is zero at zero, e.g. ![Image 126: {\displaystyle d'(x,y)=d(x,y)/(1+d(x,y))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d15fa446f7d2925af23c4c3b6c0fdd77b794d863) or ![Image 127: {\displaystyle d''(x,y)=\min(1,d(x,y))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2f898bd846d889bbc8f4806bdf9baa57a55fbe07).

### Metrics valued in structures other than the real numbers

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Metric_space&action=edit&section=38 "Edit section: Metrics valued in structures other than the real numbers")]

The requirement that the metric take values in ![Image 128: {\displaystyle [0,\infty )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8dc2d914c2df66bc0f7893bfb8da36766650fe47) can be relaxed to consider metrics with values in other structures, including:

*   [Ordered fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordered_field "Ordered field"), yielding the notion of a [generalised metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generalised_metric "Generalised metric").
*   More general [directed sets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Directed_set "Directed set"). In the absence of an addition operation, the triangle inequality does not make sense and is replaced with an [ultrametric inequality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultrametric_space "Ultrametric space"). This leads to the notion of a _generalized ultrametric_.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHitzlerSeda2016Definition_4.3.1-36)

These generalizations still induce a [uniform structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uniform_space "Uniform space") on the space.

A _pseudometric_ on ![Image 129: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) is a function ![Image 130: {\displaystyle d:X\times X\to \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cb82929b490b4868d886af0b7fd5c2bb00e13da0) which satisfies the axioms for a metric, except that instead of the second (identity of indiscernibles) only ![Image 131: {\displaystyle d(x,x)=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f8b0b392db0a4abf6c47dcd5b35f90592401e756) for all _![Image 132: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest\_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4)_ is required.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHitzlerSeda2016Definition_4.2.1-37) In other words, the axioms for a pseudometric are:

1.   ![Image 133: {\displaystyle d(x,y)\geq 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1309cec878e6d9effccd8a2d2ed065a8e82bfa82)
2.   ![Image 134: {\displaystyle d(x,x)=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f8b0b392db0a4abf6c47dcd5b35f90592401e756)
3.   ![Image 135: {\displaystyle d(x,y)=d(y,x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c7fea33d0e60116abd16287351eb6bf142a61fdd)
4.   ![Image 136: {\displaystyle d(x,z)\leq d(x,y)+d(y,z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/43ae751284c2944886e1effbfe4e0c1293f98419).

In some contexts, pseudometrics are referred to as _semimetrics_[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBuragoBuragoIvanov2001Definition_1.1.4-38) because of their relation to [seminorms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seminorm "Seminorm").

Occasionally, a **quasimetric** is defined as a function that satisfies all axioms for a metric with the possible exception of symmetry.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space#cite_note-39) The name of this generalisation is not entirely standardized.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space#cite_note-40)

1.   ![Image 137: {\displaystyle d(x,y)\geq 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1309cec878e6d9effccd8a2d2ed065a8e82bfa82)
2.   ![Image 138: {\displaystyle d(x,y)=0\iff x=y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/32fd7ce57db3ea42e1bbb4aabb703cb709c6bfec)
3.   ![Image 139: {\displaystyle d(x,z)\leq d(x,y)+d(y,z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/43ae751284c2944886e1effbfe4e0c1293f98419)

Quasimetrics are common in real life. For example, given a set X of mountain villages, the typical walking times between elements of X form a quasimetric because travel uphill takes longer than travel downhill. Another example is the [length of car rides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taxicab_geometry "Taxicab geometry") in a city with one-way streets: here, a shortest path from point A to point B goes along a different set of streets than a shortest path from B to A and may have a different length.

A quasimetric on the reals can be defined by setting ![Image 140: {\displaystyle d(x,y)={\begin{cases}x-y&{\text{if }}x\geq y,\\1&{\text{otherwise.}}\end{cases}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ae2c899c4739466aaa1c2e9aef2f0cd38f581fd5) The 1 may be replaced, for example, by infinity or by ![Image 141: {\displaystyle 1+{\sqrt {y-x}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/46c0a8f19b8e4a2bbcec11b99f3873001e41c1e7) or any other [subadditive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subadditivity "Subadditivity") function of _y_-_x_. This quasimetric describes the cost of modifying a metal stick: it is easy to reduce its size by [filing it down](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Filing_(metalworking) "Filing (metalworking)"), but it is difficult or impossible to grow it.

Given a quasimetric on X, one can define an R-ball around x to be the set ![Image 142: {\displaystyle \{y\in X|d(x,y)\leq R\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1871b0f2881bfac168b70b5e0674bf62d5f1382e). As in the case of a metric, such balls form a basis for a topology on X, but this topology need not be metrizable. For example, the topology induced by the quasimetric on the reals described above is the (reversed) [Sorgenfrey line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sorgenfrey_line "Sorgenfrey line").

### Metametrics or partial metrics

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Metric_space&action=edit&section=41 "Edit section: Metametrics or partial metrics")]

In a _metametric_, all the axioms of a metric are satisfied except that the distance between identical points is not necessarily zero. In other words, the axioms for a metametric are:

1.   ![Image 143: {\displaystyle d(x,y)\geq 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1309cec878e6d9effccd8a2d2ed065a8e82bfa82)
2.   ![Image 144: {\displaystyle d(x,y)=0\implies x=y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e9cb5aefcc6aef0b554176319ffde03fcc806576)
3.   ![Image 145: {\displaystyle d(x,y)=d(y,x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c7fea33d0e60116abd16287351eb6bf142a61fdd)
4.   ![Image 146: {\displaystyle d(x,z)\leq d(x,y)+d(y,z).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1e7072502a79725a25151d5c96b7310e7dbb97a3)

Metametrics appear in the study of [Gromov hyperbolic metric spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%CE%94-hyperbolic_space "Δ-hyperbolic space") and their boundaries. The _visual metametric_ on such a space satisfies ![Image 147: {\displaystyle d(x,x)=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f8b0b392db0a4abf6c47dcd5b35f90592401e756) for points ![Image 148: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) on the boundary, but otherwise ![Image 149: {\displaystyle d(x,x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b16cf40c69ad9e073e7a5acd8fc8cc19d85e4e9f) is approximately the distance from _![Image 150: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest\_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4)_ to the boundary. Metametrics were first defined by Jussi Väisälä.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space#cite_note-FOOTNOTEV%C3%A4is%C3%A4l%C3%A42005-41) In other work, a function satisfying these axioms is called a _partial metric_[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space#cite_note-42)[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space#cite_note-43) or a _dislocated metric_.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHitzlerSeda2016Definition_4.2.1-37)

A **semimetric** on ![Image 151: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) is a function ![Image 152: {\displaystyle d:X\times X\to \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cb82929b490b4868d886af0b7fd5c2bb00e13da0) that satisfies the first three axioms, but not necessarily the triangle inequality:

1.   ![Image 153: {\displaystyle d(x,y)\geq 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1309cec878e6d9effccd8a2d2ed065a8e82bfa82)
2.   ![Image 154: {\displaystyle d(x,y)=0\iff x=y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/32fd7ce57db3ea42e1bbb4aabb703cb709c6bfec)
3.   ![Image 155: {\displaystyle d(x,y)=d(y,x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c7fea33d0e60116abd16287351eb6bf142a61fdd)

Semimetrics satisfying these equivalent conditions have sometimes been referred to as _quasimetrics_,[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space#cite_note-FOOTNOTEXia2009-44)_nearmetrics_[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space#cite_note-FOOTNOTEXia2008-45) or **inframetrics**.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFraigniaudLebharViennot2008-46)

The ρ-inframetric inequalities were introduced to model [round-trip delay times](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Round-trip_delay_time "Round-trip delay time") in the [internet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internet "Internet").[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFraigniaudLebharViennot2008-46) The triangle inequality implies the 2-inframetric inequality, and the [ultrametric inequality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultrametric_inequality "Ultrametric inequality") is exactly the 1-inframetric inequality.

Relaxing the last three axioms leads to the notion of a **premetric**, i.e. a function satisfying the following conditions:

1.   ![Image 156: {\displaystyle d(x,y)\geq 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1309cec878e6d9effccd8a2d2ed065a8e82bfa82)
2.   ![Image 157: {\displaystyle d(x,x)=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f8b0b392db0a4abf6c47dcd5b35f90592401e756)

This is not a standard term. Sometimes it is used to refer to other generalizations of metrics such as pseudosemimetrics[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBuldyginKozachenko2000-47) or pseudometrics;[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHelemskii2006-48) in translations of Russian books it sometimes appears as "prametric".[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space#cite_note-49) A premetric that satisfies symmetry, i.e. a pseudosemimetric, is also called a distance.[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDezaLaurent1997-50)

A very basic example of a pseudoquasimetric space is the set ![Image 172: {\displaystyle \{0,1\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/28de5781698336d21c9c560fb1cbb3fb406923eb) with the premetric given by ![Image 173: {\displaystyle d(0,1)=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a0431794d4df3e6afdf278f8c68ba11013fb83e) and ![Image 174: {\displaystyle d(1,0)=0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cc226aba7f6599af4c67aee18615edbc6a0d4c5d) The associated topological space is the [Sierpiński space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sierpi%C5%84ski_space "Sierpiński space").

### Metrics on multisets

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Metric_space&action=edit&section=45 "Edit section: Metrics on multisets")]

The notion of a metric can be generalized from a distance between two elements to a number assigned to a multiset of elements. A [multiset](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiset "Multiset") is a generalization of the notion of a [set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_(mathematics) "Set (mathematics)") in which an element can occur more than once. Define the multiset union ![Image 182: {\displaystyle U=XY}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/49a53edef3399ec8e4fff563c04f6a1a96f84563) as follows: if an element x occurs m times in X and n times in Y then it occurs _m_ + _n_ times in U. A function d on the set of nonempty finite multisets of elements of a set M is a metric[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVit%C3%A1nyi2011-52) if

1.   ![Image 183: {\displaystyle d(X)=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1cb585e11d376b2037d9fd710ba5419653a5a8db) if all elements of X are equal and ![Image 184: {\displaystyle d(X)>0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c8521704ffbbd4390e7bb5eb7bc4452569a345f5) otherwise ([positive definiteness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positive_definiteness "Positive definiteness"))
2.   ![Image 185: {\displaystyle d(X)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5220998dbbd96cbcc2e4fa85026a1925313ff55e) depends only on the (unordered) multiset X ([symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry "Symmetry"))
3.   ![Image 186: {\displaystyle d(XY)\leq d(XZ)+d(ZY)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7b62bb85435fe2c989eca6f9ed62683c0040f054) ([triangle inequality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle_inequality "Triangle inequality"))

By considering the cases of axioms 1 and 2 in which the multiset X has two elements and the case of axiom 3 in which the multisets X, Y, and Z have one element each, one recovers the usual axioms for a metric. That is, every multiset metric yields an ordinary metric when restricted to sets of two elements.

A simple example is the set of all nonempty finite multisets ![Image 187: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) of integers with ![Image 188: {\displaystyle d(X)=\max(X)-\min(X)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/232bf79cf04a896880e6ffad329137068c930a60). More complex examples are [information distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information_distance "Information distance") in multisets;[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVit%C3%A1nyi2011-52) and [normalized compression distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normalized_compression_distance "Normalized compression distance") (NCD) in multisets.[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space#cite_note-FOOTNOTECohenVit%C3%A1nyi2012-53)

2.   **[^](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space#cite_ref-13 "Jump up")**In the context of [intervals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interval_(mathematics) "Interval (mathematics)") in the real line, or more generally regions in Euclidean space, bounded sets are sometimes referred to as "finite intervals" or "finite regions". However, they do not typically have a finite number of elements, and while they all have finite [volume](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebesgue_measure "Lebesgue measure"), so do many unbounded sets. Therefore this terminology is imprecise.
3.   **[^](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space#cite_ref-22 "Jump up")**This differs from usage in [Riemannian geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_geometry "Riemannian geometry"), where geodesics are only locally shortest paths. Some authors define geodesics in metric spaces in the same way.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBuragoBuragoIvanov2001Definition_2.5.27-20)[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGromov2007Definition_1.9-21)
