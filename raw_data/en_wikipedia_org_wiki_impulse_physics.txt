In [classical mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_mechanics "Classical mechanics"), **impulse** (symbolized by **J** or **Imp**) is the change in [momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum "Momentum") of an object. If the initial momentum of an object is **p**1, and a subsequent momentum is **p**2, the object has received an impulse **J**: J=p 2−p 1.{\displaystyle \mathbf {J} =\mathbf {p} _{2}-\mathbf {p} _{1}.}![Image 20: {\displaystyle \mathbf {J} =\mathbf {p} _{2}-\mathbf {p} _{1}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/56f59e30ae33348f2b71d2d79644cda761805ac9)

[Momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum "Momentum") is a [vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_(physics) "Vector (physics)") quantity, so impulse is also a vector quantity:[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Impulse_(physics)#cite_note-1)∑F×Δ t=Δ p.{\displaystyle \sum \mathbf {F} \times \Delta t=\Delta \mathbf {p} .}![Image 21: {\displaystyle \sum \mathbf {F} \times \Delta t=\Delta \mathbf {p} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6063dc4c61d480c35b66c653761ea1845f554480)[Newton's second law of motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_second_law_of_motion "Newton's second law of motion") states that the rate of change of momentum of an object is equal to the resultant force F acting on the object: F=p 2−p 1 Δ t,{\displaystyle \mathbf {F} ={\frac {\mathbf {p} _{2}-\mathbf {p} _{1}}{\Delta t}},}![Image 22: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} ={\frac {\mathbf {p} _{2}-\mathbf {p} _{1}}{\Delta t}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/85a5d219b580bfc240a8e26c43e12bb49a15a44b) so the impulse J delivered by a steady [force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Force "Force")F acting for time Δ _t_ is: J=F Δ t.{\displaystyle \mathbf {J} =\mathbf {F} \Delta t.}![Image 23: {\displaystyle \mathbf {J} =\mathbf {F} \Delta t.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c1bf6539ff349b0652cca04b476600b6d6f2e30a)

The impulse delivered by a varying force acting from time a to b is the [integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral "Integral") of the force F with respect to time: J=∫a b F d t.{\displaystyle \mathbf {J} =\int _{a}^{b}\mathbf {F} \,\mathrm {d} t.}![Image 24: {\displaystyle \mathbf {J} =\int _{a}^{b}\mathbf {F} \,\mathrm {d} t.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2936f175f0d923aa9c4b5f1eae54253c75136d11)

The [SI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Units "International System of Units") unit of impulse is the [newton-second](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton-second "Newton-second") (N⋅s), and the [dimensionally equivalent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimensional_analysis "Dimensional analysis") unit of momentum is the kilogram-metre per second (kg⋅m/s). The corresponding [English engineering unit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English_engineering_unit "English engineering unit") is the [pound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pound_(force) "Pound (force)")-second (lbf⋅s), and in the [British Gravitational System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Gravitational_System "British Gravitational System"), the unit is the [slug](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slug_(unit) "Slug (unit)")-foot per second (slug⋅ft/s).

Mathematical derivation in the case of an object of constant mass
-----------------------------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Impulse_(physics)&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Mathematical derivation in the case of an object of constant mass")]

The impulse delivered by the "sad" ball is _mv_ 0, where _v_ 0 is the speed upon impact. To the extent that it bounces back with speed _v_ 0, the "happy" ball delivers an impulse of _m_ Δ _v_ = 2 _mv_ 0.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Impulse_(physics)#cite_note-2)

Impulse **J** produced from time _t_ 1 to _t_ 2 is defined to be[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Impulse_(physics)#cite_note-FOOTNOTESerwayJewett2004chpt._9.2_Impulse_and_Momentum-3)[J=∫t 1 t 2 F d t,{\displaystyle \mathbf {J} =\int _{t_{1}}^{t_{2}}\mathbf {F} \,\mathrm {d} t,}![Image 25: {\displaystyle \mathbf {J} =\int _{t_{1}}^{t_{2}}\mathbf {F} \,\mathrm {d} t,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4ec8a4a9b6a1d65a61746949bd519dc252b07c37)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:MathWikibase&qid=Q837940) where **F** is the resultant force applied from _t_ 1 to _t_ 2.

From [Newton's second law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_laws_of_motion#Newton's_second_law "Newton's laws of motion"), force is related to [momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum "Momentum")**p** by F=d p d t.{\displaystyle \mathbf {F} ={\frac {\mathrm {d} \mathbf {p} }{\mathrm {d} t}}.}![Image 26: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} ={\frac {\mathrm {d} \mathbf {p} }{\mathrm {d} t}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/28778c96c495f62b13cb09cfc02284b0cd316266)

Therefore, [J=∫t 1 t 2 d p d t d t=∫p 1 p 2 d p=p 2−p 1=Δ p,{\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\mathbf {J} &=\int _{t_{1}}^{t_{2}}{\frac {\mathrm {d} \mathbf {p} }{\mathrm {d} t}}\,\mathrm {d} t\\&=\int _{\mathbf {p} _{1}}^{\mathbf {p} _{2}}\mathrm {d} \mathbf {p} \\&=\mathbf {p} _{2}-\mathbf {p} _{1}=\Delta \mathbf {p} ,\end{aligned}}}![Image 27: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\mathbf {J} &=\int _{t_{1}}^{t_{2}}{\frac {\mathrm {d} \mathbf {p} }{\mathrm {d} t}}\,\mathrm {d} t\\&=\int _{\mathbf {p} _{1}}^{\mathbf {p} _{2}}\mathrm {d} \mathbf {p} \\&=\mathbf {p} _{2}-\mathbf {p} _{1}=\Delta \mathbf {p} ,\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eddf0a6e22c43b3d5b1eb1158aa6e9967dc7c0cd)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:MathWikibase&qid=Q837940) where Δ**p** is the change in linear momentum from time _t_ 1 to _t_ 2. This is often called the impulse–momentum theorem (analogous to the [work–energy theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Work%E2%80%93energy_theorem "Work–energy theorem")).

As a result, an impulse may also be regarded as the change in momentum of an object to which a resultant force is applied. The impulse may be expressed in a simpler form when the mass is constant: [J=∫t 1 t 2 F d t=Δ p=m v 2−m v 1,{\displaystyle \mathbf {J} =\int _{t_{1}}^{t_{2}}\mathbf {F} \,\mathrm {d} t=\Delta \mathbf {p} =m\mathbf {v_{2}} -m\mathbf {v_{1}} ,}![Image 28: {\displaystyle \mathbf {J} =\int _{t_{1}}^{t_{2}}\mathbf {F} \,\mathrm {d} t=\Delta \mathbf {p} =m\mathbf {v_{2}} -m\mathbf {v_{1}} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/82c0371480d4409e0cb609ddd02ee543bbe476b6)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:MathWikibase&qid=Q837940) where

*   **F** is the resultant force applied,
*   _t_ 1 and _t_ 2 are times when the impulse begins and ends, respectively,
*   m is the mass of the object,
*   **v**2 is the final velocity of the object at the end of the time interval, and
*   **v**1 is the initial velocity of the object when the time interval begins.

Impulse has the same units and dimensions (MLT−1) as momentum. In the [International System of Units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Units "International System of Units"), these are [kg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kilogram "Kilogram")⋅[m/s](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meter_per_second "Meter per second") =[N](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton_(units) "Newton (units)")⋅[s](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second "Second"). In [English engineering units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English_engineering_units "English engineering units"), they are [slug](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slug_(unit) "Slug (unit)")⋅[ft/s](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foot_per_second "Foot per second") =[lbf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pound_(force) "Pound (force)")⋅[s](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second "Second").

The term "impulse" is also used to refer to a short-acting force or [impact](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Impact_(mechanics) "Impact (mechanics)"). This type of impulse is often _idealized_ so that the change in momentum produced by the force is modelled as happening instantaneously. This sort of change is a [step change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Step_function "Step function"), and is not physically possible. However, this is a useful model for computing the effects of ideal collisions (such as in videogame [physics engines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics_engine "Physics engine")). Additionally, in rocketry, the term "total impulse" is commonly used and is considered synonymous with the term "impulse".

Variable mass
-------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Impulse_(physics)&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Variable mass")]

Further information: [Specific impulse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Specific_impulse "Specific impulse")

The application of Newton's second law for variable mass allows impulse and momentum to be used as analysis tools for [jet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jet_propulsion "Jet propulsion")- or [rocket](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rocket "Rocket")-propelled vehicles. In the case of rockets, the impulse imparted can be normalized by unit of [propellant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rocket_propellant "Rocket propellant") expended, to create a performance parameter, [specific impulse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Specific_impulse "Specific impulse"). This fact can be used to derive the [Tsiolkovsky rocket equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tsiolkovsky_rocket_equation "Tsiolkovsky rocket equation"), which relates the vehicle's propulsive change in velocity to the engine's specific impulse (or nozzle exhaust velocity) and the vehicle's propellant-[mass ratio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass_ratio "Mass ratio").
