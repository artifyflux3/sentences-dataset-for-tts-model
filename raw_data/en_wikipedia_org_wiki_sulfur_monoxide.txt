**Sulfur monoxide** is an [inorganic compound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inorganic_compound "Inorganic compound") with [formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_formula "Chemical formula")[S](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur "Sulfur")[O](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen "Oxygen"). It is only found as a dilute gas phase. When concentrated or condensed, it converts to S 2 O 2 ([disulfur dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disulfur_dioxide "Disulfur dioxide")). It has been detected in space but is rarely encountered intact otherwise.

Structure and bonding
---------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_monoxide&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Structure and bonding")]

The SO molecule has a triplet ground state similar to [O 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dioxygen "Dioxygen") and [S 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disulfur "Disulfur"), that is, each molecule has two unpaired electrons.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_monoxide#cite_note-greenwood-2) The S−O [bond length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bond_length "Bond length") of 148.1 pm is similar to that found in [lower sulfur oxides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lower_sulfur_oxides "Lower sulfur oxides") (e.g. S 8 O, S−O = 148 pm) but is longer than the S−O bond in gaseous [S 2 O](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disulfur_monoxide "Disulfur monoxide") (146 pm), [SO 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide "Sulfur dioxide") (143.1 pm) and [SO 3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_trioxide "Sulfur trioxide") (142 pm).[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_monoxide#cite_note-greenwood-2)

The molecule is excited with [near infrared](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Near_infrared "Near infrared") radiation to the singlet state (with no unpaired electrons). The singlet state is believed to be more reactive than the ground triplet state, in the same way that [singlet oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Singlet_oxygen "Singlet oxygen") is more reactive than [triplet oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triplet_oxygen "Triplet oxygen").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_monoxide#cite_note-3)

Production and reactions
------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_monoxide&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Production and reactions")]

The SO molecule is thermodynamically unstable, converting initially to [S 2 O 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disulfur_dioxide "Disulfur dioxide").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_monoxide#cite_note-greenwood-2) Consequently controlled syntheses typically do not detect the presence of SO proper, but instead the reaction of a [chemical trap](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_trap "Chemical trap") or the terminal decomposition products of S 2 O 2 ([sulfur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur "Sulfur") and [sulfur dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide "Sulfur dioxide")).

Production of SO as a reagent in organic syntheses has centred on using compounds that "extrude" SO. Examples include the decomposition of the relatively simple molecule [ethylene episulfoxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene_episulfoxide "Ethylene episulfoxide"):[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_monoxide#cite_note-4)

C 2 H 4 SO → C 2 H 4 + SO
Yields directly from an episulfoxide are poor, and improve only moderately when the carbons are sterically shielded.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_monoxide#cite_note-5) A much better approach decomposes a diaryl cyclic trisulfide oxide, C 10 H 6 S 3 O, produced from [thionyl chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thionyl_chloride "Thionyl chloride") and the di[thiol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thiol "Thiol").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_monoxide#cite_note-6)

SO inserts into [alkenes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkenes "Alkenes"), [alkynes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkynes "Alkynes") and [dienes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diene "Diene") producing [thiiranes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thiiranes "Thiiranes"), molecules with three-membered rings containing sulfur.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_monoxide#cite_note-7)

Sulfur monoxide may form transiently during the metallic reduction of [thionyl bromide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thionyl_bromide "Thionyl bromide").[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_monoxide#cite_note-Sulf-8)

### Generation under extreme conditions

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_monoxide&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Generation under extreme conditions")]

In the laboratory, sulfur monoxide can be produced by treating [sulfur dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide "Sulfur dioxide") with sulfur vapor in a [glow discharge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glow_discharge "Glow discharge").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_monoxide#cite_note-greenwood-2) It has been detected in single-bubble [sonoluminescence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sonoluminescence "Sonoluminescence") of concentrated [sulfuric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfuric_acid "Sulfuric acid") containing some dissolved [noble gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noble_gas "Noble gas").[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_monoxide#cite_note-9)

Benner and Stedman developed a [chemiluminescence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemiluminescence "Chemiluminescence") detector for sulfur via the reaction between sulfur monoxide and [ozone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ozone "Ozone"):[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_monoxide#cite_note-10)

SO + O 3 → SO 2* + O 2 SO 2* → SO 2 + [h _ν_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon "Photon")
(* indicates an [excited state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Excited_state "Excited state"))

### Ligand for transition metals

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_monoxide&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Ligand for transition metals")]

As a [ligand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ligand "Ligand") SO can bond in a number different ways:[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_monoxide#cite_note-11)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_monoxide#cite_note-12)

*   a terminal ligand, with a bent M−O−S arrangement, for example with [titanium oxyfluoride](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Titanium_oxyfluoride&action=edit&redlink=1 "Titanium oxyfluoride (page does not exist)")[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_monoxide#cite_note-13)
*   a terminal ligand, with a bent M−S−O arrangement, analogous to bent [nitrosyl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrosyl "Nitrosyl")
*   [bridging](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bridging_ligand "Bridging ligand") across two or three metal centres (via sulfur), as in Fe 3(_μ_ 3-S)(_μ_ 3-SO)(CO)9
*   [_η_ 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hapticity "Hapticity") sideways-on (d–π interaction) with [vanadium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vanadium "Vanadium"), [niobium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Niobium "Niobium"), and [tantalum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tantalum "Tantalum").[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_monoxide#cite_note-14)

Sulfur monoxide has been detected around [Io](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Io_(moon) "Io (moon)"), one of [Jupiter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jupiter "Jupiter")'s moons, both in the atmosphere[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_monoxide#cite_note-15) and in the [plasma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasma_(physics) "Plasma (physics)")[torus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torus "Torus").[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_monoxide#cite_note-16) It has also been found in the atmosphere of [Venus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Venus "Venus"),[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_monoxide#cite_note-17) in [Comet Hale–Bopp](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comet_Hale%E2%80%93Bopp "Comet Hale–Bopp"),[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_monoxide#cite_note-18) in [67P/Churyumov–Gerasimenko](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/67P/Churyumov%E2%80%93Gerasimenko "67P/Churyumov–Gerasimenko"),[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_monoxide#cite_note-19) and in the [interstellar medium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_medium "Interstellar medium").[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_monoxide#cite_note-20)

On [Io](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Io_(moon) "Io (moon)"), SO is thought to be produced both by [volcanic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volcanism_on_Io "Volcanism on Io") and [photochemical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photochemistry "Photochemistry") routes. The principal photochemical reactions are proposed as follows:[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_monoxide#cite_note-21)

O + S 2 → S + SO SO 2 → SO + O
Sulfur monoxide has been found in [NML Cygni](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NML_Cygni "NML Cygni").[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_monoxide#cite_note-Marvel1996-22)

### Biological chemistry

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_monoxide&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Biological chemistry")]

Sulfur monoxide may have some biological activity. The formation of transient SO in the [coronary artery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coronary_artery "Coronary artery") of [pigs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pig "Pig") has been inferred from the reaction products, [carbonyl sulfide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonyl_sulfide "Carbonyl sulfide") and [sulfur dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide "Sulfur dioxide").[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_monoxide#cite_note-23)

Because of sulfur monoxide's rare occurrence in our atmosphere and poor stability, it is difficult to fully determine its hazards. But when condensed and compacted, it forms [disulfur dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disulfur_dioxide "Disulfur dioxide"), which is relatively toxic and corrosive. This compound is also highly flammable (similar flammability to [methane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane "Methane")) and when burned produces [sulfur dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide "Sulfur dioxide"), a poisonous gas.

Sulfur monoxide dication
------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_monoxide&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Sulfur monoxide dication")]

[Sulfur dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide "Sulfur dioxide") SO 2 in presence of [hexamethylbenzene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hexamethylbenzene "Hexamethylbenzene") C 6(CH 3)6 can be protonated under [superacidic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superacid "Superacid") conditions ([HF·AsF 5](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Fluoroarsenic_acid&action=edit&redlink=1 "Fluoroarsenic acid (page does not exist)")) to give the non-rigid [π-complex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi_complex "Pi complex") C 6(CH 3)6 SO 2+. The SO 2+ moiety can essentially move barrierless over the [benzene ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene_ring "Benzene ring"). The S−O bond length is 142.4(2)pm.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_monoxide#cite_note-24)

C 6(CH 3)6 + SO 2 + 3 HF·AsF 5 → [C 6(CH 3)6 SO][AsF 6]2 + [H 3 O][AsF 6][![Image 14](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Disulfur-dioxide-2D-dimensions.png/250px-Disulfur-dioxide-2D-dimensions.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Disulfur-dioxide-2D-dimensions.png)

The structure of disulfur dioxide, S 2 O 2

[![Image 15](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Disulfur-dioxide-3D-vdW-A.png/250px-Disulfur-dioxide-3D-vdW-A.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Disulfur-dioxide-3D-vdW-A.png)

A [space-filling model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space-filling_model "Space-filling model") of the disulfur dioxide molecule

SO converts to disulfur dioxide (S 2 O 2).[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_monoxide#cite_note-lovas-25) Disulfur dioxide is a planar molecule with _C_ 2v[symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry_group "Symmetry group"). The S−O bond length is 145.8 pm, shorter than in the monomer, and the S−S bond length is 202.45 pm. The O−S−S angle is 112.7°. S 2 O 2 has a dipole moment of 3.17[D](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Debye_(unit) "Debye (unit)").[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_monoxide#cite_note-lovas-25)
