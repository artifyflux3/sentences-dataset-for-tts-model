In mathematics, the **additive inverse** of an [element](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Element_(mathematics) "Element (mathematics)")x, denoted −x,[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Additive_inverse#cite_note-1) is the element that when [added](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Addition "Addition") to x, yields the [additive identity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Additive_identity "Additive identity").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Additive_inverse#cite_note-2) This additive identity is often the number [0 (zero)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/0 "0"), but it can also refer to a more generalized [zero element](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero_element "Zero element").

In [elementary mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementary_mathematics "Elementary mathematics"), the additive inverse is often referred to as the **opposite** number,[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Additive_inverse#cite_note-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Additive_inverse#cite_note-4) or its **negative**.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Additive_inverse#cite_note-5) The [unary operation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unary_operation "Unary operation") of **arithmetic negation**[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Additive_inverse#cite_note-6) is closely related to _[subtraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subtraction "Subtraction")_[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Additive_inverse#cite_note-7) and is important in [solving algebraic equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementary_algebra "Elementary algebra").[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Additive_inverse#cite_note-:0-8) Not all [sets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_(mathematics) "Set (mathematics)") where addition is defined have an additive inverse, such as the [natural numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number "Natural number").[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Additive_inverse#cite_note-:1-9)

When working with [integers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer"), [rational numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_number "Rational number"), [real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number"), and [complex numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number"), the additive inverse of any number can be found by multiplying it by [−1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E2%88%921 "−1").[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Additive_inverse#cite_note-:0-8)

Relation to subtraction
-----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Additive_inverse&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Relation to subtraction")]

The additive inverse is closely related to [subtraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subtraction "Subtraction"), which can be viewed as an addition using the inverse:

_a_ − _b_ = _a_ + (−_b_).
Conversely, the additive inverse can be thought of as subtraction from zero:

−_a_ = 0 − _a_.
This connection lead to the minus sign being used for both opposite magnitudes and subtraction as far back as the 17th century. While this notation is standard today, it was met with opposition at the time, as some mathematicians felt it could be unclear and lead to errors.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Additive_inverse#cite_note-10)

Given an algebraic structure defined under addition ![Image 24: {\displaystyle (S,+)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/757e19e5bf3fd2aa1fcc68e2ff18d9805acc2f9c) with an additive identity ![Image 25: {\displaystyle e\in S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f71122665efb66eb1109282c5a2c4e8e66c8e921), an element ![Image 26: {\displaystyle x\in S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/51186ba8afb2067573a9082d55dd383df1ea9214) has an additive inverse ![Image 27: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d) if and only if ![Image 28: {\displaystyle y\in S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9d26e188ccec145558c09a23bb1d25149ea67973), ![Image 29: {\displaystyle x+y=e}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/075a4fd6a9e2340d278fd43e185c715d1d25dece), and ![Image 30: {\displaystyle y+x=e}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9133436a38113f80843b61177ad5ce5387baa28e).[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Additive_inverse#cite_note-:1-9)

Addition is typically only used to refer to a [commutative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_property "Commutative property") operation, but for some systems of numbers, such as [floating point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Floating_point "Floating point"), it might not be [associative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Associative_property "Associative property").[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Additive_inverse#cite_note-11) When it is associative, so ![Image 31: {\displaystyle (a+b)+c=a+(b+c)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/46b7b8d31d5845966e6abdbb030c73f343c17d4e), the left and right inverses, if they exist, will agree, and the additive inverse will be unique. In non-associative cases, the left and right inverses may disagree, and in these cases, the inverse is not considered to exist.

The definition requires [closure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closure_(mathematics) "Closure (mathematics)"), that the additive element ![Image 32: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d) be found in ![Image 33: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2). However, despite being able to add the natural numbers together, the set of natural numbers does not include the additive inverse values. This is because the additive inverse of a natural number (e.g., ![Image 34: {\displaystyle -3}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8e8ffb51bb3837cabdd202845d15aedc67a4ac88) for ![Image 35: {\displaystyle 3}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/991e33c6e207b12546f15bdfee8b5726eafbbb2f)) is not a natural number; it is an [integer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer"). Therefore, the natural numbers in set ![Image 36: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2) do have additive inverses and their associated inverses are [negative numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Negative_number "Negative number").
