**Sulfanyl** (**HS•**), also known as the **mercapto radical**, **hydrosulfide radical**, or **hydridosulfur**, is a simple [radical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radical_(chemistry) "Radical (chemistry)") molecule consisting of one hydrogen and one sulfur atom. The S-H distance in the radical is 0.134 nm.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfanyl#cite_note-Ellingson-4) The radical is also proposed to be formed by the action of [ultraviolet radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultraviolet_radiation "Ultraviolet radiation") on hydrogen sulfide. A [wavelength](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wavelength "Wavelength") of 190 nm gives maximum absorption.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfanyl#cite_note-5)

Sulfanyl is one of the top three sulfur-containing gasses in [gas giants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas_giant "Gas giant") such as [Jupiter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jupiter "Jupiter") and is very likely to be found in [brown dwarfs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brown_dwarf "Brown dwarf") and cool stars. It was originally discovered by Margaret N. Lewis and John U. White at the [University of California](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_California "University of California") in 1939.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfanyl#cite_note-Lewis-6) They observed molecular absorption bands around 325 nm belonging to the system designated by 2 Σ+ ← 2 Π i. They generated the radical by means of a radio frequency discharge in [hydrogen sulfide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide "Hydrogen sulfide").[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfanyl#cite_note-7) HS• is formed during the degradation of hydrogen sulfide in the atmosphere of the Earth. This may be a deliberate action to destroy odours or a natural phenomenon.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfanyl#cite_note-8)

Absorption lines of sulfanyl in space were first detected in the [infrared](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infrared "Infrared") by Yamamura (2000) in a star [R And](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/R_And "R And"). In the sun •SH was detected at several [ultraviolet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultraviolet "Ultraviolet")[wavelengths](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wavelength "Wavelength"): 326.0459, 327.5468, 328.9749, 330.0892 and 330.1112 nm.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfanyl#cite_note-9)

Sulfanyl has been detected in [interstellar gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_gas "Interstellar gas"),[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfanyl#cite_note-10) and it is possibly present in comets.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfanyl#cite_note-11)

Various theoretical studies have examined HS• in atmospheres. In Earth's atmosphere HS• reacts with NO 2 to make two products HSNO 2 and HSONO. HSONO decomposes to HSO and NO. HS• also reacts with O 2 and N 2 O.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfanyl#cite_note-resende-12) HS• can also react with Cl 2 producing HSCl and a Cl• atom.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfanyl#cite_note-13) HS• destroys [ozone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ozone "Ozone") producing HSO• and oxygen.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfanyl#cite_note-yosh-14) HS• is formed in the Earth's atmosphere by the reaction of HO•, the [hydroxyl radical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroxyl_radical "Hydroxyl radical"), on [carbon disulfide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_disulfide "Carbon disulfide"), [carbon oxysulfide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_oxysulfide "Carbon oxysulfide") and [hydrogen sulfide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide "Hydrogen sulfide") with side products of [carbon dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_dioxide "Carbon dioxide") and water. Photodissociation of hydrogen sulfide also produces the radical in air.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfanyl#cite_note-Furones-15)

In a planetary atmosphere that contains H 2 S, HS• will be formed if the temperature and pressure are high enough. The ratio of H 2 S and HS• is given by:

log(_X_ H 2 S/_X_ HS) = −3.37 + 8785/_T_ + 0.5 log _P T_ + 0.5 log _X_ H 2
For a hydrogen dominated atmosphere in a gas giant or star: H 2 S has the same level as HS• at

![Image 11: {\displaystyle \log P_{T}=6.82-17570/T}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ca1ed26830fff6c1812de0628049e91e8350e755).
At higher temperatures HS• breaks up into sulfur vapour and H 2. The line of equal S and HS concentration follows the line

![Image 12: {\displaystyle \log P_{T}=4.80-14522/T}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b405655593e3597f7b2eb005738396ddff9a48fd).
The lines of equal concentration cross at 1509 K and 1.51 Pa, with HS• being left out of the mix at lower temperatures and pressures. •SH is expected to be the second or third most common sulfur containing gas in [gas giants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas_giant "Gas giant") or [brown dwarfs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brown_dwarf "Brown dwarf").[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfanyl#cite_note-16)

The [organic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_compound "Organic compound") analogue of sulfanyl is [thiyl radical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thiyl_radical "Thiyl radical") with the formula RS•, where R is some organic group (e.g., [alkyl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkyl "Alkyl") or [aryl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aryl "Aryl")). Thermal decomposition of mercaptans, such as [ethyl mercaptan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethyl_mercaptan "Ethyl mercaptan") has been proposed to involve HS•.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfanyl#cite_note-17)

In humans [superoxide dismutase [Cu-Zn]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SOD1 "SOD1") is proposed to convert the hydrosulfide ion (HS−) to HS•. In the proposed mechanism Cu 2+ is convert to Cu+.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfanyl#cite_note-lyons-18)

[Sulfide dehydrogenase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfide_dehydrogenase "Sulfide dehydrogenase") as found in [sulfur bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur-reducing_bacteria "Sulfur-reducing bacteria") catalyses the oxidation of HS−. It has been proposed that this reaction proceeds via HS•, by removing a single electron.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfanyl#cite_note-19)

When some sulfur minerals are leached with ferric ions, HS• is proposed to proceed as follows:

MS + Fe 3+ + 2H+ → M 2+ + Fe 2+ + H 2 S•+
with the H 2 S•+ radical then passing a proton to water to make the HS• radical. M is a metal such as zinc or copper.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfanyl#cite_note-schippers-20) This has potential for [bioleaching](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bioleaching "Bioleaching") in metallic ore extraction.

The [hydrosulfide ion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrosulfide_ion "Hydrosulfide ion") HS− can be oxidized with [cerium (IV) sulfate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cerium_(IV)_sulfate "Cerium (IV) sulfate"). The process again has been proposed to involve HS•.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfanyl#cite_note-pos-21)

In water HS is proposed to react with O 2 producing SO 2− and H+. This SO 2− is further proposed to react with O 2 to give SO 2 and [superoxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superoxide "Superoxide") O 2−.

HS• has an equilibrium with S− • and H+.

The hydroxyl radical •OH has also been proposed to combine with H 2 S to form HS• and water.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfanyl#cite_note-Dong-22) Other reactions investigated are:[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfanyl#cite_note-23)[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfanyl#cite_note-schippers-20)

HS• + ethylene HS• + O 2 → HO• + [SO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_monoxide "Sulfur monoxide")2 HS• → H 2 S 2 2 HS• → H 2 and S H 2 S 2 + HS• -> HS–S• + H 2 S
The [hydrogen disulfide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_disulfide "Hydrogen disulfide") is well known.

The ionization energy of HS is 10.4219 eV.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfanyl#cite_note-24) The reduction potential to go to HS− is 0.92 eV.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfanyl#cite_note-das-25) HS• in water can ionize to S•− and H+. The S•− can catalyze a [cis-trans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cis-trans "Cis-trans") conversion in [lipids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lipid "Lipid").[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfanyl#cite_note-26)

HS• reacts with [carboxylic acids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carboxylic_acids "Carboxylic acids") to make [carbonyl sulfide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonyl_sulfide "Carbonyl sulfide") (COS) and probably is the main source of this substance in the atmosphere of Earth.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfanyl#cite_note-pos-21)

HS—S• is called disufanyl with lengthening chains as trisulfanyl, tetrasulfanyl and pentasulfanyl HSSSSS•. S−* is termed sulfanidyl. HS+ is known as sulfanylium, and the common [hydrosulfide ion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrosulfide_ion "Hydrosulfide ion") HS− is also known as sulfanido for a ligand or sulfanide as an anion. Further down the periodic table, HSe• is known as selanyl, and HTe• is termed tellanyl.
