**Tricarbon monoxide** C 3 O is a reactive [radical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radical_(chemistry) "Radical (chemistry)")[oxocarbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxocarbon "Oxocarbon") molecule found in space, and which can be made as a transient substance in the laboratory. It can be trapped in an [inert gas matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inert_gas_matrix "Inert gas matrix") or made as a short lived gas. C 3 O can be classified as a [ketene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ketene "Ketene") or an [oxocumulene](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Oxocumulene&action=edit&redlink=1 "Oxocumulene (page does not exist)") a kind of [heterocumulene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heterocumulene "Heterocumulene").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tricarbon_monoxide#cite_note-rup-3)

C 3 O has been detected by its microwave spectrum in the dark cold [Taurus Molecular Cloud](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurus_Molecular_Cloud "Taurus Molecular Cloud") One[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tricarbon_monoxide#cite_note-4) and also in the protostar [Elias 18](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Elias_18&action=edit&redlink=1 "Elias 18 (page does not exist)").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tricarbon_monoxide#cite_note-abb-5)

The route to produce this is speculated to be:[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tricarbon_monoxide#cite_note-bots-6)

HC+

3 + CO 2 → HC 3 O+ + CO HC 3 O+ → C 3 O + H+
or[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tricarbon_monoxide#cite_note-abb-5)

C 2 + CO → C 3 O which is more favourable at lower temperatures.
The related C 3 S is more abundant in dark [molecular clouds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_cloud "Molecular cloud"), even though oxygen is 20 times more common than sulfur. The difference is due to the higher rate of formation and that C 3 S is less polar.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tricarbon_monoxide#cite_note-abb-5)

C 3 O can be produced by heating [Meldrum's acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meldrum%27s_acid "Meldrum's acid"). This also produces acetone, carbon monoxide and carbon dioxide.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tricarbon_monoxide#cite_note-brownacs-7)

R. L. DeKock and W. Waltner were the first to identify C 3 O by reacting atomic carbon with carbon monoxide in an [argon matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Argon_matrix&action=edit&redlink=1 "Argon matrix (page does not exist)"). They observed an infrared absorption line at 2241 cm−1.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tricarbon_monoxide#cite_note-brownacs-7) They produced carbon atoms by heating graphite inside a thin tantalum tube.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tricarbon_monoxide#cite_note-dekock-8)

M. E. Jacox photolysed [C 3 O 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_suboxide "Carbon suboxide") in an argon matrix to produce C 3 O with an IR absorption line at 2244 cm−1, however he did not recognise what was produced.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tricarbon_monoxide#cite_note-dekock-8)

By heating diazocyclopentanetrione or a similar acid anhydride, (2,4-azo-3-oxo-dipentanoic anhydride), C 3 O is produced. Also the action of light on [tetracarbon dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetracarbon_dioxide "Tetracarbon dioxide") yields C 3 O and CO.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tricarbon_monoxide#cite_note-9)

Heating [fumaryl chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Fumaryl_chloride&action=edit&redlink=1 "Fumaryl chloride (page does not exist)") also yields C 3 O.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tricarbon_monoxide#cite_note-rup-3) Heating Lead [2,4-dinitroresorcinate](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=2,4-dinitroresorcinate&action=edit&redlink=1 "2,4-dinitroresorcinate (page does not exist)") also produces C 3 O along with C 2 O, CO and [carbon suboxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_suboxide "Carbon suboxide").[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tricarbon_monoxide#cite_note-10) An electric discharge in carbon suboxide produces about 11 ppm C 3 O.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tricarbon_monoxide#cite_note-tang-11)

Roger Brown heated 3,5-dimethyl-1-propynolpyrazole to over 700°C to make C 3 O.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tricarbon_monoxide#cite_note-brown-12) Also [pyrolysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrolysis "Pyrolysis") of 5,5'-bis(2,2-dimethyl-4,6-dioxo-1,3-dioxanylidene or di-isopropylidene ethylenetetracarboxylate yields C 3 O.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tricarbon_monoxide#cite_note-brown-12)

Irradiating [carbon monoxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide "Carbon monoxide") ice with electrons yields a mixture of carbon oxides, including C 3 O. This process could happen on icy bodies in space.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tricarbon_monoxide#cite_note-13)

Irradiating [cyclopropenone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclopropenone "Cyclopropenone") with [vacuum ultraviolet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vacuum_ultraviolet "Vacuum ultraviolet") while frozen in a neon matrix causes dehydrogenation to form CCCO. Hydrogen can then react again to yield isomers [propynal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propynal "Propynal") and [propadienone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propadienone "Propadienone").[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tricarbon_monoxide#cite_note-14)

C 3 O can be stabilised as a ligand in the pentacarbonyls of group 6 elements as in Cr(CO)5 CCCO. This is formed from [_n_-Bu 4 N][CrI(CO)5] and the silver acetylide derivative of sodium propiolate (AgC≡CCOONa), and then [thiophosgene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thiophosgene "Thiophosgene"). AgC≡CCOONa in turn is made from silver ions and sodium propiolate.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tricarbon_monoxide#cite_note-tmc-15) The blue black solid complex is called pentacarbony1(3-oxopropadienylidene)chromium(0). It is quite volatile and decomposes at 32°C. Its infrared spectrum shows a band at 2028 cm−1 due to CCCO. The complex can dissolve in hexane, however it slowly decomposes, losing [dicarbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dicarbon "Dicarbon") (C 2) which goes on to form [acetylenes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylenes "Acetylenes") and [cumulenes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cumulene "Cumulene") in the solvent. [Dimethyl sulfoxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimethyl_sulfoxide "Dimethyl sulfoxide") oxidises the CCCO ligand to [carbon suboxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_suboxide "Carbon suboxide")./[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tricarbon_monoxide#cite_note-16)

C 3 O deposits a reddish-black film on glass.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tricarbon_monoxide#cite_note-brown-12)

The reaction of C 3 O and [urea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urea "Urea") is predicted to form [uracil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uracil "Uracil").[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tricarbon_monoxide#cite_note-wang-17) The pathway for this, is that firstly the two molecules react to form [isocyanuric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isocyanuric_acid "Isocyanuric acid") and [propiolamide](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Propiolamide&action=edit&redlink=1 "Propiolamide (page does not exist)"), the NH then reacts to bond with the triple bond, with the NH 2 group moving back. Then a final cyclisation occurs to make uracil.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tricarbon_monoxide#cite_note-18)

The C 3 O molecules do not last long. At the low pressure of 1 pascal, they survive about one second.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tricarbon_monoxide#cite_note-19) The force constants for the bonds are: C1-O 14.94, C1-C2 1.39 C2-C3 6.02 mdyn/Å.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tricarbon_monoxide#cite_note-dekock-8) The bond lengths are C-O 1.149, C1-C2 1.300, C2-C3 1.273 Å. The molecule is linear.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tricarbon_monoxide#cite_note-bots-6)

Proton affinity is 885 kJmol−1.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tricarbon_monoxide#cite_note-bots-6) The dipole moment is 2.391 D.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tricarbon_monoxide#cite_note-tmc-15) The oxygen end has a positive charge, and the carbon end the negative charge.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tricarbon_monoxide#cite_note-bots-6) The molecule behaves as if there are triple bonds at each end, and a single bond in the middle. This is isoelectronic to [cyanogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyanogen "Cyanogen").[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tricarbon_monoxide#cite_note-20)

Molecular constants used in determining the microwave spectrum are rotational constant B 0=4810.8862 MHz centrifugal distortion constant D 0=0.00077 MHz. Known microwave spectral lines vary from 9621.76 for J=1←0 to 182792.35 MHz for J=19←18.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tricarbon_monoxide#cite_note-tang-11)
