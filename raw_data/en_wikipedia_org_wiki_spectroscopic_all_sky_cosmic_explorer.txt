_**Euclid**_ is a wide-angle [space telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_telescope "Space telescope") with a 600-megapixel camera to record [visible light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visible_spectrum "Visible spectrum"), a [near-infrared](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Near-infrared "Near-infrared")[spectrometer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectrometer "Spectrometer"), and [photometer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photometer "Photometer"), to determine the [redshift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift "Redshift") of detected [galaxies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxies "Galaxies"). It was developed by the [European Space Agency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Space_Agency "European Space Agency") (ESA) and the Euclid Consortium and was launched on 1 July 2023 from [Cape Canaveral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cape_Canaveral "Cape Canaveral") in Florida.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-NYT-20230701-10)[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-launch-11) The mission is named after the [ancient Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greece "Ancient Greece") mathematician [Euclid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid "Euclid").

After approximately one month, it reached its destination, a [halo orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halo_orbit "Halo orbit") around the Sun-Earth second [Lagrange point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrange_point "Lagrange point") L2, at an average distance of 1.5 million kilometres beyond [Earth's orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_orbit "Earth's orbit"). There the telescope is to remain operational for at least six years. The objective of the _Euclid_ mission is to better understand [dark energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_energy "Dark energy") and [dark matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter "Dark matter") by accurately measuring the [accelerating expansion of the universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe "Accelerating expansion of the universe"). The [Korsch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korsch_telescope "Korsch telescope")-type telescope will measure the shapes of galaxies at varying distances from Earth and investigate the relationship between distance and [redshift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift "Redshift").

_Euclid_ is a medium-class ("M-class") mission and is part of the [Cosmic Vision](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_Vision "Cosmic Vision") campaign of ESA's [Science Programme](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Space_Agency_Science_Programme "European Space Agency Science Programme"). _Euclid_ was chosen in October 2011 together with [Solar Orbiter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_Orbiter "Solar Orbiter").[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-12)_Euclid_ was launched by a [Falcon 9](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Falcon_9_Block_5 "Falcon 9 Block 5") rocket.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-sn-20221020-13)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-nxsf-euclid-4) On 7 November 2023 ESA revealed _Euclid_'s first full-colour images of the cosmos, which illustrate _Euclid_'s potential to create the most extensive 3D map of the universe.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-NYT-20231107-14)[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-15) In May 2024, ESA's Euclid mission released images of galaxy clusters [Abell 2390](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abell_2390 "Abell 2390") and Abell 2764, star-forming region [Messier 78](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Messier_78 "Messier 78"), spiral galaxy [NGC 6744](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NGC_6744 "NGC 6744"), and the [Dorado group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dorado_Group "Dorado Group") of galaxies.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-16)

Scientific objectives and methods
---------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Euclid_(spacecraft)&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Scientific objectives and methods")]

_Euclid_ will probe the history of the [expansion of the universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expansion_of_the_universe "Expansion of the universe") and the [formation of cosmic structures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Structure_formation "Structure formation") by measuring the redshift of [galaxies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy "Galaxy") out to a redshift value of 2, which is equivalent to seeing 10 billion years into the past.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-science-17) The link between galactic shapes and their corresponding redshift will help to show how dark energy contributes to the increased acceleration of the universe. The methods employed exploit the phenomenon of [gravitational lensing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_lens "Gravitational lens"), measurement of [baryon acoustic oscillations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baryon_acoustic_oscillations "Baryon acoustic oscillations"), and measurement of galactic distances by [spectroscopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopy "Spectroscopy").[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-18)

[Gravitational lensing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_lens "Gravitational lens") (or gravitational shear) is the alteration of the trajectories of light rays caused by the presence of matter that locally modifies the [curvature of space-time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity"): light emitted by galaxies is deflected as it passes close to matter lying along the line of sight, distorting the resulting image. This matter is composed partly of visible galaxies but it theorized to be mostly [dark matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter "Dark matter"). By measuring this _shear_, the amount of dark matter can be inferred, furthering the understanding of how it is distributed in the [universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe "Universe").[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-19)

[Spectroscopic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopy "Spectroscopy") measurements will permit measuring the redshifts of galaxies and determining their distances using [Hubble's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble%27s_law "Hubble's law"). In this way, one can reconstruct the three-dimensional distribution of galaxies in the [universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe "Universe").[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-science-17)

From these data, it is possible to simultaneously measure the statistical properties concerning the distribution of dark matter and galaxies and measure how these properties change as the spacecraft looks further back in time. Highly precise images are required to provide sufficiently accurate measurements. Any distortion inherent in the sensors must be accounted for and calibrated out, otherwise the resultant data would be of limited use.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-science-17)

_Euclid_ in a cleanroom

_Euclid_ emerged from two mission concepts that were proposed in response to the ESA Cosmic Vision 2015–2025 Call for Proposals, issued in March 2007: DUNE, the Dark Universe Explorer, and SPACE, the Spectroscopic All-Sky Cosmic Explorer. Both missions proposed complementary techniques to measure the geometry of the universe, and after an assessment study phase, a combined mission resulted. The new mission concept was called Euclid, honouring the Greek mathematician [Euclid of Alexandria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid_of_Alexandria "Euclid of Alexandria") (~300 BC), who is considered the father of geometry. In October 2011, _Euclid_ was selected by [ESA's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Space_Agency "European Space Agency") Science Programme Committee for implementation, and on 25 June 2012 it was formally adopted.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-overview-1)

[ESA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Space_Agency "European Space Agency") selected [Thales Alenia Space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thales_Alenia_Space "Thales Alenia Space")'s Italian division for the construction of the satellite in [Turin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turin "Turin"). _Euclid_ is 4.5 metres long with a diameter of 3.1 metres and a mass of 2 tonnes.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-new-fact-sheet-3)

Meanwhile, the _Euclid_ payload module was the responsibility of [Airbus Defence and Space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Airbus_Defence_and_Space "Airbus Defence and Space")'s French division in [Toulouse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toulouse "Toulouse"). It consists of a Korsch telescope with a primary mirror 1.2 meters in diameter, which covers an area of 0.91[deg 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_degree "Square degree").[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-20)[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-21)

An international consortium of scientists, the Euclid consortium, comprising scientists from 13 European countries and the United States, provided the visible-light camera (VIS)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-euclid-VIS-6) and the [near-infrared](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Near-infrared "Near-infrared")[spectrometer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectrometer "Spectrometer") and [photometer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photometer "Photometer") (NISP).[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-euclid-NISP-7) Together, they will map the 3D distribution of up to two billion galaxies spread over more than a third of the whole sky.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-22) These large-format cameras will be used to characterise the [morphometric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morphometrics "Morphometrics"), [photometric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photometry_(astronomy) "Photometry (astronomy)"), and spectroscopic properties of galaxies.

*   VIS, a camera operating at [visible wavelengths](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visible_spectrum "Visible spectrum") (530–920 nm), made of a mosaic of 6 × 6[e2v](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/E2v "E2v")[charge coupled detectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charge-coupled_device "Charge-coupled device"), containing 600 million pixels, allows measurement of the deformation of galaxies[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-23)
*   NISP, a camera composed of a mosaic of 4×4 [Teledyne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teledyne "Teledyne") H2RG detectors sensitive to [near-infrared](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infrared "Infrared") light radiation (920–2020 nm) with 65 million pixels, is designed for the following:

1.   provide low-precision measurements of redshifts, and thus distances, of over a billion galaxies from multi-color (3-filter (Y, J and H)) [photometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photometry_(astronomy) "Photometry (astronomy)") ([photometric redshift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photometric_redshift "Photometric redshift") technique); and
2.   use a [slitless spectrometer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slitless_spectrometer "Slitless spectrometer") to analyse the spectrum of light in [near-infrared](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infrared "Infrared") (920–1850 nm), to acquire precise [redshifts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reddit "Reddit") and distances of millions of galaxies with an accuracy 10 times better than [photometric redshifts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photometric_redshift "Photometric redshift"), and to determine the [baryon acoustic oscillations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baryon_acoustic_oscillations "Baryon acoustic oscillations").[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-24)

_Euclid_'s solar panel

The telescope bus includes [solar panels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_panel "Solar panel") that provide power and stabilise the [orientation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spacecraft_attitude_control "Spacecraft attitude control") and pointing of the telescope to better than 35 [milliarcseconds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milliarcseconds "Milliarcseconds") (170 nrad). The telescope is insulated to ensure good thermal stability so as to not disturb the optical alignment.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

The telecommunications system is capable of transferring 850 [gigabits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gigabits "Gigabits") per day. It uses the [Ka band](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ka_band "Ka band") and [CCSDS File Delivery Protocol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CCSDS_File_Delivery_Protocol "CCSDS File Delivery Protocol") to send scientific data at a rate of 55 megabits per second for 4 hours per day to the 35 m dish [Cebreros ground station](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cebreros_Station "Cebreros Station") in Spain, when the telescope is above the horizon. _Euclid_ has an onboard storage capacity of 4 terabits (500[GB](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gigabyte "Gigabyte")).[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-25)

The service module (SVM) hosts most of the spacecraft subsystems:[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

*   TT&C – Telemetry and Telecommand
*   AOCS – Attitude Orbit Control System
*   CDMS – Central Data Management System
*   EPS – Electrical Power System
*   RCS – Reaction Control System
*   MPS – Micro-Propulsion System

AOCS provides stable pointing with a dispersion beneath 35 milli-arcseconds per visual exposure. A high thermal stability is required to protect the telescope assembly from optical misalignments at those accuracies.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-auto-26)

[NASA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NASA "NASA") signed a [memorandum of understanding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Memorandum_of_understanding "Memorandum of understanding") with [ESA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Space_Agency "European Space Agency") on 24 January 2013 describing its participation in the mission. NASA provided 20 detectors for the near-infrared band instrument, which operate in parallel with a camera in the visible-light band. The instruments, the telescope, and the satellite were built-in and are operated from Europe. NASA has also appointed 40 American scientists to be part of the Euclid consortium, which developed the instruments and analyse the data generated by the mission. Currently, this consortium brings together more than 1000 scientists from 13 European countries and the United States.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-27)

In 2015, _Euclid_ passed a preliminary design review, having completed a large number of technical designs as well as built and tested key components.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-28) In December 2018, _Euclid_ passed its critical design review, which validated the overall spacecraft design and mission architecture plan, and final spacecraft assembly was allowed to commence.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-Arianespace-29) In July 2020, the two instruments (visible and NIR) were delivered to Airbus, Toulouse, France for integration with the spacecraft.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-30)

After Russia withdrew in 2022 from the Soyuz-planned launch of _Euclid_, the [ESA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Space_Agency "European Space Agency") reassigned it to a SpaceX Falcon 9 launch vehicle, which launched on 1 July 2023 from [Cape Canaveral Space Launch Complex 40](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cape_Canaveral_Space_Launch_Complex_40 "Cape Canaveral Space Launch Complex 40").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-sn-20221020-13)[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-31)[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-launch-11)

Launch, operational orbit, commissioning
----------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Euclid_(spacecraft)&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Launch, operational orbit, commissioning")]

[![Image 8](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/L2_rendering.jpg/250px-L2_rendering.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:L2_rendering.jpg)

_Euclid_ is orbiting the [L 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrange_point#L2 "Lagrange point") Lagrange point

Following a travel time of 30 days after launch, it began to orbit the Sun-Earth [Lagrangian point L2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrangian_point "Lagrangian point")[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-new-fact-sheet-3) in an eclipse-free [halo orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halo_orbit "Halo orbit") about 1 million km wide.

Upon receiving the initial images, a problem surfaced as scientists discovered a small gap in the spacecraft's hull. This gap allowed sunlight to infiltrate the imaging sensor, resulting in a degradation of image quality.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-32) To tackle this issue, the team adjusted the spacecraft's orientation by a few degrees, effectively blocking sunlight from entering the identified gap. This corrective measure resolved the problem.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-33)

In May 2024 the Early Release Observations (ERO) was the first published data release.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-EuclidTimeline-34) This release contains images and catalogs of star-forming regions, globular clusters, nearby galaxies, fields of the [Fornax cluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fornax_Cluster "Fornax Cluster") and [Perseus cluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perseus_Cluster "Perseus Cluster"), as well as more distant galaxy clusters.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-EuclidERO-35) The content of the ERO is described in one paper submitted to [Astronomy & Astrophysics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy_%26_Astrophysics "Astronomy & Astrophysics"). The researchers use a [pipeline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Software_pipelining "Software pipelining") that can process the images in two ways: optimized for point-sources or optimized for extended sources.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-Cuillandre2025-36) A series of papers describe first results from ERO. These include [free-floating planetary-mass objects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rogue_planet "Rogue planet") discovered in the [Sigma Orionis cluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sigma_Orionis_cluster "Sigma Orionis cluster"),[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-Mart%C3%ADn2024-37) discovery of new [gravitational lenses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_lens "Gravitational lens"),[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-Acevedo_Barroso2024-38) and the discovery of a [dwarf satellite galaxy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dwarf_galaxy "Dwarf galaxy") around [NGC 6744](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NGC_6744 "NGC 6744").[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-Hunt2024-39)

The Quick Euclid data release 1 (Q1) went live on 19th March 2025. Three deep fields can be explored on [ESASky](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ESASky "ESASky"), a web-based tool.[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-40) The data products can be accessed via the Euclid Science Archive, hosted at [ESAC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Space_Astronomy_Centre "European Space Astronomy Centre") or via [IRSA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infrared_Science_Archive "Infrared Science Archive").[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-41)[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-42) Q1 compromises of Euclid Deep Field North (EDF-N), Euclid Deep Field South (EDF-S), and Euclid Deep Field Fornax (EDF-F). These deep fields cover an area of 63.1 square degrees. EDF-F is centered on the same region as the [Chandra Deep Field South](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chandra_Deep_Field_South "Chandra Deep Field South") (CDFS). Euclid will continue to observe these deep fields until DR3, which will be 2 mag deeper than the Euclid Wide Survey. Additionally Q1 includes an 0.5 square degree observation of [LDN 1641](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LDN_1641 "LDN 1641") in the Orion A Cloud.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-43)

A future data release is the Data Release 1 (DR1), planned for 21st October 2026.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-EuclidTimeline-34)

[![Image 9](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Euclid_begins_its_dark_Universe_survey_ESA25479326.jpg/330px-Euclid_begins_its_dark_Universe_survey_ESA25479326.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Euclid_begins_its_dark_Universe_survey_ESA25479326.jpg)

The areas _Euclid_ will cover during its nominal six-year mission

During its nominal mission, which will last at least six years, _Euclid_ will observe about 15,000 deg 2 (4.6 sr), about a third of the sky, focusing on the extragalactic sky (the sky facing away from the [Milky Way](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milky_Way "Milky Way")).[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-ecSpacecraft-2) It will generate approximately 100 gigabytes of compressed data per day throughout its six-year mission.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-44) The survey will be complemented by additional observations of three deep fields to 5 times the [signal-to-noise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Signal-to-noise_ratio "Signal-to-noise ratio") of the wide survey; the deep fields cover 50 deg 2 (15.2 msr).[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-45) The three fields will be regularly visited during the duration of the mission. They will be used as calibration fields and to monitor the telescope and instrument performance stability as well as to produce scientific data by observing the most distant galaxies and [quasars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasars "Quasars") in the universe.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-46) Two of the deep fields will overlap with deep fields of existing surveys[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-47) and the third deep field is proposed as a location for one of the LSST deep drilling fields at the [Vera C. Rubin Observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vera_C._Rubin_Observatory "Vera C. Rubin Observatory").[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-48)

To measure a photometric redshift for each galaxy with sufficient accuracy, the _Euclid_ mission depends on additional photometric data obtained in at least four filters at optical wavelengths. This data will be obtained from ground-based telescopes located in northern and southern hemispheres to cover the full 15,000 deg 2 of the mission.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-49)[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-50) In total each galaxy of the _Euclid_ mission will get photometric information in at least seven different filters covering the range 460–2000 nm.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-eoportal.org-51)

About 10 billion astronomical sources will be observed by _Euclid_, of which one billion will be used for [weak lensing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weak_lensing "Weak lensing") (to have their gravitational shear measured)[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-consortium-52) with a precision 50 times more accurate than was previously possible using ground-based telescopes. _Euclid_ measures spectroscopic redshifts for at least 30 million objects to study [galaxy clustering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_clustering "Galaxy clustering").

The scientific exploitation of this data set will be carried out by a European-led consortium of more than 1200 people in over 100 laboratories in 18 countries (Austria, Belgium, Denmark, Finland, France, Germany, Italy, the Netherlands, Norway, Portugal, Romania, Spain, Switzerland, UK, Canada, US, and Japan).[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-53) The Euclid Consortium[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-consortium-52) is also responsible for the construction of the _Euclid_ instrument payload and for the development and implementation of the Euclid [ground segment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ground_segment "Ground segment") which will process all data collected by the satellite. The laboratories contributing to the Euclid Consortium are funded and supported by their national space agencies, which also have the programmatic responsibilities of their national contribution, and by their national research structures (research agencies, observatories, universities). Overall, the Euclid Consortium contribute about 25% of the total budget cost of the mission until completion.[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-54)

The huge volume, diversity (space and ground, visible and near-infrared, [morphometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morphometrics "Morphometrics"), photometry, and spectroscopy) and the level of precision of measurements demanded considerable care and effort in the data processing, making this a critical part of the mission. [ESA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ESA "ESA"), the national agencies and the Euclid Consortium are spending considerable resources to set up teams of researchers and engineers in algorithm development, software development, testing and validation procedures, data archiving and data distribution infrastructures. In total, nine Science Data Centres spread over countries of the Euclid Consortium will process more than 170 [petabytes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petabyte "Petabyte") of raw input images over at least 6 years to deliver data products (images, catalogues spectra) in three main public data releases in the Science Archive System of the _Euclid_ mission to the scientific community.[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-55)[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-eoportal.org-51)

With its wide sky coverage and its catalogues of billions of [stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star "Star") and galaxies, the scientific value of data collected by the mission goes beyond the scope of [cosmology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmology "Cosmology"). This database will provide the worldwide astronomical community with sources and targets for the [James Webb Space Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Webb_Space_Telescope "James Webb Space Telescope") and [Atacama Large Millimeter Array](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atacama_Large_Millimeter_Array "Atacama Large Millimeter Array"), as well as future missions such as the [Extremely Large Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extremely_Large_Telescope "Extremely Large Telescope"), [Thirty Meter Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thirty_Meter_Telescope "Thirty Meter Telescope"), [Square Kilometer Array](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_Kilometer_Array "Square Kilometer Array"), and the [Vera C. Rubin Observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vera_C._Rubin_Observatory "Vera C. Rubin Observatory").[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_All_Sky_Cosmic_Explorer#cite_note-56)
