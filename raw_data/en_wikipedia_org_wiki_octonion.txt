In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), the **octonions** are a [normed division algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normed_division_algebra "Normed division algebra") over the [real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number"), a kind of [hypercomplex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypercomplex_number "Hypercomplex number")[number system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number#Classification "Number"). The octonions are usually represented by the capital letter O, using boldface **O** or [blackboard bold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blackboard_bold "Blackboard bold")![Image 2: {\displaystyle \mathbb {O} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c1ed2664a4fe515e6fbed25a7193ce663b82920c). Octonions have eight [dimensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension_(vector_space) "Dimension (vector space)"); twice the number of dimensions of the [quaternions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quaternion "Quaternion"), of which they are an extension. They are [noncommutative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_property "Commutative property") and [nonassociative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Associative_property "Associative property"), but satisfy a weaker form of associativity; namely, they are [alternative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alternative_algebra "Alternative algebra"). They are also [power associative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_associativity "Power associativity").

Octonions are not as well known as the quaternions and [complex numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number"), which are much more widely studied and used. Octonions are related to exceptional structures[_[clarification needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Please\_clarify "Wikipedia:Please clarify")_] in mathematics, among them the [exceptional Lie groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simple_Lie_group#Exceptional_cases "Simple Lie group"). Octonions have applications in fields such as [string theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/String_theory "String theory"), [special relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_relativity "Special relativity") and [quantum logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_logic "Quantum logic"). Applying the [Cayley–Dickson construction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cayley%E2%80%93Dickson_construction "Cayley–Dickson construction") to the octonions produces the [sedenions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sedenion "Sedenion").

The octonions were discovered in December 1843 by [John T. Graves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_T._Graves "John T. Graves"), inspired by his friend [William Rowan Hamilton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Rowan_Hamilton "William Rowan Hamilton")'s discovery of quaternions. Shortly before Graves' discovery of octonions, Graves wrote in a letter addressed to Hamilton on October 26, 1843, "If with your alchemy you can make three pounds of gold, why should you stop there?"[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octonion#cite_note-Baez_2002_loc=p._1-1)

Graves called his discovery "octaves", and mentioned them in a letter to Hamilton dated 26 December 1843.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octonion#cite_note-2) He first published his result slightly later than [Arthur Cayley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arthur_Cayley "Arthur Cayley")'s article.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octonion#cite_note-3) The octonions were discovered independently by Cayley[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octonion#cite_note-4) and are sometimes referred to as **Cayley numbers** or the **Cayley algebra**. Hamilton described the early history of Graves's discovery.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octonion#cite_note-5)

The octonions can be thought of as octets (or 8-tuples) of real numbers. Every octonion is a real [linear combination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_combination "Linear combination") of the **unit octonions**:

![Image 3: {\displaystyle {\bigl \{}e_{0},e_{1},e_{2},e_{3},e_{4},e_{5},e_{6},e_{7}{\bigr \}}\ ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8e686085c66c7be81b38c07d09d7b31322a0e8a9)
where _e_ 0 is the scalar or real element; it may be identified with the real number 1 . That is, every octonion x can be written in the form

![Image 4: {\displaystyle x=x_{0}e_{0}+x_{1}e_{1}+x_{2}e_{2}+x_{3}e_{3}+x_{4}e_{4}+x_{5}e_{5}+x_{6}e_{6}+x_{7}e_{7}\ ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ca37a0461cfd864e89c7e30981fff30bb0c9400f)
with real coefficients x i.

### Cayley–Dickson construction

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Octonion&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Cayley–Dickson construction")]

A more systematic way of defining the octonions is via the Cayley–Dickson construction. Applying the Cayley–Dickson construction to the quaternions produces the octonions, which can be expressed as ![Image 5: {\displaystyle \mathbb {O} ={\mathcal {CD}}(\mathbb {H} ,1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f539535491c864898081683e430182353216a11d).[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octonion#cite_note-Ensembles-6)

Much as quaternions can be defined as pairs of complex numbers, the octonions can be defined as pairs of quaternions. Addition is defined pairwise. The product of two pairs of quaternions (_a_, _b_) and (_c_, _d_) is defined by

![Image 6: {\displaystyle (a,b)(c,d)=(ac-d^{*}b,da+bc^{*})\ ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/296cdeeac4f816aedd2fcf089492f935a28f1c4a)
where _z_* denotes the [conjugate of the quaternion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quaternion#Conjugation,_the_norm,_and_reciprocal "Quaternion")z. This definition is equivalent to the one given above when the eight unit octonions are identified with the pairs

(1, 0), (_i_, 0), (_j_, 0), (_k_, 0), (0, 1), (0, _i_), (0, _j_), (0, _k_)

### Addition and subtraction

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Octonion&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Addition and subtraction")]

Addition and subtraction of octonions is done by adding and subtracting corresponding terms and hence their coefficients, like quaternions.

Multiplication of octonions is more complex. Multiplication is [distributive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distributive_property "Distributive property") over addition, so the product of two octonions can be calculated by summing the products of all the terms, again like quaternions. The product of each pair of terms can be given by multiplication of the coefficients and a [multiplication table](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication_table "Multiplication table") of the unit octonions, like this one (given both by [Arthur Cayley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arthur_Cayley "Arthur Cayley") in 1845 and [John T. Graves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_T._Graves "John T. Graves") in 1843):[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octonion#cite_note-GSSV-7)

Most off-diagonal elements of the table are antisymmetric, making it almost a [skew-symmetric matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skew-symmetric_matrix "Skew-symmetric matrix") except for the elements on the main diagonal, as well as the row and column for which _e_ 0 is an operand.

The table can be summarized as follows:[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octonion#cite_note-Shestakov-8)

![Image 90: {\displaystyle e_{\ell }e_{m}={\begin{cases}e_{m},&{\text{if }}\ell =0\\e_{\ell },&{\text{if }}m=0\\-\delta _{\ell m}e_{0}+\varepsilon _{\ell mn}e_{n},&{\text{otherwise}}\end{cases}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/44f491b0073da3e4078fc926808acc010d115a98)
where δ ℓm is the [Kronecker delta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kronecker_delta "Kronecker delta") (equal to 1 if _ℓ_ = _m_, and 0 for _ℓ_ ≠ _m_), and ε ℓmn is a [completely antisymmetric tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Completely_antisymmetric_tensor "Completely antisymmetric tensor") with value +1 when _ℓ m n_ =  1 2 3, 1 4 5, 1 7 6, 2 4 6, 2 5 7, 3 4 7, 3 6 5 , and any even number of [permutations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permutation "Permutation") of the indices, but −1 for any odd [permutations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permutation "Permutation") of the listed triples (e.g. ![Image 91: {\displaystyle \ \varepsilon _{123}=+1\ }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e16835b5234d8ac14f691d6e175f2790a783696f) but ![Image 92: {\displaystyle \ \varepsilon _{132}=\varepsilon _{213}=-1\ ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3f3f0d375fea20c9fb92644d31d19238c8867cf6) however, ![Image 93: {\displaystyle \ \varepsilon _{312}=\varepsilon _{231}=+1\ }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf1e1f8b35f210ded3784be3068524320268e529) again). Whenever any two of the three indices are the same, ε ℓmn= 0 .

The above definition is not unique, however; it is only one of 480 possible definitions for octonion multiplication with _e_ 0 = 1. The others can be obtained by permuting and changing the signs of the non-scalar basis elements {_e_ 1, _e_ 2, _e_ 3, _e_ 4, _e_ 5, _e_ 6, _e_ 7}. The 480 different algebras are [isomorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isomorphism "Isomorphism"), and there is rarely a need to consider which particular multiplication rule is used.

Each of these 480 definitions is invariant up to signs under some 7 cycle of the points  (1 2 3 4 5 6 7) , and for each 7 cycle there are four definitions, differing by signs and reversal of order. A common choice is to use the definition invariant under the 7 cycle (1234567) with _e_ 1 _e_ 2 = _e_ 4 by using the triangular multiplication diagram, or Fano plane below that also shows the sorted list of 1 2 4 based 7-cycle triads and its associated multiplication matrices in both _e_ _n_ and ![Image 94: {\displaystyle \ \mathrm {IJKL} \ }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a96f533601cb272de48b6296aba66438198e9f09) format.

[![Image 95: Octonion triads, Fano plane, and multiplication matrices](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/FanoPlane_with_GeometricAlgebra.svg/960px-FanoPlane_with_GeometricAlgebra.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:FanoPlane_with_GeometricAlgebra.svg "Octonion triads, Fano plane, and multiplication matrices")
A variant of this sometimes used is to label the elements of the basis by the elements ∞, 0, 1, 2, ..., 6, of the [projective line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_line "Projective line") over the [finite field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_field "Finite field") of order 7. The multiplication is then given by _e_∞ = 1 and _e_ 0 _e_ 1 = _e_ 3, and all equations obtained from this one by adding a constant ([modulo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modular_arithmetic "Modular arithmetic") 7) to all subscripts: In other words using the seven triples (0 1 3), (1 2 4), (2 3 5), (3 4 6), (4 5 0), ( 5 6 1), (6 0 2) . These are the nonzero codewords of the [quadratic residue code](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadratic_residue_code "Quadratic residue code") of length 7 over the [Galois field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_field "Finite field") of two elements, [_GF_(2)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GF(2) "GF(2)"). There is a symmetry of order 7 given by adding a constant [mod](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modulo_arithmetic "Modulo arithmetic")7 to all subscripts, and also a symmetry of order 3 given by multiplying all subscripts by one of the quadratic residues 1, 2, 4 mod 7.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octonion#cite_note-Parra-9)[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octonion#cite_note-Manogue-10) These seven triples can also be considered as the seven translates of the set {1,2,4} of non-zero squares forming a cyclic (7,3,1)-[difference set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Difference_set "Difference set") in the finite field [GF(7)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=GF(7)&action=edit&redlink=1 "GF(7) (page does not exist)") of seven elements.

The Fano plane shown above with ![Image 96: {\displaystyle e_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a2927145d553bce5c9187e1a226f77bb1ab59aaf) and IJKL multiplication matrices also includes the [geometric algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_algebra "Geometric algebra") basis with signature (− − − −) and is given in terms of the following 7[quaternionic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quaternion "Quaternion") triples (omitting the scalar identity element):

(_I_ , _j_ , _k_ ) , ( _i_ , _J_ , _k_) , ( _i_ , _j_ , _K_) , (_I_ , _J_ , _K_ ) , (★_I_ , _i_ , _l_ ) , (★_J_ , _j_ , _l_ ), (★_K_ , _k_ , _l_)
or alternatively:

![Image 97: {\displaystyle (\sigma _{1},j,k),(i,\sigma _{2},k),(i,j,\sigma _{3}),(\sigma _{1},\sigma _{2},\sigma _{3}),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/736e249197d18136b72016f157140f549ad9a43b)(★![Image 98: {\displaystyle \sigma _{1},i,l),(}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/800f8472148e0943d8422f60d24bfe9d0c2110af)★![Image 99: {\displaystyle \sigma _{2},j,l),(}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a2a5b204e493fe03dce718c85a899265be36cd05)★![Image 100: {\displaystyle \sigma _{3},k,l)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ff01878baa4f565cc1f9d39ca12b232fd81d2601)
in which the lower case items _{i, j, k, l}_ are [vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_(mathematics_and_physics) "Vector (mathematics and physics)") (e.g. {![Image 101: {\displaystyle \gamma _{0},\gamma _{1},\gamma _{2},\gamma _{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/22edd4a9d420b4e8f42df80f8a07562442b6041d)}, respectively) and the upper case ones {_I,J,K_}={_σ 1,σ 2,σ 3_} are [bivectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bivector "Bivector") (e.g. ![Image 102: {\displaystyle \gamma _{\{1,2,3\}}\gamma _{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c20421fb81c2f8da655adc90d3003f81b53c9d8b), respectively) and the [Hodge star operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hodge_star_operator "Hodge star operator")★ = _i j k l_ is the pseudo-scalar element. If the ★ is forced to be equal to the identity, then the multiplication ceases to be associative, but the ★ may be removed from the multiplication table resulting in an octonion multiplication table.

### Fano plane mnemonic

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Octonion&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Fano plane mnemonic")]

[![Image 104](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/FanoPlane.svg/250px-FanoPlane.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:FanoPlane.svg)

A mnemonic for the products of the unit octonions[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octonion#cite_note-Baez_2002_loc=p._6-11)

[![Image 105](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a5/Octonion-Fano_Cube.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Octonion-Fano_Cube.gif)

A 3D mnemonic visualization showing the 7 triads as [hyperplanes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperplane "Hyperplane") through the real (_e_ 0) vertex of the octonion example given above[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octonion#cite_note-Baez_2002_loc=p._6-11)

A convenient [mnemonic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mnemonic "Mnemonic") for remembering the products of unit octonions is given by the diagram, which represents the multiplication table of Cayley and Graves.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octonion#cite_note-GSSV-7)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octonion#cite_note-Ablamowicz-12) This diagram with seven points and seven lines (the circle through 1, 2, and 3 is considered a line) is called the [Fano plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fano_plane "Fano plane"). The lines are directional. The seven points correspond to the seven standard basis elements of ![Image 106: {\displaystyle \ \operatorname {\mathcal {I_{m}}} {\bigl [}\ \mathbb {O} \ {\bigr ]}\ }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b98a1d4831f41cacfdebeb9e08cecf943c07fae2) (see definition [below](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octonion#Conjugate,_norm,_and_inverse)). Each pair of distinct points lies on a unique line and each line runs through exactly three points.

Let (_a_, _b_, _c_) be an ordered triple of points lying on a given line with the order specified by the direction of the arrow. Then multiplication is given by

_ab_ = _c_ and _ba_ = −_c_
together with [cyclic permutations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclic_permutation "Cyclic permutation"). These rules together with

*   1 is the multiplicative identity,
*   ![Image 107: {\displaystyle {e_{i}}^{2}=-1\ }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/98766256a5065d8c71af3dc4ddc1cfa31acdb750) for each point in the diagram

completely defines the multiplicative structure of the octonions. Each of the seven lines generates a [subalgebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subalgebra#Subalgebras_for_algebras_over_a_ring_or_field "Subalgebra") of ![Image 108: {\displaystyle \ \mathbb {O} \ }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3303480b076c0e7f200edf6f605c00fefd9abbf5) isomorphic to the quaternions **H**.

### Exponentiation and polar form

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Octonion&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Exponentiation and polar form")]

Any octonion x can be decomposed into its real part and imaginary part:

![Image 122: {\displaystyle x={\mathfrak {R}}(x)+{\mathfrak {I}}(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f1006cc2dab3cc8603643ff3b828bd834f892953)

also sometimes called scalar and vector parts.

We define the _unit vector_ u corresponding to x as

![Image 123: {\displaystyle u={\frac {{\mathfrak {I}}(x)}{\|{\mathfrak {I}}(x)\|}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a536adac102e5693c0e3f2b486a7df9c443f1e85). It is a pure octonion of norm 1.

It can be proved[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octonion#cite_note-13) that any non-zero octonion can be written as:

![Image 124: {\displaystyle o=\|o\|(\cos \theta +u\sin \theta )=\|o\|e^{u\theta }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6ccf8d97568842112bc1016f858f088c2f008efe)

thus providing a polar form.

Octonionic multiplication is neither [commutative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative "Commutative"):

_e i e j_ = −_e j e i_ ≠ _e j e i_ if i, j are distinct and non-zero,
nor [associative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Associative "Associative"):

(_e i e j_) _e k_ = −_e i_ (_e j e k_) ≠ _e i_(_e j e k_) if i, j, k are distinct, non-zero and _e i e j_ ≠ ±_e k_.
The octonions do satisfy a weaker form of associativity: they are alternative. This means that the subalgebra generated by any two elements is associative. Actually, one can show that the subalgebra generated by any two elements of ![Image 125: {\displaystyle \ \mathbb {O} \ }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3303480b076c0e7f200edf6f605c00fefd9abbf5) is [isomorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isomorphic "Isomorphic") to [ℝ](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_numbers "Real numbers"), [ℂ](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_numbers "Complex numbers"), or [ℍ](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quaternions "Quaternions"), all of which are associative. Because of their non-associativity, octonions cannot be represented by a subalgebra of a [matrix ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_ring "Matrix ring") over ℝ, unlike the real numbers, complex numbers, and quaternions.

The octonions do retain one important property shared by ℝ, ℂ, and ℍ: the norm on ![Image 126: {\displaystyle \ \mathbb {O} \ }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3303480b076c0e7f200edf6f605c00fefd9abbf5) satisfies

![Image 127: {\displaystyle \|xy\|=\|x\|\ \|y\|~.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/722fd463d68a8d2ed70cb2ee1a3ca4447e47139e)
This equation means that the octonions form a [composition algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Composition_algebra "Composition algebra"). The higher-dimensional algebras defined by the Cayley–Dickson construction (starting with the [sedenions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sedenion "Sedenion")) all fail to satisfy this property. They all have [zero divisors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero_divisor "Zero divisor").

Wider number systems exist which have a multiplicative modulus (for example, 16 dimensional conic sedenions). Their modulus is defined differently from their norm, and they also contain zero divisors.

As shown by [Hurwitz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adolf_Hurwitz "Adolf Hurwitz"), ℝ, ℂ, or ℍ, and ![Image 128: {\displaystyle \ \mathbb {O} \ }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3303480b076c0e7f200edf6f605c00fefd9abbf5) are the only normed division algebras over the real numbers. These four algebras also form the only alternative, finite-dimensional [division algebras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_algebra "Division algebra") over the real numbers ([up to](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Up_to "Up to") an isomorphism).

Not being associative, the nonzero elements of ![Image 129: {\displaystyle \ \mathbb {O} \ }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3303480b076c0e7f200edf6f605c00fefd9abbf5) do not form a [group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_(mathematics) "Group (mathematics)"). They do, however, form a [loop](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Loop_(algebra) "Loop (algebra)"), specifically a [Moufang loop](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moufang_loop "Moufang loop").

### Commutator and cross product

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Octonion&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Commutator and cross product")]

The [commutator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutator "Commutator") of two octonions x and y is given by

![Image 130: {\displaystyle [x,y]=xy-yx~.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5af54ef7860cc3c75083c1c908826981d38dbbb0)
This is antisymmetric and imaginary. If it is considered only as a product on the imaginary subspace ![Image 131: {\displaystyle \ \operatorname {\mathcal {I_{m}}} {\bigl [}\ \mathbb {O} \ {\bigr ]}\ }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b98a1d4831f41cacfdebeb9e08cecf943c07fae2) it defines a product on that space, the [seven-dimensional cross product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seven-dimensional_cross_product "Seven-dimensional cross product"), given by

![Image 132: {\displaystyle x\times y={\tfrac {\ 1\ }{2}}\ (xy-yx)~.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/573f61a69fd04971dcf10405bd0e13156a807e0b)
Like the [cross product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product "Cross product") in three dimensions this is a vector orthogonal to x and y with magnitude

![Image 133: {\displaystyle \|x\times y\|=\|x\|\ \|y\|\ \sin \theta ~.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b7af54f09560b7e7e039864d2958bce8b7e5235c)
But like the octonion product it is not uniquely defined. Instead there are many different cross products, each one dependent on the choice of octonion product.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octonion#cite_note-14)

An [automorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Automorphism "Automorphism"), A, of the octonions is an invertible [linear transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_transformation "Linear transformation") of ![Image 134: {\displaystyle \ \mathbb {O} \ }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3303480b076c0e7f200edf6f605c00fefd9abbf5) which satisfies

![Image 135: {\displaystyle A(xy)=A(x)\ A(y)~.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bb5a5dacbd75adda6724f74d8d1a5615ac61a4b3)
The set of all automorphisms of ![Image 136: {\displaystyle \ \mathbb {O} \ }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3303480b076c0e7f200edf6f605c00fefd9abbf5) forms a group called [_G_ 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G2_(mathematics) "G2 (mathematics)") .[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octonion#cite_note-15) The group _G_ 2 is a [simply connected](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simply_connected "Simply connected"), [compact](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compact_group "Compact group"), real [Lie group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_group "Lie group") of dimension 14. This group is the smallest of the exceptional Lie groups and is isomorphic to the [subgroup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subgroup "Subgroup") of Spin(7) that preserves any chosen particular vector in its 8 dimensional real spinor representation. The group Spin(7) is in turn a subgroup of the group of isotopies described below.

_See also_: [PSL(2,7)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PSL(2,7) "PSL(2,7)") – the [automorphism group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Automorphism_group "Automorphism group") of the Fano plane.

An [isotopy of an algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotopy_of_an_algebra "Isotopy of an algebra") is a triple of [bijective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bijection "Bijection") linear maps a, b, c such that if _xy_ = _z_ then _a_(_x_)_b_(_y_) = _c_(_z_). For _a_ = _b_ = _c_ this is the same as an automorphism. The isotopy group of an algebra is the group of all isotopies, which contains the group of automorphisms as a subgroup.

The isotopy group of the octonions is the group Spin 8(ℝ), with a, b, c acting as the three 8 dimensional representations.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octonion#cite_note-16) The subgroup of elements where c fixes the identity is the subgroup Spin 7(ℝ), and the subgroup where a, b, c all fix the identity is the automorphism group _G_ 2 .

### Matrix representation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Octonion&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Matrix representation")]

Just as quaternions can be [represented as matrices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quaternion#Matrix_representations "Quaternion"), octonions can be represented as tables of quaternions. Specifically, because any octonion can be defined a pair of quaternions, we represent the octonion ![Image 137: {\displaystyle (q_{0},q_{1})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/766cba0b280cc681b301cc823d31c200541090b6) as: ![Image 138: {\displaystyle {\begin{bmatrix}q_{0}&q_{1}\\-q_{1}^{*}&q_{0}^{*}\end{bmatrix}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5cda1b0a7baab95463304d0bc3f02bb83809c5ef)

Using a slightly modified (non-associative) quaternionic matrix multiplication: ![Image 139: {\displaystyle {\begin{bmatrix}\alpha _{0}&\alpha _{1}\\\alpha _{2}&\alpha _{3}\end{bmatrix}}\circ {\begin{bmatrix}\beta _{0}&\beta _{1}\\\beta _{2}&\beta _{3}\end{bmatrix}}={\begin{bmatrix}\alpha _{0}\beta _{0}+\beta _{2}\alpha _{1}&\beta _{1}\alpha _{0}+\alpha _{1}\beta _{3}\\\beta _{0}\alpha _{2}+\alpha _{3}\beta _{2}&\alpha _{2}\beta _{1}+\alpha _{3}\beta _{3}\end{bmatrix}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9711c8b29ebb379a47e9c79f25ab7f8b87035385) we can translate octonion addition and multiplication to the respective operations on quaternionic matrices.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octonion#cite_note-Ensembles-6)

The octonions play a significant role in the classification and construction of other mathematical entities. For example, the [exceptional Lie group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exceptional_Lie_group "Exceptional Lie group")[_G_ 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G2_(mathematics) "G2 (mathematics)") is the automorphism group of the octonions, and the other exceptional Lie groups [_F_ 4](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/F4_(mathematics) "F4 (mathematics)"), [_E_ 6](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/E6_(mathematics) "E6 (mathematics)"), [_E_ 7](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/E7_(mathematics) "E7 (mathematics)") and [_E_ 8](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/E8_(mathematics) "E8 (mathematics)") can be understood as the isometries of certain [projective planes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_plane "Projective plane") defined using the octonions.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octonion#cite_note-17) The set of [self-adjoint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-adjoint "Self-adjoint") 3 × 3 octonionic [matrices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_(mathematics) "Matrix (mathematics)"), equipped with a symmetrized matrix product, defines the [Albert algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_algebra "Albert algebra"). In [discrete mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discrete_mathematics "Discrete mathematics"), the octonions provide an elementary derivation of the [Leech lattice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leech_lattice "Leech lattice"), and thus they are closely related to the [sporadic simple groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sporadic_simple_groups "Sporadic simple groups").[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octonion#cite_note-18)[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octonion#cite_note-19)

Applications of the octonions to physics have largely been conjectural. For example, in the 1970s, attempts were made to understand [quarks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quark "Quark") by way of an octonionic [Hilbert space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space "Hilbert space").[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octonion#cite_note-20) It is known that the octonions, and the fact that only four normed division algebras can exist, relates to the [spacetime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spacetime "Spacetime") dimensions in which [supersymmetric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supersymmetry "Supersymmetry")[quantum field theories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_field_theory "Quantum field theory") can be constructed.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octonion#cite_note-21)[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octonion#cite_note-22) Also, attempts have been made to obtain the [Standard Model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model "Standard Model") of elementary particle physics from octonionic constructions, for example using the "Dixon algebra" ![Image 140: {\displaystyle \ \mathbb {C} \otimes \mathbb {H} \otimes \mathbb {O} ~.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0c9fd5ff6a462c8b641738fd6551176c585f87bd)[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octonion#cite_note-wolchover-23)[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octonion#cite_note-24)

Octonions have also arisen in the study of [black hole entropy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_hole_entropy "Black hole entropy"), [quantum information science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_information_science "Quantum information science"),[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octonion#cite_note-25)[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octonion#cite_note-26)[string theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/String_theory "String theory"),[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octonion#cite_note-27) and [image processing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digital_image_processing "Digital image processing").[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octonion#cite_note-28)

Octonions have been used in solutions to the [hand eye calibration problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hand_eye_calibration_problem "Hand eye calibration problem") in [robotics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robotics "Robotics").[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octonion#cite_note-29)

Deep octonion networks provide a means of efficient and compact expression in machine learning applications.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octonion#cite_note-30)[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octonion#cite_note-31)

There are several natural ways to choose an integral form of the octonions. The simplest is just to take the octonions whose coordinates are [integers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer"). This gives a nonassociative algebra over the integers called the Gravesian octonions. However it is not a [maximal order](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Order_(ring_theory) "Order (ring theory)") (in the sense of ring theory); there are exactly seven maximal orders containing it. These seven maximal orders are all equivalent under automorphisms. The phrase "integral octonions" usually refers to a fixed choice of one of these seven orders.

These maximal orders were constructed by [Kirmse (1924)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octonion#CITEREFKirmse1924), Dickson and Bruck as follows. Label the eight basis vectors by the points of the projective line over the field with seven elements. First form the "Kirmse integers": these consist of octonions whose coordinates are integers or half integers, and that are half integers (that is, halves of odd integers) on one of the 16 sets

∅ (∞124) (∞235) (∞346) (∞450) (∞561) (∞602) (∞013) (∞0123456) (0356) (1460) (2501) (3612) (4023) (5134) (6245)
of the extended [quadratic residue code](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadratic_residue_code "Quadratic residue code") of length 8 over the field of two elements, given by ∅, (∞124) and its images under adding a constant [modulo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modular_arithmetic "Modular arithmetic") 7, and the complements of these eight sets. Then switch infinity and any one other coordinate; this operation creates a bijection of the Kirmse integers onto a different set, which is a maximal order. There are seven ways to do this, giving seven maximal orders, which are all equivalent under cyclic permutations of the seven coordinates 0123456. (Kirmse incorrectly claimed that the Kirmse integers also form a maximal order, so he thought there were eight maximal orders rather than seven, but as [Coxeter (1946)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octonion#CITEREFCoxeter1946) pointed out they are not closed under multiplication; this mistake occurs in several published papers.)

The Kirmse integers and the seven maximal orders are all isometric to the [_E_ 8 lattice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/E8_lattice "E8 lattice") rescaled by a factor of 1⁄√2. In particular there are 240 elements of minimum nonzero norm 1 in each of these orders, forming a Moufang loop of order 240.

The integral octonions have a "division with remainder" property: given integral octonions a and _b_ ≠ 0, we can find q and r with _a_ = _qb_ + _r_, where the remainder r has norm less than that of b.

In the integral octonions, all left [ideals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ideal_(ring_theory) "Ideal (ring theory)") and right ideals are 2-sided ideals, and the only 2-sided ideals are the [principal ideals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principal_ideal "Principal ideal")nO where n is a non-negative integer.

The integral octonions have a version of factorization into primes, though it is not straightforward to state because the octonions are not associative so the product of octonions depends on the order in which one does the products. The irreducible integral octonions are exactly those of prime norm, and every integral octonion can be written as a product of irreducible octonions. More precisely an integral octonion of norm mn can be written as a product of integral octonions of norms m and n.

The automorphism group of the integral octonions is the group _G_ 2(**F**2) of [order](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Order_(group_theory) "Order (group theory)") 12,096, which has a [simple](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simple_group "Simple group") subgroup of [index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Index_of_a_subgroup "Index of a subgroup") 2 isomorphic to the unitary group 2 _A_ 2(3 2). The isotopy group of the integral octonions is the perfect double cover of the group of rotations of the _E_ 8 lattice.
