Displacement measured angle-wise when a body is showing circular or rotational motion

The **angular displacement** (symbol _θ_, _ϑ_, or _φ_) – also called **angle of rotation**, **rotational displacement**, or **rotary displacement** – of a [physical body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_body "Physical body") is the [angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle "Angle") (with the [unit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_of_measurement "Unit of measurement")[radian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radian "Radian"), [degree](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_(angle) "Degree (angle)"), [turn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turn_(geometry) "Turn (geometry)"), etc.) through which the body [rotates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation "Rotation") (revolves or spins) around a centre or [axis of rotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axis_of_rotation "Axis of rotation"). Angular displacement may be signed, indicating the sense of rotation (e.g., [clockwise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clockwise "Clockwise")); it may also be greater (in [absolute value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_value "Absolute value")) than a full [turn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turn_(angle) "Turn (angle)").

Context
-------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Angular_displacement&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Context")]

[![Image 43](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Angulardisplacement1.jpg/330px-Angulardisplacement1.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Angulardisplacement1.jpg)

Rotation of a rigid body _P_ about a fixed axis _O_.

When a body rotates about its axis, the motion cannot simply be analyzed as a particle, as in [circular motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circular_motion "Circular motion") it undergoes a changing velocity and acceleration at any time. When dealing with the rotation of a body, it becomes simpler to consider the body itself rigid. A body is generally considered rigid when the separations between all the particles remains constant throughout the body's motion, so for example parts of its mass are not flying off. In a realistic sense, all things can be deformable, however this impact is minimal and negligible.

Example
-------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Angular_displacement&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Example")]

In the example illustrated to the right (or above in some mobile versions), a particle or body P is at a fixed distance _r_ from the origin, _O_, rotating counterclockwise. It becomes important to then represent the position of particle P in terms of its polar coordinates (_r_, _θ_). In this particular example, the value of _θ_ is changing, while the value of the radius remains the same. (In rectangular coordinates (_x_, _y_) both _x_ and _y_ vary with time.) As the particle moves along the circle, it travels an [arc length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arc_(geometry) "Arc (geometry)")_s_, which becomes related to the angular position through the relationship:

s=r θ.{\displaystyle s=r\theta .}![Image 44: {\displaystyle s=r\theta .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/15e0969a9c40c72dcde3faafb367438ae9e287b2)

Definition and units
--------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Angular_displacement&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Definition and units")]

Angular displacement may be expressed with the unit [radian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radian "Radian") or degree. Using the radian provides a very simple relationship between distance traveled around the circle (_[circular arc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circular\_arc "Circular arc")[length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arc\_length "Arc length")_) and the distance _r_ from the centre (_[radius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radius "Radius")_):

θ=s r r a d{\displaystyle \theta ={\frac {s}{r}}\mathrm {rad} }![Image 45: {\displaystyle \theta ={\frac {s}{r}}\mathrm {rad} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d0c54ce4bb4f268ef8fee297142e396a14c414f9)
For example, if a body rotates 360° around a circle of radius _r_, the angular displacement is given by the distance traveled around the circumference - which is 2π _r_ - divided by the radius: θ=2 π r r{\displaystyle \theta ={\frac {2\pi r}{r}}}![Image 46: {\displaystyle \theta ={\frac {2\pi r}{r}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/50300e5b081388940ccc43f5da8da0dd24e036d3) which easily simplifies to: θ=2 π{\displaystyle \theta =2\pi }![Image 47: {\displaystyle \theta =2\pi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af3479f95999b7e195e5ecc2ee808bf02286332e). Therefore, 1 [revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Revolution_(unit) "Revolution (unit)") is 2 π{\displaystyle 2\pi }![Image 48: {\displaystyle 2\pi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/73efd1f6493490b058097060a572606d2c550a06) radians.

The above definition is part of the [International System of Quantities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Quantities "International System of Quantities") (ISQ), formalized in the international standard [ISO 80000-3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO_80000-3 "ISO 80000-3") (Space and time),[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_displacement#cite_note-ISO80000-3_2019-1) and adopted in the [International System of Units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Units "International System of Units") (SI).[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_displacement#cite_note-SIBrochure_9-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_displacement#cite_note-NISTGuide_2009-3)

Angular displacement may be signed, indicating the sense of rotation (e.g., [clockwise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clockwise "Clockwise"));[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_displacement#cite_note-ISO80000-3_2019-1) it may also be greater (in [absolute value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_value "Absolute value")) than a full [turn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turn_(angle) "Turn (angle)"). In the ISQ/SI, angular displacement is used to define the _[number of revolutions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number\_of\_revolutions "Number of revolutions")_, _N_ = θ/(2π rad), a ratio and hence a [quantity of dimension one](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantity_of_dimension_one "Quantity of dimension one").

In three dimensions
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Angular_displacement&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: In three dimensions")]

Main article: [Three-dimensional rotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_rotation "Three-dimensional rotation")

[![Image 49](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Euler_Rotation_2.JPG/250px-Euler_Rotation_2.JPG)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Euler_Rotation_2.JPG)

**Figure 1**: Euler's rotation theorem. A great circle transforms to another great circle under rotations, leaving always a diameter of the sphere in its original position.

[![Image 50](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Euler_AxisAngle.png/250px-Euler_AxisAngle.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Euler_AxisAngle.png)

**Figure 2**: A rotation represented by an Euler axis and angle

In three dimensions, angular displacement is an entity with a direction and a magnitude. The direction specifies the axis of rotation, which always exists by virtue of the [Euler's rotation theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler%27s_rotation_theorem "Euler's rotation theorem"); the magnitude specifies the rotation in [radians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radian "Radian") about that axis (using the [right-hand rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right-hand_rule "Right-hand rule") to determine direction). This entity is called an [axis-angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axis-angle "Axis-angle").

Despite having direction and magnitude, angular displacement is not a [vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_(geometry) "Vector (geometry)") because it does not obey the [commutative law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_law "Commutative law") for addition.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_displacement#cite_note-4) Nevertheless, when dealing with infinitesimal rotations, second order infinitesimals can be discarded and in this case commutativity appears.

### Rotation matrices

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Angular_displacement&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Rotation matrices")]

Several ways to describe rotations exist, like [rotation matrices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation_matrix "Rotation matrix") or [Euler angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler_angles "Euler angles"). See [charts on SO(3)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charts_on_SO(3) "Charts on SO(3)") for others.

Given that any frame in the space can be described by a rotation matrix, the displacement among them can also be described by a rotation matrix. Being A 0{\displaystyle A_{0}}![Image 51: {\displaystyle A_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7c98dbd929292d464de6942e95db306b8b8a6fca) and A f{\displaystyle A_{f}}![Image 52: {\displaystyle A_{f}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5e9cba7c44dc1773496e4ffc9c7e0be19c591369) two matrices, the angular displacement matrix between them can be obtained as ⁠Δ A=A f A 0−1{\displaystyle \Delta A=A_{f}A_{0}^{-1}}![Image 53: {\displaystyle \Delta A=A_{f}A_{0}^{-1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d239d5fc91d3a205f0eef991a9b6cacd52783994)⁠. When this product is performed having a very small difference between both frames we will obtain a matrix close to the identity.

In the limit, we will have an infinitesimal rotation matrix.

### Infinitesimal rotation matrices

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Angular_displacement&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Infinitesimal rotation matrices")]

This section is an excerpt from [Infinitesimal rotation matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinitesimal_rotation_matrix "Infinitesimal rotation matrix").[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Infinitesimal_rotation_matrix&action=edit)]

An [infinitesimal rotation matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinitesimal_rotation_matrix "Infinitesimal rotation matrix") or differential rotation matrix is a [matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_(mathematics) "Matrix (mathematics)") representing an [infinitely](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinitesimal "Infinitesimal") small [rotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation "Rotation").

While a [rotation matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation_matrix "Rotation matrix") is an [orthogonal matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_matrix "Orthogonal matrix")R T=R−1{\displaystyle R^{\mathsf {T}}=R^{-1}}![Image 54: {\displaystyle R^{\mathsf {T}}=R^{-1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/85439c87cf2430c0c01e9eb25f6d6cee89ee3238) representing an element of S O(n){\displaystyle \mathrm {SO} (n)}![Image 55: {\displaystyle \mathrm {SO} (n)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9fa71842f19b6810b4bfa9eb282e92fbf285094e) (the [special orthogonal group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_orthogonal_group "Special orthogonal group")), the [differential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_(mathematics) "Differential (mathematics)") of a rotation is a [skew-symmetric matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skew-symmetric_matrix "Skew-symmetric matrix")A T=−A{\displaystyle A^{\mathsf {T}}=-A}![Image 56: {\displaystyle A^{\mathsf {T}}=-A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8f020a5cc94f0fd27929041f589affa0e575a083) in the [tangent space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_space "Tangent space")s o(n){\displaystyle {\mathfrak {so}}(n)}![Image 57: {\displaystyle {\mathfrak {so}}(n)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/57c18bd074c7bdc79ea650563edde2e2bc080321) (the [special orthogonal Lie algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_orthogonal_Lie_algebra "Special orthogonal Lie algebra")), which is not itself a rotation matrix.

An infinitesimal rotation matrix has the form

I+d θ A,{\displaystyle I+d\theta \,A,}![Image 58: {\displaystyle I+d\theta \,A,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e3da72e5ca97ba81447afc60fae1a089f4c1a20f)
where I{\displaystyle I}![Image 59: {\displaystyle I}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/535ea7fc4134a31cbe2251d9d3511374bc41be9f) is the identity matrix, d θ{\displaystyle d\theta }![Image 60: {\displaystyle d\theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/75ae6ca1248d081ef3fcfdd3e17ba0e3f6c02ee9) is vanishingly small, and ⁠A∈s o(n){\displaystyle A\in {\mathfrak {so}}(n)}![Image 61: {\displaystyle A\in {\mathfrak {so}}(n)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/890bc58f0448c24e5a51eb996e6d024b9f3d40db)⁠.

For example, if ⁠A=L x{\displaystyle A=L_{x}}![Image 62: {\displaystyle A=L_{x}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4eaa5e8d5cb15b03dfa763d36362a272c1c7fd83)⁠, representing an infinitesimal three-dimensional rotation about the x-axis, a basis element of ⁠s o(3){\displaystyle {\mathfrak {so}}(3)}![Image 63: {\displaystyle {\mathfrak {so}}(3)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fb4f1d3d3bf3da64b92af1a1018ce00545808b9d)⁠, then

L x=[0 0 0 0 0−1 0 1 0],{\displaystyle L_{x}={\begin{bmatrix}0&0&0\\0&0&-1\\0&1&0\end{bmatrix}},}![Image 64: {\displaystyle L_{x}={\begin{bmatrix}0&0&0\\0&0&-1\\0&1&0\end{bmatrix}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/17735dab574393e5b3fd014038a70e5767006575)
and

I+d θ L x=[1 0 0 0 1−d θ 0 d θ 1].{\displaystyle I+d\theta L_{x}={\begin{bmatrix}1&0&0\\0&1&-d\theta \\0&d\theta &1\end{bmatrix}}.}![Image 65: {\displaystyle I+d\theta L_{x}={\begin{bmatrix}1&0&0\\0&1&-d\theta \\0&d\theta &1\end{bmatrix}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35760f4d048001b7b2793b130aa3c2c8dc843458) The computation rules for infinitesimal rotation matrices are the usual ones except that infinitesimals of second order are dropped. With these rules, these matrices do not satisfy all the same properties as ordinary finite rotation matrices under the usual treatment of infinitesimals.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_displacement#cite_note-5) It turns out that _the order in which infinitesimal rotations are applied is irrelevant_.
