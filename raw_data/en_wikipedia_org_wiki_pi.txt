Title: Pi

URL Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi

Published Time: 2001-11-11T21:01:15Z

Markdown Content:
The number **π** (; spelled out as **pi**) is a [mathematical constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_constant "Mathematical constant"), approximately equal to 3.14159, that is the [ratio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ratio "Ratio") of a [circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle "Circle")'s [circumference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumference "Circumference") to its [diameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diameter "Diameter"). It appears in many formulae across [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics") and [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics"), and some of these formulae are commonly used for defining π, to avoid relying on the definition of the [length of a curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arc_length "Arc length").

The number π is an [irrational number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irrational_number "Irrational number"), meaning that it cannot be expressed exactly as a ratio of two integers, although fractions such as ![Image 1: {\displaystyle {\tfrac {22}{7}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6cb5d075d03ff7edd66274d09cb1e2dd0aee7bc9) are commonly [used to approximate it](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Approximations_of_%CF%80 "Approximations of π"). Consequently, its [decimal representation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_representation "Decimal representation") never ends, nor [enters a permanently repeating pattern](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Repeating_decimal "Repeating decimal"). It is a [transcendental number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transcendental_number "Transcendental number"), meaning that it cannot be a solution of an [algebraic equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_equation "Algebraic equation") involving only finite sums, products, powers, and integers. The transcendence of π implies that it is impossible to solve the ancient challenge of [squaring the circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Squaring_the_circle "Squaring the circle") with a [compass and straightedge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compass-and-straightedge_construction "Compass-and-straightedge construction"). The decimal digits of π appear to be [randomly distributed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Random_sequence "Random sequence"), but no proof of this [conjecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conjecture "Conjecture") has been found.

For thousands of years, mathematicians have attempted to extend their understanding of π, sometimes by computing its value to a high degree of accuracy. Ancient civilizations, including the [Egyptians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egyptian_mathematics "Egyptian mathematics") and [Babylonians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Babylonian_mathematics "Babylonian mathematics"), required fairly accurate approximations of π for practical computations. Around 250 BC, the [Greek mathematician](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_mathematics "Greek mathematics")[Archimedes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archimedes "Archimedes") created an algorithm to approximate π with arbitrary accuracy. In the 5th century AD, [Chinese mathematicians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_mathematics "Chinese mathematics") approximated π to seven digits, while [Indian mathematicians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_mathematics "Indian mathematics") made a five-digit approximation, both using geometrical techniques. The first computational formula for π, based on [infinite series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Series_(mathematics) "Series (mathematics)"), was discovered a millennium later. The earliest known use of the Greek letter [π](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi_(letter) "Pi (letter)") to represent the ratio of a circle's circumference to its diameter was by the Welsh mathematician [William Jones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Jones_(mathematician) "William Jones (mathematician)") in 1706. The invention of [calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus "Calculus") soon led to the calculation of hundreds of digits of π, enough for all practical scientific computations. Nevertheless, in the 20th and 21st centuries, mathematicians and [computer scientists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_science "Computer science") have pursued new approaches that, when combined with increasing computational power, extended the decimal representation of π to many trillions of digits. These computations are motivated by the development of efficient algorithms to calculate numeric series, as well as the human quest to break records. The extensive computations involved have also been used to test [supercomputers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supercomputer "Supercomputer") as well as stress testing consumer computer hardware.

Because it relates to a circle, π is found in many formulae in [trigonometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trigonometry "Trigonometry") and [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry"), especially those concerning circles, ellipses and spheres. It is also found in formulae from other topics in science, such as [cosmology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmology "Cosmology"), [fractals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal "Fractal"), [thermodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamics "Thermodynamics"), [mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanics "Mechanics"), and [electromagnetism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetism "Electromagnetism"). It also appears in areas having little to do with geometry, such as [number theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_theory "Number theory") and [statistics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistics "Statistics"), and in modern [mathematical analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_analysis "Mathematical analysis") can be defined without any reference to geometry. The ubiquity of π makes it one of the most widely known mathematical constants inside and outside of science. Several books devoted to π have been published, and record-setting calculations of the digits of π often result in news headlines.

Fundamentals
------------

### Name

The symbol used by mathematicians to represent the ratio of a circle's circumference to its diameter is the lowercase [Greek letter π](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi_(letter) "Pi (letter)"), sometimes spelled out as _pi._[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-firstPi-1) In English, π is [pronounced as "pie"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English_pronunciation_of_Greek_letters "English pronunciation of Greek letters") ( [_PY_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key "Help:Pronunciation respelling key")).[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-2) In mathematical use, the lowercase letter π is distinguished from its capitalized and enlarged counterpart Π, which denotes a [product of a sequence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication#Product_of_a_sequence "Multiplication"), analogous to how Σ denotes [summation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Summation "Summation").

The choice of the symbol π is discussed in the section [_Adoption of the symbol π_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#Adoption_of_the_symbol_%CF%80).

### Definition

[![Image 2: A diagram of a circle, with the width labelled as diameter, and the perimeter labelled as circumference](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Pi_eq_C_over_d.svg/250px-Pi_eq_C_over_d.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Pi_eq_C_over_d.svg)

The circumference of a circle is slightly more than three times as long as its diameter. The exact ratio is called π.

π is commonly defined as the [ratio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ratio "Ratio") of a [circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle "Circle")'s [circumference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumference "Circumference")_C_ to its [diameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diameter "Diameter")_d_:[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel20068-3)

![Image 3: {\displaystyle \pi ={\frac {C}{d}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3ffc6970dda2e60597854f14e2ac1e13a25a5cf)

The ratio ![Image 4: {\textstyle {\frac {C}{d}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c8ebfc4cd5de67be49da2841f490d1d55053a1c4) is constant, regardless of the circle's size. For example, if a circle has twice the diameter of another circle, it will also have twice the circumference, preserving the ratio ![Image 5: {\textstyle {\frac {C}{d}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c8ebfc4cd5de67be49da2841f490d1d55053a1c4). This definition of π implicitly makes use of [flat (Euclidean) geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_geometry "Euclidean geometry"); although the notion of a circle can be extended to any [curve (non-Euclidean) geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-Euclidean_geometry "Non-Euclidean geometry"), these new circles will no longer satisfy the formula ![Image 6: {\textstyle \pi ={\frac {C}{d}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1a38da24b191fd9293cb427b50587c924cd97824).[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel20068-3)

Here, the circumference of a circle is the [arc length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arc_length "Arc length") around the [perimeter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perimeter "Perimeter") of the circle, a quantity which can be formally defined independently of geometry using [limits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limit_(mathematics) "Limit (mathematics)")—a concept in [calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus "Calculus").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-4) For example, one may directly compute the arc length of the top half of the unit circle, given in [Cartesian coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinates "Cartesian coordinates") by the equation ![Image 7: {\textstyle x^{2}+y^{2}=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1207955c0a974b8d1849a63adbfb457d9fd4679c), as the [integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral "Integral"):[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTERemmert2012129-5)

![Image 8: {\displaystyle \pi =\int _{-1}^{1}{\frac {dx}{\sqrt {1-x^{2}}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/50725fce8b20701f737a6ecc57aff4b8969107c4)

An integral such as this was proposed as a definition of π by [Karl Weierstrass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karl_Weierstrass "Karl Weierstrass"), who defined it directly as an integral in 1841.[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-7)

Integration is no longer commonly used in a first analytical definition because, as [Remmert 2012](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#CITEREFRemmert2012) explains, [differential calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_calculus "Differential calculus") typically precedes integral calculus in the university curriculum, so it is desirable to have a definition of π that does not rely on the latter. One such definition, due to Richard Baltzer[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-8) and popularized by [Edmund Landau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edmund_Landau "Edmund Landau"),[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-9) is the following: π is twice the smallest positive number at which the [cosine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosine "Cosine") function equals 0.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel20068-3)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTERemmert2012129-5)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-Rudin_1976-10)π is also the smallest positive number at which the [sine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sine "Sine") function equals zero, and the difference between consecutive zeroes of the sine function. The cosine and sine can be defined independently of geometry as a [power series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_series "Power series"),[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-11) or as the solution of a [differential equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_equation "Differential equation").[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-Rudin_1976-10)

In a similar spirit, π can be defined using properties of the [complex exponential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_exponential "Complex exponential"), exp _z_, of a [complex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number") variable _z_. Like the cosine, the complex exponential can be defined in one of several ways. The set of complex numbers at which exp _z_ is equal to one is then an (imaginary) arithmetic progression of the form:

![Image 9: {\displaystyle \{\dots ,-2\pi i,0,2\pi i,4\pi i,\dots \}=\{2\pi ki\mid k\in \mathbb {Z} \}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9411506ae82c49d1b868a47ce5dea04adf234cba)

and there is a unique positive real number π with this property.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTERemmert2012129-5)[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-12)

A variation on the same idea, making use of sophisticated mathematical concepts of [topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology "Topology") and [algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra "Algebra"), is the following theorem:[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-13) there is a unique ([up to](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Up_to "Up to")[automorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Automorphism "Automorphism")) [continuous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_function "Continuous function")[isomorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isomorphism "Isomorphism") from the [group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_(mathematics) "Group (mathematics)")**R**/**Z** of real numbers under addition [modulo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quotient_group "Quotient group") integers (the [circle group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle_group "Circle group")), onto the multiplicative group of [complex numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_numbers "Complex numbers") of [absolute value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_value "Absolute value") one. The number π is then defined as half the magnitude of the derivative of this homomorphism.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-Nicolas_Bourbaki-14)

### Irrationality and normality

π is an [irrational number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irrational_number "Irrational number"), meaning that it cannot be written as the [ratio of two integers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_number "Rational number"). Fractions such as ⁠22/7⁠ and ⁠355/113⁠ are commonly used to approximate π, but no [common fraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Common_fraction "Common fraction") (ratio of whole numbers) can be its exact value.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel20065-15) Because π is irrational, it has an infinite number of digits in its [decimal representation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_representation "Decimal representation"), and does not settle into an infinitely [repeating pattern](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Repeating_decimal "Repeating decimal") of digits. There are several [proofs that π is irrational](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proof_that_%CF%80_is_irrational "Proof that π is irrational"); they are generally [proofs by contradiction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proofs_by_contradiction "Proofs by contradiction") and require calculus. The degree to which π can be approximated by [rational numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_number "Rational number") (called the [irrationality measure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irrationality_measure "Irrationality measure")) is not precisely known; estimates have established that the irrationality measure is larger or at least equal to the measure of _e_ but smaller than the measure of [Liouville numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liouville_number "Liouville number").[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-16)

The digits of π have no apparent pattern and have passed tests for [statistical randomness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistical_randomness "Statistical randomness"), including tests for [normality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_number "Normal number"); a number of infinite length is called normal when all possible sequences of digits (of any given length) appear equally often. The conjecture that π is [normal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_number "Normal number") has not been proven or disproven.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel200622%E2%80%9323-17)

Since the advent of computers, a large number of digits of π have been available on which to perform statistical analysis. [Yasumasa Kanada](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yasumasa_Kanada "Yasumasa Kanada") has performed detailed statistical analyses on the decimal digits of π, and found them consistent with normality; for example, the frequencies of the ten digits 0 to 9 were subjected to [statistical significance tests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistical_significance_test "Statistical significance test"), and no evidence of a pattern was found.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel200622,_28%E2%80%9330-18) Any random sequence of digits contains arbitrarily long subsequences that appear non-random, by the [infinite monkey theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinite_monkey_theorem "Infinite monkey theorem"). Thus, because the sequence of π's digits passes statistical tests for randomness, it contains some sequences of digits that may appear non-random, such as a [sequence of six consecutive 9s](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Six_nines_in_pi "Six nines in pi") that begins at the 762nd decimal place of the decimal representation of π.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel20063-19) This is also called the "Feynman point" in [mathematical folklore](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_folklore "Mathematical folklore"), after [Richard Feynman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Feynman "Richard Feynman"), although no connection to Feynman is known.

### Transcendence

[![Image 10: A diagram of a square and circle, both with identical area; the length of the side of the square is the square root of pi](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Squaring_the_circle.svg/250px-Squaring_the_circle.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Squaring_the_circle.svg)

Because π is a [transcendental number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transcendental_number "Transcendental number"), [squaring the circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Squaring_the_circle "Squaring the circle") is not possible in a finite number of steps using the classical tools of [compass and straightedge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compass-and-straightedge_construction "Compass-and-straightedge construction").

In addition to being irrational, π is also a [transcendental number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transcendental_number "Transcendental number"), which means that it is not the [solution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solution_(equation) "Solution (equation)") of any non-constant [polynomial equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial_equation "Polynomial equation") with [rational](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_number "Rational number") coefficients, such as ![Image 11: {\textstyle {\frac {x^{5}}{120}}-{\frac {x^{3}}{6}}+x=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fcb75c4d7ee0e26b7281831ffb74ea6d2496b1b6).[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel20066-20)[[b]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-21) This follows from the so-called [Lindemann–Weierstrass theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lindemann%E2%80%93Weierstrass_theorem#Transcendence_of_e_and_%CF%80 "Lindemann–Weierstrass theorem"), which also establishes the transcendence of [the constant _e_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/E_(mathematical_constant) "E (mathematical constant)").

The transcendence of π has two important consequences: First, π cannot be expressed using any finite combination of rational numbers and square roots or [_n_-th roots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nth_root "Nth root") (such as ![Image 12: {\displaystyle {\sqrt[{3}]{31}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1fb1cd8b2742403364f3adca4fc8b8ca4a21e7f5) or ![Image 13: {\displaystyle {\sqrt {10}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dd7409b0ddbc1f90280265e7bc95dd20626ebf1b)). Second, since no transcendental number can be [constructed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constructible_number "Constructible number") with [compass and straightedge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compass-and-straightedge_construction "Compass-and-straightedge construction"), it is not possible to "[square the circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Squaring_the_circle "Squaring the circle")". In other words, it is impossible to construct, using compass and straightedge alone, a square whose area is exactly equal to the area of a given circle.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPosamentierLehmann200425-22) Squaring a circle was one of the important geometry problems of the [classical antiquity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_antiquity "Classical antiquity").[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEymardLafon2004129-23) Amateur mathematicians in modern times have sometimes attempted to square the circle and claim success—despite the fact that it is mathematically impossible.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-24)

An [unsolved problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_unsolved_problems_in_mathematics "List of unsolved problems in mathematics") thus far is the question of whether or not the numbers _π_ and _e_ are [algebraically independent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_independence "Algebraic independence") ("relatively transcendental"). This would be resolved by [Schanuel's conjecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schanuel%27s_conjecture "Schanuel's conjecture")[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-25) – a currently unproven generalization of the Lindemann–Weierstrass theorem.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-26)

### Continued fractions

As an irrational number, π cannot be represented as a [common fraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Common_fraction "Common fraction"). But every number, including π, can be represented by an infinite series of nested fractions, called a [simple continued fraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simple_continued_fraction "Simple continued fraction"):

![Image 14: {\displaystyle \pi =3+\textstyle {\cfrac {1}{7+\textstyle {\cfrac {1}{15+\textstyle {\cfrac {1}{1+\textstyle {\cfrac {1}{292+\textstyle {\cfrac {1}{1+\textstyle {\cfrac {1}{1+\textstyle {\cfrac {1}{1+\ddots }}}}}}}}}}}}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e294255007892e9424e6d3f49183cad6b133bbcf)

Truncating the continued fraction at any point yields a rational approximation for π; the first four of these are 3, ⁠22/7⁠, ⁠333/106⁠, and ⁠355/113⁠. These numbers are among the best-known and most widely used historical approximations of the constant. Each approximation generated in this way is a best rational approximation; that is, each is closer to π than any other fraction with the same or a smaller denominator.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEymardLafon200478-27) Because π is transcendental, it is by definition not [algebraic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_number "Algebraic number") and so cannot be a [quadratic irrational](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadratic_irrational "Quadratic irrational"). Therefore, π cannot have a [periodic continued fraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Periodic_continued_fraction "Periodic continued fraction"). Although the simple continued fraction for π (with numerators all 1, shown above) also does not exhibit any other obvious pattern,[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel200633-28)[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-mollin-29) several non-simple [continued fractions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continued_fraction "Continued fraction") do, such as:[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-30)[[c]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-31)

![Image 15: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\pi &=3+{\cfrac {1^{2}}{6+{\cfrac {3^{2}}{6+{\cfrac {5^{2}}{6+{\cfrac {7^{2}}{6+\ddots }}}}}}}}={\cfrac {4}{1+{\cfrac {1^{2}}{2+{\cfrac {3^{2}}{2+{\cfrac {5^{2}}{2+\ddots }}}}}}}}={\cfrac {4}{1+{\cfrac {1^{2}}{3+{\cfrac {2^{2}}{5+{\cfrac {3^{2}}{7+\ddots }}}}}}}}\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/02c2f88e03c466938a66d99b268ff5a5ea756dc1)

### Complex numbers and Euler's identity

[![Image 16: A diagram of a unit circle centred at the origin in the complex plane, including a ray from the centre of the circle to its edge, with the triangle legs labelled with sine and cosine functions.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Euler%27s_formula.svg/250px-Euler%27s_formula.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Euler%27s_formula.svg)

The association between imaginary powers of the number _e_ and [points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_(geometry) "Point (geometry)") on the [unit circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_circle "Unit circle") centred at the [origin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Origin_(mathematics) "Origin (mathematics)") in the [complex plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_plane "Complex plane") given by [Euler's formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler%27s_formula "Euler's formula")

Any [complex number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number"), say z, can be expressed using a pair of [real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number"). In the [polar coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_coordinate_system#Complex_numbers "Polar coordinate system"), one number ([radius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radius "Radius") or r) is used to represent z's distance from the [origin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Origin_(mathematics) "Origin (mathematics)") of the [complex plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_plane "Complex plane"), and the other (angle or φ) the counter-clockwise [rotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation "Rotation") from the positive real line:[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAbramson2014[httpsopenstaxorgbooksprecalculuspages8-5-polar-form-of-complex-numbers_Section_8.5:_Polar_form_of_complex_numbers]-35)

![Image 17: {\displaystyle z=r\cdot (\cos \varphi +i\sin \varphi ),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8e93279bb31b398ca08d16fb9d764fd1214174ab)

where i is the [imaginary unit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imaginary_unit "Imaginary unit") satisfying ![Image 18: {\displaystyle i^{2}=-1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/88e98a401d352e5037d5043028e2d7f449e83fa6). The frequent appearance of π in [complex analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_analysis "Complex analysis") can be related to the behaviour of the [exponential function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponential_function "Exponential function") of a complex variable, described by [Euler's formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler%27s_formula "Euler's formula"):[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBronshte%C4%ADnSemendiaev1971592-36)

![Image 19: {\displaystyle e^{i\varphi }=\cos \varphi +i\sin \varphi ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3b85e2656dda51f900210b347fb30b65d3a26f11)

where [the constant _e_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/E_(mathematical_constant) "E (mathematical constant)") is the base of the [natural logarithm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_logarithm "Natural logarithm"). This formula establishes a correspondence between imaginary powers of _e_ and points on the [unit circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_circle "Unit circle") centred at the origin of the complex plane. Setting ![Image 20: {\displaystyle \varphi =\pi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3f181a336ae10f53a432232ff83696fbdeb4e622) in Euler's formula results in [Euler's identity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler%27s_identity "Euler's identity"), celebrated in mathematics due to it containing five important mathematical constants:[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBronshte%C4%ADnSemendiaev1971592-36)[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-37)

![Image 21: {\displaystyle e^{i\pi }+1=0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e76caf050d8bc37cd2350c40517face26de5ecb7)

There are _n_ different complex numbers z satisfying ![Image 22: {\displaystyle z^{n}=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/34fb31edf665701c275c44a5be8b82a95509888d), and these are called the "_n_-th [roots of unity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Root_of_unity "Root of unity")"[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAndrewsAskeyRoy199914-38) and are given by the formula:

![Image 23: {\displaystyle e^{2\pi ik/n}\qquad (k=0,1,2,\dots ,n-1).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c74d8393eb2cf4d7a3e5ef0dd9ab50a9fe58389f)

History
-------

Surviving approximations of π prior to the 2nd century AD are accurate to one or two decimal places at best. The earliest written approximations are found in [Babylon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Babylon "Babylon") and Egypt, both within one percent of the true value. In Babylon, a [clay tablet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clay_tablet "Clay tablet") dated 1900–1600 BC has a geometrical statement that, by implication, treats π as ⁠25/8⁠=3.125.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel2006167-39) In Egypt, the [Rhind Papyrus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhind_Papyrus "Rhind Papyrus"), dated around 1650 BC but copied from a document dated to 1850 BC, has a formula for the area of a circle that treats π as ![Image 24: {\textstyle {\bigl (}{\frac {16}{9}}{\bigr )}^{2}\approx 3.16}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1e578ff1826354c928fbf5aeef8660fcaa0f388d).[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-mollin-29)[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel2006167-39) Although some [pyramidologists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyramidology "Pyramidology") have theorized that the [Great Pyramid of Giza](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Pyramid_of_Giza "Great Pyramid of Giza") was built with proportions related to π, this theory is not widely accepted by scholars.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-40) In the [Shulba Sutras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shulba_Sutras "Shulba Sutras") of [Indian mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_mathematics "Indian mathematics"), dating to an oral tradition from the 1st or 2nd millennium BC, approximations are given which have been variously interpreted as approximately 3.08831, 3.08833, 3.004, 3, or 3.125.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-41)

### Polygon approximation era

[![Image 25: A painting of a man studying](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Domenico-Fetti_Archimedes_1620.jpg/250px-Domenico-Fetti_Archimedes_1620.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Domenico-Fetti_Archimedes_1620.jpg)

[Archimedes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archimedes "Archimedes") developed the polygonal approach to approximating π.

[![Image 26: diagram of a hexagon and pentagon circumscribed outside a circle](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Archimedes_pi.svg/250px-Archimedes_pi.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Archimedes_pi.svg)

π can be estimated by computing the perimeters of circumscribed and inscribed polygons.

The first recorded algorithm for rigorously calculating the value of π was a geometrical approach using polygons, devised around 250 BC by the Greek mathematician [Archimedes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archimedes "Archimedes"), implementing the [method of exhaustion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Method_of_exhaustion "Method of exhaustion").[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel2006170-42) This polygonal algorithm dominated for over 1,000 years, and as a result π is sometimes referred to as Archimedes's constant.[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel2006175,_205-43) Archimedes computed upper and lower bounds of π by drawing a regular hexagon inside and outside a circle, and successively doubling the number of sides until he reached a 96-sided regular polygon. By calculating the perimeters of these polygons, he proved that ⁠223/71⁠<π<⁠22/7⁠ (that is, 3.1408 <π< 3.1429).[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-life-of-pi-44) Archimedes' upper bound of ⁠22/7⁠ may have led to a widespread popular belief that π is equal to ⁠22/7⁠.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel2006171-45) Around 150 AD, Greco-Roman scientist [Ptolemy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ptolemy "Ptolemy"), in his _[Almagest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Almagest "Almagest")_, gave a value for π of 3.1416, which he may have obtained from Archimedes or from [Apollonius of Perga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apollonius_of_Perga "Apollonius of Perga").[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel2006176-46)[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoyerMerzbach1991168-47) Mathematicians using polygonal algorithms reached 39 digits of π in 1630, a record only broken in 1699 when infinite series were used to reach 71 digits.[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-ArPI-48)

In [ancient China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_China "Ancient China"), values for π included 3.1547 (around 1 AD), ![Image 27: {\displaystyle {\sqrt {10}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dd7409b0ddbc1f90280265e7bc95dd20626ebf1b) (100 AD, approximately 3.1623), and ⁠142/45⁠ (3rd century, approximately 3.1556).[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel2006176%E2%80%93177-49) Around 265 AD, the [Cao Wei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cao_Wei "Cao Wei") mathematician [Liu Hui](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liu_Hui "Liu Hui") created a [polygon-based iterative algorithm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liu_Hui%27s_%CF%80_algorithm "Liu Hui's π algorithm"), with which he constructed a 3,072-sided polygon to approximate π as 3.1416.[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoyerMerzbach1991202-50)[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel2006177-51) Liu later invented a faster method of calculating π and obtained a value of 3.14 with a 96-sided polygon, by taking advantage of the fact that the differences in area of successive polygons form a geometric series with a factor of 4.[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoyerMerzbach1991202-50) Around 480 AD, [Zu Chongzhi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zu_Chongzhi "Zu Chongzhi") calculated that ![Image 28: {\displaystyle 3.1415926<\pi <3.1415927}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2aca8712bcbd7836f7e1db9811a1e93ef71110aa) and suggested the approximations ![Image 29: {\textstyle \pi \approx {\frac {355}{113}}=3.14159292035...}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/06692ba6599484d7d1972c90781be9b2bdd86471) and ![Image 30: {\textstyle \pi \approx {\frac {22}{7}}=3.142857142857...}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/778369e629e3921a3abeddfa34954b1022abe1e1), which he termed the _[milü](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mil%C3%BC "Milü")_ ('close ratio') and _yuelü_ ('approximate ratio') respectively, iterating with Liu Hui's algorithm up to a 12,288-sided polygon. With a correct value for its seven first decimal digits, Zu's result remained the most accurate approximation of π for the next 800 years.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel2006178-52)

The Indian astronomer [Aryabhata](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aryabhata "Aryabhata") used a value of 3.1416 in his _[Āryabhaṭīya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C4%80ryabha%E1%B9%AD%C4%ABya "Āryabhaṭīya")_ (499 AD).[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel2006179-53) Around 1220, [Fibonacci](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fibonacci "Fibonacci") computed 3.1418 using a polygonal method devised independently of Archimedes.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel2006180-54) Italian author [Dante](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dante "Dante") apparently employed the value ![Image 31: {\textstyle 3+{\frac {\sqrt {2}}{10}}\approx 3.14142}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e7acb14f07f1be2393396e80d85e48abd0da0539).[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel2006180-54)

The Persian astronomer [Jamshīd al-Kāshī](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jamsh%C4%ABd_al-K%C4%81sh%C4%AB "Jamshīd al-Kāshī") produced nine [sexagesimal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sexagesimal "Sexagesimal") digits, roughly the equivalent of 16 decimal digits, in 1424, using a polygon with ![Image 32: {\textstyle 3\times for about 180 years.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel2006182-56) French mathematician [François Viète](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Vi%C3%A8te "François Viète") in 1579 achieved nine digits with a polygon of ![Image 33: {\textstyle 3\times 2^{17}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/21c6b820448824185aa6348247f55cc2490cceb9) sides.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel2006182-56) Flemish mathematician [Adriaan van Roomen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adriaan_van_Roomen "Adriaan van Roomen") arrived at 15 decimal places in 1593.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel2006182-56) In 1596, Dutch mathematician [Ludolph van Ceulen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ludolph_van_Ceulen "Ludolph van Ceulen") reached 20 digits, a record he later increased to 35 digits (as a result, π was called the "Ludolphian number" in Germany until the early 20th century).[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel2006182%E2%80%93183-57) Dutch scientist [Willebrord Snellius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Willebrord_Snellius ### Infinite series

[![Image 34](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Comparison_pi_infinite_series.svg/330px-Comparison_pi_infinite_series.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Comparison_pi_infinite_series.svg)

Comparison of the convergence of several historical infinite series for π. _S n_ is the approximation after taking _n_ terms. Each subsequent subplot magnifies the shaded area horizontally by 10 times. [(click for detail)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Comparison_pi_infinite_series.svg "File:Comparison pi infinite series.svg")

The calculation of π was revolutionized by the development of [infinite series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinite_series "Infinite series") techniques in the 16th and 17th centuries. An infinite series is the sum of the terms of an infinite [sequence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sequence_(mathematics) "Sequence (mathematics)"). Infinite series allowed mathematicians to compute π with much greater precision than [Archimedes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archimedes "Archimedes") and others who used geometrical techniques.[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel2006185%E2%80%93191-61) Although infinite series were exploited for π most notably by European mathematicians such as [James Gregory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Gregory_(mathematician) "James Gregory (mathematician)") and [Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gottfried_Wilhelm_Leibniz "Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz"), the approach also appeared in the [Kerala school](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kerala_school_of_astronomy_and_mathematics "Kerala school of astronomy and mathematics") sometime in the 14th or 15th century.[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-Roypp-62)[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel2006185%E2%80%93186-63) Around 1500, an infinite series that could be used to compute π, written in the form of [Sanskrit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sanskrit "Sanskrit") verse, was presented in _[Tantrasamgraha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tantrasamgraha "Tantrasamgraha")_ by [Nilakantha Somayaji](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nilakantha_Somayaji "Nilakantha Somayaji").[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-Roypp-62) The series are presented without proof, but proofs are presented in the later work _[Yuktibhāṣā](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yuktibh%C4%81%E1%B9%A3%C4%81 "Yuktibhāṣā")_, published around 1530. Several infinite series are described, including series for sine (which Nilakantha attributes to [Madhava of Sangamagrama](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Madhava_of_Sangamagrama "Madhava of Sangamagrama")), cosine, and arctangent which are now sometimes referred to as [Madhava series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Madhava_series "Madhava series"). The series for arctangent is sometimes called [Gregory's series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gregory%27s_series "Gregory's series") or the Gregory–Leibniz series.[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-Roypp-62) Madhava used infinite series to estimate π to 11 digits around 1400.[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-64)[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAndrewsAskeyRoy199959-65)[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-66)

In 1593, [François Viète](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Vi%C3%A8te "François Viète") published what is now known as [Viète's formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vi%C3%A8te%27s_formula "Viète's formula"), an [infinite product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinite_product "Infinite product") (rather than an [infinite sum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinite_sum "Infinite sum"), which is more typically used in π calculations):[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-67)

![Image 35: {\displaystyle {\frac {2}{\pi }}={\frac {\sqrt {2}}{2}}\cdot {\frac {\sqrt {2+{\sqrt {2}}}}{2}}\cdot {\frac {\sqrt {2+{\sqrt {2+{\sqrt {2}}}}}}{2}}\cdots }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/471259d8063f909988b7457a3e2d9c62f73efb01)

In 1655, [John Wallis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Wallis "John Wallis") published what is now known as the [Wallis product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wallis_product "Wallis product"), also an infinite product:[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel2006187-68)

![Image 36: {\displaystyle {\frac {\pi }{2}}={\Big (}{\frac {2}{1}}\cdot {\frac {2}{3}}{\Big )}\cdot {\Big (}{\frac {4}{3}}\cdot {\frac {4}{5}}{\Big )}\cdot {\Big (}{\frac {6}{5}}\cdot {\frac {6}{7}}{\Big )}\cdot {\Big (}{\frac {8}{7}}\cdot {\frac {8}{9}}{\Big )}\cdots }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c54735394beda8b94805a2919f1f4c0e200d2b29)

[![Image 37: A formal portrait of a man, with long hair](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/GodfreyKneller-IsaacNewton-1689.jpg/250px-GodfreyKneller-IsaacNewton-1689.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:GodfreyKneller-IsaacNewton-1689.jpg)

[Isaac Newton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaac_Newton "Isaac Newton") used [infinite series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinite_series "Infinite series") to compute π to 15 digits, later writing "I am ashamed to tell you to how many figures I carried these computations".[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-Newton-69)

In the 1660s, the English scientist [Isaac Newton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaac_Newton "Isaac Newton") and German mathematician [Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gottfried_Wilhelm_Leibniz "Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz") discovered [calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus "Calculus"), which led to the development of many infinite series for approximating π. Newton himself used an arcsine series to compute a 15-digit approximation of π in 1665 or 1666, writing, "I am ashamed to tell you to how many figures I carried these computations, having no other business at the time."[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-Newton-69)

In 1671, [James Gregory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Gregory_(mathematician) "James Gregory (mathematician)"), and independently, Leibniz in 1673, discovered the [Taylor series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taylor_series "Taylor series") expansion for [arctangent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arctangent "Arctangent"):[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-Roypp-62)[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-70)[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEymardLafon200453%E2%80%9354-71)

![Image 38: {\displaystyle \arctan z=z-{\frac {z^{3}}{3}}+{\frac {z^{5}}{5}}-{\frac {z^{7}}{7}}+\cdots }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ef0e80b93f9981701b98f8e121e27c4c50955a3d)

This series, sometimes called the [Gregory–Leibniz series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gregory%27s_series "Gregory's series"), equals ![Image 39: {\textstyle {\frac {\pi }{4}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4cc79dd123359dbfd89513870537035bcbf0dd6d) when evaluated with ![Image 40: {\displaystyle z=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/078535cde78d90bfa1d9fbb2446204593a921d57).[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEymardLafon200453%E2%80%9354-71) But for ![Image 41: {\displaystyle z=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/078535cde78d90bfa1d9fbb2446204593a921d57), [it converges impractically slowly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leibniz_formula_for_%CF%80#Convergence "Leibniz formula for π") (that is, approaches the answer very gradually), taking about ten times as many terms to calculate each additional digit.[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-72)

In 1699, English mathematician [Abraham Sharp](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abraham_Sharp "Abraham Sharp") used the Gregory–Leibniz series for ![Image 42: {\textstyle z={\frac {1}{\sqrt {3}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4ae2125e0304d50f14a14f9559d3611557bbceaa) to compute π to 71 digits, breaking the previous record of 39 digits, which was set with a polygonal algorithm.[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel2006189-73)

In 1706, [John Machin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Machin "John Machin") used the Gregory–Leibniz series to produce an algorithm that converged much faster:[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-jones-74)[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-tweddle-75)[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel2006192%E2%80%93193-76)

![Image 43: {\displaystyle {\frac {\pi }{4}}=4\arctan {\frac {1}{5}}-\arctan {\frac {1}{239}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d1e27dbc5ad6b84571aaaae627e1d0080145e6a1)

Machin reached 100 digits of π with this formula.[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel200672%E2%80%9374-77) Other mathematicians created variants, now known as [Machin-like formulae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Machin-like_formula "Machin-like formula"), that were used to set several successive records for calculating digits of π.[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-78)[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel200672%E2%80%9374-77)

Isaac Newton [accelerated the convergence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Series_acceleration "Series acceleration") of the Gregory–Leibniz series in 1684 (in an unpublished work; others independently discovered the result):[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-79)

![Image 44: {\displaystyle \arctan x={\frac {x}{1+x^{2}}}+{\frac {2}{3}}{\frac {x^{3}}{(1+x^{2})^{2}}}+{\frac {2\cdot 4}{3\cdot 5}}{\frac {x^{5}}{(1+x^{2})^{3}}}+\cdots }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6282b6fd3849a778a8897b3e40e25448e427a0bc)

[Leonhard Euler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leonhard_Euler "Leonhard Euler") popularized this series in his 1755 differential calculus textbook, and later used it with Machin-like formulae, including ![Image 45: {\textstyle {\tfrac {\pi }{4}}=5\arctan {\tfrac {1}{7}}+2\arctan {\tfrac {3}{79}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/202bbce8b648f8d1be219e9ea77aa0bc21230d08) with which he computed 20 digits of π in one hour.[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-80)

Machin-like formulae remained the best-known method for calculating π well into the age of computers, and were used to set records for 250 years, culminating in a 620-digit approximation in 1946 by Daniel Ferguson– the best approximation achieved without the aid of a calculating device.[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel2006192%E2%80%93196,_205-81)

In 1844, a record was set by [Zacharias Dase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zacharias_Dase "Zacharias Dase"), who employed a Machin-like formula to calculate 200 decimals of π in his head at the behest of German mathematician [Carl Friedrich Gauss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Friedrich_Gauss "Carl Friedrich Gauss").[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel2006194%E2%80%93196-82)

In 1853, British mathematician [William Shanks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Shanks "William Shanks") calculated π to 607 digits, but made a mistake in the 528th digit, rendering all subsequent digits incorrect. Though he calculated an additional 100 digits in 1873, bringing the total up to 707, his previous mistake rendered all the new digits incorrect as well.[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-hayes-2014-83)

#### Rate of convergence

Some infinite series for π[converge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convergent_series "Convergent series") faster than others. Given the choice of two infinite series for π, mathematicians will generally use the one that converges more rapidly because faster convergence reduces the amount of computation needed to calculate π to any given accuracy.[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-Aconverge-84) A simple infinite series for π is the [Gregory–Leibniz series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leibniz_formula_for_%CF%80 "Leibniz formula for π"):[[82]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel200669%E2%80%9372-85)

![Image 46: {\displaystyle \pi ={\frac {4}{1}}-{\frac {4}{3}}+{\frac {4}{5}}-{\frac {4}{7}}+{\frac {4}{9}}-{\frac {4}{11}}+{\frac {4}{13}}-\cdots }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e9e3959cd2d0ec735e7a6a1917df784842b76706)

As individual terms of this infinite series are added to the sum, the total gradually gets closer to π, and– with a sufficient number of terms– can get as close to π as desired. It converges quite slowly, though– after 500,000 terms, it produces only five correct decimal digits of π.[[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-86)

An infinite series for π (published by Nilakantha in the 15th century) that converges more rapidly than the Gregory–Leibniz series is:[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel2006Formula_16.10,_p._223-87)[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-88)

![Image 47: {\displaystyle \pi =3+{\frac {4}{2\times 3\times 4}}-{\frac {4}{4\times 5\times 6}}+{\frac {4}{6\times 7\times 8}}-{\frac {4}{8\times 9\times 10}}+\cdots }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fdafa8bd24ce2b6fd518a3cf253ad1ef409388a6)

The following table compares the convergence rates of these two series:

| Infinite series for π | After 1st term | After 2nd term | After 3rd term | After 4th term | After 5th term | Converges to: |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| ![Image 48: {\displaystyle \pi ={\frac {4}{1}}-{\frac {4}{3}}+{\frac {4}{5}}-{\frac {4}{7}}+{\frac {4}{9}}-{\frac {4}{11}}+{\frac {4}{13}}+\cdots }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/316235e9f73d29062935aefcb128b9be8060b5cb) | 4.0000 | 2.6666 ... | 3.4666 ... | 2.8952 ... | 3.3396 ... | π = 3.1415 ... |

After five terms, the sum of the Gregory–Leibniz series is within 0.2 of the correct value of π, whereas the sum of Nilakantha's series is within 0.002 of the correct value. Nilakantha's series converges faster and is more useful for computing digits of π. Series that converge even faster include [Machin's series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Machin-like_formula "Machin-like formula") and [Chudnovsky's series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chudnovsky_algorithm "Chudnovsky algorithm"), the latter producing 14 correct decimal digits per term.[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-Aconverge-84)

### Irrationality and transcendence

Not all mathematical advances relating to π were aimed at increasing the accuracy of approximations. When Euler solved the [Basel problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basel_problem "Basel problem") in 1735, finding the exact value of the sum of the reciprocal squares, he established a connection between π and the [prime numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_number "Prime number") that later contributed to the development and study of the [Riemann zeta function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_zeta_function "Riemann zeta function"):[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPosamentierLehmann2004284-89)

![Image 50: {\displaystyle {\frac {\pi ^{2}}{6}}={\frac {1}{1^{2}}}+{\frac {1}{2^{2}}}+{\frac {1}{3^{2}}}+{\frac {1}{4^{2}}}+\cdots }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/edf829fbf86ae73080bce0a95c497001b8d15fb1)

Swiss scientist [Johann Heinrich Lambert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johann_Heinrich_Lambert "Johann Heinrich Lambert") in 1768 proved that π is [irrational](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irrational_number "Irrational number"), meaning it is not equal to the quotient of any two integers.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel20065-15)[Lambert's proof](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proof_that_%CF%80_is_irrational "Proof that π is irrational") exploited a continued-fraction representation of the tangent function.[[87]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-90) French mathematician [Adrien-Marie Legendre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adrien-Marie_Legendre "Adrien-Marie Legendre") proved in 1794 that π 2 is also irrational. In 1882, German mathematician [Ferdinand von Lindemann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ferdinand_von_Lindemann "Ferdinand von Lindemann") proved that π is [transcendental](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transcendental_number "Transcendental number"),[[88]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-91) confirming a conjecture made by both [Legendre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adrien-Marie_Legendre "Adrien-Marie Legendre") and Euler.[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel2006196-92)[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-93) Hardy and Wright states that "the proofs were afterwards modified and simplified by Hilbert, Hurwitz, and other writers".[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-94)

### Adoption of the symbol π

[![Image 51](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/William_Jones%2C_the_Mathematician.jpg/250px-William_Jones%2C_the_Mathematician.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:William_Jones,_the_Mathematician.jpg)

The earliest known use of the Greek letter π to represent the ratio of a circle's circumference to its diameter was by Welsh mathematician [William Jones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Jones_(mathematician) "William Jones (mathematician)") in 1706.

[![Image 52](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Leonhard_Euler.jpg/250px-Leonhard_Euler.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Leonhard_Euler.jpg)

[Leonhard Euler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leonhard_Euler "Leonhard Euler") popularized the use of the Greek letter π in works he published in 1736 and 1748.

The first recorded use of the symbol π in circle geometry is in [Oughtred's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Oughtred "William Oughtred")_Clavis Mathematicae_ (1648),[[92]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-95)[[93]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel2006166-96) where the [Greek letters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_letters "Greek letters")π and _δ_ were combined into the fraction ⁠![Image 53: {\displaystyle {\tfrac {\pi }{\delta }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/07042fa248fd5ceaffdd918e7291ab50ca86bf79)⁠ for denoting the ratios [semiperimeter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semiperimeter "Semiperimeter") to [semidiameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semidiameter "Semidiameter") and perimeter to diameter, that is, what is presently denoted as π.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-firstPi-1)[[94]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-Cajori-2007-97)[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-Smith-1958-98)[[96]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-99) (Before then, mathematicians sometimes used letters such as _c_ or _p_ instead.[[93]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel2006166-96)) [Barrow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaac_Barrow "Isaac Barrow") likewise used the same notation,[[97]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-100) while [Gregory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Gregory_(mathematician) "David Gregory (mathematician)") instead used ![Image 54: {\textstyle {\frac {\pi }{\rho }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f62f00c9d88b9d6b7d4401227a9289982b4820a6) to represent 6.28... .[[98]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-101)[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-Smith-1958-98)

The earliest known use of the Greek letter π alone to represent the ratio of a circle's circumference to its diameter was by Welsh mathematician [William Jones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Jones_(mathematician) "William Jones (mathematician)") in his 1706 work _Synopsis Palmariorum Matheseos; or, a New Introduction to the Mathematics_.[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-jones-74)[[99]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel2006165-102) The Greek letter appears on p.243 in the phrase "![Image 55: {\textstyle {\tfrac {1}{2}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2558d30e006729499201220e98f83888914bef12) Periphery (π)", calculated for a circle with radius one. However, Jones writes that his equations for π are from the "ready pen of the truly ingenious Mr. [John Machin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Machin "John Machin")", leading to speculation that Machin may have employed the Greek letter before Jones.[[93]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel2006166-96) Jones' notation was not immediately adopted by other mathematicians, with the fraction notation still being used as late as 1767.[[94]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-Cajori-2007-97)[[100]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-103)

[Euler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler "Euler") started using the single-letter form beginning with his 1727 _Essay Explaining the Properties of Air_, though he used _π_ = 6.28..., the ratio of periphery to radius, in this and some later writing.[[101]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-104) Euler first used π = 3.14... in his 1736 work _[Mechanica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanica "Mechanica")_,[[102]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-105) and continued in his widely read 1748 work _[Introductio in analysin infinitorum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Introductio\_in\_analysin\_infinitorum "Introductio in analysin infinitorum")_ (he wrote: "for the sake of brevity we will write this number as π; thus π is equal to half the circumference of a circle of radius 1").[[103]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-106) Because Euler corresponded heavily with other mathematicians in Europe, the use of the Greek letter spread rapidly, and the practice was universally adopted thereafter in the [Western world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_world "Western world"),[[93]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel2006166-96) though the definition still varied between 3.14... and 6.28... as late as 1761.[[104]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-107)

Modern quest for more digits
----------------------------

### Motives for computing π

[![Image 56](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Record_pi_approximations.svg/330px-Record_pi_approximations.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Record_pi_approximations.svg)

As mathematicians discovered new algorithms, and computers became available, the number of known decimal digits of π increased dramatically. The vertical scale is [logarithmic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logarithmic_scale "Logarithmic scale").

For most numerical calculations involving π, a handful of digits provide sufficient precision. According to Jörg Arndt and Christoph Haenel, thirty-nine digits are sufficient to perform most [cosmological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmological "Cosmological") calculations, because that is the accuracy necessary to calculate the circumference of the [observable universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe "Observable universe") with a precision of one atom. Accounting for additional digits needed to compensate for computational [round-off errors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Round-off_error "Round-off error"), Arndt concludes that a few hundred digits would suffice for any scientific application. Despite this, people have worked strenuously to compute π to thousands and millions of digits.[[105]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel200617%E2%80%9319-108) This effort may be partly ascribed to the human compulsion to break records, and such achievements with π often make headlines around the world.[[106]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-109) They also have practical benefits, such as testing [supercomputers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supercomputer "Supercomputer"), testing numerical analysis algorithms (including [high-precision multiplication algorithms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication_algorithm#Fast_multiplication_algorithms_for_large_inputs "Multiplication algorithm")) –and within pure mathematics itself, providing data for evaluating the randomness of the digits of π.[[107]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel200617%E2%80%9318-110)[[108]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-111)

### Computer era and iterative algorithms

The development of computers in the mid-20th century again revolutionized the hunt for digits of π. Mathematicians [John Wrench](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Wrench "John Wrench") and Levi Smith reached 1,120 digits in 1949 using a desk calculator.[[109]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel2006205-112) Using an [inverse tangent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverse_tangent "Inverse tangent") (arctan) infinite series, a team led by George Reitwiesner and [John von Neumann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_von_Neumann "John von Neumann") that same year achieved 2,037 digits with a calculation that took 70 hours of computer time on the [ENIAC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ENIAC "ENIAC") computer.[[110]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel2006197-113)[[111]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-114) The record, always relying on an arctan series, was broken repeatedly (3089 digits in 1955,[[112]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-115) 7,480 digits in 1957; 10,000 digits in 1958; 100,000 digits in 1961) until 1 million digits was reached in 1973.[[110]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel2006197-113)

Two additional developments around 1980 once again accelerated the ability to compute π. First, the discovery of new [iterative algorithms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iterative_algorithm "Iterative algorithm") for computing π, which were much faster than the infinite series; and second, the invention of [fast multiplication algorithms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication_algorithm#Fast_multiplication_algorithms_for_large_inputs "Multiplication algorithm") that could multiply large numbers very rapidly.[[113]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel200615%E2%80%9317-116) Such algorithms are particularly important in modern π computations because most of the computer's time is devoted to multiplication.[[114]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel2006131-117) They include the [Karatsuba algorithm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karatsuba_algorithm "Karatsuba algorithm"), [Toom–Cook multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toom%E2%80%93Cook_multiplication "Toom–Cook multiplication"), and [Fourier transform-based methods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/FFT_multiplication#Fourier_transform_methods "FFT multiplication").[[115]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel2006132,_140-118)

The iterative algorithms were independently published in 1975–1976 by physicist [Eugene Salamin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eugene_Salamin_(mathematician) "Eugene Salamin (mathematician)") and scientist [Richard Brent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Brent_(scientist) "Richard Brent (scientist)").[[116]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel200687-119) These avoid reliance on infinite series. An iterative algorithm repeats a specific calculation, each iteration using the outputs from prior steps as its inputs, and produces a result in each step that converges to the desired value. The approach was actually invented over 160 years earlier by [Carl Friedrich Gauss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Friedrich_Gauss "Carl Friedrich Gauss"), in what is now termed the [arithmetic–geometric mean method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AGM_method "AGM method") (AGM method) or [Gauss–Legendre algorithm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauss%E2%80%93Legendre_algorithm "Gauss–Legendre algorithm").[[116]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel200687-119) As modified by Salamin and Brent, it is also referred to as the Brent–Salamin algorithm.

The iterative algorithms were widely used after 1980 because they are faster than infinite series algorithms: whereas infinite series typically increase the number of correct digits additively in successive terms, iterative algorithms generally _multiply_ the number of correct digits at each step. For example, the Brent–Salamin algorithm doubles the number of digits in each iteration. In 1984, brothers [John](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jonathan_Borwein "Jonathan Borwein") and [Peter Borwein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Borwein "Peter Borwein") produced an iterative algorithm that quadruples the number of digits in each step; and in 1987, one that increases the number of digits five times in each step.[[117]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-120) Iterative methods were used by Japanese mathematician [Yasumasa Kanada](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yasumasa_Kanada "Yasumasa Kanada") to set several records for computing π between 1995 and 2002.[[118]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-Background-121) This rapid convergence comes at a price: the iterative algorithms require significantly more memory than infinite series.[[118]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-Background-121)

### Rapidly convergent series

[![Image 57: Photo portrait of a man](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Srinivasa_Ramanujan_-_OPC_-_2_%28cleaned%29.jpg/250px-Srinivasa_Ramanujan_-_OPC_-_2_%28cleaned%29.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Srinivasa_Ramanujan_-_OPC_-_2_(cleaned).jpg)

[Srinivasa Ramanujan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Srinivasa_Ramanujan "Srinivasa Ramanujan"), working in isolation in India, produced many innovative series for computing π.

Modern π calculators do not use iterative algorithms exclusively. New infinite series were discovered in the 1980s and 1990s that are as fast as iterative algorithms, yet are simpler and less memory intensive.[[118]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-Background-121) The fast iterative algorithms were anticipated in 1914, when Indian mathematician [Srinivasa Ramanujan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Srinivasa_Ramanujan "Srinivasa Ramanujan") published dozens of innovative new formulae for π, remarkable for their elegance, mathematical depth and rapid convergence.[[119]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel2006103%E2%80%93104-122) One of his formulae, based on [modular equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modular_equation "Modular equation"), is

![Image 58: {\displaystyle {\frac {1}{\pi }}={\frac {2{\sqrt {2}}}{9801}}\sum _{k=0}^{\infty }{\frac {(4k)!(1103+26390k)}{k!^{4}\left(396^{4k}\right)}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0993bfb34b91c895c4a55899e71b875dc8d4dd3b)

This series converges much more rapidly than most arctan series, including Machin's formula.[[120]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel2006104-123)[Bill Gosper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bill_Gosper "Bill Gosper") was the first to use it for advances in the calculation of π, setting a record of 17 million digits in 1985.[[121]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel2006104,_206-124) Ramanujan's formulae anticipated the modern algorithms developed by the Borwein brothers ([Jonathan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jonathan_Borwein "Jonathan Borwein") and [Peter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Borwein "Peter Borwein")) and the [Chudnovsky brothers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chudnovsky_brothers "Chudnovsky brothers").[[122]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel2006110%E2%80%93111-125) The [Chudnovsky formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chudnovsky_algorithm "Chudnovsky algorithm") developed in 1987 is

![Image 59: {\displaystyle {\frac {1}{\pi }}={\frac {\sqrt {10005}}{4270934400}}\sum _{k=0}^{\infty }{\frac {(6k)!(13591409+545140134k)}{(3k)!\,k!^{3}(-640320)^{3k}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/79ab7f20d9e679a0ab580eb0d1e63bfd7ea0767a)

It produces about 14 digits of π per term[[123]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEymardLafon2004254-126) and has been used for several record-setting π calculations, including the first to surpass 1 billion (10 9) digits in 1989 by the Chudnovsky brothers, 10 trillion (10 13) digits in 2011 by Alexander Yee and Shigeru Kondo,[[124]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-NW-127) and 100 trillion digits by [Emma Haruka Iwao](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emma_Haruka_Iwao "Emma Haruka Iwao") in 2022.[[125]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-128)[[126]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-129) For similar formulae, see also the [Ramanujan–Sato series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ramanujan%E2%80%93Sato_series "Ramanujan–Sato series").

In 2006, mathematician [Simon Plouffe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simon_Plouffe "Simon Plouffe") used the PSLQ [integer relation algorithm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer_relation_algorithm "Integer relation algorithm")[[127]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-130) to generate several new formulae for π, conforming to the following template:

![Image 60: {\displaystyle \pi ^{k}=\sum _{n=1}^{\infty }{\frac {1}{n^{k}}}\left({\frac {a}{q^{n}-1}}+{\frac {b}{q^{2n}-1}}+{\frac {c}{q^{4n}-1}}\right),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/948b735a39a1bbf9a39ba0b6bd7dc63bddcd4351)

where _q_ is [_e_ _π_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gelfond%27s_constant "Gelfond's constant") (Gelfond's constant), _k_ is an [odd number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Odd_number "Odd number"), and _a_, _b_, _c_ are certain rational numbers that Plouffe computed.[[128]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-131)

### Monte Carlo methods

[![Image 61: Needles of length ℓ scattered on stripes with width t](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Buffon_needle.svg/131px-Buffon_needle.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Buffon_needle.svg)

[![Image 62: Thousands of dots randomly covering a square and a circle inscribed in the square.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Pi_30K.gif/120px-Pi_30K.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Pi_30K.gif)

Random dots are placed on a square and a circle inscribed inside.

[Monte Carlo methods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monte_Carlo_methods "Monte Carlo methods"), which evaluate the results of multiple random trials, can be used to create approximations of π.[[129]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel200639-132)[Buffon's needle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buffon%27s_needle "Buffon's needle") is one such technique: If a needle of length _ℓ_ is dropped _n_ times on a surface on which parallel lines are drawn _t_ units apart, and if _x_ of those times it comes to rest crossing a line (_x_>0), then one may approximate π based on the counts:[[130]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-bn-133)

![Image 63: {\displaystyle \pi \approx {\frac {2n\ell }{xt}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e72a1af2e84df27e3b491d5ffcf140960d0b3af)

Another Monte Carlo method for computing π is to draw a circle inscribed in a square, and randomly place dots in the square. The ratio of dots inside the circle to the total number of dots will approximately equal π/4.[[131]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-134)

[![Image 64](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/e4/Five_random_walks.png/250px-Five_random_walks.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Five_random_walks.png)

Five random walks with 200 steps. The sample mean of |_W_ 200| is _μ_ = 56/5, and so 2(200)_μ_−2 ≈ 3.19 is within 0.05 of π.

Another way to calculate π using probability is to start with a [random walk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Random_walk "Random walk"), generated by a sequence of (fair) coin tosses: independent [random variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Random_variable "Random variable")_X k_ such that _X k_ ∈ {−1,1} with equal probabilities. The associated random walk is

![Image 65: {\displaystyle W_{n}=\sum _{k=1}^{n}X_{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ff50676493705f91fc45cb5ab160083bbf7aa669)

so that, for each n, _W n_ is drawn from a shifted and scaled [binomial distribution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binomial_distribution "Binomial distribution"). As n varies, _W n_ defines a (discrete) [stochastic process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stochastic_process "Stochastic process"). Then π can be calculated by[[132]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-135)

![Image 66: {\displaystyle \pi =\lim _{n\to \infty }{\frac {2n}{E[|W_{n}|]^{2}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a691be63815c6b7d9fe15070ae98039d9c1d0384)

This Monte Carlo method is independent of any relation to circles, and is a consequence of the [central limit theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_limit_theorem "Central limit theorem"), discussed [below](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#Gaussian_integrals).

These Monte Carlo methods for approximating π are very slow compared to other methods, and do not provide any information on the exact number of digits that are obtained. Thus they are never used to approximate π when speed or accuracy is desired.[[133]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-136)

### Spigot algorithms

Two algorithms were discovered in 1995 that opened up new avenues of research into π. They are called [spigot algorithms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spigot_algorithm "Spigot algorithm") because, like water dripping from a [spigot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tap_(valve) "Tap (valve)"), they produce single digits of π that are not reused after they are calculated.[[134]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel200677%E2%80%9384-137)[[135]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-Gibbons-138) This is in contrast to infinite series or iterative algorithms, which retain and use all intermediate digits until the final result is produced.[[134]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel200677%E2%80%9384-137)

Mathematicians [Stan Wagon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stan_Wagon "Stan Wagon") and Stanley Rabinowitz produced a simple spigot algorithm in 1995.[[135]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-Gibbons-138)[[136]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel200677-139)[[137]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-140) Its speed is comparable to arctan algorithms, but not as fast as iterative algorithms.[[136]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel200677-139)

Another spigot algorithm, the [BBP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bailey%E2%80%93Borwein%E2%80%93Plouffe_formula "Bailey–Borwein–Plouffe formula")[digit extraction algorithm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digit_extraction_algorithm "Digit extraction algorithm"), was discovered in 1995 by Simon Plouffe:[[138]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel2006117,_126%E2%80%93128-141)[[139]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-bbpf-142)![Image 67: {\displaystyle \pi =\sum _{k=0}^{\infty }{\frac {1}{16^{k}}}\left({\frac {4}{8k+1}}-{\frac {2}{8k+4}}-{\frac {1}{8k+5}}-{\frac {1}{8k+6}}\right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c092c788f1ff39174a3f78c5a329d5aa83ca2add)

This formula, unlike others before it, can produce any individual [hexadecimal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hexadecimal "Hexadecimal") digit of π without calculating all the preceding digits.[[138]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel2006117,_126%E2%80%93128-141) Individual binary digits may be extracted from individual hexadecimal digits, and [octal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octal "Octal") digits can be extracted from one or two hexadecimal digits. An important application of digit extraction algorithms is to validate new claims of record π computations: After a new record is claimed, the decimal result is converted to hexadecimal, and then a digit extraction algorithm is used to calculate several randomly selected hexadecimal digits near the end; if they match, this provides a measure of confidence that the entire computation is correct.[[124]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-NW-127)

Between 1998 and 2000, the [distributed computing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distributed_computing "Distributed computing") project [PiHex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PiHex "PiHex") used [Bellard's formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bellard%27s_formula "Bellard's formula") (a modification of the BBP algorithm) to compute the quadrillionth (10 15 th) bit of π, which turned out to be 0.[[140]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-143) In September 2010, a [Yahoo!](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yahoo! "Yahoo!") employee used the company's [Hadoop](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apache_Hadoop "Apache Hadoop") application on one thousand computers over a 23-day period to compute 256 [bits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bit "Bit") of π at the two-quadrillionth (2×10 15 th) bit, which also happens to be zero.[[141]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-144)

In 2022, Plouffe found a base-10 algorithm for calculating digits of π.[[142]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-145)

Role and characterizations in mathematics
-----------------------------------------

Because π is closely related to the circle, it is found in [many formulae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_formulae_involving_%CF%80 "List of formulae involving π") from the fields of geometry and trigonometry, particularly those concerning circles, spheres, or ellipses. Other branches of science, such as statistics, physics, [Fourier analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourier_analysis "Fourier analysis"), and number theory, also include π in some of their important formulae.

### Geometry and trigonometry

[![Image 68: A diagram of a circle with a square coving the circle's upper right quadrant.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Circle_Area.svg/250px-Circle_Area.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Circle_Area.svg)

The area of the circle equals π times the shaded area. The area of the [unit circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_circle "Unit circle") is π.

π appears in formulae for areas and volumes of geometrical shapes based on circles, such as [ellipses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse "Ellipse"), [spheres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere "Sphere"), [cones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cone_(geometry) "Cone (geometry)"), and [tori](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torus "Torus").[[143]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-146) Below are some of the more common formulae that involve π.[[144]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-147)

*   The circumference of a circle with radius _r_ is 2π _r_.[[145]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-148)
*   The [area of a circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area_of_a_disk "Area of a disk") with radius _r_ is π _r_ 2.
*   The area of an ellipse with semi-major axis _a_ and semi-minor axis _b_ is π _ab_.[[146]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-149)
*   The volume of a sphere with radius _r_ is ⁠4/3⁠π _r_ 3.
*   The surface area of a sphere with radius _r_ is 4π _r_ 2.

Some of the formulae above are special cases of the volume of the [_n_-dimensional ball](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/N-ball "N-ball") and the surface area of its boundary, the [(_n_−1)-dimensional sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/N-sphere "N-sphere"), given [below](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#The_gamma_function_and_Stirling's_approximation).

Apart from circles, there are other [curves of constant width](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curve_of_constant_width "Curve of constant width"). By [Barbier's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barbier%27s_theorem "Barbier's theorem"), every curve of constant width has perimeter π times its width. The [Reuleaux triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reuleaux_triangle "Reuleaux triangle") (formed by the intersection of three circles with the sides of an [equilateral triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equilateral_triangle "Equilateral triangle") as their radii) has the smallest possible area for its width and the circle the largest. There also exist non-circular [smooth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smoothness "Smoothness") and even [algebraic curves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_curve "Algebraic curve") of constant width.[[147]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-150)

[Definite integrals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral "Integral") that describe circumference, area, or volume of shapes generated by circles typically have values that involve π. For example, an integral that specifies half the area of a circle of radius one is given by:[[148]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-151)

![Image 69: {\displaystyle \int _{-1}^{1}{\sqrt {1-x^{2}}}\,dx={\frac {\pi }{2}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1955a15a053adcb91a83af56ea651085c2c18353)

In that integral, the function ![Image 70: {\displaystyle {\sqrt {1-x^{2}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/875fba356d7f4befcacf6573012100b3ef95cd19) represents the height over the ![Image 71: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4)-axis of a [semicircle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semicircle "Semicircle") (the [square root](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_root "Square root") is a consequence of the [Pythagorean theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagorean_theorem "Pythagorean theorem")), and the integral computes the area below the semicircle. The existence of such integrals makes π an [algebraic period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Period_(algebraic_geometry) "Period (algebraic geometry)").[[149]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-152)

### Units of angle

[![Image 72: Diagram showing graphs of functions](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Sine_cosine_one_period.svg/500px-Sine_cosine_one_period.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Sine_cosine_one_period.svg)

[Sine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sine "Sine") and [cosine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosine "Cosine") functions repeat with period 2 π.

The [trigonometric functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trigonometric_function "Trigonometric function") rely on angles, and mathematicians generally use [radians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radian "Radian") as units of measurement. π plays an important role in angles measured in radians, which are defined so that a complete circle spans an angle of 2 π radians. The angle measure of 180° is equal to π radians, and 1° = π/180 radians.[[150]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAbramson2014[httpsopenstaxorgbooksprecalculuspages5-1-angles_Section_5.1:_Angles]-153)

Common trigonometric functions have periods that are multiples of π; for example, sine and cosine have period 2 π, so for any angle _θ_ and any integer _k_,[[151]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBronshte%C4%ADnSemendiaev1971210%E2%80%93211-154)![Image 73: {\displaystyle \sin \theta =\sin \left(\theta +2\pi k\right){\text{ and }}\cos \theta =\cos \left(\theta +2\pi k\right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f3b3c88ab8201f4f0c6f18dd73cb951ebcd07e2a)

### Eigenvalues

[![Image 74](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Harmonic_partials_on_strings.svg/250px-Harmonic_partials_on_strings.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Harmonic_partials_on_strings.svg)

The [overtones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Overtone "Overtone") of a vibrating string are [eigenfunctions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eigenfunction "Eigenfunction") of the second derivative, and form a [harmonic progression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harmonic_series_(music) "Harmonic series (music)"). The associated eigenvalues form the [arithmetic progression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic_progression "Arithmetic progression") of integer multiples of π.

Many of the appearances of π in the formulae of mathematics and the sciences have to do with its close relationship with geometry. However, π also appears in many natural situations having apparently nothing to do with geometry.

In many applications, it plays a distinguished role as an [eigenvalue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eigenvalue "Eigenvalue"). For example, an idealized [vibrating string](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vibrating_string "Vibrating string") can be modelled as the graph of a function _f_ on the unit interval [0, 1], with [fixed ends](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boundary_conditions "Boundary conditions")_f_(0) = _f_(1) = 0. The modes of vibration of the string are solutions of the [differential equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_equation "Differential equation")![Image 75: {\displaystyle f''(x)+\lambda f(x)=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dd94cf1cb3f339145da51a6494cf8ea95c5644c6), or ![Image 76: {\displaystyle f''(t)=-\lambda f(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/36bfe6a78ecda33af55f241344a0ce2cc5862794). Thus λ is an eigenvalue of the second derivative [operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_operator "Differential operator")![Image 77: {\displaystyle f\mapsto f''}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8d92f8b043c42cc84705fe283f2e325b8df6b20a), and is constrained by [Sturm–Liouville theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sturm%E2%80%93Liouville_theory "Sturm–Liouville theory") to take on only certain specific values. It must be positive, since the operator is [negative definite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Negative_definite "Negative definite"), so it is convenient to write _λ_ = _ν_ 2, where _ν_> 0 is called the [wavenumber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wavenumber "Wavenumber"). Then _f_(_x_) = sin(_π_ _x_) satisfies the boundary conditions and the differential equation with _ν_ = _π_.[[152]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-155)

The value π is, in fact, the _least_ such value of the wavenumber, and is associated with the [fundamental mode](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_mode "Fundamental mode") of vibration of the string. One way to show this is by estimating the [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy "Energy"), which satisfies [Wirtinger's inequality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wirtinger%27s_inequality_for_functions "Wirtinger's inequality for functions"):[[153]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDymMcKean197247-156) for a function ![Image 78: {\displaystyle f:[0,1]\to \mathbb {C} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f0468f15485d405d64092878cda0fc0cbdab2f62) with _f_(0) = _f_(1) = 0 and _f_, _f_′ both [square integrable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_integrable "Square integrable"), we have:

![Image 79: {\displaystyle \pi ^{2}\int _{0}^{1}|f(x)|^{2}\,dx\leq \int _{0}^{1}|f'(x)|^{2}\,dx,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9021233d9bdca65d39dc801347f5901f04e55204)

with equality precisely when _f_ is a multiple of sin(π _x_). Here π appears as an optimal constant in Wirtinger's inequality, and it follows that it is the smallest wavenumber, using the [variational characterization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variational_theorem "Variational theorem") of the eigenvalue. As a consequence, π is the smallest [singular value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Singular_value "Singular value") of the derivative operator on the space of functions on [0, 1] vanishing at both endpoints (the [Sobolev space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sobolev_space "Sobolev space")![Image 80: {\displaystyle H_{0}^{1}[0,1]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7113d194de39d54621e9da47782ad5263b9f1790)).

### Inequalities

[![Image 81](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Sir_William_Thompson_illustration_of_Carthage.png/250px-Sir_William_Thompson_illustration_of_Carthage.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Sir_William_Thompson_illustration_of_Carthage.png)

The [ancient city of Carthage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Carthage "Ancient Carthage") was the solution to an isoperimetric problem, according to a legend recounted by [Lord Kelvin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lord_Kelvin "Lord Kelvin"):[[154]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-157) those lands bordering the sea that [Queen Dido](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dido "Dido") could enclose on all other sides within a single given oxhide, cut into strips.

The number π serves appears in similar eigenvalue problems in higher-dimensional analysis. As mentioned [above](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#Definition), it can be characterized via its role as the best constant in the [isoperimetric inequality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isoperimetric_inequality "Isoperimetric inequality"): the area A enclosed by a plane [Jordan curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jordan_curve "Jordan curve") of perimeter P satisfies the inequality

![Image 82: {\displaystyle 4\pi A\leq P^{2},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2da151eb3d06c6538dfa721d85c36e2b052a5114)

and equality is clearly achieved for the circle, since in that case _A_ = π _r_ 2 and _P_ = 2π _r_.[[155]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-158)

Ultimately, as a consequence of the isoperimetric inequality, π appears in the optimal constant for the critical [Sobolev inequality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sobolev_inequality "Sobolev inequality") in _n_ dimensions, which thus characterizes the role of π in many physical phenomena as well, for example those of classical [potential theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potential_theory "Potential theory").[[156]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-159)[[157]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-160)[[158]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-161) In two dimensions, the critical Sobolev inequality is

![Image 83: {\displaystyle 2\pi \|f\|_{2}\leq \|\nabla f\|_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a80eaaf07fddda0c919000c07fda359208e10cf9)

for _f_ a smooth function with compact support in **R**2, ![Image 84: {\displaystyle \nabla f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b7b4d6de89b52c5a5e6e1583cb63eaee263e307b) is the [gradient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gradient "Gradient") of _f_, and ![Image 85: {\displaystyle \|f\|_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7d0c64fd6ef8f74eac7ace4d9b3db3f4d148385d) and ![Image 86: {\displaystyle \|\nabla f\|_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7de8dc3d0605c979ebae27c6791e3df7b1c19887) refer respectively to the [L 2 and L 1-norm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lp_space "Lp space"). The Sobolev inequality is equivalent to the isoperimetric inequality (in any dimension), with the same best constants.

Wirtinger's inequality also generalizes to higher-dimensional [Poincaré inequalities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poincar%C3%A9_inequality "Poincaré inequality") that provide best constants for the [Dirichlet energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dirichlet_energy "Dirichlet energy") of an _n_-dimensional membrane. Specifically, π is the greatest constant such that

![Image 87: {\displaystyle \pi \leq {\frac {\left(\int _{G}|\nabla u|^{2}\right)^{1/2}}{\left(\int _{G}|u|^{2}\right)^{1/2}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/72b72bae66777c4041fd32d69be2a43a740bd885)

for all [convex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_set "Convex set") subsets _G_ of **R**_n_ of diameter 1, and square-integrable functions _u_ on _G_ of mean zero.[[159]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-162) Just as Wirtinger's inequality is the [variational](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus_of_variations "Calculus of variations") form of the [Dirichlet eigenvalue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dirichlet_eigenvalue "Dirichlet eigenvalue") problem in one dimension, the Poincaré inequality is the variational form of the [Neumann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neumann_problem "Neumann problem") eigenvalue problem, in any dimension.

### Fourier transform and Heisenberg uncertainty principle

[![Image 88](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Animation_of_Heisenberg_geodesic.gif/250px-Animation_of_Heisenberg_geodesic.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Animation_of_Heisenberg_geodesic.gif)

An animation of a [geodesic in the Heisenberg group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heisenberg_group#As_a_sub-Riemannian_manifold "Heisenberg group")

The constant π also appears as a critical spectral parameter in the [Fourier transform](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourier_transform "Fourier transform"). This is the [integral transform](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral_transform "Integral transform"), that takes a complex-valued integrable function _f_ on the real line to the function defined as:

![Image 89: {\displaystyle {\hat {f}}(\xi )=\int _{-\infty }^{\infty }f(x)e^{-2\pi ix\xi }\,dx.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4a78cc05a0945443f61906a7c66822e38839e84f)

Although there are several different conventions for the Fourier transform and its inverse, any such convention must involve π _somewhere_. The above is the most canonical definition, however, giving the unique unitary operator on _L_ 2 that is also an algebra homomorphism of _L_ 1 to _L_∞.[[160]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-163)

The [Heisenberg uncertainty principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heisenberg_uncertainty_principle "Heisenberg uncertainty principle") also contains the number π. The uncertainty principle gives a sharp lower bound on the extent to which it is possible to localize a function both in space and in frequency: with our conventions for the Fourier transform,

![Image 90: {\displaystyle \left(\int _{-\infty }^{\infty }x^{2}|f(x)|^{2}\,dx\right)\left(\int _{-\infty }^{\infty }\xi ^{2}|{\hat {f}}(\xi )|^{2}\,d\xi \right)\geq \left({\frac {1}{4\pi }}\int _{-\infty }^{\infty }|f(x)|^{2}\,dx\right)^{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/08ed97af513c67ad7eb0f44a51c76696255f16ab)

The physical consequence, about the uncertainty in simultaneous position and momentum observations of a [quantum mechanical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanical "Quantum mechanical") system, is [discussed below](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#Describing_physical_phenomena). The appearance of π in the formulae of Fourier analysis is ultimately a consequence of the [Stone–von Neumann theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stone%E2%80%93von_Neumann_theorem "Stone–von Neumann theorem"), asserting the uniqueness of the [Schrödinger representation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schr%C3%B6dinger_representation "Schrödinger representation") of the [Heisenberg group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heisenberg_group "Heisenberg group").[[161]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-howe-164)

### Gaussian integrals

[![Image 91](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/E%5E%28-x%5E2%29.svg/250px-E%5E%28-x%5E2%29.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:E%5E(-x%5E2).svg)

A graph of the [Gaussian function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaussian_function "Gaussian function")_ƒ_(_x_) = _e_−_x_ 2. The coloured region between the function and the _x_-axis has area √π.

The fields of [probability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability "Probability") and [statistics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistics "Statistics") frequently use the [normal distribution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_distribution "Normal distribution") as a simple model for complex phenomena; for example, scientists generally assume that the observational error in most experiments follows a normal distribution.[[162]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-165) The [Gaussian function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaussian_function "Gaussian function"), which is the [probability density function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability_density_function "Probability density function") of the normal distribution with [mean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mean "Mean")μ and [standard deviation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_deviation "Standard deviation")σ, naturally contains π:[[163]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-GaussProb-166)

![Image 92: {\displaystyle f(x)={1 \over \sigma {\sqrt {2\pi }}}\,e^{-(x-\mu )^{2}/(2\sigma ^{2})}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/824d182749fb4097ca7bf06c7435c066756892db)

The factor of ![Image 93: {\displaystyle {\tfrac {1}{\sqrt {2\pi }}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7ef676eb866fd962ce8fe724b168c57499b2d8af) makes the area under the graph of _f_ equal to one, as is required for a probability distribution. This follows from a [change of variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integration_by_substitution "Integration by substitution") in the [Gaussian integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaussian_integral "Gaussian integral"):[[163]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-GaussProb-166)

![Image 94: {\displaystyle \int _{-\infty }^{\infty }e^{-u^{2}}\,du={\sqrt {\pi }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2a3890ce940b1efd925d23c908144db3c4049dc8)

which says that the area under the basic [bell curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bell_curve "Bell curve") in the figure is equal to the square root of π.

The [central limit theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_limit_theorem "Central limit theorem") explains the central role of normal distributions, and thus of π, in probability and statistics. This theorem is ultimately connected with the [spectral characterization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#Fourier_transform_and_Heisenberg_uncertainty_principle) of π as the eigenvalue associated with the Heisenberg uncertainty principle, and the fact that equality holds in the uncertainty principle only for the Gaussian function.[[164]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDymMcKean1972Section_2.7-167) Equivalently, π is the unique constant making the Gaussian normal distribution _e_−π _x_ 2 equal to its own Fourier transform.[[165]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-168) Indeed, according to [Howe (1980)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#CITEREFHowe1980), the "whole business" of establishing the fundamental theorems of Fourier analysis reduces to the Gaussian integral.[[161]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-howe-164)

### Topology

[![Image 95](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Order-7_triangular_tiling.svg/250px-Order-7_triangular_tiling.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Order-7_triangular_tiling.svg)

[Uniformization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uniformization_theorem "Uniformization theorem") of the [Klein quartic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Klein_quartic "Klein quartic"), a surface of [genus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genus_(mathematics) "Genus (mathematics)") three and Euler characteristic −4, as a quotient of the [hyperbolic plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poincar%C3%A9_disk_model "Poincaré disk model") by the [symmetry group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry_group "Symmetry group")[PSL(2,7)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PSL(2,7) "PSL(2,7)") of the [Fano plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fano_plane "Fano plane"). The hyperbolic area of a fundamental domain is 8π, by Gauss–Bonnet.

The constant π appears in the [Gauss–Bonnet formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauss%E2%80%93Bonnet_formula "Gauss–Bonnet formula") which relates the [differential geometry of surfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry_of_surfaces "Differential geometry of surfaces") to their [topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology "Topology"). Specifically, if a [compact](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compact_space "Compact space") surface Σ has [Gauss curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauss_curvature "Gauss curvature")_K_, then

![Image 96: {\displaystyle \int _{\Sigma }K\,dA=2\pi \chi (\Sigma )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/00c846ab9590f095656ecf41398c7d91b9f3a00e)

where _χ_(Σ) is the [Euler characteristic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler_characteristic "Euler characteristic"), which is an integer.[[166]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-169) An example is the surface area of a sphere _S_ of curvature 1 (so that its [radius of curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radius_of_curvature "Radius of curvature"), which coincides with its radius, is also 1.) The Euler characteristic of a sphere can be computed from its [homology groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homology_group "Homology group") and is found to be equal to two. Thus we have

![Image 97: {\displaystyle A(S)=\int _{S}1\,dA=2\pi \cdot 2=4\pi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f541185019255fc11e6ef909d681e99b9490cf88)

reproducing the formula for the surface area of a sphere of radius 1.

The constant appears in many other integral formulae in topology, in particular, those involving [characteristic classes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Characteristic_class "Characteristic class") via the [Chern–Weil homomorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chern%E2%80%93Weil_homomorphism "Chern–Weil homomorphism").[[167]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-170)

### Cauchy's integral formula

[![Image 98](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Factorial05.jpg/250px-Factorial05.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Factorial05.jpg)

Complex analytic functions can be visualized as a collection of streamlines and equipotentials, systems of curves intersecting at right angles. Here illustrated is the complex logarithm of the Gamma function.

One of the key tools in [complex analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_analysis "Complex analysis") is [contour integration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contour_integration "Contour integration") of a function over a positively oriented ([rectifiable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectifiable_curve "Rectifiable curve")) [Jordan curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jordan_curve "Jordan curve")_γ_. A form of [Cauchy's integral formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cauchy%27s_integral_formula "Cauchy's integral formula") states that if a point _z_ 0 is interior to _γ_, then[[168]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-171)

![Image 99: {\displaystyle \oint _{\gamma }{\frac {dz}{z-z_{0}}}=2\pi i.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/17569b627f1050cac4952724e6e56209e44dc425)

Although the curve _γ_ is not a circle, and hence does not have any obvious connection to the constant π, a standard proof of this result uses [Morera's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morera%27s_theorem "Morera's theorem"), which implies that the integral is invariant under [homotopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homotopy "Homotopy") of the curve, so that it can be deformed to a circle and then integrated explicitly in polar coordinates. More generally, it is true that if a rectifiable closed curve γ does not contain _z_ 0, then the above integral is 2π _i_ times the [winding number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Winding_number "Winding number") of the curve.

The general form of Cauchy's integral formula establishes the relationship between the values of a [complex analytic function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_analytic_function "Complex analytic function")_f_(_z_) on the Jordan curve _γ_ and the value of _f_(_z_) at any interior point _z_ 0 of γ:[[169]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-172)

![Image 100: {\displaystyle \oint _{\gamma }{f(z) \over z-z_{0}}\,dz=2\pi if(z_{0})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f239d18251cef9208918cba90587fe2d7f618ae2)

provided _f_(_z_) is analytic in the region enclosed by _γ_ and extends continuously to _γ_. Cauchy's integral formula is a special case of the [residue theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Residue_theorem "Residue theorem"), that if _g_(_z_) is a [meromorphic function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meromorphic_function "Meromorphic function") the region enclosed by _γ_ and is continuous in a neighbourhood of _γ_, then

![Image 101: {\displaystyle \oint _{\gamma }g(z)\,dz=2\pi i\sum \operatorname {Res} (g,a_{k})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/36fffa5445c67252fba1e631674f6202925ebe3a)

where the sum is of the [residues](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Residue_(mathematics) "Residue (mathematics)") at the [poles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pole_(complex_analysis) "Pole (complex analysis)") of _g_(_z_).

### Vector calculus and physics

The constant π is ubiquitous in [vector calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_calculus "Vector calculus") and [potential theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potential_theory "Potential theory"), for example in [Coulomb's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coulomb%27s_law "Coulomb's law"),[[170]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-173)[Gauss's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauss%27s_law "Gauss's law"), [Maxwell's equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxwell%27s_equations "Maxwell's equations"), and even the [Einstein field equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_field_equations "Einstein field equations").[[171]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-174)[[172]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-175) Perhaps the simplest example of this is the two-dimensional [Newtonian potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newtonian_potential "Newtonian potential"), representing the potential of a point source at the origin, whose associated field has unit outward [flux](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flux "Flux") through any smooth and oriented closed surface enclosing the source:

![Image 102: {\displaystyle \Phi (\mathbf {x} )={\frac {1}{2\pi }}\log |\mathbf {x} |.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/173b53b5c1644e0cd4d59433349d3faf3ac98d72)

The factor of ![Image 103: {\displaystyle 1/2\pi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2d777af7ccaf5b0339d298cedf9757fe87586da1) is necessary to ensure that ![Image 104: {\displaystyle \Phi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/aed80a2011a3912b028ba32a52dfa57165455f24) is the [fundamental solution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_solution "Fundamental solution") of the [Poisson equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poisson_equation "Poisson equation") in ![Image 105: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e150115ab9f63023215109595b76686a1ff890fd):[[173]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-Elliptic_PDE2-176)

![Image 106: {\displaystyle \Delta \Phi =\delta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ed7b32c7fab3ef26726f4d1040a0e553d1e99eb9)

where ![Image 107: {\displaystyle \delta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c5321cfa797202b3e1f8620663ff43c4660ea03a) is the [Dirac delta function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dirac_delta_function "Dirac delta function").

In higher dimensions, factors of π are present because of a normalization by the n-dimensional volume of the unit [n sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/N_sphere "N sphere"). For example, in three dimensions, the Newtonian potential is:[[173]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-Elliptic_PDE2-176)

![Image 108: {\displaystyle \Phi (\mathbf {x} )=-{\frac {1}{4\pi |\mathbf {x} |}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d621580a0535c73a9a18911166a0c95010d47c3e)

which has the 2-dimensional volume (i.e., the area) of the unit 2-sphere in the denominator.

### Total curvature

[![Image 109](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Winding_Number_Around_Point.svg/250px-Winding_Number_Around_Point.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Winding_Number_Around_Point.svg)

This curve has total curvature 6 _π_ and turning number 3; it has [winding number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Winding_number "Winding number")2 about p and an additional loop which does not contain p.

In the [differential geometry of curves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry_of_curves "Differential geometry of curves"), the _[total curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Total\_curvature "Total curvature")_ of a smooth plane curve is the amount it turns anticlockwise, in radians, from start to finish, computed as the integral of signed [curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvature "Curvature") with respect to arc length:

![Image 110: {\displaystyle \int _{a}^{b}k(s)\,ds}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/931e270d1b5571b7eb17b1e51093efe82f6a12fc)

For a closed curve, this quantity is equal to 2 _πN_ for an integer N called the _[turning number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turning\_number "Turning number")_ or _index_ of the curve. N is the [winding number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Winding_number "Winding number") about the origin of the [hodograph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hodograph "Hodograph") of the curve parametrized by arclength, a new curve lying on the unit circle, described by the normalized [tangent vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_vector "Tangent vector") at each point on the original curve. Equivalently, N is the [degree of the map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_of_a_continuous_mapping "Degree of a continuous mapping") taking each point on the curve to the corresponding point on the hodograph, analogous to the [Gauss map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauss_map "Gauss map") for surfaces.

### The gamma function and Stirling's approximation

[![Image 111](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Gamma_plot_points_marked.svg/250px-Gamma_plot_points_marked.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Gamma_plot_points_marked.svg)

Plot of the gamma function on the real axis

The [factorial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Factorial "Factorial") function ![Image 112: {\displaystyle n!}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bae971720be3cc9b8d82f4cdac89cb89877514a6) is the product of all of the positive integers through _n_. The [gamma function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamma_function "Gamma function") extends the concept of [factorial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Factorial "Factorial") (normally defined only for non-negative integers) to all complex numbers, except the negative real integers, with the identity ![Image 113: {\displaystyle \Gamma (n)=(n-1)!}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ef3f7eebd96f717c5f1fd154b3905af7fbcabf24). When the gamma function is evaluated at half-integers, the result contains π. For example, ![Image 114: {\displaystyle \Gamma {\bigl (}{\tfrac {1}{2}}{\bigr )}={\sqrt {\pi }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b6afa161e0e91dc74cc43fe541d377f80b314768) and ![Image 115: {\textstyle \Gamma {\bigl (}{\tfrac {5}{2}}{\bigr )}={\tfrac {3}{4}}{\sqrt {\pi }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/55339e33eb7e75fde60d015496696602983c6c03).[[174]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-177)

The gamma function is defined by its [Weierstrass product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weierstrass_product "Weierstrass product") development:[[175]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-178)

![Image 116: {\displaystyle \Gamma (z)={\frac {e^{-\gamma z}}{z}}\prod _{n=1}^{\infty }{\frac {e^{z/n}}{1+z/n}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/39107a18b7680b95e8d2af238137b27fe1221168)

where γ is the [Euler–Mascheroni constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler%E2%80%93Mascheroni_constant "Euler–Mascheroni constant"). Evaluated at ⁠![Image 117: {\displaystyle z={\tfrac {1}{2}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8704f74b7e33c3f4a4d23bc1b341f09939a17b70)⁠ and squared, the equation ⁠![Image 118: {\displaystyle \textstyle \gamma {\bigl (}{\tfrac {1}{2}}{\bigr )}{\vphantom {)}}^{2}=\pi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cc98c6f81829d694374c8bd7fcdfe4e7ea310a15)⁠ reduces to the Wallis product formula. The gamma function is also connected to the [Riemann zeta function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_zeta_function "Riemann zeta function") and identities for the [functional determinant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functional_determinant "Functional determinant"), in which the constant π[plays an important role](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#Number_theory_and_Riemann_zeta_function).

The gamma function is used to calculate the volume _V_ _n_(_r_) of the [_n_-dimensional ball](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/N-ball "N-ball") of radius _r_ in Euclidean _n_-dimensional space, and the surface area _S_ _n_−1(_r_) of its boundary, the [(_n_−1)-dimensional sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/N-sphere "N-sphere"):[[176]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-179)

![Image 119: {\displaystyle V_{n}(r)={\frac {\pi ^{n/2}}{\Gamma {\bigl (}{\frac {n}{2}}+1{\bigr )}}}r^{n},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/58111b8e9cf73015f83a1437ec3a2a71e191e3de)

![Image 120: {\displaystyle S_{n-1}(r)={\frac {n\pi ^{n/2}}{\Gamma {\bigl (}{\tfrac {n}{2}}+1{\bigr )}}}r^{n-1}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bbbec1f199018ea873885cf27a49cf35f14ae253)

Further, it follows from the [functional equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functional_equation "Functional equation") that

![Image 121: {\displaystyle 2\pi r={\frac {S_{n+1}(r)}{V_{n}(r)}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/807a85f4bb84937de20521b0db1fc83295a48532)

The gamma function can be used to create a simple approximation to the factorial function _n_! for large _n_: ![Image 122: {\textstyle n!\sim {\sqrt {2\pi n}}\left({\frac {n}{e}}\right)^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4f32274cb48ebb4eb4b2379884758a61f17c05db) which is known as [Stirling's approximation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stirling%27s_approximation "Stirling's approximation").[[177]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-180) Equivalently,

![Image 123: {\displaystyle \pi =\lim _{n\to \infty }{\frac {e^{2n}n!^{2}}{2n^{2n+1}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/04bedea644b29f119cf4d2175576c12d61010d01)

As a geometrical application of Stirling's approximation, let Δ _n_ denote the [standard simplex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simplex "Simplex") in _n_-dimensional Euclidean space, and (_n_+1)Δ _n_ denote the simplex having all of its sides scaled up by a factor of _n_+1. Then

![Image 124: {\displaystyle \operatorname {Vol} ((n+1)\Delta _{n})={\frac {(n+1)^{n}}{n!}}\sim {\frac {e^{n+1}}{\sqrt {2\pi n}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fae31c667990be5b0307c3ab5233509497d33bac)

[Ehrhart's volume conjecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ehrhart%27s_volume_conjecture "Ehrhart's volume conjecture") is that this is the (optimal) upper bound on the volume of a [convex body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_body "Convex body") containing only one [lattice point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lattice_point "Lattice point").[[178]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-181)

### Number theory and Riemann zeta function

[![Image 125](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Pr%C3%BCfer.png/250px-Pr%C3%BCfer.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Pr%C3%BCfer.png)

Each prime has an associated [Prüfer group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pr%C3%BCfer_group "Prüfer group"), which are arithmetic localizations of the circle. The [L-functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L-function "L-function") of analytic number theory are also localized in each prime _p_.

[![Image 126](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/ModularGroup-FundamentalDomain.svg/250px-ModularGroup-FundamentalDomain.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:ModularGroup-FundamentalDomain.svg)

Solution of the Basel problem using the [Weil conjecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weil_conjecture_on_Tamagawa_numbers "Weil conjecture on Tamagawa numbers"): the value of _ζ_(2) is the [hyperbolic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poincar%C3%A9_half-plane_model "Poincaré half-plane model") area of a fundamental domain of the [modular group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modular_group "Modular group"), times π/2.

The [Riemann zeta function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_zeta_function "Riemann zeta function")_ζ_(_s_) is used in many areas of mathematics. When evaluated at _s_ = 2 it can be written as

![Image 127: {\displaystyle \zeta (2)={\frac {1}{1^{2}}}+{\frac {1}{2^{2}}}+{\frac {1}{3^{2}}}+\cdots }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/581714c279e80acf87edfb221004f575d4770e97)

Finding a [simple solution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed-form_expression "Closed-form expression") for this infinite series was a famous problem in mathematics called the [Basel problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basel_problem "Basel problem"). [Leonhard Euler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leonhard_Euler "Leonhard Euler") solved it in 1735 when he showed it was equal to π 2/6.[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPosamentierLehmann2004284-89) Euler's result leads to the [number theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_theory "Number theory") result that the probability of two random numbers being [relatively prime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relatively_prime "Relatively prime") (that is, having no shared factors) is equal to 6/π 2.[[179]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-182) This probability is based on the observation that the probability that any number is [divisible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divisible "Divisible") by a prime _p_ is 1/_p_ (for example, every 7th integer is divisible by 7.) Hence the probability that two numbers are both divisible by this prime is 1/_p_ 2, and the probability that at least one of them is not is 1−1/_p_ 2. For distinct primes, these divisibility events are mutually independent; so the probability that two numbers are relatively prime is given by a product over all primes:[[180]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-183)

![Image 128: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\prod _{p}^{\infty }\left(1-{\frac {1}{p^{2}}}\right)&=\left(\prod _{p}^{\infty }{\frac {1}{1-p^{-2}}}\right)^{-1}\\[4pt]&={\frac {1}{1+{\frac {1}{2^{2}}}+{\frac {1}{3^{2}}}+\cdots }}\\[4pt]&={\frac {1}{\zeta (2)}}={\frac {6}{\pi ^{2}}}\approx 61\%.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/33ad11b6609d91487577949c7a42872afdc33a36)

This probability can be used in conjunction with a [random number generator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Random_number_generator "Random number generator") to approximate π using a Monte Carlo approach.[[181]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-184)

The solution to the Basel problem implies that the geometrically derived quantity π is connected in a deep way to the distribution of prime numbers. This is a special case of [Weil's conjecture on Tamagawa numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weil%27s_conjecture_on_Tamagawa_numbers "Weil's conjecture on Tamagawa numbers"), which asserts the equality of similar such infinite products of _arithmetic_ quantities, localized at each prime _p_, and a _geometrical_ quantity: the reciprocal of the volume of a certain [locally symmetric space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Locally_symmetric_space "Locally symmetric space"). In the case of the Basel problem, it is the [hyperbolic 3-manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_3-manifold "Hyperbolic 3-manifold")[SL 2(**R**)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SL2(R) "SL2(R)")/[SL 2(**Z**)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modular_group "Modular group").[[182]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-185)

The zeta function also satisfies Riemann's functional equation, which involves π as well as the gamma function:

![Image 129: {\displaystyle \zeta (s)=2^{s}\pi ^{s-1}\ \sin \left({\frac {\pi s}{2}}\right)\ \Gamma (1-s)\ \zeta (1-s).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/37b4eab44e38b188e9cdde9f8d8344ca79a980ec)

Furthermore, the derivative of the zeta function satisfies

![Image 130: {\displaystyle \exp(-\zeta '(0))={\sqrt {2\pi }}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d2907a3d1a8927b4fd2a276f0463569cc1f77b6f)

A consequence is that π can be obtained from the [functional determinant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functional_determinant "Functional determinant") of the [harmonic oscillator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harmonic_oscillator "Harmonic oscillator"). This functional determinant can be computed via a product expansion, and is equivalent to the Wallis product formula.[[183]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-186) The calculation can be recast in [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics"), specifically the [variational approach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus_of_variations "Calculus of variations") to the [spectrum of the hydrogen atom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bohr_model "Bohr model").[[184]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-187)

### Fourier series

[![Image 131](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/2-adic_integers_with_dual_colorings.svg/250px-2-adic_integers_with_dual_colorings.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:2-adic_integers_with_dual_colorings.svg)

π appears in characters of [p-adic numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/P-adic_numbers "P-adic numbers") (shown), which are elements of a [Prüfer group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pr%C3%BCfer_group "Prüfer group"). [Tate's thesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tate%27s_thesis "Tate's thesis") makes heavy use of this machinery.[[185]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-188)

The constant π also appears naturally in [Fourier series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourier_series "Fourier series") of [periodic functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Periodic_function "Periodic function"). Periodic functions are functions on the group **T** =**R**/**Z** of fractional parts of real numbers. The Fourier decomposition shows that a complex-valued function _f_ on **T** can be written as an infinite linear superposition of [unitary characters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unitary_character "Unitary character") of **T**. That is, continuous [group homomorphisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_homomorphism "Group homomorphism") from **T** to the [circle group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle_group "Circle group")_U_(1) of unit modulus complex numbers. It is a theorem that every character of **T** is one of the complex exponentials ![Image 132: {\displaystyle e_{n}(x)=e^{2\pi inx}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/142bcbcd2610f5640cdfd50166a105624164d007).

There is a unique character on **T**, up to complex conjugation, that is a group isomorphism. Using the [Haar measure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haar_measure "Haar measure") on the circle group, the constant π is half the magnitude of the [Radon–Nikodym derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radon%E2%80%93Nikodym_derivative "Radon–Nikodym derivative") of this character. The other characters have derivatives whose magnitudes are positive integral multiples of 2 π.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-Nicolas_Bourbaki-14) As a result, the constant π is the unique number such that the group **T**, equipped with its Haar measure, is [Pontrjagin dual](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pontrjagin_dual "Pontrjagin dual") to the [lattice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lattice_(group) "Lattice (group)") of integral multiples of 2 π.[[186]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDymMcKean1972Chapter_4-189) This is a version of the one-dimensional [Poisson summation formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poisson_summation_formula "Poisson summation formula").

### Modular forms and theta functions

[![Image 133](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/ca/Lattice_with_tau.svg/250px-Lattice_with_tau.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Lattice_with_tau.svg)

Theta functions transform under the [lattice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lattice_(group) "Lattice (group)") of periods of an elliptic curve.

The constant π is connected in a deep way with the theory of [modular forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modular_form "Modular form") and [theta functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theta_function "Theta function"). For example, the [Chudnovsky algorithm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chudnovsky_algorithm "Chudnovsky algorithm") involves in an essential way the [j-invariant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J-invariant "J-invariant") of an [elliptic curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptic_curve "Elliptic curve").

[Modular forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modular_form "Modular form") are [holomorphic functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holomorphic_function "Holomorphic function") in the [upper half plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Upper_half_plane "Upper half plane") characterized by their transformation properties under the [modular group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modular_group "Modular group")![Image 134: {\displaystyle \mathrm {SL} _{2}(\mathbb {Z} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ca2d95ba975aa85bd6c46f0a696872d3aa385869) (or its various subgroups), a lattice in the group ![Image 135: {\displaystyle \mathrm {SL} _{2}(\mathbb {R} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0bb95adfe7c34f38de771bb5fa44e17ec26deeee). An example is the [Jacobi theta function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacobi_theta_function "Jacobi theta function")

![Image 136: {\displaystyle \theta (z,\tau )=\sum _{n=-\infty }^{\infty }e^{2\pi inz\ +\ \pi in^{2}\tau }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e1f50d5773dc1a50d50774df58ca32b47d507f26)

which is a kind of modular form called a [Jacobi form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacobi_form "Jacobi form").[[187]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-Mumford_1983_1%E2%80%93117-190) This is sometimes written in terms of the [nome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nome_(mathematics) "Nome (mathematics)")![Image 137: {\displaystyle q=e^{\pi i\tau }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b04b5fc2797318028727bd06733bb52300c99e9b).

The constant π is the unique constant making the Jacobi theta function an [automorphic form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Automorphic_form "Automorphic form"), which means that it transforms in a specific way. Certain identities hold for all automorphic forms. An example is

![Image 138: {\displaystyle \theta (z+\tau ,\tau )=e^{-\pi i\tau -2\pi iz}\theta (z,\tau ),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/94df3eda20c1a72c699a40cf8b4e0ef49801f601)

which implies that θ transforms as a representation under the discrete [Heisenberg group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heisenberg_group "Heisenberg group"). General modular forms and other [theta functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theta_function "Theta function") also involve π, once again because of the [Stone–von Neumann theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stone%E2%80%93von_Neumann_theorem "Stone–von Neumann theorem").[[187]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-Mumford_1983_1%E2%80%93117-190)

### Cauchy distribution and potential theory

[![Image 139](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Witch_of_Agnesi%2C_construction.svg/250px-Witch_of_Agnesi%2C_construction.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Witch_of_Agnesi,_construction.svg)

The [Witch of Agnesi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Witch_of_Agnesi "Witch of Agnesi"), named for [Maria Agnesi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maria_Gaetana_Agnesi "Maria Gaetana Agnesi") (1718–1799), is a geometrical construction of the graph of the Cauchy distribution.

[![Image 140](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/2d_random_walk_ag_adatom_ag111.gif/250px-2d_random_walk_ag_adatom_ag111.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:2d_random_walk_ag_adatom_ag111.gif)

The Cauchy distribution governs the passage of [Brownian particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brownian_motion "Brownian motion") through a membrane.

The [Cauchy distribution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cauchy_distribution "Cauchy distribution")

![Image 141: {\displaystyle g(x)={\frac {1}{\pi }}\cdot {\frac {1}{x^{2}+1}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/19fbbd1733585d1552298a9352029f90887c23a8)

is a [probability density function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability_density_function "Probability density function"). The total probability is equal to one, owing to the integral:

![Image 142: {\displaystyle \int _{-\infty }^{\infty }{\frac {1}{x^{2}+1}}\,dx=\pi .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c1f17dafb3b274d126111151564a8666bb4ac93a)

The [Shannon entropy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shannon_entropy "Shannon entropy") of the Cauchy distribution is equal to ln(4π), which also involves π.

The Cauchy distribution plays an important role in [potential theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potential_theory "Potential theory") because it is the simplest [Furstenberg measure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Furstenberg_boundary "Furstenberg boundary"), the classical [Poisson kernel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poisson_kernel "Poisson kernel") associated with a [Brownian motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brownian_motion "Brownian motion") in a half-plane.[[188]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-191)[Conjugate harmonic functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conjugate_harmonic_function "Conjugate harmonic function") and so also the [Hilbert transform](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_transform "Hilbert transform") are associated with the asymptotics of the Poisson kernel. The Hilbert transform _H_ is the integral transform given by the [Cauchy principal value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cauchy_principal_value "Cauchy principal value") of the [singular integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Singular_integral "Singular integral")

![Image 143: {\displaystyle Hf(t)={\frac {1}{\pi }}\int _{-\infty }^{\infty }{\frac {f(x)\,dx}{x-t}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2cd815ba69c72a4cb8e65180491f6db68eda5a12)

The constant π is the unique (positive) normalizing factor such that _H_ defines a [linear complex structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_complex_structure "Linear complex structure") on the Hilbert space of square-integrable real-valued functions on the real line.[[189]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-192) The Hilbert transform, like the Fourier transform, can be characterized purely in terms of its transformation properties on the Hilbert space L 2(**R**): up to a normalization factor, it is the unique bounded linear operator that commutes with positive dilations and anti-commutes with all reflections of the real line.[[190]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-193) The constant π is the unique normalizing factor that makes this transformation unitary.

### In the Mandelbrot set

[![Image 144: An complex black shape on a blue background.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Mandel_zoom_00_mandelbrot_set.jpg/250px-Mandel_zoom_00_mandelbrot_set.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Mandel_zoom_00_mandelbrot_set.jpg)

The [Mandelbrot set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mandelbrot_set "Mandelbrot set") can be used to approximate π.

An occurrence of π in the [fractal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal "Fractal") called the [Mandelbrot set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mandelbrot_set "Mandelbrot set") was discovered by David Boll in 1991. He examined the behaviour of the Mandelbrot set near the "neck" at (−0.75, 0). When the number of iterations until divergence for the point (−0.75, _ε_) is multiplied by ε, the result approaches π as ε approaches zero. The point (0.25 + _ε_, 0) at the cusp of the large "valley" on the right side of the Mandelbrot set behaves similarly: the number of iterations until divergence multiplied by the square root of ε tends to π.[[191]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-194)

### Memorizing digits

[Piphilology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Piphilology "Piphilology") is the practice of memorizing large numbers of digits of π,[[197]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel200644%E2%80%9345-200) and world-records are kept by the _[Guinness World Records](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guinness\_World\_Records "Guinness World Records")_. The record for memorizing digits of π, certified by Guinness World Records, is 70,000 digits, recited in India by Rajveer Meena in 9 hours and 27 minutes on 21 March 2015.[[198]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-201) In 2006, [Akira Haraguchi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Akira_Haraguchi "Akira Haraguchi"), a retired Japanese engineer, claimed to have recited 100,000 decimal places, but the claim was not verified by Guinness World Records.[[199]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-japantimes-202)

One common technique is to memorize a story or poem in which the word lengths represent the digits of π: The first word has three letters, the second word has one, the third has four, the fourth has one, the fifth has five, and so on. Such memorization aids are called [mnemonics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mnemonic "Mnemonic"). An early example of a mnemonic for pi, originally devised by English scientist [James Jeans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Hopwood_Jeans "James Hopwood Jeans"), is "How I want a drink, alcoholic of course, after the heavy lectures involving quantum mechanics."[[197]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel200644%E2%80%9345-200) When a poem is used, it is sometimes referred to as a _piem_.[[200]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-203) Poems for memorizing π have been composed in several languages in addition to English.[[197]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArndtHaenel200644%E2%80%9345-200) Record-setting π memorizers typically do not rely on poems, but instead use methods such as remembering number patterns and the [method of loci](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Method_of_loci "Method of loci").[[201]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-204)

A few authors have used the digits of π to establish a new form of [constrained writing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constrained_writing "Constrained writing"), where the word lengths are required to represent the digits of π. The _[Cadaeic Cadenza](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cadaeic\_Cadenza "Cadaeic Cadenza")_ contains the first 3835 digits of π in this manner,[[202]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-205) and the full-length book _Not a Wake_ contains 10,000 words, each representing one digit of π.[[203]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-KeithNAW-206)

### In popular culture

[![Image 151: Pi Pie at Delft University](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Pi_pie2.jpg/250px-Pi_pie2.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Pi_pie2.jpg)

A pi pie. Many [pies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pies "Pies") are circular, and "pie" and π are [homophones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homophones "Homophones"), making pie a frequent subject of pi [puns](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pun "Pun").

Perhaps because of the simplicity of its definition and its ubiquitous presence in formulae, π has been represented in popular culture more than other mathematical constructs.[[204]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-207)

In the [Palais de la Découverte](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palais_de_la_D%C3%A9couverte "Palais de la Découverte") (a science museum in Paris) there is a circular room known as the _pi room_. On its wall are inscribed 707 digits of π. The digits are large wooden characters attached to the dome-like ceiling. The digits were based on an 1873 calculation by English mathematician [William Shanks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Shanks "William Shanks"), which included an error beginning at the 528th digit. The error was detected in 1946 and corrected in 1949.[[205]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-208)

In [Carl Sagan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Sagan "Carl Sagan")'s 1985 novel _[Contact](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contact\_(novel) "Contact (novel)")_ it is suggested that the creator of the universe buried a message deep within the digits of π. This part of the story was omitted from the [film](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contact_(1997_American_film) "Contact (1997 American film)") adaptation of the novel.[[206]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-209) The digits of π have also been incorporated into the lyrics of the song "Pi" from the 2005 album _[Aerial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aerial\_(album) "Aerial (album)")_ by [Kate Bush](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kate_Bush "Kate Bush").[[207]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-210) In the 1967 _[Star Trek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star\_Trek:\_The\_Original\_Series "Star Trek: The Original Series")_ episode "[Wolf in the Fold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolf_in_the_Fold "Wolf in the Fold")", a computer possessed by a demonic entity is contained by being instructed to "Compute to the last digit the value of π".[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-life-of-pi-44)

In the United States, [Pi Day](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi_Day "Pi Day") falls on 14 March (written 3/14 in the US style), and is popular among students.[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-life-of-pi-44)π and its digital representation are often used by self-described "math [geeks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geek "Geek")" for [inside jokes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inside_joke "Inside joke") among mathematically and technologically minded groups. A [college cheer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cheering#Chants_in_North_American_sports "Cheering") variously attributed to the [Massachusetts Institute of Technology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology "Massachusetts Institute of Technology") or the [Rensselaer Polytechnic Institute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rensselaer_Polytechnic_Institute "Rensselaer Polytechnic Institute") includes "3.14159".[[208]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-211) Pi Day in 2015 was particularly significant because the date and time 3/14/15 9:26:53 reflected many more digits of pi.[[209]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-212) In parts of the world where dates are commonly noted in day/month/year format, 22 July represents "Pi Approximation Day", as 22/7≈3.142857.[[210]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-213)

Some have proposed replacing π by [_τ_ = 2 _π_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tau_(mathematical_constant) "Tau (mathematical constant)"), arguing that τ, as the number of radians in one [turn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turn_(angle) "Turn (angle)") or the ratio of a circle's circumference to its radius, is more natural than π and simplifies many formulae.[[211]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-214)[[212]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-215) This use of τ has not made its way into mainstream mathematics,[[213]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-216) but since 2010 this has led to people celebrating Two Pi Day or Tau Day on June 28.[[214]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-217)

In 1897, an amateur mathematician attempted to persuade the [Indiana legislature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indiana_General_Assembly "Indiana General Assembly") to pass the [Indiana Pi Bill](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indiana_Pi_Bill "Indiana Pi Bill"), which described a method to [square the circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Squaring_the_circle "Squaring the circle") and contained text that implied various incorrect values for π, including 3.2. The bill is notorious as an attempt to establish a value of mathematical constant by legislative fiat. The bill was passed by the Indiana House of Representatives, but rejected by the Senate, and thus it did not become a law.[[215]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-218)

In contemporary [internet culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internet_culture "Internet culture"), individuals and organizations frequently pay homage to the number π. For instance, the [computer scientist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_scientist "Computer scientist")[Donald Knuth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Donald_Knuth "Donald Knuth") let the version numbers of his program [TeX](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TeX "TeX") approach π. The versions are 3, 3.1, 3.14, and so forth.[[216]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi#cite_note-219)
