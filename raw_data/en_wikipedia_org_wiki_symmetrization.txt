In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), **symmetrization** is a process that converts any [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)") in ![Image 1: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) variables to a [symmetric function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetric_function "Symmetric function") in ![Image 2: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) variables. Similarly, **antisymmetrization** converts any function in ![Image 3: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) variables into an [antisymmetric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antisymmetric_relation "Antisymmetric relation") function.

Let ![Image 4: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2) be a [set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_(mathematics) "Set (mathematics)") and ![Image 5: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3) be an [additive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Additive_group "Additive group")[abelian group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abelian_group "Abelian group"). A map ![Image 6: {\displaystyle \alpha :S\times S\to A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dacd1fb9360a65bc2148e9cce19bbeb50c5c7064) is called a **symmetric map** if ![Image 7: {\displaystyle \alpha (s,t)=\alpha (t,s)\quad {\text{ for all }}s,t\in S.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9986b0166dda5e822a6855609b9ab2d1f234ca6b) It is called an **antisymmetric map** if instead ![Image 8: {\displaystyle \alpha (s,t)=-\alpha (t,s)\quad {\text{ for all }}s,t\in S.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ec48a45edf6281f1308aede3e15f3ff161532e4a)

The **symmetrization** of a map ![Image 9: {\displaystyle \alpha :S\times S\to A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dacd1fb9360a65bc2148e9cce19bbeb50c5c7064) is the map ![Image 10: {\displaystyle (x,y)\mapsto \alpha (x,y)+\alpha (y,x).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/49daebb8d69ced597a1296a286e6b660e40efc96) Similarly, the **antisymmetrization** or **skew-symmetrization** of a map ![Image 11: {\displaystyle \alpha :S\times S\to A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dacd1fb9360a65bc2148e9cce19bbeb50c5c7064) is the map ![Image 12: {\displaystyle (x,y)\mapsto \alpha (x,y)-\alpha (y,x).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b0489f16f22106b9eb0135bb431c1ce818c12813)

The sum of the symmetrization and the antisymmetrization of a map ![Image 13: {\displaystyle \alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b79333175c8b3f0840bfb4ec41b8072c83ea88d3) is ![Image 14: {\displaystyle 2\alpha .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/402497e62d701767bfaa33b5a265cdac42cac53b) Thus, [away from 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Localization_of_a_ring#Terminology_explained_by_the_context "Localization of a ring"), meaning if 2 is [invertible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_(ring_theory) "Unit (ring theory)"), such as for the [real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number"), one can divide by 2 and express every function as a sum of a symmetric function and an anti-symmetric function.

The symmetrization of a symmetric map is its double, while the symmetrization of an [alternating map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alternating_map "Alternating map") is zero; similarly, the antisymmetrization of a symmetric map is zero, while the antisymmetrization of an anti-symmetric map is its double.

The symmetrization and antisymmetrization of a [bilinear map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bilinear_map "Bilinear map") are bilinear; thus away from 2, every bilinear form is a sum of a symmetric form and a skew-symmetric form, and there is no difference between a symmetric form and a quadratic form.

At 2, not every form can be decomposed into a symmetric form and a skew-symmetric form. For instance, over the [integers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer"), the associated symmetric form (over the [rationals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_number "Rational number")) may take half-integer values, while over ![Image 15: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} /2\mathbb {Z} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/49b068d1b3377be64785a60d11d0f135fe529631) a function is skew-symmetric if and only if it is symmetric (as ![Image 16: {\displaystyle 1=-1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7fe2156ea89dbb501ff119c337fb4819b1fca5ca)).

This leads to the notion of [ε-quadratic forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/E-quadratic_form "E-quadratic form") and ε-symmetric forms.

### Representation theory

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Symmetrization&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Representation theory")]

In terms of [representation theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Representation_theory "Representation theory"):

*   exchanging variables gives a representation of the [symmetric group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetric_group "Symmetric group") on the space of functions in two variables,
*   the symmetric and antisymmetric functions are the [subrepresentations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subrepresentation "Subrepresentation") corresponding to the [trivial representation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trivial_representation "Trivial representation") and the [sign representation](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sign_representation&action=edit&redlink=1 "Sign representation (page does not exist)"), and
*   symmetrization and antisymmetrization map a function into these subrepresentations – if one divides by 2, these yield [projection maps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projection_map "Projection map").

As the symmetric group of order two equals the [cyclic group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclic_group "Cyclic group") of order two (![Image 17: {\displaystyle \mathrm {S} _{2}=\mathrm {C} _{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0ba99f8b712b472c46c7045fd1dc3c9bd7fd8783)), this corresponds to the [discrete Fourier transform](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discrete_Fourier_transform "Discrete Fourier transform") of order two.

More generally, given a function in ![Image 18: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) variables, one can symmetrize by taking the sum over all ![Image 19: {\displaystyle n!}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bae971720be3cc9b8d82f4cdac89cb89877514a6) permutations of the variables,[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetrization#cite_note-1) or [antisymmetrize](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Antisymmetrize&action=edit&redlink=1 "Antisymmetrize (page does not exist)") by taking the sum over all ![Image 20: {\displaystyle n!/2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d49cbf4afe860e7c1fbd848756cd63c566c2aae0)[even permutations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Even_permutation "Even permutation") and subtracting the sum over all ![Image 21: {\displaystyle n!/2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d49cbf4afe860e7c1fbd848756cd63c566c2aae0) odd permutations (except that when ![Image 22: {\displaystyle n\leq 1,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dbc76934a064a3769a069726a856f92c76d060e5) the only permutation is even).

Here symmetrizing a symmetric function multiplies by ![Image 23: {\displaystyle n!}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bae971720be3cc9b8d82f4cdac89cb89877514a6) – thus if ![Image 24: {\displaystyle n!}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bae971720be3cc9b8d82f4cdac89cb89877514a6) is invertible, such as when working over a [field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)") of [characteristic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Characteristic_(algebra) "Characteristic (algebra)")![Image 25: {\displaystyle 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2aae8864a3c1fec9585261791a809ddec1489950) or ![Image 26: {\displaystyle p>n,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d364f1705bdea7538aba1050f5e75a8c8064dfa9) then these yield projections when divided by ![Image 27: {\displaystyle n!.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c6684f576eb668466f2ca09257d020f297343eb7)

In terms of representation theory, these only yield the subrepresentations corresponding to the trivial and sign representation, but for ![Image 28: {\displaystyle n>2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/44e71ac55b9fbf1e9f341b946cda63d61d3ef2cd) there are others – see [representation theory of the symmetric group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Representation_theory_of_the_symmetric_group "Representation theory of the symmetric group") and [symmetric polynomials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetric_polynomials "Symmetric polynomials").

Given a function in ![Image 29: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40) variables, one can obtain a symmetric function in ![Image 30: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) variables by taking the sum over ![Image 31: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40)-element [subsets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subset "Subset") of the variables. In statistics, this is referred to as [bootstrapping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bootstrapping_(statistics) "Bootstrapping (statistics)"), and the associated statistics are called [U-statistics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U-statistics "U-statistics").

*   [Alternating multilinear map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alternating_multilinear_map "Alternating multilinear map")– Multilinear map that is 0 whenever arguments are linearly dependent
*   [Antisymmetric tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antisymmetric_tensor "Antisymmetric tensor")– Tensor equal to the negative of any of its transpositions
