Title: Non-equilibrium thermodynamics

URL Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics

Published Time: 2004-02-16T21:10:11Z

Markdown Content:
**Non-equilibrium thermodynamics** is a branch of [thermodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamics "Thermodynamics") that deals with physical systems that are not in [thermodynamic equilibrium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamic_equilibrium "Thermodynamic equilibrium") but can be described in terms of macroscopic quantities (non-equilibrium state variables) that represent an extrapolation of the variables used to specify the system in thermodynamic equilibrium. Non-equilibrium thermodynamics is concerned with [transport processes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transport_phenomena "Transport phenomena") and with the rates of [chemical reactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_reactions "Chemical reactions").

Almost all systems found in nature are not in thermodynamic equilibrium, for they are changing or can be triggered to change over time, and are continuously and discontinuously subject to flux of matter and energy to and from other systems and to chemical reactions. Many systems and processes can, however, be considered to be in equilibrium locally, thus allowing description by currently known equilibrium thermodynamics.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-:0-1) Nevertheless, some natural systems and processes remain beyond the scope of equilibrium thermodynamic methods due to the existence of non variational dynamics, where the concept of [free energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamic_free_energy "Thermodynamic free energy") is lost.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-2)

The thermodynamic study of non-equilibrium systems requires more general concepts than are dealt with by [equilibrium thermodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equilibrium_thermodynamics "Equilibrium thermodynamics").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-3) One fundamental difference between equilibrium thermodynamics and non-equilibrium thermodynamics lies in the behaviour of inhomogeneous systems, which require for their study knowledge of rates of reaction which are not considered in equilibrium thermodynamics of homogeneous systems. This is discussed below. Another fundamental and very important difference is the difficulty, in defining [entropy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy "Entropy") at an instant of time in macroscopic terms for systems not in thermodynamic equilibrium. However, it can be done locally, and the macroscopic entropy will then be given by the integral of the locally defined entropy density. It has been found that many systems far outside global equilibrium still obey the concept of local equilibrium.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-4)

### Difference between equilibrium and non-equilibrium thermodynamics

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Difference between equilibrium and non-equilibrium thermodynamics")]

A profound difference separates equilibrium from non-equilibrium thermodynamics. Equilibrium thermodynamics ignores the time-courses of physical processes. In contrast, non-equilibrium thermodynamics attempts to describe their time-courses in continuous detail.

Equilibrium thermodynamics restricts its considerations to processes that have initial and final states of thermodynamic equilibrium; the time-courses of processes are deliberately ignored. Non-equilibrium thermodynamics, on the other hand, attempting to describe continuous time-courses, needs its [state variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_function "State function") to have a very close connection with those of equilibrium thermodynamics.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-Glansdorff&Pr1971-5) This conceptual issue is overcome under the assumption of local equilibrium, which entails that the relationships that hold between macroscopic state variables at equilibrium hold locally, also outside equilibrium.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-:0-1) Throughout the past decades, the assumption of local equilibrium has been tested, and found to hold, under increasingly extreme conditions, such as in the shock front of violent explosions, on reacting surfaces, and under extreme thermal gradients.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-De_Groot_Mazur_1962-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-:1-7)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-:2-8)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-:3-9)

Thus, non-equilibrium thermodynamics provides a consistent framework for modelling not only the initial and final states of a system, but also the evolution of the system in time. Together with the concept of entropy production, this provides a powerful tool in [process optimisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Process_optimization "Process optimization"), and provides a theoretical foundation for [exergy analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exergy_efficiency "Exergy efficiency").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-:0-1)

### Non-equilibrium state variables

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Non-equilibrium state variables")]

The suitable relationship that defines non-equilibrium [thermodynamic state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamic_state "Thermodynamic state") variables is as follows. When the system is in local equilibrium, non-equilibrium state variables are such that they can be measured locally with sufficient accuracy by the same techniques as are used to measure thermodynamic state variables, or by corresponding time and space derivatives, including fluxes of matter and energy. In general, non-equilibrium thermodynamic systems are spatially and temporally non-uniform, but their non-uniformity still has a sufficient degree of smoothness to support the existence of suitable time and space derivatives of non-equilibrium state variables.

Because of the spatial non-uniformity, non-equilibrium state variables that correspond to extensive thermodynamic state variables have to be defined as spatial densities of the corresponding extensive equilibrium state variables. When the system is in local equilibrium, intensive non-equilibrium state variables, for example temperature and pressure, correspond closely with equilibrium state variables. It is necessary that measuring probes be small enough, and rapidly enough responding, to capture relevant non-uniformity. Further, the non-equilibrium state variables are required to be mathematically functionally related to one another in ways that suitably resemble corresponding relations between equilibrium thermodynamic state variables.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-Gyarmati_1970-10) In reality, these requirements, although strict, have been shown to be fulfilled even under extreme conditions, such as during phase transitions, at reacting interfaces, and in plasma droplets surrounded by ambient air.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-11)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-12)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-13) There are, however, situations where there are appreciable non-linear effects even at the local scale.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-14)[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-15)

Some concepts of particular importance for non-equilibrium thermodynamics include time rate of dissipation of energy (Rayleigh 1873,[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-Rayleigh_1873-16)[Onsager](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lars_Onsager "Lars Onsager") 1931,[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-Onsager_1931_I-17) also[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-Gyarmati_1970-10)[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-Lavenda_1978-18)), time rate of entropy production (Onsager 1931),[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-Onsager_1931_I-17) thermodynamic fields,[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-19)[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-Ziegler_1983-20)[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-Balescu-21)[dissipative structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dissipative_structure "Dissipative structure"),[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-Glansdorff&Pr1971-5) and non-linear dynamical structure.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-Lavenda_1978-18)

One problem of interest is the thermodynamic study of non-equilibrium [steady states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steady_state "Steady state"), in which [entropy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy "Entropy") production and some [flows](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flux "Flux") are non-zero, but there is no [time variation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time-variant_system "Time-variant system") of physical variables.

One initial approach to non-equilibrium thermodynamics is sometimes called 'classical irreversible thermodynamics'.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-Lebon_Jou_Casas-V%C3%A1zquez_2008-22) There are other approaches to non-equilibrium thermodynamics, for example [extended irreversible thermodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extended_irreversible_thermodynamics "Extended irreversible thermodynamics"),[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-Lebon_Jou_Casas-V%C3%A1zquez_2008-22)[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-JCVL_1993-23) and generalized thermodynamics,[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-24) but they are hardly touched on in the present article.

### Quasi-radiationless non-equilibrium thermodynamics of matter in laboratory conditions

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Quasi-radiationless non-equilibrium thermodynamics of matter in laboratory conditions")]

According to Wildt[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-Wildt_1972-25) (see also Essex[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-Essex_1984a-26)[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-Essex_1984b-27)[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-Essex_1984c-28)), current versions of non-equilibrium thermodynamics ignore radiant heat; they can do so because they refer to laboratory quantities of matter under laboratory conditions with temperatures well below those of stars. At laboratory temperatures, in laboratory quantities of matter, thermal radiation is weak and can be practically nearly ignored. But, for example, atmospheric physics is concerned with large amounts of matter, occupying cubic kilometers, that, taken as a whole, are not within the range of laboratory quantities; then thermal radiation cannot be ignored.

### Local equilibrium thermodynamics

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Local equilibrium thermodynamics")]

The terms 'classical irreversible thermodynamics'[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-Lebon_Jou_Casas-V%C3%A1zquez_2008-22) and 'local equilibrium thermodynamics' are sometimes used to refer to a version of non-equilibrium thermodynamics that demands certain simplifying assumptions, as follows. The assumptions have the effect of making each very small [volume element](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume_element "Volume element") of the system effectively homogeneous, or well-mixed, or without an effective spatial structure. Even within the thought-frame of classical irreversible thermodynamics, care[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-Lavenda_1978-18) is needed in choosing the independent variables[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-29) for systems. In some writings, it is assumed that the intensive variables of equilibrium thermodynamics are sufficient as the independent variables for the task (such variables are considered to have no 'memory', and do not show [hysteresis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hysteresis "Hysteresis")); in particular, local flow intensive variables are not admitted as independent variables; local flows are considered as dependent on quasi-static local intensive variables.

Also it is assumed that the local entropy density is the same function of the other local intensive variables as in equilibrium; this is called the local thermodynamic equilibrium assumption[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-Gyarmati_1970-10)[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-Lavenda_1978-18)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-Glansdorff&Pr1971-5)[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-JCVL_1993-23)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-De_Groot_Mazur_1962-6)[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-Balescu_1975-30)[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-Mihalas_Mihalas_1984-31)[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-Schloegl_1989-32) (see also Keizer (1987)[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-Keizer_1987-33)). Radiation is ignored because it is transfer of energy between regions, which can be remote from one another. In the classical irreversible thermodynamic approach, there is allowed spatial variation from infinitesimal volume element to adjacent infinitesimal volume element, but it is assumed that the global entropy of the system can be found by simple spatial integration of the local entropy density. This approach assumes spatial and temporal continuity and even differentiability of locally defined intensive variables such as temperature and internal energy density. While these demands may appear severely constrictive, it has been found that the assumptions of local equilibrium hold for a wide variety of systems, including reacting interfaces, on the surfaces of catalysts, in confined systems such as zeolites, under temperature gradients as large as ![Image 1: {\displaystyle 10^{12}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9c90fc8874dafeb075a01985c74c1d31f51e7c3b) K m![Image 2: {\displaystyle ^{-1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d21ba9ae71e157827da571c142bb49c24b78e1a0), and even in shock fronts moving at up to six times the speed of sound.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-:1-7)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-:2-8)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-:3-9)[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-34)

In other writings, local flow variables are considered; these might be considered as classical by analogy with the time-invariant long-term time-averages of flows produced by endlessly repeated cyclic processes; examples with flows are in the [thermoelectric phenomena](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermoelectric_effect "Thermoelectric effect") known as the Seebeck and the Peltier effects, considered by [Kelvin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Thomson,_1st_Baron_Kelvin "William Thomson, 1st Baron Kelvin") in the nineteenth century and by [Lars Onsager](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lars_Onsager "Lars Onsager") in the twentieth.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-De_Groot_Mazur_1962-6)[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-35) These effects occur at metal junctions, which were originally effectively treated as two-dimensional surfaces, with no spatial volume, and no spatial variation.

#### Local equilibrium thermodynamics with materials with "memory"

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Local equilibrium thermodynamics with materials with \"memory\"")]

A further extension of local equilibrium thermodynamics is to allow that materials may have "memory", so that their [constitutive equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitutive_equation "Constitutive equation") depend not only on present values but also on past values of local equilibrium variables. Thus time comes into the picture more deeply than for time-dependent local equilibrium thermodynamics with memoryless materials, but fluxes are not independent variables of state.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-36)

### Extended irreversible thermodynamics

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Extended irreversible thermodynamics")]

**Extended irreversible thermodynamics** is a branch of non-equilibrium thermodynamics that goes outside the restriction to the local equilibrium hypothesis. The space of state variables is enlarged by including the [fluxes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flux "Flux") of mass, momentum and energy and eventually higher order fluxes. The formalism is well-suited for describing high-frequency processes and small-length scales materials.

There are many examples of stationary non-equilibrium systems, some very simple, like a system confined between two thermostats at different temperatures or the ordinary [Couette flow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Couette_flow "Couette flow"), a fluid enclosed between two flat walls moving in opposite directions and defining non-equilibrium conditions at the walls. [Laser](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laser "Laser") action is also a non-equilibrium process, but it depends on departure from local thermodynamic equilibrium and is thus beyond the scope of classical irreversible thermodynamics; here a strong temperature difference is maintained between two molecular degrees of freedom (with molecular laser, vibrational and rotational molecular motion), the requirement for two component 'temperatures' in the one small region of space, precluding local thermodynamic equilibrium, which demands that only one temperature be needed. Damping of acoustic perturbations or shock waves are non-stationary non-equilibrium processes. Driven [complex fluids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_fluids "Complex fluids"), turbulent systems and glasses are other examples of non-equilibrium systems.

The mechanics of macroscopic systems depends on a number of extensive quantities. It should be stressed that all systems are permanently interacting with their surroundings, thereby causing unavoidable fluctuations of [extensive quantities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extensive_quantity "Extensive quantity"). Equilibrium conditions of thermodynamic systems are related to the maximum property of the entropy. If the only extensive quantity that is allowed to fluctuate is the internal energy, all the other ones being kept strictly constant, the temperature of the system is measurable and meaningful. The system's properties are then most conveniently described using the thermodynamic potential [Helmholtz free energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helmholtz_free_energy "Helmholtz free energy") (_A_ = _U_ - _TS_), a [Legendre transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legendre_transformation "Legendre transformation") of the energy. If, next to fluctuations of the energy, the macroscopic dimensions (volume) of the system are left fluctuating, we use the [Gibbs free energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gibbs_free_energy "Gibbs free energy") (_G_ = _U_ + _PV_ - _TS_), where the system's properties are determined both by the temperature and by the pressure.

Non-equilibrium systems are much more complex and they may undergo fluctuations of more extensive quantities. The boundary conditions impose on them particular intensive variables, like temperature gradients or distorted collective motions (shear motions, vortices, etc.), often called thermodynamic forces. If free energies are very useful in equilibrium thermodynamics, it must be stressed that there is no general law defining stationary non-equilibrium properties of the energy as is the second law of thermodynamics for the [entropy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy "Entropy") in equilibrium thermodynamics. That is why in such cases a more generalized Legendre transformation should be considered. This is the extended Massieu potential. By definition, the [entropy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy "Entropy") (_S_) is a function of the collection of [extensive quantities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extensive_quantity "Extensive quantity")![Image 3: {\displaystyle E_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8ba9f6e3041b052cf13a0ede4ecf35fb4c9cd16c). Each extensive quantity has a conjugate intensive variable ![Image 4: {\displaystyle I_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d896cad5ad71ce7719175ca29d4d109333d4c320) (a restricted definition of intensive variable is used here by comparison to the definition given in this link) so that:

![Image 5: {\displaystyle I_{i}={\frac {\partial {S}}{\partial {E_{i}}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/958ecd58c38e72fb06585bb41b2ddfb87c979b31)
We then define the extended [Massieu function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Massieu_function "Massieu function") as follows:

![Image 6: {\displaystyle \ k_{\rm {B}}M=S-\sum _{i}(I_{i}E_{i}),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/21b48faaed34cd46e2b7bde2768a1a1b4c9c38fc)
where ![Image 7: {\displaystyle \ k_{\rm {B}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/70e5f7e678a654bc10d1b5f97e81c289a8f578a7) is the [Boltzmann constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boltzmann_constant "Boltzmann constant"), whence

The independent variables are the intensities.

Intensities are global values, valid for the system as a whole. When boundaries impose to the system different local conditions, (e.g. temperature differences), there are intensive variables representing the average value and others representing gradients or higher moments. The latter are the thermodynamic forces driving fluxes of extensive properties through the system.

It may be shown that the Legendre transformation changes the maximum condition of the entropy (valid at equilibrium) in a minimum condition of the extended Massieu function for stationary states, no matter whether at equilibrium or not.

Stationary states, fluctuations, and stability
----------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Stationary states, fluctuations, and stability")]

In thermodynamics one is often interested in a stationary state of a process, allowing that the stationary state include the occurrence of unpredictable and experimentally unreproducible fluctuations in the state of the system. The fluctuations are due to the system's internal sub-processes and to exchange of matter or energy with the system's surroundings that create the constraints that define the process.

If the stationary state of the process is stable, then the unreproducible fluctuations involve local transient decreases of entropy. The reproducible response of the system is then to increase the entropy back to its maximum by irreversible processes: the fluctuation cannot be reproduced with a significant level of probability. Fluctuations about stable stationary states are extremely small except near critical points (Kondepudi and Prigogine 1998, page 323).[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-37) The stable stationary state has a local maximum of entropy and is locally the most reproducible state of the system. There are theorems about the irreversible dissipation of fluctuations. Here 'local' means local with respect to the abstract space of thermodynamic coordinates of state of the system.

If the stationary state is unstable, then any fluctuation will almost surely trigger the virtually explosive departure of the system from the unstable stationary state. This can be accompanied by increased export of entropy.

Local thermodynamic equilibrium
-------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Local thermodynamic equilibrium")]

The scope of present-day non-equilibrium thermodynamics does not cover all physical processes. A condition for the validity of many studies in non-equilibrium thermodynamics of matter is that they deal with what is known as _local thermodynamic equilibrium_.

_Local thermodynamic equilibrium of matter_[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-Gyarmati_1970-10)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-Glansdorff&Pr1971-5)[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-Balescu_1975-30)[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-Mihalas_Mihalas_1984-31)[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-Schloegl_1989-32) (see also Keizer (1987)[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-Keizer_1987-33) means that conceptually, for study and analysis, the system can be spatially and temporally divided into 'cells' or 'micro-phases' of small (infinitesimal) size, in which classical thermodynamical equilibrium conditions for matter are fulfilled to good approximation. These conditions are unfulfilled, for example, in very rarefied gases, in which molecular collisions are infrequent; and in the boundary layers of a star, where radiation is passing energy to space; and for interacting fermions at very low temperature, where dissipative processes become ineffective. When these 'cells' are defined, one admits that matter and energy may pass freely between contiguous 'cells', slowly enough to leave the 'cells' in their respective individual local thermodynamic equilibria with respect to intensive variables.

One can think here of two 'relaxation times' separated by order of magnitude.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-Zubarev_1971/1974-38) The longer relaxation time is of the order of magnitude of times taken for the macroscopic dynamical structure of the system to change. The shorter is of the order of magnitude of times taken for a single 'cell' to reach local thermodynamic equilibrium. If these two relaxation times are not well separated, then the classical non-equilibrium thermodynamical concept of local thermodynamic equilibrium loses its meaning[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-Zubarev_1971/1974-38) and other approaches have to be proposed, see for instance [Extended irreversible thermodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extended_irreversible_thermodynamics "Extended irreversible thermodynamics"). For example, in the atmosphere, the speed of sound is much greater than the wind speed; this favours the idea of local thermodynamic equilibrium of matter for atmospheric heat transfer studies at altitudes below about 60 km where sound propagates, but not above 100 km, where, because of the paucity of intermolecular collisions, sound does not propagate.

### Milne's definition in terms of radiative equilibrium

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Milne's definition in terms of radiative equilibrium")]

[Edward A. Milne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edward_Arthur_Milne "Edward Arthur Milne"), thinking about stars, gave a definition of 'local thermodynamic equilibrium' in terms of the [thermal radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermal_radiation "Thermal radiation") of the [matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matter "Matter") in each small local 'cell'.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-Milne_1928-39) He defined 'local thermodynamic equilibrium' in a 'cell' by requiring that it macroscopically absorb and spontaneously emit radiation as if it were in radiative equilibrium in a cavity at the [temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperature "Temperature") of the matter of the 'cell'. Then it strictly obeys Kirchhoff's law of equality of radiative emissivity and absorptivity, with a black body source function. The key to local thermodynamic equilibrium here is that the rate of collisions of ponderable matter particles such as molecules should far exceed the rates of creation and annihilation of photons.

Entropy in evolving systems
---------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Entropy in evolving systems")]

It is pointed out by W.T. Grandy Jr,[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-40)[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-41)[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-42)[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-43) that entropy, though it may be defined for a non-equilibrium system is—when strictly considered—only a macroscopic quantity that refers to the whole system, and is not a dynamical variable and in general does not act as a local potential that describes local physical forces. Under special circumstances, however, one can metaphorically think as if the thermal variables behaved like local physical forces. The approximation that constitutes classical irreversible thermodynamics is built on this metaphoric thinking.

This point of view shares many points in common with the concept and the use of entropy in continuum thermomechanics,[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-44)[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-45)[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-46)[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-47) which evolved completely independently of statistical mechanics and maximum-entropy principles.

### Entropy in non-equilibrium

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: Entropy in non-equilibrium")]

To describe deviation of the thermodynamic system from equilibrium, in addition to constitutive variables ![Image 9: {\displaystyle x_{1},x_{2},...,x_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/67ba2d5eca79eb4f7088ba3bd32c1800779d038a) that are used to fix the equilibrium state, as was described above, a set of variables ![Image 10: {\displaystyle \xi _{1},\xi _{2},\ldots }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8a3e189b3fe1a6429f8bbe0416f2f83befa515a0) that are called _internal variables_ have been introduced. The equilibrium state is considered to be stable and the main property of the internal variables, as measures of non-equilibrium of the system, is their tending to disappear; the local law of disappearing can be written as relaxation equation for each internal variable

![Image 11: {\displaystyle {\frac {d\xi _{i}}{dt}}=-{\frac {1}{\tau _{i}}}\,\left(\xi _{i}-\xi _{i}^{(0)}\right),\quad i=1,\,2,\ldots ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/31dd3110991e9b30247aca7d92c2a6acc262e76d)1

where ![Image 12: {\displaystyle \tau _{i}=\tau _{i}(T,x_{1},x_{2},\ldots ,x_{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/74deeeb02f7dea0d346358595241245100ef2f9a) is a relaxation time of a corresponding variables. It is convenient to consider the initial value ![Image 13: {\displaystyle \xi _{i}^{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/24b8808d0463fedc2e0a15af6c64fb422006e846) are equal to zero. The above equation is valid for small deviations from equilibrium; The dynamics of internal variables in general case is considered by Pokrovskii.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-dx.doi.org-48)

Entropy of the system in non-equilibrium is a function of the total set of variables

![Image 14: {\displaystyle S=S(T,x_{1},x_{2},,x_{n};\xi _{1},\xi _{2},\ldots )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c2fb5aedb7f035d4f04abe93df29f828fa0c5082)1

The essential contribution to the thermodynamics of the non-equilibrium systems was brought by the [Nobel Prize](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nobel_Prize "Nobel Prize") winner Ilya [Prigogine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prigogine "Prigogine"), when he and his collaborators investigated the systems of chemically reacting substances. The stationary states of such systems exists due to exchange both particles and energy with the environment. In section 8 of the third chapter of his book,[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-49) Prigogine has specified three contributions to the variation of entropy of the considered system at the given volume and constant temperature ![Image 15: {\displaystyle T}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ec7200acd984a1d3a3d7dc455e262fbe54f7f6e0) . The increment of [entropy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy "Entropy")![Image 16: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2) can be calculated according to the formula

![Image 17: {\displaystyle T\,dS=\Delta Q-\sum _{j}\,\Xi _{j}\,\Delta \xi _{j}+\sum _{\alpha =1}^{k}\,\eta _{\alpha }\,\Delta N_{\alpha }.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/490c3347f6770214cfbba847081320dfe8b83f1b)1

The first term on the right hand side of the equation presents a stream of thermal energy into the system; the last term—a part of a stream of energy ![Image 18: {\displaystyle h_{\alpha }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a364c19a306fa879b0ac9f394fdf0ce396d700f7) coming into the system with the stream of particles of substances ![Image 19: {\displaystyle \Delta N_{\alpha }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3b6283736e515b5edf3db46dbc97769bb95fc4f5) that can be positive or negative, ![Image 20: {\displaystyle \eta _{\alpha }=h_{\alpha }-\mu _{\alpha }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e5ddbc7a99825f0111bb086825c59ba222ef51ec), where ![Image 21: {\displaystyle \mu _{\alpha }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0393f09a0a781a6f6867efd4fcf1d3dca3cb1ebe) is [chemical potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_potential "Chemical potential") of substance ![Image 22: {\displaystyle \alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b79333175c8b3f0840bfb4ec41b8072c83ea88d3). The middle term in (1) depicts [energy dissipation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_dissipation "Energy dissipation") ([entropy production](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy_production "Entropy production")) due to the relaxation of internal variables ![Image 23: {\displaystyle \xi _{j}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4c210bf2eff47302d0a01f03a56856cdad76dc7). In the case of chemically reacting substances, which was investigated by Prigogine, the internal variables appear to be measures of incompleteness of chemical reactions, that is measures of how much the considered system with chemical reactions is out of equilibrium. The theory can be generalised,[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-50)[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-dx.doi.org-48) to consider any deviation from the equilibrium state as an internal variable, so that we consider the set of internal variables ![Image 24: {\displaystyle \xi _{j}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4c210bf2eff47302d0a01f03a56856cdad76dc7) in equation (1) to consist of the quantities defining not only degrees of completeness of all chemical reactions occurring in the system, but also the structure of the system, gradients of temperature, difference of concentrations of substances and so on.

The fundamental relation of classical equilibrium thermodynamics [[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-W._Greiner_et._al._1997-51)

![Image 25: {\displaystyle dS={\frac {1}{T}}dU+{\frac {p}{T}}dV-\sum _{i=1}^{s}{\frac {\mu _{i}}{T}}dN_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fd6dd26b4bd6632d3f2633d9bc3d7538f18b58c7)
expresses the change in [entropy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy "Entropy")![Image 26: {\displaystyle dS}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/66f6ab8a2a8b85df78efbea896fcfeb3d4ea39e4) of a system as a function of the intensive quantities [temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperature "Temperature")![Image 27: {\displaystyle T}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ec7200acd984a1d3a3d7dc455e262fbe54f7f6e0), [pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pressure "Pressure")![Image 28: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) and ![Image 29: {\displaystyle i^{th}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9c454bb35f050556d361ee85e06ca923b16a3bf4)[chemical potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_potential "Chemical potential")![Image 30: {\displaystyle \mu _{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dea0a0293841cce9eef98b55e53a92b82ae59ee4) and of the differentials of the extensive quantities [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy "Energy")![Image 31: {\displaystyle U}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/458a728f53b9a0274f059cd695e067c430956025), [volume](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume_(thermodynamics) "Volume (thermodynamics)")![Image 32: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) and ![Image 33: {\displaystyle i^{th}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9c454bb35f050556d361ee85e06ca923b16a3bf4)[particle number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_number "Particle number")![Image 34: {\displaystyle N_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fef58cebf23adff9199f17325aefb5515fdca99d).

Following Onsager (1931,I),[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-Onsager_1931_I-17) let us extend our considerations to thermodynamically non-equilibrium systems. As a basis, we need locally defined versions of the extensive macroscopic quantities ![Image 35: {\displaystyle U}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/458a728f53b9a0274f059cd695e067c430956025), ![Image 36: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) and ![Image 37: {\displaystyle N_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fef58cebf23adff9199f17325aefb5515fdca99d) and of the intensive macroscopic quantities ![Image 38: {\displaystyle T}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ec7200acd984a1d3a3d7dc455e262fbe54f7f6e0), ![Image 39: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) and ![Image 40: {\displaystyle \mu _{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dea0a0293841cce9eef98b55e53a92b82ae59ee4).

For classical non-equilibrium studies, we will consider some new locally defined intensive macroscopic variables. We can, under suitable conditions, derive these new variables by locally defining the gradients and flux densities of the basic locally defined macroscopic quantities.

Such locally defined gradients of intensive macroscopic variables are called 'thermodynamic forces'. They 'drive' flux densities, perhaps misleadingly often called 'fluxes', which are dual to the forces. These quantities are defined in the article on [Onsager reciprocal relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Onsager_reciprocal_relations "Onsager reciprocal relations").

Establishing the relation between such forces and flux densities is a problem in statistical mechanics. Flux densities (![Image 41: {\displaystyle J_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/07a98dbd556141d7f0a16eed84fe0ae6467dcd23)) may be coupled. The article on Onsager reciprocal relations considers the stable near-steady thermodynamically non-equilibrium regime, which has dynamics linear in the forces and flux densities.

In stationary conditions, such forces and associated flux densities are by definition time invariant, as also are the system's locally defined entropy and rate of entropy production. Notably, according to [Ilya Prigogine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ilya_Prigogine "Ilya Prigogine") and others, when an open system is in conditions that allow it to reach a stable stationary thermodynamically non-equilibrium state, it organizes itself so as to minimize total entropy production defined locally. This is considered further below.

One wants to take the analysis to the further stage of describing the behaviour of surface and volume integrals of non-stationary local quantities; these integrals are macroscopic fluxes and production rates. In general the dynamics of these integrals are not adequately described by linear equations, though in special cases they can be so described.

### Onsager reciprocal relations

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics&action=edit&section=17 "Edit section: Onsager reciprocal relations")]

Following Section III of Rayleigh (1873),[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-Rayleigh_1873-16) Onsager (1931, I)[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-Onsager_1931_I-17) showed that in the regime where both the flows (![Image 42: {\displaystyle J_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/07a98dbd556141d7f0a16eed84fe0ae6467dcd23)) are small and the thermodynamic forces (![Image 43: {\displaystyle F_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/18f9a5ed62f131c8ad9acdf7f8539f103f1e0ec9)) vary slowly, the rate of creation of entropy ![Image 44: {\displaystyle (\sigma )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/24123087fce149a1c5c8e9ca3e8db078bc97dd76) is [linearly related](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_relation "Linear relation") to the flows:

![Image 45: {\displaystyle \sigma =\sum _{i}J_{i}{\frac {\partial F_{i}}{\partial x_{i}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b7275cdbe4b108870f7fb738347177c0b27cdadd)
and the flows are related to the gradient of the forces, parametrized by a [matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_(mathematics) "Matrix (mathematics)") of coefficients conventionally denoted ![Image 46: {\displaystyle L}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/103168b86f781fe6e9a4a87b8ea1cebe0ad4ede8):

![Image 47: {\displaystyle J_{i}=\sum _{j}L_{ij}{\frac {\partial F_{j}}{\partial x_{j}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7e97a64431f1a776f876b0241ed148af66a46f43)
from which it follows that:

![Image 48: {\displaystyle \sigma =\sum _{i,j}L_{ij}{\frac {\partial F_{i}}{\partial x_{i}}}{\frac {\partial F_{j}}{\partial x_{j}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e3ba3db8d8f78da84d23c4b063987bb0ef84ae14)
The [second law of thermodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_law_of_thermodynamics "Second law of thermodynamics") requires that the matrix ![Image 49: {\displaystyle L}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/103168b86f781fe6e9a4a87b8ea1cebe0ad4ede8) be [positive definite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positive-definite_matrix "Positive-definite matrix"). [Statistical mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistical_mechanics "Statistical mechanics") considerations involving microscopic reversibility of dynamics imply that the matrix ![Image 50: {\displaystyle L}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/103168b86f781fe6e9a4a87b8ea1cebe0ad4ede8) is [symmetric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetric_matrix "Symmetric matrix"). This fact is called the _Onsager reciprocal relations_.

The generalization of the above equations for the rate of creation of entropy was given by Pokrovskii.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-dx.doi.org-48)

Speculated extremal principles for non-equilibrium processes
------------------------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics&action=edit&section=18 "Edit section: Speculated extremal principles for non-equilibrium processes")]

Until recently, prospects for useful extremal principles in this area have seemed clouded. Nicolis (1999)[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-52) concludes that one model of atmospheric dynamics has an attractor which is not a regime of maximum or minimum dissipation; she says this seems to rule out the existence of a global organizing principle, and comments that this is to some extent disappointing; she also points to the difficulty of finding a thermodynamically consistent form of entropy production. Another top expert offers an extensive discussion of the possibilities for principles of extrema of entropy production and of dissipation of energy: Chapter 12 of Grandy (2008)[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-Grandy_2008-53) is very cautious, and finds difficulty in defining the 'rate of internal entropy production' in many cases, and finds that sometimes for the prediction of the course of a process, an extremum of the quantity called the rate of dissipation of energy may be more useful than that of the rate of entropy production; this quantity appeared in Onsager's 1931[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-Onsager_1931_I-17) origination of this subject. Other writers have also felt that prospects for general global extremal principles are clouded. Such writers include Glansdorff and Prigogine (1971), Lebon, Jou and Casas-Vásquez (2008), and Šilhavý (1997). There is good experimental evidence that heat convection does not obey extremal principles for time rate of entropy production.[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-Attard_2012-54) Theoretical analysis shows that chemical reactions do not obey extremal principles for the second differential of time rate of entropy production.[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-Keizer_1974-55) The development of a general extremal principle seems infeasible in the current state of knowledge.

Non-equilibrium thermodynamics has been successfully applied to describe biological processes such as [protein folding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protein_folding "Protein folding")/unfolding and [transport through membranes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Membrane_transport "Membrane transport").[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-56)[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-57) It is also used to give a description of the dynamics of [nanoparticles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nanoparticle "Nanoparticle"), which can be out of equilibrium in systems where catalysis and electrochemical conversion is involved.[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-58) Also, ideas from non-equilibrium thermodynamics and the informatic theory of entropy have been adapted to describe general economic systems.[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-59)[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics#cite_note-60)
