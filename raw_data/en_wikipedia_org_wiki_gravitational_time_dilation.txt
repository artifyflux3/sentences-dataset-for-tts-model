For broader coverage of this topic, see [Time dilation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_dilation "Time dilation").

**Gravitational time dilation** is a form of [time dilation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_dilation "Time dilation"), an actual difference of elapsed time between two [events](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Event_(relativity) "Event (relativity)"), as measured by [observers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observer_(special_relativity) "Observer (special relativity)") situated at varying distances from a gravitating [mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass "Mass"). The lower the [gravitational potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_potential "Gravitational potential") (the closer the clock is to the source of gravitation), the slower time passes, speeding up as the gravitational potential increases (the clock moving away from the source of gravitation). [Albert Einstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Einstein "Albert Einstein") originally predicted this in his [theory of relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theory_of_relativity "Theory of relativity"), and it has since been confirmed by [tests of general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity "Tests of general relativity").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_time_dilation#cite_note-1)

This effect has been demonstrated by noting that [atomic clocks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_clock "Atomic clock") at differing [altitudes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Altitude "Altitude") (and thus different gravitational potential) will eventually show different times. The effects detected in such Earth-bound experiments are extremely small, with differences being measured in [nanoseconds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nanosecond "Nanosecond"). Relative to Earth's age in billions of years, Earth's core is in effect 2.5 years younger than its surface.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_time_dilation#cite_note-2) Demonstrating larger effects would require measurements at greater distances from the Earth, or a larger gravitational source.

Gravitational time dilation was first described by Albert Einstein in 1907[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_time_dilation#cite_note-3) as a consequence of [special relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_relativity "Special relativity") in accelerated frames of reference. In [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity"), it is considered to be a difference in the passage of [proper time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proper_time "Proper time") at different positions as described by a [metric tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_tensor_(general_relativity) "Metric tensor (general relativity)") of spacetime. The existence of gravitational time dilation was first confirmed directly by the [Pound–Rebka experiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pound%E2%80%93Rebka_experiment "Pound–Rebka experiment") in 1959, and later refined by [Gravity Probe A](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity_Probe_A "Gravity Probe A") and other experiments.

Gravitational time dilation is closely related to [gravitational redshift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_redshift "Gravitational redshift"),[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_time_dilation#cite_note-Cheng_2010_p._72-4) in which the closer a body emitting light of constant frequency is to a gravitating body, the more its time is slowed by gravitational time dilation, and the lower (more "redshifted") the frequency of the emitted light would seem, as measured by a fixed observer.

[Clocks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clock "Clock") that are far from massive bodies (or at higher gravitational potentials) run more quickly, and clocks close to massive bodies (or at lower gravitational potentials) run more slowly. For example, considered over the total time-span of Earth (4.6 billion years), a clock set in a geostationary position at an altitude of 9,000 meters above sea level, such as perhaps at the top of [Mount Everest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mount_Everest "Mount Everest") ([prominence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topographic_prominence "Topographic prominence") 8,848 m), would be about 39 hours ahead of a clock set at sea level.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_time_dilation#cite_note-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_time_dilation#cite_note-6) This is because gravitational time dilation is manifested in accelerated [frames of reference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frame_of_reference "Frame of reference") or, by virtue of the [equivalence principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equivalence_principle "Equivalence principle"), in the gravitational field of massive objects.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_time_dilation#cite_note-7)

According to general relativity, [inertial mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass#Inertial_mass "Mass") and [gravitational mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_mass "Gravitational mass") are the same, and all accelerated reference frames (such as a [uniformly rotating reference frame](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Born_coordinates "Born coordinates") with its proper time dilation) are physically equivalent to a gravitational field of the same strength.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_time_dilation#cite_note-8)

Outside a non-rotating sphere
-----------------------------

Important features of gravitational time dilation
-------------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Gravitational_time_dilation&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Important features of gravitational time dilation")]

*   According to [the general theory of relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity"), gravitational time dilation is copresent with the existence of an [accelerated reference frame](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerated_reference_frame "Accelerated reference frame"). Additionally, all physical phenomena in similar circumstances undergo time dilation equally according to the [equivalence principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equivalence_principle "Equivalence principle") used in [the general theory of relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity").
*   The speed of light in a locale is always equal to _c_ according to the observer who is there. That is, every infinitesimal region of spacetime may be assigned its own proper time, and the speed of light according to the proper time at that region is always _c_. This is the case whether or not a given region is occupied by an observer. A [time delay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shapiro_effect "Shapiro effect") can be measured for photons which are emitted from Earth, bend near the Sun, travel to Venus, and then return to Earth along a similar path. There is no violation of the constancy of the speed of light here, as any observer observing the speed of photons in their region will find the speed of those photons to be _c_, while the speed at which we observe light travel finite distances in the vicinity of the Sun will differ from _c_.
*   If an observer is able to track the light in a remote, distant locale which intercepts a remote, time dilated observer nearer to a more massive body, that first observer tracks that both the remote light and that remote time dilated observer have a slower time clock than other light which is coming to the first observer at _c_, like all other light the first observer _really_ can observe (at their own location). If the other, remote light eventually intercepts the first observer, it too will be measured at _c_ by the first observer.
*   Gravitational time dilation ![Image 31: {\displaystyle T}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ec7200acd984a1d3a3d7dc455e262fbe54f7f6e0) in a gravitational well is equal to the [velocity time dilation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity_time_dilation "Velocity time dilation") for a speed that is needed to escape that gravitational well (given that the metric is of the form ![Image 32: {\displaystyle g=(dt/T(x))^{2}-g_{space}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/94fb53d0a514c76c4d2c348c913ff142f95e5cc4), i. e. it is time invariant and there are no "movement" terms ![Image 33: {\displaystyle dxdt}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/75fc498b9d84152701c470c2779b0d48eeea9363)). To show that, one can apply [Noether's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noether%27s_theorem "Noether's theorem") to a body that freely falls into the well from infinity. Then the time invariance of the metric implies conservation of the quantity ![Image 34: {\displaystyle g(v,dt)=v^{0}/T^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1862f5a4c92dc5bc568e9aab85d8ff4ea5b62f75), where ![Image 35: {\displaystyle v^{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/275a49c03403b8831a57e605ad95944519a1776d) is the time component of the [4-velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Four-velocity "Four-velocity")![Image 36: {\displaystyle v}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e07b00e7fc0847fbd16391c778d65bc25c452597) of the body. At the infinity ![Image 37: {\displaystyle g(v,dt)=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8d8b1a95fecd06cd26c32e235c729d3071866d5c), so ![Image 38: {\displaystyle v^{0}=T^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/be54085123fc466db948b9d71b80d3a6b6baf34f), or, in coordinates adjusted to the local time dilation, ![Image 39: {\displaystyle v_{loc}^{0}=T}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/909a703c4df54ecf818ae85302f48e586cf41c1f); that is, time dilation due to acquired velocity (as measured at the falling body's position) equals to the gravitational time dilation in the well the body fell into. Applying this argument more generally one gets that (under the same assumptions on the metric) the relative gravitational time dilation between two points equals to the time dilation due to velocity needed to climb from the lower point to the higher.

Experimental confirmation
-------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Gravitational_time_dilation&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Experimental confirmation")]

[![Image 40](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Orbit_times.svg/330px-Orbit_times.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Orbit_times.svg)

Satellite clocks are slowed by their orbital speed, but accelerated by their distance out of Earth's gravitational well.

Gravitational time dilation has been experimentally measured using atomic clocks on airplanes, such as the [Hafele–Keating experiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hafele%E2%80%93Keating_experiment "Hafele–Keating experiment"). The clocks aboard the airplanes were slightly faster than clocks on the ground. The effect is significant enough that the [Global Positioning System's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_Positioning_System "Global Positioning System")[artificial satellites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satellite "Satellite") had their atomic clocks permanently corrected.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_time_dilation#cite_note-Wolfson-13)

Additionally, time dilations due to height differences of less than one metre have been experimentally verified in the laboratory.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_time_dilation#cite_note-14)

Gravitational time dilation in the form of [gravitational redshift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_redshift "Gravitational redshift") has also been confirmed by the [Pound–Rebka experiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pound%E2%80%93Rebka_experiment "Pound–Rebka experiment") and observations of the spectra of the [white dwarf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_dwarf "White dwarf")[Sirius B](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sirius_B "Sirius B").

Gravitational time dilation has been measured in experiments with time signals sent to and from the [Viking 1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viking_1 "Viking 1") Mars lander.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_time_dilation#cite_note-15)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_time_dilation#cite_note-16)
