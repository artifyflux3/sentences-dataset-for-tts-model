The **foot** (standard symbol: **ft**)[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_foot#cite_note-IEEE-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_foot#cite_note-2) is a [unit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Units_of_measurement "Units of measurement") of [length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Length "Length") in the [British imperial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imperial_units "Imperial units") and [United States customary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_customary_units "United States customary units") systems of [measurement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metrology "Metrology"). The [prime symbol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_(symbol) "Prime (symbol)"), ′, is commonly used to represent the foot.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_foot#cite_note-3) In both customary and imperial units, one foot comprises 12[inches](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inch "Inch"), and one [yard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yard "Yard") comprises three feet. Since [an international agreement in 1959](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_yard_and_pound "International yard and pound"), the foot is defined as equal to exactly 0.3048[meters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meter "Meter").

Historically, the "foot" was a part of many local systems of units, including the [Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek_units_of_measurement "Ancient Greek units of measurement"), [Roman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Roman_units_of_measurement "Ancient Roman units of measurement"), [Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_units_of_measurement "Chinese units of measurement"), [French](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Units_of_measurement_in_France_before_the_French_Revolution "Units of measurement in France before the French Revolution"), and [English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English_units "English units") systems. It varied in length from country to country, from city to city, and sometimes from trade to trade. Its length was usually between 250 mm (9.8 in) and 335 mm (13.2 in) and was generally, but not always, subdivided into twelve inches or 16[digits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digit_(unit) "Digit (unit)").

The United States is the only [industrialized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Industrialisation "Industrialisation") country that uses the (international) foot in preference to the meter in its commercial, engineering, and standards activities.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_foot#cite_note-World_Factbook-4) The foot is legally recognized in the United Kingdom; road distance signs _must_ use imperial units (however, distances on road signs are always marked in miles or yards, not feet; bridge clearances are given in meters as well as feet and inches), while its usage is widespread among the British public as a measurement of height.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_foot#cite_note-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_foot#cite_note-6) The foot is recognized as an alternative expression of length in Canada.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_foot#cite_note-7) Both the UK and Canada have partially [metricated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metrication "Metrication") their units of measurement. The measurement of [altitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Altitude#Aviation "Altitude") in international [aviation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aviation "Aviation") (the [flight level](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flight_level "Flight level") unit) is one of the few areas where the foot is used outside the English-speaking world.

The most common plural of foot is **feet**. However, the singular form may be used like a plural when it is preceded by a number, as in "he is six foot tall."[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_foot#cite_note-8)

Historically, the human body has been used to provide the basis for units of length.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_foot#cite_note-Dilke1987-23-9) The foot of an adult European-American male is typically about 15.3% of his height,[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_foot#cite_note-10) giving a person of 175 cm (5 ft 9 in) a foot-length of about 268 mm (10.6 in), on average.

Archaeologists believe that, in the past, the people of [Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Egyptian_units_of_measurement "Ancient Egyptian units of measurement"), [India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_units_of_measurement "Indian units of measurement"), and [Mesopotamia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Mesopotamian_units_of_measurement "Ancient Mesopotamian units of measurement") preferred the [cubit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cubit "Cubit"), while the people of [Rome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Rome "Ancient Rome"), [Greece](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greece "Ancient Greece"), and [China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_units_of_measurement "Chinese units of measurement") preferred the foot[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]. Under the Harappan linear measures, Indus cities during the Bronze Age used a foot of 13.2 inches (335 mm) and a cubit of 20.8 inches (528 mm).[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_foot#cite_note-11) The [Egyptian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Egyptian_units_of_measurement "Ancient Egyptian units of measurement") equivalent of the foot—a measure of four palms or 16 digits—was known as the _djeser_ and has been reconstructed as about 30 cm (11.8 in).

The Greek foot (πούς, _pous_) had a length of ⁠1/600⁠ of a [stadion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stadion_(unit) "Stadion (unit)"),[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_foot#cite_note-12) one stadion being about 181.2 m (594 ft);[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_foot#cite_note-13) therefore a foot was, at the time, about 302 mm (11.9 in). Its exact size varied from city to city and could range between 270 mm (10.6 in) and 350 mm (13.8 in), but lengths used for temple construction appear to have been about 295 mm (11.6 in) to 325 mm (12.8 in); the former was close to the size of the Roman foot.

The standard [Roman foot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_foot "Roman foot") (_pes_) was normally about 295.7 mm (11.6 in) (97% of today's measurement),[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_foot#cite_note-brit-14) but in some provinces, particularly [Germania Inferior](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germania_Inferior "Germania Inferior"), the so-called _pes Drusianus_ (foot of [Nero Claudius Drusus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nero_Claudius_Drusus "Nero Claudius Drusus")) was sometimes used, with a length of about 334 mm (13.1 in). (In reality, this foot predated Drusus.)[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_foot#cite_note-Dilke1987-26-15)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_foot#cite_note-16)

Originally both the Greeks and the Romans subdivided the foot into 16 [digits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digit_(unit) "Digit (unit)"), but in later years, the Romans also subdivided the foot into 12 _unciae_ (from which both the English words "inch" and "[ounce](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ounce "Ounce")" are derived).

After the fall of the Roman Empire, some Roman traditions were continued but others fell into disuse. In AD 790 [Charlemagne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charlemagne "Charlemagne") attempted to reform the units of measure in his domains. His units of length were based on the _[toise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toise "Toise")_ and in particular the _toise de l'Écritoire_, the distance between the fingertips of the outstretched arms of a man.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_foot#cite_note-17) The _toise_ has 6 _pieds_ (feet) each of 326.6 mm (12.9 in).

He was unsuccessful in introducing a standard unit of length throughout his realm: an analysis of the measurements of [Charlieu Abbey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charlieu_Abbey "Charlieu Abbey") shows that during the 9th century the Roman foot of 296.1 mm (11.66 in) was used; when it was rebuilt in the 10th century, a foot of about 320 mm (12.6 in)[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_foot#cite_note-cm-18) was used. At the same time, monastic buildings used the Carolingian foot of 340 mm (13.4 in).[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_foot#cite_note-cm-18)[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_foot#cite_note-19)

The procedure for verification of the foot as described in the 16th century posthumously published work by [Jacob Köbel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacob_K%C3%B6bel "Jacob Köbel") in his book _Geometrei. Von künstlichem Feldmessen und absehen_ is:[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_foot#cite_note-20)[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_foot#cite_note-21)

> Stand at the door of a church on a Sunday and bid 16 men to stop, tall ones and small ones, as they happen to pass out when the service is finished; then make them put their left feet one behind the other, and the length thus obtained shall be a right and lawful [rood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rood_(unit)#Linear_measure "Rood (unit)") to measure and survey the land with, and the 16th part of it shall be the right and lawful foot.

The unofficial public [imperial measurement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imperial_units "Imperial units")[standards](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_(metrology) "Standard (metrology)") erected at the [Royal Observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Observatory,_Greenwich "Royal Observatory, Greenwich") in [Greenwich](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenwich,_England "Greenwich, England") in the 19th century

The [Neolithic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neolithic "Neolithic")**long foot**, first proposed by archeologists [Mike Parker Pearson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mike_Parker_Pearson "Mike Parker Pearson") and Andrew Chamberlain, is based upon calculations from surveys of [Phase 1 elements at Stonehenge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stonehenge#Stonehenge_1_(c._3100_BC) "Stonehenge"). They found that the underlying diameters of the stone circles had been consistently laid out using multiples of a base unit amounting to 30 **long feet**, which they calculated to be 1.056 of a modern [international foot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_foot#International_foot) (thus 12.672 inches or 0.3219 m). Furthermore, this unit is identifiable in the dimensions of some stone [lintels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lintels "Lintels") at the site, and in the diameter of the "southern circle" at nearby [Durrington Walls](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Durrington_Walls "Durrington Walls"). Evidence that this unit was in widespread use across southern Britain is available from the [Folkton Drums](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Folkton_Drums "Folkton Drums") from [Yorkshire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yorkshire "Yorkshire") ([neolithic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neolithic "Neolithic") artifacts, made from chalk, with circumferences that exactly divide as [integers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integers "Integers") into ten long feet) and a similar object, the [Lavant drum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lavant_drum "Lavant drum"), excavated at [Lavant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lavant,_West_Sussex "Lavant, West Sussex"), Sussex, again with a circumference divisible as a whole number into ten long feet.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_foot#cite_note-22)

The measures of [Iron Age Britain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron_Age_Britain "Iron Age Britain") are uncertain, and proposed reconstructions such as the [Megalithic Yard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Megalithic_Yard "Megalithic Yard") are controversial. Later [Welsh legend](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Welsh_legend "Welsh legend") credited [Dyfnwal Moelmud](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dyfnwal_Moelmud "Dyfnwal Moelmud") with the establishment of [their units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Welsh_units "Welsh units"), including a foot of 9 inches. The Belgic or North German foot of 335 mm (13.2 in) was introduced to England either by the [Belgic Celts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belgae "Belgae") during their invasions prior to the [Roman conquest of England](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_conquest_of_England "Roman conquest of England") (AD 43), or by the [Anglo-Saxons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anglo-Saxons "Anglo-Saxons") in the 5th and 6th centuries.

[Roman units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_units "Roman units") were introduced following their conquest. After the [Roman withdrawal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_withdrawal_from_Britain "Roman withdrawal from Britain") and the [Saxon invasions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saxon_invasion_of_England "Saxon invasion of England"), the Roman foot continued to be used in the construction crafts, while the Belgic foot was used for land measurement. Both the Welsh and Belgic feet seem to have been based on multiples of the [barleycorn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barleycorn_(unit) "Barleycorn (unit)"), but by as early as 950 the English kings seem to have (ineffectually) ordered measures to be based upon an iron yardstick at [Winchester](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Winchester "Winchester") and then [London](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/London "London"). [Henry I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry_I_of_England "Henry I of England") was said to have ordered a new standard to be based upon the length of his own arm and, by the c. 1300 Act concerning the [Composition of Yards and Perches](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Composition_of_Yards_and_Perches "Composition of Yards and Perches")[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_foot#cite_note-Britain1762-23) traditionally credited to [Edward I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edward_I_of_England "Edward I of England") or [II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edward_II_of_England "Edward II of England"), the statute foot was a different measure, exactly ⁠10/11⁠ of the old (Belgic) foot. The [barleycorn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barleycorn_(unit) "Barleycorn (unit)"), [inch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inch "Inch"), [ell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ell_(unit) "Ell (unit)"), and [yard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yard "Yard") were likewise shrunk, while [rods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rod_(unit) "Rod (unit)") and [furlongs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Furlong "Furlong") remained the same.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_foot#cite_note-1977zupko-24) The ambiguity over the length of the [mile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mile "Mile") was resolved by the 1593 [Act against Converting of Great Houses into Several Tenements and for Restraint of Inmates and Inclosures in and near about the City of London and Westminster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Act_against_Converting_of_Great_Houses_into_Several_Tenements_and_for_Restraint_of_Inmates_and_Inclosures_in_and_near_about_the_City_of_London_and_Westminster "Act against Converting of Great Houses into Several Tenements and for Restraint of Inmates and Inclosures in and near about the City of London and Westminster"), which codified the [statute mile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statute_mile "Statute mile") as comprising 5,280 feet. The 1959 adoption of the [international foot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_foot#International_foot) completed a redefinition of the foot in terms of the meter.

The [international yard and pound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_yard_and_pound "International yard and pound") agreement of July 1959 defined the length of the international yard in the United States and countries of the [Commonwealth of Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations "Commonwealth of Nations") as exactly 0.9144 [meters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meter "Meter"). Consequently, since a foot is one third of a yard, the international foot is defined as exactly 0.3048 meters. This was 2[ppm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parts_per_million "Parts per million") shorter than the previous US definition and 1.7 ppm longer than the previous British definition.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_foot#cite_note-25)

The 1959 agreement concluded a series of step-by-step events, set off in particular by the [British Standards Institution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Standards_Institution "British Standards Institution")'s adoption of a scientific standard inch of 25.4 [millimeters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Millimeter "Millimeter") in 1930.

The IEEE standard symbol for a foot is "ft".[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_foot#cite_note-IEEE-1) In some cases, the foot is denoted by a [prime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_(symbol) "Prime (symbol)"), often approximated by an [apostrophe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apostrophe "Apostrophe"), and the inch by a double prime; for example, 2 feet 4 inches is sometimes denoted 2′4″.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_foot#cite_note-CMOS-26)

In [Imperial units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imperial_units "Imperial units"), the foot was defined as ⁠1/3⁠yard, with the yard being realized as a physical standard (separate from the standard meter). The yard standards of the different [Commonwealth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations "Commonwealth of Nations") countries were periodically compared with one another.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_foot#cite_note-27) The value of the United Kingdom primary standard of the yard was determined in terms of the meter by the [National Physical Laboratory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Physical_Laboratory_(United_Kingdom) "National Physical Laboratory (United Kingdom)") in 1964 to be 0.914 3969 m,[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_foot#cite_note-28) implying a pre-1959 UK foot of 0.304 7990 m.

The UK adopted the international yard for all purposes through the [Weights and Measures Act 1963](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weights_and_Measures_Act_1963 "Weights and Measures Act 1963"), effective January 1, 1964.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_foot#cite_note-29)

When the international foot was defined in 1959, a great deal of survey data was already available based on the former definitions, especially in the United States and in [India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/India "India"). The small difference between the survey foot and the international foot would not be detectable on a survey of a small parcel, but becomes significant for mapping, or when the [state plane coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_Plane_Coordinate_System "State Plane Coordinate System") (SPCS) is used in the US, because the origin of the system may be hundreds of thousands of feet (hundreds of miles) from the point of interest. Hence the previous definitions continued to be used for surveying in the United States and India for many years, and are denoted **survey feet** to distinguish them from the international foot. The United Kingdom was unaffected by this problem, as the [retriangulation of Great Britain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Retriangulation_of_Great_Britain "Retriangulation of Great Britain") (1936–62) had been done in meters.

In the United States, the foot was defined as 12 inches, with the inch being defined by the [Mendenhall Order](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mendenhall_Order "Mendenhall Order") of 1893 via 39.37 inches= 1 m (making a US foot exactly ⁠1200/3937⁠meters, approximately 0.304 800 61 m).[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_foot#cite_note-NYT_2020-08-18-30)[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_foot#cite_note-31)

On December 31, 2022, the [National Institute of Standards and Technology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Institute_of_Standards_and_Technology "National Institute of Standards and Technology"), the [National Geodetic Survey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Geodetic_Survey "National Geodetic Survey"), and the [United States Department of Commerce](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Commerce "United States Department of Commerce") deprecated use of the US survey foot and recommended conversion to either the meter or the [international foot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_foot "International foot") (0.3048 m).[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_foot#cite_note-32)[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_foot#cite_note-33)[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_foot#cite_note-NYT_2020-08-18-30) However, the historic relevance of the US survey foot persists, as the _[Federal Register](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federal\_Register "Federal Register")_ notes:[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_foot#cite_note-34)

> The date of December 31, 2022, was selected to accompany the modernization of the [National Spatial Reference System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Spatial_Reference_System "National Spatial Reference System") (NSRS) by [NOAA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration "National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration")'s National Geodetic Survey (NGS). The reason for associating the deprecation of the U.S. survey foot with the modernization of the NSRS is that the biggest impact of the uniform adoption of the international foot will be for users of the NSRS, due to very large coordinate values currently given in U.S. survey feet in many areas of the U.S. Impacts related to the change to international feet will be minimized if a transition occurs concurrently with others [_[sic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sic "Sic")_] changes in the NSRS. ...
> 
> 
> The difference in timelines will have no effect on users of the existing NSRS (National Spatial Reference System), because NGS (NOAA's National Geodetic Survey) _will continue to support the U.S. survey foot for components of the NSRS where it is used now and in the past_ [emphasis added]. In other words, to minimize disruption in the use of U.S. survey foot for existing NSRS coordinate systems, the change will apply only to the modernized NSRS.

State legislation is also important for determining the conversion factor to be used for everyday land surveying and real estate transactions, although the difference (two [parts per million](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parts_per_million "Parts per million")) is of no practical significance given the precision of normal surveying measurements over short distances (usually much less than a mile). Out of 50 states and six other jurisdictions, 40 have legislated that surveying measures should be based on the US survey foot, six have legislated that they be made on the basis of the international foot, and ten have not specified.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_foot#cite_note-35)

The Indian survey foot is defined as exactly 0.304 7996 m,[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_foot#cite_note-36) presumably derived from a measurement of the previous Indian standard of the yard. The current National Topographic Database of the [Survey of India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Survey_of_India "Survey of India") is based on the metric [WGS-84](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WGS-84 "WGS-84")[datum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Datum_(geodesy) "Datum (geodesy)"),[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_foot#cite_note-37) which is also used by the [Global Positioning System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_Positioning_System "Global Positioning System").

An [ISO 2848](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO_2848 "ISO 2848") measure of 3 basic modules (30 cm) is called a "[metric foot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_foot "Metric foot")",[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] but there were earlier distinct definitions of a metric foot during [metrication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metrication "Metrication") in France and Germany.

In 1799 the meter became the official unit of length in [France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/France "France"). This was not fully enforced, and in 1812 [Napoleon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Napoleon "Napoleon") introduced the system of _[mesures usuelles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mesures\_usuelles "Mesures usuelles")_ which restored the traditional French measurements in the retail trade, but redefined them in terms of metric units. The foot, or _pied métrique_, was defined as one third of a meter. This unit continued in use until 1837.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_foot#cite_note-historique-39)

In southwestern Germany in 1806, the [Confederation of the Rhine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confederation_of_the_Rhine "Confederation of the Rhine") was founded and three different _reformed feet_ were defined, all of which were based on the metric system:[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_foot#cite_note-DE1842-40)

*   In [Hesse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hesse "Hesse"), the _Fuß_ (lit.'foot') was redefined as 25 cm (9.8 in).
*   In [Baden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baden "Baden"), the _Fuß_ was redefined as 30 cm (12 in).
*   In the [Palatinate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palatinate_(region) "Palatinate (region)"), the _Fuß_ was redefined as being 33+1⁄3 cm (13.1 in) (as in France).

### Other obsolete feet

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Foot_(unit)&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Other obsolete feet")]

Prior to the introduction of the metric system, many European cities and countries used the foot, but it varied considerably in length: the _voet_ in [Ypres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ypres "Ypres"), Belgium, was 273.8 mm (10.78 in) while the _piede_ in Venice was 347.73 mm (13.690 in). Lists of conversion factors between the various units of measure were given in many European reference works including:

*   Traité, [Paris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paris "Paris") – 1769[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_foot#cite_note-Traite-41)
*   Palaiseau – [Bordeaux](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bordeaux "Bordeaux"): 1816 [[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_foot#cite_note-Palaiseau-42)
*   de Gelder, [Amsterdam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amsterdam "Amsterdam") and [The Hague](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Hague "The Hague") – 1824[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_foot#cite_note-NL2-43)
*   Horace, [Brussels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brussels "Brussels") – 1840[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_foot#cite_note-Horace-44)
*   Noback & Noback (2 volumes), [Leipzig](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leipzig "Leipzig") – 1851[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_foot#cite_note-DE1851-45)[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_foot#cite_note-DE1851V2-46)
*   Bruhns, Leipzig – 1881[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_foot#cite_note-Bruhns-47)

Many of these standards were peculiar to a particular city, especially in Germany (which, before [German unification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_unification "German unification") in 1871, consisted of many kingdoms, principalities, free cities and so on). In many cases the length of the unit was not uniquely fixed: for example, the English foot was stated as 11 _pouces_ 2.6 _lignes_ ([French inches and lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Units_of_measurement_in_France_before_the_French_Revolution "Units of measurement in France before the French Revolution")) by [Picard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_Picard "Jean Picard"), 11 _pouces_ 3.11 _lignes_ by [Maskelyne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nevil_Maskelyne "Nevil Maskelyne"), and 11 _pouces_ 3 _lignes_ by [D'Alembert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_le_Rond_d%27Alembert "Jean le Rond d'Alembert").[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_foot#cite_note-48)

Most of the various feet in this list ceased to be used when the countries adopted the metric system. The Netherlands and modern Belgium adopted the metric system in 1817, having used the _mesures usuelles_ under Napoleon[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_foot#cite_note-49) and the newly formed [German Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_Empire "German Empire") adopted the metric system in 1871.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_foot#cite_note-50)

The [palm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palm_(unit) "Palm (unit)") (typically 200–280 mm, ie. 7⁠7/8⁠ to 11⁠1/32⁠ inches) was used in many Mediterranean cities instead of the foot. Horace Doursther, whose reference was published[_[clarification needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Please\_clarify "Wikipedia:Please clarify")_] in Belgium which had the smallest foot measurements, grouped both units together, while J. F. G. Palaiseau devoted three chapters to units of length: one for linear measures (palms and feet); one for cloth measures (ells); and one for distances traveled (miles and leagues).[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

### International ISO-standard and other intermodal shipping containers

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Foot_(unit)&action=edit&section=18 "Edit section: International ISO-standard and other intermodal shipping containers")]

[International Organization for Standardization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization "International Organization for Standardization") (ISO)-defined [intermodal containers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intermodal_containers "Intermodal containers") for efficient global freight/cargo shipping, were defined using feet rather than meters for their leading outside (corner) dimensions. All ISO-standard containers to this day are eight feet (2.4 m) wide, and their outer heights and lengths are also primarily defined in, or derived from feet. Quantities of global shipping containers are still primarily counted in [twenty-foot equivalent units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Twenty-foot_equivalent_unit "Twenty-foot equivalent unit") (TEUs).

Everyday global (civilian) air traffic and aviation continues to be controlled in [flight levels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flight_level "Flight level") (flying altitudes) separated by thousands of feet (although typically read out in hundreds; for example, "flight level 330" means 33,000 feet (10,000 m) in altitude.

### Relation to shoe size

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Foot_(unit)&action=edit&section=20 "Edit section: Relation to shoe size")]

The length of the (international) foot corresponds to a human foot with [shoe size](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shoe_size "Shoe size") of 13 (UK), 14 (US male), 15.5 (US female) or 48 (EU sizing).[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_foot#cite_note-64)[_[better source needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTRS "Wikipedia:NOTRS")_]

In measurement, the term "linear foot" (sometimes incorrectly referred to as "lineal foot") refers to the number of feet in a length of material (such as lumber or fabric) without regard to the width; it is used to distinguish from [surface area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_area "Surface area") in _[square foot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square\_foot "Square foot")_.[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_foot#cite_note-65)
