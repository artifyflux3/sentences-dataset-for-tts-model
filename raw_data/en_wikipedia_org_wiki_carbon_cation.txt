From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

(Redirected from [Carbon cation](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Carbon_cation&redirect=no "Carbon cation"))

Not to be confused with [Ethyl group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethyl_group "Ethyl group").

Chemical group (–CH₃) derived from methane

[![Image 5](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Methyl_Group_General_Formulae_V.1.png/120px-Methyl_Group_General_Formulae_V.1.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Methyl_Group_General_Formulae_V.1.png)

Different ways of representing a methyl group (highlighted in **blue**)

In [organic chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_chemistry "Organic chemistry"), a **methyl group** is an [alkyl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkyl "Alkyl") derived from [methane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane "Methane"), containing one [carbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon "Carbon") atom [bonded](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_bond "Chemical bond") to three [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen") atoms, having chemical formula CH 3 (whereas normal methane has the formula CH 4). In [formulas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_formula "Chemical formula"), the group is often [abbreviated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skeletal_formula#Pseudoelement_symbols "Skeletal formula") as **Me**. This [hydrocarbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrocarbon "Hydrocarbon") group occurs in many [organic compounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_compounds "Organic compounds"). It is a very stable group in most molecules. While the methyl group is usually part of a larger [molecule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecule "Molecule"), bonded to the rest of the molecule by a single covalent bond (−CH 3), it can be found on its own in any of three forms: methanide [anion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anion "Anion") (CH−3), methylium [cation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cation "Cation") (CH+3) or methyl [radical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radical_(chemistry) "Radical (chemistry)") (CH•

3). The anion has eight [valence electrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Valence_electron "Valence electron"), the radical seven and the cation six. All three forms are highly reactive and rarely observed.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_cation#cite_note-1)

Methyl cation, anion, and radical
---------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Methyl_group&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Methyl cation, anion, and radical")]

### Methyl cation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Methyl_group&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Methyl cation")]

Main article: [Methenium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methenium "Methenium")

The methylium cation (CH+3) exists in the [gas phase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas_phase "Gas phase"), but is otherwise not encountered. Some compounds are considered to be sources of the CH+3 cation, and this simplification is used pervasively in organic chemistry. For example, [protonation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protonation "Protonation") of methanol gives an electrophilic methylating reagent that reacts by the [S N 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SN2_reaction "SN2 reaction") pathway:

CH 3 OH + H+ → [CH 3 OH 2]+
Similarly, [methyl iodide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methyl_iodide "Methyl iodide") and methyl [triflate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triflate "Triflate") are viewed as the equivalent of the methyl cation because they readily undergo S N 2 reactions by weak [nucleophiles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleophile "Nucleophile").

The methyl cation has been detected in [interstellar space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_space "Interstellar space").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_cation#cite_note-MSH-20230627-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_cation#cite_note-NAT-20230626-3)

### Methyl anion

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Methyl_group&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Methyl anion")]

The methanide anion (CH−3) exists only in rarefied gas phase or under exotic conditions. It can be produced by electrical discharge in [ketene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ketene "Ketene") at low pressure (less than one [torr](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torr "Torr")) and its [enthalpy of reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enthalpy_of_reaction "Enthalpy of reaction") is determined to be about 252.2 ± 3.3 [kJ](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kilojoule "Kilojoule")/[mol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mol_(unit) "Mol (unit)").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_cation#cite_note-Ellison78-4) It is a powerful [superbase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superbase "Superbase"); only the [lithium monoxide anion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lithium_monoxide_anion "Lithium monoxide anion") (LiO−) and the [diethynylbenzene dianions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diethynylbenzene_dianion "Diethynylbenzene dianion") are known to be stronger.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_cation#cite_note-5)

In discussing mechanisms of organic reactions, [methyl lithium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methyl_lithium "Methyl lithium") and related [Grignard reagents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grignard_reagents "Grignard reagents") are often considered to be salts of CH−3; and though the model may be useful for description and analysis, it is only a useful fiction. Such reagents are generally prepared from the [methyl halides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methyl_halide "Methyl halide"):

2 M + CH 3 X → MCH 3 + MX
where M is an [alkali metal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkali_metal "Alkali metal").

### Methyl radical

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Methyl_group&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Methyl radical")]

Main article: [Methyl radical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methyl_radical "Methyl radical")

The methyl [radical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radical_(chemistry) "Radical (chemistry)") has the formula CH•

3. It exists in dilute gases, but in more concentrated form it readily [dimerizes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimer_(chemistry) "Dimer (chemistry)") to [ethane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethane "Ethane"). It is routinely produced by various enzymes of the [radical SAM](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radical_SAM "Radical SAM") and [methylcobalamin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methylcobalamin "Methylcobalamin") varieties.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_cation#cite_note-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_cation#cite_note-7)

Reactivity
----------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Methyl_group&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Reactivity")]

The reactivity of a methyl group depends on the adjacent [substituents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Substituent "Substituent"). Methyl groups can be quite unreactive. For example, in organic compounds, the methyl group resists attack by even the strongest [acids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acid "Acid").[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_cation#cite_note-8)

### Oxidation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Methyl_group&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Oxidation")]

The [oxidation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxidation "Oxidation") of a methyl group occurs widely in nature and industry. The oxidation products derived from methyl are [hydroxymethyl group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroxymethyl_group "Hydroxymethyl group")−CH 2 OH, [formyl group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formyl_group "Formyl group")−CHO, and [carboxyl group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carboxyl_group "Carboxyl group")−COOH. For example, [permanganate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permanganate "Permanganate") often converts a methyl group to a carboxyl (−COOH) group, e.g. the conversion of [toluene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toluene "Toluene") to [benzoic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzoic_acid "Benzoic acid"). Ultimately oxidation of methyl groups gives [protons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton "Proton") and [carbon dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_dioxide "Carbon dioxide"), as seen in combustion.

### Methylation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Methyl_group&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Methylation")]

Main article: [Methylation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methylation "Methylation")

Demethylation (the transfer of the methyl group to another compound) is a common process, and [reagents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reagent "Reagent") that undergo this reaction are called methylating agents. Common methylating agents are [dimethyl sulfate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimethyl_sulfate "Dimethyl sulfate"), [methyl iodide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methyl_iodide "Methyl iodide"), and [methyl triflate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methyl_triflate "Methyl triflate"). [Methanogenesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanogenesis "Methanogenesis"), the source of natural gas, arises via a demethylation reaction.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_cation#cite_note-9) Together with ubiquitin and phosphorylation, methylation is a major biochemical process for modifying protein function.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_cation#cite_note-10) The field of [epigenetics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epigenetic "Epigenetic") focuses on the influence of methylation on gene expression.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_cation#cite_note-11)

### Deprotonation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Methyl_group&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Deprotonation")]

Certain methyl groups can be deprotonated. For example, the acidity of the methyl groups in [acetone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone "Acetone") ((CH 3)2 CO) is about 10 20 times more acidic than methane. The resulting [carbanions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbanion "Carbanion") are key intermediates in many reactions in [organic synthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_synthesis "Organic synthesis") and [biosynthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosynthesis "Biosynthesis"). [Fatty acids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fatty_acid "Fatty acid") are produced in this way.

### Free radical reactions

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Methyl_group&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Free radical reactions")]

When placed in [benzylic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzylic "Benzylic") or [allylic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allylic "Allylic") positions, the strength of the C−H bond is decreased, and the reactivity of the methyl group increases. One manifestation of this enhanced reactivity is the [photochemical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photochemistry "Photochemistry")[chlorination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halogenation "Halogenation") of the methyl group in [toluene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toluene "Toluene") to give [benzyl chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzyl_chloride "Benzyl chloride").[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_cation#cite_note-Ullmann-12)

Chiral methyl
-------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Methyl_group&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Chiral methyl")]

In the special case where one hydrogen is replaced by [deuterium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deuterium "Deuterium") (D) and another hydrogen by [tritium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tritium "Tritium") (T), the methyl substituent becomes [chiral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chiral "Chiral").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_cation#cite_note-13) Methods exist to produce optically pure methyl compounds, e.g., chiral [acetic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid "Acetic acid") (deuterotritoacetic acid CHDTCO 2 H). Through the use of chiral methyl groups, the [stereochemical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stereochemistry "Stereochemistry") course of several [biochemical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biochemistry "Biochemistry") transformations have been analyzed.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_cation#cite_note-14)

Rotation
--------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Methyl_group&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Rotation")]

See also: [Rotamer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotamer "Rotamer")

A methyl group may rotate around the R−C axis. This is a free rotation only in the simplest cases like gaseous [methyl chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methyl_chloride "Methyl chloride")CH 3 Cl. In most molecules, the remainder R breaks the _C_∞ symmetry of the R−C axis and creates a potential _V_(_φ_) that restricts the free motion of the three protons. For the model case of [ethane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethane "Ethane")CH 3 CH 3, this is discussed under the name [ethane barrier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethane_barrier "Ethane barrier"). In condensed phases, neighbour molecules also contribute to the potential. Methyl group rotation can be experimentally studied using [quasielastic neutron scattering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasielastic_neutron_scattering "Quasielastic neutron scattering").[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_cation#cite_note-15)

Etymology
---------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Methyl_group&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Etymology")]

French chemists [Jean-Baptiste Dumas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_Dumas "Jean-Baptiste Dumas") and [Eugene Peligot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eugene_Peligot "Eugene Peligot"), after determining methanol's chemical structure, introduced "[methylene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methylene_(disambiguation) "Methylene (disambiguation)")" from the [Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek "Ancient Greek")[μέθυ](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%BC%CE%AD%CE%B8%CF%85#Ancient_Greek "wikt:μέθυ") (_methy_) "wine" and [ὕλη](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E1%BD%95%CE%BB%CE%B7#Ancient_Greek "wikt:ὕλη") (_hȳlē_) "wood, patch of trees" with the intention of highlighting its origins, "alcohol made from wood (substance)".[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_cation#cite_note-16)[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_cation#cite_note-17) The term "methyl" was derived in about 1840 by [back-formation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back-formation "Back-formation") from "methylene", and was then applied to describe "methyl alcohol" (which since 1892 is called "[methanol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol "Methanol")").

_Methyl_ is the [IUPAC nomenclature of organic chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IUPAC_nomenclature_of_organic_chemistry "IUPAC nomenclature of organic chemistry") term for an [alkane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkane "Alkane") (or alkyl) molecule, using the prefix "meth-" to indicate the presence of a single carbon.
