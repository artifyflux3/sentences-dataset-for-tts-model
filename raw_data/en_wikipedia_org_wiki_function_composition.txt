In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), the **composition operator**![Image 1: {\displaystyle \circ }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/99add39d2b681e2de7ff62422c32704a05c7ec31) takes two [functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)"), ![Image 2: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) and ![Image 3: {\displaystyle g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3556280e66fe2c0d0140df20935a6f057381d77), and returns a new function ![Image 4: {\displaystyle h(x):=(g\circ f)(x)=g(f(x))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af1d3bdd5113520bb528c47d1da5c6c5214e36c5). Thus, the function _g_ is [applied](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_application "Function application") after applying _f_ to _x_. ![Image 5: {\displaystyle (g\circ f)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7e846ff0dc70e3b328ac7c28bf8cbd0753663fd6) is pronounced "the composition of _g_ and _f_".[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_composition#cite_note-1)

**Reverse composition**, sometimes denoted ![Image 6: {\displaystyle f\mapsto g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/13cf61cbe2a1a9edbb59985228425ba58b59be8b) , applies the operation in the opposite order, applying ![Image 7: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) first and ![Image 8: {\displaystyle g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3556280e66fe2c0d0140df20935a6f057381d77) second. Intuitively, reverse composition is a chaining process in which the output of function _f_ feeds the input of function _g_.

The composition of functions is a special case of the [composition of relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Composition_of_relations "Composition of relations"), sometimes also denoted by ![Image 9: {\displaystyle \circ }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/99add39d2b681e2de7ff62422c32704a05c7ec31). As a result, all properties of composition of relations are true of composition of functions,[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_composition#cite_note-Velleman_2006-2) such as [associativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_composition#Properties).

[![Image 10](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Example_for_a_composition_of_two_functions.svg/250px-Example_for_a_composition_of_two_functions.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Example_for_a_composition_of_two_functions.svg)

Concrete example for the composition of two functions.

*   Composition of functions on a finite [set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_(mathematics) "Set (mathematics)"): If _f_ = {(1, 1), (2, 3), (3, 1), (4, 2)}, and _g_ = {(1, 2), (2, 3), (3, 1), (4, 2)}, then _g_ ∘ _f_ = {(1, 2), (2, 1), (3, 2), (4, 3)}, as shown in the figure.
*   Composition of functions on an [infinite set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinite_set "Infinite set"): If _f_: **R** → **R** (where **R** is the set of all [real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number")) is given by _f_(_x_) = 2 _x_ + 4 and _g_: **R** → **R** is given by _g_(_x_) = _x_ 3, then: 
(_f_ ∘ _g_)(_x_) = _f_(_g_(_x_)) = _f_(_x_ 3) = 2 _x_ 3 + 4, and

(_g_ ∘ _f_)(_x_) = _g_(_f_(_x_)) = _g_(2 _x_ + 4) = (2 _x_ + 4)3.

*   If an airplane's altitude at time t is _a_(_t_), and the air pressure at altitude x is _p_(_x_), then (_p_ ∘ _a_)(_t_) is the pressure around the plane at time t.
*   Function defined on finite sets which change the order of their elements such as [permutations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permutation "Permutation") can be composed on the same set, this being composition of permutations.

The composition of functions is always [associative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Associative "Associative")—a property inherited from the [composition of relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Composition_of_relations "Composition of relations").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_composition#cite_note-Velleman_2006-2) That is, if f, g, and h are composable, then _f_ ∘ (_g_ ∘ _h_) = (_f_ ∘ _g_) ∘ _h_.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_composition#cite_note-:0-3) Since the parentheses do not change the result, they are generally omitted.

In a strict sense, the composition _g_ ∘ _f_ is only meaningful if the codomain of f equals the domain of g; in a wider sense, it is sufficient that the former be an improper [subset](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subset "Subset") of the latter.[[nb 1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_composition#cite_note-NB_Strict-4) Moreover, it is often convenient to tacitly restrict the domain of f, such that f produces only values in the domain of g. For example, the composition _g_ ∘ _f_ of the functions _f_ : [**R**](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number") → [(−∞,+9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interval_(mathematics)#Infinite_endpoints "Interval (mathematics)") defined by _f_(_x_) = 9 − _x_ 2 and _g_ : [[0,+∞)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interval_(mathematics)#Infinite_endpoints "Interval (mathematics)") → **R** defined by ![Image 11: {\displaystyle g(x)={\sqrt {x}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/200d7544a5ab10c9b757bc078ebed1c1f1102cee) can be defined on the [interval](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interval_(mathematics) "Interval (mathematics)")[−3,+3].

[![Image 12](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Absolute_value_composition.svg/250px-Absolute_value_composition.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Absolute_value_composition.svg)

Compositions of two [real](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number") functions, the [absolute value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_value "Absolute value") and a [cubic function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cubic_function "Cubic function"), in different orders, show a non-commutativity of composition.

The functions g and f are said to [commute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative "Commutative") with each other if _g_ ∘ _f_ = _f_ ∘ _g_. Commutativity is a special property, attained only by particular functions, and often in special circumstances. For example, |_x_| + 3 = |_x_ + 3| only when _x_ ≥ 0. The picture shows another example.

The composition of [one-to-one](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One-to-one_function "One-to-one function") (injective) functions is always one-to-one. Similarly, the composition of [onto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Onto_function "Onto function") (surjective) functions is always onto. It follows that the composition of two [bijections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bijection "Bijection") is also a bijection. The [inverse function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverse_function "Inverse function") of a composition (assumed invertible) has the property that (_f_ ∘ _g_)−1 = _g_−1∘ _f_−1.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_composition#cite_note-Rodgers_2000-5)

[Derivatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative "Derivative") of compositions involving differentiable functions can be found using the [chain rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chain_rule "Chain rule"). [Higher derivatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Higher_derivative "Higher derivative") of such functions are given by [Faà di Bruno's formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fa%C3%A0_di_Bruno%27s_formula "Faà di Bruno's formula").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_composition#cite_note-:0-3)

Composition of functions is sometimes described as a kind of [multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication "Multiplication") on a function space, but has very different properties from [pointwise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pointwise "Pointwise") multiplication of functions (e.g. composition is not [commutative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_property "Commutative property")).[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_composition#cite_note-6)

Composition monoids
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Function_composition&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Composition monoids")]

Suppose one has two (or more) functions _f_: _X_ → _X_,_g_: _X_ → _X_ having the same domain and codomain; these are often called _[transformations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transformation\_(function) "Transformation (function)")_. Then one can form chains of transformations composed together, such as _f_ ∘ _f_ ∘ _g_ ∘ _f_. Such chains have the [algebraic structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_structure "Algebraic structure") of a [monoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monoid "Monoid"), called a _[transformation monoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transformation\_monoid "Transformation monoid")_ or (much more seldom) a _composition monoid_. In general, transformation monoids can have remarkably complicated structure. One particular notable example is the [de Rham curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_Rham_curve "De Rham curve"). The set of _all_ functions _f_: _X_ → _X_ is called the [full transformation semigroup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Full_transformation_semigroup "Full transformation semigroup")[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_composition#cite_note-Hollings_2014-7) or _symmetric semigroup_[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_composition#cite_note-Grillet_1995-8) on X. (One can actually define two semigroups depending how one defines the semigroup operation as the left or right composition of functions.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_composition#cite_note-D%C3%B6m%C3%B6si-Nehaniv_2005-9))

[![Image 13](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/SVG_skew_and_rotation.svg/250px-SVG_skew_and_rotation.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:SVG_skew_and_rotation.svg)

Composition of a [shear mapping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shear_mapping "Shear mapping")(red) and a clockwise rotation by 45° (green). On the left is the original object. Above is shear, then rotate. Below is rotate, then shear.

If the given transformations are [bijective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bijective "Bijective") (and thus invertible), then the set of all possible combinations of these functions forms a [transformation group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transformation_group "Transformation group") (also known as a [permutation group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permutation_group "Permutation group")); and one says that the group is [generated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_generator "Group generator") by these functions.

The set of all bijective functions _f_: _X_ → _X_ (called [permutations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permutation "Permutation")) forms a group with respect to function composition. This is the [symmetric group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetric_group "Symmetric group"), also sometimes called the _composition group_. A fundamental result in group theory, [Cayley's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cayley%27s_theorem "Cayley's theorem"), essentially says that any group is in fact just a subgroup of a symmetric group ([up to](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Up_to "Up to") isomorphism).[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_composition#cite_note-Carter_2009-10)

In the symmetric semigroup (of all transformations) one also finds a weaker, non-unique notion of inverse (called a pseudoinverse) because the symmetric semigroup is a [regular semigroup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_semigroup "Regular semigroup").[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_composition#cite_note-Ganyushkin-Mazorchuk_2008-11)

If _Y_[⊆](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subset "Subset")_X_, then ![Image 14: {\displaystyle f:X\to Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/abd1e080abef4bbdab67b43819c6431e7561361c) may compose with itself; this is sometimes denoted as ![Image 15: {\displaystyle f^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81efa211d9d04493b68b24bf9843d1967ae22cdc). That is:

![Image 16: {\displaystyle (f\circ f)(x)=f(f(x))=f^{2}(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/617df79b1b1c1d47a5d9469cdac2a9e0f4abd6d3)

![Image 17: {\displaystyle (f\circ f\circ f)(x)=f(f(f(x)))=f^{3}(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf7c694f0ca1e9f15ec09e782c7f77b85550435e)

![Image 18: {\displaystyle (f\circ f\circ f\circ f)(x)=f(f(f(f(x))))=f^{4}(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ef78c881fd358d4d0c83ab93a5b990b986b752c8)

More generally, for any [natural number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number "Natural number")_n_ ≥ 2, the n th **functional [power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponentiation "Exponentiation")** can be defined inductively by _f_ _n_ = _f_ ∘ _f_ _n_−1 = _f_ _n_−1 ∘ _f_, a notation introduced by [Hans Heinrich Bürmann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hans_Heinrich_B%C3%BCrmann "Hans Heinrich Bürmann")[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_][[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_composition#cite_note-Herschel_1820-12)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_composition#cite_note-Cajori_1929-13) and [John Frederick William Herschel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Frederick_William_Herschel "John Frederick William Herschel").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_composition#cite_note-Herschel_1813-14)[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_composition#cite_note-Herschel_1820-12)[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_composition#cite_note-Peano_1903-15)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_composition#cite_note-Cajori_1929-13) Repeated composition of such a function with itself is called **[function iteration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iterated_function "Iterated function")**.

*   By convention, _f_ 0 is defined as the identity map on _f_'s domain, id _X_.
*   If _Y_ = _X_ and _f_: _X_ → _X_ admits an [inverse function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverse_function "Inverse function")_f_−1, negative functional powers _f_−_n_ are defined for _n_> 0 as the [negated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Additive_inverse "Additive inverse") power of the inverse function: _f_−_n_ = (_f_−1)_n_.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_composition#cite_note-Herschel_1813-14)[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_composition#cite_note-Herschel_1820-12)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_composition#cite_note-Cajori_1929-13)

**Note:** If f takes its values in a [ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ring_(mathematics) "Ring (mathematics)") (in particular for real or complex-valued _f_), there is a risk of confusion, as _f_ _n_ could also stand for the n-fold product of f, e.g. _f_ 2(_x_) = _f_(_x_) · _f_(_x_).[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_composition#cite_note-Cajori_1929-13) For trigonometric functions, usually the latter is meant, at least for positive exponents.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_composition#cite_note-Cajori_1929-13) For example, in [trigonometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trigonometry "Trigonometry"), this superscript notation represents standard [exponentiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponentiation "Exponentiation") when used with [trigonometric functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trigonometric_functions "Trigonometric functions"):

sin 2(_x_) = sin(_x_) · sin(_x_).

However, for negative exponents (especially −1), it nevertheless usually refers to the inverse function, e.g., tan−1 = arctan ≠ 1/tan.

In some cases, when, for a given function f, the equation _g_ ∘ _g_ = _f_ has a unique solution g, that function can be defined as the [functional square root](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functional_square_root "Functional square root") of f, then written as _g_ = _f_ 1/2.

More generally, when _g_ _n_ = _f_ has a unique solution for some natural number _n_> 0, then _f_ _m_/_n_ can be defined as _g_ _m_.

Under additional restrictions, this idea can be generalized so that the [iteration count](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iterated_function "Iterated function") becomes a continuous parameter; in this case, such a system is called a [flow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flow_(mathematics) "Flow (mathematics)"), specified through solutions of [Schröder's equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schr%C3%B6der%27s_equation "Schröder's equation"). Iterated functions and flows occur naturally in the study of [fractals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractals "Fractals") and [dynamical systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynamical_systems "Dynamical systems").

To avoid ambiguity, some mathematicians[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] choose to use ∘ to denote the compositional meaning, writing _f_∘_n_(_x_) for the n-th iterate of the function _f_(_x_), as in, for example, _f_∘3(_x_) meaning _f_(_f_(_f_(_x_))). For the same purpose, _f_[_n_](_x_) was used by [Benjamin Peirce](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benjamin_Peirce "Benjamin Peirce")[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_composition#cite_note-Peirce_1852-16)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_composition#cite_note-Cajori_1929-13) whereas [Alfred Pringsheim](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alfred_Pringsheim "Alfred Pringsheim") and [Jules Molk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jules_Molk "Jules Molk") suggested _n_ _f_(_x_) instead.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_composition#cite_note-Pringsheim-Molk_1907-17)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_composition#cite_note-Cajori_1929-13)[[nb 2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_composition#cite_note-NB_Rucker-18)

Alternative notations
---------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Function_composition&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Alternative notations")]

Many mathematicians, particularly in [group theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_theory "Group theory"), omit the composition symbol, writing _gf_ for _g_ ∘ _f_.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_composition#cite_note-Ivanov_2009-19)

During the mid-20th century, some mathematicians adopted [postfix notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postfix_notation "Postfix notation"), writing _xf_ for _f_(_x_) and (_xf_)_g_ for _g_(_f_(_x_)).[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_composition#cite_note-Gallier_2011-20) This can be more natural than [prefix notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prefix_notation "Prefix notation") in many cases, such as in [linear algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_algebra "Linear algebra") when x is a [row vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Row_vector "Row vector") and f and g denote [matrices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_(mathematics) "Matrix (mathematics)") and the composition is by [matrix multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_multiplication "Matrix multiplication"). The order is important because function composition is not necessarily commutative. Having successive transformations applying and composing to the right agrees with the left-to-right reading sequence.

Mathematicians who use postfix notation may write "_fg_", meaning first apply f and then apply g, in keeping with the order the symbols occur in postfix notation, thus making the notation "_fg_" ambiguous. Computer scientists may write "_f_; _g_" for this,[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_composition#cite_note-Barr-Wells_1990-21) thereby disambiguating the order of composition. To distinguish the left composition operator from a text semicolon, in the [Z notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Z_notation "Z notation") the ⨾ character is used for left [relation composition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relation_composition "Relation composition").[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_composition#cite_note-ISOIEC13568-22) Since all functions are [binary relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_relation#Special_types_of_binary_relations "Binary relation"), it is correct to use the [fat] semicolon for function composition as well (see the article on [composition of relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Composition_of_relations "Composition of relations") for further details on this notation).

Function composition appears in one form or another in numerous [programming languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Programming_language "Programming language").

Multivariate functions
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Function_composition&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Multivariate functions")]

Partial composition is possible for [multivariate functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multivariate_function "Multivariate function"). The function resulting when some argument _x_ _i_ of the function f is replaced by the function g is called a composition of f and g in some computer engineering contexts, and is denoted _f_ |_x_ _i_ = _g_![Image 20: {\displaystyle f|_{x_{i}=g}=f(x_{1},\ldots ,x_{i-1},g(x_{1},x_{2},\ldots ,x_{n}),x_{i+1},\ldots ,x_{n}).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bc270194f367f13d63fb539a6819a4df18103d37)

When g is a simple constant b, composition degenerates into a (partial) valuation, whose result is also known as [restriction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Restriction_(mathematics) "Restriction (mathematics)") or _co-factor_.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_composition#cite_note-Bryant_1986-23)

![Image 21: {\displaystyle f|_{x_{i}=b}=f(x_{1},\ldots ,x_{i-1},b,x_{i+1},\ldots ,x_{n}).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/70fb7cece4d40a63588df2477eaf0646e1f71a3d)

In general, the composition of multivariate functions may involve several other functions as arguments, as in the definition of [primitive recursive function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primitive_recursive_function "Primitive recursive function"). Given f, a n-ary function, and n m-ary functions _g_ 1, ..., _g_ _n_, the composition of f with _g_ 1, ..., _g_ _n_, is the m-ary function ![Image 22: {\displaystyle h(x_{1},\ldots ,x_{m})=f(g_{1}(x_{1},\ldots ,x_{m}),\ldots ,g_{n}(x_{1},\ldots ,x_{m})).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9337e4ba92aaa4b92f7844dc1749b59f3cb84574)

This is sometimes called the **generalized composite** or **superposition** of _f_ with _g_ 1, ..., _g_ _n_.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_composition#cite_note-Bergman_2011-24) The partial composition in only one argument mentioned previously can be instantiated from this more general scheme by setting all argument functions except one to be suitably chosen [projection functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projection_function "Projection function"). Here _g_ 1, ..., _g_ _n_ can be seen as a single vector/[tuple](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tuple "Tuple")-valued function in this generalized scheme, in which case this is precisely the standard definition of function composition.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_composition#cite_note-Tourlakis_2012-25)

A set of finitary [operations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operation_(mathematics) "Operation (mathematics)") on some base set _X_ is called a [clone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clone_(algebra) "Clone (algebra)") if it contains all projections and is closed under generalized composition. A clone generally contains operations of various [arities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arity "Arity").[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_composition#cite_note-Bergman_2011-24) The notion of commutation also finds an interesting generalization in the multivariate case; a function _f_ of arity _n_ is said to commute with a function _g_ of arity _m_ if _f_ is a [homomorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homomorphism "Homomorphism") preserving _g_, and vice versa, that is:[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_composition#cite_note-Bergman_2011-24)![Image 23: {\displaystyle f(g(a_{11},\ldots ,a_{1m}),\ldots ,g(a_{n1},\ldots ,a_{nm}))=g(f(a_{11},\ldots ,a_{n1}),\ldots ,f(a_{1m},\ldots ,a_{nm})).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/974e564b09c2795a9d68d0ef77eed321975e2d44)

A unary operation always commutes with itself, but this is not necessarily the case for a binary (or higher arity) operation. A binary (or higher arity) operation that commutes with itself is called [medial or entropic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medial_magma "Medial magma").[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_composition#cite_note-Bergman_2011-24)

[Composition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Composition_of_relations "Composition of relations") can be generalized to arbitrary [binary relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_relation "Binary relation"). If _R_ ⊆ _X_[×](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_product "Cartesian product")_Y_ and _S_ ⊆ _Y_ × _Z_ are two binary relations, then their composition amounts to

![Image 24: {\displaystyle R\circ S=\{(x,z)\in X\times Z:(\exists y\in Y)((x,y)\in R\,\land \,(y,z)\in S)\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cec535d75479cdfc64d32a082bea7e6acfd8390c).

Considering a function as a special case of a binary relation (namely [functional relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_relation#Specific_types_of_binary_relations "Binary relation")), function composition satisfies the definition for relation composition. A small circle _R_∘_S_ has been used for the [infix notation of composition of relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Composition_of_relations#Notational_variations "Composition of relations"), as well as functions. When used to represent composition of functions ![Image 25: {\displaystyle (g\circ f)(x)\ =\ g(f(x))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2858fb7743fce11c1d3d3704308972d55072afa6) however, the text sequence is reversed to illustrate the different operation sequences accordingly.

The composition is defined in the same way for [partial functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_function "Partial function") and Cayley's theorem has its analogue called the [Wagner–Preston theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wagner%E2%80%93Preston_theorem "Wagner–Preston theorem").[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_composition#cite_note-Lipcomb_1997-26)

The [category of sets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category_of_sets "Category of sets") with functions as [morphisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morphism "Morphism") is the prototypical [category](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category_(mathematics) "Category (mathematics)"). The axioms of a category are in fact inspired from the properties (and also the definition) of function composition.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_composition#cite_note-Hilton-Wu_1989-27) The structures given by composition are axiomatized and generalized in [category theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category_theory "Category theory") with the concept of [morphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morphism "Morphism") as the category-theoretical replacement of functions. The reversed order of composition in the formula (_f_ ∘ _g_)−1 = (_g_−1 ∘ _f_−1) applies for [composition of relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Composition_of_relations "Composition of relations") using [converse relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Converse_relation "Converse relation"), and thus in [group theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_theory "Group theory"). These structures form [dagger categories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dagger_category "Dagger category").

> _The standard "foundation" for mathematics starts with [sets and their elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set\_theory "Set theory"). It is possible to start differently, by axiomatising not elements of sets but functions between sets. This can be done by using the language of categories and universal constructions._
> 
> 
> _. . . the membership relation for sets can often be replaced by the composition operation for functions. This leads to an alternative foundation for Mathematics upon categories -- specifically, on the category of all functions. Now much of Mathematics is dynamic, in that it deals with morphisms of an object into another object of the same kind. Such morphisms_ (_like functions_) _form categories, and so the approach via categories fits well with the objective of organizing and understanding Mathematics. That, in truth, should be the goal of a proper philosophy of Mathematics._
> 
> 
> - [Saunders Mac Lane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saunders_Mac_Lane "Saunders Mac Lane"), [Mathematics: Form and Function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics,_Form_and_Function "Mathematics, Form and Function")[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_composition#cite_note-28)

In [TeX](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TeX "TeX"), it is written `\circ`.
