**Jyeṣṭhadeva** (c. 1500– c. 1575)[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jye%E1%B9%A3%E1%B9%ADhadeva#cite_note-Sarma-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jye%E1%B9%A3%E1%B9%ADhadeva#cite_note-2) was an [astronomer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomer "Astronomer")-mathematician of the [Kerala school of astronomy and mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kerala_school_of_astronomy_and_mathematics "Kerala school of astronomy and mathematics") founded by [Madhava of Sangamagrama](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Madhava_of_Sangamagrama "Madhava of Sangamagrama") (c. 1350– c. 1425). He is best known as the author of _[Yuktibhāṣā](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yuktibh%C4%81%E1%B9%A3%C4%81 "Yuktibhāṣā")_, a commentary in [Malayalam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malayalam "Malayalam") of [Tantrasamgraha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tantrasamgraha "Tantrasamgraha") by [Nilakantha Somayaji](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nilakantha_Somayaji "Nilakantha Somayaji") (1444–1544). In Yuktibhāṣā, Jyeṣṭhadeva had given complete [proofs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_proof "Mathematical proof") and rationale of the statements in [Tantrasamgraha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tantrasamgraha "Tantrasamgraha"). This was unusual for traditional [Indian mathematicians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_mathematicians "Indian mathematicians") of the time. The Yuktibhāṣā is now believed to contain some elements of [calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus "Calculus") like [Taylor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taylor_series "Taylor series") and [infinity series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinite_series "Infinite series") of some trigonometric functions.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jye%E1%B9%A3%E1%B9%ADhadeva#cite_note-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jye%E1%B9%A3%E1%B9%ADhadeva#cite_note-4)However they did not combine many differing ideas under the two unifying themes of the [derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative "Derivative") and the [integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral "Integral"), show the connection between the two, and turn calculus into the powerful problem-solving tool we have today.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jye%E1%B9%A3%E1%B9%ADhadeva#cite_note-katz-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jye%E1%B9%A3%E1%B9%ADhadeva#cite_note-6)Jyeṣṭhadeva also authored _Drk-karana,_ a treatise on astronomical observations.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jye%E1%B9%A3%E1%B9%ADhadeva#cite_note-7)

According to [K. V. Sarma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K._V._Sarma "K. V. Sarma"), the name "Jyeṣṭhadeva" is most probably the [Sanskritised](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sanskritisation "Sanskritisation") form of his personal name in the local language Malayalam. [[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jye%E1%B9%A3%E1%B9%ADhadeva#cite_note-8)

Life period of Jyeṣṭhadeva
--------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Jye%E1%B9%A3%E1%B9%ADhadeva&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Life period of Jyeṣṭhadeva")]

There are a few references to Jyeṣṭhadeva scattered across several old manuscripts.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jye%E1%B9%A3%E1%B9%ADhadeva#cite_note-Sarma-1) From these manuscripts, one can deduce a few bare facts about the life of Jyeṣṭhadeva. He was a [Nambudiri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nambudiri "Nambudiri") belonging to the Parangngottu family (Sanskrtised as _Parakroda_) born about the year 1500 CE. He was a pupil of [Damodara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Damodara "Damodara") and a younger contemporary of [Nilakantha Somayaji](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nilakantha_Somayaji "Nilakantha Somayaji"). [Achyuta Pisharati](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Achyuta_Pisharati "Achyuta Pisharati") was a pupil of Jyeṣṭhadeva. In the concluding verse of his work titled _Uparagakriyakrama_, completed in 1592, [Achyuta Pisharati](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Achyuta_Pisharati "Achyuta Pisharati") has referred to Jyeṣṭhadeva as his _aged benign teacher_. From a few references in _Drkkarana_, a work believed to be of Jyeṣṭhadeva, one may conclude that Jyeṣṭhadeva lived up to about 1610 CE.

Parangngottu, the family house of Jyeṣṭhadeva, still exists in the vicinity of [Trikkandiyur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thrikkandiyoor "Thrikkandiyoor") and [Alathiyur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alattiyur,_Malappuram "Alattiyur, Malappuram").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jye%E1%B9%A3%E1%B9%ADhadeva#cite_note-Sarma-1) There are also several [legends](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kottarathil_Sankunni "Kottarathil Sankunni") connected with members of Parangngottu family.

Mathematical lineage
--------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Jye%E1%B9%A3%E1%B9%ADhadeva&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Mathematical lineage")]

Little is known about the mathematical traditions in [Kerala](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kerala "Kerala") prior to [Madhava of Sangamagrama](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Madhava_of_Sangamagrama "Madhava of Sangamagrama"). [Vatasseri Paramesvara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paramesvara "Paramesvara") was a direct disciple of Madhava. [Damodara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Damodara "Damodara") was a son of [Paramesvara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paramesvara "Paramesvara"). [Nilakantha Somayaji](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nilakantha_Somayaji "Nilakantha Somayaji") and Jyeshthadeva were pupils of Damodara. Jyeṣṭhadeva's pupil was [Achyuta Pisharati](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Achyuta_Pisharati "Achyuta Pisharati") and [Melpathur Narayana Bhattathiri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Melpathur_Narayana_Bhattathiri "Melpathur Narayana Bhattathiri") was [Achyuta Pisharati](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Achyuta_Pisharati "Achyuta Pisharati")'s student.

Jyeshthadeva's works
--------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Jye%E1%B9%A3%E1%B9%ADhadeva&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Jyeshthadeva's works")]

Main articles: [Yuktibhāṣā](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yuktibh%C4%81%E1%B9%A3%C4%81 "Yuktibhāṣā") and [Ganita-yukti-bhasa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ganita-yukti-bhasa "Ganita-yukti-bhasa")

[![Image 5](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Pages_from_Yuktibhasa.jpg/500px-Pages_from_Yuktibhasa.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Pages_from_Yuktibhasa.jpg)

Pages from the [Yuktibhasa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yuktibh%C4%81%E1%B9%A3%C4%81 "Yuktibhāṣā") c.1530

Jyeṣṭhadeva is known to have composed only two works, namely, _[Yuktibhāṣā](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yuktibh%C4%81%E1%B9%A3%C4%81 "Yuktibhāṣā")_ and _Drkkarana_. The former is commentary with rationales of [Tantrasamgraha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tantrasamgraha "Tantrasamgraha") of [Nilakantha Somayaji](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nilakantha_Somayaji "Nilakantha Somayaji") and the latter is a treatise on astronomical computations.

Three factors make [Yuktibhāṣā](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yuktibh%C4%81%E1%B9%A3%C4%81 "Yuktibhāṣā") unique in the history of the development of mathematical thinking in the Indian subcontinent:

*   It is composed in the spoken language of the local people, namely, the [Malayalam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malayalam "Malayalam") language. This is in contrast to the centuries-old Indian tradition of composing scholarly works in the [Sanskrit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sanskrit "Sanskrit") language which was the language of the learned.
*   The work is in prose, again in contrast to the prevailing style of writing even technical manuals in verse. All the other notable works of the [Kerala school](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kerala_school_of_astronomy_and_mathematics "Kerala school of astronomy and mathematics") are in verse.
*   Most importantly, [Yuktibhāṣā](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yuktibh%C4%81%E1%B9%A3%C4%81 "Yuktibhāṣā") was composed intentionally as a manual of proofs. The very purpose of writing the book was to record in full detail the rationales of the various results discovered by mathematicians-astronomers of the Kerala school, especially of [Nilakantha Somayaji](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nilakantha_Somayaji "Nilakantha Somayaji"). This book is proof enough to establish that the concept of proof was not unknown to Indian mathematical traditions.
