[Coordinate surfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_surfaces "Coordinate surfaces") of elliptic cylindrical coordinates. The yellow sheet is the prism of a half-hyperbola corresponding to ν=-45°, whereas the red tube is an elliptical prism corresponding to μ=1. The blue sheet corresponds to _z_=1. The three surfaces intersect at the point **P** (shown as a black sphere) with [Cartesian coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system "Cartesian coordinate system") roughly (2.182, −1.661, 1.0). The foci of the ellipse and hyperbola lie at _x_ = ±2.0.

**Elliptic cylindrical coordinates** are a three-dimensional [orthogonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_coordinates "Orthogonal coordinates")[coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_system "Coordinate system") that results from projecting the two-dimensional [elliptic coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptic_coordinates "Elliptic coordinates") in the perpendicular ![Image 2: {\displaystyle z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf368e72c009decd9b6686ee84a375632e11de98)-direction. Hence, the [coordinate surfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_surfaces "Coordinate surfaces") are [prisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prism_(geometry) "Prism (geometry)") of confocal [ellipses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse "Ellipse") and [hyperbolae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbola "Hyperbola"). The two [foci](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Focus_(geometry) "Focus (geometry)")![Image 3: {\displaystyle F_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/100c7fbf174fe8b06eacc2a6b0bb2e1badd1c7ce) and ![Image 4: {\displaystyle F_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0fd17e0779153d765b40ebef91533489b87b2e37) are generally taken to be fixed at ![Image 5: {\displaystyle -a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e0982b5868a66be1ed3ad7ef4bcd3d3db20f982) and ![Image 6: {\displaystyle +a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/23558d20acf58179a3e9a08ea7c80ba017095873), respectively, on the ![Image 7: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4)-axis of the [Cartesian coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system "Cartesian coordinate system").

The most common definition of elliptic cylindrical coordinates ![Image 8: {\displaystyle (\mu ,\nu ,z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4419a311f23b8ba793ccdb762f7a769176552cc9) is

![Image 9: {\displaystyle x=a\ \cosh \mu \ \cos \nu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/621096e98c92af8d13796b0649a19843f309ff48)![Image 10: {\displaystyle y=a\ \sinh \mu \ \sin \nu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1e890e7094475d3045ff0fb252f36e773e6ca236)![Image 11: {\displaystyle z=z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68fd4b155af06002932cef8fc09cbc67743cde8f)
where ![Image 12: {\displaystyle \mu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9fd47b2a39f7a7856952afec1f1db72c67af6161) is a nonnegative real number and ![Image 13: {\displaystyle \nu \in [0,2\pi ]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/897ecdc1452e088bb1e0ead69b41c66168df73a1).

These definitions correspond to ellipses and hyperbolae. The trigonometric identity

![Image 14: {\displaystyle {\frac {x^{2}}{a^{2}\cosh ^{2}\mu }}+{\frac {y^{2}}{a^{2}\sinh ^{2}\mu }}=\cos ^{2}\nu +\sin ^{2}\nu =1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9bcf4627ac63b279cebcefa7be483163f6b3eee6)
shows that curves of constant ![Image 15: {\displaystyle \mu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9fd47b2a39f7a7856952afec1f1db72c67af6161) form [ellipses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse "Ellipse"), whereas the hyperbolic trigonometric identity

![Image 16: {\displaystyle {\frac {x^{2}}{a^{2}\cos ^{2}\nu }}-{\frac {y^{2}}{a^{2}\sin ^{2}\nu }}=\cosh ^{2}\mu -\sinh ^{2}\mu =1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/52234cbc08b6a007fef69057d7979c3deec90ef9)
shows that curves of constant ![Image 17: {\displaystyle \nu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c15bbbb971240cf328aba572178f091684585468) form [hyperbolae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbola "Hyperbola").

The scale factors for the elliptic cylindrical coordinates ![Image 18: {\displaystyle \mu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9fd47b2a39f7a7856952afec1f1db72c67af6161) and ![Image 19: {\displaystyle \nu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c15bbbb971240cf328aba572178f091684585468) are equal

![Image 20: {\displaystyle h_{\mu }=h_{\nu }=a{\sqrt {\sinh ^{2}\mu +\sin ^{2}\nu }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/83ef5b578241ab1267b6442b0617b1c50a5a96b9)
whereas the remaining scale factor ![Image 21: {\displaystyle h_{z}=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/21caf494fc4bbb0ac6f8d7fe11be1982f03533e5). Consequently, an infinitesimal volume element equals

![Image 22: {\displaystyle dV=a^{2}\left(\sinh ^{2}\mu +\sin ^{2}\nu \right)d\mu d\nu dz}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6796b4a95bef2b669a75ea68360f0d1c018bf8ae)
and the Laplacian equals

![Image 23: {\displaystyle \nabla ^{2}\Phi ={\frac {1}{a^{2}\left(\sinh ^{2}\mu +\sin ^{2}\nu \right)}}\left({\frac {\partial ^{2}\Phi }{\partial \mu ^{2}}}+{\frac {\partial ^{2}\Phi }{\partial \nu ^{2}}}\right)+{\frac {\partial ^{2}\Phi }{\partial z^{2}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f31028b7883f24687c4a53312127867095b16aa3)
Other differential operators such as ![Image 24: {\displaystyle \nabla \cdot \mathbf {F} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1f4f5b9e6560d7fdaf1a69d9fdeff6beb1f327cc) and ![Image 25: {\displaystyle \nabla \times \mathbf {F} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c6e2429b7e3cb6965d1a6651703ff5ef0c88b378) can be expressed in the coordinates ![Image 26: {\displaystyle (\mu ,\nu ,z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4419a311f23b8ba793ccdb762f7a769176552cc9) by substituting the scale factors into the general formulae found in [orthogonal coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_coordinates "Orthogonal coordinates").

Alternative definition
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Elliptic_cylindrical_coordinates&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Alternative definition")]

An alternative and geometrically intuitive set of elliptic coordinates ![Image 27: {\displaystyle (\sigma ,\tau ,z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b3bd510376be13a47c5f0d984e365cb324833daa) are sometimes used, where ![Image 28: {\displaystyle \sigma =\cosh \mu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0503faf9f70eddcdd66fafab8606d712a0f6d19d) and ![Image 29: {\displaystyle \tau =\cos \nu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/29699d5b3ac1b50d52834f425186ebc8923f7a8d). Hence, the curves of constant ![Image 30: {\displaystyle \sigma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/59f59b7c3e6fdb1d0365a494b81fb9a696138c36) are ellipses, whereas the curves of constant ![Image 31: {\displaystyle \tau }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38a7dcde9730ef0853809fefc18d88771f95206c) are hyperbolae. The coordinate ![Image 32: {\displaystyle \tau }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38a7dcde9730ef0853809fefc18d88771f95206c) must belong to the interval [−1, 1], whereas the ![Image 33: {\displaystyle \sigma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/59f59b7c3e6fdb1d0365a494b81fb9a696138c36) coordinate must be greater than or equal to one.

The coordinates ![Image 34: {\displaystyle (\sigma ,\tau ,z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b3bd510376be13a47c5f0d984e365cb324833daa) have a simple relation to the distances to the foci ![Image 35: {\displaystyle F_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/100c7fbf174fe8b06eacc2a6b0bb2e1badd1c7ce) and ![Image 36: {\displaystyle F_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0fd17e0779153d765b40ebef91533489b87b2e37). For any point in the (x,y) plane, the _sum_![Image 37: {\displaystyle d_{1}+d_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/028d9a26c6c05e0531d8476389cb73cc8d5e8520) of its distances to the foci equals ![Image 38: {\displaystyle 2a\sigma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3c33546d2cc2f56c633756909048382e5283eb2c), whereas their _difference_![Image 39: {\displaystyle d_{1}-d_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/de2341ef255cb1d411333857ddd1f0dbf76c43f4) equals ![Image 40: {\displaystyle 2a\tau }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/99604fd548ab96ea6fe35259c07a0631fcf3cad5). Thus, the distance to ![Image 41: {\displaystyle F_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/100c7fbf174fe8b06eacc2a6b0bb2e1badd1c7ce) is ![Image 42: {\displaystyle a(\sigma +\tau )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9723f0deeb63d3cddabdc059f2fee30c96c85818), whereas the distance to ![Image 43: {\displaystyle F_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0fd17e0779153d765b40ebef91533489b87b2e37) is ![Image 44: {\displaystyle a(\sigma -\tau )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7b6a3c2b6f276dca7a3991f109af701f5858c3d1). (Recall that ![Image 45: {\displaystyle F_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/100c7fbf174fe8b06eacc2a6b0bb2e1badd1c7ce) and ![Image 46: {\displaystyle F_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0fd17e0779153d765b40ebef91533489b87b2e37) are located at ![Image 47: {\displaystyle x=-a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9f726a9f4cff586cefe8198e5c628a55d70b5571) and ![Image 48: {\displaystyle x=+a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8814caa6e38d62bcfb2abd0536056ce084338746), respectively.)

A drawback of these coordinates is that they do not have a 1-to-1 transformation to the [Cartesian coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinates "Cartesian coordinates")

![Image 49: {\displaystyle x=a\sigma \tau }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8e19c8f54882a5db5c20c0e99227b469dfff2631)![Image 50: {\displaystyle y^{2}=a^{2}\left(\sigma ^{2}-1\right)\left(1-\tau ^{2}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ae86dd0cd2422924ddab1eee4936eaf47ea3f868)

Alternative scale factors
-------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Elliptic_cylindrical_coordinates&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Alternative scale factors")]

The scale factors for the alternative elliptic coordinates ![Image 51: {\displaystyle (\sigma ,\tau ,z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b3bd510376be13a47c5f0d984e365cb324833daa) are

![Image 52: {\displaystyle h_{\sigma }=a{\sqrt {\frac {\sigma ^{2}-\tau ^{2}}{\sigma ^{2}-1}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/51390ae73787e24f7da85fb8b3edc6eae21f6607)![Image 53: {\displaystyle h_{\tau }=a{\sqrt {\frac {\sigma ^{2}-\tau ^{2}}{1-\tau ^{2}}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/80aad5a0d14eee8a270198a955e758a14ec9327b)
and, of course, ![Image 54: {\displaystyle h_{z}=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/21caf494fc4bbb0ac6f8d7fe11be1982f03533e5). Hence, the infinitesimal volume element becomes

![Image 55: {\displaystyle dV=a^{2}{\frac {\sigma ^{2}-\tau ^{2}}{\sqrt {\left(\sigma ^{2}-1\right)\left(1-\tau ^{2}\right)}}}d\sigma d\tau dz}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1470a16cf39162db3ef0bb808e493c50259a0884)
and the Laplacian equals

![Image 56: {\displaystyle \nabla ^{2}\Phi ={\frac {1}{a^{2}\left(\sigma ^{2}-\tau ^{2}\right)}}\left[{\sqrt {\sigma ^{2}-1}}{\frac {\partial }{\partial \sigma }}\left({\sqrt {\sigma ^{2}-1}}{\frac {\partial \Phi }{\partial \sigma }}\right)+{\sqrt {1-\tau ^{2}}}{\frac {\partial }{\partial \tau }}\left({\sqrt {1-\tau ^{2}}}{\frac {\partial \Phi }{\partial \tau }}\right)\right]+{\frac {\partial ^{2}\Phi }{\partial z^{2}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e06e8fa095edfa33c9d293a9c583bb8f81fe658e)
Other differential operators such as ![Image 57: {\displaystyle \nabla \cdot \mathbf {F} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1f4f5b9e6560d7fdaf1a69d9fdeff6beb1f327cc) and ![Image 58: {\displaystyle \nabla \times \mathbf {F} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c6e2429b7e3cb6965d1a6651703ff5ef0c88b378) can be expressed in the coordinates ![Image 59: {\displaystyle (\sigma ,\tau )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c2d60596d44a9bc5043b60b76c1fac959eb65eb0) by substituting the scale factors into the general formulae found in [orthogonal coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_coordinates "Orthogonal coordinates").

The classic applications of elliptic cylindrical coordinates are in solving [partial differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_differential_equations "Partial differential equations"), e.g., [Laplace's equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laplace%27s_equation "Laplace's equation") or the [Helmholtz equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helmholtz_equation "Helmholtz equation"), for which elliptic cylindrical coordinates allow a [separation of variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separation_of_variables "Separation of variables"). A typical example would be the [electric field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_field "Electric field") surrounding a flat conducting plate of width ![Image 60: {\displaystyle 2a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d325c24be7d760207674a169b078892bdd5cbc76).

The three-dimensional [wave equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wave_equation "Wave equation"), when expressed in elliptic cylindrical coordinates, may be solved by separation of variables, leading to the [Mathieu differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathieu_differential_equation "Mathieu differential equation").

The geometric properties of elliptic coordinates can also be useful. A typical example might involve an integration over all pairs of vectors ![Image 61: {\displaystyle \mathbf {p} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dd73e3862cb92b016721b8c492eadb4e8a577527) and ![Image 62: {\displaystyle \mathbf {q} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7be005a326b7ac3fe4c24bca391369f44c4c2876) that sum to a fixed vector ![Image 63: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} =\mathbf {p} +\mathbf {q} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4fe5d685b902c704282408b4c203201842b75b2f), where the integrand was a function of the vector lengths ![Image 64: {\displaystyle \left|\mathbf {p} \right|}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/659d13b0d421479de930d4ca9e548718c0c9b6d3) and ![Image 65: {\displaystyle \left|\mathbf {q} \right|}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/83cf8ffd28d9c1932f17563af351a74b75d57c27). (In such a case, one would position ![Image 66: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eca0f46511c4c986c48b254073732c0bd98ae0c1) between the two foci and aligned with the ![Image 67: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4)-axis, i.e., ![Image 68: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} =2a\mathbf {\hat {x}} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2fbcd16f0c2b2f37867792153631c7e9b642de92).) For concreteness, ![Image 69: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eca0f46511c4c986c48b254073732c0bd98ae0c1), ![Image 70: {\displaystyle \mathbf {p} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dd73e3862cb92b016721b8c492eadb4e8a577527) and ![Image 71: {\displaystyle \mathbf {q} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7be005a326b7ac3fe4c24bca391369f44c4c2876) could represent the [momenta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum "Momentum") of a particle and its decomposition products, respectively, and the integrand might involve the kinetic energies of the products (which are proportional to the squared lengths of the momenta).
