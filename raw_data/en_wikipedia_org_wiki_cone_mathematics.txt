A double cone, not infinitely extended

In [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry"), a **cone** is a [three-dimensional figure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_figure "Three-dimensional figure") that tapers smoothly from a [flat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_(geometry) "Plane (geometry)") base (typically a [circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle "Circle")) to a point not contained in the base, called the _[apex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apex\_(geometry) "Apex (geometry)")_ or _[vertex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex\_(geometry) "Vertex (geometry)")_.

A cone is formed by a set of [line segments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_segment "Line segment"), [half-lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ray_(geometry) "Ray (geometry)"), or [lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_(geometry) "Line (geometry)") connecting a common point, the apex, to all of the points on a base. In the case of line segments, the cone does not extend beyond the base, while in the case of half-lines, it extends infinitely far. In the case of lines, the cone extends infinitely far in both directions from the apex, in which case it is sometimes called a _double cone_. Each of the two halves of a double cone split at the apex is called a _nappe_.

Depending on the author, the base may be restricted to a circle, any one-dimensional [quadratic form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadratic_form "Quadratic form") in the plane, any closed [one-dimensional figure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One-dimensional_space "One-dimensional space"), or any of the above plus all the enclosed points. If the enclosed points are included in the base, the cone is a [solid object](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_geometry "Solid geometry"); otherwise it is an [open surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_surface "Open surface"), a [two-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two-dimensional "Two-dimensional") object in three-dimensional space. In the case of a solid object, the boundary formed by these lines or partial lines is called the _lateral surface_; if the lateral surface is [unbounded](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unbounded_set "Unbounded set"), it is a _[conical surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conical\_surface "Conical surface")_.

The [axis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_symmetry "Rotational symmetry") of a cone is the straight line passing through the apex about which the cone has a [circular symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circular_symmetry "Circular symmetry"). In common usage in elementary geometry, cones are assumed to be _right circular_, i.e., with a circle base [perpendicular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular "Perpendicular") to the axis.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cone_(mathematics)#cite_note-:1-1) If the cone is right circular the intersection of a plane with the lateral surface is a [conic section](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conic_section "Conic section"). In general, however, the base may be any shape[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cone_(mathematics)#cite_note-grunbaum-2) and the apex may lie anywhere (though it is usually assumed that the base is bounded and therefore has finite [area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area_(geometry) "Area (geometry)"), and that the apex lies outside the plane of the base). Contrasted with right cones are _oblique cones_, in which the axis passes through the centre of the base non-perpendicularly.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cone_(mathematics)#cite_note-MathWorld-3)

Depending on context, _cone_ may refer more narrowly to either a [convex cone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_cone "Convex cone") or [projective cone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_cone "Projective cone"). Cones can be generalized to [higher dimensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension#Additional_dimensions "Dimension").

Further terminology
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cone&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Further terminology")]

The perimeter of the base of a cone is called the _directrix_, and each of the line segments between the directrix and apex is a _generatrix_ or _generating line_ of the lateral surface. (For the connection between this sense of the term _directrix_ and the [directrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Directrix_(conic_section) "Directrix (conic section)") of a conic section, see [Dandelin spheres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dandelin_spheres "Dandelin spheres").)

The _base radius_ of a circular cone is the [radius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radius "Radius") of its base; often this is simply called the radius of the cone. The _aperture_ of a right circular cone is the maximum angle between two generatrix lines; if the generatrix makes an angle _Œ∏_ to the axis, the aperture is 2 _Œ∏_. In [optics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optics "Optics"), the angle _Œ∏_ is called the _half-angle_ of the cone, to distinguish it from the aperture.

A cone with a region including its apex cut off by a plane is called a _truncated cone_; if the [truncation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Truncation_(geometry) "Truncation (geometry)") plane is parallel to the cone's base, it is called a _[frustum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frustum "Frustum")_.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cone_(mathematics)#cite_note-:1-1) An _[elliptical cone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptical\_cone "Elliptical cone")_ is a cone with an [elliptical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse "Ellipse") base.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cone_(mathematics)#cite_note-:1-1) A _generalized cone_ is the surface created by the set of lines passing through a vertex and every point on a boundary (see [Visual hull](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visual_hull "Visual hull")).

Measurements and equations
--------------------------

[Proof without words](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proof_without_words "Proof without words") that the volume of a cone is a third of a cylinder of equal diameter and height 
1.A cone and a cylinder have radius _r_ and height _h_.
2.The volume ratio is maintained when the height is scaled to _h'_= _r_ ‚àöœÄ.
3.Decompose it into thin slices.
4.Using Cavalieri's principle, reshape each slice into a square of the same area.
5.The pyramid is replicated twice.
6.Combining them into a cube shows that the volume ratio is 1:3.

The [volume](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume "Volume")![Image 7: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) of any conic solid is one third of the product of the area of the base ![Image 8: {\displaystyle A_{B}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/40c23902854ca17ed340b014fda4b3e6adc02b46) and the height ![Image 9: {\displaystyle h}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b26be3e694314bc90c3215047e4a2010c6ee184a)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cone_(mathematics)#cite_note-:0-4)

![Image 10: {\displaystyle V={\frac {1}{3}}A_{B}h.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e37289a6c713019fa691146d8af5a49a81eadf16)

In modern mathematics, this formula can easily be computed using calculus ‚Äî it is, up to scaling, the integral

![Image 11: {\displaystyle \int x^{2}\,dx={\tfrac {1}{3}}x^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2220beda63bd2cb350b048d4651c94c6d9de18d1)

Without using calculus, the formula can be proven by comparing the cone to a pyramid and applying [Cavalieri's principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cavalieri%27s_principle "Cavalieri's principle") ‚Äì specifically, comparing the cone to a (vertically scaled) right square pyramid, which forms one third of a cube. This formula cannot be proven without using such infinitesimal arguments ‚Äì unlike the 2-dimensional formulae for polyhedral area, though similar to the area of the circle ‚Äì and hence admitted less rigorous proofs before the advent of calculus, with the ancient Greeks using the [method of exhaustion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Method_of_exhaustion "Method of exhaustion"). This is essentially the content of [Hilbert's third problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert%27s_third_problem "Hilbert's third problem") ‚Äì more precisely, not all polyhedral pyramids are _scissors congruent_ (can be cut apart into finite pieces and rearranged into the other), and thus volume cannot be computed purely by using a decomposition argument.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cone_(mathematics)#cite_note-5)

The [center of mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Center_of_mass "Center of mass") of a conic solid of uniform density lies one-quarter of the way from the center of the base to the vertex, on the straight line joining the two.

Projective geometry
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cone&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Projective geometry")]

[![Image 64](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Australia_Square_building_in_George_Street_Sydney.jpg/250px-Australia_Square_building_in_George_Street_Sydney.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Australia_Square_building_in_George_Street_Sydney.jpg)

In [projective geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_geometry "Projective geometry"), a [cylinder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylinder_(geometry) "Cylinder (geometry)") is simply a cone whose apex is at infinity, which corresponds visually to a cylinder in perspective appearing to be a cone towards the sky.

In [projective geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_geometry "Projective geometry"), a [cylinder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylinder_(geometry) "Cylinder (geometry)") is simply a cone whose apex is at infinity.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cone_(mathematics)#cite_note-7) Intuitively, if one keeps the base fixed and takes the limit as the apex goes to infinity, one obtains a cylinder, the angle of the side increasing as [arctan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arctan "Arctan"), in the limit forming a [right angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_angle "Right angle"). This is useful in the definition of [degenerate conics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degenerate_conic "Degenerate conic"), which require considering the [cylindrical conics](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cylindrical_conic&action=edit&redlink=1 "Cylindrical conic (page does not exist)").

According to [G. B. Halsted](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G._B._Halsted "G. B. Halsted"), a cone is generated similarly to a [Steiner conic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steiner_conic "Steiner conic") only with a projectivity and [axial pencils](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pencil_(mathematics) "Pencil (mathematics)") (not in perspective) rather than the projective ranges used for the Steiner conic:

"If two copunctual non-costraight axial pencils are projective but not perspective, the meets of correlated planes form a 'conic surface of the second order', or 'cone'."[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cone_(mathematics)#cite_note-8)

The definition of a cone may be extended to higher dimensions; see [convex cone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_cone "Convex cone"). In this case, one says that a [convex set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_set "Convex set")_C_ in the [real](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number")[vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space")![Image 65: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d) is a cone (with apex at the origin) if for every vector _x_ in _C_ and every nonnegative real number _a_, the vector _ax_ is in _C_.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cone_(mathematics)#cite_note-grunbaum-2) In this context, the analogues of circular cones are not usually special; in fact one is often interested in [polyhedral cones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_cone#Polyhedral_and_finitely_generated_cones "Convex cone").

An even more general concept is the [topological cone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_cone "Topological cone"), which is defined in arbitrary topological spaces.
