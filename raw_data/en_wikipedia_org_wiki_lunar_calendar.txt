A **lunar calendar** is a [calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calendar "Calendar") based on the [monthly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Month "Month") cycles of the [Moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moon "Moon")'s [phases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunar_phase "Lunar phase") ([synodic months](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunar_month#Synodic_month "Lunar month"), [lunations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunation "Lunation")), in contrast to [solar calendars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_calendar "Solar calendar"), whose annual cycles are based on the [solar year](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_year "Solar year"), and [lunisolar calendars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunisolar_calendar "Lunisolar calendar"), whose [lunar months](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunar_month "Lunar month") are brought into alignment with the solar year through some process of [intercalation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intercalation_(timekeeping) "Intercalation (timekeeping)")– such as by insertion of a [leap month](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leap_month "Leap month"). The most widely observed lunar calendar is the [Islamic calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamic_calendar "Islamic calendar").[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunar_calendar#cite_note-1) The details of when months begin vary from calendar to calendar, with some using [new](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_moon "New moon"), [full](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Full_moon "Full moon"), or [crescent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crescent "Crescent") moons and others employing detailed calculations.

Since each lunation is approximately 29+1⁄2 days,[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunar_calendar#cite_note-ESAA-2) it is common for the months of a lunar calendar to alternate between 29 and 30 days. Since the period of 12 such lunations, a **lunar year**, is 354 days, 8 hours, 48 minutes, 34 seconds (354.36707 days),[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunar_calendar#cite_note-ESAA-2) lunar calendars are 11 to 12 days shorter than the [solar year](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tropical_year "Tropical year"). In lunar calendars, which do not make use of lunisolar calendars' intercalation, the lunar months cycle through all the seasons of a solar year over the course of a 33–34 lunar-year cycle (see, e.g., [list of Islamic years](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_Islamic_years "List of Islamic years")).

History
-------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Lunar_calendar&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: History")]

Scholars have argued that ancient hunters conducted regular astronomical observations of the Moon back in the [Upper Palaeolithic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Upper_Paleolithic "Upper Paleolithic").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunar_calendar#cite_note-3) Samuel L. Macey dates the earliest uses of the Moon as a time-measuring device back to 28,000–30,000 years ago.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunar_calendar#cite_note-4)

Start of the lunar month
------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Lunar_calendar&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Start of the lunar month")]

Lunar and lunisolar calendars differ as to which day is the first day of the month. Some are based on the first sighting of the [lunar crescent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunar_phase "Lunar phase"), such as the [Hijri calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamic_calendar "Islamic calendar") observed by most of Islam. Alternatively, in some lunisolar calendars, such as the [Hebrew calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hebrew_calendar "Hebrew calendar") and [Chinese calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_calendar "Chinese calendar"), the first day of a month is the day when an astronomical [new moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_moon "New moon") occurs in a particular time zone. In others, such as some [Hindu calendars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hindu_calendar "Hindu calendar"), each month begins on the day after the full moon.

Length of the lunar month
-------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Lunar_calendar&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Length of the lunar month")]

The length of each lunar cycle varies slightly from the average value. In addition, observations are subject to uncertainty and weather conditions. Thus, to minimise uncertainty, there have been attempts to create fixed arithmetical rules to determine the start of each calendar month. The best known of these is the [Tabular Islamic calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tabular_Islamic_calendar "Tabular Islamic calendar"): in brief, it has a 30-year cycle with 11 [leap years](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leap_year "Leap year") of 355 days and 19 years of 354 days. In the long term, it is accurate to one day in about 2,500 solar years or 2,570 lunar years. It also deviates from observation by up to about one or two days in the short term. The algorithm was introduced by Muslim astronomers in the 8th century to predict the approximate date of the first crescent moon, which is used to determine the first day of each month in the [Islamic lunar calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamic_calendar "Islamic calendar").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunar_calendar#cite_note-5)

Lunisolar calendars
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Lunar_calendar&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Lunisolar calendars")]

Main article: [Lunisolar calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunisolar_calendar "Lunisolar calendar")

Most calendars referred to as "lunar" calendars are [lunisolar calendars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunisolar_calendar "Lunisolar calendar"). Their months are based on observations of the lunar cycle, with periodic [intercalation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intercalation_(timekeeping) "Intercalation (timekeeping)") being used to restore them into general agreement with the solar year. The solar "[civic calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egyptian_calendar "Egyptian calendar")" that was used in [ancient Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Egypt "Ancient Egypt") showed traces of its origin in the earlier lunar calendar, which continued to be used alongside it for religious and agricultural purposes. Present-day lunisolar calendars include the [Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_calendar "Chinese calendar"), [Korean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_calendar "Korean calendar"), [Vietnamese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vietnamese_calendar "Vietnamese calendar"), [Hindu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hindu_calendar "Hindu calendar"), [Hebrew](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hebrew_calendar "Hebrew calendar") and [Thai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thai_lunar_calendar "Thai lunar calendar") calendars.

The most common form of intercalation is to add an additional month every second or third year. Some lunisolar calendars are also calibrated by annual natural events which are affected by lunar cycles as well as the solar cycle. An example of this is the lunisolar calendar of the [Banks Islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Banks_Islands "Banks Islands"), which includes three months in which the edible [palolo worms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palolo_worm "Palolo worm") mass on the beaches. These events occur at the last quarter of the lunar month, as the reproductive cycle of the palolos is synchronized with the moon.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunar_calendar#cite_note-8)
