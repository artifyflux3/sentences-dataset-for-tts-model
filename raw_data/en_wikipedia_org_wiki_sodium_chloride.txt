**Sodium chloride** ,[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride#cite_note-8) commonly known as [edible salt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salt#Edible_salt "Salt"), is an [ionic compound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ionic_compound "Ionic compound") with the [chemical formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_formula "Chemical formula")**NaCl**, representing a 1:1 ratio of [sodium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium "Sodium") and [chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chloride "Chloride") ions. It is transparent or translucent, brittle, [hygroscopic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hygroscopic "Hygroscopic"), and occurs as the mineral [halite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halite "Halite"). In its edible form, it is commonly used as a [condiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condiment "Condiment") and [food preservative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curing_(food_preservation) "Curing (food preservation)"). Large quantities of sodium chloride are used in many industrial processes, and it is a major source of sodium and chlorine compounds used as [feedstocks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feedstock "Feedstock") for further [chemical syntheses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_synthesis "Chemical synthesis"). Another major application of sodium chloride is [deicing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deicing "Deicing") of roadways in sub-freezing weather.

In addition to the many familiar domestic uses of salt, more dominant applications of the approximately 250 million tonnes per year production (2008 data) include chemicals and de-icing.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride#cite_note-Ullmann-9)

Salt is used, directly or indirectly, in the production of many chemicals, which consumes most of the world's production.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride#cite_note-usgs-10)

#### Chlor-alkali industry

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sodium_chloride&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Chlor-alkali industry")]

It is the starting point for the [chloralkali process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chloralkali_process "Chloralkali process"), the industrial process to produce [chlorine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chlorine "Chlorine") and [sodium hydroxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide "Sodium hydroxide"), according to the [chemical equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_equation "Chemical equation")

![Image 17: {\displaystyle {2\,\mathrm {NaCl} {}+{}2\,\mathrm {H} {\vphantom {A}}_{\smash[{t}]{2}}\mathrm {O} {}\mathrel {\xrightarrow {\text{electrolysis}} } {}\mathrm {Cl} {\vphantom {A}}_{\smash[{t}]{2}}{}+{}\mathrm {H} {\vphantom {A}}_{\smash[{t}]{2}}{}+{}2\,\mathrm {NaOH} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3f72bb1cc246a12890d490ed7aaf684eb3374691)
This electrolysis is conducted in either a mercury cell, a diaphragm cell, or a membrane cell. Each of those uses a different method to separate the chlorine from the sodium hydroxide. Other technologies are under development due to the high energy consumption of the electrolysis, whereby small improvements in the efficiency can have large economic paybacks. Some applications of chlorine include [PVC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PVC "PVC")[thermoplastics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermoplastic "Thermoplastic") production, disinfectants, and solvents.

Sodium hydroxide is extensively used in many different industries enabling production of paper, soap, aluminum, and more.

Sodium chloride is used in the [Solvay process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solvay_process "Solvay process") to produce [sodium carbonate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_carbonate "Sodium carbonate") and [calcium chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calcium_chloride "Calcium chloride"). Sodium carbonate, in turn, is used to produce [glass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glass "Glass"), [sodium bicarbonate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_bicarbonate "Sodium bicarbonate"), and [dyes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dye "Dye"), as well as a myriad of other chemicals. In the [Mannheim process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mannheim_process "Mannheim process"), sodium chloride is used for the production of [sodium sulfate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_sulfate "Sodium sulfate") and [hydrochloric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid "Hydrochloric acid").

### Miscellaneous industrial uses

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sodium_chloride&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Miscellaneous industrial uses")]

Sodium chloride is heavily used, so even relatively minor applications can consume massive quantities. In [oil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crude_oil "Crude oil") and [gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_gas "Natural gas") exploration, salt is an important component of drilling fluids in well drilling.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride#cite_note-11) It is used to [flocculate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flocculation "Flocculation") and increase the [density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Density "Density") of the drilling fluid to overcome high downwell gas pressures. Whenever a drill hits a salt formation, salt is added to the drilling fluid to saturate the solution in order to minimize the dissolution within the salt stratum.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride#cite_note-Ullmann-9) Salt is also used to increase the curing of concrete in cemented casings.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride#cite_note-usgs-10)

In textiles and dyeing, salt is used as a brine rinse to separate organic contaminants,[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride#cite_note-12) to promote "salting out" of dyestuff precipitates, and to blend with concentrated dyes to increase yield in dyebaths and make the colors look sharper. One of its main roles is to provide the positive ion charge to promote the absorption of negatively charged ions of dyes.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride#cite_note-usgs-10)

For use in the [pulp and paper industry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulp_and_paper_industry "Pulp and paper industry"), it is used to manufacture [sodium chlorate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chlorate "Sodium chlorate"), which is then reacted with [sulfuric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfuric_acid "Sulfuric acid") and a reducing agent such as [methanol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol "Methanol") to manufacture [chlorine dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chlorine_dioxide "Chlorine dioxide"), a [bleaching](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bleach "Bleach") chemical that is widely used to [bleach wood pulp](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bleaching_of_wood_pulp "Bleaching of wood pulp").

In tanning and leather treatment, salt is added to animal [hides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hide_(skin) "Hide (skin)") to inhibit microbial activity on the underside of the hides and to attract moisture back into the hides.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride#cite_note-usgs-10)

In rubber manufacture, salt is used to make [buna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synthetic_rubber "Synthetic rubber"), [neoprene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neoprene "Neoprene"), and white rubber types. Salt brine and sulfuric acid are used to coagulate an emulsified [latex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latex "Latex") made from chlorinated [butadiene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Butadiene "Butadiene").[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride#cite_note-usgs-10)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride#cite_note-Ullmann-9)

Salt also is added to secure the soil and to provide firmness to the foundation on which highways are built. The salt acts to minimize the effects of shifting caused in the subsurface by changes in humidity and traffic load.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride#cite_note-usgs-10)

[Hard water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hard_water "Hard water") contains [calcium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calcium "Calcium") and [magnesium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnesium "Magnesium") ions that interfere with action of [soap](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soap "Soap") and contribute to the buildup of a scale or film of alkaline mineral deposits in household and industrial equipment and pipes. Commercial and residential water-softening units use [ion-exchange resins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ion-exchange_resin "Ion-exchange resin") to remove ions that cause the hardness. These resins are generated and regenerated using sodium chloride.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride#cite_note-usgs-10)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride#cite_note-Ullmann-9)

The second major application of salt is for [deicing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deicing "Deicing") and anti-icing of roads, both in [grit bins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grit_bin "Grit bin") and spread by [winter service vehicles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Winter_service_vehicle "Winter service vehicle"). In anticipation of snowfall, roads are optimally "anti-iced" with brine (concentrated [solution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solution_(chemistry) "Solution (chemistry)") of salt in water), which prevents bonding between the snow-ice and the road surface. This procedure obviates the heavy use of salt after the snowfall. For deicing, mixtures of brine and salt are used, sometimes with additional agents such as [calcium chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calcium_chloride "Calcium chloride") and/or [magnesium chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnesium_chloride "Magnesium chloride"). The use of salt or brine becomes ineffective below −10°C (14°F).

[![Image 18](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Winter_road_salt.jpg/330px-Winter_road_salt.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Winter_road_salt.jpg)

Mounds of road salt for use in winter

Salt for deicing in the United Kingdom predominantly comes from a single mine in [Winsford](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Winsford "Winsford") in [Cheshire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salt_in_Cheshire "Salt in Cheshire"). Prior to distribution it is mixed with <100 ppm of [sodium ferrocyanide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_ferrocyanide "Sodium ferrocyanide") as an [anticaking agent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anticaking_agent "Anticaking agent"), which enables rock salt to flow freely out of the gritting vehicles despite being stockpiled prior to use. In recent years this additive has also been used in table salt. Other additives have been used in road salt to reduce the total costs. For example, in the US, a byproduct carbohydrate solution from sugar-beet processing was mixed with rock salt and adhered to road surfaces about 40% better than loose rock salt alone. Because it stayed on the road longer, the treatment did not have to be repeated several times, saving time and money.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride#cite_note-usgs-10)

In the technical terms of physical chemistry, the minimum freezing point of a water-salt mixture is −21.12°C (−6.02°F) for 23.31 wt% of salt. Freezing near this concentration is however so slow that the [eutectic point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eutectic_point "Eutectic point") of −22.4°C (−8.3°F) can be reached with about 25 wt% of salt.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride#cite_note-u1-13)

#### Environmental effects

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sodium_chloride&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Environmental effects")]

Road salt ends up in fresh-water bodies and could harm aquatic plants and animals by disrupting their [osmoregulation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osmoregulation "Osmoregulation") ability.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride#cite_note-14) The omnipresence of salt in coastal areas poses a problem in any [coating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coating "Coating") application, because trapped salts cause great problems in adhesion. Naval authorities and ship builders monitor the salt concentrations on surfaces during construction. Maximal salt concentrations on surfaces are dependent on the authority and application. The [IMO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Maritime_Organization "International Maritime Organization") regulation is mostly used and sets salt levels to a maximum of 50 mg/m 2 soluble salts measured as sodium chloride. These measurements are done by means of a [Bresle test](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bresle_test "Bresle test"). Salinization (increasing salinity, aka _[freshwater salinization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freshwater\_salinization "Freshwater salinization") syndrome_) and subsequent increased metal leaching is an ongoing problem throughout North America and European fresh waterways.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride#cite_note-15)

In highway de-icing, salt has been associated with [corrosion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corrosion "Corrosion") of bridge decks, motor vehicles, reinforcement bar and wire, and unprotected steel structures used in road construction. [Surface runoff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_runoff "Surface runoff"), vehicle spraying, and windblown salt also affect soil, roadside vegetation, and local surface water and groundwater supplies. Although evidence of environmental loading of salt has been found during peak usage, the spring rains and thaws usually dilute the concentrations of sodium in the area where salt was applied.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride#cite_note-usgs-10) A 2009 study found that approximately 70% of the road salt being applied in the [Minneapolis-St Paul](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minneapolis-St_Paul "Minneapolis-St Paul") metro area is retained in the local watershed.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride#cite_note-16)

Some agencies are substituting [beer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beer "Beer"), [molasses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molasses "Molasses"), and [beet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beet "Beet") juice instead of road salt.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride#cite_note-17) Airlines utilize more [glycol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycol "Glycol") and [sugar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sugar "Sugar") rather than salt-based solutions for deicing.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride#cite_note-18)

### Food industry and agriculture

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sodium_chloride&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Food industry and agriculture")]

Main article: [Salt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salt "Salt")

Salt is added to food, either by the food producer or by the consumer, as a flavor enhancer, preservative, binder, [fermentation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermentation_(food) "Fermentation (food)")-control additive, texture-control agent, and color developer. The salt consumption in the food industry is subdivided, in descending order of consumption, into other food processing, meat packers, [canning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canning "Canning"), baking, dairy, and grain mill products. Salt is added to promote color development in bacon, ham and other processed meat products. As a preservative, salt inhibits the growth of bacteria. Salt acts as a binder in [sausages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sausage "Sausage") to form a binding gel made up of meat, fat, and moisture. Salt also acts as a flavor enhancer and as a [tenderizer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tenderizer "Tenderizer").[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride#cite_note-usgs-10)

It is used as a cheap and safe [desiccant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Desiccant "Desiccant") because of its [hygroscopic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hygroscopic "Hygroscopic") properties, making [salting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salting_(food) "Salting (food)") an effective method of [food preservation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Food_preservation "Food preservation") historically; the salt draws water out of bacteria through [osmotic pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osmotic_pressure "Osmotic pressure"), keeping it from reproducing, a major source of food spoilage. Even though more effective desiccants are available, few are safe for humans to ingest. Many [microorganisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism "Microorganism") cannot live in a salty environment: water is drawn out of their [cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_(biology) "Cell (biology)") by [osmosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osmosis "Osmosis"). For this reason salt is used to [preserve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Food_preservation "Food preservation") some foods, such as bacon, fish, or cabbage.

In many dairy industries, salt is added to cheese as a color-, fermentation-, and texture-control agent. The dairy subsector includes companies that manufacture creamery butter, condensed and evaporated milk, frozen desserts, ice cream, natural and processed cheese, and specialty dairy products. In canning, salt is primarily added as a flavor enhancer and [preservative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Preservative "Preservative"). It also is used as a carrier for other ingredients, dehydrating agent, enzyme inhibitor and tenderizer. In baking, salt is added to control the rate of fermentation in bread dough. It also is used to strengthen the [gluten](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gluten "Gluten") (the elastic protein-water complex in certain doughs) and as a flavor enhancer, such as a topping on baked goods. The food-processing category also contains grain mill products. These products consist of milling flour and rice and manufacturing cereal breakfast food and blended or prepared flour. Salt is also used a seasoning agent in products such as potato chips, [pretzels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pretzel "Pretzel"), and cat and dog food.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride#cite_note-usgs-10)

Sodium chloride is used in veterinary medicine as [emesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emesis "Emesis")-causing agent. It is given as warm saturated solution. Emesis can also be caused by [pharyngeal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pharynx "Pharynx") placement of small amount of plain salt or salt crystals.

For watering plants to use sodium chloride (NaCl) as a fertilizer, moderate concentration is used to avoid potential toxicity: 1–3 grams (0.035–0.106 oz) per liter is generally safe and effective for most plants.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride#cite_note-19)[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride#cite_note-20)[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride#cite_note-21)

Sodium chloride is used together with water as one of the primary solutions for [intravenous therapy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intravenous_therapy "Intravenous therapy"). [Nasal spray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nasal_spray "Nasal spray") often contains a [saline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saline_(medicine) "Saline (medicine)") solution.

Sodium chloride is also available as an oral tablet and is taken to treat low sodium levels.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride#cite_note-22)

[![Image 19](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Metlx.jpg/250px-Metlx.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Metlx.jpg)

A class-D fire extinguisher for various metals

Sodium chloride is the principal extinguishing agent in dry-powder [fire extinguishers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fire_extinguisher "Fire extinguisher") that are used on combustible metal fires, for metals such as magnesium, zirconium, titanium, and lithium (Class D extinguishers). The salt forms an oxygen-excluding crust that smothers the fire.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride#cite_note-23)

Since at least [medieval](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval "Medieval") times, people have used salt as a cleansing agent rubbed on household surfaces. It is also used in many brands of [shampoo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shampoo "Shampoo") and toothpaste and is commonly used to deice driveways, parking lots, and walkways.

Sodium chloride crystals have a [transmittance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transmittance "Transmittance") of at least 90% (through 1 mm) for [infrared](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infrared "Infrared") light having wavelengths in the range 0.2– 18[μm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%CE%9Cm "Μm").[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride#cite_note-24) They were used in optical components such as windows and lenses, where few non-absorbing alternatives existed in that spectral range. While inexpensive, NaCl crystals are soft and [hygroscopic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hygroscopy "Hygroscopy") – when exposed to the water in ambient air, they gradually cover with "frost". This limits application of NaCl to dry environments, vacuum-sealed areas, or short-term uses such as prototyping. Materials that are mechanically stronger and less sensitive to moisture, such as [zinc selenide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zinc_selenide "Zinc selenide") and [chalcogenide glasses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chalcogenide_glass "Chalcogenide glass"), more widely used than NaCl.

### Solid sodium chloride

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sodium_chloride&action=edit&section=16 "Edit section: Solid sodium chloride")]

In solid sodium chloride, each ion is surrounded by six ions of the opposite charge as expected on electrostatic grounds. The surrounding ions are located at the vertices of a regular [octahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octahedron "Octahedron"). In the language of [close-packing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Close-packing "Close-packing"), the larger [chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chloride "Chloride")[ions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ion "Ion") (167 pm in size[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride#cite_note-Shannon-25)) are arranged in a cubic array whereas the smaller [sodium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium "Sodium") ions (116 pm[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride#cite_note-Shannon-25)) fill all the cubic gaps (octahedral voids) between them. This same basic structure is found in many other [compounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_compound "Chemical compound") and is commonly known as the [NaCl structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NaCl_structure "NaCl structure") or rock salt crystal structure. It can be represented as a [face-centered cubic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cubic_crystal_system "Cubic crystal system") (fcc) lattice with a two-atom basis or as two interpenetrating face centered cubic lattices. The first atom is located at each lattice point, and the second atom is located halfway between lattice points along the fcc unit cell edge.

Solid sodium chloride has a melting point of 801°C and liquid sodium chloride boils at 1465°C. Atomic-resolution real-time video imaging allows visualization of the initial stage of crystal nucleation of sodium chloride.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride#cite_note-26)

The [Thermal conductivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermal_conductivity "Thermal conductivity") of sodium chloride as a function of temperature has a maximum of 2.03 W/(cm K) at 8 K (−265.15°C; −445.27°F) and decreases to 0.069 at 314 K (41°C; 106°F). It also decreases with [doping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doping_(semiconductor) "Doping (semiconductor)").[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride#cite_note-27)

[![Image 22](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/NaCl%28H2O%292slab.png/330px-NaCl%28H2O%292slab.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:NaCl(H2O)2slab.png)

View of one slab of [hydrohalite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrohalite "Hydrohalite"), NaCl·2H 2 O. (red = O, white = H, green = Cl, purple = Na).[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride#cite_note-28)

From cold (sub-freezing) solutions, salt crystallises with [water of hydration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water_of_hydration "Water of hydration") as [hydrohalite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrohalite "Hydrohalite") (the dihydrate NaCl·2 H 2 O).[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride#cite_note-29)

In 2023, it was discovered that under pressure, sodium chloride can form the hydrates NaCl·8.5H 2 O and NaCl·13H 2 O.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride#cite_note-30)

[![Image 23](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/WatNaCl.png/330px-WatNaCl.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:WatNaCl.png)

Phase diagram of water–NaCl mixture

The attraction between the Na+ and Cl− ions in the solid is so strong that only highly [polar solvents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_solvent "Polar solvent") like water dissolve NaCl well.

When dissolved in water, the sodium chloride framework disintegrates as the Na+ and Cl− ions become surrounded by polar water molecules. These solutions consist of [metal aquo complex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metal_aquo_complex "Metal aquo complex") with the formula [Na(H 2 O)8]+, with the Na–O distance of 250[pm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Picometer "Picometer"). The chloride ions are also strongly solvated, each being surrounded by an average of six molecules of water.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride#cite_note-Lincoln-31) Solutions of sodium chloride have very different properties from pure water. The [eutectic point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eutectic_point "Eutectic point") is −21.12°C (−6.02°F) for 23.31% [mass fraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass_fraction_(chemistry)#Mass_percentage "Mass fraction (chemistry)") of salt, and the boiling point of saturated salt solution is near 108.7°C (227.7°F).[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride#cite_note-u1-13)

### pH of sodium chloride solutions

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sodium_chloride&action=edit&section=18 "Edit section: pH of sodium chloride solutions")]

The [pH](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PH "PH") of a sodium chloride solution remains ≈7 due to the extremely weak basicity of the Cl− ion, which is the conjugate base of the [strong acid HCl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid "Hydrochloric acid"). In other words, NaCl has no effect on system pH[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride#cite_note-32) in diluted solutions where the effects of ionic strength and activity coefficients are negligible.

### Stoichiometric and structure variants

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sodium_chloride&action=edit&section=19 "Edit section: Stoichiometric and structure variants")]

Common salt has a 1:1 molar ratio of sodium and chlorine. In 2013, compounds of sodium and chloride of different [stoichiometries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stoichiometry "Stoichiometry") have been discovered; five new compounds were predicted (e.g., [Na 3 Cl](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Na3Cl&action=edit&redlink=1 "Na3Cl (page does not exist)"), [Na 2 Cl](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Na2Cl&action=edit&redlink=1 "Na2Cl (page does not exist)"), [Na 3 Cl 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Na3Cl2&action=edit&redlink=1 "Na3Cl2 (page does not exist)"), [NaCl 3](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=NaCl3&action=edit&redlink=1 "NaCl3 (page does not exist)"), and [NaCl 7](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=NaCl7&action=edit&redlink=1 "NaCl7 (page does not exist)")). The existence of some of them has been experimentally confirmed at high pressures and other conditions: cubic and orthorhombic NaCl 3, two-dimensional metallic tetragonal Na 3 Cl and exotic hexagonal NaCl.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride#cite_note-34) This indicates that compounds violating chemical intuition are possible, in simple systems under non-ambient conditions.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride#cite_note-35)

Salt is found in the [Earth's crust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_crust "Earth's crust") as the mineral [halite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halite "Halite") (rock salt), and a tiny amount exists as suspended [sea salt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sea_salt "Sea salt") particles in the atmosphere.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride#cite_note-36) These particles are the dominant [cloud condensation nuclei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cloud_condensation_nuclei "Cloud condensation nuclei") far out at sea, which allow the formation of [clouds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cloud "Cloud") in otherwise [non-polluted](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pollution "Pollution")[air](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Air "Air").[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride#cite_note-37)

Salt is currently [mass-produced](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass_production "Mass production") by [evaporation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evaporation "Evaporation") of [seawater](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seawater "Seawater") or [brine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brine "Brine") from [brine wells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brine_well "Brine well") and [salt lakes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salt_lake_(geography) "Salt lake (geography)"). [Mining](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salt_mine "Salt mine") of rock salt is also a major source. China is the world's main supplier of salt.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride#cite_note-usgs-10) In 2017, world production was estimated at 280 million [tonnes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tonne "Tonne"), the top five producers (in million tonnes) being China (68.0), United States (43.0), India (26.0), Germany (13.0), and Canada (13.0).[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride#cite_note-38) Salt is also a byproduct of [potassium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium "Potassium") mining.
