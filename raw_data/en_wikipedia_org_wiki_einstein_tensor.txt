In [differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry"), the **Einstein tensor** (named after [Albert Einstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Einstein "Albert Einstein"); also known as the **trace-reversed [Ricci tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_curvature "Ricci curvature")**) is used to express the [curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvature "Curvature") of a [pseudo-Riemannian manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudo-Riemannian_manifold "Pseudo-Riemannian manifold"). In [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity"), it occurs in the [Einstein field equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_field_equations "Einstein field equations") for [gravitation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitation "Gravitation") that describe [spacetime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spacetime "Spacetime") curvature in a manner that is consistent with conservation of energy and momentum.

The Einstein tensor ![Image 1: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {G}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5ef237517a5b4d24e57a1e81481f18ec67256b99) is a [tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor "Tensor") of order 2 defined over [pseudo-Riemannian manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudo-Riemannian_manifold "Pseudo-Riemannian manifold"). In index-free notation it is defined as ![Image 2: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {G}}={\boldsymbol {R}}-{\frac {1}{2}}{\boldsymbol {g}}R,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/363e600a623d32270a107054efac9086fe6e403b) where ![Image 3: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {R}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7945da372695d49cd4229e2a84ac6dc8ae6c99b8) is the [Ricci tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_tensor "Ricci tensor"), ![Image 4: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {g}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1bf5f1a2eb4941f1829b2da426988cb7ff9e328b) is the [metric tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_tensor_(general_relativity) "Metric tensor (general relativity)") and ![Image 5: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) is the [scalar curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_curvature "Scalar curvature"), which is computed as the [trace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trace_(linear_algebra) "Trace (linear algebra)") of the Ricci tensor ![Image 6: {\displaystyle R_{\mu \nu }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a5ee22d1a052bee0115efb8b5ffdaf10b04e42aa) by ⁠![Image 7: {\displaystyle R=g^{\mu \nu }R_{\mu \nu }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5f33af69081f0596df0623dbe7fe6ab2f0aa21b8)⁠. In component form, the previous equation reads as ![Image 8: {\displaystyle G_{\mu \nu }=R_{\mu \nu }-{1 \over 2}g_{\mu \nu }R.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/da62337e1ee6f4c3fc7c7e6eff5160b67b238a24)

The Einstein tensor is symmetric ![Image 9: {\displaystyle G_{\mu \nu }=G_{\nu \mu }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/664ad8b35a5ae328cb9ac825723cc77af2c14643) and, like the [on-shell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/On_shell_and_off_shell "On shell and off shell")[stress–energy tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stress%E2%80%93energy_tensor "Stress–energy tensor"), has zero [divergence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divergence "Divergence"): ![Image 10: {\displaystyle \nabla _{\mu }G^{\mu \nu }=0\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/afbeff43b4ae8f4b7677179e8dfbdcfffdc4af0c)

The Ricci tensor depends only on the metric tensor, so the Einstein tensor can be defined directly with just the metric tensor. However, this expression is complex and rarely quoted in textbooks. In the special case of a locally [inertial reference frame](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inertial_reference_frame "Inertial reference frame") near a point, the first derivatives of the metric tensor vanish and the component form of the Einstein tensor is considerably simplified: ![Image 19: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}G_{\alpha \beta }&=g^{\gamma \mu }\left[g_{\gamma [\beta ,\mu ]\alpha }+g_{\alpha [\mu ,\beta ]\gamma }-{\frac {1}{2}}g_{\alpha \beta }g^{\epsilon \sigma }\left(g_{\epsilon [\mu ,\sigma ]\gamma }+g_{\gamma [\sigma ,\mu ]\epsilon }\right)\right]\\&=g^{\gamma \mu }\left(\delta _{\alpha }^{\epsilon }\delta _{\beta }^{\sigma }-{\frac {1}{2}}g^{\epsilon \sigma }g_{\alpha \beta }\right)\left(g_{\epsilon [\mu ,\sigma ]\gamma }+g_{\gamma [\sigma ,\mu ]\epsilon }\right),\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/481e46b5e96696a2a46cbb8b7ff8205792abb41b) where square brackets conventionally denote [antisymmetrization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antisymmetric_tensor "Antisymmetric tensor") over bracketed indices, i.e. ![Image 20: {\displaystyle g_{\alpha [\beta ,\gamma ]\epsilon }\,={\frac {1}{2}}\left(g_{\alpha \beta ,\gamma \epsilon }-g_{\alpha \gamma ,\beta \epsilon }\right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f590e8f71826ac9be809363879c4f489cd13f4f4)

The [trace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trace_(linear_algebra) "Trace (linear algebra)") of the Einstein tensor can be computed by [contracting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_contraction "Tensor contraction") the equation in the [definition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_tensor#Definition) with the [metric tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_tensor "Metric tensor")⁠![Image 21: {\displaystyle g^{\mu \nu }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/55a5070ab85fb1f8797a669a98003bcfcc62182d)⁠. In ![Image 22: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) dimensions (of arbitrary signature): ![Image 23: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}g^{\mu \nu }G_{\mu \nu }&=g^{\mu \nu }R_{\mu \nu }-{1 \over 2}g^{\mu \nu }g_{\mu \nu }R\\G&=R-{1 \over 2}(nR)={{2-n} \over 2}R\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/55fe0c99b07747e3287038cb9b0cd486cfe4cfda)

Therefore, in the special case of ⁠![Image 24: {\displaystyle n=4}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d928ec15aeef83aade867992ee473933adb6139d)⁠ dimensions, ⁠![Image 25: {\displaystyle G=-R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c715067551fb38c76d902b43cb7d752f7138440f)⁠. That is, the trace of the Einstein tensor is the negative of the [Ricci tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_tensor "Ricci tensor")'s trace. Thus, another name for the Einstein tensor is the _trace-reversed Ricci tensor_. This ![Image 26: {\displaystyle n=4}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d928ec15aeef83aade867992ee473933adb6139d) case is especially relevant in the [theory of general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theory_of_general_relativity "Theory of general relativity").

Use in general relativity
-------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Einstein_tensor&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Use in general relativity")]

The Einstein tensor allows the [Einstein field equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_field_equations "Einstein field equations") to be written in the concise form: ![Image 27: {\displaystyle G_{\mu \nu }+\Lambda g_{\mu \nu }=\kappa T_{\mu \nu },}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af02c490b2f4c2a53af6cb41e344ec514a7b1f5f) where ![Image 28: {\displaystyle \Lambda }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0ac0a4a98a414e3480335f9ba652d12571ec6733) is the [cosmological constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmological_constant "Cosmological constant") and ![Image 29: {\displaystyle \kappa }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54ddec2e922c5caea4e47d04feef86e782dc8e6d) is the [Einstein gravitational constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_gravitational_constant "Einstein gravitational constant").

From the [explicit form of the Einstein tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_tensor#Explicit_form), the Einstein tensor is a [nonlinear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nonlinear "Nonlinear") function of the metric tensor, but is linear in the second [partial derivatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_derivative "Partial derivative") of the metric. As a symmetric order-2 tensor, the Einstein tensor has 10 independent components in a 4-dimensional space. It follows that the Einstein field equations are a set of 10 [quasilinear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Types_of_differential_equations "Types of differential equations") second-order partial differential equations for the metric tensor.

The [contracted Bianchi identities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contracted_Bianchi_identities "Contracted Bianchi identities") can also be easily expressed with the aid of the Einstein tensor: ![Image 30: {\displaystyle \nabla _{\mu }G^{\mu \nu }=0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a030cd71a1fda487d4f3391145541ece0c04fb3f)

The (contracted) Bianchi identities automatically ensure the covariant conservation of the [stress–energy tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stress%E2%80%93energy_tensor "Stress–energy tensor") in curved spacetimes: ![Image 31: {\displaystyle \nabla _{\mu }T^{\mu \nu }=0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1b323c7848fe786e02b64d9ee2ebb4657a448122)

The physical significance of the Einstein tensor is highlighted by this identity. In terms of the densitized stress tensor contracted on a [Killing vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Killing_vector "Killing vector")⁠![Image 32: {\displaystyle \xi ^{\mu }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7af230baed6ad669a739e8a90b1ca699fd999c89)⁠, an ordinary conservation law holds: ![Image 33: {\displaystyle \partial _{\mu }\left({\sqrt {-g}}\ T^{\mu }{}_{\nu }\xi ^{\nu }\right)=0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38a08a97c5092acb54f79ad1187cde757a917eea)

[David Lovelock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Lovelock "David Lovelock") has shown that, in a four-dimensional [differentiable manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differentiable_manifold "Differentiable manifold"), the Einstein tensor is the only [tensorial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor "Tensor") and [divergence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divergence "Divergence")-free function of the ![Image 34: {\displaystyle g_{\mu \nu }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e5bf4140993a891f5782167dc8a0c236dc7667b8) and at most their first and second partial derivatives.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_tensor#cite_note-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_tensor#cite_note-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_tensor#cite_note-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_tensor#cite_note-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_tensor#cite_note-5)

However, the [Einstein field equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_field_equation "Einstein field equation") is not the only equation which satisfies the three conditions:[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_tensor#cite_note-6)

1.   Resemble but generalize [Newton–Poisson gravitational equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauss%27s_law_for_gravity "Gauss's law for gravity")
2.   Apply to all coordinate systems, and
3.   Guarantee local covariant conservation of energy–momentum for any metric tensor.

Many alternative theories have been proposed, such as the [Einstein–Cartan theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein%E2%80%93Cartan_theory "Einstein–Cartan theory"), that also satisfy the above conditions.
