Elliptic coordinate system

In [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry"), the **elliptic coordinate system** is a two-dimensional [orthogonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_coordinates "Orthogonal coordinates")[coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_system "Coordinate system") in which the [coordinate lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_system#Coordinate_line "Coordinate system") are [confocal ellipses and hyperbolae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confocal_conic_sections "Confocal conic sections"). The two [foci](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Focus_(geometry) "Focus (geometry)")![Image 2: {\displaystyle F_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/100c7fbf174fe8b06eacc2a6b0bb2e1badd1c7ce) and ![Image 3: {\displaystyle F_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0fd17e0779153d765b40ebef91533489b87b2e37) are generally taken to be fixed at ![Image 4: {\displaystyle -a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e0982b5868a66be1ed3ad7ef4bcd3d3db20f982) and ![Image 5: {\displaystyle +a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/23558d20acf58179a3e9a08ea7c80ba017095873), respectively, on the ![Image 6: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4)-axis of the [Cartesian coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system "Cartesian coordinate system").

The most common definition of elliptic coordinates ![Image 7: {\displaystyle (\mu ,\nu )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ea1a07b0389d3f118fb3e2e81473a105c9c70705) is

![Image 8: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}x&=a\ \cosh \mu \ \cos \nu \\y&=a\ \sinh \mu \ \sin \nu \end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/438582baebf72344f40d977a47242db3d4a37a7e)
where ![Image 9: {\displaystyle \mu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9fd47b2a39f7a7856952afec1f1db72c67af6161) is a nonnegative real number and ![Image 10: {\displaystyle \nu \in [0,2\pi ].}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ad13894fea18f0bc355ad1eca69f2378bef70659)

On the [complex plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_plane "Complex plane"), an equivalent relationship is

![Image 11: {\displaystyle x+iy=a\ \cosh(\mu +i\nu )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6ae7eb5a03e0f018ca506374e13a7c560b3ce92a)
These definitions correspond to ellipses and hyperbolae. The trigonometric identity

![Image 12: {\displaystyle {\frac {x^{2}}{a^{2}\cosh ^{2}\mu }}+{\frac {y^{2}}{a^{2}\sinh ^{2}\mu }}=\cos ^{2}\nu +\sin ^{2}\nu =1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9bcf4627ac63b279cebcefa7be483163f6b3eee6)
shows that curves of constant ![Image 13: {\displaystyle \mu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9fd47b2a39f7a7856952afec1f1db72c67af6161) form [ellipses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse "Ellipse"), whereas the hyperbolic trigonometric identity

![Image 14: {\displaystyle {\frac {x^{2}}{a^{2}\cos ^{2}\nu }}-{\frac {y^{2}}{a^{2}\sin ^{2}\nu }}=\cosh ^{2}\mu -\sinh ^{2}\mu =1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/52234cbc08b6a007fef69057d7979c3deec90ef9)
shows that curves of constant ![Image 15: {\displaystyle \nu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c15bbbb971240cf328aba572178f091684585468) form [hyperbolae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbola "Hyperbola").

In an [orthogonal coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_coordinate_system "Orthogonal coordinate system") the lengths of the basis vectors are known as scale factors. The scale factors for the elliptic coordinates ![Image 16: {\displaystyle (\mu ,\nu )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ea1a07b0389d3f118fb3e2e81473a105c9c70705) are equal to

![Image 17: {\displaystyle h_{\mu }=h_{\nu }=a{\sqrt {\sinh ^{2}\mu +\sin ^{2}\nu }}=a{\sqrt {\cosh ^{2}\mu -\cos ^{2}\nu }}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2a358dd24a552586051e16f2e896f1cf3d0b1054)
Using the _double argument identities_ for [hyperbolic functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_functions#Identities "Hyperbolic functions") and [trigonometric functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trigonometric_function#Identities "Trigonometric function"), the scale factors can be equivalently expressed as

![Image 18: {\displaystyle h_{\mu }=h_{\nu }=a{\sqrt {{\frac {1}{2}}(\cosh 2\mu -\cos 2\nu )}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3c1beecfc5ebe3dabeea39a496d6a28d18db968d)
Consequently, an infinitesimal element of area equals

![Image 19: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}dA&=h_{\mu }h_{\nu }d\mu d\nu \\&=a^{2}\left(\sinh ^{2}\mu +\sin ^{2}\nu \right)d\mu d\nu \\&=a^{2}\left(\cosh ^{2}\mu -\cos ^{2}\nu \right)d\mu d\nu \\&={\frac {a^{2}}{2}}\left(\cosh 2\mu -\cos 2\nu \right)d\mu d\nu \end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f3f2304e89bc3b08b7323a492678dd3e13e809f3)
and the Laplacian reads

![Image 20: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\nabla ^{2}\Phi &={\frac {1}{a^{2}\left(\sinh ^{2}\mu +\sin ^{2}\nu \right)}}\left({\frac {\partial ^{2}\Phi }{\partial \mu ^{2}}}+{\frac {\partial ^{2}\Phi }{\partial \nu ^{2}}}\right)\\&={\frac {1}{a^{2}\left(\cosh ^{2}\mu -\cos ^{2}\nu \right)}}\left({\frac {\partial ^{2}\Phi }{\partial \mu ^{2}}}+{\frac {\partial ^{2}\Phi }{\partial \nu ^{2}}}\right)\\&={\frac {2}{a^{2}\left(\cosh 2\mu -\cos 2\nu \right)}}\left({\frac {\partial ^{2}\Phi }{\partial \mu ^{2}}}+{\frac {\partial ^{2}\Phi }{\partial \nu ^{2}}}\right)\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/26ecf22b97b8f72e1b2abe61d813d0b104d6b9ba)
Other differential operators such as ![Image 21: {\displaystyle \nabla \cdot \mathbf {F} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1f4f5b9e6560d7fdaf1a69d9fdeff6beb1f327cc) and ![Image 22: {\displaystyle \nabla \times \mathbf {F} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c6e2429b7e3cb6965d1a6651703ff5ef0c88b378) can be expressed in the coordinates ![Image 23: {\displaystyle (\mu ,\nu )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ea1a07b0389d3f118fb3e2e81473a105c9c70705) by substituting the scale factors into the general formulae found in [orthogonal coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_coordinates "Orthogonal coordinates").

Alternative definition
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Elliptic_coordinate_system&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Alternative definition")]

An alternative and geometrically intuitive set of elliptic coordinates ![Image 24: {\displaystyle (\sigma ,\tau )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c2d60596d44a9bc5043b60b76c1fac959eb65eb0) are sometimes used, where ![Image 25: {\displaystyle \sigma =\cosh \mu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0503faf9f70eddcdd66fafab8606d712a0f6d19d) and ![Image 26: {\displaystyle \tau =\cos \nu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/29699d5b3ac1b50d52834f425186ebc8923f7a8d). Hence, the curves of constant ![Image 27: {\displaystyle \sigma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/59f59b7c3e6fdb1d0365a494b81fb9a696138c36) are ellipses, whereas the curves of constant ![Image 28: {\displaystyle \tau }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38a7dcde9730ef0853809fefc18d88771f95206c) are hyperbolae. The coordinate ![Image 29: {\displaystyle \tau }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38a7dcde9730ef0853809fefc18d88771f95206c) must belong to the interval [-1, 1], whereas the ![Image 30: {\displaystyle \sigma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/59f59b7c3e6fdb1d0365a494b81fb9a696138c36) coordinate must be greater than or equal to one.

The coordinates ![Image 31: {\displaystyle (\sigma ,\tau )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c2d60596d44a9bc5043b60b76c1fac959eb65eb0) have a simple relation to the distances to the foci ![Image 32: {\displaystyle F_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/100c7fbf174fe8b06eacc2a6b0bb2e1badd1c7ce) and ![Image 33: {\displaystyle F_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0fd17e0779153d765b40ebef91533489b87b2e37). For any point in the plane, the _sum_![Image 34: {\displaystyle d_{1}+d_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/028d9a26c6c05e0531d8476389cb73cc8d5e8520) of its distances to the foci equals ![Image 35: {\displaystyle 2a\sigma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3c33546d2cc2f56c633756909048382e5283eb2c), whereas their _difference_![Image 36: {\displaystyle d_{1}-d_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/de2341ef255cb1d411333857ddd1f0dbf76c43f4) equals ![Image 37: {\displaystyle 2a\tau }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/99604fd548ab96ea6fe35259c07a0631fcf3cad5). Thus, the distance to ![Image 38: {\displaystyle F_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/100c7fbf174fe8b06eacc2a6b0bb2e1badd1c7ce) is ![Image 39: {\displaystyle a(\sigma +\tau )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9723f0deeb63d3cddabdc059f2fee30c96c85818), whereas the distance to ![Image 40: {\displaystyle F_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0fd17e0779153d765b40ebef91533489b87b2e37) is ![Image 41: {\displaystyle a(\sigma -\tau )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7b6a3c2b6f276dca7a3991f109af701f5858c3d1). (Recall that ![Image 42: {\displaystyle F_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/100c7fbf174fe8b06eacc2a6b0bb2e1badd1c7ce) and ![Image 43: {\displaystyle F_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0fd17e0779153d765b40ebef91533489b87b2e37) are located at ![Image 44: {\displaystyle x=-a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9f726a9f4cff586cefe8198e5c628a55d70b5571) and ![Image 45: {\displaystyle x=+a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8814caa6e38d62bcfb2abd0536056ce084338746), respectively.)

A drawback of these coordinates is that the points with [Cartesian coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinates "Cartesian coordinates") (x,y) and (x,-y) have the same coordinates ![Image 46: {\displaystyle (\sigma ,\tau )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c2d60596d44a9bc5043b60b76c1fac959eb65eb0), so the conversion to Cartesian coordinates is not a function, but a [multifunction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multivalued_function "Multivalued function").

![Image 47: {\displaystyle x=a\left.\sigma \right.\tau }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f63cd218d47400dcad60a82012f28580d0962c48)![Image 48: {\displaystyle y^{2}=a^{2}\left(\sigma ^{2}-1\right)\left(1-\tau ^{2}\right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6bf4d6db89d89e13b6eae73d528d4404330e9a69)

### Alternative scale factors

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Elliptic_coordinate_system&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Alternative scale factors")]

The scale factors for the alternative elliptic coordinates ![Image 49: {\displaystyle (\sigma ,\tau )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c2d60596d44a9bc5043b60b76c1fac959eb65eb0) are

![Image 50: {\displaystyle h_{\sigma }=a{\sqrt {\frac {\sigma ^{2}-\tau ^{2}}{\sigma ^{2}-1}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/51390ae73787e24f7da85fb8b3edc6eae21f6607)![Image 51: {\displaystyle h_{\tau }=a{\sqrt {\frac {\sigma ^{2}-\tau ^{2}}{1-\tau ^{2}}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a780133f015a39d5e26224844f2746d5d896f17)
Hence, the infinitesimal area element becomes

![Image 52: {\displaystyle dA=a^{2}{\frac {\sigma ^{2}-\tau ^{2}}{\sqrt {\left(\sigma ^{2}-1\right)\left(1-\tau ^{2}\right)}}}d\sigma d\tau }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dd67f3f609e29c5e8ffac27bfa41eb16197785b8)
and the Laplacian equals

![Image 53: {\displaystyle \nabla ^{2}\Phi ={\frac {1}{a^{2}\left(\sigma ^{2}-\tau ^{2}\right)}}\left[{\sqrt {\sigma ^{2}-1}}{\frac {\partial }{\partial \sigma }}\left({\sqrt {\sigma ^{2}-1}}{\frac {\partial \Phi }{\partial \sigma }}\right)+{\sqrt {1-\tau ^{2}}}{\frac {\partial }{\partial \tau }}\left({\sqrt {1-\tau ^{2}}}{\frac {\partial \Phi }{\partial \tau }}\right)\right].}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/387a412b798ac56009b9b3afe86bff2995fc2ba2)
Other differential operators such as ![Image 54: {\displaystyle \nabla \cdot \mathbf {F} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1f4f5b9e6560d7fdaf1a69d9fdeff6beb1f327cc) and ![Image 55: {\displaystyle \nabla \times \mathbf {F} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c6e2429b7e3cb6965d1a6651703ff5ef0c88b378) can be expressed in the coordinates ![Image 56: {\displaystyle (\sigma ,\tau )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c2d60596d44a9bc5043b60b76c1fac959eb65eb0) by substituting the scale factors into the general formulae found in [orthogonal coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_coordinates "Orthogonal coordinates").

Elliptic coordinates form the basis for several sets of three-dimensional [orthogonal coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_coordinates "Orthogonal coordinates"):

1.   The [elliptic cylindrical coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptic_cylindrical_coordinates "Elliptic cylindrical coordinates") are produced by projecting in the ![Image 57: {\displaystyle z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf368e72c009decd9b6686ee84a375632e11de98)-direction.
2.   The [prolate spheroidal coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prolate_spheroidal_coordinates "Prolate spheroidal coordinates") are produced by rotating the elliptic coordinates about the ![Image 58: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4)-axis, i.e., the axis connecting the foci, whereas the [oblate spheroidal coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oblate_spheroidal_coordinates "Oblate spheroidal coordinates") are produced by rotating the elliptic coordinates about the ![Image 59: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d)-axis, i.e., the axis separating the foci.
3.   [Ellipsoidal coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipsoidal_coordinates "Ellipsoidal coordinates") are a formal extension of elliptic coordinates into 3-dimensions, which is based on confocal ellipsoids, hyperboloids of one and two sheets.

Note that (ellipsoidal) [Geographic coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geographic_coordinate_system "Geographic coordinate system") is a different concept from above.

The classic applications of elliptic coordinates are in solving [partial differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_differential_equations "Partial differential equations"), e.g., [Laplace's equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laplace%27s_equation "Laplace's equation") or the [Helmholtz equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helmholtz_equation "Helmholtz equation"), for which elliptic coordinates are a natural description of a system thus allowing a [separation of variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separation_of_variables "Separation of variables") in the [partial differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_differential_equations "Partial differential equations"). Some traditional examples are solving systems such as electrons orbiting a molecule or planetary orbits that have an elliptical shape.

The geometric properties of elliptic coordinates can also be useful. A typical example might involve an integration over all pairs of vectors ![Image 60: {\displaystyle \mathbf {p} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dd73e3862cb92b016721b8c492eadb4e8a577527) and ![Image 61: {\displaystyle \mathbf {q} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7be005a326b7ac3fe4c24bca391369f44c4c2876) that sum to a fixed vector ![Image 62: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} =\mathbf {p} +\mathbf {q} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4fe5d685b902c704282408b4c203201842b75b2f), where the integrand was a function of the vector lengths ![Image 63: {\displaystyle \left|\mathbf {p} \right|}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/659d13b0d421479de930d4ca9e548718c0c9b6d3) and ![Image 64: {\displaystyle \left|\mathbf {q} \right|}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/83cf8ffd28d9c1932f17563af351a74b75d57c27). (In such a case, one would position ![Image 65: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eca0f46511c4c986c48b254073732c0bd98ae0c1) between the two foci and aligned with the ![Image 66: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4)-axis, i.e., ![Image 67: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} =2a\mathbf {\hat {x}} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2fbcd16f0c2b2f37867792153631c7e9b642de92).) For concreteness, ![Image 68: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eca0f46511c4c986c48b254073732c0bd98ae0c1), ![Image 69: {\displaystyle \mathbf {p} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dd73e3862cb92b016721b8c492eadb4e8a577527) and ![Image 70: {\displaystyle \mathbf {q} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7be005a326b7ac3fe4c24bca391369f44c4c2876) could represent the [momenta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum "Momentum") of a particle and its decomposition products, respectively, and the integrand might involve the kinetic energies of the products (which are proportional to the squared lengths of the momenta).
