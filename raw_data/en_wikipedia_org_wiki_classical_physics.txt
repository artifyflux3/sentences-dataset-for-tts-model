Category of theories

[![Image 5](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Modernphysicsfields_2.svg/500px-Modernphysicsfields_2.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Modernphysicsfields_2.svg)

Impact of relativity and quantum mechanics on classical mechanics

**Classical physics** refers to [scientific theories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_theory "Scientific theory") in the field of [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics") that are non-quantum or both non-quantum and non-relativistic, depending on the context. In historical discussions, _classical physics_ refers to pre-1900 physics, while _[modern physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modern\_physics "Modern physics")_ refers to post-1900 physics, which incorporates elements of [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics") and the [theory of relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theory_of_relativity "Theory of relativity").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_physics#cite_note-1) However, relativity is based on [classical field theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_field_theory "Classical field theory") rather than [quantum field theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_field_theory "Quantum field theory"), and is often categorized as a part of "classical physics".[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

_Classical theory_ has at least two distinct meanings in physics. It can include all those areas of physics that do not make use of [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics"), which includes [classical mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_mechanics "Classical mechanics") (using any of the [Newtonian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_laws_of_motion "Newton's laws of motion"), [Lagrangian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrangian_mechanics "Lagrangian mechanics"), or [Hamiltonian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamiltonian_mechanics "Hamiltonian mechanics") formulations), as well as [classical electrodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_electrodynamics "Classical electrodynamics") and [relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theory_of_relativity "Theory of relativity").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_physics#cite_note-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_physics#cite_note-3) Alternatively, the term can refer to theories that are neither quantum or relativistic.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_physics#cite_note-4)

Depending on point of view, among the branches of theory sometimes included in classical physics are:[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_physics#cite_note-Krane-2019-5): 2

*   [Classical mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_mechanics "Classical mechanics")
    *   [Newton's laws of motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_laws_of_motion "Newton's laws of motion")
    *   Classical [Lagrangian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrangian_mechanics "Lagrangian mechanics") and [Hamiltonian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamiltonian_mechanics "Hamiltonian mechanics") formalisms

*   [Classical electrodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_electrodynamics "Classical electrodynamics") ([Maxwell's equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxwell%27s_equations "Maxwell's equations"))
*   Classical [thermodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamics "Thermodynamics")

Comparison with modern physics
------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Classical_physics&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Comparison with modern physics")]

In contrast to classical physics, "[modern physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modern_physics "Modern physics")" is usually used to focus on those revolutionary changes created by [quantum physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_physics "Quantum physics") and the [theory of relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theory_of_relativity "Theory of relativity").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_physics#cite_note-Krane-2019-5): 2

A [physical system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_system "Physical system") can be described by classical physics when it satisfies conditions such that the laws of classical physics are approximately valid.

In practice, physical objects ranging from those larger than [atoms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atom "Atom") and [molecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecule "Molecule") to macroscopic and [astronomical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy "Astronomy") objects, can be well-described (understood) with classical mechanics. Beginning at the atomic level and lower, the laws of classical physics break down and generally do not provide a correct description of nature. Electromagnetic fields and forces can be described well by classical electrodynamics at length scales and field strengths large enough that quantum mechanical effects are negligible. Unlike quantum physics, classical physics is generally characterized by the principle of complete [determinism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_determinism "Scientific determinism"), although deterministic interpretations of quantum mechanics do exist.

From the point of view of classical physics as being non-relativistic physics, the predictions of general and special relativity are significantly different from those of classical theories, particularly concerning the passage of time, the geometry of space, the motion of bodies in free fall, and the propagation of light. Historically, light was reconciled with classical mechanics by assuming the existence of a stationary medium through which light propagated, the [luminiferous aether](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luminiferous_aether "Luminiferous aether"), which was later shown not to exist.

Comparison to quantum physics
-----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Classical_physics&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Comparison to quantum physics")]

Mathematically, quantum physics equations are those containing the [Planck constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant "Planck constant").[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] According to the [correspondence principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Correspondence_principle "Correspondence principle") and [Ehrenfest's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ehrenfest%27s_theorem "Ehrenfest's theorem"), as a system becomes larger or more massive the classical dynamics tends to emerge, with some exceptions, such as [superfluidity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superfluidity "Superfluidity"). This is why we can usually ignore quantum mechanics when dealing with everyday objects and the classical description will suffice. [Decoherence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence "Decoherence") is the field of research concerned with the discovery of how the laws of quantum physics give rise to classical physics.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]
