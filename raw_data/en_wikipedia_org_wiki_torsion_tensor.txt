Development of the unit circle in the Euclidean space ![Image 2: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5), with four different choices of flat connection preserving the Euclidean metric, defined by ![Image 3: {\displaystyle \nabla _{e_{i}}e_{j}=\tau \,e_{i}\times e_{j}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/799ea5dd4c28f1c9b1340812da3d3a3d181b9140), where ![Image 4: {\displaystyle \tau }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38a7dcde9730ef0853809fefc18d88771f95206c) is a constant scalar, respectively: ![Image 5: {\displaystyle \tau =0.01,0.1,0.5,1.0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1c8749dabf64128a5f1f337551f136ad95ff9d13). The resulting curves all have arc length ![Image 6: {\displaystyle 2\pi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/73efd1f6493490b058097060a572606d2c550a06), curvature ![Image 7: {\displaystyle 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/92d98b82a3778f043108d4e20960a9193df57cbf), and respective torsion ![Image 8: {\displaystyle \tau }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38a7dcde9730ef0853809fefc18d88771f95206c) (in the sense of [Frenet-Serret](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frenet-Serret "Frenet-Serret")).

In [differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry"), the **torsion tensor** is a [tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor "Tensor") that is associated to any [affine connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_connection "Affine connection"). The torsion tensor is a [bilinear map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bilinear_map "Bilinear map") of two input vectors ![Image 9: {\displaystyle X,Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8705438171d938b7f59cd1bfa5b7d99b6afa5cd), that produces an output vector ![Image 10: {\displaystyle T(X,Y)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0dc462da21ec8e84bc586f67adf0ce303c85ae1c) representing the displacement within a tangent space when the tangent space is developed (or "rolled") along an infinitesimal parallelogram whose sides are ![Image 11: {\displaystyle X,Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8705438171d938b7f59cd1bfa5b7d99b6afa5cd). It is [skew symmetric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skew_symmetric "Skew symmetric") in its inputs, because developing over the parallelogram in the opposite sense produces the opposite displacement, similarly to how a [screw](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Screw "Screw") moves in opposite ways when it is twisted in two directions.

Torsion is particularly useful in the study of the geometry of [geodesics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic "Geodesic"). Given a system of parametrized geodesics, one can specify a class of affine connections having those geodesics, but differing by their torsions. There is a unique connection which _absorbs the torsion_, generalizing the [Levi-Civita connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_connection "Levi-Civita connection") to other, possibly non-metric situations (such as [Finsler geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finsler_geometry "Finsler geometry")). The difference between a connection with torsion, and a corresponding connection without torsion is a tensor, called the [contorsion tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contorsion_tensor "Contorsion tensor"). Absorption of torsion also plays a fundamental role in the study of [G-structures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G-structure "G-structure") and [Cartan's equivalence method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartan%27s_equivalence_method "Cartan's equivalence method"). Torsion is also useful in the study of unparametrized families of geodesics, via the associated [projective connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_connection "Projective connection"). In [relativity theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativity_theory "Relativity theory"), such ideas have been implemented in the form of [Einstein–Cartan theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein%E2%80%93Cartan_theory "Einstein–Cartan theory").

Let _M_ be a manifold with an [affine connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_connection "Affine connection") on the [tangent bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_bundle "Tangent bundle") (aka [covariant derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covariant_derivative "Covariant derivative")) ∇. The **torsion tensor** (sometimes called the _Cartan_ (_torsion_) _tensor_) of ∇ is the [vector-valued 2-form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector-valued_form "Vector-valued form") defined on [vector fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_field "Vector field")_X_ and _Y_ by[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torsion_tensor#cite_note-1)

![Image 12: {\displaystyle T(X,Y):=\nabla _{X}Y-\nabla _{Y}X-[X,Y]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/55760fe285c2f2c64f86ec729c788172c93bb0b0)
where [_X_, _Y_] is the [Lie bracket](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_bracket_of_vector_fields "Lie bracket of vector fields") of two vector fields. By the [Leibniz rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leibniz_rule_(generalized_product_rule) "Leibniz rule (generalized product rule)"), _T_(_fX_, _Y_) = _T_(_X_, _fY_) = _fT_(_X_, _Y_) for any [smooth function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smooth_function "Smooth function")_f_. So _T_ is [tensorial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensorial "Tensorial"), despite being defined in terms of the [connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connection_(vector_bundle) "Connection (vector bundle)") which is a first order differential operator: it gives a 2-form on tangent vectors, while the covariant derivative is only defined for vector fields.

### Components of the torsion tensor

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Torsion_tensor&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Components of the torsion tensor")]

The components of the torsion tensor ![Image 13: {\displaystyle T^{c}{}_{ab}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5163249363be8e4d177241255fc62642bcf126e9) in terms of a local [basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basis_of_a_vector_space "Basis of a vector space")(**e**1, ..., **e**_n_) of [sections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Section_(fiber_bundle) "Section (fiber bundle)") of the tangent bundle can be derived by setting _X_ = **e**_i_, _Y_ = **e**_j_ and by introducing the commutator coefficients _γ k ij_**e**_k_:= [**e**_i_, **e**_j_]. The components of the torsion are then[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torsion_tensor#cite_note-2)

![Image 14: {\displaystyle T^{k}{}_{ij}:=\Gamma ^{k}{}_{ij}-\Gamma ^{k}{}_{ji}-\gamma ^{k}{}_{ij},\quad i,j,k=1,2,\ldots ,n.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/84ca3fffa0eb44190f5e92e1654745fa398fcaa8)
Here ![Image 15: {\displaystyle {\Gamma ^{k}}_{ij}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0cfbad24c9375f3eae294ed58691700b2161a08e) are the [connection coefficients](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connection_coefficient "Connection coefficient") defining the connection. If the basis is [holonomic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holonomic_basis "Holonomic basis") then the Lie brackets vanish, ![Image 16: {\displaystyle \gamma ^{k}{}_{ij}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/141feeae97f555027c8319bcae789deda9eaf57c). So ![Image 17: {\displaystyle T^{k}{}_{ij}=2\Gamma ^{k}{}_{[ij]}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ab8247e0713e7e599e045a0a5c174d8dc6bd4e81). In particular (see below), while the [geodesic equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic "Geodesic") determine the symmetric part of the connection, the torsion tensor determines the antisymmetric part.

The **torsion form**, an alternative characterization of torsion, applies to the [frame bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frame_bundle "Frame bundle") F _M_ of the manifold _M_. This [principal bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principal_bundle "Principal bundle") is equipped with a [connection form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connection_(principal_bundle) "Connection (principal bundle)")_ω_, a **gl**(_n_)-valued one-form which maps vertical vectors to the generators of the right action in **gl**(_n_) and equivariantly intertwines the right action of GL(_n_) on the tangent bundle of F _M_ with the [adjoint representation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adjoint_representation_of_a_Lie_group "Adjoint representation of a Lie group") on **gl**(_n_). The frame bundle also carries a [canonical one-form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solder_form "Solder form") θ, with values in **R**_n_, defined at a frame _u_ ∈ F x _M_ (regarded as a linear function _u_: **R**_n_ → T x _M_) by[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torsion_tensor#cite_note-3)

![Image 18: {\displaystyle \theta (X)=u^{-1}(\pi _{*}(X))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/159194cb15cf1f4e63649797816054ac85e204e8)
where _π_: F _M_ → _M_ is the projection mapping for the principal bundle and _π∗_ is its push-forward. The torsion form is then[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torsion_tensor#cite_note-4)

![Image 19: {\displaystyle \Theta =d\theta +\omega \wedge \theta .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/26f8f49bc09182a00c5d2d1b715659c395cf1bed)
Equivalently, Θ = _Dθ_, where _D_ is the [exterior covariant derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_covariant_derivative "Exterior covariant derivative") determined by the connection.

The torsion form is a (horizontal) [tensorial form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensorial_form "Tensorial form") with values in **R**_n_, meaning that under the right action of _g_ ∈ GL(_n_) it transforms [equivariantly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equivariant "Equivariant"):

![Image 20: {\displaystyle R_{g}^{*}\Theta =g^{-1}\cdot \Theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c4a839604e4b6938bcc20a7cf653642250bf6393)
where ![Image 21: {\displaystyle g^{-1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8ae77eeb0200a3b0e26ff4b251fb845ca1b385c3) acts on the right-hand side by its canonical action on **R**_n_.

#### Torsion form in a frame

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Torsion_tensor&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Torsion form in a frame")]

The torsion form may be expressed in terms of a [connection form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connection_form "Connection form") on the base manifold _M_, written in a particular frame of the tangent bundle (**e**1, ..., **e**_n_). The connection form expresses the exterior covariant derivative of these basic sections:[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torsion_tensor#cite_note-5)

![Image 22: {\displaystyle D\mathbf {e} _{i}=\mathbf {e} _{j}{\omega ^{j}}_{i}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9e180989a1ec5082598344def9ab2c0abe1ce115)
The [solder form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solder_form "Solder form") for the tangent bundle (relative to this frame) is the [dual basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_basis "Dual basis")_θ i_ ∈ T∗_M_ of the **e**_i_, so that _θ i_(**e**j) = _δ i j_ (the [Kronecker delta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kronecker_delta "Kronecker delta")). Then the torsion 2-form has components

![Image 23: {\displaystyle \Theta ^{k}=d\theta ^{k}+{\omega ^{k}}_{j}\wedge \theta ^{j}={T^{k}}_{ij}\theta ^{i}\wedge \theta ^{j}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c1e51c30317158e2e4ec01adb8c4a0acfe45bde8)
In the rightmost expression,

![Image 24: {\displaystyle {T^{k}}_{ij}=\theta ^{k}\left(\nabla _{\mathbf {e} _{i}}\mathbf {e} _{j}-\nabla _{\mathbf {e} _{j}}\mathbf {e} _{i}-\left[\mathbf {e} _{i},\mathbf {e} _{j}\right]\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/003a8e60b5f698367219e5da83250e4a51b53c16)

It can be easily shown that Θ _i_ transforms tensorially in the sense that if a different frame

![Image 25: {\displaystyle {\tilde {\mathbf {e} }}_{i}=\mathbf {e} _{j}{g^{j}}_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5886b2ab5c9eef15afef24f1783675d395de7f23)

![Image 26: {\displaystyle {\tilde {\Theta }}^{i}={\left(g^{-1}\right)^{i}}_{j}\Theta ^{j}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fd420851f55a45b933dd6e38c62d0fcf46a0aad5)

Alternatively, the solder form can be characterized in a frame-independent fashion as the T _M_-valued one-form _θ_ on _M_ corresponding to the identity endomorphism of the tangent bundle under the duality isomorphism End(T _M_) ≈ T _M_ ⊗ T∗_M_. Then the torsion 2-form is a section

Curvature and the Bianchi identities
------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Torsion_tensor&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Curvature and the Bianchi identities")]

The [curvature tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_curvature_tensor "Riemann curvature tensor") of ∇ is a mapping T _M_ × T _M_ → End(T _M_) defined on vector fields _X_, _Y_, and _Z_ by

![Image 35: {\displaystyle R(X,Y)Z=\nabla _{X}\nabla _{Y}Z-\nabla _{Y}\nabla _{X}Z-\nabla _{[X,Y]}Z.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9b4c06786c30c0e5937bbce502e4f812c73026b8)

Characterizations and interpretations
-------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Torsion_tensor&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Characterizations and interpretations")]

The torsion is a manner of characterizing the amount of slipping or twisting that a plane does when rolling along a surface or higher dimensional [affine manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_manifold "Affine manifold").[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torsion_tensor#cite_note-9)

For example, consider rolling a plane along a small circle drawn on a sphere. If the plane does not slip or twist, then when the plane is rolled all the way along the circle, it will also trace a circle in the plane. It turns out that the plane will have rotated (despite there being no twist whilst rolling it), an effect due to the [curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvature "Curvature") of the sphere. But the curve traced out will still be a circle, and so in particular a closed curve that begins and ends at the same point. On the other hand, if the plane were rolled along the sphere, but it was allowed it to slip or twist in the process, then the path the circle traces on the plane could be a much more general curve that need not even be closed. The torsion is a way to quantify this additional slipping and twisting while rolling a plane along a curve.

Thus the torsion tensor can be intuitively understood by taking a small parallelogram circuit with sides given by vectors _v_ and _w_, in a space and rolling the [tangent space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_space "Tangent space") along each of the four sides of the parallelogram, marking the point of contact as it goes. When the circuit is completed, the marked curve will have been displaced out of the plane of the parallelogram by a vector, denoted ![Image 44: {\displaystyle T(v,w)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6978758887b0d8e5a6bd76901954cd8b12b87a51). Thus the torsion tensor is a tensor: a (bilinear) function of two input vectors _v_ and _w_ that produces an output vector ![Image 45: {\displaystyle T(v,w)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6978758887b0d8e5a6bd76901954cd8b12b87a51). It is [skew symmetric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skew_symmetric "Skew symmetric") in the arguments _v_ and _w_, a reflection of the fact that traversing the circuit in the opposite sense undoes the original displacement, in much the same way that twisting a [screw](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Screw "Screw") in opposite directions displaces the screw in opposite ways. The torsion tensor thus is related to, although distinct from, the [torsion of a curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torsion_of_curves "Torsion of curves"), as it appears in the [Frenet–Serret formulas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frenet%E2%80%93Serret_formulas "Frenet–Serret formulas"): the torsion of a connection measures a dislocation of a developed curve out of its plane, while the torsion of a curve is also a dislocation out of its [osculating plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osculating_plane "Osculating plane"). In the geometry of surfaces, the _geodesic torsion_ describes how a surface twists about a curve on the surface. The companion notion of [curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvature "Curvature") measures how moving frames roll along a curve without slipping or twisting.

Consider the (flat) [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space")![Image 46: {\displaystyle M=\mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7f43abaf9eea56410f58a7ab6cbeb882b82bc376). On it, we put a connection that is flat, but with non-zero torsion, defined on the standard Euclidean frame ![Image 47: {\displaystyle e_{1},e_{2},e_{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e35821963b9761d80f5b4079f1ba179ac101da80) by the (Euclidean) [cross product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product "Cross product"): ![Image 48: {\displaystyle \nabla _{e_{i}}e_{j}=e_{i}\times e_{j}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e6ef755fa23bddcd2d67917e2dc6a565b8cfe403) Consider now the parallel transport of the vector ![Image 49: {\displaystyle e_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4045b5c7cee9bd0681153bbb077489b13269355e) along the ![Image 50: {\displaystyle e_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e81caf3d4bcb929315801cbabc83543829484ee) axis, starting at the origin. The parallel vector field ![Image 51: {\displaystyle X(x)=a(x)e_{2}+b(x)e_{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b3b7c9200b2bac816adfe0926f241b7100a0b015) thus satisfies ![Image 52: {\displaystyle X(0)=e_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cada6dd81cc71b78bd10b184fdd152fa786842ae), and the differential equation ![Image 53: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}0={\dot {X}}&=\nabla _{e_{1}}X={\dot {a}}e_{2}+{\dot {b}}e_{3}+ae_{1}\times e_{2}+be_{1}\times e_{3}\\&=({\dot {a}}-b)e_{2}+({\dot {b}}+a)e_{3}.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/12664b725dd6c55d1c3d6e138ccd2d906c94ea57) Thus ![Image 54: {\displaystyle {\dot {a}}=b,{\dot {b}}=-a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0054338ec9d2a67951524066e1373094f16bae4e), and the solution is ![Image 55: {\displaystyle X=\cos x\,e_{2}-\sin x\,e_{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8e86fb3ab8c122bffe64f25fd859b732aa726d99).

Now the tip of the vector ![Image 56: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab), as it is transported along the ![Image 57: {\displaystyle e_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e81caf3d4bcb929315801cbabc83543829484ee) axis traces out the helix ![Image 58: {\displaystyle x\,e_{1}+\cos x\,e_{2}-\sin x\,e_{3}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2c40335f8c430e4bc21c5b611a64e622593ff57e) Thus we see that, in the presence of torsion, parallel transport tends to twist a frame around the direction of motion, analogously to the role played by torsion in the classical [differential geometry of curves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry_of_curves "Differential geometry of curves").

One interpretation of the torsion involves the development of a curve.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torsion_tensor#cite_note-10) Suppose that a piecewise smooth closed loop ![Image 59: {\displaystyle \gamma :[0,1]\to M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0066953642fb00abb394327531cea098815cd1c8) is given, based at the point ![Image 60: {\displaystyle p\in M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35ad2c18a15749505c928763cd4fdb56f4982816), where ![Image 61: {\displaystyle \gamma (0)=\gamma (1)=p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1de848718109d270e79dfdf7d286e67b82f66c21). We assume that ![Image 62: {\displaystyle \gamma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a223c880b0ce3da8f64ee33c4f0010beee400b1a) is homotopic to zero. The curve can be developed into the tangent space at ![Image 63: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) in the following manner. Let ![Image 64: {\displaystyle \theta ^{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/842a8b679590096930aa1d7f75a6cea3a31340b2) be a parallel coframe along ![Image 65: {\displaystyle \gamma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a223c880b0ce3da8f64ee33c4f0010beee400b1a), and let ![Image 66: {\displaystyle x^{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/be8cd88951c7c0e3b181f956531b0a878bbed203) be the coordinates on ![Image 67: {\displaystyle T_{p}M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/71b20e0304a65ead2cafb33412a383b34fe527ca) induced by ![Image 68: {\displaystyle \theta ^{i}(p)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b09acf148bb9d1ac598ea610a8816d7f77401499). A development of ![Image 69: {\displaystyle \gamma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a223c880b0ce3da8f64ee33c4f0010beee400b1a) is a curve ![Image 70: {\displaystyle {\tilde {\gamma }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a2b090b5a4f599c62fc511fa45ba936b140c7541) in ![Image 71: {\displaystyle T_{p}M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/71b20e0304a65ead2cafb33412a383b34fe527ca) whose coordinates ![Image 72: {\displaystyle x^{i}=x^{i}(t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4a4a30c0955317022c5198c09a0028f7a92388e1) sastify the differential equation ![Image 73: {\displaystyle dx^{i}=\gamma ^{*}\theta ^{i}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/25cf65015436ee1855b331c2c26f037b32af3503) If the torsion is zero, then the developed curve ![Image 74: {\displaystyle {\tilde {\gamma }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a2b090b5a4f599c62fc511fa45ba936b140c7541) is also a closed loop (so that ![Image 75: {\displaystyle {\tilde {\gamma }}(0)={\tilde {\gamma }}(1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/703c85952c7f4115475de3156d4c7366cb4cd604)). On the other hand, if the torsion is non-zero, then the developed curve may not be closed, so that ![Image 76: {\displaystyle {\tilde {\gamma }}(0)\not ={\tilde {\gamma }}(1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/472324e29c23e5cc7d7ba7a0f981f6ab1430ccd6). Thus the development of a loop in the presence of torsion can become dislocated, analogously to a [screw dislocation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Screw_dislocation "Screw dislocation").[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torsion_tensor#cite_note-11)

The foregoing considerations can be made more quantitative by considering a small parallelogram, originating at the point ![Image 77: {\displaystyle p\in M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35ad2c18a15749505c928763cd4fdb56f4982816), with sides ![Image 78: {\displaystyle v,w\in T_{p}M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bd99b11e1c18f437b4fa006ce407e8303c8a035d). Then the tangent bivector to the parallelogram is ![Image 79: {\displaystyle v\wedge w\in \Lambda ^{2}T_{p}M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c1d4929db0658fc3b9dc6904e6eacd40026e494d). The development of this parallelogram, using the connection, is no longer closed in general, and the displacement in going around the loop is translation by the vector ![Image 80: {\displaystyle \Theta (v,w)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/52db2a23823f716ad054d6b55a858912383814b7), where ![Image 81: {\displaystyle \Theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bc927b19f46d005b4720db7a0f96cd5b6f1a0d9b) is the torsion tensor, up to higher order terms in ![Image 82: {\displaystyle v,w}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6425c6e94fa47976601cb44d7564b5d04dcfbfef). This displacement is directly analogous to the [Burgers vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burgers_vector "Burgers vector") of crystallography.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torsion_tensor#cite_note-12)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torsion_tensor#cite_note-13)

More generally, one can also transport a [moving frame](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moving_frame "Moving frame") along the curve ![Image 83: {\displaystyle {\tilde {\gamma }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a2b090b5a4f599c62fc511fa45ba936b140c7541). The _linear_ transformation that the frame undergoes between ![Image 84: {\displaystyle t=0,t=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c029117c742b4951e572f6991e0f5cd33a27abcf) is then determined by the curvature of the connection. Together, the linear transformation of the frame and the translation of the starting point from ![Image 85: {\displaystyle {\tilde {\gamma }}(0)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ed4b8822e3074965064164da7a8ed892b70c7861) to ![Image 86: {\displaystyle {\tilde {\gamma }}(1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7072a440ed521ab98866412fbfc28402913a194f) comprise the [holonomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holonomy "Holonomy") of the connection.

### The torsion of a filament

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Torsion_tensor&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: The torsion of a filament")]

In [materials science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Materials_science "Materials science"), and especially [elasticity theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elasticity_theory "Elasticity theory"), ideas of torsion also play an important role. One problem models the growth of vines, focusing on the question of how vines manage to twist around objects.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torsion_tensor#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGorielyRobertson-TessiTaborVandiver2006-14) The vine itself is modeled as a pair of elastic filaments twisted around one another. In its energy-minimizing state, the vine naturally grows in the shape of a [helix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helix "Helix"). But the vine may also be stretched out to maximize its extent (or length). In this case, the torsion of the vine is related to the torsion of the pair of filaments (or equivalently the surface torsion of the ribbon connecting the filaments), and it reflects the difference between the length-maximizing (geodesic) configuration of the vine and its energy-minimizing configuration.

### Torsion and vorticity

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Torsion_tensor&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Torsion and vorticity")]

In [fluid dynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluid_dynamics "Fluid dynamics"), torsion is naturally associated to [vortex lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vortex_line "Vortex line").

Suppose that a connection ![Image 87: {\displaystyle D}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f34a0c600395e5d4345287e21fb26efd386990e6) is given in three dimensions, with curvature 2-form ![Image 88: {\displaystyle \Omega _{a}^{b}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/89aa1b05f7701871e1cdcc0aae49af6595036876) and torsion 2-form ![Image 89: {\displaystyle \Theta ^{a}=D\theta ^{a}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3ef62823d54b01387a224049ae6b90eb55f02fb5). Let ![Image 90: {\displaystyle \eta _{abc}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7a69f2df65c9d952a6404bccb971e06f03a3f58b) be the skew-symmetric [Levi-Civita tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_tensor "Levi-Civita tensor"), and ![Image 91: {\displaystyle t_{a}={\tfrac {1}{2}}\eta _{abc}\wedge \Omega ^{bc},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b5145ad40c33b05b9c157e73909167c30d49cab1)![Image 92: {\displaystyle s_{ab}=-\eta _{abc}\wedge \Theta ^{c}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3a6bad3b9251d7fe7fdaceaac60bc029be15525b) Then the Bianchi identities The Bianchi identities are ![Image 93: {\displaystyle D\Omega _{b}^{a}=0,\quad D\Theta ^{a}=\Omega _{b}^{a}\wedge \theta ^{b}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3d755753b6aa2184f2400596aede8ce0ea54f6e4) imply that ![Image 94: {\displaystyle Dt_{a}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e134ae027cd11c37888eb5e6d7f0d82fa3a56d2a) and ![Image 95: {\displaystyle Ds_{ab}=\theta _{a}\wedge t_{b}-\theta _{b}\wedge t_{a}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/be59c064b9e54950eb88ccd9a71371f4a6e019db) These are the equations satisfied by an equilibrium continuous medium with moment density ![Image 96: {\displaystyle s_{ab}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e03f0696ab9462e37b39efc6595e122531d08cf7).[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torsion_tensor#cite_note-15)

Geodesics and the absorption of torsion
---------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Torsion_tensor&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Geodesics and the absorption of torsion")]

Suppose that _γ_(_t_) is a curve on _M_. Then _γ_ is an **affinely parametrized geodesic** provided that

![Image 97: {\displaystyle \nabla _{{\dot {\gamma }}(t)}{\dot {\gamma }}(t)=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/85345c0b0be91d3df5147dfccffbc98a3a7b99ba)
for all time _t_ in the domain of _γ_. (Here the dot denotes differentiation with respect to _t_, which associates with γ the tangent vector pointing along it.) Each geodesic is uniquely determined by its initial tangent vector at time _t_ = 0, ![Image 98: {\displaystyle {\dot {\gamma }}(0)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1479c5439158b0681da11338d0ef23f5506448f6).

One application of the torsion of a connection involves the [geodesic spray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic_spray "Geodesic spray") of the connection: roughly the family of all affinely parametrized geodesics. Torsion is the ambiguity of classifying connections in terms of their geodesic sprays:

*   Two connections ∇ and ∇′ which have the same affinely parametrized geodesics (i.e., the same geodesic spray) differ only by torsion.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torsion_tensor#cite_note-16)

More precisely, if _X_ and _Y_ are a pair of tangent vectors at _p_ ∈ _M_, then let

![Image 99: {\displaystyle \Delta (X,Y)=\nabla _{X}{\tilde {Y}}-\nabla '_{X}{\tilde {Y}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8c7b4b56b34cb1415409190cc905e171a20319d6)
be the difference of the two connections, calculated in terms of arbitrary extensions of _X_ and _Y_ away from _p_. By the [Leibniz product rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Product_rule "Product rule"), one sees that Δ does not actually depend on how _X_ and _Y_′ are extended (so it defines a tensor on _M_). Let _S_ and _A_ be the symmetric and alternating parts of Δ:

![Image 100: {\displaystyle S(X,Y)={\tfrac {1}{2}}\left(\Delta (X,Y)+\Delta (Y,X)\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8b957537fd248f638a792d1baf5a1f5a2cb5f40a)![Image 101: {\displaystyle A(X,Y)={\tfrac {1}{2}}\left(\Delta (X,Y)-\Delta (Y,X)\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6704108ef52a12d8c3a4464048f8896d4164002c)
Then

*   ![Image 102: {\displaystyle A(X,Y)={\tfrac {1}{2}}\left(T(X,Y)-T'(X,Y)\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8eb41ba7267c7eb87da5c63b0a1ec75a60c165c8) is the difference of the torsion tensors.
*   ∇ and ∇′ define the same families of affinely parametrized geodesics if and only if _S_(_X_, _Y_) = 0.

In other words, the symmetric part of the difference of two connections determines whether they have the same parametrized geodesics, whereas the skew part of the difference is determined by the relative torsions of the two connections. Another consequence is:

*   Given any affine connection ∇, there is a unique torsion-free connection ∇′ with the same family of affinely parametrized geodesics. The difference between these two connections is in fact a tensor, the [contorsion tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contorsion_tensor "Contorsion tensor").

This is a generalization of the [fundamental theorem of Riemannian geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_theorem_of_Riemannian_geometry "Fundamental theorem of Riemannian geometry") to general affine (possibly non-metric) connections. Picking out the unique torsion-free connection subordinate to a family of parametrized geodesics is known as **absorption of torsion**, and it is one of the stages of [Cartan's equivalence method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartan%27s_equivalence_method "Cartan's equivalence method").
