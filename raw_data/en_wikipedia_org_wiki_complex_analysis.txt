**Complex analysis**, traditionally known as the **theory of functions of a complex variable**, is the branch of [mathematical analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_analysis "Mathematical analysis") that investigates [functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)") of [complex numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_numbers "Complex numbers"). It is helpful in many branches of mathematics, including [algebraic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_geometry "Algebraic geometry"), [number theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_theory "Number theory"), [analytic combinatorics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytic_combinatorics "Analytic combinatorics"), and [applied mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Applied_mathematics "Applied mathematics"), as well as in [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics"), including the branches of [hydrodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrodynamics "Hydrodynamics"), [thermodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamics "Thermodynamics"), [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics"), and [twistor theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Twistor_theory "Twistor theory"). By extension, use of complex analysis also has applications in engineering fields such as [nuclear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_engineering "Nuclear engineering"), [aerospace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aerospace_engineering "Aerospace engineering"), [mechanical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanical_engineering "Mechanical engineering") and [electrical engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrical_engineering "Electrical engineering").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_analysis#cite_note-1)

As a [differentiable function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differentiable_function "Differentiable function") of a complex variable is equal to the [sum function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_series "Function series") given by its [Taylor series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taylor_series "Taylor series") (that is, it is [analytic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analyticity_of_holomorphic_functions "Analyticity of holomorphic functions")), complex analysis is particularly concerned with [analytic functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytic_function "Analytic function") of a complex variable, that is, _[holomorphic functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holomorphic\_function "Holomorphic function")_. The concept can be extended to [functions of several complex variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functions_of_several_complex_variables "Functions of several complex variables").

Complex analysis is contrasted with [real analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_analysis "Real analysis"), which deals with the study of [real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number") and [functions of a real variable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_of_a_real_variable "Function of a real variable").

[Augustin-Louis Cauchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Augustin-Louis_Cauchy "Augustin-Louis Cauchy"), one of the founders of complex analysis

Complex analysis is one of the classical branches in mathematics, with roots in the 18th century and just prior. Important mathematicians associated with complex numbers include [Euler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler "Euler"), [Gauss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Friedrich_Gauss "Carl Friedrich Gauss"), [Riemann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bernhard_Riemann "Bernhard Riemann"), [Cauchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cauchy "Cauchy"), [Weierstrass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weierstrass "Weierstrass"), and many more in the 20th century. Complex analysis, in particular the theory of [conformal mappings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conformal_mapping "Conformal mapping"), has many physical applications and is also used throughout [analytic number theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytic_number_theory "Analytic number theory"). In modern times, it has become very popular through a new boost from [complex dynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_dynamics "Complex dynamics") and the pictures of [fractals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal "Fractal") produced by iterating [holomorphic functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holomorphic_functions "Holomorphic functions"). Another important application of complex analysis is in [string theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/String_theory "String theory") which examines conformal invariants in [quantum field theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_field_theory "Quantum field theory").

An [exponential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponentiation "Exponentiation") function _A_ _n_ of a discrete ([integer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer")) variable n, similar to [geometric progression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_progression "Geometric progression")

A complex function is a [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)") from [complex numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number") to complex numbers. In other words, it is a function that has a (not necessarily proper) subset of the complex numbers as a [domain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_of_a_function "Domain of a function") and the complex numbers as a [codomain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Codomain "Codomain"). Complex functions are generally assumed to have a domain that contains a nonempty [open subset](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_subset "Open subset") of the [complex plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_plane "Complex plane").

For any complex function, the values ![Image 3: {\displaystyle z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf368e72c009decd9b6686ee84a375632e11de98) from the domain and their images ![Image 4: {\displaystyle f(z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d8dd568d570b390c337c0a911f0a1c5c214e8240) in the range may be separated into [real](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number") and [imaginary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imaginary_number "Imaginary number") parts:

![Image 5: {\displaystyle z=x+iy\quad {\text{ and }}\quad f(z)=f(x+iy)=u(x,y)+iv(x,y),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/86025940b06aa6ca9aa3289d7a320106bd2af2af)
where ![Image 6: {\displaystyle x,y,u(x,y),v(x,y)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e14e1e37e7cb9bcb6968ea7026f66b5d659f77fc) are all real-valued.

In other words, a complex function ![Image 7: {\displaystyle f:\mathbb {C} \to \mathbb {C} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/61c4e3a3b342f10ee3f8556c374a36eafa559d0f) may be decomposed into

![Image 8: {\displaystyle u:\mathbb {R} ^{2}\to \mathbb {R} \quad }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4e815a1e0691cc33c8369581a4ee81ef5fc7e20b) and ![Image 9: {\displaystyle \quad v:\mathbb {R} ^{2}\to \mathbb {R} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c5de9188d9bf5e66a6cf2fd2359754cc29d1a6ca)
i.e., into two real-valued functions (![Image 10: {\displaystyle u}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3e6bb763d22c20916ed4f0bb6bd49d7470cffd8), ![Image 11: {\displaystyle v}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e07b00e7fc0847fbd16391c778d65bc25c452597)) of two real variables (![Image 12: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4), ![Image 13: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d)).

Similarly, any complex-valued function f on an arbitrary [set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_(mathematics) "Set (mathematics)")X (is [isomorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isomorphic "Isomorphic") to, and therefore, in that sense, it) can be considered as an [ordered pair](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordered_pair "Ordered pair") of two [real-valued functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real-valued_function "Real-valued function"): (Re _f_, Im _f_) or, alternatively, as a [vector-valued function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector-valued_function "Vector-valued function") from X into ![Image 14: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/066b155c535a38739cc0c4b288324cbb7a4a227a)

Some properties of complex-valued functions (such as [continuity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_function "Continuous function")) are nothing more than the corresponding properties of vector valued functions of two real variables. Other concepts of complex analysis, such as [differentiability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differentiability "Differentiability"), are direct generalizations of the similar concepts for real functions, but may have very different properties. In particular, every [differentiable complex function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holomorphic_function "Holomorphic function") is [analytic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytic_function "Analytic function") (see next section), and two differentiable functions that are equal in a [neighborhood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neighborhood_(mathematics) "Neighborhood (mathematics)") of a point are equal on the intersection of their domain (if the domains are [connected](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connected_space "Connected space")). The latter property is the basis of the principle of [analytic continuation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytic_continuation "Analytic continuation") which allows extending every real [analytic function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytic_function "Analytic function") in a unique way for getting a complex analytic function whose domain is the whole complex plane with a finite number of [curve arcs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arc_(geometry) "Arc (geometry)") removed. Many basic and [special](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_functions "Special functions") complex functions are defined in this way, including the [complex exponential function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponential_function#Complex_plane "Exponential function"), [complex logarithm functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_logarithm "Complex logarithm"), and [trigonometric functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trigonometric_functions#In_the_complex_plane "Trigonometric functions").

Holomorphic functions
---------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Complex_analysis&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Holomorphic functions")]

Complex functions that are [differentiable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differentiable "Differentiable") at every point of an [open subset](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_set "Open set")![Image 15: {\displaystyle \Omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/24b0d5ca6f381068d756f6337c08e0af9d1eeb6f) of the complex plane are said to be _holomorphic on_![Image 16: {\displaystyle \Omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/24b0d5ca6f381068d756f6337c08e0af9d1eeb6f). In the context of complex analysis, the derivative of ![Image 17: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) at ![Image 18: {\displaystyle z_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9e72d1d86e86355892b39b8eb32b964834e113bf) is defined to be[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_analysis#cite_note-2)

![Image 19: {\displaystyle f'(z_{0})=\lim _{z\to z_{0}}{\frac {f(z)-f(z_{0})}{z-z_{0}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/34df99111d0727236ade3f006e89f736e2a7222c)
Superficially, this definition is formally analogous to that of the derivative of a real function. However, complex derivatives and differentiable functions behave in significantly different ways compared to their real counterparts. In particular, for this limit to exist, the value of the difference quotient must approach the same complex number, regardless of the manner in which we approach ![Image 20: {\displaystyle z_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9e72d1d86e86355892b39b8eb32b964834e113bf) in the complex plane. Consequently, complex differentiability has much stronger implications than real differentiability. For instance, holomorphic functions are [infinitely differentiable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinitely_differentiable "Infinitely differentiable"), whereas the existence of the _n_ th derivative need not imply the existence of the (_n_ + 1)th derivative for real functions. Furthermore, all holomorphic functions satisfy the stronger condition of [analyticity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytic_function "Analytic function"), meaning that the function is, at every point in its domain, locally given by a convergent power series. In essence, this means that functions holomorphic on ![Image 21: {\displaystyle \Omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/24b0d5ca6f381068d756f6337c08e0af9d1eeb6f) can be approximated arbitrarily well by polynomials in some neighborhood of every point in ![Image 22: {\displaystyle \Omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/24b0d5ca6f381068d756f6337c08e0af9d1eeb6f). This stands in sharp contrast to differentiable real functions; there are infinitely differentiable real functions that are _nowhere_ analytic; see [Non-analytic smooth function §A smooth function which is nowhere real analytic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-analytic_smooth_function#A_smooth_function_which_is_nowhere_real_analytic "Non-analytic smooth function").

Most elementary functions, including the [exponential function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponential_function "Exponential function"), the [trigonometric functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trigonometric_function "Trigonometric function"), and all [polynomial functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial "Polynomial"), extended appropriately to complex arguments as functions ![Image 23: {\displaystyle \mathbb {C} \to \mathbb {C} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e75067bdd107a9ed06c72de34a4bca18e427ca22), are holomorphic over the entire complex plane, making them _[entire functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entire\_functions "Entire functions")_, while rational functions ![Image 24: {\displaystyle p/q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8fa5bd4cf049744deac0ac4a04c07998bd6befa9), where _p_ and _q_ are polynomials, are holomorphic on domains that exclude points where _q_ is zero. Such functions that are holomorphic everywhere except a set of isolated points are known as _meromorphic functions_. On the other hand, the functions ![Image 25: {\displaystyle z\mapsto \Re (z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/67f8895d4033efe380374a430630600b6c097b23),![Image 26: {\displaystyle z\mapsto |z|}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dcf1ef78ab357dc17fcb3c68b61f4787530e08ae), and ![Image 27: {\displaystyle z\mapsto {\bar {z}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c4049edc8b476632bf03063651c9b076e379b913) are not holomorphic anywhere on the complex plane, as can be shown by their failure to satisfy the Cauchy–Riemann conditions (see below).

An important property of holomorphic functions is the relationship between the partial derivatives of their real and imaginary components, known as the [Cauchy–Riemann conditions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cauchy%E2%80%93Riemann_conditions "Cauchy–Riemann conditions"). If ![Image 28: {\displaystyle f:\mathbb {C} \to \mathbb {C} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/61c4e3a3b342f10ee3f8556c374a36eafa559d0f), defined by ![Image 29: {\displaystyle f(z)=f(x+iy)=u(x,y)+iv(x,y)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/07fa0c5ef6ae36c4b48104ead58d2949891ce19f), where ![Image 30: {\displaystyle x,y,u(x,y),v(x,y)\in \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d5e1e2620066fd827b876ae8606eac987f7a6ec6), is holomorphic on a [region](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Region_(mathematics) "Region (mathematics)")![Image 31: {\displaystyle \Omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/24b0d5ca6f381068d756f6337c08e0af9d1eeb6f), then for all ![Image 32: {\displaystyle z_{0}\in \Omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7d623682aed0b658d48900e7e25ba8814b8998ac),

![Image 33: {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial f}{\partial {\bar {z}}}}(z_{0})=0,\ {\text{where }}{\frac {\partial }{\partial {\bar {z}}}}\mathrel {:=} {\frac {1}{2}}\left({\frac {\partial }{\partial x}}+i{\frac {\partial }{\partial y}}\right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f2b8d55fec75ca73d4a82f3969086594b127a3d8)
In terms of the real and imaginary parts of the function, _u_ and _v_, this is equivalent to the pair of equations ![Image 34: {\displaystyle u_{x}=v_{y}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/383bad63dfc8b966525d0bbf8b96cdf948fdc35f) and ![Image 35: {\displaystyle u_{y}=-v_{x}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/92f2c2e2240bd29bbc0b2731501af69e7d2f7195), where the subscripts indicate partial differentiation. However, the Cauchy–Riemann conditions do not characterize holomorphic functions, without additional continuity conditions (see [Looman–Menchoff theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Looman%E2%80%93Menchoff_theorem "Looman–Menchoff theorem")).

Holomorphic functions exhibit some remarkable features. For instance, [Picard's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Picard_theorem "Picard theorem") asserts that the range of an entire function can take only three possible forms: ![Image 36: {\displaystyle \mathbb {C} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f9add4085095b9b6d28d045fd9c92c2c09f549a7),![Image 37: {\displaystyle \mathbb {C} \setminus \{z_{0}\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a3ec15546a090988b5414e1e54a82343428dbaa4), or ![Image 38: {\displaystyle \{z_{0}\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a232a53cce19e680d87c87f0d07b3877e6e4f583) for some ![Image 39: {\displaystyle z_{0}\in \mathbb {C} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7f9230ede854d9253de13e7548acc5aaa66ac1ac). In other words, if two distinct complex numbers ![Image 40: {\displaystyle z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf368e72c009decd9b6686ee84a375632e11de98) and ![Image 41: {\displaystyle w}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/88b1e0c8e1be5ebe69d18a8010676fa42d7961e6) are not in the range of an entire function ![Image 42: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61), then ![Image 43: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) is a constant function. Moreover, a holomorphic function on a connected open set is determined by its restriction to any nonempty open subset.

[![Image 44](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Conformal_map.svg/250px-Conformal_map.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Conformal_map.svg)

A rectangular grid (top) and its image under a conformal map ![Image 45: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) (bottom). It is seen that ![Image 46: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) maps pairs of lines intersecting at 90° to pairs of curves still intersecting at 90°.

In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), a [conformal map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conformal_map "Conformal map") is a [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)") that locally preserves [angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle "Angle"), but not necessarily lengths.

More formally, let ![Image 47: {\displaystyle U}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/458a728f53b9a0274f059cd695e067c430956025) and ![Image 48: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) be open subsets of ![Image 49: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d). A function ![Image 50: {\displaystyle f:U\to V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/99d431c81a43bf61447672a1c6e62d56fc73026a) is called conformal (or angle-preserving) at a point ![Image 51: {\displaystyle u_{0}\in U}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/133e383f80a11def53dfb9316d0d4f71d32b20da) if it preserves angles between directed [curves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curve "Curve") through ![Image 52: {\displaystyle u_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9c7425f9c7ab645587060423c0af62f8a61fbc65), as well as preserving orientation. Conformal maps preserve both angles and the shapes of infinitesimally small figures, but not necessarily their size or [curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvature "Curvature").

The conformal property may be described in terms of the [Jacobian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacobian_matrix_and_determinant "Jacobian matrix and determinant") derivative matrix of a [coordinate transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_transformation "Coordinate transformation"). The transformation is conformal whenever the Jacobian at each point is a positive scalar times a [rotation matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation_matrix "Rotation matrix") ([orthogonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_matrix "Orthogonal matrix") with determinant one). Some authors define conformality to include orientation-reversing mappings whose Jacobians can be written as any scalar times any orthogonal matrix.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_analysis#cite_note-3)

For mappings in two dimensions, the (orientation-preserving) conformal mappings are precisely the locally invertible [complex analytic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holomorphic_function "Holomorphic function") functions. In three and higher dimensions, [Liouville's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liouville%27s_theorem_(conformal_mappings) "Liouville's theorem (conformal mappings)") sharply limits the conformal mappings to a few types.

The notion of conformality generalizes in a natural way to maps between [Riemannian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_manifold "Riemannian manifold") or [semi-Riemannian manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semi-Riemannian_manifold "Semi-Riemannian manifold").

[Hue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hue "Hue") represents the [argument](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Argument_(complex_analysis) "Argument (complex analysis)"), [brightness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brightness "Brightness") the [magnitude.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_value#Complex_numbers "Absolute value")

One of the central tools in complex analysis is the [line integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_integral "Line integral"). The line integral around a closed path of a function that is holomorphic everywhere inside the area bounded by the closed path is always zero, as is stated by the [Cauchy integral theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cauchy_integral_theorem "Cauchy integral theorem"). The values of such a holomorphic function inside a disk can be computed by a path integral on the disk's boundary (as shown in [Cauchy's integral formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cauchy%27s_integral_formula "Cauchy's integral formula")). Path integrals in the complex plane are often used to determine complicated real integrals, and here the theory of [residues](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Residue_(complex_analysis) "Residue (complex analysis)") among others is applicable (see [methods of contour integration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methods_of_contour_integration "Methods of contour integration")). A "pole" (or [isolated singularity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isolated_singularity "Isolated singularity")) of a function is a point where the function's value becomes unbounded, or "blows up". If a function has such a pole, then one can compute the function's residue there, which can be used to compute path integrals involving the function; this is the content of the powerful [residue theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Residue_theorem "Residue theorem"). The remarkable behavior of holomorphic functions near essential singularities is described by [Picard's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Picard_theorem#Big_Picard "Picard theorem"). Functions that have only poles but no [essential singularities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Essential_singularity "Essential singularity") are called [meromorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meromorphic "Meromorphic"). [Laurent series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laurent_series "Laurent series") are the complex-valued equivalent to [Taylor series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taylor_series "Taylor series"), but can be used to study the behavior of functions near singularities through infinite sums of more well understood functions, such as polynomials.

A [bounded function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bounded_function "Bounded function") that is holomorphic in the entire complex plane must be constant; this is [Liouville's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liouville%27s_theorem_(complex_analysis) "Liouville's theorem (complex analysis)"). It can be used to provide a natural and short proof for the [fundamental theorem of algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_Theorem_of_Algebra "Fundamental Theorem of Algebra") which states that the [field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)") of complex numbers is [algebraically closed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraically_closed_field "Algebraically closed field").

If a function is holomorphic throughout a [connected](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connected_space "Connected space") domain then its values are fully determined by its values on any smaller subdomain. The function on the larger domain is said to be [analytically continued](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytic_continuation "Analytic continuation") from its values on the smaller domain. This allows the extension of the definition of functions, such as the [Riemann zeta function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_zeta_function "Riemann zeta function"), which are initially defined in terms of infinite sums that converge only on limited domains to almost the entire complex plane. Sometimes, as in the case of the [natural logarithm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_logarithm "Natural logarithm"), it is impossible to analytically continue a holomorphic function to a non-simply connected domain in the complex plane but it is possible to extend it to a holomorphic function on a closely related surface known as a [Riemann surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_surface "Riemann surface").

All this refers to complex analysis in one variable. There is also a very rich theory of [complex analysis in more than one complex dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_of_several_complex_variables "Function of several complex variables") in which the analytic properties such as [power series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_series "Power series") expansion carry over whereas most of the geometric properties of holomorphic functions in one complex dimension (such as [conformality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conformality "Conformality")) do not carry over. The [Riemann mapping theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_mapping_theorem "Riemann mapping theorem") about the conformal relationship of certain domains in the complex plane, which may be the most important result in the one-dimensional theory, fails dramatically in higher dimensions.

A major application of certain [complex spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_Hilbert_space "Complex Hilbert space") is in [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics") as [wave functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wave_function "Wave function").
