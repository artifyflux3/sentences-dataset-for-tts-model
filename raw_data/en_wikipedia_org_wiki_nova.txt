Artist's conception of a white dwarf, right, [accreting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accretion_(astrophysics) "Accretion (astrophysics)") hydrogen from the [Roche lobe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roche_lobe "Roche lobe") of its larger companion star

A **nova** is a [transient astronomical event](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transient_astronomical_event "Transient astronomical event") that causes the sudden appearance of a bright, apparently "new" star (hence the name "nova", Latin for "new") that slowly fades over weeks or months. All observed novae involve [white dwarfs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_dwarf "White dwarf") in close [binary systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_star "Binary star"), but causes of the dramatic appearance of a nova vary, depending on the circumstances of the two progenitor stars. The main sub-classes of novae are classical novae, recurrent novae (RNe), and [dwarf novae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dwarf_nova "Dwarf nova"). They are all considered to be [cataclysmic variable stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cataclysmic_variable_star "Cataclysmic variable star").

Classical nova eruptions are the most common type. This type is usually created in a close binary star system consisting of a white dwarf and either a [main sequence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Main_sequence "Main sequence"), [subgiant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subgiant "Subgiant"), or [red giant star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_giant_star "Red giant star"). If the orbital period of the system is a few days or less, the white dwarf is close enough to its companion star to draw [accreted](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accretion_(astrophysics) "Accretion (astrophysics)")[matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matter "Matter") onto its surface, creating a dense but shallow [atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_atmosphere "Stellar atmosphere"). This atmosphere, mostly consisting of [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen"), is heated by the hot white dwarf and eventually reaches a critical temperature, causing ignition of rapid [runaway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermal_runaway "Thermal runaway")[fusion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_fusion "Nuclear fusion"). The sudden increase in energy expels the atmosphere into interstellar space, creating the envelope seen as visible light during the nova event. In past centuries such an event was thought to be a new star. A few novae produce short-lived [nova remnants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nova_remnant "Nova remnant"), lasting for perhaps several centuries.

A recurrent nova involves the same processes as a classical nova, except that the nova event repeats in cycles of a few decades or less as the companion star again feeds the dense atmosphere of the white dwarf after each ignition, as in the star [T Coronae Borealis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/T_Coronae_Borealis "T Coronae Borealis").

Under certain conditions, mass accretion can eventually trigger runaway fusion that destroys the white dwarf rather than merely expelling its atmosphere. In this case, the event is usually classified as a [Type Ia supernova](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Type_Ia_supernova "Type Ia supernova").

Novae most often occur in the sky along the path of the [Milky Way](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milky_Way "Milky Way"), especially near the observed [Galactic Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galactic_Center "Galactic Center") in Sagittarius; however, they can appear anywhere in the sky. They occur far more [frequently](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_novae_in_the_Milky_Way_galaxy "List of novae in the Milky Way galaxy") than galactic [supernovae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supernova "Supernova"), averaging about ten per year in the Milky Way. Most are found telescopically, perhaps only one every 12–18 months reaching [naked-eye](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naked_eye "Naked eye") visibility. Novae reaching first or second [magnitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnitude_(astronomy) "Magnitude (astronomy)") occur only a few times per century. The last bright nova was [V1369 Centauri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/V1369_Centauri "V1369 Centauri"), which reached 3.3 magnitude on 14 December 2013.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nova#cite_note-1)

During the sixteenth century, astronomer [Tycho Brahe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tycho_Brahe "Tycho Brahe") observed the [supernova](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supernova "Supernova")[SN 1572](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SN_1572 "SN 1572") in the [constellation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constellation "Constellation")[Cassiopeia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cassiopeia_(constellation) "Cassiopeia (constellation)"). He described it in his book _De nova stella_ ([Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin "Latin") for "concerning the new star"), giving rise to the adoption of the name _nova_. In this work he argued that a nearby object should be seen to move relative to the fixed stars, and thus the nova had to be very far away. Although SN 1572 was later found to be a supernova and not a nova, the terms were considered interchangeable until the 1930s.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nova#cite_note-encyc-2) After this, novae were called _classical novae_ to distinguish them from [supernovae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supernova "Supernova"), as their causes and energies were thought to be different, based solely on the observational evidence.

Although the term "stella nova" means "new star", novae most often take place on [white dwarfs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_dwarf "White dwarf"), which are remnants of extremely old stars.

Stellar evolution of novae
--------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Nova&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Stellar evolution of novae")]

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Nova-Eridani-2009-LB4.jpg/250px-Nova-Eridani-2009-LB4.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Nova-Eridani-2009-LB4.jpg)

[Nova Eridani 2009](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/KT_Eridani "KT Eridani") ([apparent magnitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apparent_magnitude "Apparent magnitude") ~8.4)

Evolution of potential novae begins with two [main sequence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Main_sequence "Main sequence") stars in a binary system. One of the two [evolves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_evolution "Stellar evolution") into a [red giant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_giant "Red giant"), leaving its remnant white dwarf core in orbit with the remaining star. The second star—which may be either a [main-sequence star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Main_sequence "Main sequence") or an aging [giant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giant_star "Giant star")—begins to shed its envelope onto its white dwarf companion when it overflows its [Roche lobe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roche_lobe "Roche lobe"). As a result, the white dwarf steadily captures matter from the companion's outer atmosphere in an accretion disk, and in turn, the accreted matter falls into the atmosphere. As the white dwarf consists of [degenerate matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degenerate_matter "Degenerate matter"), the [accreted](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accretion_(astrophysics) "Accretion (astrophysics)")[hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen") is unable to expand even though its temperature increases. Runaway fusion occurs when the temperature of this atmospheric layer reaches ~20 million [K](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvin "Kelvin"), initiating nuclear burning via the [CNO cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CNO_cycle "CNO cycle").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nova#cite_note-3)

If the accretion rate is just right, hydrogen fusion may occur in a stable manner on the surface of the white dwarf, giving rise to a [super soft X-ray source](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Super_soft_X-ray_source "Super soft X-ray source"), but for most binary system parameters, the hydrogen burning is thermally unstable and rapidly converts a large amount of the hydrogen into other, [heavier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weight "Weight")[chemical elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_element "Chemical element") in a [runaway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermal_runaway "Thermal runaway") reaction,[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nova#cite_note-encyc-2) liberating an enormous amount of energy. This blows the remaining gases away from the surface of the white dwarf and produces an extremely bright outburst of light.

The rise to peak brightness may be very rapid, or gradual; after the peak, the brightness declines steadily.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nova#cite_note-4) The time taken for a nova to decay by 2 or 3 magnitudes from maximum optical brightness is used for grouping novae into speed classes. Fast novae typically will take less than 25 days to decay by 2 magnitudes, while slow novae will take more than 80 days.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nova#cite_note-5)

Despite its violence, usually the amount of [material](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Material "Material") ejected in a nova is only about 1⁄10,000 of a [solar mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_mass "Solar mass"), quite small relative to the mass of the white dwarf. Furthermore, only five percent of the accreted mass is fused during the power outburst.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nova#cite_note-encyc-2) Nonetheless, this is enough energy to accelerate nova ejecta to velocities as high as several thousand kilometers per second—higher for fast novae than slow ones—with a concurrent rise in [luminosity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luminosity "Luminosity") from a few times solar to 50,000–100,000 times solar.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nova#cite_note-encyc-2)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nova#cite_note-6) In 2010 scientists using NASA's [Fermi Gamma-ray Space Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermi_Gamma-ray_Space_Telescope "Fermi Gamma-ray Space Telescope") discovered that a nova also can emit [gamma rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamma_ray "Gamma ray") (>100 MeV).[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nova#cite_note-7)

Potentially, a [white dwarf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_dwarf "White dwarf") can generate multiple novae over time as additional [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen") continues to accrete onto its surface from its [companion star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_star "Binary star"). Where this repeated flaring is observed, the object is called a recurrent nova. An example is [RS Ophiuchi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RS_Ophiuchi "RS Ophiuchi"), which is known to have flared seven times (in 1898, 1933, 1958, 1967, 1985, 2006, and 2021). Eventually, the [white dwarf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_dwarf "White dwarf") can [explode](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Explosion "Explosion") as a [Type Ia supernova](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Type_Ia_supernova "Type Ia supernova") if it approaches the [Chandrasekhar limit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chandrasekhar_limit "Chandrasekhar limit").

Occasionally, novae are bright enough and close enough to Earth to be conspicuous to the unaided eye. The brightest recent example was [Nova Cygni 1975](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/V1500_Cygni "V1500 Cygni"). This nova appeared on 29 August 1975, in the constellation [Cygnus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cygnus_(constellation) "Cygnus (constellation)") about 5 degrees north of [Deneb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deneb "Deneb"), and reached [magnitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apparent_magnitude "Apparent magnitude")2.0 (nearly as bright as [Deneb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deneb "Deneb")). The most recent were [V1280 Scorpii](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/V1280_Scorpii "V1280 Scorpii"), which reached magnitude 3.7 on 17 February 2007, and [Nova Delphini 2013](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nova_Delphini_2013 "Nova Delphini 2013"). [Nova Centauri 2013](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nova_Centauri_2013 "Nova Centauri 2013") was discovered 2 December 2013 and so far is the brightest nova of this [millennium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Millennium "Millennium"), reaching magnitude 3.3.

A [helium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium "Helium") nova (undergoing a [helium flash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_flash "Helium flash")) is a proposed category of nova event that lacks [hydrogen lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_spectral_series "Hydrogen spectral series") in its [spectrum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectral_line "Spectral line"). The absence of [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen") lines may be caused by the [explosion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Explosion "Explosion") of a [helium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium "Helium") shell on a [white dwarf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_dwarf "White dwarf"). The [theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theory "Theory") was first proposed in 1989, and the first candidate [helium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium "Helium") nova to be observed was [V445 Puppis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/V445_Puppis "V445 Puppis"), in 2000.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nova#cite_note-8) Since then, four other novae have been proposed as [helium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium "Helium") novae.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nova#cite_note-9)

Occurrence rate and astrophysical significance
----------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Nova&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Occurrence rate and astrophysical significance")]

Astronomers have estimated that the [Milky Way](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milky_Way "Milky Way") experiences roughly 25 to 75 novae per year.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nova#cite_note-Shafter2017-10) The number of novae actually observed in the Milky Way each year is much lower, about 10,[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nova#cite_note-nova_list-11) probably because distant novae are obscured by gas and dust absorption.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nova#cite_note-nova_list-11) As of 2019, 407 probable novae had been recorded in the Milky Way.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nova#cite_note-nova_list-11) In the [Andromeda Galaxy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andromeda_Galaxy "Andromeda Galaxy"), roughly 25 novae brighter than about 20th magnitude are discovered each year, and smaller numbers are seen in other nearby galaxies.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nova#cite_note-M31-Nova-12)

[Spectroscopic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopy "Spectroscopy") observation of nova ejecta [nebulae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nebulae "Nebulae") has shown that they are enriched in elements such as helium, carbon, nitrogen, oxygen, neon, and magnesium.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nova#cite_note-encyc-2) Classical nova [explosions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Explosion "Explosion") are galactic producers of the element [lithium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lithium "Lithium").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nova#cite_note-EA-20200601-13)[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nova#cite_note-AJ-20200527-14) The contribution of novae to the [interstellar medium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_medium "Interstellar medium") is not great; novae supply only 1⁄50 as much material to the galaxy as do supernovae, and only 1⁄200 as much as [red giant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_giant "Red giant") and [supergiant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supergiant "Supergiant") stars.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nova#cite_note-encyc-2)

Observed recurrent novae such as [RS Ophiuchi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RS_Ophiuchi "RS Ophiuchi") (those with periods on the order of decades) are rare. Astronomers theorize, however, that most, if not all, novae recur, albeit on time scales ranging from 1,000 to 100,000 years.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nova#cite_note-15) The recurrence interval for a nova is less dependent on the accretion rate of the white dwarf than on its mass; with their powerful gravity, massive white dwarfs require less accretion to fuel an eruption than lower-mass ones.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nova#cite_note-encyc-2) Consequently, the interval is shorter for high-mass white dwarfs.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nova#cite_note-encyc-2)

[V Sagittae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/V_Sagittae "V Sagittae") is unusual in that the time of its next eruption can be predicted fairly accurately; it is expected to recur in approximately 2083, plus or minus about 11 years.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nova#cite_note-16)

Novae are classified according to the [light curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_curve "Light curve") decay speed, referred to as either type A, B, C and R,[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nova#cite_note-17) or using the prefix "N":

*   **NA**: fast novae, with a rapid brightness increase, followed by a brightness decline of 3 magnitudes—to about 1⁄16 brightness—within 100 days.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nova#cite_note-heasarc-18)
*   **NB**: slow novae, with a brightness decline of 3 magnitudes in 150 days or more.
*   **NC**: very slow novae, also known as [symbiotic novae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symbiotic_nova "Symbiotic nova"), staying at maximum light for a decade or more and then fading very slowly.
*   **NR**/**RN**: recurrent novae, where two or more eruptions separated by 80 years or less have been observed.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nova#cite_note-19) These are generally also fast.

[GK Persei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GK_Persei "GK Persei"): Nova of 1901

Some novae leave behind visible [nebulosity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nebulosity "Nebulosity"), material expelled in the nova explosion or in multiple explosions.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nova#cite_note-Liimets2014-20)

Novae as distance indicators
----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Nova&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Novae as distance indicators")]

Novae have some promise for use as [standard candle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_candle "Standard candle") measurements of distances. For instance, the distribution of their [absolute magnitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_magnitude "Absolute magnitude") is [bimodal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bimodal_distribution "Bimodal distribution"), with a main peak at magnitude −8.8, and a lesser one at −7.5. Novae also have roughly the same absolute magnitude 15 days after their peak (−5.5). Nova-based distance estimates to various nearby [galaxies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy "Galaxy") and [galaxy clusters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_cluster "Galaxy cluster") have been shown to be of comparable accuracy to those measured with [Cepheid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cepheid "Cepheid")[variable stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variable_star "Variable star").[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nova#cite_note-21)

A **recurrent nova** (**RN**) is an object that has been seen to experience repeated nova eruptions. The recurrent nova typically brightens by about 9 magnitudes, whereas a classical nova may brighten by more than 12 magnitudes.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nova#cite_note-Schaefer2009-22)

Although it is estimated that as many as a quarter of nova systems experience multiple eruptions, only ten recurrent novae (listed below) have been observed in the Milky Way.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nova#cite_note-23)

Several [extragalactic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extragalactic_astronomy "Extragalactic astronomy") recurrent novae have been observed in the [Andromeda Galaxy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andromeda_Galaxy "Andromeda Galaxy") (M31) and the [Large Magellanic Cloud](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Large_Magellanic_Cloud "Large Magellanic Cloud"). One of these extragalactic novae, [M31N 2008-12a](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=M31N_2008-12a&action=edit&redlink=1 "M31N 2008-12a (page does not exist)"), erupts as frequently as once every 12 months.

On 20 April 2016, the _[Sky & Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sky\_%26\_Telescope "Sky & Telescope")_ website reported a sustained brightening of [T Coronae Borealis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/T_Coronae_Borealis "T Coronae Borealis") from magnitude 10.5 to about 9.2 starting in February 2015. A similar event had been reported in 1938, followed by another outburst in 1946.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nova#cite_note-sandt-24) By June 2018, the star had dimmed slightly but still remained at an unusually high level of activity. In March or April 2023, it dimmed to magnitude 12.3.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nova#cite_note-25) A similar dimming occurred in the year before the 1945 outburst, indicating that it would likely erupt between March and September 2024.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nova#cite_note-26) As of August 16, 2025, this predicted outburst has not yet occurred.

Nova in [Andromeda Galaxy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andromeda_Galaxy "Andromeda Galaxy")

Novae are relatively common in the [Andromeda Galaxy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andromeda_Galaxy "Andromeda Galaxy") (M31); including recurrent ones.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nova#cite_note-32)[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nova#cite_note-33)[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nova#cite_note-34) several dozen novae (brighter than [apparent magnitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apparent_magnitude "Apparent magnitude") +20) are discovered in M31 each year.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nova#cite_note-M31-Nova-12) The [Central Bureau for Astronomical Telegrams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Bureau_for_Astronomical_Telegrams "Central Bureau for Astronomical Telegrams") (CBAT) has tracked novae in M31, [M33](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangulum_Galaxy "Triangulum Galaxy"), and [M81](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Messier_81 "Messier 81").[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nova#cite_note-supernovae.net-35)

*   [Cosmic distance ladder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_distance_ladder "Cosmic distance ladder")– Succession of methods by which astronomers determine the distances to celestial objects
*   [Crab Nebula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crab_Nebula "Crab Nebula")– Supernova remnant in the constellation Taurus
*   [Guest star (astronomy)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guest_star_(astronomy) "Guest star (astronomy)")– Ancient Chinese name for cataclysmic variable stars
*   [Hypernova](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypernova "Hypernova")– Supernova that ejects a large mass at unusually high velocity
*   [Kilonova](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kilonova "Kilonova")– Neutron star merger
*   [Micronova](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Micronova "Micronova")– Explosive stellar phenomenon about one millionth the strength of a classical nova
*   [Superluminous supernova](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superluminous_supernova "Superluminous supernova")– Supernova at least ten times more luminous than a standard supernova
*   [Supernova](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supernova "Supernova")– Astrophysical phenomenon
*   [Supernova impostor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supernova_impostor "Supernova impostor")– Stellar explosions that appear to be supernovae
*   [X-ray burster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray_burster "X-ray burster")– Class of X-ray binary stars
