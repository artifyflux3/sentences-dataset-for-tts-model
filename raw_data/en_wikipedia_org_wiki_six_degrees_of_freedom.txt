Types of movement possible for a rigid body in three-dimensional space

For the episode, see [The Twilight Zone (2019 TV series)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Twilight_Zone_(2019_TV_series)#Six_Degrees_of_Freedom "The Twilight Zone (2019 TV series)").

"Six axis" redirects here. For the video game console controller, see [Sixaxis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sixaxis "Sixaxis").

Not to be confused with [Six degrees of separation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Six_degrees_of_separation "Six degrees of separation").

[![Image 6](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/6DOF.svg/330px-6DOF.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:6DOF.svg)

The six degrees of freedom: forward/back, up/down, left/right, yaw, pitch, roll

**Six degrees of freedom** (**6DOF**), or sometimes **six degrees of movement**, refers to the six [mechanical degrees of freedom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degrees_of_freedom_(mechanics) "Degrees of freedom (mechanics)") of movement of a [rigid body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rigid_body "Rigid body") in [three-dimensional space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_space "Three-dimensional space"). Specifically, the body is free to change [position](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Position_(geometry) "Position (geometry)") as forward/backward (surge), up/down (heave), left/right (sway) [translation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Translation_(physics) "Translation (physics)") in three [perpendicular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular "Perpendicular")[axes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_axis "Coordinate axis"), combined with changes in [orientation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orientation_(geometry) "Orientation (geometry)") through [rotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation "Rotation") about three perpendicular axes, often termed yaw (normal axis), pitch (transverse axis), and roll (longitudinal axis).

**Three degrees of freedom** (**3DOF**), a term often used in the context of [virtual reality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virtual_reality "Virtual reality"), typically refers to tracking of rotational motion only: pitch, yaw, and roll.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Six_degrees_of_freedom#cite_note-roadtovr-dof-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Six_degrees_of_freedom#cite_note-googledev-dof-2)

Robotics
--------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Six_degrees_of_freedom&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Robotics")]

[Serial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Serial_manipulator "Serial manipulator") and [parallel manipulator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_manipulator "Parallel manipulator") systems are generally designed to position an [end-effector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robot_end_effector "Robot end effector") with six [degrees of freedom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degrees_of_freedom "Degrees of freedom"), consisting of three in translation and three in orientation. This provides a direct relationship between actuator positions and the configuration of the manipulator defined by its [forward](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forward_kinematics "Forward kinematics") and [inverse kinematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverse_kinematics "Inverse kinematics").

[Robot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robot "Robot") arms are described by their [degrees of freedom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degrees_of_freedom_(mechanics) "Degrees of freedom (mechanics)"). This is a practical metric, in contrast to the abstract definition of degrees of freedom which measures the aggregate positioning capability of a system.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Six_degrees_of_freedom#cite_note-3)

In 2007, [Dean Kamen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dean_Kamen "Dean Kamen"), inventor of the [Segway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Segway_PT "Segway PT"), unveiled a prototype robotic arm[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Six_degrees_of_freedom#cite_note-4) with 14 degrees of freedom for [DARPA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DARPA "DARPA"). [Humanoid robots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Humanoid_robot "Humanoid robot") typically have 30 or more degrees of freedom, with six degrees of freedom per arm, five or six in each leg, and several more in [torso](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torso "Torso") and [neck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neck "Neck").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Six_degrees_of_freedom#cite_note-5)

Engineering
-----------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Six_degrees_of_freedom&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Engineering")]

The term is important in [mechanical systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degrees_of_freedom_(mechanics) "Degrees of freedom (mechanics)"), especially [biomechanical systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biomechanics "Biomechanics"), for analyzing and measuring properties of these types of systems that need to account for all six degrees of freedom. Measurement of the six degrees of freedom is accomplished today through both AC and DC magnetic or electromagnetic fields in sensors that transmit positional and angular data to a processing unit. The data is made relevant through software that integrates the data based on the needs and programming of the users.

[![Image 7](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Roll_pitch_yaw_mnemonic.svg/250px-Roll_pitch_yaw_mnemonic.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Roll_pitch_yaw_mnemonic.svg)

Mnemonics to remember angle names

The six degrees of freedom of a mobile unit are divided in two motional classes as described below.

**Translational envelopes:**

1.   Moving forward and backward on the X-axis. (Surge)
2.   Moving left and right on the Y-axis. (Sway)
3.   Moving up and down on the Z-axis. (Heave)

**Rotational envelopes:**

1.   Tilting side to side on the X-axis. ([Roll](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flight_dynamics "Flight dynamics"))
2.   Tilting forward and backward on the Y-axis. ([Pitch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flight_dynamics "Flight dynamics"))
3.   Turning left and right on the Z-axis. ([Yaw](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flight_dynamics "Flight dynamics"))

In terms of a [headset](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Head-mounted_display "Head-mounted display"), such as the kind used for [virtual reality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virtual_reality "Virtual reality"), rotational envelopes can also be thought of in the following terms:

*   Pitch: [Nodding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nod_(gesture) "Nod (gesture)") "yes"
*   Yaw: [Shaking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Head_shake "Head shake") "no"
*   Roll: [Bobbling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Head_bobble "Head bobble") from side to side

Operational envelope types
--------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Six_degrees_of_freedom&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Operational envelope types")]

There are three types of operational envelope in the Six degrees of freedom. These types are _Direct_, _Semi-direct_ (conditional) and _Non-direct,_ all regardless of the time remaining for the execution of the maneuver, the energy remaining to execute the maneuver and finally, if the motion is commanded via a biological entity (e.g. human), a robotical entity (e.g. computer) or both.

1.   _Direct type_: Involved a degree can be commanded directly without particularly conditions and described as a normal operation. (An [aileron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aileron "Aileron") on a basic airplane)
2.   _Semi-direct type_: Involved a degree can be commanded when some specific conditions are met. ([Reverse thrust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thrust_reversal "Thrust reversal") on an aircraft)
3.   _Non-direct type_: Involved a degree when is achieved via the interaction with its environment and cannot be commanded. (Pitching motion of a [vessel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boat "Boat") at sea)

Transitional type also exists in some vehicles. For example, when the [Space Shuttle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_Shuttle "Space Shuttle") operated in [low Earth orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit "Low Earth orbit"), the craft was described as fully-direct-six because in the vacuum of space, its six degrees could be commanded via [reaction wheels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reaction_wheels "Reaction wheels") and [RCS thrusters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reaction_control_thruster "Reaction control thruster"). However, when the Space Shuttle was descending through the Earth's atmosphere for its return, the fully-direct-six degrees were no longer applicable as it was [gliding through the air using its wings and control surfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glider_(aircraft) "Glider (aircraft)").

Game controllers
----------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Six_degrees_of_freedom&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Game controllers")]

_Six degrees of freedom_ also refers to movement in video game-play.

[First-person shooter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First-person_shooter "First-person shooter") (FPS) games generally provide five degrees of freedom: forwards/backwards, slide left/right, up/down (jump/crouch/lie), yaw (turn left/right), and pitch (look up/down). If the game allows leaning control, then some consider it a sixth DOF; however, this may not be completely accurate, as a lean is a limited partial rotation.

The term _6DOF_ has sometimes been used to describe games which allow freedom of movement, but do not necessarily meet the full 6DOF criteria. For example, _[Dead Space 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dead\_Space\_2 "Dead Space 2")_, and to a lesser extent, _[Homeworld](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homeworld "Homeworld")_ and _[Zone Of The Enders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zone\_of\_the\_Enders\_(series) "Zone of the Enders (series)")_ allow freedom of movement.

Some examples of true 6DOF games, which allow independent control of all three movement axes and all three rotational axes, include _[Elite Dangerous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elite\_Dangerous "Elite Dangerous")_, _[Shattered Horizon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shattered\_Horizon "Shattered Horizon")_, the _[Descent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Descent\_(video\_game) "Descent (video game)")_ franchise, the [Everspace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Everspace "Everspace") franchise, _[Retrovirus](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Retrovirus\_(PC\_game)&action=edit&redlink=1 "Retrovirus (PC game) (page does not exist)")_, _[Miner Wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Miner\_Wars "Miner Wars")_, _[Space Engineers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space\_Engineers "Space Engineers")_, _[Forsaken](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forsaken\_(video\_game) "Forsaken (video game)")_ and _[Overload](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Overload\_(video\_game) "Overload (video game)")_ (from the same creators of _Descent_). The space MMO _[Vendetta Online](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vendetta\_Online "Vendetta Online")_ also features 6 degrees of freedom.

Motion tracking hardware devices such as [TrackIR](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TrackIR "TrackIR") and software-based apps like [Eyeware Beam](https://apps.apple.com/us/app/eyeware-beam/id1538790472) are used for 6DOF head tracking. This device often finds its places in [flight simulators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flight_simulators "Flight simulators") and other vehicle simulators that require looking around the cockpit to locate enemies or simply avoiding accidents in-game.

The acronym **3DOF**, meaning movement in the three dimensions but not rotation, is sometimes encountered.

The [Razer Hydra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Razer_Hydra "Razer Hydra"), a motion controller for PC, tracks position and rotation of two wired [nunchucks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wii_Remote#Nunchuk "Wii Remote"), providing six degrees of freedom on each hand.

The [SpaceOrb 360](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SpaceOrb_360 "SpaceOrb 360") is a 6DOF computer input device released in 1996 originally manufactured and sold by the SpaceTec IMC company (first bought by [Labtec](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Labtec "Labtec"), which itself was later bought by [Logitech](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logitech "Logitech")). They now offer the [3Dconnexion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3Dconnexion "3Dconnexion") range of 6DOF controllers, primarily targeting the professional [CAD](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CAD "CAD") industry.

The controllers sold with HTC VIVE provide 6DOF information by the lighthouse, which adopts Time of Flight (TOF) technology to determine the position of controllers.
