[![Image 1](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Champagne_vent_white_smokers.jpg/500px-Champagne_vent_white_smokers.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Champagne_vent_white_smokers.jpg)

Evidence of possibly the oldest forms of life on Earth has been found in [hydrothermal vent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrothermal_vent "Hydrothermal vent")[precipitates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Precipitates "Precipitates").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-SCA-20210714-1)

The **earliest known life forms** on [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth "Earth") may be as old as 4.1 billion years (or [Ga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Year#SI_prefix_multipliers "Year")) according to biologically fractionated [graphite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graphite "Graphite") inside a single [zircon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zircon "Zircon") grain in the [Jack Hills](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jack_Hills "Jack Hills") range of [Australia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Australia "Australia").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-PNAS-20151019-2) The earliest evidence of life found in a [stratigraphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stratigraphic_unit "Stratigraphic unit") unit, not just a single [mineral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mineral "Mineral") grain, is the 3.7 Ga [metasedimentary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metasedimentary_rock "Metasedimentary rock") rocks containing graphite from the [Isua Supracrustal Belt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isua_Greenstone_Belt "Isua Greenstone Belt") in [Greenland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenland "Greenland").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-NG-20131208-3) The earliest direct known life on Earth are [stromatolite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stromatolite "Stromatolite") fossils which have been found in 3.480-billion-year-old [geyserite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geyserite "Geyserite") uncovered in the [Dresser](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dresser_Formation "Dresser Formation") Formation of the [Pilbara Craton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pilbara_craton "Pilbara craton") of [Western Australia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_Australia "Western Australia").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-AST-201311082-4) Various [microfossils](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Micropaleontology#Microfossils "Micropaleontology") of [microorganisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism "Microorganism") have been found in 3.4 Ga rocks, including 3.465-billion-year-old [Apex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pilbara_craton "Pilbara craton")[chert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chert "Chert") rocks from the same Australian [craton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Craton "Craton") region,[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-PNAS-2017-5) and in 3.42 Ga [hydrothermal vent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrothermal_vent "Hydrothermal vent")[precipitates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Precipitates "Precipitates") from [Barberton, South Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barberton,_South_Africa "Barberton, South Africa").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-SCA-20210714-1) Much later in the geologic record, likely starting in 1.73 Ga, preserved [molecular compounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecule "Molecule") of [biologic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life "Life") origin are indicative of [aerobic life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aerobic_organism "Aerobic organism").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-6) Therefore, the earliest time for the [origin of life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abiogenesis "Abiogenesis") on Earth is at least 3.5 billion years ago and possibly as early as 4.1 billion years ago — not long after the [oceans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ocean "Ocean")[formed 4.5 billion years ago](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Origin_of_water_on_Earth#History_of_water_on_Earth "Origin of water on Earth") and after the [formation of the Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Age_of_Earth "Age of Earth") 4.54 billion years ago.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-USGS1997-7)

Earth is the only place in the [universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe "Universe") known to harbor [life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life "Life"), where it exists in myriad environments.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-NASA-1990-8)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-Alterman-2009-9) The origin of life on Earth was at least 3.5 billion years ago, possibly as early as 3.8-4.1 billion years ago.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-PNAS-20151019-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-NG-20131208-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-AST-201311082-4) Since its emergence, life has persisted in several geological environments. The Earth's [biosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere "Biosphere") extends down to at least 10 km (6.2 mi) below the seafloor,[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-NYT-20181219-10)[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-11) up to 41–77 km (25–48 mi)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-SA-20191104-12)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-13) into the [atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth "Atmosphere of Earth"),[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-SD-19980625-UG-14)[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-ABM-20150112-15)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-BBC-20151124-16) and includes [soil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pedosphere "Pedosphere"), [hydrothermal vents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrothermal_vent#Biological_communities "Hydrothermal vent"), and [rock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endolith "Endolith").[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-NC-20200402-17)[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-EA-20200402-18) Further, the biosphere has been found to extend at least 914.4 m (3,000 ft; 0.5682 mi) below the ice of [Antarctica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antarctica "Antarctica")[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-FR-20210215-19)[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-NAT-20140820-20) and includes the [deepest parts of the ocean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deep_sea#Biology "Deep sea").[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-LS-20130317-21)[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-NG-20130317-22)[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-LS-20130314-23)[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-BBC-20141215-RM-24) In July 2020, [marine biologists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marine_biology "Marine biology") reported that [aerobic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aerobic_organism "Aerobic organism")[microorganisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism "Microorganism") (mainly) in "quasi-[suspended animation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suspended_animation "Suspended animation")" were found in organically poor [sediment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sediment "Sediment") 76.2 m (250 ft) below the [seafloor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seabed "Seabed") in the [South Pacific Gyre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Pacific_Gyre "South Pacific Gyre") (SPG) ("the deadest spot in the ocean").[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-NC-20200728-25)[Microbes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism "Microorganism") have been found in the [Atacama Desert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atacama_Desert "Atacama Desert") in [Chile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chile "Chile"), one of the driest places on Earth,[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-26) and in [deep-sea hydrothermal vent environments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrothermal_vent_microbial_communities "Hydrothermal vent microbial communities") which can reach temperatures over 400°C.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-27) Microbial communities can also survive in cold [permafrost](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permafrost "Permafrost") conditions down to -25°C.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-28) Under certain test conditions, life forms have been observed to [survive in the vacuum of outer space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Panspermia#Counterarguments "Panspermia").[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-Dose-29)[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-Horneck-30) More recently, studies conducted on the [International Space Station](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Space_Station "International Space Station") found that [bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacteria "Bacteria") could survive in [outer space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_space "Outer space").[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-FM-20200826-31) In February 2023, findings of a "[dark microbiome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_microbiome "Dark microbiome")" of [microbial dark matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microbial_dark_matter "Microbial dark matter") of unfamiliar [microorganisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism "Microorganism") in the [Atacama Desert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atacama_Desert "Atacama Desert") in [Chile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chile "Chile"), a [Mars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mars "Mars")-like region of planet [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth "Earth"), were reported.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-NC-20230221-32)

Geochemical evidence
--------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Earliest_known_life_forms&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Geochemical evidence")]

The [age of Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Age_of_Earth "Age of Earth") is about 4.54 billion years;[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-USGS1997-7)[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-33)[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-34) the earliest undisputed evidence of life on Earth dates from at least 3.5 billion years ago according to the stromatolite record.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-MS-2023-35) Some computer models suggest life began as early as 4.5 billion years ago.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-PHY-201808203-36)[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-NAT-201808203-37) The oldest evidence of life is _indirect_ in the form of [isotopic fractionation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotope_fractionation "Isotope fractionation") processes. Microorganisms will preferentially use the lighter [isotope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotope "Isotope") of an atom to build [biomass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biomass "Biomass"), as it takes less energy to break the bonds for [metabolic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metabolism "Metabolism") processes.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-38) Biologic material will often have a composition that is enriched in lighter isotopes compared to the surrounding rock it's found in. [Carbon isotopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotopes_of_carbon "Isotopes of carbon"), expressed scientifically in parts per thousand difference from a standard as [δ 13 C](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%CE%9413C "Δ13C"), are frequently used to detect [carbon fixation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biological_carbon_fixation "Biological carbon fixation") by organisms and assess if purported early life evidence has biological origins. Typically, life will preferentially metabolize the isotopically light 12 C isotope instead of the heavier 13 C isotope. Biologic material can record this fractionation of [carbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon "Carbon").

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Quartz-pebble_metaconglomerate_%28Jack_Hills_Quartzite%2C_Archean%2C_2.65_to_3.05_Ga%3B_Jack_Hills%2C_Western_Australia%29_1_%2826668804034%29.jpg/250px-Quartz-pebble_metaconglomerate_%28Jack_Hills_Quartzite%2C_Archean%2C_2.65_to_3.05_Ga%3B_Jack_Hills%2C_Western_Australia%29_1_%2826668804034%29.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Quartz-pebble_metaconglomerate_(Jack_Hills_Quartzite,_Archean,_2.65_to_3.05_Ga;_Jack_Hills,_Western_Australia)_1_(26668804034).jpg)

[Zircons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zircon "Zircon") in [metaconglomerates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metaconglomerate "Metaconglomerate") from the [Jack Hills](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jack_Hills "Jack Hills") in Australia show carbon isotopic evidence for early life.

The oldest disputed geochemical evidence of life is isotopically light [graphite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graphite "Graphite") inside a single [zircon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zircon "Zircon") grain from the [Jack Hills](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jack_Hills "Jack Hills") in Western Australia.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-PNAS-20151019-2)[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-39) The graphite showed a δ 13 C signature consistent with biogenic carbon on Earth. Other early evidence of life is found in rocks both from the [Akilia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Akilia "Akilia") Sequence[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-40) and the [Isua Supracrustal Belt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isua_Greenstone_Belt "Isua Greenstone Belt") (ISB) in Greenland.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-NG-20131208-3)[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-Hassenkam-2017-41) These 3.7 Ga [metasedimentary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metasediment "Metasediment") rocks also contain graphite or graphite inclusions with carbon isotope signatures that suggest biological fractionation.

The primary issue with isotopic evidence of life is that [abiotic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abiotic_component "Abiotic component") processes can fractionate isotopes and produce similar signatures to biotic processes.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-van_Zuilen-2002-42) Reassessment of the Akilia graphite show that metamorphism, [Fischer-Tropsch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fischer%E2%80%93Tropsch_process "Fischer–Tropsch process") mechanisms in hydrothermal environments, and volcanic processes may be responsible for enrichment lighter carbon isotopes.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-43)[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-44)[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-45) The ISB rocks that contain the graphite may have experienced a change in composition from hot fluids, i.e. [metasomatism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metasomatism "Metasomatism"), thus the graphite may have been formed by abiotic chemical reactions.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-van_Zuilen-2002-42) However, the ISB's graphite is generally more accepted as biologic in origin after further [spectral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectral_line "Spectral line") analysis.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-NG-20131208-3)[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-Hassenkam-2017-41)

Metasedimentary rocks from the 3.5 Ga Dresser Formation, which experienced less metamorphism than the sequences in [Greenland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenland "Greenland"), contain better preserved [geochemical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geochemistry "Geochemistry") evidence.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-Van_Kranendonk-2019-46) Carbon isotopes as well as [sulfur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur "Sulfur") isotopes found in [barite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baryte "Baryte"), which are [fractionated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractionation "Fractionation") by microbial metabolisms during sulfate reduction,[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-47) are consistent with biological processes.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-48)[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-49) However, the Dresser formation was deposited in an active [volcanic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volcano "Volcano") and [hydrothermal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrothermal_vent "Hydrothermal vent") environment,[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-Van_Kranendonk-2019-46) and abiotic processes could still be responsible for these fractionations.[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-Lollar06-50) Many of these findings are supplemented by direct evidence, typically by the presence of [microfossils](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microfossils "Microfossils"), however.

[Fossils](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fossil "Fossil") are direct evidence of life. In the search for the earliest life, fossils are often supplemented by geochemical evidence. The fossil record does not extend as far back as the geochemical record due to metamorphic processes that erase fossils from geologic units.

[Stromatolites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stromatolite "Stromatolite") are laminated sedimentary structures created by [photosynthetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photosynthesis "Photosynthesis") organisms as they establish a [microbial mat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microbial_mat "Microbial mat") on a sediment surface. An important distinction for biogenicity is their convex-up structures and wavy laminations, which are typical of microbial communities who build preferentially toward the sun.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-51) A disputed report of stromatolites is from the 3.7 Ga Isua metasediments that show convex-up, conical, and domical morphologies.[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-52)[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-NYT-20160831-53)[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-NAT-20160922-54) Further mineralogical analysis disagrees with the initial findings of internal convex-up laminae, a critical criterion for stromatolite identification, suggesting that the structures may be deformation features (i.e. [boudins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boudinage "Boudinage")) caused by extensional [tectonics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tectonics "Tectonics") in the Isua [Supracrustal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supracrustal_rock "Supracrustal rock") Belt.[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-55)[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-NG-20181017-56)

[Stromatolite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stromatolite "Stromatolite") fossil showing convex-up structures.

The earliest direct evidence of life are stromatolites found in 3.48 billion-year-old [chert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chert "Chert") in the Dresser formation of the Pilbara Craton in Western Australia.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-AST-201311082-4) Several features in these fossils are difficult to explain with abiotic processes, for example, the thickening of laminae over flexure crests that is expected from more sunlight.[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-57) Sulfur isotopes from barite veins in the stromatolites also favor a biologic origin.[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-58) However, while most scientists accept their biogenicity, abiotic explanations for these fossils cannot be fully discarded due to their hydrothermal depositional environment and debated geochemical evidence.[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-Lollar06-50)

Most [archean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archean "Archean") stromatolites older than 3.0 Ga are found in Australia or South Africa. Stratiform stromatolites from the Pilbara Craton have been identified in the 3.47 Ga Mount Ada Basalt.[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-59)[Barberton, South Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barberton,_South_Africa "Barberton, South Africa") hosts stratiform stromatolites in the 3.46 Hooggenoeg, 3.42 Kromberg and 3.33 Ga Mendon Formations of the [Onverwacht Group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Onverwacht_Group "Onverwacht Group").[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-60)[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-61) The 3.43 Ga [Strelley Pool Formation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strelley_Pool "Strelley Pool") in Western Australia hosts stromatolites that demonstrate vertical and horizontal changes that may demonstrate microbial communities responding to transient environmental conditions.[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-62) Thus, it is likely [anoxygenic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anoxygenic_photosynthesis "Anoxygenic photosynthesis") or oxygenic [photosynthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photosynthesis "Photosynthesis") has been occurring since at least 3.43 Ga Strelley Pool Formation.[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-63)

Claims of the earliest life using fossilized microorganisms ([microfossils](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Micropaleontology#Microfossils "Micropaleontology")) are from [hydrothermal vent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrothermal_vent "Hydrothermal vent")[precipitates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Precipitates "Precipitates") from an ancient sea-bed in the [Nuvvuagittuq Belt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuvvuagittuq_Greenstone_Belt "Nuvvuagittuq Greenstone Belt") of Quebec, Canada. These may be as old as 4.28 billion years, which would make it the oldest evidence of life on Earth, suggesting "an almost instantaneous emergence of life" after [ocean formation 4.41 billion years ago](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Origin_of_water_on_Earth#History_of_water_on_Earth "Origin of water on Earth").[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-NAT-20170301-64)[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-65) These findings may be better explained by abiotic processes: for example, silica-rich waters,[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-66) "chemical gardens,"[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-67) circulating hydrothermal fluids,[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-68) and volcanic ejecta[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-69) can produce morphologies similar to those presented in Nuvvuagittuq.

[![Image 4](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Halobacteria.jpg/250px-Halobacteria.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Halobacteria.jpg)

[Archaea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archaea "Archaea") ([prokaryotic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prokaryotes "Prokaryotes")[microbes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism "Microorganism")) were first found in [extreme environments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extreme_environment "Extreme environment"), such as [hydrothermal vents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrothermal_vent "Hydrothermal vent").

The 3.48 Ga Dresser formation hosts microfossils of [prokaryotic filaments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prokaryotic_cytoskeleton "Prokaryotic cytoskeleton") in silica veins, the earliest fossil evidence of life on Earth,[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-70) but their origins may be volcanic.[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-71) 3.465-billion-year-old [Australian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Australia "Australia")[Apex chert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apex_chert "Apex chert") rocks may once have contained [microorganisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism "Microorganism"),[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-WU-20171218-72)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-PNAS-2017-5) although the validity of these findings has been contested.[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-Brasier_Green_Lindsay_2005-73)[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-74) "Putative filamentous microfossils," possibly of [methanogens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanogen "Methanogen") and/or [methanotrophs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanotroph "Methanotroph") that lived about 3.42-billion-year-old in "a paleo-subseafloor [hydrothermal vein system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrothermal_circulation#Seafloor_hydrothermal_circulation "Hydrothermal circulation") of the [Barberton greenstone belt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barberton_greenstone_belt "Barberton greenstone belt"), have been identified in [South Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Africa "South Africa")."[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-SCA-20210714-1) A diverse set of microfossil morphologies have been found in the 3.43 Ga Strelley Pool Formation including spheroid, lenticular, and film-like microstructures.[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-75) Their biogenicity are strengthened by their observed chemical preservation.[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-76) The early lithification of these structures allowed important chemical tracers, such as the [carbon-to-nitrogen ratio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon-to-nitrogen_ratio "Carbon-to-nitrogen ratio"), to be retained at levels higher than is typical in older, metamorphosed rock units.

### Molecular biomarkers

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Earliest_known_life_forms&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Molecular biomarkers")]

Biomarkers are compounds of biologic origin found in the geologic record that can be linked to past life.[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-77) Although they aren't preserved until the late Archean, they are important indicators of early [photosynthetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photosynthesis "Photosynthesis") life. [Lipids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lipid "Lipid") are particularly useful biomarkers because they can survive for long periods of geologic time and reconstruct past environments.[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-78)

[![Image 5](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Common_lipid_types.svg/250px-Common_lipid_types.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Common_lipid_types.svg)

[Lipids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lipid "Lipid") are commonly used in geologic studies to find evidence of oxygenic [photosynthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photosynthesis "Photosynthesis").

Fossilized lipids were reported from 2.7 Ga laminated [shales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shale "Shale") from the Pilbara Craton[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-79) and the 2.67 Ga [Kaapvaal craton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaapvaal_craton "Kaapvaal craton") in South Africa.[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-80) However, the age of these biomarkers and whether their deposition was synchronous with their host rocks were debated,[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-81) and further work showed that the lipids were contaminants.[[82]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-82) The oldest "clearly indigenous"[[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-Vinnichenko-2020-83) biomarkers are from the 1.64 Ga Barney Creek Formation in the [McArthur Basin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/McArthur_Basin "McArthur Basin") in Northern Australia,[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-84)[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-85) but [hydrocarbons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrocarbon "Hydrocarbon") from the 1.73 Ga Wollogorang Formation in the same basin have also been detected.[[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-Vinnichenko-2020-83)

Other indigenous biomarkers can be dated to the [Mesoproterozoic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mesoproterozoic "Mesoproterozoic") era (1.6-1.0 Ga). The 1.4 Ga Hongshuizhuang Formation in the [North China Craton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_China_craton "North China craton") contains hydrocarbons in shales that were likely sourced from [prokaryotes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prokaryote "Prokaryote").[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-86) Biomarkers were found in [siltstones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siltstone "Siltstone") from the 1.38 Ga Roper Group of the McArthur Basin.[[87]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-87) Hydrocarbons possibly derived from bacteria and algae were reported in 1.37 Ga Xiamaling Formation of the NCC.[[88]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-88) The 1.1 Ga Atar/El Mreïti Group in the [Taoudeni Basin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taoudeni_Basin "Taoudeni Basin"), Mauritania show indigenous biomarkers in black shales.[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-89)

By [comparing the genomes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phylogenetic_bracketing "Phylogenetic bracketing") of modern organisms (in the domains [Bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacteria "Bacteria") and [Archaea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archaea "Archaea")), it is evident that there was a [last universal common ancestor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Last_universal_common_ancestor "Last universal common ancestor") (LUCA). Another term for the LUCA is the cenancestor and can be viewed as a population of organisms rather than a single entity.[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-90) LUCA is not thought to be the first life on Earth, but rather the only type of organism of its time to still have living descendants. In 2016, M. C. Weiss and colleagues proposed a minimal set of genes that each occurred in at least two groups of Bacteria and two groups of Archaea. They argued that such a distribution of genes would be unlikely to arise by [horizontal gene transfer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horizontal_gene_transfer "Horizontal gene transfer"), and so any such genes must have derived from the LUCA.[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-nature-91) A [molecular clock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_clock "Molecular clock") model suggests that the LUCA may have lived 4.477—4.519 billion years ago, within the [Hadean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hadean "Hadean") eon.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-PHY-201808203-36)[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-NAT-201808203-37)

Model Hadean-like [geothermal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geothermal_activity "Geothermal activity")[microenvironments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microenvironment_(ecology) "Microenvironment (ecology)") were demonstrated to have the potential to support the synthesis and replication of [RNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RNA "RNA") and thus possibly the evolution of primitive life.[[92]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-Salditt2023-92) Porous rock systems, comprising heated air-water interfaces, were shown to facilitate [ribozyme](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ribozyme "Ribozyme")[catalyzed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catalysis "Catalysis") RNA replication of sense and antisense strands and then subsequent strand-dissociation.[[92]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-Salditt2023-92) This enabled combined synthesis, release and folding of active ribozymes.[[92]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-Salditt2023-92)

Theories for the origin of life on Earth
----------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Earliest_known_life_forms&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Theories for the origin of life on Earth")]

[![Image 6](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Porous_chondriteIDP.jpg/250px-Porous_chondriteIDP.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Porous_chondriteIDP.jpg)

The theory of [panspermia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Panspermia "Panspermia") speculates that [life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life "Life") on [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth "Earth") may have come from biological matter carried by [space dust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_dust "Space dust")[[93]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-ARX-20171106-93) or [meteorites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteorite "Meteorite").[[94]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-SA-20180110-94)

While current geochemical evidence dates the origin of life to possibly as early as 4.1 Ga, and fossil evidence shows life at 3.5 Ga, some researchers speculate that life may have started nearly 4.5 billion years ago.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-PHY-201808203-36)[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-NAT-201808203-37) According to biologist [Stephen Blair Hedges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stephen_Blair_Hedges "Stephen Blair Hedges"), "If life arose relatively quickly on Earth... then it could be common in the [universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe "Universe")."[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-AP-20151019-95)[[96]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-CSM-20151020-96)[[97]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-IND-20171002-97) The possibility that terrestrial life forms may have been seeded from outer space has been considered.[[98]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-PBMB-20180801-98)[[99]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-SA-20211228-99) In January 2018, a study found that 4.5 billion-year-old [meteorites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteorite "Meteorite") found on Earth contained [liquid water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water#Liquid_water "Water") along with [prebiotic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abiogensis "Abiogensis")[complex organic substances](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_compound "Organic compound") that may be ingredients for [life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life "Life").[[94]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-SA-20180110-94)

Hydrothermal vents have long been hypothesized to be the grounds from which life originated. The properties of ancient hydrothermal vents, such as the geochemistry, pressure, and temperatures, have the potential to create organic molecules from inorganic molecules.[[100]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-100) In experiments performed by NASA, it was shown that the organic compounds formate and methane could be created from inorganics in the conditions of ancient hydrothermal vents.[[101]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-101) The production of organic molecules could have led to the formation of more complex organic molecules, such as amino acids that can eventually form RNA or DNA.

### Darwin’s Hypothesis

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Earliest_known_life_forms&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Darwin’s Hypothesis")]

Charles Darwin is well-known for his theory of evolution via natural selection. His theory for the origin of life was a “warm little pond” that harbored necessary elements for the creation of life such as “ammonia and phosphoric salts, lights, heat, electricity … so that a protein compound was chemically formed ready to undergo still more complex changes.”[[102]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-102) However, he mentioned that such an environment today would likely have been destroyed faster than it would take to form life. With this, Darwin’s ideas are generally regarded as the spontaneous generation hypothesis.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

### Oparin-Haldane Hypothesis

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Earliest_known_life_forms&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Oparin-Haldane Hypothesis")]

In 1924, Aleksander Oparin suggested that the early atmosphere on Earth was full of reducing components such as ammonia, methane, water vapor, and hydrogen gas.[[103]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-103) This was proposed after atmospheric methane was discovered on other planets. Later in 1929, J.B.S. Haldane published an article that proposed the same conditions for early life on Earth as Oparin suggested. Their hypothesis was later supported by the Miller-Urey experiment.

### Miller Urey Experiment

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Earliest_known_life_forms&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Miller Urey Experiment")]

[![Image 7](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Miller-Urey_experiment-en.svg/250px-Miller-Urey_experiment-en.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Miller-Urey_experiment-en.svg)

Schematic of the Miller-Urey experiment.

At the University of Chicago in 1953, a graduate student named Stanley Miller carried out an experiment under his professor, Harold Urey.[[104]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-104) The method would allow for reducing gases to simulate the atmosphere early on Earth and a “spark” to simulate lightning. There was a reflux apparatus that would heat water and mix into the atmosphere where it would then cool and run into the “primordial ocean.” The gases that were used to mimic the reducing atmosphere were methane, ammonia, water vapor, and hydrogen gas. Within a day of allowing the apparatus to run, the experiment yielded a “brown sludge” which was later tested and found to include the following amino acids: glycine, alanine, aspartic acid, and aminobutyric acid. In the following years, many scientists attempted to replicate the results of the experiment and is now known as a fundamental approach to the study of “abiogenesis”. The Miller-Urey experiment was able to simulate the early conditions of Earth’s atmosphere and produced essential amino acids that likely contributed to the production of life.[[105]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-105)

Cairns-Smith first introduced this hypothesis in 1966, where they proposed that any crystallization process is likely to involve a basic biological evolution.[[106]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms#cite_note-106) Hartman then added on to this hypothesis by proposing in 1975 that metabolism could have developed from a simple environment such as clays. Clays have the ability to synthesize monomers such as amino acids, nucleotides, and other building blocks and polymerize them to create macromolecules. This makes it possible for nucleic acids like RNA or DNA to be created from clay, and cells could further evolve from there.
