**Ellipsoidal coordinates** are a three-dimensional [orthogonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_coordinates "Orthogonal coordinates")[coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_system "Coordinate system")![Image 1: {\displaystyle (\lambda ,\mu ,\nu )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5f59dbb541f8ddddbc4afb943f4ea6cf03a6afc2) that generalizes the two-dimensional [elliptic coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptic_coordinates "Elliptic coordinates"). Unlike most three-dimensional [orthogonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_coordinates "Orthogonal coordinates")[coordinate systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_system "Coordinate system") that feature [quadratic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadratic_function "Quadratic function")[coordinate surfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_surfaces "Coordinate surfaces"), the ellipsoidal coordinate system is based on [confocal quadrics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confocal_quadrics "Confocal quadrics").

The Cartesian coordinates ![Image 2: {\displaystyle (x,y,z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/22a8c93372e8f8b6e24d523bd5545aed3430baf4) can be produced from the ellipsoidal coordinates ![Image 3: {\displaystyle (\lambda ,\mu ,\nu )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5f59dbb541f8ddddbc4afb943f4ea6cf03a6afc2) by the equations

![Image 4: {\displaystyle x^{2}={\frac {\left(a^{2}+\lambda \right)\left(a^{2}+\mu \right)\left(a^{2}+\nu \right)}{\left(a^{2}-b^{2}\right)\left(a^{2}-c^{2}\right)}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38cd21f63bb2e7ea1702204bef202d5ca70b16f1)![Image 5: {\displaystyle y^{2}={\frac {\left(b^{2}+\lambda \right)\left(b^{2}+\mu \right)\left(b^{2}+\nu \right)}{\left(b^{2}-a^{2}\right)\left(b^{2}-c^{2}\right)}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/73f7da67544d9304d084b778aafed362a2f09d1a)![Image 6: {\displaystyle z^{2}={\frac {\left(c^{2}+\lambda \right)\left(c^{2}+\mu \right)\left(c^{2}+\nu \right)}{\left(c^{2}-b^{2}\right)\left(c^{2}-a^{2}\right)}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a41cf78c6d0133ce1883da4bde2265feb3bdf8d8)
where the following limits apply to the coordinates

![Image 7: {\displaystyle -\lambda <c^{2}<-\mu <b^{2}<-\nu <a^{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bdb156b67ff5a751b143ad09f12e88b1fd8157e4)
Consequently, surfaces of constant ![Image 8: {\displaystyle \lambda }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b43d0ea3c9c025af1be9128e62a18fa74bedda2a) are [ellipsoids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipsoid "Ellipsoid")

![Image 9: {\displaystyle {\frac {x^{2}}{a^{2}+\lambda }}+{\frac {y^{2}}{b^{2}+\lambda }}+{\frac {z^{2}}{c^{2}+\lambda }}=1,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5d8aa0390bbb997dff612a45b0461259436e3973)
whereas surfaces of constant ![Image 10: {\displaystyle \mu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9fd47b2a39f7a7856952afec1f1db72c67af6161) are [hyperboloids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperboloid "Hyperboloid") of one sheet

![Image 11: {\displaystyle {\frac {x^{2}}{a^{2}+\mu }}+{\frac {y^{2}}{b^{2}+\mu }}+{\frac {z^{2}}{c^{2}+\mu }}=1,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0baf64c42325c96427062d1f688fa01771b7d33f)
because the last term in the lhs is negative, and surfaces of constant ![Image 12: {\displaystyle \nu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c15bbbb971240cf328aba572178f091684585468) are [hyperboloids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperboloid "Hyperboloid") of two sheets

![Image 13: {\displaystyle {\frac {x^{2}}{a^{2}+\nu }}+{\frac {y^{2}}{b^{2}+\nu }}+{\frac {z^{2}}{c^{2}+\nu }}=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6cc9c15cfa9b6e4aaa469416cd0fa99af43f3b70)
because the last two terms in the lhs are negative.

The orthogonal system of quadrics used for the ellipsoidal coordinates are [confocal quadrics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confocal_quadrics "Confocal quadrics").

Scale factors and differential operators
----------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ellipsoidal_coordinates&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Scale factors and differential operators")]

For brevity in the equations below, we introduce a function

where ![Image 15: {\displaystyle \sigma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/59f59b7c3e6fdb1d0365a494b81fb9a696138c36) can represent any of the three variables ![Image 16: {\displaystyle (\lambda ,\mu ,\nu )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5f59dbb541f8ddddbc4afb943f4ea6cf03a6afc2). Using this function, the [scale factors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvilinear_coordinates#Differential_elements "Curvilinear coordinates") can be written

and the [Laplacian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laplacian "Laplacian") is defined by

-----------------------
