**Felix Christian Klein** (;[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Felix_Klein#cite_note-1)German:[[klaɪn]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German "Help:IPA/Standard German"); 25 April 1849– 22 June 1925) was a German [mathematician](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematician "Mathematician"), [mathematics educator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics_education "Mathematics education") and [historian of mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_mathematics "History of mathematics"), known for his work in [group theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_theory "Group theory"), [complex analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_analysis "Complex analysis"), [non-Euclidean geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-Euclidean_geometry "Non-Euclidean geometry"), and the associations between [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry") and [group theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_theory "Group theory"). His 1872 [Erlangen program](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erlangen_program "Erlangen program") classified geometries by their basic [symmetry groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry_group "Symmetry group") and was an influential synthesis of much of the mathematics of the time.

During his tenure at the [University of Göttingen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_G%C3%B6ttingen "University of Göttingen"), Klein was able to turn it into a center for mathematical and scientific research through the establishment of new lectures, professorships, and institutes. His [seminars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Felix_Klein_Protocols "Felix Klein Protocols") covered most areas of mathematics then known as well as their applications. Klein also devoted considerable time to mathematical instruction and promoted mathematics education reform at all grade levels in Germany and abroad. He became the first president of the [International Commission on Mathematical Instruction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Commission_on_Mathematical_Instruction "International Commission on Mathematical Instruction") in 1908 at the Fourth International Congress of Mathematicians in Rome.

Klein during his Leipzig period.

Felix Klein was born on 25 April 1849 in [Düsseldorf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D%C3%BCsseldorf "Düsseldorf"),[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Felix_Klein#cite_note-2) to [Prussian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prussia "Prussia") parents. His father, Caspar Klein (1809–1889), was a Prussian government official's secretary stationed in the [Rhine Province](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhine_Province "Rhine Province"). His mother was Sophie Elise Klein (1819–1890, [née](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Name_at_birth "Name at birth") Kayser).[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Felix_Klein#cite_note-3) He attended the [Gymnasium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gymnasium_(school) "Gymnasium (school)") in Düsseldorf, then studied mathematics and physics at the [University of Bonn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Bonn "University of Bonn"),[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Felix_Klein#cite_note-4) 1865–1866, intending to become a physicist. At that time, [Julius Plücker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Julius_Pl%C3%BCcker "Julius Plücker") had Bonn's professorship of mathematics and experimental physics, but by the time Klein became his assistant, in 1866, Plücker's interest was mainly geometry. Klein received his doctorate, supervised by Plücker, from the University of Bonn in 1868.

Plücker died in 1868, leaving his book concerning the basis of [line geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_geometry "Line geometry") incomplete. Klein was the obvious person to complete the second part of Plücker's _Neue Geometrie des Raumes_, and thus became acquainted with [Alfred Clebsch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alfred_Clebsch "Alfred Clebsch"), who had relocated to Göttingen in 1868. Klein visited Clebsch the next year, along with visits to [Berlin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berlin "Berlin") and Paris. In July 1870, at the beginning of the [Franco-Prussian War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Franco-Prussian_War "Franco-Prussian War"), he was in Paris and had to leave the country. For a brief time he served as a medical orderly in the [Prussian army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prussian_army "Prussian army") before being appointed _Privatdozent_ (lecturer) at Göttingen in early 1871.

The [University of Erlangen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Erlangen%E2%80%93Nuremberg "University of Erlangen–Nuremberg") appointed Klein professor in 1872, when he was only 23 years old.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Felix_Klein#cite_note-5) For this, he was endorsed by Clebsch, who regarded him as likely to become the best mathematician of his time. Klein did not wish to remain in Erlangen, where there were very few students, and was pleased to be offered a professorship at the [Technische Hochschule München](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Technical_University_of_Munich "Technical University of Munich") in 1875. There he and [Alexander von Brill](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_von_Brill "Alexander von Brill") taught advanced courses to many excellent students, including [Adolf Hurwitz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adolf_Hurwitz "Adolf Hurwitz"), [Walther von Dyck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Walther_von_Dyck "Walther von Dyck"), [Karl Rohn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karl_Rohn "Karl Rohn"), [Carl Runge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Runge "Carl Runge"), [Max Planck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Max_Planck "Max Planck"), [Luigi Bianchi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luigi_Bianchi "Luigi Bianchi"), and [Gregorio Ricci-Curbastro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gregorio_Ricci-Curbastro "Gregorio Ricci-Curbastro").

In 1875, Klein married Anne Hegel, granddaughter of the philosopher [Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georg_Wilhelm_Friedrich_Hegel "Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Felix_Klein#cite_note-6)

After spending five years at the Technische Hochschule, Klein was appointed to a chair of [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry") at [Leipzig University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leipzig_University "Leipzig University"). His colleagues included [Walther von Dyck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Walther_von_Dyck "Walther von Dyck"), Rohn, [Eduard Study](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eduard_Study "Eduard Study") and [Friedrich Engel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Engel_(mathematician) "Friedrich Engel (mathematician)"). Klein's years at Leipzig, 1880 to 1886, fundamentally changed his life. In 1882, his health collapsed and he battled with depression for the next two years.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Felix_Klein#cite_note-7) Nevertheless, his research continued; his seminal work on hyperelliptic sigma functions, published between 1886 and 1888, dates from around this period.

Klein (1912). Painting of [Max Liebermann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Max_Liebermann "Max Liebermann").

Klein accepted a professorship at the [University of Göttingen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_G%C3%B6ttingen "University of Göttingen") in 1886. From then on, until his 1913 retirement, he sought to re-establish Göttingen as the world's prime center for mathematics research. However, he never managed to transfer from Leipzig to Göttingen his own leading role as developer of [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry"). He taught a variety of courses at Göttingen, mainly concerning the interface between mathematics and physics, in particular, [mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanics "Mechanics") and [potential theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potential_theory "Potential theory").

The research facility Klein established at Göttingen served as model for the best such facilities throughout the world. He introduced weekly discussion meetings, and created a mathematical reading room and library. In 1895, Klein recruited [David Hilbert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Hilbert "David Hilbert") from the [University of Königsberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_K%C3%B6nigsberg "University of Königsberg"). This appointment proved of great importance; Hilbert continued to enhance Göttingen's primacy in mathematics until his own retirement in 1932. Klein and Hilbert jointly invited [Emmy Noether](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emmy_Noether "Emmy Noether") to Göttingen in 1915 where she introduced Einstein to group theory and the relationship between symmetries and conservation principles.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Felix_Klein#cite_note-8)

Under Klein's editorship, _[Mathematische Annalen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematische\_Annalen "Mathematische Annalen")_ became one of the best mathematical journals in the world. Founded by Clebsch, it grew under Klein's management, to rival, and eventually surpass _[Crelle's Journal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crelle%27s\_Journal "Crelle's Journal")_, based at the [University of Berlin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Berlin "University of Berlin"). Klein established a small team of editors who met regularly, making decisions in a democratic spirit. The journal first specialized in [complex analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_analysis "Complex analysis"), [algebraic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_geometry "Algebraic geometry"), and [invariant theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invariant_theory "Invariant theory"). It also provided an important outlet for [real analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_analysis "Real analysis") and the new [group theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_theory "Group theory").

In 1893, Klein was a major speaker at the International Mathematical Congress held in Chicago as part of the [World's Columbian Exposition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World%27s_Columbian_Exposition "World's Columbian Exposition").[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Felix_Klein#cite_note-9) Due partly to Klein's efforts, Göttingen began admitting women in 1893. He supervised the first Ph.D. thesis in mathematics written at Göttingen by a woman, by [Grace Chisholm Young](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grace_Chisholm_Young "Grace Chisholm Young"), an English student of [Arthur Cayley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arthur_Cayley "Arthur Cayley")'s, whom Klein admired. In 1897, Klein became a foreign member of the [Royal Netherlands Academy of Arts and Sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Netherlands_Academy_of_Arts_and_Sciences "Royal Netherlands Academy of Arts and Sciences").[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Felix_Klein#cite_note-10)

Around 1900, Klein began to become interested in mathematical instruction in schools. In 1905, he was instrumental in formulating a plan recommending that [analytic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytic_geometry "Analytic geometry"), the rudiments of differential and integral [calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus "Calculus"), and the [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)") concept be taught in secondary schools.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Felix_Klein#cite_note-11)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Felix_Klein#cite_note-12) This recommendation was gradually implemented in many countries around the world. In 1908, Klein was elected president of the [International Commission on Mathematical Instruction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Commission_on_Mathematical_Instruction "International Commission on Mathematical Instruction") at the Rome [International Congress of Mathematicians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Congress_of_Mathematicians "International Congress of Mathematicians").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Felix_Klein#cite_note-13) Under his guidance, the German part of the Commission published many volumes on the teaching of mathematics at all levels in Germany. In 1892 the [Manchester Literary and Philosophical Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manchester_Literary_and_Philosophical_Society "Manchester Literary and Philosophical Society") awarded Klein Honorary membership of the Society[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Felix_Klein#cite_note-14). The [London Mathematical Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/London_Mathematical_Society "London Mathematical Society") awarded Klein its [De Morgan Medal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_Morgan_Medal "De Morgan Medal") in 1893. He was elected a member of the [Royal Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Society "Royal Society") in 1885, and was awarded its [Copley Medal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Copley_Medal "Copley Medal") in 1912. He retired the following year due to ill health, but continued to teach mathematics at his home for several further years.

Klein was one of ninety-three signatories of the [Manifesto of the Ninety-Three](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manifesto_of_the_Ninety-Three "Manifesto of the Ninety-Three"), a document penned in support of the German invasion of Belgium in the early stages of [World War I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_I "World War I").

He died in Göttingen in 1925.

[![Image 4](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/87/Mobius_to_Klein.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Mobius_to_Klein.gif)

Construction of a Klein Bottle from two Möbius strips

Klein's dissertation, on [line geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_geometry "Line geometry") and its applications to [mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanics "Mechanics"), classified second degree line complexes using [Weierstrass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weierstrass "Weierstrass")'s theory of elementary divisors.

Klein's first important mathematical discoveries were made in 1870. In collaboration with [Sophus Lie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sophus_Lie "Sophus Lie"), he discovered the fundamental properties of the asymptotic lines on the [Kummer surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kummer_surface "Kummer surface"). They later investigated [W-curves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/W-curve "W-curve"), curves invariant under a group of [projective transformations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_transformation "Projective transformation"). It was Lie who introduced Klein to the concept of group, which was to have a major role in his later work. Klein also learned about groups from [Camille Jordan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camille_Jordan "Camille Jordan").[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Felix_Klein#cite_note-15)

Klein devised the "[Klein bottle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Klein_bottle "Klein bottle")" named after him, a one-sided closed surface which cannot be embedded in three-dimensional [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space"), but it may be immersed as a cylinder looped back through itself to join with its other end from the "inside". It may be embedded in the Euclidean space of dimensions 4 and higher. The concept of a Klein Bottle was devised as a 3-Dimensional [Möbius strip](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M%C3%B6bius_strip "Möbius strip"), with one method of construction being the attachment of the edges of two [Möbius strips](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M%C3%B6bius_strip "Möbius strip").[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Felix_Klein#cite_note-16)

During the 1890s, Klein began studying [mathematical physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_physics "Mathematical physics") more intensively, writing on the [gyroscope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gyroscope "Gyroscope") with [Arnold Sommerfeld](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arnold_Sommerfeld "Arnold Sommerfeld").[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Felix_Klein#cite_note-17) During 1894, he initiated the idea of an encyclopedia of mathematics including its applications, which became the [_Encyklopädie der mathematischen Wissenschaften_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Klein%27s_Encyclopedia_of_Mathematical_Sciences "Klein's Encyclopedia of Mathematical Sciences"). This enterprise, which endured until 1935, provided an important standard reference of enduring value.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Felix_Klein#cite_note-18)

[![Image 5](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/Non-euclidean-geometry-model.svg/330px-Non-euclidean-geometry-model.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Non-euclidean-geometry-model.svg)

Non-Euclidean geometry models proposed by Klein (left) and Poincaré (right)

In 1871, while at Göttingen, Klein made major discoveries in geometry. He published two papers _On the So-called Non-Euclidean Geometry_ showing that Euclidean and non-Euclidean geometries could be considered [metric spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space "Metric space") determined by a [Cayley–Klein metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cayley%E2%80%93Klein_metric "Cayley–Klein metric"). This insight had the corollary that [non-Euclidean geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-Euclidean_geometry "Non-Euclidean geometry") was consistent if and only if [Euclidean geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_geometry "Euclidean geometry") was, giving the same status to geometries Euclidean and non-Euclidean, and ending all controversy about non-Euclidean geometry. [Arthur Cayley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arthur_Cayley "Arthur Cayley") never accepted Klein's argument, believing it to be circular.

Klein's synthesis of [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry") as the study of the properties of a space that is invariant under a given [group of transformations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transformation_group "Transformation group"), known as the _[Erlangen program](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erlangen\_program "Erlangen program")_ (1872), profoundly influenced the evolution of mathematics. This program was initiated by Klein's inaugural lecture as professor at Erlangen, although it was not the actual speech he gave on the occasion. The program proposed a unified system of geometry that has become the accepted modern method. Klein showed how the essential properties of a given geometry could be represented by the group of [transformations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transformation_group "Transformation group") that preserve those properties. Thus the program's definition of geometry encompassed both Euclidean and non-Euclidean geometry.

Currently, the significance of Klein's contributions to geometry is evident. They have become so much part of mathematical thinking that it is difficult to appreciate their novelty when first presented, and understand the fact that they were not immediately accepted by all his contemporaries.

Klein saw his work on [complex analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_analysis "Complex analysis") as his major contribution to mathematics, specifically his work on:

*   The link between certain ideas of [Riemann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bernhard_Riemann "Bernhard Riemann") and [invariant theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invariant_theory "Invariant theory"),
*   [Number theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_theory "Number theory") and [abstract algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abstract_algebra "Abstract algebra");
*   [Group theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_theory "Group theory");
*   [Geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry") in more than 3 dimensions and [differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_equations "Differential equations"), especially equations he invented, satisfied by [elliptic modular functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptic_modular_function "Elliptic modular function") and [automorphic functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Automorphic_function "Automorphic function").

Klein showed that the [modular group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modular_group "Modular group") moves the fundamental region of the [complex plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_plane "Complex plane") so as to [tessellate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tessellation "Tessellation") the plane. In 1879, he examined the action of [PSL(2,7)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PSL(2,7) "PSL(2,7)"), considered as an image of the [modular group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modular_group "Modular group"), and obtained an explicit representation of a [Riemann surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_surface "Riemann surface") now termed the [Klein quartic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Klein_quartic "Klein quartic"). He showed that it was a complex curve in [projective space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_space "Projective space"), that its equation was _x_ 3 _y_+_y_ 3 _z_+_z_ 3 _x_=0, and that its group of [symmetries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry_group "Symmetry group") was [PSL(2,7)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PSL(2,7) "PSL(2,7)") of [order](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Order_(group_theory) "Order (group theory)") 168. His _Ueber Riemann's Theorie der algebraischen Funktionen und ihre Integrale_ (1882) treats complex analysis in a geometric way, connecting [potential theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potential_theory "Potential theory") and [conformal mappings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conformal_mapping "Conformal mapping"). This work drew on notions from [fluid dynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluid_dynamics "Fluid dynamics").

Klein considered equations of degree > 4, and was especially interested in using transcendental methods to solve the general equation of the fifth degree. Building on methods of [Charles Hermite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Hermite "Charles Hermite") and [Leopold Kronecker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leopold_Kronecker "Leopold Kronecker"), he produced similar results to those of Brioschi and later completely solved the problem by means of the [icosahedral group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Icosahedral_group "Icosahedral group"). This work enabled him to write a series of papers on [elliptic modular functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptic_modular_function "Elliptic modular function").

In his 1884 book on the [icosahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Icosahedron "Icosahedron"), Klein established a theory of [automorphic functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Automorphic_function "Automorphic function"), associating algebra and geometry. [Poincaré](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Poincar%C3%A9 "Henri Poincaré") had published an outline of his theory of automorphic functions in 1881, which resulted in a friendly rivalry between the two men. Both sought to state and prove a grand [uniformization theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uniformization_theorem "Uniformization theorem") that would establish the new theory more completely. Klein succeeded in formulating such a theorem and in describing a strategy for proving it. He came up with his proof during an [asthma attack](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asthma_attack "Asthma attack") at 2:30 A.M. on 23 March 1882.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Felix_Klein#cite_note-19)

Klein summarized his work on [automorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Automorphic_function "Automorphic function") and [elliptic modular functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptic_modular_function "Elliptic modular function") in a four volume treatise, written with [Robert Fricke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Fricke "Robert Fricke") over a period of about 20 years.
