[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Alembert.jpg/250px-Alembert.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Alembert.jpg)

[Jean d'Alembert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_d%27Alembert "Jean d'Alembert") (1717–1783)

**D'Alembert's principle**, also known as the **Lagrange–d'Alembert principle**, is a statement of the fundamental [classical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_physics "Classical physics") laws of motion. It is named after its discoverer, the French physicist and mathematician [Jean le Rond d'Alembert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_le_Rond_d%27Alembert "Jean le Rond d'Alembert"), and Italian-French mathematician [Joseph Louis Lagrange](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Louis_Lagrange "Joseph Louis Lagrange"). D'Alembert's principle generalizes the [principle of virtual work](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principle_of_virtual_work "Principle of virtual work") from [static](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statics "Statics") to [dynamical systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynamical_system "Dynamical system") by introducing _forces of inertia_ which, when added to the applied forces in a system, result in _dynamic equilibrium_.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D'Alembert's_principle#cite_note-Lanczos-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D'Alembert's_principle#cite_note-2)

D'Alembert's principle can be applied in cases of [kinematic constraints](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nonholonomic_constraint "Nonholonomic constraint") that depend on velocities.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D'Alembert's_principle#cite_note-Lanczos-1): 92  The principle does not apply for irreversible displacements, such as sliding [friction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friction "Friction"), and more general specification of the irreversibility is required.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D'Alembert's_principle#cite_note-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D'Alembert's_principle#cite_note-Gay2018-4)

Statement of the principle
--------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=D%27Alembert%27s_principle&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Statement of the principle")]

The principle states that the sum of the differences between the [forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Force "Force") acting on a system of massive particles and the time [derivatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative "Derivative") of the [momenta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum "Momentum") of the system itself projected onto any [virtual displacement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virtual_displacement "Virtual displacement") consistent with the constraints of the system is zero.[_[clarification needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Please\_clarify "Wikipedia:Please clarify")_] Thus, in mathematical notation, d'Alembert's principle is written as follows, ![Image 3: {\displaystyle \sum _{i}(\mathbf {F} _{i}-m_{i}{\dot {\mathbf {v} }}_{i}-{\dot {m}}_{i}\mathbf {v} _{i})\cdot \delta \mathbf {r} _{i}=0,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9768b0ed9f171a272faaae341a448b5ac083e77d)

where:

Newton's dot notation is used to represent the derivative with respect to time. The above equation is often called d'Alembert's principle, but it was first written in this variational form by [Joseph Louis Lagrange](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Louis_Lagrange "Joseph Louis Lagrange").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D'Alembert's_principle#cite_note-5) D'Alembert's contribution was to demonstrate that in the totality of a dynamic system the forces of constraint vanish. That is to say that the [generalized forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generalized_forces "Generalized forces")![Image 4: {\displaystyle \mathbf {Q} _{j}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f00b18e86dfa7c5aab74007ab36cca660a8ba6a7) need not include constraint forces. It is equivalent to the somewhat more cumbersome [Gauss's principle of least constraint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauss%27s_principle_of_least_constraint "Gauss's principle of least constraint").

### General case with variable mass

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=D%27Alembert%27s_principle&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: General case with variable mass")]

The general statement of d'Alembert's principle mentions "the time [derivatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative "Derivative") of the [momenta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum "Momentum") of the system." By Newton's second law, the first time derivative of momentum is the force. The momentum of the ![Image 5: {\displaystyle i}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/add78d8608ad86e54951b8c8bd6c8d8416533d20)-th mass is the product of its mass and velocity: ![Image 6: {\displaystyle \mathbf {p} _{i}=m_{i}\mathbf {v} _{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c28e54c5e37ede6999487907d71ee8499d0d69ef) and its time derivative is ![Image 7: {\displaystyle {\dot {\mathbf {p} }}_{i}={\dot {m}}_{i}\mathbf {v} _{i}+m_{i}{\dot {\mathbf {v} }}_{i}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0e6a356b02a56fc5185e4983aada84d78f41b7aa)

In many applications, the masses are constant and this equation reduces to ![Image 8: {\displaystyle {\dot {\mathbf {p} }}_{i}=m_{i}{\dot {\mathbf {v} }}_{i}=m_{i}\mathbf {a} _{i}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5eb05773b08f65e4049877a902ae0d3e6aa5f93a)

However, some applications involve changing masses (for example, chains being rolled up or being unrolled) and in those cases both terms ![Image 9: {\displaystyle {\dot {m}}_{i}\mathbf {v} _{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/88c1fc364c60fd8ce080b4be91d32c6f797d5859) and ![Image 10: {\displaystyle m_{i}{\dot {\mathbf {v} }}_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f8a22ada9b8435eecce5f9767cbf03282cab1199) have to remain present, giving[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D'Alembert's_principle#cite_note-6)![Image 11: {\displaystyle \sum _{i}(\mathbf {F} _{i}-m_{i}\mathbf {a} _{i}-{\dot {m}}_{i}\mathbf {v} _{i})\cdot \delta \mathbf {r} _{i}=0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4401c427486a5243eadb96b396cfdeb89444e879) If the variable mass is ejected with a velocity ![Image 12: {\displaystyle \mathbf {w} _{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/738edcf12148b7bd3c07ba5459be825cb6089aa1) the principle has an additional term:[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D'Alembert's_principle#cite_note-7)![Image 13: {\displaystyle \sum _{i}(\mathbf {F} _{i}-m_{i}\mathbf {a} _{i}-{\dot {m}}_{i}(\mathbf {v} _{i}-\mathbf {w} _{i}))\cdot \delta \mathbf {r} _{i}=0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b256316d677792387aabb7955a14e0f2f5c3e0eb)

### Special case with constant mass

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=D%27Alembert%27s_principle&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Special case with constant mass")]

Consider Newton's law for a system of particles of constant mass, ![Image 14: {\displaystyle i}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/add78d8608ad86e54951b8c8bd6c8d8416533d20). The total force on each particle is[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D'Alembert's_principle#cite_note-Torby1984-8)![Image 15: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} _{i}^{(T)}=m_{i}\mathbf {a} _{i},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/72b2353d5ebe23047b42092f1359460d8d9b6f47) where

*   ![Image 16: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} _{i}^{(T)}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b2c93a58c3b628bcf0e429f2fd20172827662bad) are the total forces acting on the system's particles,
*   ![Image 17: {\displaystyle m_{i}\mathbf {a} _{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fb0b7015885937d38b6ab4fec236ecd571657906) are the inertial forces that result from the total forces.

Moving the inertial forces to the left gives an expression that can be considered to represent quasi-static equilibrium, but which is really just a small algebraic manipulation of Newton's law:[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D'Alembert's_principle#cite_note-Torby1984-8)![Image 18: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} _{i}^{(T)}-m_{i}\mathbf {a} _{i}=\mathbf {0} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/16f16df19142f0acd1e09b4fbe28ccb0f2d52f3a)

Considering the [virtual work](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virtual_work "Virtual work"), ![Image 19: {\displaystyle \delta W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/973e6b089758466f338610e76ab50fd4093efbc8), done by the total and inertial forces together through an arbitrary virtual displacement, ![Image 20: {\displaystyle \delta \mathbf {r} _{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a56ef713418eb51bfcd06f4833292c71b063af42), of the system leads to a zero identity, since the forces involved sum to zero for each particle.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D'Alembert's_principle#cite_note-Torby1984-8)

![Image 21: {\displaystyle \delta W=\sum _{i}\mathbf {F} _{i}^{(T)}\cdot \delta \mathbf {r} _{i}-\sum _{i}m_{i}\mathbf {a} _{i}\cdot \delta \mathbf {r} _{i}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b42995b6dd8da9e115f96d40a6b37dd7376c243)

The original vector equation could be recovered by recognizing that the work expression must hold for arbitrary displacements. Separating the total forces into applied forces, ![Image 22: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} _{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5d49039f831a156e0c33270962d8d159d553a8bc), and constraint forces, ![Image 23: {\displaystyle \mathbf {C} _{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/61e9b87195beeab6d53f1debbc855c8d7b9e2d1f), yields[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D'Alembert's_principle#cite_note-Torby1984-8)![Image 24: {\displaystyle \delta W=\sum _{i}\mathbf {F} _{i}\cdot \delta \mathbf {r} _{i}+\sum _{i}\mathbf {C} _{i}\cdot \delta \mathbf {r} _{i}-\sum _{i}m_{i}\mathbf {a} _{i}\cdot \delta \mathbf {r} _{i}=0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c8582ce07595a08ee9c96995f16f98737854536d)

If arbitrary virtual displacements are assumed to be in directions that are orthogonal to the constraint forces (which is not usually the case, so this derivation works only for special cases), the constraint forces don't do any work, ![Image 25: {\textstyle \sum _{i}\mathbf {C} _{i}\cdot \delta \mathbf {r} _{i}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/090e3604b0d18a75602cfe65b89894c5916d47b6). Such displacements are said to be _consistent_ with the constraints.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D'Alembert's_principle#cite_note-9) This leads to the formulation of _d'Alembert's principle_, which states that the difference of applied forces and inertial forces for a dynamic system does no virtual work:[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D'Alembert's_principle#cite_note-Torby1984-8)![Image 26: {\displaystyle \delta W=\sum _{i}(\mathbf {F} _{i}-m_{i}\mathbf {a} _{i})\cdot \delta \mathbf {r} _{i}=0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dca52a98f5f9d2cfc052d876cb859414de5f9b26)

There is also a corresponding principle for static systems called the [principle of virtual work for applied forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virtual_work#Principle_of_virtual_work_for_applied_forces "Virtual work").

D'Alembert's principle of inertial forces
-----------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=D%27Alembert%27s_principle&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: D'Alembert's principle of inertial forces")]

D'Alembert showed that one can transform an accelerating rigid body into an equivalent static system by adding the so-called "[inertial force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inertial_force "Inertial force")" and "inertial torque" or moment. The inertial force must act through the center of mass and the inertial torque can act anywhere. The system can then be analyzed exactly as a static system subjected to this "inertial force and moment" and the external forces. The advantage is that in the equivalent static system one can take moments about any point (not just the center of mass). This often leads to simpler calculations because any force (in turn) can be eliminated from the moment equations by choosing the appropriate point about which to apply the moment equation (sum of moments = zero). Even in the course of Fundamentals of Dynamics and Kinematics of machines, this principle helps in analyzing the forces that act on a link of a mechanism when it is in motion. In textbooks of engineering dynamics, this is sometimes referred to as _d'Alembert's principle_.

Some educators caution that attempts to use d'Alembert inertial mechanics lead students to make frequent sign errors.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D'Alembert's_principle#cite_note-:0-10) A potential cause for these errors is the sign of the [inertial forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inertial_Force "Inertial Force"). Inertial forces can be used to describe an apparent force in a [non-inertial reference frame](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-inertial_reference_frame "Non-inertial reference frame") that has an acceleration ![Image 27: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1a957216653a9ee0d0133dcefd13fb75e36b8b9d) with respect to an [inertial reference frame](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inertial_reference_frame "Inertial reference frame"). In such a non-inertial reference frame, a mass that is at rest and has zero acceleration in an inertial reference system, because no forces are acting on it, will still have an acceleration ![Image 28: {\displaystyle -\mathbf {a} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2a3cf31849160dbc62a43fc0f601083f526edac1) and an apparent inertial, or pseudo or [fictitious force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fictitious_force "Fictitious force")![Image 29: {\displaystyle -m\mathbf {a} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/040f0b07b224d2cf09bad79500239ad901b15597) will seem to act on it: in this situation the inertial force has a minus sign.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D'Alembert's_principle#cite_note-:0-10)

Dynamic equilibrium
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=D%27Alembert%27s_principle&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Dynamic equilibrium")]

D'Alembert's form of the principle of virtual work states that a system of rigid bodies is in dynamic equilibrium when the virtual work of the sum of the applied forces and the inertial forces is zero for any virtual displacement of the system. Thus, dynamic equilibrium of a system of ![Image 30: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) rigid bodies with ![Image 31: {\displaystyle m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a07d98bb302f3856cbabc47b2b9016692e3f7bc) generalized coordinates requires ![Image 32: {\displaystyle \delta W=\left(Q_{1}+Q_{1}^{*}\right)\delta q_{1}+\dots +\left(Q_{m}+Q_{m}^{*}\right)\delta q_{m}=0,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7bd5c1b4fe5c2de2649857b9721d370261a23b38) for any set of virtual displacements ![Image 33: {\displaystyle \delta q_{j}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/441a20906f8cb69dffa8848cd7593c525cfc9a8c) with ![Image 34: {\displaystyle Q_{j}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5db0ad37e47589c5a9f270ca9c06affdacd6c66f) being a [generalized applied force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generalized_forces#D'Alembert's_principle "Generalized forces") and ![Image 35: {\displaystyle Q_{j}^{*}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9a551182a25fb29d5afdae30915c7f02d6271aa7) being a generalized inertia force. This condition yields ![Image 36: {\displaystyle m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a07d98bb302f3856cbabc47b2b9016692e3f7bc) equations, ![Image 37: {\displaystyle Q_{j}+Q_{j}^{*}=0,\quad j=1,\ldots ,m,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/abdc1026adbba50c42e5d87d85441336a6f2d9f4) which can also be written as ![Image 38: {\displaystyle {\frac {d}{dt}}{\frac {\partial T}{\partial {\dot {q}}_{j}}}-{\frac {\partial T}{\partial q_{j}}}=Q_{j},\quad j=1,\ldots ,m.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/588bfb13eedb4ccb044296df1df54a9a409cfb53) The result is a set of m equations of motion that define the dynamics of the rigid body system.

Formulation using the Lagrangian
--------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=D%27Alembert%27s_principle&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Formulation using the Lagrangian")]

D'Alembert's principle can be rewritten in terms of the Lagrangian ![Image 39: {\displaystyle L=T-V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/00644a2f8d4ddaa3b1ae36d472aabaeb63e4d9dc) of the system as a generalized version of [Hamilton's principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamilton%27s_principle "Hamilton's principle") for the case of point particles, as follows, ![Image 40: {\displaystyle \delta \int _{t_{1}}^{t_{2}}L(\mathbf {r} ,{\dot {\mathbf {r} }},t)dt+\sum _{i}\int _{t_{1}}^{t_{2}}\mathbf {F} _{i}\cdot \delta \mathbf {r} _{i}dt=0,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3f53d8aa945c97894bb85fa48d602342b4703086) where:

*   ![Image 41: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} =(\mathbf {r} _{1},...,\mathbf {r} _{N})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a18dda81ea83087fcd9ee3deb6b5880512e484af)

*   ![Image 42: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} _{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5d49039f831a156e0c33270962d8d159d553a8bc) are the applied forces

With the Lagrangian ![Image 43: {\displaystyle L(\mathbf {r} ,{\dot {\mathbf {r} }},t)=\sum _{i}{\frac {1}{2}}m_{i}{\dot {\mathbf {r} }}_{i}^{2},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4c704294dcd87e616d63f47d618ab2e609589cfb) the previous statement of d'Alembert principle is recovered.

Generalization for thermodynamics
---------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=D%27Alembert%27s_principle&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Generalization for thermodynamics")]

An extension of d'Alembert's principle can be used in thermodynamics.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D'Alembert's_principle#cite_note-Gay2018-4) For instance, for an adiabatically closed [thermodynamic system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamic_system "Thermodynamic system") described by a Lagrangian depending on a single entropy _S_ and with constant masses ![Image 44: {\displaystyle m_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/95ec8e804f69706d3f5ad235f4f983220c8df7c2), such as ![Image 45: {\displaystyle L(\mathbf {r} ,{\dot {\mathbf {r} }},S,t)=\sum _{i}{\frac {1}{2}}m_{i}{\dot {\mathbf {r} }}_{i}^{2}-V(\mathbf {r} ,S),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cda9a00e970b369709ae9939b1ba0550974103b7) it is written as follows ![Image 46: {\displaystyle \delta \int _{t_{1}}^{t_{2}}L(\mathbf {r} ,{\dot {\mathbf {r} }},S,t)dt+\sum _{i}\int _{t_{1}}^{t_{2}}\mathbf {F} _{i}\cdot \delta \mathbf {r} _{i}dt=0,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f9dcf1873575ffc79da7a75f4ceb81a9ca8b5210) where the previous constraints ![Image 47: {\textstyle \sum _{i}\mathbf {C} _{i}\cdot \delta \mathbf {r} _{i}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/090e3604b0d18a75602cfe65b89894c5916d47b6) and ![Image 48: {\textstyle \sum _{i}\mathbf {C} _{i}\cdot {\dot {\mathbf {r} }}_{i}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/12d25943759198352b4fc3a9706ccc4d363fdac4) are generalized to involve the entropy as:

*   ![Image 49: {\displaystyle \sum _{i}\mathbf {C} _{i}\cdot \delta \mathbf {r} _{i}+T\delta S=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bccda4ea8b30f8ac58214f071b00ed88e5e51eae)
*   ![Image 50: {\displaystyle \sum _{i}\mathbf {C} _{i}\cdot {\dot {\mathbf {r} }}_{i}+T{\dot {S}}=0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1d563397c94d74353ff890e3b7e3e1c260f6a614)

Here ![Image 51: {\displaystyle T=\partial V/\partial S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0cc6c2cd0b54cf080bed8094a1f9c64415b1fa4e) is the temperature of the system, ![Image 52: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} _{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5d49039f831a156e0c33270962d8d159d553a8bc) are the external forces, ![Image 53: {\displaystyle \mathbf {C} _{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/61e9b87195beeab6d53f1debbc855c8d7b9e2d1f) are the internal dissipative forces. It results in the mechanical and thermal balance equations:[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D'Alembert's_principle#cite_note-Gay2018-4)![Image 54: {\displaystyle m_{i}\mathbf {a} _{i}=-{\frac {\partial V}{\partial \mathbf {r} _{i}}}+\mathbf {C} _{i}+\mathbf {F} _{i},\;\;i=1,...,N\qquad \qquad T{\dot {S}}=-\sum _{i}\mathbf {C} _{i}\cdot {\dot {\mathbf {r} }}_{i}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38f549c65268bc46d74743722363e2f4d3c2bee2) Typical applications of the principle include thermo-mechanical systems, membrane transport, and chemical reactions.

For ![Image 55: {\displaystyle \delta S={\dot {S}}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/db451cade3308f2063a6320943828144398ec027) the classical d'Alembert principle and equations are recovered.
