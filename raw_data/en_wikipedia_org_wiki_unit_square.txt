From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Square with side length one

Not to be confused with [Square (unit)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_(unit) "Square (unit)").

[![Image 5](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Unit_Square.svg/330px-Unit_Square.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Unit_Square.svg)

The unit square in the [real plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_geometry "Euclidean geometry")

In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), a **unit square** is a [square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_(geometry) "Square (geometry)") whose sides have length 1. Often, _the_ unit square refers specifically to the square in the [Cartesian plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system#Cartesian_coordinates_in_two_dimensions "Cartesian coordinate system") with corners at the four points (0, 0), (1, 0), (0, 1), and (1, 1).[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_square#cite_note-1)

Cartesian coordinates
---------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Unit_square&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Cartesian coordinates")]

In a [Cartesian coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system "Cartesian coordinate system") with coordinates (_x_, _y_), a unit square is defined as a [square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_(geometry) "Square (geometry)") consisting of the points where both x and y lie in a closed [unit interval](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_interval "Unit interval") from 0 to 1.

That is, a unit square is the [Cartesian product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_product "Cartesian product")_I_ × _I_, where I denotes the closed unit interval.

Complex coordinates
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Unit_square&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Complex coordinates")]

The unit square can also be thought of as a subset of the [complex plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_plane "Complex plane"), the topological space formed by the [complex numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number"). In this view, the four corners of the unit square are at the four complex numbers 0, 1, i, and 1 + _i_.

Rational distance problem
-------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Unit_square&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Rational distance problem")]

Unsolved problem in mathematics

Is there a point in the plane at a rational distance from all four corners of a unit square?

[More unsolved problems in mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_unsolved_problems_in_mathematics "List of unsolved problems in mathematics")

It is not known whether any point in the plane is a [rational](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_number "Rational number") distance from all four vertices of the unit square.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_square#cite_note-2)
