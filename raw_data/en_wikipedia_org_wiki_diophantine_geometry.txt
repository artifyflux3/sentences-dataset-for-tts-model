In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), **Diophantine geometry** is the study of [Diophantine equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diophantine_equation "Diophantine equation") by means of powerful methods in [algebraic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_geometry "Algebraic geometry"). By the 20th century it became clear for some mathematicians that methods of algebraic geometry are ideal tools to study these equations.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diophantine_geometry#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHindrySilverman2000viiPreface-1) Diophantine geometry is part of the broader field of [arithmetic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic_geometry "Arithmetic geometry").

Four [theorems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theorem "Theorem") in Diophantine geometry that are of fundamental importance include:[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diophantine_geometry#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHindrySilverman2000viiiPreface-2)

*   [Mordell–Weil theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mordell%E2%80%93Weil_theorem "Mordell–Weil theorem")
*   [Roth's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roth%27s_theorem "Roth's theorem")
*   [Siegel's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siegel%27s_theorem_on_integral_points "Siegel's theorem on integral points")
*   [Faltings's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Faltings%27s_theorem "Faltings's theorem")

Background
----------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Diophantine_geometry&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Background")]

[Serge Lang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Serge_Lang "Serge Lang") published a book _Diophantine Geometry_ in the area in 1962, and by this book he coined the term "Diophantine geometry".[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diophantine_geometry#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHindrySilverman2000viiPreface-1) The traditional arrangement of material on Diophantine equations was by [degree](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_of_a_polynomial "Degree of a polynomial") and number of [variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variable_(mathematics) "Variable (mathematics)"), as in [Mordell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis_Mordell "Louis Mordell")'s _Diophantine Equations_ (1969). Mordell's book starts with a remark on [homogeneous equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homogeneous_polynomial "Homogeneous polynomial")_f_ = 0 over the [rational field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_field "Rational field"), attributed to [C. F. Gauss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C._F._Gauss "C. F. Gauss"), that non-zero solutions in [integers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer") (even primitive lattice points) exist if non-zero rational solutions do, and notes a caveat of [L. E. Dickson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L._E._Dickson "L. E. Dickson"), which is about parametric solutions.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diophantine_geometry#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMordell19691-3) The [Hilbert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert "Hilbert")–[Hurwitz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adolf_Hurwitz "Adolf Hurwitz") result from 1890 reducing the Diophantine geometry of curves of genus 0 to degrees 1 and 2 ([conic sections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conic_section "Conic section")) occurs in Chapter 17, as does [Mordell's conjecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mordell%27s_conjecture "Mordell's conjecture"). [Siegel's theorem on integral points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siegel%27s_theorem_on_integral_points "Siegel's theorem on integral points") occurs in Chapter 28. [Mordell's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mordell%27s_theorem "Mordell's theorem") on the finite generation of the group of [rational points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_point "Rational point") on an [elliptic curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptic_curve "Elliptic curve") is in Chapter 16, and integer points on the [Mordell curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mordell_curve "Mordell curve") in Chapter 26.

In a hostile review of Lang's book, Mordell wrote:

> In recent times, powerful new geometric ideas and methods have been developed by means of which important new arithmetical theorems and related results have been found and proved and some of these are not easily proved otherwise. Further, there has been a tendency to clothe the old results, their extensions, and proofs in the new geometrical language. Sometimes, however, the full implications of results are best described in a geometrical setting. Lang has these aspects very much in mind in this book, and seems to miss no opportunity for geometric presentation. This accounts for his title "Diophantine Geometry."[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diophantine_geometry#cite_note-4)

He notes that the content of the book is largely versions of the [Mordell–Weil theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mordell%E2%80%93Weil_theorem "Mordell–Weil theorem"), [Thue–Siegel–Roth theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thue%E2%80%93Siegel%E2%80%93Roth_theorem "Thue–Siegel–Roth theorem"), Siegel's theorem, with a treatment of [Hilbert's irreducibility theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert%27s_irreducibility_theorem "Hilbert's irreducibility theorem") and applications (in the style of Siegel). Leaving aside issues of generality, and a completely different style, the major mathematical difference between the two books is that Lang used [abelian varieties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abelian_varieties "Abelian varieties") and offered a proof of Siegel's theorem, while Mordell noted that the proof "is of a very advanced character" (p.263).

Despite a bad press initially, Lang's conception has been sufficiently widely accepted for a 2006 tribute to call the book "visionary".[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diophantine_geometry#cite_note-5) A larger field sometimes called [arithmetic of abelian varieties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic_of_abelian_varieties "Arithmetic of abelian varieties") now includes Diophantine geometry along with [class field theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Class_field_theory "Class field theory"), [complex multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_multiplication "Complex multiplication"), [local zeta-functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_zeta-function "Local zeta-function") and [L-functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L-function "L-function").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diophantine_geometry#cite_note-6)[Paul Vojta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Vojta "Paul Vojta") wrote:

While others at the time shared this viewpoint (e.g., [Weil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Weil "André Weil"), [Tate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Tate_(mathematician) "John Tate (mathematician)"), [Serre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean-Pierre_Serre "Jean-Pierre Serre")), it is easy to forget that others did not, as Mordell's review of _Diophantine Geometry_ attests.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diophantine_geometry#cite_note-7)

Approaches
----------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Diophantine_geometry&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Approaches")]

A single equation defines a [hypersurface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypersurface "Hypersurface"), and [simultaneous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simultaneous_equations "Simultaneous equations") Diophantine equations give rise to a general [algebraic variety](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_variety "Algebraic variety")_V_ over _K_; the typical question is about the nature of the set _V_(_K_) of points on _V_ with co-ordinates in _K_, and by means of [height functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Height_function "Height function"), quantitative questions about the "size" of these solutions may be posed, as well as the qualitative issues of whether any points exist, and if so whether there are an infinite number. Given the geometric approach, the consideration of [homogeneous equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homogeneous_polynomial "Homogeneous polynomial") and [homogeneous co-ordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homogeneous_co-ordinates "Homogeneous co-ordinates") is fundamental, for the same reasons that [projective geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_geometry "Projective geometry") is the dominant approach in algebraic geometry. Rational number solutions therefore are the primary consideration; but integral solutions (i.e. [lattice points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lattice_point "Lattice point")) can be treated in the same way as an [affine variety](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_variety "Affine variety") may be considered inside a projective variety that has extra [points at infinity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Points_at_infinity "Points at infinity").

The general approach of Diophantine geometry is illustrated by [Faltings's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Faltings%27s_theorem "Faltings's theorem") (a conjecture of [L. J. Mordell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L._J._Mordell "L. J. Mordell")) stating that an [algebraic curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_curve "Algebraic curve")_C_ of [genus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genus_(curve) "Genus (curve)")_g_> 1 over the rational numbers has only finitely many [rational points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_point "Rational point"). The first result of this kind may have been the theorem of Hilbert and Hurwitz dealing with the case _g_ = 0. The theory consists both of theorems and many conjectures and open questions.
