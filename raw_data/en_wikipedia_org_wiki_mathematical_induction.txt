Mathematical induction can be informally illustrated by reference to the sequential effect of [falling dominoes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domino_toppling "Domino toppling").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_induction#cite_note-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_induction#cite_note-2)

**Mathematical induction** is a method for [proving](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_proof "Mathematical proof") that a statement ![Image 2: {\displaystyle P(n)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5e303d2c14cd399b6f52b468c9fd44a542bed422) is true for every [natural number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number "Natural number")![Image 3: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b), that is, that the infinitely many cases ![Image 4: {\displaystyle P(0),P(1),P(2),P(3),\dots }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b711489e1c1a6623f75d4cb531a06f8204cf65b) all hold. This is done by first proving a simple case, then also showing that if we assume the claim is true for a given case, then the next case is also true. Informal metaphors help to explain this technique, such as falling dominoes or climbing a ladder:

> Mathematical induction proves that we can climb as high as we like on a ladder, by proving that we can climb onto the bottom rung (the **basis**) and that from each rung we can climb up to the next one (the **step**).

A **proof by induction** consists of two cases. The first, the **base case**, proves the statement for ![Image 5: {\displaystyle n=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/26819344e55f5e671c76c07c18eb4291fcec85ae) without assuming any knowledge of other cases. The second case, the **induction step**, proves that _if_ the statement holds for any given case ![Image 6: {\displaystyle n=k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/809fa04eb15b435447fe64b3ca71f4feaac3d2c6), _then_ it must also hold for the next case ![Image 7: {\displaystyle n=k+1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/41a27ec53b870485ae4eda0335db05ecdfd933e1). These two steps establish that the statement holds for every natural number ![Image 8: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b). The base case does not necessarily begin with ![Image 9: {\displaystyle n=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/26819344e55f5e671c76c07c18eb4291fcec85ae), but often with ![Image 10: {\displaystyle n=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d9ec7e1edc2e6d98f5aec2a39ae5f1c99d1e1425), and possibly with any fixed natural number ![Image 11: {\displaystyle n=N}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/78fd6116383f2004f7dd59ff8a81ede1c2f6d3ae), establishing the truth of the statement for all natural numbers ![Image 12: {\displaystyle n\geq N}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b67a4f8e2ce89617f08316bfdcc6f33887b5629).

The method can be extended to prove statements about more general [well-founded](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Well-founded "Well-founded") structures, such as [trees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tree_(set_theory) "Tree (set theory)"); this generalization, known as [structural induction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Structural_induction "Structural induction"), is used in [mathematical logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_logic "Mathematical logic") and [computer science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_science "Computer science"). Mathematical induction in this extended sense is closely related to [recursion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recursion "Recursion"). Mathematical induction is an [inference rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inference_rule "Inference rule") used in [formal proofs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formal_proof "Formal proof"), and is the foundation of most [correctness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Correctness_(computer_science) "Correctness (computer science)") proofs for computer programs.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_induction#cite_note-3)

Despite its name, mathematical induction differs fundamentally from [inductive reasoning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inductive_reasoning "Inductive reasoning") as [used in philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Problem_of_induction "Problem of induction"), in which the examination of many cases results in a probable conclusion. The mathematical method examines infinitely many cases to prove a general statement, but it does so by a finite chain of [deductive reasoning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deductive_reasoning "Deductive reasoning") involving the [variable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variable_(mathematics) "Variable (mathematics)")![Image 13: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b), which can take infinitely many values. The result is a rigorous proof of the statement, not an assertion of its probability.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_induction#cite_note-4)

In 370 BC, [Plato](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plato "Plato")'s [Parmenides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parmenides_(dialogue) "Parmenides (dialogue)") may have contained traces of an early example of an implicit inductive proof,[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_induction#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAcerbi2000-5) however, the earliest implicit proof by mathematical induction was written by [al-Karaji](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Karaji "Al-Karaji") around 1000 AD, who applied it to [arithmetic sequences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic_progression "Arithmetic progression") to prove the [binomial theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binomial_theorem "Binomial theorem") and properties of [Pascal's triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pascal%27s_triangle "Pascal's triangle"). Whilst the original work was lost, it was later referenced by [Al-Samawal al-Maghribi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Samawal_al-Maghribi "Al-Samawal al-Maghribi") in his treatise _al-Bahir fi'l-jabr (The Brilliant in Algebra)_ in around 1150 AD.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_induction#cite_note-FOOTNOTERashed199462%E2%80%9384-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_induction#cite_note-7)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_induction#cite_note-8)

Katz says in his history of mathematics

> Another important idea introduced by al-Karaji and continued by al-Samaw'al and others was that of an inductive argument for dealing with certain arithmetic sequences. Thus al-Karaji used such an argument to prove the result on the sums of integral cubes already known to [Aryabhata](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aryabhata "Aryabhata") [...] Al-Karaji did not, however, state a general result for arbitrary _n_. He stated his theorem for the particular integer 10 [...] His proof, nevertheless, was clearly designed to be extendable to any other integer. [...] Al-Karaji's argument includes in essence the two basic components of a modern argument by induction, namely the [truth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Truth "Truth") of the statement for _n_ = 1 (1 = 1 3) and the deriving of the truth for _n_ = _k_ from that of _n_ = _k_ − 1. Of course, this second component is not explicit since, in some sense, al-Karaji's argument is in reverse; this is, he starts from _n_ = 10 and goes down to 1 rather than proceeding upward. Nevertheless, his argument in _al-Fakhri_ is the earliest extant proof of [the sum formula for integral cubes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Squared_triangular_number "Squared triangular number").[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_induction#cite_note-9)

In India, early implicit proofs by mathematical induction appear in [Bhaskara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bh%C4%81skara_II "Bhāskara II")'s "[cyclic method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chakravala_method "Chakravala method")".[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_induction#cite_note-Induction_Bussey-10)

None of these ancient mathematicians, however, explicitly stated the induction hypothesis. Another similar case (contrary to what Vacca has written, as Freudenthal carefully showed)[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_induction#cite_note-FOOTNOTERashed199462-11) was that of [Francesco Maurolico](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Francesco_Maurolico "Francesco Maurolico") in his _Arithmeticorum libri duo_ (1575), who used the technique to prove that the sum of the first n[odd](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parity_(mathematics) "Parity (mathematics)")[integers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer") is _n_ 2.

The earliest [rigorous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rigour#Mathematical_proof "Rigour") use of induction was by [Gersonides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gersonides "Gersonides") (1288–1344).[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_induction#cite_note-FOOTNOTESimonson2000-12)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_induction#cite_note-FOOTNOTERabinovitch1970-13) The first explicit formulation of the principle of induction was given by [Pascal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blaise_Pascal "Blaise Pascal") in his _Traité du triangle arithmétique_ (1665). Another Frenchman, [Fermat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre_de_Fermat "Pierre de Fermat"), made ample use of a related principle: indirect proof by [infinite descent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinite_descent "Infinite descent").

The induction hypothesis was also employed by the Swiss [Jakob Bernoulli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jakob_Bernoulli "Jakob Bernoulli"), and from then on it became well known. The modern formal treatment of the principle came only in the 19th century, with [George Boole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_Boole "George Boole"),[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_induction#cite_note-14)[Augustus De Morgan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Augustus_De_Morgan "Augustus De Morgan"), [Charles Sanders Peirce](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Sanders_Peirce "Charles Sanders Peirce"),[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_induction#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPeirce1881-15)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_induction#cite_note-FOOTNOTEShields1997-16)[Giuseppe Peano](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giuseppe_Peano "Giuseppe Peano"), and [Richard Dedekind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Dedekind "Richard Dedekind").[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_induction#cite_note-Induction_Bussey-10)

The simplest and most common form of mathematical induction infers that a statement involving a [natural number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number "Natural number")n (that is, an integer _n_ ≥ 0 or 1) holds for all values of n. The proof consists of two steps:

1.   The **base case** (or **initial case**): prove that the statement holds for 0, or 1.
2.   The **induction step** (or **inductive step**, or **step case**): prove that for every n, if the statement holds for n, then it holds for _n_ + 1. In other words, assume that the statement holds for some arbitrary natural number n, and prove that the statement holds for _n_ + 1.

The hypothesis in the induction step, that the statement holds for a particular n, is called the **induction hypothesis** or **inductive hypothesis**. To prove the induction step, one assumes the induction hypothesis for n and then uses this assumption to prove that the statement holds for _n_ + 1.

Authors who prefer to define natural numbers to begin at 0 use that value in the base case; those who define natural numbers to begin at 1 use that value.

### Sum of consecutive natural numbers

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Mathematical_induction&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Sum of consecutive natural numbers")]

Mathematical induction can be used to prove the following statement _P_(_n_) for all natural numbers n. ![Image 14: {\displaystyle P(n)\!:\ \ 0+1+2+\cdots +n={\frac {n(n+1)}{2}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f71f7909df7374df61735ad6b1fbccdb11f0a5d2)

This states a general formula for the sum of the natural numbers less than or equal to a given number; in fact an infinite sequence of statements: ![Image 15: {\displaystyle 0={\tfrac {(0)(0+1)}{2}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/20722b1548fdda3b520085dc31c7a800f8596dda), ![Image 16: {\displaystyle 0+1={\tfrac {(1)(1+1)}{2}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/466ecc3e21f80a9cf48383e7d3674f5f0db32bb6), ![Image 17: {\displaystyle 0+1+2={\tfrac {(2)(2+1)}{2}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4f75217d762154e21f44d881f396c4558995ddf7), etc.

**Proposition.** For every ![Image 18: {\displaystyle n\in \mathbb {N} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d059936e77a2d707e9ee0a1d9575a1d693ce5d0b), ![Image 19: {\displaystyle 0+1+2+\cdots +n={\tfrac {n(n+1)}{2}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9bf0be7dc58fe4f2239432f8c455b43c81fc1915)

**Proof.** Let _P_(_n_) be the statement ![Image 20: {\displaystyle 0+1+2+\cdots +n={\tfrac {n(n+1)}{2}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9bf0be7dc58fe4f2239432f8c455b43c81fc1915) We give a proof by induction on n.

_Base case:_ Show that the statement holds for the smallest natural number _n_ = 0.

_P_(0) is clearly true: ![Image 21: {\displaystyle 0={\tfrac {0(0+1)}{2}}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e6362a08a500f5fb6a222d694977f624f1b6a01f)

_Induction step:_ Show that for every _k_ ≥ 0, if _P_(_k_) holds, then _P_(_k_ + 1) also holds.

Assume the induction hypothesis that for a particular k, the single case _n_ = _k_ holds, meaning _P_(_k_) is true:![Image 22: {\displaystyle 0+1+\cdots +k={\frac {k(k+1)}{2}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f8836d7410f8647a0e47edb087a580dd312f1694) It follows that: ![Image 23: {\displaystyle (0+1+2+\cdots +k)+(k+1)={\frac {k(k+1)}{2}}+(k+1).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/233e4ed7ce920229cc241e45ecd575a2ad38f6d8)

[Algebraically](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra "Algebra"), the right hand side simplifies as: ![Image 24: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}{\frac {k(k+1)}{2}}+(k+1)&={\frac {k(k+1)+2(k+1)}{2}}\\&={\frac {(k+1)(k+2)}{2}}\\&={\frac {(k+1)((k+1)+1)}{2}}.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c8f7aa8426ee8017ea5146530412c0e80efe19ce)

Equating the extreme left hand and right hand sides, we deduce that:![Image 25: {\displaystyle 0+1+2+\cdots +k+(k+1)={\frac {(k+1)((k+1)+1)}{2}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/42f873071efcd5268ae0aee53954dbb5ca5f5c58) That is, the statement _P_(_k_ + 1) also holds true, establishing the induction step.

_Conclusion:_ Since both the base case and the induction step have been proved as true, by mathematical induction the statement _P_(_n_) holds for every natural number n. [Q.E.D.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Q.E.D. "Q.E.D.")

### A trigonometric inequality

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Mathematical_induction&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: A trigonometric inequality")]

Induction is often used to prove [inequalities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inequality_(mathematics) "Inequality (mathematics)"). As an example, we prove that ![Image 26: {\displaystyle \left|\sin nx\right|\leq n\left|\sin x\right|}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/403c2cef4db86b65faf0301acfe9b521c035cb20) for any [real number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number")![Image 27: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) and natural number ![Image 28: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b).

At first glance, it may appear that a more general version, ![Image 29: {\displaystyle \left|\sin nx\right|\leq n\left|\sin x\right|}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/403c2cef4db86b65faf0301acfe9b521c035cb20) for any _real_ numbers ![Image 30: {\displaystyle n,x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/09bbd04fc7f01076e6c76a3b09b03cae2e4da159), could be proven without induction; but the case ![Image 31: {\textstyle n={\frac {1}{2}},\,x=\pi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/41c09e883cbf35f44e0a9956d65d0c196c20e599) shows it may be false for non-integer values of ![Image 32: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b). This suggests we examine the statement specifically for _natural_ values of ![Image 33: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b), and induction is the readiest tool.

**Proposition.** For any ![Image 34: {\displaystyle x\in \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a9c6d458566aec47a7259762034790c8981aefab) and ![Image 35: {\displaystyle n\in \mathbb {N} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d059936e77a2d707e9ee0a1d9575a1d693ce5d0b), ![Image 36: {\displaystyle \left|\sin nx\right|\leq n\left|\sin x\right|}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/403c2cef4db86b65faf0301acfe9b521c035cb20).

**Proof.** Fix an arbitrary real number ![Image 37: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4), and let ![Image 38: {\displaystyle P(n)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5e303d2c14cd399b6f52b468c9fd44a542bed422) be the statement ![Image 39: {\displaystyle \left|\sin nx\right|\leq n\left|\sin x\right|}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/403c2cef4db86b65faf0301acfe9b521c035cb20). We induce on ![Image 40: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b).

_Base case:_ The calculation ![Image 41: {\displaystyle \left|\sin 0x\right|=0\leq 0=0\left|\sin x\right|}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5a3907fecd4f60639b771e2dd58c2bf5a72952cb) verifies ![Image 42: {\displaystyle P(0)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/60a4af3ee31a3a4e26e7cb20b4a6aed37f6e8a5e).

_Induction step:_ We show the [implication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logical_consequence "Logical consequence")![Image 43: {\displaystyle P(k)\implies P(k+1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ed02b0eb1f165f752ce4463a34a0de48db3b0147) for any natural number ![Image 44: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40). Assume the induction hypothesis: for a given value ![Image 45: {\displaystyle n=k\geq 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/abf7e80555f327cec9d84de4a190c1fdb73d82ab), the single case ![Image 46: {\displaystyle P(k)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b41614fb84549b21f2c7f2793bbd8a87a2105027) is true. Using the [angle addition formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_trigonometric_identities "List of trigonometric identities") and the [triangle inequality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_value#Real_numbers "Absolute value"), we deduce: ![Image 47: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\left|\sin(k+1)x\right|&=\left|\sin kx\cos x+\sin x\cos kx\right|&&{\text{(angle addition)}}\\&\leq \left|\sin kx\cos x\right|+\left|\sin x\,\cos kx\right|&&{\text{(triangle inequality)}}\\&=\left|\sin kx\right|\left|\cos x\right|+\left|\sin x\right|\left|\cos kx\right|\\&\leq \left|\sin kx\right|+\left|\sin x\right|&&(\left|\cos t\right|\leq 1)\\&\leq k\left|\sin x\right|+\left|\sin x\right|&&{\text{(induction hypothesis}})\\&=(k+1)\left|\sin x\right|.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4bee3607e0fdab6d9f5c8fb2f956f1bb664338c7)

The inequality between the extreme left-hand and right-hand quantities shows that ![Image 48: {\displaystyle P(k+1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c82953120fe42f14025a07cd09ba7eaee65fcee1) is true, which completes the induction step.

_Conclusion:_ The proposition ![Image 49: {\displaystyle P(n)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5e303d2c14cd399b6f52b468c9fd44a542bed422) holds for all natural numbers ![Image 50: {\displaystyle n.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e59df02a9f67a5da3c220f1244c99a46cc4eb1c6) Q.E.D.

In practice, proofs by induction are often structured differently, depending on the exact nature of the property to be proven. All variants of induction are special cases of [transfinite induction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transfinite_induction "Transfinite induction"); see [below](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_induction#Transfinite_induction).

### Base case other than 0 or 1

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Mathematical_induction&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Base case other than 0 or 1")]

If one wishes to prove a statement, not for all natural numbers, but only for all numbers n greater than or equal to a certain number b, then the proof by induction consists of the following:

1.   Showing that the statement holds when _n_ = _b_.
2.   Showing that if the statement holds for an arbitrary number _n_ ≥ _b_, then the same statement also holds for _n_ + 1.

This can be used, for example, to show that 2 _n_ ≥ _n_ + 5 for _n_ ≥ 3.

In this way, one can prove that some statement _P_(_n_) holds for all _n_ ≥ 1, or even for all _n_ ≥ −5. This form of mathematical induction is actually a special case of the previous form, because if the statement to be proved is _P_(_n_) then proving it with these two rules is equivalent with proving _P_(_n_ + _b_) for all natural numbers n with an induction base case 0.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_induction#cite_note-17)

#### Example: forming dollar amounts by coins

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Mathematical_induction&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Example: forming dollar amounts by coins")]

Assume an infinite supply of 4- and 5-dollar coins. Induction can be used to prove that any whole amount of dollars greater than or equal to 12 can be formed by a combination of such coins. Let _S_(_k_) denote the statement "k dollars can be formed by a combination of 4- and 5-dollar coins". The proof that _S_(_k_) is true for all _k_ ≥ 12 can then be achieved by induction on k as follows:

_Base case:_ Showing that _S_(_k_) holds for _k_ = 12 is simple: take three 4-dollar coins.

_Induction step:_ Given that _S_(_k_) holds for some value of _k_ ≥ 12 (_induction hypothesis_), prove that _S_(_k_ + 1) holds, too. Assume _S_(_k_) is true for some arbitrary _k_ ≥ 12. If there is a solution for k dollars that includes at least one 4-dollar coin, replace it by a 5-dollar coin to make _k_ + 1 dollars. Otherwise, if only 5-dollar coins are used, k must be a multiple of 5 and so at least 15; but then we can replace three 5-dollar coins by four 4-dollar coins to make _k_ + 1 dollars. In each case, _S_(_k_ + 1) is true.

Therefore, by the principle of induction, _S_(_k_) holds for all _k_ ≥ 12, and the proof is complete.

In this example, although _S_(_k_) also holds for ![Image 51: {\textstyle k\in \{4,5,8,9,10\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/55e12a526c9c0e242d868ad6cef14a8a79ee74f4), the above proof cannot be modified to replace the minimum amount of 12 dollar to any lower value m. For _m_ = 11, the base case is actually false; for _m_ = 10, the second case in the induction step (replacing three 5- by four 4-dollar coins) will not work; let alone for even lower m.

### Induction on more than one counter

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Mathematical_induction&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Induction on more than one counter")]

It is sometimes desirable to prove a statement involving two natural numbers, n and m, by iterating the induction process. That is, one proves a base case and an induction step for n, and in each of those proves a base case and an induction step for m. See, for example, the [proof of commutativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proofs_involving_the_addition_of_natural_numbers "Proofs involving the addition of natural numbers") accompanying _[addition of natural numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Addition\_of\_natural\_numbers "Addition of natural numbers")_. More complicated arguments involving three or more counters are also possible.

The method of infinite descent is a variation of mathematical induction which was used by [Pierre de Fermat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre_de_Fermat "Pierre de Fermat"). It is used to show that some statement _Q_(_n_) is false for all natural numbers n. Its traditional form consists of showing that if _Q_(_n_) is true for some natural number n, it also holds for some strictly smaller natural number m. Because there are no infinite decreasing sequences of natural numbers, this situation would be impossible, thereby showing ([by contradiction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proof_by_contradiction "Proof by contradiction")) that _Q_(_n_) cannot be true for any n.

The validity of this method can be verified from the usual principle of mathematical induction. Using mathematical induction on the statement _P_(_n_) defined as "_Q_(_m_) is false for all natural numbers m less than or equal to n", it follows that _P_(_n_) holds for all n, which means that _Q_(_n_) is false for every natural number n.

### Limited mathematical induction

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Mathematical_induction&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Limited mathematical induction")]

If one wishes to prove that a property _P_ holds for all natural numbers less than or equal to a fixed N, proving that _P_ satisfies the following conditions suffices:[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_induction#cite_note-18)

1.   _P_ holds for 0,
2.   For any natural number x less than N, if _P_ holds for x, then _P_ holds for _x_ + 1

The most common form of proof by mathematical induction requires proving in the induction step that ![Image 52: {\displaystyle \forall k\,(P(k)\to P(k+1))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/57345a4d146861cf82548d792735e2adc3cc9dc9)

whereupon the induction principle "automates" n applications of this step in getting from _P_(0) to _P_(_n_). This could be called "predecessor induction" because each step proves something about a number from something about that number's predecessor.

A variant of interest in [computational complexity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computational_complexity "Computational complexity") is "prefix induction", in which one proves the following statement in the induction step: ![Image 53: {\displaystyle \forall k\,(P(k)\to P(2k)\land P(2k+1))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c67a02d3cb0c7256ef73cee96f45696a5ffef29f) or equivalently ![Image 54: {\displaystyle \forall k\,\left(P\!\left(\left\lfloor {\frac {k}{2}}\right\rfloor \right)\to P(k)\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bfe1fb30c50a4437fbf6dd3c310364585822621f)

The induction principle then "automates" [log 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_logarithm "Binary logarithm")_n_ applications of this inference in getting from _P_(0) to _P_(_n_). In fact, it is called "prefix induction" because each step proves something about a number from something about the "prefix" of that number — as formed by truncating the low bit of its [binary representation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_representation "Binary representation"). It can also be viewed as an application of traditional induction on the length of that binary representation.

If traditional predecessor induction is interpreted computationally as an n-step loop, then prefix induction would correspond to a log-n-step loop. Because of that, proofs using prefix induction are "more feasibly constructive" than proofs using predecessor induction.

Predecessor induction can trivially simulate prefix induction on the same statement. Prefix induction can simulate predecessor induction, but only at the cost of making the statement more syntactically complex (adding a [bounded](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bounded_quantifier "Bounded quantifier")[universal quantifier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universal_quantifier "Universal quantifier")), so the interesting results relating prefix induction to [polynomial-time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial-time "Polynomial-time") computation depend on excluding unbounded quantifiers entirely, and limiting the alternation of bounded universal and [existential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existential_quantifier "Existential quantifier") quantifiers allowed in the statement.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_induction#cite_note-Buss:BA-19)

One can take the idea a step further: one must prove ![Image 55: {\displaystyle \forall k\,\left(P\!\left(\left\lfloor {\sqrt {k}}\right\rfloor \right)\to P(k)\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d6977849c80fedf2af8118f769ace44a3920207) whereupon the induction principle "automates" log log _n_ applications of this inference in getting from _P_(0) to _P_(_n_). This form of induction has been used, analogously, to study log-time parallel computation.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

### Complete (strong) induction

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Mathematical_induction&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Complete (strong) induction")]

Another variant, called **complete induction**, **course of values induction** or **strong induction** (in contrast to which the basic form of induction is sometimes known as **weak induction**), makes the induction step easier to prove by using a stronger hypothesis: one proves the statement ![Image 56: {\displaystyle P(m+1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a1fb60af65623b68e475ce367bc049778a82d7ac) under the assumption that ![Image 57: {\displaystyle P(n)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5e303d2c14cd399b6f52b468c9fd44a542bed422) holds for _all_ natural numbers ![Image 58: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) less than ![Image 59: {\displaystyle m+1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c6f7ed29a2b4a62d3b6af05cd91a58ffc6094201); by contrast, the basic form only assumes ![Image 60: {\displaystyle P(m)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b3a233906e02f973dc3ce3d3fc3cacca780e3714). The name "strong induction" does not mean that this method can prove more than "weak induction", but merely refers to the stronger hypothesis used in the induction step.

In fact, it can be shown that the two methods are actually equivalent, as explained below. In this form of complete induction, one still has to prove the base case, ![Image 61: {\displaystyle P(0)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/60a4af3ee31a3a4e26e7cb20b4a6aed37f6e8a5e), and it may even be necessary to prove extra-base cases such as ![Image 62: {\displaystyle P(1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1fdf0a9cc2b05be736085c7422ebe1d8d019329a) before the general argument applies, as in the example below of the [Fibonacci number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fibonacci_number "Fibonacci number")![Image 63: {\displaystyle F_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/76cdf519c21deec43f984815e57e15d2dd3575d7).

Although the form just described requires one to prove the base case, this is unnecessary if one can prove ![Image 64: {\displaystyle P(m)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b3a233906e02f973dc3ce3d3fc3cacca780e3714) (assuming ![Image 65: {\displaystyle P(n)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5e303d2c14cd399b6f52b468c9fd44a542bed422) for all lower ![Image 66: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)) for all ![Image 67: {\displaystyle m\geq 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b0d2d765e4cfd7adfbca9ae0e37e75a2811c0333). This is a special case of [transfinite induction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_induction#Transfinite_induction) as described below, although it is no longer equivalent to ordinary induction. In this form the base case is subsumed by the case ![Image 68: {\displaystyle m=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e57f21007575fd03e3be0da20af34d25829cc9a7), where ![Image 69: {\displaystyle P(0)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/60a4af3ee31a3a4e26e7cb20b4a6aed37f6e8a5e) is proved with no other ![Image 70: {\displaystyle P(n)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5e303d2c14cd399b6f52b468c9fd44a542bed422) assumed; this case may need to be handled separately, but sometimes the same argument applies for ![Image 71: {\displaystyle m=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e57f21007575fd03e3be0da20af34d25829cc9a7) and ![Image 72: {\displaystyle m>0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/501173910e6da8425b4e9d44a4e8643620bc2464), making the proof simpler and more elegant. In this method, however, it is vital to ensure that the proof of ![Image 73: {\displaystyle P(m)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b3a233906e02f973dc3ce3d3fc3cacca780e3714) does not implicitly assume that ![Image 74: {\displaystyle m>0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/501173910e6da8425b4e9d44a4e8643620bc2464), e.g. by saying "choose an arbitrary ![Image 75: {\displaystyle n<m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5cff096773597d7223f9d90162eb2d780dfc18dc)", or by assuming that a set of m elements has an element.

#### Equivalence with ordinary induction

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Mathematical_induction&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Equivalence with ordinary induction")]

Complete induction is equivalent to ordinary mathematical induction as described above, in the sense that a proof by one method can be transformed into a proof by the other. Suppose there is a proof of ![Image 76: {\displaystyle P(n)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5e303d2c14cd399b6f52b468c9fd44a542bed422) by complete induction. Then, this proof can be transformed into an ordinary induction proof by assuming a stronger inductive hypothesis. Let ![Image 77: {\displaystyle Q(n)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/39f1477b71cf9622d2d860e02dbbcffaeac2f13a) be the statement "![Image 78: {\displaystyle P(m)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b3a233906e02f973dc3ce3d3fc3cacca780e3714) holds for all ![Image 79: {\displaystyle m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a07d98bb302f3856cbabc47b2b9016692e3f7bc) such that ![Image 80: {\displaystyle 0\leq m\leq n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/697dd51930c94bcdf31d76ab181a7a20fb3823f5)"—this becomes the inductive hypothesis for ordinary induction. We can then show ![Image 81: {\displaystyle Q(0)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f363840cf8e41129830f221f8fadcbccf196c556) and ![Image 82: {\displaystyle Q(n+1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/20593934bb192fa10304f0fecd3a4b0e9f57648d) for ![Image 83: {\displaystyle n\in \mathbb {N} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d059936e77a2d707e9ee0a1d9575a1d693ce5d0b) assuming only ![Image 84: {\displaystyle Q(n)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/39f1477b71cf9622d2d860e02dbbcffaeac2f13a) and show that ![Image 85: {\displaystyle Q(n)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/39f1477b71cf9622d2d860e02dbbcffaeac2f13a) implies ![Image 86: {\displaystyle P(n)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5e303d2c14cd399b6f52b468c9fd44a542bed422).[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_induction#cite_note-20)

If, on the other hand, ![Image 87: {\displaystyle P(n)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5e303d2c14cd399b6f52b468c9fd44a542bed422) had been proven by ordinary induction, the proof would already effectively be one by complete induction: ![Image 88: {\displaystyle P(0)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/60a4af3ee31a3a4e26e7cb20b4a6aed37f6e8a5e) is proved in the base case, using no assumptions, and ![Image 89: {\displaystyle P(n+1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d2846b7cbc67a6e521b30d90ba22d6400eb10c36) is proved in the induction step, in which one may assume all earlier cases but need only use the case ![Image 90: {\displaystyle P(n)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5e303d2c14cd399b6f52b468c9fd44a542bed422).

#### Example: Fibonacci numbers

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Mathematical_induction&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: Example: Fibonacci numbers")]

Complete induction is most useful when several instances of the inductive hypothesis are required for each induction step. For example, complete induction can be used to show that ![Image 91: {\displaystyle F_{n}={\frac {\varphi ^{n}-\psi ^{n}}{\varphi -\psi }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e06fe0f5b1ed7b005610e78f60cc4518399c5c9) where ![Image 92: {\displaystyle F_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/76cdf519c21deec43f984815e57e15d2dd3575d7) is the n-th [Fibonacci number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fibonacci_number "Fibonacci number"), and ![Image 93: {\textstyle \varphi ={\frac {1}{2}}(1+{\sqrt {5}})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/429266b9138e961cfc92ea684924da5128ae3311) (the [golden ratio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden_ratio "Golden ratio")) and ![Image 94: {\textstyle \psi ={\frac {1}{2}}(1-{\sqrt {5}})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2be28b81a86659abfd719527da3e412fb44dfa8b) are the [roots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Root_of_a_polynomial "Root of a polynomial") of the [polynomial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial "Polynomial")![Image 95: {\displaystyle x^{2}-x-1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8d9a6454bbe84939273de34c70735b86dfcbc88e). By using the fact that ![Image 96: {\displaystyle F_{n+2}=F_{n+1}+F_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4181a6c72e594296eba3faa89618e10dbd3e12ed) for each ![Image 97: {\displaystyle n\in \mathbb {N} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d059936e77a2d707e9ee0a1d9575a1d693ce5d0b), the identity above can be verified by direct calculation for ![Image 98: {\textstyle F_{n+2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1ac4075aa37c49541fa8430a14ec6caf7b618bce) if one assumes that it already holds for both ![Image 99: {\textstyle F_{n+1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3f58fd46e131a8f1824d5a4c1160587819b6dd2) and ![Image 100: {\textstyle F_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3451cc5f8710d90fe02bb5f2e98848b37d0b8ac8). To complete the proof, the identity must be verified in the two base cases: ![Image 101: {\displaystyle n=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/26819344e55f5e671c76c07c18eb4291fcec85ae) and ![Image 102: {\textstyle n=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5300467b7ad341a5e79a0414b0293a43c5c6ddc2).

#### Example: prime factorization

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Mathematical_induction&action=edit&section=16 "Edit section: Example: prime factorization")]

Another proof by complete induction uses the hypothesis that the statement holds for _all_ smaller ![Image 103: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) more thoroughly. Consider the statement that "every [natural number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number "Natural number") greater than 1 is a product of (one or more) [prime numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_number "Prime number")", which is the "[existence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_theorem_of_arithmetic#Existence "Fundamental theorem of arithmetic")" part of the [fundamental theorem of arithmetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_theorem_of_arithmetic "Fundamental theorem of arithmetic"). For proving the induction step, the induction hypothesis is that for a given ![Image 104: {\displaystyle m>1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7f27527902d05e4c32bcbe28d425d7790f8ae191) the statement holds for all smaller ![Image 105: {\displaystyle n>1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ee74e1cc07e7041edf0fcbd4481f5cd32ad17b64). If ![Image 106: {\displaystyle m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a07d98bb302f3856cbabc47b2b9016692e3f7bc) is prime then it is certainly a product of primes, and if not, then by definition it is a product: ![Image 107: {\displaystyle m=n_{1}n_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/da77709ff5c4259e722478808dc1e88d9dbf48e4), where neither of the factors is equal to 1; hence neither is equal to ![Image 108: {\displaystyle m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a07d98bb302f3856cbabc47b2b9016692e3f7bc), and so both are greater than 1 and smaller than ![Image 109: {\displaystyle m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a07d98bb302f3856cbabc47b2b9016692e3f7bc). The induction hypothesis now applies to ![Image 110: {\displaystyle n_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ee784b70e772f55ede5e6e0bdc929994bff63413) and ![Image 111: {\displaystyle n_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/840e456e3058bc0be28e5cf653b170cdbfcc3be4), so each one is a product of primes. Thus ![Image 112: {\displaystyle m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a07d98bb302f3856cbabc47b2b9016692e3f7bc) is a product of products of primes, and hence by extension a product of primes itself.

#### Example: dollar amounts revisited

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Mathematical_induction&action=edit&section=17 "Edit section: Example: dollar amounts revisited")]

We shall look to prove the same example as [above](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_induction#Example:_forming_dollar_amounts_by_coins), this time with _strong induction_. The statement remains the same: ![Image 113: {\displaystyle S(n):\,\,n\geq 12\implies \,\exists \,a,b\in \mathbb {N} .\,\,n=4a+5b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4dc2627d17c6cca5ca63288a098e410e2cd03b60)

However, there will be slight differences in the structure and the assumptions of the proof, starting with the extended base case.

**Proof.**

_Base case:_ Show that ![Image 114: {\displaystyle S(k)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/04a593db47be6f284bc1330c62f692ff5cd5e749) holds for ![Image 115: {\displaystyle k=12,13,14,15}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/94b46976fc51b9191accdb2d703ec6e7c3058992). ![Image 116: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}4\cdot 3+5\cdot 0=12\\4\cdot 2+5\cdot 1=13\\4\cdot 1+5\cdot 2=14\\4\cdot 0+5\cdot 3=15\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/04a060c64cab7abfe684fab1e9222cfb51276f3d)

The base case holds.

_Induction step:_ Given some ![Image 117: {\displaystyle j>15}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/449821ed8dd9fc2feedf14db7e9def269b6e2597), assume ![Image 118: {\displaystyle S(m)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/da5ab66b1231fb42c70285826dacad7d55b6bc48) holds for all ![Image 119: {\displaystyle m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a07d98bb302f3856cbabc47b2b9016692e3f7bc) with ![Image 120: {\displaystyle 12\leq m<j}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/59ed2020caf0cb282baf69756180a2ab1247df0d). Prove that ![Image 121: {\displaystyle S(j)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5436f5d29dbadbfba26a5b9ab6b685ba2026e0b0) holds.

Choosing ![Image 122: {\displaystyle m=j-4}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1944a371c7ee101e7defaccf3dcaa96bbdc4634e), and observing that ![Image 123: {\displaystyle 15<j\implies 12\leq j-4<j}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fcc12cb72589d178e5c030f59aeb1619382db10f) shows that ![Image 124: {\displaystyle S(j-4)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d7f6359738ec380534f7ced306cf1f6673c3f70e) holds, by the inductive hypothesis. That is, the sum ![Image 125: {\displaystyle j-4}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/66ba0faa63ff486716b00755d001dfa072e7fd5a) can be formed by some combination of ![Image 126: {\displaystyle 4}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/295b4bf1de7cd3500e740e0f4f0635db22d87b42) and ![Image 127: {\displaystyle 5}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/29483407999b8763f0ea335cf715a6a5e809f44b) dollar coins. Then, simply adding a ![Image 128: {\displaystyle 4}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/295b4bf1de7cd3500e740e0f4f0635db22d87b42) dollar coin to that combination yields the sum ![Image 129: {\displaystyle j}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2f461e54f5c093e92a55547b9764291390f0b5d0). That is, ![Image 130: {\displaystyle S(j)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5436f5d29dbadbfba26a5b9ab6b685ba2026e0b0) holds[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_induction#cite_note-yorku-21) Q.E.D.

### Forward-backward induction

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Mathematical_induction&action=edit&section=18 "Edit section: Forward-backward induction")]

Sometimes, it is more convenient to deduce backwards, proving the statement for ![Image 131: {\displaystyle n-1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fbd0b0f32b28f51962943ee9ede4fb34198a2521), given its validity for ![Image 132: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b). However, proving the validity of the statement for no single number suffices to establish the base case; instead, one needs to prove the statement for an infinite subset of the natural numbers. For example, [Augustin Louis Cauchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Augustin_Louis_Cauchy "Augustin Louis Cauchy") first used forward (regular) induction to prove the [inequality of arithmetic and geometric means](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inequality_of_arithmetic_and_geometric_means#Proof_by_Cauchy_using_forward%E2%80%93backward_induction "Inequality of arithmetic and geometric means") for all [powers of 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Powers_of_2 "Powers of 2"), and then used backwards induction to show it for all natural numbers.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_induction#cite_note-22)[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_induction#cite_note-23)

Example of error in the induction step
--------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Mathematical_induction&action=edit&section=19 "Edit section: Example of error in the induction step")]

The induction step must be proved for all values of n. To illustrate this, Joel E. Cohen proposed the following argument, which purports to prove by mathematical induction that [all horses are of the same color](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/All_horses_are_the_same_color "All horses are the same color"):[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_induction#cite_note-24)

_Base case:_ in a set of only _one_ horse, there is only one color.

_Induction step:_ assume as induction hypothesis that within any set of ![Image 133: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) horses, there is only one color. Now look at any set of ![Image 134: {\displaystyle n+1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2a135e65a42f2d73cccbfc4569523996ca0036f1) horses. Number them: ![Image 135: {\displaystyle 1,2,3,\dotsc ,n,n+1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9e890e2c1cb034bdc2bfebcd6dbcdf7f45942b61). Consider the sets ![Image 136: {\textstyle \left\{1,2,3,\dotsc ,n\right\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/98c9d3f3d5329a65f3a7585a7cb0eda646045e91) and ![Image 137: {\textstyle \left\{2,3,4,\dotsc ,n+1\right\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eb581ffc8f6e88652258cbccffedc6bf53156ca4). Each is a set of only ![Image 138: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) horses, therefore within each there is only one color. But the two sets overlap, so there must be only one color among all ![Image 139: {\displaystyle n+1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2a135e65a42f2d73cccbfc4569523996ca0036f1) horses.

The base case ![Image 140: {\displaystyle n=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d9ec7e1edc2e6d98f5aec2a39ae5f1c99d1e1425) is trivial, and the induction step is correct in all cases ![Image 141: {\displaystyle n>1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ee74e1cc07e7041edf0fcbd4481f5cd32ad17b64). However, the argument used in the induction step is incorrect for ![Image 142: {\displaystyle n+1=2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9e5a41ae3cb687d0836e9cb57c42dbc86d97624e), because the statement that "the two sets overlap" is false for ![Image 143: {\textstyle \left\{1\right\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/254bf77826263f68d2bca544ebc09802c97f7ec8) and ![Image 144: {\textstyle \left\{2\right\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e66e470e4c16ac78dfc56adea4237f0f0227bc30).

In **[second-order logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second-order_logic "Second-order logic")**, one can write down the "[axiom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiom "Axiom") of induction" as follows: ![Image 145: {\displaystyle \forall P\,{\Bigl (}P(0)\land \forall k{\bigl (}P(k)\to P(k+1){\bigr )}\to \forall n\,{\bigl (}P(n){\bigr )}{\Bigr )},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/97c73121166dfc11f8b6853a3192bedd695c4880) where _P_(·) is a variable for [predicates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Predicate_(mathematical_logic) "Predicate (mathematical logic)") involving one natural number and k and n are variables for [natural numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number "Natural number").

In words, the base case _P_(0) and the induction step (namely, that the induction hypothesis _P_(_k_) implies _P_(_k_ + 1)) together imply that _P_(_n_) for any natural number n. The axiom of induction asserts the validity of inferring that _P_(_n_) holds for any natural number n from the base case and the induction step.

The first quantifier in the axiom ranges over _predicates_ rather than over individual numbers. This is a second-order quantifier, which means that this axiom is stated in [second-order logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second-order_logic "Second-order logic"). Axiomatizing arithmetic induction in [first-order logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First-order_logic "First-order logic") requires an [axiom schema](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiom_schema "Axiom schema") containing a separate axiom for each possible predicate. The article [Peano axioms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peano_axioms "Peano axioms") contains further discussion of this issue.

The axiom of structural induction for the natural numbers was first formulated by Peano, who used it to specify the natural numbers together with the following four other axioms:

1.   0 is a natural number.
2.   The successor function s of every natural number yields a natural number (_s_(_x_) = _x_ + 1).
3.   The successor function is [injective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Injective "Injective").
4.   0 is not in the [range](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Range_of_a_function "Range of a function") of s.

In **[first-order](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First-order_logic "First-order logic")[ZFC set theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ZFC_set_theory "ZFC set theory")**, quantification over predicates is not allowed, but one can still express induction by quantification over sets: ![Image 146: {\displaystyle \forall A{\Bigl (}0\in A\land \forall k\in \mathbb {N} {\bigl (}k\in A\to (k+1)\in A{\bigr )}\to \mathbb {N} \subseteq A{\Bigr )}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4757318e068a3d55ce71fb1205b13ca936b2a392)A may be read as a set representing a proposition, and containing natural numbers, for which the proposition holds. This is not an axiom, but a theorem, given that natural numbers are defined in the language of ZFC set theory by axioms, analogous to Peano's. See [construction of the natural numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiom_of_infinity#Alternative_method "Axiom of infinity") using the [axiom of infinity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiom_of_infinity "Axiom of infinity") and [axiom schema of specification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiom_schema_of_specification "Axiom schema of specification").

Transfinite induction
---------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Mathematical_induction&action=edit&section=21 "Edit section: Transfinite induction")]

One variation of the principle of complete induction can be generalized for statements about elements of any [well-founded set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Well-founded_set "Well-founded set"), that is, a set with an [irreflexive relation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflexive_relation "Reflexive relation")< that contains no [infinite descending chains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinite_descending_chain "Infinite descending chain"). Every set representing an [ordinal number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordinal_number "Ordinal number") is well-founded, the set of natural numbers is one of them.

Applied to a well-founded set, transfinite induction can be formulated as a single step. To prove that a statement _P_(_n_) holds for each ordinal number:

1.   Show, for each ordinal number n, that if _P_(_m_) holds for all _m_<_n_, then _P_(_n_) also holds.

This form of induction, when applied to a set of ordinal numbers (which form a [well-ordered](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Well-order "Well-order") and hence well-founded [class](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Class_(set_theory) "Class (set theory)")), is called _[transfinite induction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transfinite\_induction "Transfinite induction")_. It is an important proof technique in [set theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_theory "Set theory"), [topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology "Topology") and other fields.

Proofs by transfinite induction typically distinguish three cases:

1.   when n is a minimal element, i.e. there is no element smaller than n;
2.   when n has a direct predecessor, i.e. the set of elements which are smaller than n has a largest element;
3.   when n has no direct predecessor, i.e. n is a so-called [limit ordinal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limit_ordinal "Limit ordinal").

Strictly speaking, it is not necessary in transfinite induction to prove a base case, because it is a [vacuous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vacuous_truth "Vacuous truth") special case of the proposition that if _P_ is true of all _n_<_m_, then _P_ is true of m. It is vacuously true precisely because there are no values of _n_<_m_ that could serve as counterexamples. So the special cases are special cases of the general case.

Relationship to the well-ordering principle
-------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Mathematical_induction&action=edit&section=22 "Edit section: Relationship to the well-ordering principle")]

The principle of mathematical induction is usually stated as an [axiom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiom "Axiom") of the natural numbers; see [Peano axioms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peano_axioms "Peano axioms"). It is strictly stronger than the [well-ordering principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Well-ordering_principle "Well-ordering principle") in the context of the other Peano axioms. Suppose the following:

*   The [trichotomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trichotomy_(mathematics) "Trichotomy (mathematics)") axiom: For any natural numbers n and m, n is less than or equal to m if and only if m is not less than n.
*   For any natural number n, _n_ + 1 is greater than _n_.
*   For any natural number n, no natural number is between _n_ and _n_ + 1.
*   No natural number is less than zero.

It can then be proved that induction, given the above-listed axioms, implies the well-ordering principle. The following proof uses complete induction and the first and fourth axioms.

**Proof.** Suppose there exists a [non-empty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empty_set "Empty set") set, S, of natural numbers that has no least element. Let _P_(_n_) be the assertion that n is not in S. Then _P_(0) is true, for if it were false then 0 is the least element of S. Furthermore, let n be a natural number, and suppose _P_(_m_) is true for all natural numbers m less than _n_ + 1. Then if _P_(_n_ + 1) is false _n_ + 1 is in S, thus being a minimal element in S, a contradiction. Thus _P_(_n_ + 1) is true. Therefore, by the complete induction principle, _P_(_n_) holds for all natural numbers n; so S is empty, a contradiction. Q.E.D.

[![Image 147](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/OmegaPlusOmega_svg.svg/600px-OmegaPlusOmega_svg.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:OmegaPlusOmega_svg.svg)

"[Number line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_line "Number line")" for the set {(0, _n_): _n_ ∈ **N**} ∪ {(1, _n_): _n_ ∈ **N**}. Numbers refer to the second component of pairs; the first can be obtained from color or location.

On the other hand, the set ![Image 148: {\displaystyle \{(0,n):n\in \mathbb {N} \}\cup \{(1,n):n\in \mathbb {N} \}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d85e9def332c72f23f810066e30e343243237581), shown in the picture, is well-ordered[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_induction#cite_note-Ohman2019-25): 35lf  by the [lexicographic order](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lexicographic_order "Lexicographic order"). Moreover, except for the induction axiom, it satisfies all Peano axioms, where Peano's constant 0 is interpreted as the pair (0, 0), and Peano's _successor_ function is defined on pairs by succ(_x_, _n_) = (_x_, _n_ + 1) for all ![Image 149: {\displaystyle x\in \{0,1\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/05013ded6c9801acf787923a2b8aa521f3542c57) and ![Image 150: {\displaystyle n\in \mathbb {N} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d059936e77a2d707e9ee0a1d9575a1d693ce5d0b). As an example for the violation of the induction axiom, define the predicate _P_(_x_, _n_) as (_x_, _n_) = (0, 0) or (_x_, _n_) = succ(_y_, _m_) for some ![Image 151: {\displaystyle y\in \{0,1\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7c66170a51fd7b916d42c2cf8e8512c75c85a594) and ![Image 152: {\displaystyle m\in \mathbb {N} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/42411e85d874a733209223302bbd8d5e3ad04cb0). Then the base case _P_(0, 0) is trivially true, and so is the induction step: if _P_(_x_, _n_), then _P_(succ(_x_, _n_)). However, _P_ is not true for all pairs in the set, since _P_(1,0) is false.

Peano's axioms with the induction principle uniquely model the natural numbers. Replacing the induction principle with the well-ordering principle allows for more exotic models that fulfill all the axioms.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_induction#cite_note-Ohman2019-25)

It is mistakenly printed in several books[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_induction#cite_note-Ohman2019-25) and sources that the well-ordering principle is equivalent to the induction axiom. In the context of the other Peano axioms, this is not the case, but in the context of other axioms, they are equivalent;[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_induction#cite_note-Ohman2019-25) specifically, the well-ordering principle implies the induction axiom in the context of the first two above listed axioms and

*   Every natural number is either 0 or _n_ + 1 for some natural number n.

A common mistake in many erroneous proofs is to assume that _n_ − 1 is a unique and well-defined natural number, a property which is not implied by the other Peano axioms.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_induction#cite_note-Ohman2019-25)
