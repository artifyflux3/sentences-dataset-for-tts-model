In [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics"), a **quantum state space** is an abstract [space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space "Space") in which different "positions" represent not literal locations, but rather [quantum states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_state "Quantum state") of some [physical system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_system "Physical system"). It is the quantum analog of the [phase space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phase_space "Phase space") of [classical mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_mechanics "Classical mechanics").

Relative to Hilbert space
-------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Quantum_state_space&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Relative to Hilbert space")]

In [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics") a state space is a [separable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space#Separable_spaces "Hilbert space")[complex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number")[Hilbert space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space "Hilbert space"). The [dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension_(vector_space) "Dimension (vector space)") of this [Hilbert space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space "Hilbert space") depends on the system we choose to describe.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_state_space#cite_note-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_state_space#cite_note-2) The different states that could come out of any particular measurement form an [orthonormal basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthonormal_basis "Orthonormal basis"), so any [state vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_state "Quantum state") in the state space can be written as a [linear combination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_combination "Linear combination") of these basis vectors. Having a nonzero component along multiple dimensions is called a [superposition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_superposition "Quantum superposition"). In the [formalism of quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_formulation_of_quantum_mechanics "Mathematical formulation of quantum mechanics") these [state vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_state "Quantum state") are often written using [Dirac's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Dirac "Paul Dirac") compact [bra–ket notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bra%E2%80%93ket_notation "Bra–ket notation").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_state_space#cite_note-Schiff-3): 165

The [spin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_(physics) "Spin (physics)") state of a silver atom in the [Stern–Gerlach experiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stern%E2%80%93Gerlach_experiment "Stern–Gerlach experiment") can be represented in a two state space. The spin can be aligned with a measuring apparatus (arbitrarily called 'up') or oppositely ('down').[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_state_space#cite_note-QM-minimum-4) In Dirac's notation these two states can be written as ![Image 1: {\displaystyle |u\rangle ,|d\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/df1713e5d9df621e1455034860b781f96432230f). The space of a two spin system has four states, ![Image 2: {\displaystyle |uu\rangle ,|ud\rangle ,|du\rangle ,|dd\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/219c94adca3b97c46daa1d3b1779098cece6c691).

The spin state is a discrete [degree of freedom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degrees_of_freedom_(physics_and_chemistry) "Degrees of freedom (physics and chemistry)"); quantum state spaces can have continuous degrees of freedom. For example, a particle in one space dimension has one degree of freedom ranging from ![Image 3: {\displaystyle -\infty }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ca2608c4b5fd3bffc73585f8c67e379b4e99b6f1) to ![Image 4: {\displaystyle \infty }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c26c105004f30c27aa7c2a9c601550a4183b1f21). In Dirac notation, the states in this space might be written as ![Image 5: {\displaystyle |q\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1ae4f9854b373804675d2c417921f4ad2c36f810) or ![Image 6: {\displaystyle |\psi \rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cc27f1893b769a08cd6b296e115a29e61cab675e).[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_state_space#cite_note-Messiah-5): 302

Relative to 3D space
--------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Quantum_state_space&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Relative to 3D space")]

Even in the early days of quantum mechanics, the state space (or configurations as they were called at first) was understood to be essential for understanding simple quantum-mechanical problems. In 1929, [Nevill Mott](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nevill_Mott "Nevill Mott") showed that "tendency to picture the wave as existing in ordinary three dimensional space, whereas we are really dealing with wave functions in multispace" makes analysis of simple interaction problems more difficult.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_state_space#cite_note-6) Mott analyzes [![Image 7: {\displaystyle \alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b79333175c8b3f0840bfb4ec41b8072c83ea88d3)-particle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_particle "Alpha particle") emission in a [cloud chamber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cloud_chamber "Cloud chamber"). The emission process is isotropic, a spherical wave in quantum mechanics, but the tracks observed are linear.

[![Image 8](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Indeterminacy_principle.gif/250px-Indeterminacy_principle.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Indeterminacy_principle.gif)

Spherical wave

[![Image 9](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/AlphaTrackRutherfordScattering3.jpg/250px-AlphaTrackRutherfordScattering3.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:AlphaTrackRutherfordScattering3.jpg)

Alpha particle track in a cloud chamber

As Mott says, "it is a little difficult to picture how it is that an outgoing spherical wave can produce a straight track; we think intuitively that it should ionise atoms at random throughout space". This issue became known at the [Mott problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mott_problem "Mott problem"). Mott then derives the straight track by considering correlations between the positions of the source and two representative atoms, showing that consecutive ionization results from just that state in which all three positions are co-linear. [[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_state_space#cite_note-7)

Relative to classical phase space
---------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Quantum_state_space&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Relative to classical phase space")]

[Classical mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_mechanics "Classical mechanics") for multiple objects describes their motion in terms of a list or [vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_vector "Euclidean vector") of every object's coordinates and velocity. As the objects move, the values in the vector change; the set of all possible values is called a [phase space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phase_space "Phase space").[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_state_space#cite_note-8): 88  In quantum mechanics a state space is similar, however in the state space two vectors which are scalar multiples of each other represent the same state. Furthermore, the character of values in the quantum state differ from the classical values: in the quantum case the values can only be measured statistically (by repetition over many examples) and thus do not have well defined values at every instant of time. [[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_state_space#cite_note-Messiah-5): 294
