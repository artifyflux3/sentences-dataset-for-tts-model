In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), a **fractal** is a [geometric shape](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shape "Shape") containing detailed structure at arbitrarily small scales, usually having a [fractal dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension "Fractal dimension") strictly exceeding the [topological dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_dimension "Topological dimension"). Many fractals appear similar at various scales, as illustrated in successive magnifications of the [Mandelbrot set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mandelbrot_set "Mandelbrot set").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-Mandelbrot1983-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-Falconer-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-patterns-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-vicsek-4) This exhibition of similar patterns at increasingly smaller scales is called [self-similarity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-similarity "Self-similarity"), also known as expanding symmetry or unfolding symmetry; if this replication is exactly the same at every scale, as in the [Menger sponge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Menger_sponge "Menger sponge"), the shape is called [affine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_geometry "Affine geometry") self-similar.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-Gouyet-5) Fractal geometry lies within the mathematical branch of [measure theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Measure_theory "Measure theory").

One way that fractals are different from finite [geometric figures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_figures "Geometric figures") is how they [scale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scaling_(geometry) "Scaling (geometry)"). Doubling the edge lengths of a filled [polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon "Polygon") multiplies its area by four, which is two (the ratio of the new to the old side length) raised to the power of two (the conventional dimension of the filled polygon). Likewise, if the [radius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radius "Radius") of a filled sphere is doubled, its [volume](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume "Volume") scales by eight, which is two (the ratio of the new to the old radius) to the power of three (the conventional dimension of the filled sphere). However, if a fractal's one-dimensional lengths are all doubled, the spatial content of the fractal scales by a power that is not necessarily an [integer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer") and is in general greater than its conventional dimension.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-Mandelbrot1983-1) This power is called the [fractal dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension "Fractal dimension") of the geometric object, to distinguish it from the conventional dimension (which is formally called the [topological dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_dimension "Topological dimension")).[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-Mandelbrot_Chaos-6)

Analytically, many fractals are nowhere [differentiable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differentiable_function "Differentiable function").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-Mandelbrot1983-1)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-vicsek-4) An infinite [fractal curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_curve "Fractal curve") can be conceived of as winding through space differently from an ordinary line – although it is still [topologically 1-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_dimension "Topological dimension"), its fractal dimension indicates that it locally fills space more efficiently than an ordinary line.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-Mandelbrot1983-1)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-Mandelbrot_Chaos-6)

Starting in the 17th century with notions of [recursion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recursion "Recursion"), fractals have moved through increasingly rigorous mathematical treatment to the study of [continuous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_function "Continuous function") but not [differentiable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differentiable_function "Differentiable function") functions in the 19th century by the seminal work of [Bernard Bolzano](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bernard_Bolzano "Bernard Bolzano"), [Bernhard Riemann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bernhard_Riemann "Bernhard Riemann"), and [Karl Weierstrass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karl_Weierstrass "Karl Weierstrass"),[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-7) and on to the coining of the word _[fractal](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/fractal "wikt:fractal")_ in the 20th century with a subsequent burgeoning of interest in fractals and computer-based modelling in the 20th century.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-classics-8)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-MacTutor-9)

There is some disagreement among mathematicians about how the concept of a fractal should be formally defined. Mandelbrot himself summarized it as "beautiful, damn hard, increasingly useful. That's fractals."[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-10) More formally, in 1982 Mandelbrot defined _fractal_ as follows: "A fractal is by definition a set for which the [Hausdorff–Besicovitch dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hausdorff_dimension "Hausdorff dimension") strictly exceeds the [topological dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_dimension "Topological dimension")."[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-11) Later, seeing this as too restrictive, he simplified and expanded the definition to this: "A fractal is a rough or fragmented [geometric shape](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shape "Shape") that can be split into parts, each of which is (at least approximately) a reduced-size copy of the whole."[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-Mandelbrot1983-1) Still later, Mandelbrot proposed "to use _fractal_ without a pedantic definition, to use _[fractal dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal\_dimension "Fractal dimension")_ as a generic term applicable to _all_ the variants".[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-12)

The consensus among mathematicians is that theoretical fractals are infinitely self-similar [iterated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iteration "Iteration") and detailed mathematical constructs, of which many [examples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_fractals_by_Hausdorff_dimension "List of fractals by Hausdorff dimension") have been formulated and studied.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-Mandelbrot1983-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-Falconer-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-patterns-3) Fractals are not limited to geometric patterns, but can also describe processes in time.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-Gouyet-5)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-vicsek-4)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-time_series-13) Fractal patterns with various degrees of self-similarity have been rendered or studied in visual, physical, and aural media[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-music-14) and found in [nature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#fractals_in_nature),[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-heart-15)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-cerebellum-16)[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-neuroscience-17)[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-branching-18)[technology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#fractals_in_technology),[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-soil-19)[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-diagnostic_imaging-20)[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-medicine-21)[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-seismology-22)[art](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#In_creative_works),[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-novel-23)[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-African_art-24) and [architecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Architecture "Architecture").[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-springer.com_9783319324241-25) Fractals are of particular relevance in the field of [chaos theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chaos_theory "Chaos theory") because they show up in the geometric depictions of most chaotic processes (typically either as attractors or as boundaries between basins of attraction).[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-26)

The term "fractal" was coined by the mathematician [Benoît Mandelbrot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beno%C3%AEt_Mandelbrot "Benoît Mandelbrot") in 1975.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-27) Mandelbrot based it on the Latin _[frāctus](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/fractus#Latin "wikt:fractus")_, meaning "broken" or "fractured", and used it to extend the concept of theoretical fractional [dimensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension "Fractal dimension") to geometric [patterns in nature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patterns_in_nature "Patterns in nature").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-Mandelbrot1983-1)[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-Mandelbrot_quote-28)[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-29)

The word "fractal" often has different connotations for mathematicians and the general public, where the public is more likely to be familiar with [fractal art](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_art "Fractal art") than the mathematical concept. The mathematical concept is difficult to define formally, even for mathematicians, but key features can be understood with a little mathematical background.

The feature of "self-similarity", for instance, is easily understood by analogy to zooming in with a lens or other device that zooms in on digital images to uncover finer, previously invisible, new structure. If this is done on fractals, however, no new detail appears; nothing changes and the same pattern repeats over and over, or for some fractals, nearly the same pattern reappears over and over. Self-similarity itself is not necessarily counter-intuitive (e.g., people have pondered self-similarity informally such as in the [infinite regress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinite_regress "Infinite regress") in parallel mirrors or the [homunculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homunculus "Homunculus"), the little man inside the head of the little man inside the head ...). The difference for fractals is that the pattern reproduced must be detailed.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-Mandelbrot1983-1): 166, 18 [[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-Falconer-2)[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-Mandelbrot_quote-28)

This idea of being detailed relates to another feature that can be understood without much mathematical background: Having a [fractal dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension "Fractal dimension") greater than its topological dimension, for instance, refers to how a fractal scales compared to how geometric [shapes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shapes "Shapes") are usually perceived. A straight line, for instance, is conventionally understood to be one-dimensional; if such a figure is [rep-tiled](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rep-tile "Rep-tile") into pieces each 1/3 the length of the original, then there are always three equal pieces. A solid square is understood to be two-dimensional; if such a figure is rep-tiled into pieces each scaled down by a factor of 1/3 in both dimensions, there are a total of 3 2 = 9 pieces.

We see that for ordinary self-similar objects, being n-dimensional means that when it is rep-tiled into pieces each scaled down by a scale-factor of 1/_r_, there are a total of _r_ _n_ pieces. Now, consider the [Koch curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Koch_curve "Koch curve"). It can be rep-tiled into four sub-copies, each scaled down by a scale-factor of 1/3. So, strictly by analogy, we can consider the "dimension" of the Koch curve as being the unique real number _D_ that satisfies 3 _D_ = 4. This number is called the _fractal dimension_ of the Koch curve; it is not the conventionally perceived dimension of a curve. In general, a key property of fractals is that the fractal dimension differs from the _conventionally understood_ dimension (formally called the topological dimension).

3D computer-generated fractal

This also leads to understanding a third feature, that fractals as mathematical equations are "nowhere [differentiable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differentiable_function "Differentiable function")". In a concrete sense, this means fractals cannot be measured in traditional ways.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-Mandelbrot1983-1)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-vicsek-4)[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-Gordon-30) To elaborate, in trying to find the length of a wavy non-fractal curve, one could find straight segments of some measuring tool small enough to lay end to end over the waves, where the pieces could get small enough to be considered to conform to the curve in the normal manner of [measuring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectifiable_curve "Rectifiable curve") with a tape measure. But in measuring an infinitely "wiggly" fractal curve such as the Koch snowflake, one would never find a small enough straight segment to conform to the curve, because the jagged pattern would always re-appear, at arbitrarily small scales, essentially pulling a little more of the tape measure into the total length measured each time one attempted to fit it tighter and tighter to the curve. The result is that one must need infinite tape to perfectly cover the entire curve, i.e. the snowflake has an infinite perimeter.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-Mandelbrot1983-1)

A [Koch snowflake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Koch_snowflake "Koch snowflake") is a fractal that begins with an equilateral triangle and then replaces the middle third of every line segment with a pair of line segments that form an equilateral bump

The history of fractals traces a path from chiefly theoretical studies to modern applications in [computer graphics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_graphics "Computer graphics"), with several notable people contributing canonical fractal forms along the way.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-classics-8)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-MacTutor-9) A common theme in traditional [African architecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Architecture_of_Africa "Architecture of Africa") is the use of fractal scaling, whereby small parts of the structure tend to look similar to larger parts, such as a circular village made of circular houses.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-31) According to [Pickover](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_A._Pickover "Clifford A. Pickover"), the mathematics behind fractals began to take shape in the 17th century when the mathematician and philosopher [Gottfried Leibniz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gottfried_Leibniz "Gottfried Leibniz") pondered [recursive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recursion "Recursion")[self-similarity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-similarity "Self-similarity") (although he made the mistake of thinking that only the [straight line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Straight_line "Straight line") was self-similar in this sense).[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-Pickover-32)

In his writings, Leibniz used the term "fractional exponents", but lamented that "Geometry" did not yet know of them.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-Mandelbrot1983-1): 405  Indeed, according to various historical accounts, after that point few mathematicians tackled the issues and the work of those who did remained obscured largely because of resistance to such unfamiliar emerging concepts, which were sometimes referred to as mathematical "monsters".[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-Gordon-30)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-classics-8)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-MacTutor-9) Thus, it was not until two centuries had passed that on July 18, 1872 [Karl Weierstrass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karl_Weierstrass "Karl Weierstrass") presented the first definition of a [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weierstrass_function "Weierstrass function") with a [graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_of_a_function "Graph of a function") that would today be considered a fractal, having the non-[intuitive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intuition_(knowledge) "Intuition (knowledge)") property of being everywhere [continuous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_function "Continuous function") but [nowhere differentiable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nowhere_differentiable "Nowhere differentiable") at the Royal Prussian Academy of Sciences.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-classics-8): 7 [[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-MacTutor-9)

In addition, the quotient difference becomes arbitrarily large as the summation index increases.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-33) Not long after that, in 1883, [Georg Cantor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georg_Cantor "Georg Cantor"), who attended lectures by Weierstrass,[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-MacTutor-9) published examples of [subsets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subset "Subset") of the real line known as [Cantor sets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cantor_set "Cantor set"), which had unusual properties and are now recognized as fractals.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-classics-8): 11–24  Also in the last part of that century, [Felix Klein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Felix_Klein "Felix Klein") and [Henri Poincaré](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Poincar%C3%A9 "Henri Poincaré") introduced a category of fractal that has come to be called "self-inverse" fractals.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-Mandelbrot1983-1): 166

One of the next milestones came in 1904, when [Helge von Koch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helge_von_Koch "Helge von Koch"), extending ideas of Poincaré and dissatisfied with Weierstrass's abstract and analytic definition, gave a more geometric definition including hand-drawn images of a similar function, which is now called the [Koch snowflake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Koch_snowflake "Koch snowflake").[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-classics-8): 25 [[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-MacTutor-9) Another milestone came a decade later in 1915, when [Wacław Sierpiński](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wac%C5%82aw_Sierpi%C5%84ski "Wacław Sierpiński") constructed his famous [triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sierpinski_triangle "Sierpinski triangle") then, one year later, his [carpet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sierpinski_carpet "Sierpinski carpet"). By 1918, two French mathematicians, [Pierre Fatou](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre_Fatou "Pierre Fatou") and [Gaston Julia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaston_Julia "Gaston Julia"), though working independently, arrived essentially simultaneously at results describing what is now seen as fractal behaviour associated with mapping [complex numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_numbers "Complex numbers") and iterative functions and leading to further ideas about [attractors and repellors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strange_attractors "Strange attractors") (i.e., points that attract or repel other points), which have become very important in the study of fractals.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-vicsek-4)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-classics-8)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-MacTutor-9)

Very shortly after that work was submitted, by March 1918, [Felix Hausdorff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Felix_Hausdorff "Felix Hausdorff") expanded the definition of "dimension", significantly for the evolution of the definition of fractals, to allow for sets to have non-integer dimensions.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-MacTutor-9) The idea of self-similar curves was taken further by [Paul Lévy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_L%C3%A9vy_(mathematician) "Paul Lévy (mathematician)"), who, in his 1938 paper _Plane or Space Curves and Surfaces Consisting of Parts Similar to the Whole_, described a new fractal curve, the [Lévy C curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L%C3%A9vy_C_curve "Lévy C curve").[[notes 1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-levy_note-34)

Different researchers have postulated that without the aid of modern computer graphics, early investigators were limited to what they could depict in manual drawings, so lacked the means to visualize the beauty and appreciate some of the implications of many of the patterns they had discovered (the Julia set, for instance, could only be visualized through a few iterations as very simple drawings).[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-Mandelbrot1983-1): 179 [[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-Gordon-30)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-MacTutor-9) That changed, however, in the 1960s, when [Benoit Mandelbrot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benoit_Mandelbrot "Benoit Mandelbrot") started writing about self-similarity in papers such as _[How Long Is the Coast of Britain? Statistical Self-Similarity and Fractional Dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/How\_Long\_Is\_the\_Coast\_of\_Britain%3F\_Statistical\_Self-Similarity\_and\_Fractional\_Dimension "How Long Is the Coast of Britain? Statistical Self-Similarity and Fractional Dimension")_,[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-35)[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-36) which built on earlier work by [Lewis Fry Richardson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lewis_Fry_Richardson "Lewis Fry Richardson").

In 1975,[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-Mandelbrot_quote-28) Mandelbrot solidified hundreds of years of thought and mathematical development in coining the word "fractal" and illustrated his mathematical definition with striking computer-constructed visualizations. These images, such as of his canonical [Mandelbrot set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mandelbrot_set "Mandelbrot set"), captured the popular imagination; many of them were based on recursion, leading to the popular meaning of the term "fractal".[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-37)[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-Gordon-30)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-classics-8)[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-Pickover-32)

In 1980, [Loren Carpenter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Loren_Carpenter "Loren Carpenter") gave a presentation at the [SIGGRAPH](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SIGGRAPH "SIGGRAPH") where he introduced his software for generating and rendering fractally generated landscapes.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-38)

Definition and characteristics
------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Fractal&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Definition and characteristics")]

One often cited description that Mandelbrot published to describe geometric fractals is "a rough or fragmented [geometric shape](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shape "Shape") that can be split into parts, each of which is (at least approximately) a reduced-size copy of the whole";[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-Mandelbrot1983-1) this is generally helpful but limited. Authors disagree on the exact definition of _fractal_, but most usually elaborate on the basic ideas of self-similarity and the unusual relationship fractals have with the space they are embedded in.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-Mandelbrot1983-1)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-Gouyet-5)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-Falconer-2)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-vicsek-4)[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-39)

One point agreed on is that fractal patterns are characterized by [fractal dimensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension "Fractal dimension"), but whereas these numbers quantify [complexity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complexity "Complexity") (i.e., changing detail with changing scale), they neither uniquely describe nor specify details of how to construct particular fractal patterns.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-40) In 1975 when Mandelbrot coined the word "fractal", he did so to denote an object whose [Hausdorff–Besicovitch dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hausdorff%E2%80%93Besicovitch_dimension "Hausdorff–Besicovitch dimension") is greater than its [topological dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebesgue_covering_dimension "Lebesgue covering dimension").[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-Mandelbrot_quote-28) However, this requirement is not met by [space-filling curves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space-filling_curve "Space-filling curve") such as the [Hilbert curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_curve "Hilbert curve").[[notes 2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-space_filling_note-41)

Because of the trouble involved in finding one definition for fractals, some argue that fractals should not be strictly defined at all. According to [Falconer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kenneth_Falconer_(mathematician) "Kenneth Falconer (mathematician)"), fractals should be only generally characterized by a [gestalt](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/gestalt "wikt:gestalt") of the following features;[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-Falconer-2)

*   Self-similarity, which may include:

*   Exact self-similarity: identical at all scales, such as the [Koch snowflake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#koch)
*   Quasi self-similarity: approximates the same pattern at different scales; may contain small copies of the entire fractal in distorted and degenerate forms; e.g., the [Mandelbrot set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mandelbrot_set "Mandelbrot set")'s satellites are approximations of the entire set, but not exact copies.
*   Statistical self-similarity: repeats a pattern [stochastically](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stochastic "Stochastic") so numerical or statistical measures are preserved across scales; e.g., [randomly generated fractals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#random) like the well-known example of the [coastline of Britain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/How_Long_Is_the_Coast_of_Britain%3F_Statistical_Self-Similarity_and_Fractional_Dimension "How Long Is the Coast of Britain? Statistical Self-Similarity and Fractional Dimension") for which one would not expect to find a segment scaled and repeated as neatly as the repeated unit that defines fractals like the Koch snowflake.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-vicsek-4)
*   Qualitative self-similarity: as in a time series[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-time_series-13)
*   [Multifractal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multifractal "Multifractal") scaling: characterized by more than one fractal dimension or scaling rule

*   Fine or detailed structure at arbitrarily small scales. A consequence of this structure is fractals may have [emergent properties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emergent_properties "Emergent properties")[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-42) (related to the next criterion in this list).
*   Irregularity locally and globally that cannot easily be described in the language of traditional [Euclidean geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_geometry "Euclidean geometry") other than as the limit of a [recursively](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recursion "Recursion") defined sequence of stages. For images of fractal patterns, this has been expressed by phrases such as "smoothly piling up surfaces" and "swirls upon swirls";[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-Mandelbrot_Chaos-6)_see [Common techniques for generating fractals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#algorithms)_.

As a group, these criteria form guidelines for excluding certain cases, such as those that may be self-similar without having other typically fractal features. A straight line, for instance, is self-similar but not fractal because it lacks detail, and is easily described in Euclidean language without a need for recursion.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-Mandelbrot1983-1)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-vicsek-4)

When Mandelbrot introduced the term fractal, he excluded magnification range as a defining characteristic in order to accommodate physical fractals with more limited ranges than their mathematical counterparts.[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-43)

Common techniques for generating fractals
-----------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Fractal&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Common techniques for generating fractals")]

[![Image 18](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/KarperienFractalBranch.jpg/250px-KarperienFractalBranch.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:KarperienFractalBranch.jpg)

Self-similar branching pattern modeled [in silico](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/In_silico "In silico") using [L-systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L-systems "L-systems") principles[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-branching-18)

Images of fractals can be created by [fractal generating programs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal-generating_software "Fractal-generating software"). Because of the [butterfly effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Butterfly_effect "Butterfly effect"), a small change in a single variable can have an [unpredictable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Predictability "Predictability") outcome.

*   _[Iterated function systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iterated\_function\_systems "Iterated function systems") (IFS)_ – use fixed geometric replacement rules; may be stochastic or deterministic;[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-IFS-44) e.g., [Koch snowflake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Koch_snowflake "Koch snowflake"), [Cantor set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cantor_set "Cantor set"), Haferman carpet,[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-45)[Sierpinski carpet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sierpinski_carpet "Sierpinski carpet"), [Sierpinski gasket](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sierpinski_gasket "Sierpinski gasket"), [Peano curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peano_curve "Peano curve"), [Harter-Heighway dragon curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dragon_curve "Dragon curve"), [T-square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/T-square_(fractal) "T-square (fractal)"), [Menger sponge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Menger_sponge "Menger sponge")
*   _[Strange attractors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strange\_attractor "Strange attractor")_ – use iterations of a map or solutions of a system of initial-value differential or difference equations that exhibit chaos (e.g., see [multifractal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#multifractal) image, or the [logistic map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logistic_map "Logistic map"))
*   _[L-systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L-system "L-system")_ – use string rewriting; may resemble branching patterns, such as in plants, biological cells (e.g., neurons and immune system cells[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-branching-18)), blood vessels, pulmonary structure,[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-modeling_vasculature-46) etc. or [turtle graphics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turtle_graphics "Turtle graphics") patterns such as [space-filling curves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space-filling_curves "Space-filling curves") and tilings
*   _Escape-time fractals_ – use a [formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formula "Formula") or [recurrence relation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recurrence_relation "Recurrence relation") at each point in a space (such as the [complex plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_plane "Complex plane")); usually quasi-self-similar; also known as "orbit" fractals; e.g., the [Mandelbrot set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mandelbrot_set "Mandelbrot set"), [Julia set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Julia_set "Julia set"), [Burning Ship fractal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burning_Ship_fractal "Burning Ship fractal"), [Nova fractal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nova_fractal "Nova fractal") and [Lyapunov fractal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lyapunov_fractal "Lyapunov fractal"). The 2d vector fields that are generated by one or two iterations of escape-time formulae also give rise to a fractal form when points (or pixel data) are passed through this field repeatedly.
*   _Random fractals_ – use stochastic rules; e.g., [Lévy flight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L%C3%A9vy_flight "Lévy flight"), [percolation clusters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Percolation_theory "Percolation theory"), [self avoiding walks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-avoiding_walk "Self-avoiding walk"), [fractal landscapes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_landscapes "Fractal landscapes"), trajectories of [Brownian motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brownian_motion "Brownian motion") and the [Brownian tree](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brownian_tree "Brownian tree") (i.e., dendritic fractals generated by modeling [diffusion-limited aggregation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffusion-limited_aggregation "Diffusion-limited aggregation") or reaction-limited aggregation clusters).[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-vicsek-4)

[![Image 19](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Finite_subdivision_of_a_radial_link.png/250px-Finite_subdivision_of_a_radial_link.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Finite_subdivision_of_a_radial_link.png)

A fractal generated by a [finite subdivision rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_subdivision_rule "Finite subdivision rule") for an [alternating link](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alternating_link "Alternating link")

*   _[Finite subdivision rules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite\_subdivision\_rule "Finite subdivision rule")_ – use a recursive [topological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological "Topological") algorithm for refining tilings[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-finite-47) and they are similar to the process of [cell division](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_division "Cell division").[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-biol-48) The iterative processes used in creating the [Cantor set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cantor_set "Cantor set") and the [Sierpinski carpet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sierpinski_carpet "Sierpinski carpet") are examples of finite subdivision rules, as is [barycentric subdivision](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barycentric_subdivision "Barycentric subdivision").

[![Image 20](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Julia-Set_z2%2Bc_ani.gif/250px-Julia-Set_z2%2Bc_ani.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Julia-Set_z2%2Bc_ani.gif)

[Fractal art](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_art "Fractal art") made from a [Julia-Set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Julia_set "Julia set")

Fractal patterns have been modeled extensively, albeit within a range of scales rather than infinitely, owing to the practical limits of physical time and space. Models may simulate theoretical fractals or [natural phenomena with fractal features](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#fractals_in_nature). The outputs of the modelling process may be highly artistic renderings, outputs for investigation, or benchmarks for [fractal analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_analysis "Fractal analysis"). Some specific applications of fractals to technology are listed [elsewhere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#fractals_in_technology). Images and other outputs of modelling are normally referred to as being "fractals" even if they do not have strictly fractal characteristics, such as when it is possible to zoom into a region of the fractal image that does not exhibit any fractal properties. Also, these may include calculation or display [artifacts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artifact_(error) "Artifact (error)") which are not characteristics of true fractals.

Modeled fractals may be sounds,[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-music-14) digital images, electrochemical patterns, [circadian rhythms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circadian_rhythm "Circadian rhythm"),[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-49) etc. Fractal patterns have been reconstructed in physical 3-dimensional space[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-medicine-21): 10  and virtually, often called "[in silico](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/In_silico "In silico")" modeling.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-modeling_vasculature-46) Models of fractals are generally created using [fractal-generating software](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal-generating_software "Fractal-generating software") that implements techniques such as those outlined above.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-vicsek-4)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-time_series-13)[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-medicine-21) As one illustration, trees, ferns, cells of the nervous system,[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-branching-18) blood and lung vasculature,[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-modeling_vasculature-46) and other branching [patterns in nature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patterns_in_nature "Patterns in nature") can be modeled on a computer by using recursive [algorithms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algorithm "Algorithm") and [L-systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L-systems "L-systems") techniques.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-branching-18)

The recursive nature of some patterns is obvious in certain examples—a branch from a tree or a [frond](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frond "Frond") from a [fern](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fern "Fern") is a miniature replica of the whole: not identical, but similar in nature. Similarly, random fractals have been used to describe/create many highly irregular real-world objects, such as coastlines and mountains. A limitation of modeling fractals is that resemblance of a fractal model to a natural phenomenon does not prove that the phenomenon being modeled is formed by a process similar to the modeling algorithms.

### Fractals in cell biology

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Fractal&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Fractals in cell biology")]

Fractals often appear in the realm of living organisms where they arise through branching processes and other complex pattern formation. Richard Taylor and co-workers have shown that the dendritic branches of neurons form fractal patterns.[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-74) Ian Wong and co-workers have shown that migrating cells can form fractals by clustering and [branching](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Branching_process "Branching process").[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-75)[Nerve cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neuron "Neuron") function through processes at the cell surface, with phenomena that are enhanced by largely increasing the surface to volume ratio. As a consequence nerve cells often are found to form into fractal patterns.[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-76) These processes are crucial in cell [physiology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physiology "Physiology") and different [pathologies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pathology "Pathology").[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-77)

Multiple subcellular structures also are found to assemble into fractals. [Diego Krapf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diego_Krapf "Diego Krapf") has shown that through branching processes the [actin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Actin "Actin") filaments in human cells assemble into fractal patterns.[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-78) Similarly Matthias Weiss showed that the [endoplasmic reticulum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endoplasmic_reticulum "Endoplasmic reticulum") displays fractal features.[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-79) The current understanding is that fractals are ubiquitous in cell biology, from [proteins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protein "Protein"), to [organelles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organelle "Organelle"), to whole cells.

**Fractal expressionism** is used to distinguish [fractal art](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_art "Fractal art") generated directly by artists from fractal art generated using mathematics and/or computers.[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-80)

Since 1999 numerous scientific groups have performed fractal analysis on over 50 paintings created by [Jackson Pollock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jackson_Pollock "Jackson Pollock") by pouring paint directly onto horizontal canvasses, see for example.[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-81)[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-82)[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-C._Redies,_J_2007-83)[[82]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-S._Lee,_S_2007-84)[[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-85)[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-D.J._Field,_2008-86)[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-87)[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-88)[[87]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-89)[[88]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-90)[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-91)[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-92)[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-93) In 2015, fractal analysis was used to achieve a 93% success rate in distinguishing real from imitation Pollocks. [[92]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-94) A 2024 study used an artificial intelligence technique based on fractals to achieve a 99% success rate. [[93]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-95)

[Decalcomania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decalcomania "Decalcomania"), a technique used by artists such as [Max Ernst](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Max_Ernst "Max Ernst"), can produce fractal-like patterns.[[94]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-96) It involves pressing paint between two surfaces and pulling them apart.

Cyberneticist [Ron Eglash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ron_Eglash "Ron Eglash") has suggested that fractal geometry and mathematics are prevalent in [African art](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/African_art "African art"), games, [divination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divination "Divination"), trade, and architecture. Circular houses appear in circles of circles, rectangular houses in rectangles of rectangles, and so on. Such scaling patterns can also be found in African textiles, sculpture, and even cornrow hairstyles.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-African_art-24)[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-97)[Hokky Situngkir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hokky_Situngkir "Hokky Situngkir") also suggested the similar properties in Indonesian traditional art, [batik](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Batik "Batik"), and [ornaments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ornament_(art) "Ornament (art)") found in traditional houses.[[96]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-98)[[97]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-99)

Ethnomathematician Ron Eglash has discussed the planned layout of [Benin city](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benin_city "Benin city") using fractals as the basis, not only in the city itself and the villages but even in the rooms of houses. He commented that "When Europeans first came to Africa, they considered the architecture very disorganised and thus primitive. It never occurred to them that the Africans might have been using a form of mathematics that they hadn't even discovered yet."[[98]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-100)

In a 1996 interview with [Michael Silverblatt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michael_Silverblatt "Michael Silverblatt"), [David Foster Wallace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Foster_Wallace "David Foster Wallace") explained that the structure of the first draft of _[Infinite Jest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinite\_Jest "Infinite Jest")_ he gave to his editor Michael Pietsch was inspired by fractals, specifically the [Sierpinski triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sierpinski_triangle "Sierpinski triangle") (a.k.a. Sierpinski gasket), but that the edited novel is "more like a lopsided Sierpinsky Gasket".[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-novel-23)

Some works by the Dutch artist [M. C. Escher](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M._C._Escher "M. C. Escher"), such as [Circle Limit III](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle_Limit_III "Circle Limit III"), contain shapes repeated to infinity that become smaller and smaller as they get near to the edges, in a pattern that would always look the same if zoomed in.

Biophilic fractals are patterns designed to induce the health and well-being benefits associated with exposure to nature's scenery.[[99]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-101) These include stress-reduction and enhanced cognitive capacity. Designers and architects incorporate biophilic fractals into the built environment to counter the fact that people spend 92% of their time indoors and away from nature's scenery. The Fractal Chapel designed by INNOCAD architecture in the state hospital in Graz, Austria, is a prominent example and recipient of the 2025 IIDA (International Interior Design Association) Best of Competition Award.

### Physiological responses: Fractal Fluency

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Fractal&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Physiological responses: Fractal Fluency")]

Fractal fluency is a [neuroscience](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neuroscience "Neuroscience") model that proposes that, through exposure to nature's fractal scenery, people's [visual systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visual_system "Visual system") have adapted to efficiently process fractals with ease. This adaptation occurs at many stages of the visual system, from the way people's eyes move to which regions of the brain get activated.[[100]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-102) Fluency puts the viewer in a ‘comfort zone’ so inducing an aesthetic experience. Neuroscience experiments have shown that Jackson Pollock's fractal paintings induce the same positive [physiological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physiology "Physiology") responses in the observer as nature's fractals and mathematical fractals.[[101]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-R.P._Taylor,_B._Spehar_2011-103) This shows that fractal expressionism is related to fractal fluency by providing motivation for artists, such as Pollock, to use Fractal Expressionism in their art to appeal to people.

Humans appear to be especially well-adapted to processing fractal patterns with [fractal dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension "Fractal dimension") between 1.3 and 1.5. [[102]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-104) When humans view fractal patterns with fractal dimensions in this range, these fractals reduce physiological stress and boost cognitive abilities.[[103]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#cite_note-Taylor_2006-105)
