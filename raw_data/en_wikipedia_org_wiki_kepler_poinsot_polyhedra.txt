In [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry"), a **Kepler–Poinsot polyhedron** is any of four [regular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polyhedron "Regular polyhedron")[star polyhedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_polyhedron "Star polyhedron").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler-Poinsot_polyhedra#cite_note-1)

They may be obtained by [stellating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellation "Stellation") the regular [convex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_polyhedron "Convex polyhedron")[dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dodecahedron "Dodecahedron") and [icosahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Icosahedron "Icosahedron"), and differ from these in having regular [pentagrammic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentagram "Pentagram")[faces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Face_(geometry) "Face (geometry)") or [vertex figures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_figure "Vertex figure"). They can all be seen as three-dimensional analogues of the pentagram in one way or another.

The Kepler–Poinsot polyhedra are the regular [star polyhedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_polyhedra "Star polyhedra"), obtained by extending both [regular icosahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron "Regular icosahedron") and [regular dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron "Regular dodecahedron"), an operation named [stellation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellation "Stellation"). This operation results in four different polyhedra:[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler-Poinsot_polyhedra#cite_note-barnes-2)

*   [Great dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_dodecahedron "Great dodecahedron"): constructed from attaching twelve [pentagonal pyramids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentagonal_pyramid "Pentagonal pyramid") (with [regular polygonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polygon "Regular polygon") faces) onto the face of a regular dodecahedron, and attached again with thirty [wedges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wedge_(geometry) "Wedge (geometry)").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler-Poinsot_polyhedra#cite_note-cromwell-3) However, this can be constructed alternatively by [removing its polygonal faces without changing or creating new vertices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Faceting "Faceting") of a regular icosahedron.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler-Poinsot_polyhedra#cite_note-inchbald-4)
*   [Small stellated dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Small_stellated_dodecahedron "Small stellated dodecahedron"): attaching twelve pentagonal pyramids onto a regular dodecahedron's faces.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler-Poinsot_polyhedra#cite_note-kappraff-5)[Topologically](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology "Topology"), this shares the same surface as the [pentakis dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentakis_dodecahedron "Pentakis dodecahedron").
*   [Great icosahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_icosahedron "Great icosahedron"); and
*   [Great stellated dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_stellated_dodecahedron "Great stellated dodecahedron"): constructed from a great dodecahedron with twenty asymmetric triangular bipyramids, attaching to the hollow between the wedges.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler-Poinsot_polyhedra#cite_note-FOOTNOTECromwell1997[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidOJowej1QWpoCpgPA266_266&ndash;267]-6)

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Relationship_among_regular_star_polyhedra_%28direction_colors%29.png/500px-Relationship_among_regular_star_polyhedra_%28direction_colors%29.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Relationship_among_regular_star_polyhedra_(direction_colors).png)

Conway's system of relations between the six polyhedra (ordered vertically by [density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Density_(polytope) "Density (polytope)")). Here, the green arrow represents the connection of [duality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_polyhedron "Dual polyhedron"), blue represents the greatening (g), and orange represents the stellation (s).[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler-Poinsot_polyhedra#cite_note-conway-2008-7)

[John Conway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Horton_Conway "John Horton Conway") introduces operators for the Kepler–Poinsot polyhedra known as _greatenings_—(g), maintaining the type of faces, shifting and resizing them into parallel planes—and _stellations_—(s), changing pentagonal faces into pentagrams—of the convex solids. In his [naming convention](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellation#Naming_stellations "Stellation"), the small stellated dodecahedron is just the _stellated dodecahedron_.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler-Poinsot_polyhedra#cite_note-conway-2008-7)

By the construction above, these figures have [pentagrams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentagram "Pentagram") (star pentagons) as faces or vertex figures.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler-Poinsot_polyhedra#cite_note-barnes-2) The [dual polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_polyhedron "Dual polyhedron") of a great dodecahedron is the small stellated dodecahedron, and the dual of a great icosahedron is the great stellated dodecahedron.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler-Poinsot_polyhedra#cite_note-wenninger-8) The four share the symmetry as both regular icosahedron and regular dodecahedron, the [icosahedral symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Icosahedral_symmetry "Icosahedral symmetry").[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler-Poinsot_polyhedra#cite_note-dubrovin-9)

### Euler characteristic

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Kepler%E2%80%93Poinsot_polyhedron&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Euler characteristic")]

A Kepler–Poinsot polyhedron covers its circumscribed sphere more than once, with the centers of faces acting as winding points in the figures which have pentagrammic faces, and the vertices in the others. Because of this, they are not necessarily topologically equivalent to the sphere as Platonic solids are, and in particular, the [Euler relation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler_characteristic "Euler characteristic")![Image 3: {\displaystyle \chi =V-E+F=2\ }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eb9fa257b4c8ec254119066bfa8f11ea09f84806) does not always hold. Schläfli held that all polyhedra must have χ = 2, and he rejected the small stellated dodecahedron and great dodecahedron as proper polyhedra. This view was never widely held.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler-Poinsot_polyhedra#cite_note-10)

A modified form of Euler's formula, using [density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Density_(polytope) "Density (polytope)") (![Image 4: {\displaystyle D}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f34a0c600395e5d4345287e21fb26efd386990e6)) of the [vertex figures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_figure "Vertex figure") (![Image 5: {\displaystyle d_{v}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4034edfd613443b0938cb4da1899ab1ce102da2a)) and faces (![Image 6: {\displaystyle d_{f}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bb4b64bf957b1bcba033e0b27e5575a530b5d46c)) was given by [Arthur Cayley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arthur_Cayley "Arthur Cayley"), and holds both for convex polyhedra (where the correction factors are all 1), and the Kepler–Poinsot polyhedra:[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler-Poinsot_polyhedra#cite_note-huylebrouck-11)![Image 7: {\displaystyle d_{v}V-E+d_{f}F=2D,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b554cfaaf7be4cc5397434eadecbfe51b61f811f) and by this calculation, the density of the great icosahedron and the great stellated dodecahedron are 7, whereas the great dodecahedron and the small stellated dodecahedron are 3.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler-Poinsot_polyhedra#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarnes2012[httpsbooksgooglecombooksid7YCUBUd-4BQCpgPA47_47]-12)

### Duality and Petrie polygons

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Kepler%E2%80%93Poinsot_polyhedron&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Duality and Petrie polygons")]

The Kepler–Poinsot polyhedra exist in [dual](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_polyhedron "Dual polyhedron") pairs. Duals have the same [Petrie polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petrie_polygon "Petrie polygon"), or more precisely, Petrie polygons with the same two-dimensional projection.

The following images show the two [dual compounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_compound "Dual compound") with the same [edge radius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Midsphere "Midsphere"). They also show that the Petrie polygons are [skew](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skew_polygon "Skew polygon"). Two relationships described in the article below are also easily seen in the images: That the violet edges are the same, and that the green faces lie in the same planes.

Most, if not all, of the Kepler–Poinsot polyhedra were known of in some form or other before Kepler. A small stellated dodecahedron appears in a marble tarsia (inlay panel) on the floor of [St. Mark's Basilica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/St._Mark%27s_Basilica "St. Mark's Basilica"), [Venice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Venice "Venice"), Italy. It dates from the 15th century and is sometimes attributed to [Paolo Uccello](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paolo_Uccello "Paolo Uccello").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler-Poinsot_polyhedra#cite_note-coxeter-2013-13)

In his _[Perspectiva corporum regularium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perspectiva\_corporum\_regularium "Perspectiva corporum regularium")_, a book of woodcuts published in 1568, [Wenzel Jamnitzer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wenzel_Jamnitzer "Wenzel Jamnitzer") depicts the [great stellated dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_stellated_dodecahedron "Great stellated dodecahedron") and a [great dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_dodecahedron "Great dodecahedron"). It is clear from the general arrangement of the book that he regarded only the five Platonic solids as regular.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler-Poinsot_polyhedra#cite_note-innocenzi-14)[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler-Poinsot_polyhedra#cite_note-ss-15)

The small and great stellated dodecahedra, sometimes called the **Kepler polyhedra**, were first recognized as regular by [Johannes Kepler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johannes_Kepler "Johannes Kepler") around 1619.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler-Poinsot_polyhedra#cite_note-coxeter-1999-16) He obtained them by [stellating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellation "Stellation") the regular convex dodecahedron, for the first time treating it as a surface rather than a solid. He noticed that by extending the edges or faces of the convex dodecahedron until they met again, he could obtain star pentagons. Further, he recognized that these star pentagons are also regular. In this way, he constructed the two stellated dodecahedra. Each has the central convex region of each face "hidden" within the interior, with only the triangular arms visible. Kepler's final step was to recognize that these polyhedra fit the definition of regularity, even though they were not [convex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_polyhedron "Convex polyhedron"), as the traditional [Platonic solids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platonic_solid "Platonic solid") were.

In 1809, [Louis Poinsot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis_Poinsot "Louis Poinsot") rediscovered Kepler's figures by assembling star pentagons around each vertex. He also assembled convex polygons around star vertices to discover two more regular stars, the great icosahedron and great dodecahedron. Some people call these two the **Poinsot polyhedra**. Poinsot did not know if he had discovered all the regular star polyhedra. Three years later, [Augustin Cauchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Augustin_Cauchy "Augustin Cauchy") proved the list complete by [stellating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellation "Stellation") the [Platonic solids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platonic_solid "Platonic solid"),[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler-Poinsot_polyhedra#cite_note-cauchy-17) and almost half a century after that, in 1858, [Bertrand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Bertrand "Joseph Bertrand") provided a more elegant proof by [faceting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Faceting "Faceting") them.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler-Poinsot_polyhedra#cite_note-bertrand-18) The following year, [Arthur Cayley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arthur_Cayley "Arthur Cayley") gave the Kepler–Poinsot polyhedra the names by which they are generally known today.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler-Poinsot_polyhedra#cite_note-cayley-19) A hundred years later, [John Conway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Horton_Conway "John Horton Conway") developed a [systematic terminology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellation#Naming_stellations "Stellation") for stellations in up to four dimensions. Within this scheme, the [small stellated dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Small_stellated_dodecahedron "Small stellated dodecahedron") is just the _stellated dodecahedron_.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler-Poinsot_polyhedra#cite_note-conway-2008-7)

Artist [M. C. Escher](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M._C._Escher "M. C. Escher")'s interest in geometric forms often led to works based on or including regular solids; _[Gravitation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitation\_(M.\_C.\_Escher) "Gravitation (M. C. Escher)")_ is based on a small stellated dodecahedron.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler-Poinsot_polyhedra#cite_note-barnes-2) A [dissection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dissection_(geometry) "Dissection (geometry)") of the great dodecahedron was used for the 1980s puzzle [Alexander's Star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander%27s_Star "Alexander's Star").[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler-Poinsot_polyhedra#cite_note-alexander's-20) Norwegian artist [Vebjørn Sand's sculpture _The Kepler Star_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vebj%C3%B8rn_Sand#Kepler_Star "Vebjørn Sand") is displayed near [Oslo Airport, Gardermoen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oslo_Airport,_Gardermoen "Oslo Airport, Gardermoen"). The star spans 14 meters and consists of both a regular icosahedron and a regular dodecahedron inside a great stellated dodecahedron.
