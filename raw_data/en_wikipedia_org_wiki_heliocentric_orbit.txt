Orbit around the barycenter of the Sun

"Solar orbit" redirects here. For the orbit of the Sun, see [Sun ¬ßMotion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun#Motion "Sun").

[![Image 18](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Solar_system_barycenter.svg/250px-Solar_system_barycenter.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Solar_system_barycenter.svg)

Motion of the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System")'s barycenter relative to the Sun

A **heliocentric orbit** (also called **circumsolar orbit**) is an [orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbit "Orbit") around the [barycenter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barycenter_(astronomy) "Barycenter (astronomy)") of the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System"), which is usually located within or very near the surface of the [Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun "Sun"). All [planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planet "Planet"), [comets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comet "Comet"), and [asteroids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asteroid "Asteroid") in the Solar System, and the Sun itself are in such orbits, as are many artificial [probes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uncrewed_spacecraft#Space_probes_and_observatories "Uncrewed spacecraft") and pieces of [debris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_debris "Space debris"). The moons of planets in the Solar System, by contrast, are not in heliocentric orbits, as they orbit their respective planet (although the Moon has a convex orbit around the Sun).

The barycenter of the Solar System, while always very near the Sun, moves through space as time passes, depending on where other large bodies in the Solar System, such as [Jupiter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jupiter "Jupiter") and other large [gas giants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas_giant "Gas giant"), are located at that time. A similar phenomenon allows the detection of [exoplanets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exoplanet "Exoplanet") by way of the [radial-velocity method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doppler_spectroscopy "Doppler spectroscopy").

The _helio-_ prefix is derived from the [Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_language "Greek language") word "·º•ŒªŒπŒøœÇ", meaning "Sun", and also [Helios](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helios "Helios"), the [personification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Personification "Personification") of the Sun in [Greek mythology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_mythology "Greek mythology").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heliocentric_orbit#cite_note-dictionary.com-1)

The first spacecraft to be put in a heliocentric orbit was [Luna 1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luna_1 "Luna 1") in 1959. An incorrectly timed upper-stage burn caused it to miss its planned impact on the [Moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moon "Moon").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heliocentric_orbit#cite_note-2)

Trans-Mars injection
--------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Heliocentric_orbit&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Trans-Mars injection")]

[![Image 19](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Trans-Mars_injection.svg/250px-Trans-Mars_injection.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Trans-Mars_injection.svg)

Trans-Mars injection diagram.

A = Hohmann transfer orbit. B = Conjunction mission. C = Opposition mission

A _trans-Mars injection (TMI)_ is a heliocentric orbit in which a [propulsive maneuver](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_maneuver "Orbital maneuver") is used to set a [spacecraft](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spacecraft "Spacecraft") on a [trajectory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trajectory "Trajectory"), also known as **Mars transfer orbit**, which will place it as far as [Mars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mars "Mars") orbit.

Every two years, [low-energy transfer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Low-energy_transfer "Low-energy transfer") windows open up, which allow movement between the two planets with the lowest possible [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Delta-v "Delta-v") requirements. Transfer injections can place spacecraft into either a [Hohmann transfer orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hohmann_transfer_orbit "Hohmann transfer orbit") or [bi-elliptic transfer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bi-elliptic_transfer "Bi-elliptic transfer") orbit. Trans-Mars injections can be either a single maneuver burn, such as that used by the [NASA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NASA "NASA")[MAVEN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MAVEN "MAVEN") orbiter in 2013, or a series of perigee kicks, such as that used by the [ISRO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISRO "ISRO")[Mars Orbiter Mission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mars_Orbiter_Mission "Mars Orbiter Mission") in 2013.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heliocentric_orbit#cite_note-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heliocentric_orbit#cite_note-4)
