**Plants** are the [eukaryotes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eukaryote "Eukaryote") that comprise the [kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_(biology) "Kingdom (biology)")**Plantae**; they are predominantly [photosynthetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photosynthesis "Photosynthesis"). This means that they obtain their energy from [sunlight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sunlight "Sunlight"), using [chloroplasts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chloroplast "Chloroplast") derived from [endosymbiosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endosymbiosis "Endosymbiosis") with [cyanobacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyanobacteria "Cyanobacteria") to produce [sugars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sugar "Sugar") from [carbon dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_dioxide "Carbon dioxide") and water, using the green pigment [chlorophyll](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chlorophyll "Chlorophyll"). Exceptions are [parasitic plants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parasitic_plant "Parasitic plant") that have lost the genes for chlorophyll and photosynthesis, and obtain their energy from other plants or fungi. Most plants are [multicellular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multicellular_organism "Multicellular organism"), except for some green algae.

Historically, as in [Aristotle's biology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotle%27s_biology "Aristotle's biology"), the plant kingdom encompassed all living things that were not [animals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Animal "Animal"), and included [algae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algae "Algae") and [fungi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fungi "Fungi"). Definitions have narrowed since then; current definitions exclude fungi and some of the algae. By the definition used in this article, plants form the [clade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clade "Clade")[Viridiplantae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viridiplantae "Viridiplantae") (green plants), which consists of the [green algae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green_algae "Green algae") and the [embryophytes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Embryophyte "Embryophyte") or land plants ([hornworts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hornwort "Hornwort"), [liverworts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liverworts "Liverworts"), [mosses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moss "Moss"), [lycophytes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lycophyte "Lycophyte"), [ferns](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fern "Fern"), [conifers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conifer "Conifer") and other [gymnosperms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gymnosperm "Gymnosperm"), and [flowering plants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flowering_plant "Flowering plant")). A definition based on [genomes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genome "Genome") includes the Viridiplantae, along with the [red algae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_algae "Red algae") and the [glaucophytes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glaucophyte "Glaucophyte"), in the clade [Archaeplastida](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archaeplastida "Archaeplastida").

There are about 380,000 known [species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Species "Species") of plants, of which the majority, some 260,000, [produce seeds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spermatophyte "Spermatophyte"). They range in size from single cells to the tallest [trees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tree "Tree"). Green plants provide a substantial proportion of the world's molecular oxygen; the sugars they create supply the energy for most of Earth's [ecosystems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem "Ecosystem"), and other [organisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism "Organism"), including animals, either [eat plants directly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herbivore "Herbivore") or rely on organisms which do so.

[Grain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grain "Grain"), [fruit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fruit "Fruit"), and [vegetables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vegetable "Vegetable") are basic human foods and have been [domesticated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domesticated "Domesticated") for millennia. People use plants [for many purposes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_uses_of_plants "Human uses of plants"), such as [building materials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Building_material "Building material"), ornaments, [writing materials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Writing_material "Writing material"), and, in great variety, [for medicines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medicinal_plant "Medicinal plant"). The scientific study of plants is known as [botany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Botany "Botany"), a branch of [biology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology "Biology").

Definition
----------

### Taxonomic history

All living things were traditionally placed into one of two groups, plants and [animals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Animal "Animal"). This classification dates from [Aristotle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotle "Aristotle") (384–322 BC), who distinguished different levels of beings in [his biology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotle%27s_biology#Classification "Aristotle's biology"),[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-5) based on whether living things had a "sensitive soul" or like plants only a "vegetative soul".[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-6)[Theophrastus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theophrastus "Theophrastus"), Aristotle's student, continued his work in plant taxonomy and classification.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-OxBib-7) Much later, [Linnaeus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus "Carl Linnaeus") (1707–1778) created the basis of the modern system of [scientific classification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_classification "Scientific classification"), but retained the animal and plant [kingdoms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_(biology) "Kingdom (biology)"), naming the plant kingdom the Vegetabilia.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-OxBib-7)

### Evolutionary history

The ancestors of land plants evolved in water. An algal scum formed on the land [1,200](https://geoltime.github.io/?Ma=1,200)million years ago, but it was not until the [Ordovician](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordovician "Ordovician"), around [450](https://geoltime.github.io/?Ma=450)million years ago, that the first land plants appeared, with a level of organisation like that of bryophytes.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-34)[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-35) However, fossils of organisms with a flattened [thallus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thallus "Thallus") in [Precambrian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Precambrian "Precambrian") rocks suggest that multicellular freshwater eukaryotes existed over 1000 mya.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-36)

Primitive land plants began to diversify in the late [Silurian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silurian "Silurian"), around [420](https://geoltime.github.io/?Ma=420)million years ago. Bryophytes, club mosses, and ferns then appear in the fossil record.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-37) Early plant anatomy is preserved in cellular detail in an early [Devonian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Devonian "Devonian") fossil assemblage from the [Rhynie chert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhynie_chert "Rhynie chert"). These early plants were preserved by being petrified in [chert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chert "Chert") formed in silica-rich volcanic hot springs.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-GarwoodOliver2019-38)

By the end of the Devonian, most of the basic features of plants today were present, including roots, leaves and [secondary wood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xylem "Xylem") in trees such as _[Archaeopteris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archaeopteris "Archaeopteris")_.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-39)[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-Rothwelletal989-40) The [Carboniferous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carboniferous "Carboniferous") period saw the development of forests in swampy environments dominated by clubmosses and horsetails, including some as large as trees, and the appearance of early [gymnosperms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gymnosperm "Gymnosperm"), the first [seed plants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spermatophyte "Spermatophyte").[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-41) The [Permo-Triassic extinction event](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permo-Triassic_extinction_event "Permo-Triassic extinction event") radically changed the structures of communities.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-McElwain_Punyasena_2007_pp._548%E2%80%93557-42) This may have set the scene for the [evolution of flowering plants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flowering_plant#Fossil_history "Flowering plant") in the [Triassic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triassic "Triassic") (~[200](https://geoltime.github.io/?Ma=200)million years ago), with an [adaptive radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adaptive_radiation "Adaptive radiation") in the [Cretaceous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cretaceous "Cretaceous") so rapid that Darwin called it an "[abominable mystery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abominable_mystery "Abominable mystery")".[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-Friedman-2009-43)[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-Berendse_Scheffer_2009_pp._865%E2%80%93872-44)[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-Herendeen-2017-45)[Conifers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conifer ### Phylogeny

In 2019, a [phylogeny](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phylogeny "Phylogeny") based on [genomes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genome "Genome") and [transcriptomes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transcriptome "Transcriptome") from 1,153 plant species was proposed.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-1000plants-48) The placing of algal groups is supported by phylogenies based on genomes from the [Mesostigmatophyceae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mesostigmatophyceae "Mesostigmatophyceae") and [Chlorokybophyceae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chlorokybophyceae "Chlorokybophyceae") that have since been sequenced. Both the "chlorophyte algae" and the "streptophyte algae" are treated as [paraphyletic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paraphyletic "Paraphyletic") (vertical bars beside phylogenetic tree diagram) in this analysis, as the land plants arose from within those groups.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-MvirideGenome1-49)[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-Mv_and_Ca_Genomes-50) The classification of Bryophyta is supported both by Puttick _et al._ 2018,[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-Puttick2018-51) and by phylogenies involving the hornwort genomes that have also since been sequenced.[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-HornwortGenome1-52)[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-HornwortGenome2-53)

Physiology
----------

### Plant cells

[![Image 17](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Plant_cell_structure-en.svg/500px-Plant_cell_structure-en.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Plant_cell_structure-en.svg)

Plant cell structure

Plant cells have distinctive features that other eukaryotic cells (such as those of animals) lack. These include the large water-filled central [vacuole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vacuole "Vacuole"), [chloroplasts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chloroplast "Chloroplast"), and the strong flexible [cell wall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_wall "Cell wall"), which is outside the [cell membrane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_membrane "Cell membrane"). Chloroplasts are [derived from what was once a symbiosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symbiogenesis "Symbiogenesis") of a non-photosynthetic cell and photosynthetic [cyanobacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyanobacteria "Cyanobacteria"). The cell wall, made mostly of [cellulose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cellulose "Cellulose"), allows plant cells to [swell up with water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turgor_pressure "Turgor pressure") without bursting. The vacuole allows the cell to change in size while the amount of [cytoplasm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cytoplasm "Cytoplasm") stays the same.[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-54)

### Plant structure

[![Image 18](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Plant_Anatomy.svg/250px-Plant_Anatomy.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Plant_Anatomy.svg)

Anatomy of a seed plant. 1. [Shoot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shoot_(botany) "Shoot (botany)") system. 2. [Root](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Root "Root") system. 3. [Hypocotyl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypocotyl "Hypocotyl"). 4. [Terminal bud](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terminal_bud "Terminal bud"). 5. [Leaf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leaf "Leaf") blade. 6. Internode. 7. [Axillary bud](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axillary_bud "Axillary bud"). 8. [Petiole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petiole_(botany) "Petiole (botany)"). 9. Stem. 10. Node. 11. [Tap root](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taproot "Taproot"). 12. [Root hairs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Root_hair "Root hair"). 13. Root tip. 14. [Root cap](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Root_cap "Root cap")

Most plants are [multicellular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multicellular "Multicellular"). Plant cells [differentiate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cellular_differentiation "Cellular differentiation") into multiple cell types, forming tissues such as the [vascular tissue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vascular_tissue "Vascular tissue") with specialized [xylem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xylem "Xylem") and [phloem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phloem "Phloem") of leaf veins and [stems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant_stem "Plant stem"), and organs with different physiological functions such as [roots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Root "Root") to absorb water and minerals, stems for support and to transport water and synthesized molecules, [leaves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leaf "Leaf") for photosynthesis, and [flowers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flower "Flower") for reproduction.[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-55)

### Photosynthesis

Plants [photosynthesize](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photosynthesis "Photosynthesis"), manufacturing food molecules ([sugars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sugar "Sugar")) using energy obtained from [light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light "Light"). Plant cells contain [chlorophylls](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chlorophyll "Chlorophyll") inside their chloroplasts, which are green pigments that are used to capture light energy. The end-to-end chemical equation for photosynthesis is:[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-56)

![Image 19: {\displaystyle {6\,\mathrm {CO} {\vphantom {A}}_{\smash[{t}]{2}}{}+{}6\,\mathrm {H} {\vphantom {A}}_{\smash[{t}]{2}}\mathrm {O} {}\mathrel {\xrightarrow {\text{light}} } {}\mathrm {C} {\vphantom {A}}_{\smash[{t}]{6}}\mathrm {H} {\vphantom {A}}_{\smash[{t}]{12}}\mathrm {O} {\vphantom {A}}_{\smash[{t}]{6}}{}+{}6\,\mathrm {O} {\vphantom {A}}_{\smash[{t}]{2}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/100302228047a00799cc68db892940dd5e3adc9e)
This causes plants to release [oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen "Oxygen") into the atmosphere. Green plants provide a substantial proportion of the world's molecular oxygen, alongside the contributions from photosynthetic algae and cyanobacteria.[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-Reinhard_Planavsky_Olson_Lyons_2016-57)[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-behrenfeld-58)[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-Tivy_2014-59)

Plants that have secondarily adopted a parasitic lifestyle may lose the genes involved in photosynthesis and the production of chlorophyll.[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-Qu-etal-2019-60)

### Growth and repair

Growth is determined by the interaction of a plant's [genome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genome "Genome") with its physical and biotic environment.[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-Baucom_Heath_Chambers_2020_pp._175%E2%80%93178-61) Factors of the physical or abiotic environment include [temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperature "Temperature"), [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water "Water"), light, [carbon dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_dioxide "Carbon dioxide"), and [nutrients](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nutrient "Nutrient") in the soil.[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-62) Biotic factors that affect plant growth include crowding, grazing, beneficial symbiotic bacteria and fungi, and attacks by insects or [plant diseases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant_pathology "Plant pathology").[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-Bareja_2022-63)

Frost and dehydration can damage or kill plants. Some plants have [antifreeze proteins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antifreeze_protein "Antifreeze protein"), [heat-shock proteins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat_shock_protein "Heat shock protein") and sugars in their cytoplasm that enable them to [tolerate these stresses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hardiness_(plants) "Hardiness (plants)").[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-Ambroise_Legay_Guerriero_Hausman_2019_pp._3%E2%80%9320-64) Plants are continuously exposed to a range of physical and biotic stresses which cause [DNA damage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNA_damage_(naturally_occurring) "DNA damage (naturally occurring)"), but they can tolerate and repair much of this damage.[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-pmid18707020-65)

### Reproduction

Plants reproduce to generate offspring, whether [sexually](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant_reproductive_morphology "Plant reproductive morphology"), involving [gametes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamete "Gamete"), or [asexually](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asexual_reproduction "Asexual reproduction"), involving ordinary growth. Many plants use both mechanisms.[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-Yang_Kim_2016-66)

#### Sexual

[![Image 20](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Alternation_of_generations_simpler.svg/500px-Alternation_of_generations_simpler.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Alternation_of_generations_simpler.svg)

[Alternation of generations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alternation_of_generations "Alternation of generations") between a [haploid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haploid "Haploid") (n) gametophyte (top) and a [diploid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diploid "Diploid") (2n) sporophyte (bottom), in all types of plant

When reproducing sexually, plants have complex lifecycles involving [alternation of generations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alternation_of_generations "Alternation of generations"). One generation, the [sporophyte](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sporophyte "Sporophyte"), which is [diploid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diploid "Diploid") (with 2 sets of [chromosomes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chromosome "Chromosome")), gives rise to the next generation, the [gametophyte](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gametophyte "Gametophyte"), which is [haploid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haploid "Haploid") (with one set of chromosomes). Some plants also reproduce asexually via [spores](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spore "Spore"). In some non-flowering plants such as mosses, the sexual gametophyte forms most of the visible plant.[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-67) In seed plants (gymnosperms and flowering plants), the sporophyte forms most of the visible plant, and the gametophyte is very small. Flowering plants reproduce sexually using flowers, which contain male and female parts: these may be within the same ([hermaphrodite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermaphrodite_flower "Hermaphrodite flower")) flower, on [different flowers on the same plant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monoecy "Monoecy"), or [on different plants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dioecy "Dioecy"). The [stamens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stamen "Stamen") create [pollen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pollen "Pollen"), which produces male gametes that enter the [ovule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ovule "Ovule") to fertilize the egg cell of the female gametophyte. Fertilization takes place within the [carpels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carpel "Carpel") or [ovaries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ovary_(botany) "Ovary (botany)"), which develop into [fruits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fruit "Fruit") that contain [seeds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seed "Seed"). Fruits may be dispersed whole, or they may split open and the [seeds dispersed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seed_dispersal "Seed dispersal") individually.[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-68)

#### Asexual

[![Image 21](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/RowOfPlants.JPG/250px-RowOfPlants.JPG)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:RowOfPlants.JPG)

_[Ficinia spiralis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ficinia\_spiralis "Ficinia spiralis")_ spreads [asexually](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asexual_reproduction "Asexual reproduction") with [runners](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stolon "Stolon") in the sand.

Plants reproduce asexually by growing any of a wide variety of structures capable of growing into new plants. At the simplest, plants such as mosses or liverworts may be broken into pieces, each of which may regrow into whole plants. The propagation of flowering plants by [cuttings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cutting_(plant) "Cutting (plant)") is a similar process. Structures such as [runners](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stolon "Stolon") enable plants to grow to cover an area, forming a [clone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cloning "Cloning"). Many plants grow food storage structures such as [tubers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tuber "Tuber") or [bulbs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bulb "Bulb") which may each develop into a new plant.[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-69)

Some non-flowering plants, such as many liverworts, mosses and some clubmosses, along with a few flowering plants, grow small clumps of cells called [gemmae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gemma_(botany) "Gemma (botany)") which can detach and grow.[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-Kato_Yasui_Ishizaki_2020_pp._459%E2%80%93465-70)[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-Moody_Diggle_Steingraeber_1999_pp._1512%E2%80%931522-71)

### Disease resistance

Plants use pattern-recognition receptors to recognize [pathogens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pathogen "Pathogen") such as bacteria that cause plant diseases. This recognition triggers a protective response. The first such plant receptors were identified in [rice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rice "Rice")[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-72) and in _[Arabidopsis thaliana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis\_thaliana "Arabidopsis thaliana")_.[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-73)

### Genomics

Plants have some of the largest genomes of all organisms.[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-74) The largest plant genome (in terms of gene number) is that of [wheat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wheat "Wheat") (_Triticum aestivum_), predicted to encode ≈94,000 genes[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-75) and thus almost 5 times as many as the [human genome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_genome "Human genome"). The first plant genome sequenced was that of _Arabidopsis thaliana_ which encodes about 25,500 genes.[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-76) In terms of sheer DNA sequence, the smallest published genome is that of the carnivorous [bladderwort](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Utricularia_gibba "Utricularia gibba") (_Utricularia gibba)_ at 82 Mb (although it still encodes 28,500 genes)[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-77) while the largest, from the [Norway spruce](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Picea_abies "Picea abies") (_Picea abies_), extends over 19.6 Gb (encoding about 28,300 genes).[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-78)

Ecology
-------

### Distribution

[![Image 22](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Vegetation.png/960px-Vegetation.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Vegetation.png)

A map of a classification of the world's vegetation into [biomes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biome "Biome"). Those named here include [tundra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tundra "Tundra"), [taiga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiga "Taiga"), [temperate broadleaf forest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperate_broadleaf_forest "Temperate broadleaf forest"), [temperate steppe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperate_steppe "Temperate steppe"), [subtropical rainforest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subtropical_rainforest "Subtropical rainforest"), [Mediterranean vegetation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mediterranean_Vegetation "Mediterranean Vegetation"), [monsoon forest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monsoon_forest "Monsoon forest"), [arid desert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Desert "Desert"), [xeric shrubland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xeric_shrubland "Xeric shrubland"), [dry steppe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dry_steppe "Dry steppe"), semiarid desert, grass [savanna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Savanna "Savanna"), tree savanna, [subtropical and tropical dry forest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tropical_and_subtropical_dry_broadleaf_forests "Tropical and subtropical dry broadleaf forests"), [tropical rainforest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tropical_rainforest "Tropical rainforest"), [alpine tundra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpine_tundra "Alpine tundra"), and [montane forests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Montane_Forest "Montane Forest"). Shown in gray is "[ice sheet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ice_sheet "Ice sheet") and polar desert" devoid of plants.

Plants are distributed almost worldwide. While they inhabit many [biomes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biome "Biome") which can be divided into a multitude of [ecoregions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecoregion "Ecoregion"),[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-Olson_2001-79) only the hardy plants of the [Antarctic flora](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antarctic_flora "Antarctic flora"), consisting of algae, mosses, liverworts, lichens, and just two flowering plants, have adapted to the prevailing conditions on that southern continent.[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-Schulze_Beck_Buchmann_Clemens_2018_pp._657%E2%80%93688-80)

Plants are often the dominant physical and structural component of the habitats where they occur. Many of the Earth's biomes are named for the type of vegetation because plants are the dominant organisms in those biomes, such as [grassland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grassland "Grassland"), [savanna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Savanna "Savanna"), and [tropical rainforest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tropical_rainforest "Tropical rainforest").[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-81)

### Primary producers

The photosynthesis conducted by land plants and algae is the ultimate source of energy and organic material in nearly all ecosystems. Photosynthesis, at first by cyanobacteria and later by photosynthetic eukaryotes, radically changed the composition of the early Earth's anoxic atmosphere, which as a result is now 21% [oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen "Oxygen"). Animals and most other organisms are [aerobic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aerobic_organism "Aerobic organism"), relying on oxygen; those that do not are confined to relatively rare [anaerobic environments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anaerobic_environment "Anaerobic environment"). Plants are the [primary producers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autotroph "Autotroph") in most terrestrial ecosystems and form the basis of the [food web](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Food_web "Food web") in those ecosystems.[[82]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-82) Plants form about 80% of the world [biomass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biomass_(ecology) "Biomass (ecology)") at about 450 gigatonnes (4.4×10 11 long tons; 5.0×10 11 short tons) of carbon.[[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-Bar-On-83)

### Ecological relationships

Numerous animals have coevolved with plants; flowering plants have evolved [pollination syndromes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pollination_syndrome "Pollination syndrome"), suites of flower traits that [favour their reproduction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pollinator-mediated_selection "Pollinator-mediated selection"). Many, including [insect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entomophily "Entomophily") and [bird partners](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ornithophily "Ornithophily"), are [pollinators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pollinator "Pollinator"), visiting flowers and accidentally transferring pollen in exchange for food in the form of pollen or [nectar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nectar "Nectar").[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-Lunau-84)

Many animals [disperse seeds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biological_dispersal "Biological dispersal") that are adapted for such dispersal. Various mechanisms of dispersal have evolved. Some fruits offer nutritious outer layers attractive to animals, while the seeds are adapted to survive the passage through the animal's gut; others have hooks that enable them to attach to a mammal's fur.[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-Schaefer_Ruxton_2011_pp._48%E2%80%9367-85)[Myrmecophytes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Myrmecophyte "Myrmecophyte") are plants that have coevolved with [ants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ant "Ant"). The plant provides a home, and sometimes food, for the ants. In exchange, the ants defend the plant from [herbivores](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herbivore "Herbivore") and sometimes competing plants. Ant wastes serve as organic [fertilizer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fertilizer "Fertilizer").[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-86)

The majority of plant species have fungi associated with their root systems in a [mutualistic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mutualism_(biology) "Mutualism (biology)")[symbiosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symbiosis "Symbiosis") known as [mycorrhiza](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mycorrhiza "Mycorrhiza"). The fungi help the plants gain water and mineral nutrients from the soil, while the plant gives the fungi carbohydrates manufactured in photosynthesis.[[87]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-87) Some plants serve as homes for [endophytic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endophyte "Endophyte") fungi that protect the plant from herbivores by producing toxins. The fungal endophyte _[Neotyphodium coenophialum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neotyphodium\_coenophialum "Neotyphodium coenophialum")_ in [tall fescue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tall_fescue "Tall fescue") grass has pest status in the American cattle industry.[[88]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-Lyons1986-88)

Many [legumes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legume "Legume") have _[Rhizobium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhizobium "Rhizobium")_ nitrogen-fixing bacteria in nodules of their roots, which fix nitrogen from the air for the plant to use; in return, the plants supply sugars to the bacteria.[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-89) Nitrogen fixed in this way can become available to other plants, and is important in agriculture; for example, farmers may grow a [crop rotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crop_rotation "Crop rotation") of a legume such as beans, followed by a cereal such as wheat, to provide [cash crops](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cash_crop "Cash crop") with a reduced input of [nitrogen fertilizer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen_fertilizer "Nitrogen fertilizer").[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-90)

Some 1% of [plants are parasitic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parasitic_plant "Parasitic plant"). They range from the semi-parasitic [mistletoe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mistletoe "Mistletoe") that merely takes some nutrients from its host, but still has photosynthetic leaves, to the fully-parasitic [broomrape](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orobanche "Orobanche") and [toothwort](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lathraea "Lathraea") that acquire all their nutrients through connections to the roots of other plants, and so have no chlorophyll. Full parasites can be extremely harmful to their plant hosts.[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-Kokla_Melnyk_2010-91)

Plants that grow on other plants, usually trees, without parasitizing them, are called [epiphytes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epiphyte "Epiphyte"). These may support diverse arboreal ecosystems. Some may indirectly harm their host plant, such as by intercepting light. [Hemiepiphytes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hemiepiphyte "Hemiepiphyte") like the [strangler fig](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strangler_fig "Strangler fig") begin as epiphytes, but eventually set their own roots and overpower and kill their host. Many [orchids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orchid "Orchid"), [bromeliads](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bromeliad "Bromeliad"), ferns, and mosses grow as epiphytes.[[92]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-Zotz_2016-92) Among the epiphytes, the bromeliads accumulate water in their leaf axils; these [water-filled cavities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phytotelma "Phytotelma") can support complex aquatic food webs.[[93]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-93)

Some 630 species of plants are [carnivorous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carnivorous_plant "Carnivorous plant"), such as the [Venus flytrap](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Venus_flytrap "Venus flytrap") (_Dionaea muscipula_) and [sundew](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sundew "Sundew") (_Drosera_ species). They trap small animals and digest them to obtain mineral nutrients, especially [nitrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen "Nitrogen") and [phosphorus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphorus "Phosphorus").[[94]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-Ellison_Adamec_2018-94)

### Competition

Competition for shared resources reduces a plant's growth.[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-Ecology-2012-95)[[96]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-Pocheville-2015-96) Shared resources include sunlight, water and nutrients. Light is a critical resource because it is necessary for photosynthesis.[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-Ecology-2012-95) Plants use their leaves to shade other plants from sunlight and grow quickly to maximize their own expose.[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-Ecology-2012-95) Water too is essential for photosynthesis; roots compete to maximize water uptake from soil.[[97]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-Casper-1997-97) Some plants have deep roots that are able to locate water stored deep underground, and others have shallower roots that are capable of extending longer distances to collect recent rainwater.[[97]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-Casper-1997-97) Minerals are important for plant growth and development.[[98]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-98) Common nutrients competed for amongst plants include nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium.[[99]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-Oborny-2000-99)

Importance to humans
--------------------

### Food

[![Image 28](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Harvest_Time_-_geograph.org.uk_-_747095.jpg/250px-Harvest_Time_-_geograph.org.uk_-_747095.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Harvest_Time_-_geograph.org.uk_-_747095.jpg)

Harvesting [oats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oat "Oat") with a [combine harvester](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Combine_harvester "Combine harvester")

Human cultivation of plants is the core of [agriculture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agriculture "Agriculture"), which in turn has [played a key role in the history of world civilizations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_agriculture "History of agriculture").[[100]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-100) Humans depend on flowering plants for [food](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Food "Food"), either directly or as feed in [animal husbandry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Animal_husbandry "Animal husbandry"). More broadly, agriculture includes [agronomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agronomy "Agronomy") for arable crops, [horticulture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horticulture "Horticulture") for vegetables and fruit, and [forestry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forestry "Forestry"), including both flowering plants and conifers, for timber.[[101]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-101)[[102]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-NatGeogAgriculture-102) About 7,000 species of plant have been used for food, though most of today's food is derived from only 30 species. The major [staples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Staple_crop "Staple crop") include [cereals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cereal "Cereal") such as rice and wheat, starchy roots and tubers such as [cassava](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cassava "Cassava") and [potato](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potato "Potato"), and legumes such as [peas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peas "Peas") and [beans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bean "Bean"). [Vegetable oils](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vegetable_oil "Vegetable oil") such as [olive oil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Olive_oil "Olive oil") and [palm oil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palm_oil "Palm oil") provide [lipids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lipids "Lipids"), while fruit and [vegetables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vegetable "Vegetable") contribute [vitamins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vitamin "Vitamin") and minerals to the diet.[[103]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-103)[Coffee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coffee "Coffee"), [tea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tea "Tea"), and [chocolate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chocolate "Chocolate") are major crops whose [caffeine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caffeine "Caffeine")-containing products serve as mild stimulants.[[104]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-Hopper_2015-104) The study of plant uses by people is called economic botany or [ethnobotany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethnobotany "Ethnobotany").[[105]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-105)

### Medicines

[![Image 29](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Folio_Materia_Medica_Dioscurides_Met_13.152.6_%28cropped%29.jpg/250px-Folio_Materia_Medica_Dioscurides_Met_13.152.6_%28cropped%29.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Folio_Materia_Medica_Dioscurides_Met_13.152.6_(cropped).jpg)

A medieval physician preparing an extract from a [medicinal plant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medicinal_plants "Medicinal plants"), from an Arabic [Dioscorides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_materia_medica "De materia medica"), 1224

[Medicinal plants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medicinal_plants "Medicinal plants") are a primary source of [organic compounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_compound "Organic compound"), both for their medicinal and physiological effects, and for the industrial [synthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_synthesis "Organic synthesis") of a vast array of organic chemicals.[[106]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-106) Many hundreds of medicines, as well as [narcotics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Narcotic "Narcotic"), are derived from plants, both traditional medicines used in [herbalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herbalism "Herbalism")[[107]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-tapsell-107)[[108]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-lai-108) and chemical substances purified from plants or first identified in them, sometimes by ethnobotanical search, and then synthesised for use in modern medicine. Modern medicines derived from plants include [aspirin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aspirin "Aspirin"), [taxol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taxol "Taxol"), [morphine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morphine "Morphine"), [quinine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quinine "Quinine"), [reserpine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reserpine "Reserpine"), [colchicine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colchicine "Colchicine"), [digitalis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digitalis "Digitalis") and [vincristine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vincristine "Vincristine"). [Plants used in herbalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_plants_used_in_herbalism "List of plants used in herbalism") include [ginkgo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ginkgo_biloba "Ginkgo biloba"), [echinacea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Echinacea "Echinacea"), [feverfew](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feverfew "Feverfew"), and [Saint John's wort](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saint_John%27s_wort "Saint John's wort"). The [pharmacopoeia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pharmacopoeia "Pharmacopoeia") of [Dioscorides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dioscorides "Dioscorides"), _[De materia medica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De\_materia\_medica "De materia medica")_, describing some 600 medicinal plants, was written between 50 and 70 CE and remained in use in Europe and the Middle East until around 1600 CE; it was the precursor of all modern pharmacopoeias.[[109]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-NIH-109)[[110]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-110)[[111]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-111)

### Nonfood products

[![Image 30](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Timber_DonnellyMills2005_SeanMcClean.jpg/250px-Timber_DonnellyMills2005_SeanMcClean.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Timber_DonnellyMills2005_SeanMcClean.jpg)

[Timber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timber "Timber") in storage for later processing at a [sawmill](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sawmill "Sawmill")

Plants grown as industrial crops are the source of a wide range of products used in manufacturing.[[112]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-112) Nonfood products include [essential oils](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Essential_oil "Essential oil"), [natural dyes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_dye "Natural dye"), pigments, [waxes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wax "Wax"), [resins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resin "Resin"), [tannins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tannin "Tannin"), alkaloids, [amber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amber "Amber") and [cork](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cork_material "Cork material"). Products derived from plants include soaps, shampoos, perfumes, cosmetics, paint, varnish, turpentine, rubber, [latex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latex "Latex"), lubricants, linoleum, plastics, inks, and [gums](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gum_(botany) "Gum (botany)"). Renewable fuels from plants include [firewood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Firewood "Firewood"), [peat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peat "Peat") and other [biofuels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biofuel "Biofuel").[[113]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-113)[[114]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-CruzDierig2014-114) The [fossil fuels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fossil_fuel "Fossil fuel")[coal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coal "Coal"), [petroleum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petroleum "Petroleum") and [natural gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_gas "Natural gas") are derived from the remains of aquatic organisms including [phytoplankton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phytoplankton "Phytoplankton") in [geological time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geological_time "Geological time").[[115]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-115) Many of the coal fields date to the [Carboniferous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carboniferous "Carboniferous") period of [Earth's history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_Earth "History of Earth"). Terrestrial plants also form [type III kerogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kerogen#Type_III:_humic "Kerogen"), a source of natural gas.[[116]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-116)[[117]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-117)

Structural resources and fibres from plants are used to construct dwellings and to manufacture clothing. [Wood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wood "Wood") is used for buildings, boats, and furniture, and for smaller items such as [musical instruments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Musical_instrument "Musical instrument") and sports equipment. Wood is [pulped](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulp_(paper) "Pulp (paper)") to make [paper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paper "Paper") and [cardboard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardboard "Cardboard").[[118]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-118) Cloth is often made from [cotton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cotton "Cotton"), [flax](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flax "Flax"), [ramie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ramie "Ramie") or synthetic fibres such as [rayon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rayon "Rayon"), derived from plant cellulose. [Thread](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thread_(yarn) "Thread (yarn)") used to sew cloth likewise comes in large part from cotton.[[119]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-119)

### Ornamental plants

[![Image 31](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Rose_espalier_Niedernhall.JPG/250px-Rose_espalier_Niedernhall.JPG)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Rose_espalier_Niedernhall.JPG)

A rose [espalier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Espalier "Espalier") at Niedernhall in Germany

Thousands of plant species are cultivated for their beauty and to provide shade, modify temperatures, reduce wind, abate noise, provide privacy, and reduce soil erosion. Plants are the basis of a multibillion-dollar per year tourism industry, which includes travel to [historic gardens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Garden_tourism "Garden tourism"), [national parks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_park "National park"), [rainforests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rainforest "Rainforest"), [forests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forest "Forest") with colourful autumn leaves, and festivals such as [Japan's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hanami "Hanami")[[120]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-120) and [America's cherry blossom festivals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Cherry_Blossom_Festival "National Cherry Blossom Festival").[[121]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-NCBF-121)

Plants may be grown indoors as [houseplants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Houseplant "Houseplant"), or in specialized buildings such as [greenhouses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenhouse "Greenhouse"). Plants such as Venus flytrap, [sensitive plant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sensitive_plant "Sensitive plant") and [resurrection plant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resurrection_plant "Resurrection plant") are sold as novelties. Art forms specializing in the arrangement of cut or living plant include [bonsai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bonsai "Bonsai"), [ikebana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ikebana "Ikebana"), and the arrangement of cut or dried flowers. [Ornamental plants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ornamental_plant "Ornamental plant") have sometimes changed the course of history, as in [tulipomania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tulip_mania "Tulip mania").[[122]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-Lambert-122)

### In science

[![Image 32](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Barbara_McClintock_%281902-1992%29_shown_in_her_laboratory_in_1947.jpg/250px-Barbara_McClintock_%281902-1992%29_shown_in_her_laboratory_in_1947.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Barbara_McClintock_(1902-1992)_shown_in_her_laboratory_in_1947.jpg)

[Barbara McClintock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barbara_McClintock "Barbara McClintock") used [maize](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maize "Maize") to study inheritance of traits.

The [traditional study of plants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_botany "History of botany") is the science of [botany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Botany "Botany").[[123]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-123) Basic biological research has often used plants as its [model organisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Model_organism "Model organism"). In [genetics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genetics "Genetics"), the breeding of pea plants allowed [Gregor Mendel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gregor_Mendel "Gregor Mendel") to derive the [basic laws governing inheritance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mendelian_inheritance "Mendelian inheritance"),[[124]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-124) and examination of chromosomes in maize allowed [Barbara McClintock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barbara_McClintock "Barbara McClintock") to demonstrate their connection to inherited traits.[[125]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-125) The plant _Arabidopsis thaliana_ is used in laboratories as a model organism to understand how [genes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gene "Gene") control the growth and development of plant structures.[[126]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-126)[Tree rings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tree_ring "Tree ring") provide a method of dating in [archeology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archaeology "Archaeology"), and a record of [past climates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paleoclimatology "Paleoclimatology").[[127]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-127) The study of plant fossils, or [Paleobotany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paleobotany "Paleobotany"), provides information about the evolutions of plants, [paleogeographical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palaeogeography "Palaeogeography") reconstructions, and past climate change. Plant fossils can also help determine the age of rocks.[[128]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-128)

### In mythology, religion, and culture

Plants including [trees appear in mythology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trees_in_mythology "Trees in mythology"), religion, and [literature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_fictional_plants "List of fictional plants").[[129]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-129)[[130]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-130)[[131]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-131) In multiple [Indo-European](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indo-European_religion "Indo-European religion"), Siberian, and [Native American religions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Native_American_religion "Native American religion"), the [world tree](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_tree "World tree") motif is depicted as a colossal tree growing on the earth, supporting the heavens, and with its roots reaching into the [underworld](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Underworld "Underworld"). It may also appear as a cosmic tree or an eagle and serpent tree.[[132]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-Annus2009-132)[[133]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-133) Forms of the world tree include the archetypal [tree of life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tree_of_life "Tree of life"), which is in turn connected to the Eurasian concept of the [sacred tree](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sacred_tree "Sacred tree").[[134]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-134) Another widespread ancient motif, found for example in Iran, has a tree of life flanked by a pair of [confronted animals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confronted_animals "Confronted animals").[[135]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-135)

Flowers are often used as memorials, gifts and to mark special occasions such as births, deaths, weddings and holidays. Flower arrangements may be used to send [hidden messages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Language_of_flowers "Language of flowers").[[136]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-136) Plants and especially flowers form the subjects of many paintings.[[137]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-137)[[138]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-138)

### Negative effects

[![Image 33](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Texas_invasive_Musk_Thistle_1.jpg/250px-Texas_invasive_Musk_Thistle_1.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Texas_invasive_Musk_Thistle_1.jpg)

The [musk thistle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carduus_nutans "Carduus nutans") is an [invasive species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invasive_species "Invasive species") in [Texas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Texas "Texas").

[Weeds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weed "Weed") are commercially or aesthetically undesirable plants growing in managed environments such as in agriculture and gardens.[[139]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-139) People have spread many plants beyond their native ranges; some of these plants have become [invasive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invasive_species "Invasive species"), damaging existing ecosystems by displacing native species, and sometimes becoming serious weeds of cultivation.[[140]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-140)

Some plants that produce [windblown pollen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anemophily "Anemophily"), including grasses, invoke [allergic reactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allergy "Allergy") in people who suffer from [hay fever](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hay_fever "Hay fever").[[141]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-NIH2015Cause-141) Many plants [produce toxins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_poisonous_plants "List of poisonous plants") to [protect themselves from herbivores](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant_defense_against_herbivory "Plant defense against herbivory"). Major classes of plant toxins include [alkaloids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkaloid "Alkaloid"), [terpenoids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terpenoid "Terpenoid"), and [phenolics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phenols "Phenols").[[142]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-142) These can be harmful to humans and livestock by ingestion[[143]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-143)[[144]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-144) or, as with [poison ivy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poison_ivy "Poison ivy"), by contact.[[145]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-145) Some plants have negative effects on other plants, preventing seedling growth or the growth of nearby plants by releasing [allopathic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allelopathy "Allelopathy") chemicals.[[146]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant#cite_note-146)
