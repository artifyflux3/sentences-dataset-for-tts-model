From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Philosophical belief that principles depend on societal agreements, not external reality

**Conventionalism** is the [philosophical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophy "Philosophy") attitude that fundamental principles of a certain kind are grounded on (explicit or implicit) agreements in society, rather than on external reality. [Unspoken rules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unspoken_rule "Unspoken rule") play a key role in the philosophy's structure. Although this attitude is commonly held with respect to the rules of [grammar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grammar "Grammar"), its application to the propositions of [ethics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethics "Ethics"), [law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law "Law"), [science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Science "Science"), [biology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology "Biology"), [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), and [logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logic "Logic") is more controversial.

Linguistics
-----------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Conventionalism&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Linguistics")]

See also: [Cratylism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cratylism "Cratylism") and [True name](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/True_name "True name")

The debate on linguistic conventionalism goes back to [Plato](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plato "Plato")'s _[Cratylus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cratylus\_(dialogue) "Cratylus (dialogue)")_ and the philosophy of [Kumārila Bhaṭṭa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kum%C4%81rila_Bha%E1%B9%AD%E1%B9%ADa "Kumārila Bhaṭṭa").[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] It has been the standard position of modern [linguistics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linguistics "Linguistics") since [Ferdinand de Saussure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ferdinand_de_Saussure "Ferdinand de Saussure")'s _[l'arbitraire du signe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L%27arbitraire\_du\_signe "L'arbitraire du signe")_, but there have always been dissenting positions of [phonosemantics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phonosemantics "Phonosemantics"), recently defended by [Margaret Magnus](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Margaret_Magnus&action=edit&redlink=1 "Margaret Magnus (page does not exist)") and [Vilayanur S. Ramachandran](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vilayanur_S._Ramachandran "Vilayanur S. Ramachandran").[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

Philosophy of mathematics
-------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Conventionalism&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Philosophy of mathematics")]

See also: [Preintuitionism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Preintuitionism "Preintuitionism")

The French [mathematician](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematician "Mathematician")[Henri Poincaré](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Poincar%C3%A9 "Henri Poincaré") was among the first to articulate a conventionalist view. Poincaré's use of [non-Euclidean geometries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-Euclidean_geometries "Non-Euclidean geometries") in his work on [differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_equation "Differential equation") convinced him that [Euclidean geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_geometry "Euclidean geometry") should not be regarded as an _[a priori](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A\_priori\_and\_a\_posteriori "A priori and a posteriori")_ truth. He held that [axioms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axioms "Axioms") in geometry should be chosen for the results they produce, not for their apparent coherence with – possibly flawed – human intuitions about the physical world.

Epistemology
------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Conventionalism&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Epistemology")]

Conventionalism was adopted by [logical positivists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logical_positivism "Logical positivism"), chiefly [A. J. Ayer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alfred_Jules_Ayer "Alfred Jules Ayer") and [Carl Hempel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Hempel "Carl Hempel"), and extended to both mathematics and logic. To deny [rationalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rationalism "Rationalism"), Ayer sees two options for [empiricism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empiricism "Empiricism") regarding the necessity of the truth of formal logic (and mathematics): 1) deny that they actually are necessary, and then account for why they only appear so, or 2) claim that the truths of logic and mathematics lack factual content – they are not "truths about the world" – and then explain how they are nevertheless true and informative.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conventionalism#cite_note-1)[John Stuart Mill](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Stuart_Mill "John Stuart Mill") adopted the former, which Ayer criticized, opting himself for the latter. Ayer's argument relies primarily on the [analytic/synthetic distinction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytic-synthetic_distinction "Analytic-synthetic distinction").

The French [philosopher](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosopher "Philosopher")[Pierre Duhem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre_Duhem "Pierre Duhem") espoused a broader conventionalist view encompassing all of science.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conventionalism#cite_note-2) Duhem was skeptical that human perceptions are sufficient to understand the "true," metaphysical nature of reality and argued that scientific laws should be valued mainly for their predictive power and correspondence with observations.

[Karl Popper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karl_Popper "Karl Popper") broadened the meaning of conventionalism still more. In _[The Logic of Scientific Discovery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The\_Logic\_of\_Scientific\_Discovery "The Logic of Scientific Discovery")_, he defined a "conventionalist stratagem" as any technique that is used by a theorist to evade the consequences of a falsifying observation or experiment. Popper identified four such stratagems:

*   introducing an ad hoc hypothesis that makes the refuting evidence seem irrelevant;
*   modifying the [ostensive definitions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ostensive_definition "Ostensive definition") so as to alter the content of a theory;
*   doubting the reliability of the experimenter; declaring that the observations that threaten the tested theory are irrelevant;
*   casting doubt on the acumen of the theorist when he does not produce ideas that can save the theory.

Popper argued that it was crucial to avoid conventionalist stratagems if [falsifiability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Falsifiability "Falsifiability") of a theory was to be preserved. It has been argued that the [standard model of cosmology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_model_of_cosmology "Standard model of cosmology") is built upon a set of conventionalist stratagems.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conventionalism#cite_note-3)

In the 1930s, a Polish philosopher [Kazimierz Ajdukiewicz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kazimierz_Ajdukiewicz "Kazimierz Ajdukiewicz") proposed a view that he called radical conventionalism – as opposed to moderate conventionalism developed by [Henri Poincaré](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Poincar%C3%A9 "Henri Poincaré") and [Pierre Duhem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre_Duhem "Pierre Duhem"). Radical conventionalism was originally outlined in _The World-Picture and the Conceptual Apparatus_, an article published in “Erkenntnis” in 1934. The theory can be characterized by the following theses: (1) there are languages or – as Ajdukiewicz used to say – conceptual apparatuses (schemes) which are not intertranslatable, (2) any knowledge must be articulate in one of those languages, (3) the choice of a language is arbitrary, and it is possible to change from one language to another.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conventionalism#cite_note-4) Therefore, there is a conventional or decisional element in all knowledge (including perceptual). In his later writings – under the influence of [Alfred Tarski](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alfred_Tarski "Alfred Tarski") – Ajdukiewicz rejected radical conventionalism in favour of a semantic epistemology.

Legal philosophy
----------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Conventionalism&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Legal philosophy")]

Conventionalism, as applied to [legal philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legal_philosophy "Legal philosophy") is one of the three rival conceptions of law constructed by American legal philosopher [Ronald Dworkin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ronald_Dworkin "Ronald Dworkin") in his work _[Law's Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law%27s\_Empire "Law's Empire")_. The other two conceptions of law are [legal pragmatism](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Legal_pragmatism&action=edit&redlink=1 "Legal pragmatism (page does not exist)") and [law as integrity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law_as_integrity "Law as integrity").

According to conventionalism as defined by Dworkin, a community's legal institutions should contain clear social [conventions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convention_(norm) "Convention (norm)") relied upon which rules are promulgated. Such rules will serve as the sole source of information for all the community members because they demarcate clearly all the circumstances in which [state coercion](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=State_coercion&action=edit&redlink=1 "State coercion (page does not exist)") will and will not be exercised.

Dworkin nonetheless has argued that this justification fails to fit with facts as there are many occasions wherein clear applicable legal rules are absent. It follows that, as he maintained, conventionalism can provide no valid ground for [state coercion](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=State_coercion&action=edit&redlink=1 "State coercion (page does not exist)"). Dworkin himself favored [law as integrity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law_as_integrity "Law as integrity") as the best justification of state coercion.

One famous criticism of Dworkin's idea comes from [Stanley Fish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stanley_Fish "Stanley Fish") who argues that Dworkin, like the [Critical Legal Studies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Critical_Legal_Studies "Critical Legal Studies") movement, [Marxists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marxists "Marxists") and adherents of [feminist jurisprudence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feminist_jurisprudence "Feminist jurisprudence"), was guilty of a false 'Theory Hope'. Fish claims that such mistake stems from their mistaken belief that there exists a general or higher 'theory' that explains or constrains all fields of activity like state coercion.

Another criticism is based on Dworkin's assertion that positivists' claims amount to conventionalism. [H. L. A. Hart](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/H._L._A._Hart "H. L. A. Hart"), as a soft positivist, denies such claim as he had pointed out that citizens cannot always discover the law as plain matter of fact. It is however unclear as to whether [Joseph Raz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Raz "Joseph Raz"), an avowed hard positivist, can be classified as conventionalist as Raz has claimed that law is composed "exclusively" of social facts, which could be complex, and thus difficult to be discovered.

In particular, Dworkin has characterized law as having the main function of restraining state's coercion.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_][Nigel Simmonds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nigel_Simmonds "Nigel Simmonds") has rejected Dworkin's disapproval of conventionalism, claiming that his characterization of law is too narrow.
