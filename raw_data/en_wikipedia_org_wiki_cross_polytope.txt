In [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry"), a **cross-polytope**,[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross-polytope#cite_note-FOOTNOTECoxeter1973121%E2%80%93122%C2%A77.21._illustration_Fig_7-2%3Csmall%3EB%3C/small%3E-1)**hyperoctahedron**, **orthoplex**,[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross-polytope#cite_note-2)**staurotope**,[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross-polytope#cite_note-3) or **cocube** is a [regular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polytope "Regular polytope"), [convex polytope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_polytope "Convex polytope") that exists in _n_-[dimensional Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimensions "Dimensions"). A 2-dimensional cross-polytope is a square, a 3-dimensional cross-polytope is a regular [octahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octahedron "Octahedron"), and a 4-dimensional cross-polytope is a [16-cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/16-cell "16-cell"). Its facets are [simplexes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simplex "Simplex") of the previous dimension, while the cross-polytope's [vertex figure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_figure "Vertex figure") is another cross-polytope from the previous dimension.

The vertices of a cross-polytope can be chosen as the unit vectors pointing along each co-ordinate axis – i.e. all the permutations of (±1, 0, 0, ..., 0). The cross-polytope is the [convex hull](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_hull "Convex hull") of its vertices. The _n_-dimensional cross-polytope can also be defined as the closed [unit ball](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_ball "Unit ball") (or, according to some authors, its boundary) in the [ℓ 1-norm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L1-norm "L1-norm") on **R**_n_, those points _x_ = (_x_ 1, _x_ 2..., _x_ _n_) satisfying

![Image 5: {\displaystyle |x_{1}|+|x_{2}|+\cdots +|x_{n}|\leq 1.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a711559c92d08749c68f22618f0b252f80750e5a)
An _n_-orthoplex can be constructed as a [bipyramid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bipyramid#Other_dimensions "Bipyramid") with an (_n_−1)-orthoplex base.

The cross-polytope is the [dual polytope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_polytope "Dual polytope") of the [hypercube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypercube "Hypercube"). The [vertex-edge graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_of_a_polytope "Graph of a polytope") of an _n_-dimensional cross-polytope is the [Turán graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tur%C3%A1n_graph "Turán graph")_T_(2 _n_, _n_) (also known as a _cocktail party graph_[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross-polytope#cite_note-4)).

Low-dimensional examples
------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cross-polytope&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Low-dimensional examples")]

In 1 dimension the cross-polytope is a [line segment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_segment "Line segment"), which can be chosen as the [interval](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_interval "Real interval") [−1, +1].

In 2 dimensions the cross-polytope is a [square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_(geometry) "Square (geometry)"). If the vertices are chosen as {(±1, 0), (0, ±1)}, the square's sides are at right angles to the axes; in this orientation a square is often called a _diamond_.

In 3 dimensions the cross-polytope is a [regular octahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_octahedron "Regular octahedron")—one of the five convex regular [polyhedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyhedron "Polyhedron") known as the [Platonic solids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platonic_solid "Platonic solid").

The 4-dimensional cross-polytope also goes by the name **hexadecachoron** or **[16-cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/16-cell "16-cell")**. It is one of the six [convex regular 4-polytopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_regular_4-polytope "Convex regular 4-polytope"). These [4-polytopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/4-polytope "4-polytope") were first described by the Swiss mathematician [Ludwig Schläfli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ludwig_Schl%C3%A4fli "Ludwig Schläfli") in the mid-19th century. The vertices of the 4-dimensional hypercube, or [tesseract](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tesseract "Tesseract"), can be divided into two sets of eight, the convex hull of each set forming a cross-polytope. Moreover, the polytope known as the [24-cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/24-cell "24-cell") can be constructed by symmetrically arranging three cross-polytopes.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross-polytope#cite_note-5)

The cross-polytope family is one of three [regular polytope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polytope "Regular polytope") families, labeled by [Coxeter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coxeter "Coxeter") as _β n_, the other two being the [hypercube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypercube "Hypercube") family, labeled as _γ n_, and the [simplex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simplex "Simplex") family, labeled as _α n_. A fourth family, the [infinite tessellations of hypercubes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypercubic_honeycomb "Hypercubic honeycomb"), he labeled as _δ n_.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross-polytope#cite_note-FOOTNOTECoxeter1973120%E2%80%93124%C2%A77.2-6)

The _n_-dimensional cross-polytope has 2 _n_ vertices, and 2 _n_ facets ((_n_−1)-dimensional components) all of which are (_n_−1)-[simplices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simplex "Simplex"). The [vertex figures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_figure "Vertex figure") are all (_n_−1)-cross-polytopes. The [Schläfli symbol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schl%C3%A4fli_symbol "Schläfli symbol") of the cross-polytope is {3,3,...,3,4}.

The [dihedral angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihedral_angle#Geometry "Dihedral angle") of the _n_-dimensional cross-polytope is ![Image 6: {\displaystyle \delta _{n}=\arccos \left({\frac {2-n}{n}}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/933a2ac0b37f9f9f97a39ea29aff11c251ef5927). This gives: δ 2 = arccos(0/2) = 90°, δ 3 = arccos(−1/3) = 109.47°, δ 4 = arccos(−2/4) = 120°, δ 5 = arccos(−3/5) = 126.87°, ... δ∞ = arccos(−1) = 180°.

The hypervolume of the _n_-dimensional cross-polytope is

![Image 7: {\displaystyle {\frac {2^{n}}{n!}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/46461c896c17260a514ead32605a9d109129dd27)
For each pair of non-opposite vertices, there is an edge joining them. More generally, each set of _k_+1 orthogonal vertices corresponds to a distinct _k_-dimensional component which contains them. The number of _k_-dimensional components (vertices, edges, faces, ..., facets) in an _n_-dimensional cross-polytope is thus given by (see [binomial coefficient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binomial_coefficient "Binomial coefficient")):

![Image 8: {\displaystyle 2^{k+1}{n \choose {k+1}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f7c32d2c92a1e4f90614d8a29d7dd066cccdede2)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross-polytope#cite_note-FOOTNOTECoxeter1973121%C2%A77.2.2.-7)
The extended [f-vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/F-vector "F-vector") for an _n_-orthoplex can be computed by (**1**,2)_n_, like the coefficients of [polynomial products](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial#Multiplication "Polynomial"). For example a 16-cell is (**1**,2)4 = (**1**,4,4)2 = (**1**,8,24,32,16).

There are many possible [orthographic projections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthographic_projection "Orthographic projection") that can show the cross-polytopes as 2-dimensional graphs. [Petrie polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petrie_polygon "Petrie polygon") projections map the points into a regular 2 _n_-gon or lower order regular polygons. A second projection takes the 2(_n_−1)-gon petrie polygon of the lower dimension, seen as a [bipyramid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bipyramid "Bipyramid"), projected down the axis, with 2 vertices mapped into the center.

The vertices of an axis-aligned cross polytope are all at equal distance from each other in the [Manhattan distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taxicab_geometry "Taxicab geometry") ([L 1 norm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lp_space "Lp space")). [Kusner's conjecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kusner%27s_conjecture "Kusner's conjecture") states that this set of 2 _d_ points is the largest possible [equidistant set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equidistant_set "Equidistant set") for this distance.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross-polytope#cite_note-8)

Generalized orthoplex
---------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cross-polytope&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Generalized orthoplex")]

Regular [complex polytopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_polytope#Regular_complex_polytopes "Complex polytope") can be defined in [complex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number")[Hilbert space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space "Hilbert space") called _generalized orthoplexes_ (or cross polytopes), β _p_

_n_ = 2{3}2{3}...2{4}_p_, or ![Image 143](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/bd/CDel_node_1.png)![Image 144](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c3/CDel_3.png)![Image 145](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/5e/CDel_node.png)![Image 146](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c3/CDel_3.png)..![Image 147](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c3/CDel_3.png)![Image 148](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/5e/CDel_node.png)![Image 149](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/8c/CDel_4.png)![Image 150](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b4/CDel_pnode.png). Real solutions exist with _p_ = 2, i.e. β 2

_n_ = β _n_ = 2{3}2{3}...2{4}2 = {3,3,..,4}. For _p_> 2, they exist in ![Image 151: {\displaystyle \mathbb {\mathbb {C} } ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8570f53647f626a6ce111fab2520925c9929ccf5). A _p_-generalized _n_-orthoplex has _pn_ vertices. _Generalized orthoplexes_ have regular [simplexes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simplex "Simplex") (real) as [facets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Facet_(geometry) "Facet (geometry)").[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross-polytope#cite_note-9) Generalized orthoplexes make [complete multipartite graphs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complete_multipartite_graph "Complete multipartite graph"), β _p_

2 make K _p_,_p_ for [complete bipartite graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complete_bipartite_graph "Complete bipartite graph"), β _p_

3 make K _p_,_p_,_p_ for complete tripartite graphs. β _p_

_n_ creates K _p_ _n_. An [orthogonal projection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_projection "Orthogonal projection") can be defined that maps all the vertices equally-spaced on a circle, with all pairs of vertices connected, except multiples of _n_. The [regular polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polygon "Regular polygon") perimeter in these orthogonal projections is called a [petrie polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petrie_polygon "Petrie polygon").

Cross-polytopes can be combined with their dual cubes to form compound polytopes:

*   In two dimensions, we obtain the [octagrammic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octagram "Octagram") star figure {⁠8/2⁠},
*   In three dimensions we obtain the [compound of cube and octahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compound_of_cube_and_octahedron "Compound of cube and octahedron"),
*   In four dimensions we obtain the compound of tesseract and 16-cell.
