The **observable universe** is a [spherical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ball_(mathematics) "Ball (mathematics)") region of the [universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe "Universe") consisting of all [matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matter "Matter") that can be [observed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observation "Observation") from [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth "Earth"); the [electromagnetic radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation "Electromagnetic radiation") from these [objects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_object "Astronomical object") has had time to reach the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System") and Earth since the beginning of the [cosmological expansion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_expansion_of_space "Metric expansion of space"). Assuming the universe is [isotropic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotropy "Isotropy"), the distance to the edge of the observable universe is [the same](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equidistant "Equidistant") in every direction. That is, the observable universe is a [spherical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere "Sphere") region centered on the observer. Every location in the universe has its own observable universe, which may or may not overlap with the one centered on Earth.

The word _observable_ in this sense does not refer to the capability of modern technology to detect [light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light "Light") or other information from an object, or whether there is anything to be detected. It refers to the physical limit created by the [speed of light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light "Speed of light") itself. No signal can travel faster than light, hence there is a maximum distance, called the [particle horizon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_horizon "Particle horizon"), beyond which nothing can be detected, as the signals could not have reached the observer yet.

According to calculations, the current [comoving distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comoving_distance "Comoving distance") to particles from which the [cosmic microwave background radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation "Cosmic microwave background radiation") (CMBR) was emitted, which represents the radius of the visible universe, is about 14.0 billion [parsecs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parsec "Parsec") (about 45.7 billion light-years). The comoving distance to the edge of the observable universe is about 14.3 billion parsecs (about 46.6 billion light-years),[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-mapofuniverse-7) about 2% larger. The [radius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radius "Radius") of the observable universe is therefore estimated to be about 46.5 billion light-years.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-8)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-ly93-9) Using the [critical density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedmann_equations "Friedmann equations") and the diameter of the observable universe, the total mass of ordinary matter in the universe can be calculated to be about 1.5×10 53 kg.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-10) In November 2018, astronomers reported that [extragalactic background light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extragalactic_background_light "Extragalactic background light") (EBL) amounted to 4×10 84 photons.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-NYT-20181203-11)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-SCI-20181130-12)

As the universe's expansion is accelerating, all currently observable objects, outside the local [supercluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supercluster "Supercluster"), will eventually appear to freeze in time, while emitting progressively redder and fainter light. For instance, objects with the current [redshift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift "Redshift")_[z](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift#Measurement,\_characterization,\_and\_interpretation "Redshift")_ from 5 to 10 will only be observable up to an age of 4–6 billion years. In addition, light emitted by objects currently situated beyond a certain comoving distance (currently about 19 gigaparsecs (62 Gly)) will never reach Earth.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-Loeb2002-13)

Observable Universe as a function of time and distance, in context of the [expanding Universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expanding_Universe "Expanding Universe")

The universe's size is unknown, and it may be infinite in extent.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-14) Some parts of the universe are too far away for the light emitted since the [Big Bang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang "Big Bang") to have had enough time to reach Earth or space-based instruments, and therefore lie outside the observable universe. In the future, light from distant galaxies will have had more time to travel, so one might expect that additional regions will become observable. Regions distant from observers (such as us) are expanding away faster than the speed of light, at rates estimated by [Hubble's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble%27s_law "Hubble's law").[[note 1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-15) The [expansion rate appears to be accelerating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe "Accelerating expansion of the universe"), which [dark energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_energy "Dark energy") was proposed to explain.

Assuming dark energy remains constant (an unchanging [cosmological constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmological_constant "Cosmological constant")) so that the expansion rate of the universe continues to accelerate, there is a "future visibility limit" beyond which objects will never enter the observable universe at any time in the future because light emitted by objects outside that limit could never reach the Earth. Note that, because the [Hubble parameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble%27s_law#Interpretation "Hubble's law") is decreasing with time, there can be cases where a galaxy that is receding from Earth only slightly faster than light emits a signal that eventually reaches Earth.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-ly93-9)[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-16) This future visibility limit is calculated at a [comoving distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comoving_distance "Comoving distance") of 19 billion parsecs (62 billion light-years), assuming the universe will keep expanding forever, which implies the number of galaxies that can ever be theoretically observed in the infinite future is only larger than the number currently observable by a factor of 2.36 (ignoring redshift effects).[[note 2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-mapofuniverse2-17)

In principle, more galaxies will become observable in the future; in practice, an increasing number of galaxies will become extremely [redshifted](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift "Redshift") due to ongoing expansion, so much so that they will seem to disappear from view and become invisible.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-18)[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-19)[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-20) A galaxy at a given comoving distance is defined to lie within the "observable universe" if we can receive signals emitted by the galaxy at any age in its history, say, a signal sent from the galaxy only 500 million years after the Big Bang. Because of the universe's expansion, there may be some later age at which a signal sent from the same galaxy can never reach the Earth at any point in the infinite future, so, for example, we might never see what the galaxy looked like 10 billion years after the Big Bang,[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-Loeb2002-13) even though it remains at the same comoving distance less than that of the observable universe.

This can be used to define a type of cosmic [event horizon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Event_horizon "Event horizon") whose distance from the Earth changes over time. For example, the current distance to this horizon is about 16 billion light-years, meaning that a signal from an event happening at present can eventually reach the Earth if the event is less than 16 billion light-years away, but the signal will never reach the Earth if the event is further away.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-ly93-9)

The space before this cosmic event horizon can be called "reachable universe", that is all galaxies closer than that could be reached if we left for them today, at the speed of light; all galaxies beyond that are unreachable.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-21)[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-22) Simple observation will show the future visibility limit (62 billion light-years) is exactly equal to the reachable limit (16 billion light-years) added to the current visibility limit (46 billion light-years).[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-23)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-mapofuniverse-7)

"The universe" versus "the observable universe"
-----------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Observable_universe&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: \"The universe\" versus \"the observable universe\"")]

Both popular and professional research articles in cosmology often use the term "universe" to mean "observable universe".[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] This can be justified on the grounds that we can never know anything by direct observation about any part of the universe that is [causally disconnected](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Causality_(physics) "Causality (physics)") from the Earth, although many credible theories require a total universe much larger than the observable universe.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] No evidence exists to suggest that the boundary of the observable universe constitutes a boundary on the universe as a whole, nor do any of the mainstream cosmological models propose that the universe has any physical boundary in the first place. However, some models propose it could be finite but unbounded,[[note 3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-24) like a higher-dimensional analogue of the 2D surface of a sphere that is finite in area but has no edge.

It is plausible that the [galaxies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy "Galaxy") within the observable universe represent only a minuscule fraction of the galaxies in the universe. According to the theory of [cosmic inflation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_inflation "Cosmic inflation") initially introduced by [Alan Guth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alan_Guth "Alan Guth") and [D. Kazanas](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=D._Kazanas&action=edit&redlink=1 "D. Kazanas (page does not exist)"),[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-25) if it is assumed that inflation began about 10−37 seconds after the Big Bang and that the pre-inflation size of the universe was approximately equal to the speed of light times its age, that would suggest that at present the entire universe's size is at least 1.5×10 34 light-years — this is at least 3×10 23 times the radius of the observable universe.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-26)

If the universe is finite but unbounded, it is also possible that the universe is _smaller_ than the observable universe. In this case, what we take to be very distant galaxies may actually be duplicate images of nearby galaxies, formed by light that has circumnavigated the universe. It is difficult to test this hypothesis experimentally because different images of a galaxy would show different eras in its history, and consequently might appear quite different. Bielewicz et al.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-27) claim to establish a lower bound of 27.9 gigaparsecs (91 billion light-years) on the diameter of the last scattering surface. This value is based on matching-circle analysis of the [WMAP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WMAP "WMAP") 7-year data. This approach has been disputed.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-28)

[![Image 4](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/HubbleUltraDeepFieldwithScaleComparison.jpg/500px-HubbleUltraDeepFieldwithScaleComparison.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:HubbleUltraDeepFieldwithScaleComparison.jpg)

[Hubble Ultra-Deep Field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble_Ultra-Deep_Field "Hubble Ultra-Deep Field") image of a region of the observable universe (equivalent sky area size shown in bottom left corner), near the [constellation Fornax](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fornax "Fornax"). Each spot is a [galaxy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy "Galaxy"), consisting of billions of stars. The light from the smallest, most [redshifted](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift "Redshift") galaxies originated around 12.6 billion years ago,[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-Malhotra-29) close to the [age of the universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Age_of_the_universe "Age of the universe").

The [comoving distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comoving_and_proper_distances "Comoving and proper distances") from Earth to the edge of the observable universe is about 14.26 giga[parsecs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parsec "Parsec") (46.5 [billion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1000000000_(number) "1000000000 (number)")[light-years](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light-year "Light-year") or 4.40×10 26 m) in any direction. The observable universe is thus a sphere with a [diameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diameter "Diameter") of about 28.5 gigaparsecs[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-30) (93 billion light-years or 8.8×10 26 m).[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-31) Assuming that space is roughly [flat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shape_of_the_universe#Universe_with_zero_curvature "Shape of the universe") (in the sense of being a [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space")), this size corresponds to a comoving volume of about 1.22×10 4 Gpc 3 (4.22×10 5 Gly 3 or 3.57×10 80 m 3).[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-32)

These are distances now (in [cosmological time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmological_time "Cosmological time")), not distances at the time the light was emitted. For example, the cosmic microwave background radiation that we see right now was emitted at the [time of photon decoupling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recombination_(cosmology) "Recombination (cosmology)"), estimated to have occurred about 380,000 years after the Big Bang,[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-wmap7parameters-33)[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-34) which occurred around 13.8 billion years ago. This radiation was emitted by matter that has, in the intervening time, mostly condensed into galaxies, and those galaxies are now calculated to be about 46 billion light-years from Earth.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-mapofuniverse-7)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-ly93-9) To estimate the distance to that matter at the time the light was emitted, we may first note that according to the [Friedmann–Lemaître–Robertson–Walker metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedmann%E2%80%93Lema%C3%AEtre%E2%80%93Robertson%E2%80%93Walker_metric "Friedmann–Lemaître–Robertson–Walker metric"), which is used to model the expanding universe, if we receive light with a [redshift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift "Redshift") of _z_, then the [scale factor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scale_factor_(cosmology) "Scale factor (cosmology)") at the time the light was originally emitted is given by[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-35)[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-36)

> ![Image 5: {\displaystyle a(t)={\frac {1}{1+z}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3fc50f64b94c0a8d7356a0726ca0a303db1c8b95).

[WMAP nine-year results](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe#Nine-year_data_release "Wilkinson Microwave Anisotropy Probe") combined with other measurements give the redshift of photon decoupling as _z_=1 091.64±0.47,[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-bennet-wmap9year-2012-37) which implies that the scale factor at the time of photon [decoupling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoupling_(cosmology) "Decoupling (cosmology)") would be 1⁄1092.64. So if the matter that originally emitted the oldest CMBR [photons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon "Photon") has a present distance of 46 billion light-years, then the distance would have been only about 42 million light-years at the time of decoupling.

The [light-travel distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light-travel_distance "Light-travel distance") to the edge of the observable universe is the [age of the universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Age_of_the_universe "Age of the universe") times the [speed of light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light "Speed of light"), 13.8 billion light years. This is the distance that a photon emitted shortly after the Big Bang, such as one from the [cosmic microwave background](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background "Cosmic microwave background"), has traveled to reach observers on Earth. Because [spacetime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spacetime "Spacetime") is curved, corresponding to the [expansion of space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expansion_of_the_universe "Expansion of the universe"), this distance does not correspond to the true distance at any moment in time.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-38)

### Number of galaxies and stars

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Observable_universe&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Number of galaxies and stars")]

The observable universe contains as many as an estimated 2 trillion galaxies[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-BBC-20231129-39)[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-40)[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-41) and, overall, as many as an estimated 10 24 stars[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-ESA-2019-42)[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-43) – more stars (and, potentially, Earth-like planets) than all the [grains of beach sand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sand "Sand") on planet [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth "Earth").[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-SU-20020201-44)[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-CNET-20150319-45)[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-MNRAS-20150313-46) Other estimates are in the hundreds of billions rather than trillions.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-:0-47)[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-ann21001-48)[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-arxiv:2011.03052-49) The estimated total number of stars in an [inflationary universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_inflation "Cosmic inflation") (observed and unobserved) is 10 100.[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-SR-20200203-50)

### Matter content—number of atoms

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Observable_universe&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Matter content—number of atoms")]

Assuming the mass of ordinary matter is about 1.45×10 53 kg as discussed above, and assuming all atoms are [hydrogen atoms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_atom "Hydrogen atom") (which are about 74% of all atoms in the Milky Way by mass), the estimated total number of atoms in the observable universe is obtained by dividing the mass of ordinary matter by the mass of a hydrogen atom. The result is approximately 10 80 hydrogen atoms, also known as the [Eddington number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eddington_number "Eddington number").[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-51)

### Mass of ordinary matter

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Observable_universe&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Mass of ordinary matter")]

The mass of the observable universe is often quoted as 10 53 kg.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-Paul_Davies_2006_43-52) In this context, mass refers to ordinary (baryonic) matter and includes the [interstellar medium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_medium "Interstellar medium") (ISM) and the [intergalactic medium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intergalactic_medium "Intergalactic medium") (IGM). However, it excludes [dark matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter "Dark matter") and [dark energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_energy "Dark energy"). This quoted value for the mass of ordinary matter in the universe can be estimated based on critical density. The calculations are for the observable universe only as the volume of the whole is unknown and may be infinite.

### Estimates based on critical density

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Observable_universe&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Estimates based on critical density")]

Critical density is the energy density for which the universe is flat.[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-53) If there is no dark energy, it is also the [density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Density "Density") for which the expansion of the universe is poised between continued expansion and collapse.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-54) From the [Friedmann equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedmann_equations "Friedmann equations"), the value for ![Image 6: {\displaystyle \rho _{\text{c}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eb5e0129465414c2e5fbbb5ca317fcee3678c277) critical density, is:[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-55)

![Image 7: {\displaystyle \rho _{\text{c}}={\frac {3H^{2}}{8\pi G}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/844811610634c6446119c46172f0302e06f7d493)
where _G_ is the [gravitational constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_constant "Gravitational constant") and _H_ = _H_ 0 is the present value of the [Hubble constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble_constant "Hubble constant"). The value for _H_ 0, as given by the European Space Agency's Planck Telescope, is _H_ 0 = 67.15 kilometres per second per megaparsec. This gives a critical density of 0.85×10−26 kg/m 3, or about 5 hydrogen atoms per cubic metre. This density includes four significant types of energy/mass: ordinary matter (4.8%), neutrinos (0.1%), [cold dark matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cold_dark_matter "Cold dark matter") (26.8%), and [dark energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_energy "Dark energy") (68.3%).[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-planck_cosmological_parameters-56)

Although neutrinos are [Standard Model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model "Standard Model") particles, they are listed separately because they are [ultra-relativistic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scale_factor_(cosmology)#Radiation-dominated_era "Scale factor (cosmology)") and hence [behave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equation_of_state_(cosmology)#Ultra-relativistic_particles "Equation of state (cosmology)") like radiation rather than like matter. The density of ordinary matter, as measured by Planck, is 4.8% of the total critical density or 4.08×10−28 kg/m 3. To convert this density to mass we must multiply by volume, a value based on the radius of the "observable universe". Since the universe has been expanding for 13.8 billion years, the [comoving distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comoving_and_proper_distances "Comoving and proper distances") (radius) is now about 46.6 billion light-years. Thus, volume (⁠4/3⁠_πr_ 3) equals 3.58×10 80 m 3 and the mass of ordinary matter equals density (4.08×10−28 kg/m 3) times volume (3.58×10 80 m 3) or 1.46×10 53 kg.

Large-scale structure
---------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Observable_universe&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Large-scale structure")]

[![Image 8](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Galactic_treasure_chest_RXC_J0142.9%2B4438.jpg/250px-Galactic_treasure_chest_RXC_J0142.9%2B4438.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Galactic_treasure_chest_RXC_J0142.9%2B4438.jpg)

Galaxy clusters, like [RXC J0142.9+4438](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RXC_J0142.9%2B4438 "RXC J0142.9+4438"), are the nodes of the cosmic web that permeates the entire Universe.[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-57)

Video of a [cosmological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmology "Cosmology")[simulation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_simulation "Computer simulation") of the local universe, showing the large-scale structure of galaxy clusters and dark matter.[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-58)

[Sky surveys](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift_survey "Redshift survey") and mappings of the various [wavelength](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wavelength "Wavelength") bands of [electromagnetic radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation "Electromagnetic radiation") (in particular [21-cm emission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_line "Hydrogen line")) have yielded much information on the content and character of the [universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe "Universe")'s structure. The organization of structure appears to follow a [hierarchical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hierarchy "Hierarchy") model with organization up to the [scale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scale_(spatial) "Scale (spatial)") of [superclusters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supercluster "Supercluster") and [filaments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_filament "Galaxy filament"). Larger than this (at scales between 30 and 200 megaparsecs),[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-59) there seems to be no continued structure, a phenomenon that has been referred to as the _End of Greatness_.[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-Kirshner-60) The shape of the large scale structure can be summarized by the [matter power spectrum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matter_power_spectrum "Matter power spectrum").

### Cosmic Web: walls, filaments, nodes, and voids

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Observable_universe&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Cosmic Web: walls, filaments, nodes, and voids")]

[![Image 9](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Map_of_the_Cosmic_Web_Generated_from_Slime_Mould_Algorithm.jpg/500px-Map_of_the_Cosmic_Web_Generated_from_Slime_Mould_Algorithm.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Map_of_the_Cosmic_Web_Generated_from_Slime_Mould_Algorithm.jpg)

Map of the cosmic web generated from a slime mould-inspired algorithm[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-61)

The organization of structure arguably begins at the stellar level, though most cosmologists rarely address [astrophysics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrophysics "Astrophysics") on that scale. [Stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star "Star") are organized into [galaxies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy "Galaxy"), which in turn form [galaxy groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_group "Galaxy group"), [galaxy clusters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_cluster "Galaxy cluster"), [superclusters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supercluster "Supercluster"), sheets, [walls and filaments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_filament "Galaxy filament"), which are separated by immense [voids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Void_(astronomy) "Void (astronomy)"), creating a vast foam-like structure[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-62) sometimes called the "cosmic web". Prior to 1989, it was commonly assumed that [virialized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virial_theorem "Virial theorem") galaxy clusters were the largest structures in existence, and that they were distributed more or less uniformly throughout the universe in every direction. However, since the early 1980s, more and more structures have been discovered. In 1983, Adrian Webster identified the [Webster LQG](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Webster_LQG "Webster LQG"), a [large quasar group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Large_quasar_group "Large quasar group") consisting of 5 quasars. The discovery was the first identification of a large-scale structure, and has expanded the information about the known grouping of matter in the universe.

In 1987, [Robert Brent Tully](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/R._Brent_Tully "R. Brent Tully") identified the [Pisces–Cetus Supercluster Complex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pisces%E2%80%93Cetus_Supercluster_Complex "Pisces–Cetus Supercluster Complex"), the galaxy filament in which the [Milky Way](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milky_Way "Milky Way") resides. It is about 1 billion light-years across. That same year, an unusually large region with a much lower than average distribution of galaxies was discovered, the [Giant Void](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giant_Void "Giant Void"), which measures 1.3 billion light-years across. Based on [redshift survey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift_survey "Redshift survey") data, in 1989 [Margaret Geller](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Margaret_Geller "Margaret Geller") and [John Huchra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Huchra "John Huchra") discovered the "[Great Wall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CfA2_Great_Wall "CfA2 Great Wall")",[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-redshift-63) a sheet of galaxies more than 500 million [light-years](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light-year "Light-year") long and 200 million light-years wide, but only 15 million light-years thick. The existence of this structure escaped notice for so long because it requires locating the position of galaxies in three dimensions, which involves combining location information about the galaxies with distance information from [redshifts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift "Redshift").

Two years later, astronomers Roger G. Clowes and Luis E. Campusano discovered the [Clowes–Campusano LQG](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clowes%E2%80%93Campusano_LQG "Clowes–Campusano LQG"), a [large quasar group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Large_quasar_group "Large quasar group") measuring two billion light-years at its widest point, which was the largest known structure in the universe at the time of its announcement. In April 2003, another large-scale structure was discovered, the [Sloan Great Wall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sloan_Great_Wall "Sloan Great Wall"). In August 2007, a possible supervoid was detected in the constellation [Eridanus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eridanus_(constellation) "Eridanus (constellation)").[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-64) It coincides with the '[CMB cold spot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CMB_cold_spot "CMB cold spot")', a cold region in the microwave sky that is highly improbable under the currently favored cosmological model. This supervoid could cause the cold spot, but to do so it would have to be improbably big, possibly a billion light-years across, almost as big as the Giant Void mentioned above.

Unsolved problem in physics

The largest structures in the universe are larger than expected. Are these actual structures or random density fluctuations?

[![Image 10](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Large-scale_structure_of_light_distribution_in_the_universe.jpg/500px-Large-scale_structure_of_light_distribution_in_the_universe.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Large-scale_structure_of_light_distribution_in_the_universe.jpg)

Computer simulated image of an area of space more than 50 million light-years across, presenting a possible large-scale distribution of light sources in the universe—precise relative contributions of galaxies and [quasars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasar "Quasar") are unclear.

Another large-scale structure is the [SSA22 Protocluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SSA22_Protocluster "SSA22 Protocluster"), a collection of galaxies and enormous gas bubbles that measures about 200 million light-years across.

In 2011, a large quasar group was discovered, [U1.11](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U1.11 "U1.11"), measuring about 2.5 billion light-years across. On January 11, 2013, another large quasar group, the [Huge-LQG](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Huge-LQG "Huge-LQG"), was discovered, which was measured to be four billion light-years across, the largest known structure in the universe at that time.[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-65) In November 2013, astronomers discovered the [Hercules–Corona Borealis Great Wall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hercules%E2%80%93Corona_Borealis_Great_Wall "Hercules–Corona Borealis Great Wall"),[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-2014paper-66)[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-original-67) an even bigger structure twice as large as the former. It was defined by the mapping of [gamma-ray bursts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamma-ray_burst "Gamma-ray burst").[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-2014paper-66)[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-68)

In 2021, the [American Astronomical Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Astronomical_Society "American Astronomical Society") announced the detection of the [Giant Arc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Giant_Arc "The Giant Arc"); a crescent-shaped string of galaxies that span 3.3 billion light years in length, located 9.2 billion light years from Earth in the constellation [Boötes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bo%C3%B6tes "Boötes") from observations captured by the [Sloan Digital Sky Survey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sloan_Digital_Sky_Survey "Sloan Digital Sky Survey").[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-69)

The _End of Greatness_ is the name occasionally given to an observational scale around 100[Mpc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Megaparsec "Megaparsec") (roughly 300 million light-years) where the lumpiness seen in the large-scale structure of the [universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe "Universe") is [homogenized](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/homogeneous "wikt:homogeneous") and [isotropized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotropic "Isotropic") in accordance with the [cosmological principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmological_principle "Cosmological principle").[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-70) The "lumpiness" is quantified by computing a [fractal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal "Fractal") dimension from observations.[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-71)[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-72) The [superclusters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supercluster "Supercluster") and [filaments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_filament "Galaxy filament") seen in smaller surveys are [randomized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Random "Random") to the extent that the smooth distribution of the universe is visually apparent. It was not until the [redshift surveys](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift_survey "Redshift survey") of the 1990s were completed that this scale could accurately be observed.[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-Kirshner-60)

Another indicator of large-scale structure is the '[Lyman-alpha forest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lyman-alpha_forest "Lyman-alpha forest")'. This is a collection of [absorption lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectral_line "Spectral line") that appear in the spectra of light from [quasars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasar "Quasar"), which are interpreted as indicating the existence of huge thin sheets of intergalactic (mostly [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen")) gas. These sheets appear to collapse into filaments, which can feed galaxies as they grow where filaments either cross or are dense. An early direct evidence for this cosmic web of gas was the 2019 detection, by astronomers from the RIKEN Cluster for Pioneering Research in Japan and Durham University in the U.K., of light from the brightest part of this web, surrounding and illuminated by a cluster of forming galaxies, acting as cosmic flashlights for intercluster medium hydrogen fluorescence via Lyman-alpha emissions.[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-74)[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-75)

In 2021, an international team, headed by Roland Bacon from the Centre de Recherche Astrophysique de Lyon (France), reported the first observation of diffuse extended Lyman-alpha emission from redshift 3.1 to 4.5 that traced several cosmic web filaments on scales of 2.5−4[cMpc](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=CMpc&action=edit&redlink=1 "CMpc (page does not exist)") (comoving mega-parsecs), in filamentary environments outside massive structures typical of web nodes.[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-76)

Some caution is required in describing structures on a cosmic scale because they are often different from how they appear. [Gravitational lensing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_lens "Gravitational lens") can make an image appear to originate in a different direction from its real source, when foreground objects curve surrounding spacetime (as predicted by [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity")) and deflect passing light rays. Rather usefully, strong gravitational lensing can sometimes magnify distant galaxies, making them easier to detect. [Weak lensing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weak_gravitational_lensing "Weak gravitational lensing") by the intervening universe in general also subtly changes the observed large-scale structure.

The large-scale structure of the universe also looks different if only redshift is used to measure distances to galaxies. For example, galaxies behind a galaxy cluster are attracted to it and fall towards it, and so are [blueshifted](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blueshift "Blueshift") (compared to how they would be if there were no cluster). On the near side, objects are redshifted. Thus, the environment of the cluster looks somewhat pinched if using redshifts to measure distance. The opposite effect is observed on galaxies already within a cluster: the galaxies have some random motion around the cluster center, and when these random motions are converted to redshifts, the cluster appears elongated. This creates a "_[finger of God](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift-space\_distortions "Redshift-space distortions")_"—the illusion of a long chain of galaxies pointed at Earth.

### Cosmography of Earth's cosmic neighborhood

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Observable_universe&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Cosmography of Earth's cosmic neighborhood")]

At the centre of the [Hydra–Centaurus Supercluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydra%E2%80%93Centaurus_Supercluster "Hydra–Centaurus Supercluster"), a gravitational anomaly called the [Great Attractor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Attractor "Great Attractor") affects the motion of galaxies over a region hundreds of millions of light-years across. These galaxies are all [redshifted](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift "Redshift"), in accordance with [Hubble's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble%27s_law "Hubble's law"). This indicates that they are receding from us and from each other, but the variations in their redshift are sufficient to reveal the existence of a concentration of mass equivalent to tens of thousands of galaxies.

The Great Attractor, discovered in 1986, lies at a distance of between 150 million and 250 million light-years in the direction of the [Hydra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydra_(constellation) "Hydra (constellation)") and [Centaurus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centaurus "Centaurus")[constellations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constellation "Constellation"). In its vicinity there is a preponderance of large old galaxies, many of which are colliding with their neighbours, or radiating large amounts of radio waves.

In 1987, [astronomer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomer "Astronomer")[R. Brent Tully](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/R._Brent_Tully "R. Brent Tully") of the [University of Hawaii](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Hawaii "University of Hawaii")'s Institute of Astronomy identified what he called the [Pisces–Cetus Supercluster Complex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pisces%E2%80%93Cetus_Supercluster_Complex "Pisces–Cetus Supercluster Complex"), a structure one billion [light-years](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light-year "Light-year") long and 150 million light-years across in which, he claimed, the Local Supercluster is embedded.[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-77)

Most distant objects
--------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Observable_universe&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Most distant objects")]

The most distant [astronomical object](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_object "Astronomical object") identified is a galaxy classified as [MoM-z14](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MoM-z14 "MoM-z14"),[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-78) at a redshift of 14.44. In 2009, a [gamma ray burst](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamma_ray_burst "Gamma ray burst"), [GRB 090423](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GRB_090423 "GRB 090423"), was found to have a [redshift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift "Redshift") of 8.2, which indicates that the collapsing star that caused it exploded when the universe was only 630 million years old.[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-NASAGRB-79) The burst happened approximately 13 billion years ago,[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-80) so a distance of about 13 billion light-years was widely quoted in the media, or sometimes a more precise figure of 13.035 billion light-years.[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-NASAGRB-79)

This would be the "light travel distance" (see [Distance measures (cosmology)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distance_measures_(cosmology) "Distance measures (cosmology)")) rather than the "[proper distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comoving_and_proper_distances#Uses_of_the_proper_distance "Comoving and proper distances")" used in both [Hubble's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble%27s_law "Hubble's law") and in defining the size of the observable universe. Cosmologist [Ned Wright](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edward_L._Wright "Edward L. Wright") argues against using this measure.[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-81) The proper distance for a redshift of 8.2 would be about 9.2 [Gpc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Megaparsecs "Megaparsecs"),[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_note-82) or about 30 billion light-years.

The limit of observability in the universe is set by cosmological horizons which limit—based on various physical constraints—the extent to which information can be obtained about various events in the universe. The most famous horizon is the [particle horizon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_horizon "Particle horizon") which sets a limit on the precise distance that can be seen due to the finite [age of the universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Age_of_the_universe "Age of the universe"). Additional horizons are associated with the possible future extent of observations, larger than the particle horizon owing to the [expansion of space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expansion_of_space "Expansion of space"), an "optical horizon" at the [surface of last scattering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background "Cosmic microwave background"), and associated horizons with the surface of last scattering for [neutrinos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_neutrino_background "Cosmic neutrino background") and [gravitational waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_wave_background "Gravitational wave background").

1.   **[^](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_ref-15)**[Special relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_relativity "Special relativity") prevents nearby objects in the same local region from moving faster than the speed of light with respect to each other, but there is no such constraint for distant objects when the space between them is expanding; see [uses of the proper distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comoving_and_proper_distances#Uses_of_the_proper_distance "Comoving and proper distances") for a discussion.
2.   **[^](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_ref-mapofuniverse2_17-0)**The comoving distance of the future visibility limit is calculated on p. 8 of Gott et al.'s [A Map of the Universe](http://www.astro.princeton.edu/universe/ms.pdf) to be 4.50 times the [Hubble radius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble_radius "Hubble radius"), given as 4.220 billion parsecs (13.76 billion light-years), whereas the current comoving radius of the observable universe is calculated on p. 7 to be 3.38 times the Hubble radius. The number of galaxies in a sphere of a given comoving radius is proportional to the cube of the radius, so as shown on p. 8 the ratio between the number of galaxies observable in the future visibility limit to the number of galaxies observable today would be (4.50/3.38)3 = 2.36.
3.   **[^](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#cite_ref-24)**This does not mean "unbounded" in the mathematical sense; a finite universe would have an upper bound on the distance between two points. Rather, it means that there is no boundary past which there is nothing. See _[Geodesic manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic\_manifold "Geodesic manifold")_.
