In [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry"), a **dodecahedron** (from [Ancient Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language "Ancient Greek language")[δωδεκάεδρον](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%B4%CF%89%CE%B4%CE%B5%CE%BA%CE%AC%CE%B5%CE%B4%CF%81%CE%BF%CE%BD#Ancient_Greek "wikt:δωδεκάεδρον")_(\_dōdekáedron\_)_; from [δώδεκα](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%B4%CF%8E%CE%B4%CE%B5%CE%BA%CE%B1#Ancient_Greek "wikt:δώδεκα")_(\_dṓdeka\_)_'twelve' and [ἕδρα](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E1%BC%95%CE%B4%CF%81%CE%B1#Ancient_Greek "wikt:ἕδρα")_(\_hédra\_)_'base, seat, face') or **duodecahedron**[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dodecahedron#cite_note-1) is any [polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyhedron "Polyhedron") with twelve flat faces. The most familiar dodecahedron is the [regular dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron "Regular dodecahedron") with regular pentagons as faces, which is a [Platonic solid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platonic_solid "Platonic solid"). There are also three [regular star dodecahedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler%E2%80%93Poinsot_polyhedron "Kepler–Poinsot polyhedron"), which are constructed as [stellations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellation "Stellation") of the convex form. All of these have [icosahedral symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Icosahedral_symmetry "Icosahedral symmetry"), order 120.

Some dodecahedra have the same combinatorial structure as the regular dodecahedron (in terms of the graph formed by its vertices and edges), but their pentagonal faces are not regular: The [pyritohedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dodecahedron#Pyritohedron), a common crystal form in [pyrite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrite "Pyrite"), has [pyritohedral symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyritohedral_symmetry "Pyritohedral symmetry"), while the [tetartoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dodecahedron#Tetartoid) has [tetrahedral symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedral_symmetry "Tetrahedral symmetry").

The [rhombic dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhombic_dodecahedron "Rhombic dodecahedron") can be seen as a limiting case of the pyritohedron, and it has [octahedral symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octahedral_symmetry "Octahedral symmetry"). The [elongated dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elongated_dodecahedron "Elongated dodecahedron") and [trapezo-rhombic dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezo-rhombic_dodecahedron "Trapezo-rhombic dodecahedron") variations, along with the rhombic dodecahedra, are [space-filling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space-filling_polyhedra "Space-filling polyhedra"). There are numerous [other dodecahedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dodecahedron#Other_dodecahedra).

While the regular dodecahedron shares many features with other Platonic solids, one unique property of it is that one can start at a corner of the surface and draw an infinite number of straight lines across the figure that return to the original point without crossing over any other corner.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dodecahedron#cite_note-cross-2)

Regular dodecahedron
--------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Dodecahedron&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Regular dodecahedron")]

The [regular dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron "Regular dodecahedron") is a convex polyhedron with regular pentagonal faces, three meeting at each vertex. It has 12 faces, 30 edges, and 20 vertices.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dodecahedron#cite_note-sutton-3) It is one of the five regular [Platonic solids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platonic_solid "Platonic solid"), named after [Plato](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plato "Plato") who classified the five solids, resembling the [classical elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_element "Classical element"); the regular dodecahedron is a constellation.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dodecahedron#cite_note-cromwell-4) Its [dual](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_polyhedron "Dual polyhedron") is the [regular icosahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron "Regular icosahedron").

The regular dodecahedron has three [stellations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellation "Stellation"), all of which are regular star dodecahedra. They form three of the four [Kepler–Poinsot polyhedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler%E2%80%93Poinsot_polyhedron "Kepler–Poinsot polyhedron"). They are the [small stellated dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Small_stellated_dodecahedron "Small stellated dodecahedron"), the [great dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_dodecahedron "Great dodecahedron"), and the [great stellated dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_stellated_dodecahedron "Great stellated dodecahedron").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dodecahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTECromwell1997[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidOJowej1QWpoCpgPA265_265%E2%80%93266]-5) The small stellated dodecahedron and great dodecahedron are dual to each other; the great stellated dodecahedron is dual to the [great icosahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_icosahedron "Great icosahedron"). All of these regular star dodecahedra have regular pentagonal or [pentagrammic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentagram "Pentagram") faces. The convex regular dodecahedron and great stellated dodecahedron are different realisations of the same [abstract regular polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abstract_polytope "Abstract polytope"); the small stellated dodecahedron and great dodecahedron are different realisations of another abstract regular polyhedron.

Other pentagonal dodecahedra
----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Dodecahedron&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Other pentagonal dodecahedra")]

In [crystallography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystallography "Crystallography"), two important dodecahedra can occur as crystal forms in some [symmetry classes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystallographic_point_groups "Crystallographic point groups") of the [cubic crystal system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cubic_crystal_system "Cubic crystal system") that are topologically equivalent to the regular dodecahedron but less symmetrical: the pyritohedron with [pyritohedral symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyritohedral_symmetry "Pyritohedral symmetry"), and the [tetartoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetartoid "Tetartoid") with [tetrahedral symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedral_symmetry "Tetrahedral symmetry"):

[![Image 17](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Polyhedron_pyritohedron_transparent_max.gif/250px-Polyhedron_pyritohedron_transparent_max.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Polyhedron_pyritohedron_transparent_max.gif)

Pyritohedron

A **pyritohedron** (or pentagonal dodecahedron) is a dodecahedron with [pyritohedral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyritohedral_symmetry "Pyritohedral symmetry") symmetry T h. Like the [regular dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron "Regular dodecahedron"), it has twelve identical [pentagonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentagon "Pentagon") faces, with three meeting in each of the 20 vertices. However, the pentagons are not constrained to be regular, and the underlying atomic arrangement has no true fivefold symmetry axis. Its 30 edges are divided into two sets, containing 24 and 6 edges of the same length. The only axes of [rotational symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_symmetry "Rotational symmetry") are three mutually perpendicular twofold axes and four threefold axes.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dodecahedron#cite_note-hs-6)

Although regular dodecahedra do not exist in crystals, the pyritohedron form occurs in the crystals of the [mineral pyrite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrite "Pyrite"),[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dodecahedron#cite_note-hs-6) and it may be an inspiration for the discovery of the regular [Platonic solid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platonic_solid "Platonic solid") form.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dodecahedron#cite_note-stephenson-7) The true regular dodecahedron can occur as a shape for [quasicrystals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasicrystal "Quasicrystal") (such as [holmium–magnesium–zinc quasicrystal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holmium%E2%80%93magnesium%E2%80%93zinc_quasicrystal "Holmium–magnesium–zinc quasicrystal")) with [icosahedral symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Icosahedral_symmetry "Icosahedral symmetry"), which includes true fivefold rotation axes.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dodecahedron#cite_note-cf-8)

The name _crystal pyrite_ comes from one of the two common [crystal habits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystal_habit "Crystal habit") shown by [pyrite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrite "Pyrite") (the other one being the [cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube "Cube")). #### Cartesian coordinates

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Dodecahedron&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Cartesian coordinates")]

The eight vertices of a cube have the coordinates (±1, ±1, ±1).

The coordinates of the 12 additional vertices are (0, ±(1 + _h_), ±(1 − _h_ 2)), (±(1 + _h_), ±(1 − _h_ 2), 0) and (±(1 − _h_ 2), 0, ±(1 + _h_)).

_h_ is the height of the [wedge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wedge_(geometry) "Wedge (geometry)")-shaped "roof" above the faces of that cube with edge length 2.

An important case is _h_ = ⁠1/2⁠ (a quarter of the cube edge length) for perfect natural pyrite (also the pyritohedron in the [Weaire–Phelan structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weaire%E2%80%93Phelan_structure "Weaire–Phelan structure")).

Another one is _h_ = ⁠1/[φ](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden_ratio "Golden ratio")⁠ = 0.618... for the [regular dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron "Regular dodecahedron"). See section _[Geometric freedom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dodecahedron#Geometric\_freedom)_ for other cases.

Two pyritohedra with swapped nonzero coordinates are in dual positions to each other like the dodecahedra in the [compound of two dodecahedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compound_of_two_dodecahedra "Compound of two dodecahedra").

The pyritohedron has a geometric degree of freedom with [limiting cases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limiting_case_(mathematics) "Limiting case (mathematics)") of a cubic [convex hull](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_hull "Convex hull") at one limit of collinear edges, and a [rhombic dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhombic_dodecahedron "Rhombic dodecahedron") as the other limit as 6 edges are degenerated to length zero. The regular dodecahedron represents a special intermediate case where all edges and angles are equal.

It is possible to go past these limiting cases, creating concave or nonconvex pyritohedra. The _endo-dodecahedron_ is concave and equilateral; it can tessellate space with the convex regular dodecahedron. Continuing from there in that direction, we pass through a degenerate case where twelve vertices coincide in the centre, and on to the regular [great stellated dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_stellated_dodecahedron "Great stellated dodecahedron") where all edges and angles are equal again, and the faces have been distorted into regular [pentagrams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentagram_(geometry) "Pentagram (geometry)"). On the other side, past the rhombic dodecahedron, we get a nonconvex equilateral dodecahedron with fish-shaped self-intersecting equilateral pentagonal faces.

Tetartoid

A **tetartoid** (also **tetragonal pentagonal dodecahedron**, **pentagon-tritetrahedron**, and **tetrahedric pentagon dodecahedron**) is a dodecahedron with chiral [tetrahedral symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedral_symmetry "Tetrahedral symmetry") (T). Like the [regular dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron "Regular dodecahedron"), it has twelve identical [pentagonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentagon "Pentagon") faces, with three meeting in each of the 20 vertices. However, the pentagons are not regular and the figure has no fivefold symmetry axes.

[Cobaltite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cobaltite "Cobaltite")

Although regular dodecahedra do not exist in crystals, the tetartoid form does. The name tetartoid comes from the Greek root for one-fourth because it has one fourth of full octahedral symmetry, and half of pyritohedral symmetry.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dodecahedron#cite_note-9) The mineral [cobaltite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cobaltite "Cobaltite") can have this symmetry form.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dodecahedron#cite_note-10)

Abstractions sharing the solid's [topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology "Topology") and symmetry can be created from the cube and the tetrahedron. In the cube each face is bisected by a slanted edge. In the tetrahedron each edge is trisected, and each of the new vertices connected to a face center. (In [Conway polyhedron notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conway_polyhedron_notation "Conway polyhedron notation") this is a gyro tetrahedron.)

The following points are vertices of a tetartoid pentagon under [tetrahedral symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedral_symmetry "Tetrahedral symmetry"):

(_a_, _b_, _c_); (−_a_, −_b_, _c_); (−⁠_n_/_d_ 1⁠, −⁠_n_/_d_ 1⁠, ⁠_n_/_d_ 1⁠); (−_c_, −_a_, _b_); (−⁠_n_/_d_ 2⁠, ⁠_n_/_d_ 2⁠, ⁠_n_/_d_ 2⁠),

Rhombic dodecahedron
--------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Dodecahedron&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Rhombic dodecahedron")]

[![Image 57](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Rhombicdodecahedron.jpg/250px-Rhombicdodecahedron.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Rhombicdodecahedron.jpg)

Rhombic dodecahedron

The _[rhombic dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhombic\_dodecahedron "Rhombic dodecahedron")_ is a [zonohedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zonohedron "Zonohedron") with twelve rhombic faces and octahedral symmetry. It is dual to the [quasiregular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasiregular_polyhedron "Quasiregular polyhedron")[cuboctahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cuboctahedron "Cuboctahedron") (an [Archimedean solid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archimedean_solid "Archimedean solid")) and occurs in nature as a crystal form. The rhombic dodecahedron packs together to fill space.

The _rhombic dodecahedron_ can be seen as a degenerate [pyritohedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyritohedron "Pyritohedron") where the 6 special edges have been reduced to zero length, reducing the pentagons into rhombic faces.

The rhombic dodecahedron has several [stellations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellation "Stellation"), the [first of which](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_stellation_of_rhombic_dodecahedron "First stellation of rhombic dodecahedron") is also a [parallelohedral spacefiller](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhombic_dodecahedron#Parallelohedron "Rhombic dodecahedron").

Another important rhombic dodecahedron, the [Bilinski dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bilinski_dodecahedron "Bilinski dodecahedron"), has twelve faces congruent to those of the [rhombic triacontahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhombic_triacontahedron "Rhombic triacontahedron"), i.e. the diagonals are in the ratio of the [golden ratio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden_ratio "Golden ratio"). It is also a [zonohedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zonohedron "Zonohedron") and was described by [Bilinski](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stanko_Bilinski "Stanko Bilinski") in 1960.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dodecahedron#cite_note-12) This figure is another spacefiller, and can also occur in non-periodic [spacefillings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Honeycomb_(geometry) "Honeycomb (geometry)") along with the rhombic triacontahedron, the rhombic icosahedron and rhombic hexahedra.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dodecahedron#cite_note-13)

There are 6,384,634 topologically distinct _convex_ dodecahedra, excluding mirror images—the number of vertices ranges from 8 to 20.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dodecahedron#cite_note-14) Two polyhedra are _topologically distinct_ if they have intrinsically different arrangements of faces and vertices, such that it is impossible to distort one into the other simply by changing the lengths of edges or the angles between edges or faces.

[Rhombo-hexagonal dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhombo-hexagonal_dodecahedron "Rhombo-hexagonal dodecahedron") (also known as "elongated dodecahedron"): a polyhedron with eight rhombi and four equilateral [hexagons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hexagon "Hexagon"). it has [D 4h](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihedral_symmetry "Dihedral symmetry") symmetry of order 16. It is one of five [Federov polyhedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federov_polyhedra "Federov polyhedra") or parallelohedra, generated to create its [honeycomb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Honeycomb_(geometry) "Honeycomb (geometry)").[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dodecahedron#cite_note-alexandrov-27) 

[Armand Spitz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Armand_Spitz "Armand Spitz") used a dodecahedron as the "globe" equivalent for his [Digital Dome planetarium projector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetarium_projector "Planetarium projector"),[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dodecahedron#cite_note-ley196502-28) based upon a suggestion from [Albert Einstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Einstein "Albert Einstein").
