Title: Levi-Civita symbol

Markdown Content:
In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), particularly in [linear algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_algebra "Linear algebra"), [tensor analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_analysis "Tensor analysis"), and [differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry"), the **Levi-Civita symbol** or **Levi-Civita epsilon** represents a collection of numbers defined from the [sign of a permutation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parity_of_a_permutation "Parity of a permutation") of the [natural numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number "Natural number")1, 2, ..., _n_, for some positive integer n. It is named after the Italian mathematician and physicist [Tullio Levi-Civita](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tullio_Levi-Civita "Tullio Levi-Civita"). Other names include the **[permutation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permutation "Permutation") symbol**, **antisymmetric symbol**, or **alternating symbol**, which refer to its [antisymmetric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antisymmetric_tensor "Antisymmetric tensor") property and definition in terms of permutations.

The standard letters to denote the Levi-Civita symbol are the Greek lower case [epsilon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epsilon "Epsilon")ε or ϵ, or less commonly the Latin lower case e. Index notation allows one to display permutations in a way compatible with tensor analysis: ![Image 1: {\displaystyle \varepsilon _{i_{1}i_{2}\dots i_{n}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/75be000de23194d18b8e0426ea0e43f592964fd8) where _each_ index _i_ 1, _i_ 2, ..., _i_ _n_ takes values 1, 2, ..., _n_. There are _n_ _n_ indexed values of _ε_ _i_ 1 _i_ 2..._i n_, which can be arranged into an n-dimensional array. The key defining property of the symbol is _total antisymmetry_ in the indices. When any two indices are interchanged, equal or not, the symbol is negated: ![Image 2: {\displaystyle \varepsilon _{\dots i_{p}\dots i_{q}\dots }=-\varepsilon _{\dots i_{q}\dots i_{p}\dots }.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35d766798eabbe2dee596bc417931838a4ac3842)

If any two indices are equal, the symbol is zero. When all indices are unequal, we have: ![Image 3: {\displaystyle \varepsilon _{i_{1}i_{2}\dots i_{n}}=(-1)^{p}\varepsilon _{1\,2\,\dots n},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ac125c56b2a3ac7919961e27096a4b2569f186d2) where p (called the parity of the permutation) is the number of pairwise interchanges of indices necessary to unscramble _i_ 1, _i_ 2, ..., _i_ _n_ into the order 1, 2, ..., _n_, and the factor (−1)_p_ is called the [sign, or signature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parity_of_a_permutation "Parity of a permutation") of the permutation. The value _ε_ 1 2 ... _n_ must be defined, else the particular values of the symbol for all permutations are indeterminate. Most authors choose _ε_ 1 2 ... _n_ = +1, which means the Levi-Civita symbol equals the sign of a permutation when the indices are all unequal. This choice is used throughout this article.

The term "n-dimensional Levi-Civita symbol" refers to the fact that the number of indices on the symbol n matches the [dimensionality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension "Dimension") of the [vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space") in question, which may be [Euclidean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space") or [non-Euclidean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-Euclidean_space "Non-Euclidean space"), for example, ![Image 4: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5) or [Minkowski space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minkowski_space "Minkowski space"). The values of the Levi-Civita symbol are independent of any [metric tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_tensor "Metric tensor") and [coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_system "Coordinate system"). Also, the specific term "symbol" emphasizes that it is not a [tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor "Tensor") because of how it transforms between coordinate systems; however it can be interpreted as a [tensor density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_density "Tensor density").

The Levi-Civita symbol allows the [determinant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Determinant "Determinant") of a square matrix, and the [cross product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product "Cross product") of two vectors in three-dimensional Euclidean space, to be expressed in [Einstein index notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_notation "Einstein notation").

The Levi-Civita symbol is most often used in three and four dimensions, and to some extent in two dimensions, so these are given here before defining the general case.

In [two dimensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two_dimensions "Two dimensions"), the Levi-Civita symbol is defined by: ![Image 5: {\displaystyle \varepsilon _{ij}={\begin{cases}+1&{\text{if }}(i,j)=(1,2)\\-1&{\text{if }}(i,j)=(2,1)\\\;\;\,0&{\text{if }}i=j\end{cases}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8515954e478faa56dc63174923dada31cfd90244) The values can be arranged into a 2×2 [antisymmetric matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antisymmetric_matrix "Antisymmetric matrix"): ![Image 6: {\displaystyle {\begin{pmatrix}\varepsilon _{11}&\varepsilon _{12}\\\varepsilon _{21}&\varepsilon _{22}\end{pmatrix}}={\begin{pmatrix}0&1\\-1&0\end{pmatrix}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7d71a29ba9609b6b42b24748f3c19c9a7df9a884)

Use of the two-dimensional symbol is common in condensed matter, and in certain specialized high-energy topics like [supersymmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supersymmetry "Supersymmetry")[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_symbol#cite_note-1) and [twistor theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Twistor_theory "Twistor theory"),[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_symbol#cite_note-2) where it appears in the context of 2-[spinors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spinors "Spinors").

[![Image 7](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Permutation_indices_3d_numerical.svg/250px-Permutation_indices_3d_numerical.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Permutation_indices_3d_numerical.svg)

For the indices (_i_, _j_, _k_) in _ε_ _ijk_, the values 1, 2, 3 occurring in the  cyclic order (1, 2, 3) correspond to _ε_ = +1, while occurring in the  reverse cyclic order correspond to _ε_ = −1, otherwise _ε_ = 0.

In [three dimensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three_dimensions "Three dimensions"), the Levi-Civita symbol is defined by:[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_symbol#cite_note-Tyldesley-3)![Image 8: {\displaystyle \varepsilon _{ijk}={\begin{cases}+1&{\text{if }}(i,j,k){\text{ is }}(1,2,3),(2,3,1),{\text{ or }}(3,1,2),\\-1&{\text{if }}(i,j,k){\text{ is }}(3,2,1),(1,3,2),{\text{ or }}(2,1,3),\\\;\;\,0&{\text{if }}i=j,{\text{ or }}j=k,{\text{ or }}k=i\end{cases}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/903ee1efe5f3e041da93aac5f55c82679c3329ad)

That is, _ε_ _ijk_ is 1 if (_i_, _j_, _k_) is an [even permutation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Even_and_odd_permutations "Even and odd permutations") of (1, 2, 3), −1 if it is an [odd permutation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Odd_permutation "Odd permutation"), and 0 if any index is repeated. In three dimensions only, the [cyclic permutations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclic_permutation "Cyclic permutation") of (1, 2, 3) are all even permutations, similarly the [anticyclic permutations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anticyclic_permutation "Anticyclic permutation") are all odd permutations. This means in 3d it is sufficient to take cyclic or anticyclic permutations of (1, 2, 3) and easily obtain all the even or odd permutations.

where i is the depth (blue: _i_ = 1; red: _i_ = 2; green: _i_ = 3), j is the row and k is the column.

### Generalization to _n_ dimensions

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Levi-Civita_symbol&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Generalization to n dimensions")]

More generally, in [_n_ dimensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/N-dimensional_space "N-dimensional space"), the Levi-Civita symbol is defined by:[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_symbol#cite_note-Kay-4)![Image 13: {\displaystyle \varepsilon _{a_{1}a_{2}a_{3}\ldots a_{n}}={\begin{cases}+1&{\text{if }}(a_{1},a_{2},a_{3},\ldots ,a_{n}){\text{ is an even permutation of }}(1,2,3,\dots ,n)\\-1&{\text{if }}(a_{1},a_{2},a_{3},\ldots ,a_{n}){\text{ is an odd permutation of }}(1,2,3,\dots ,n)\\\;\;\,0&{\text{otherwise}}\end{cases}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/23c0782db4dd1de2fb3c92a86bc0275bc5dae0cf)

Thus, it is the [sign of the permutation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Even_and_odd_permutations "Even and odd permutations") in the case of a permutation, and zero otherwise.

Using the [capital pi notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication#Capital_pi_notation "Multiplication")Π for ordinary multiplication of numbers, an explicit expression for the symbol is:[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]![Image 14: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\varepsilon _{a_{1}a_{2}a_{3}\ldots a_{n}}&=\prod _{1\leq i<j\leq n}\operatorname {sgn}(a_{j}-a_{i})\\&=\operatorname {sgn}(a_{2}-a_{1})\operatorname {sgn}(a_{3}-a_{1})\dotsm \operatorname {sgn}(a_{n}-a_{1})\operatorname {sgn}(a_{3}-a_{2})\operatorname {sgn}(a_{4}-a_{2})\dotsm \operatorname {sgn}(a_{n}-a_{2})\dotsm \operatorname {sgn}(a_{n}-a_{n-1})\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c6c1b64bf16e4deedbda479359870756c7b313ee) where the [signum function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Signum_function "Signum function") (denoted sgn) returns the sign of its argument while discarding the [absolute value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_value "Absolute value") if nonzero. The formula is valid for all index values, and for any n (when _n_ = 0 or _n_ = 1, this is the [empty product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empty_product "Empty product")). However, computing the formula above naively has a [time complexity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_complexity "Time complexity") of O(_n_ 2), whereas the sign can be computed from the parity of the permutation from its [disjoint cycles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permutation#Cycle_notation "Permutation") in only O(_n_ log(_n_)) cost.

A tensor whose components in an [orthonormal basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthonormal_basis "Orthonormal basis") are given by the Levi-Civita symbol (a tensor of [covariant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covariance_and_contravariance_of_vectors "Covariance and contravariance of vectors") rank n) is sometimes called a **permutation tensor**.

Under the ordinary transformation rules for tensors the Levi-Civita symbol is unchanged under pure rotations, consistent with that it is (by definition) the same in all coordinate systems related by orthogonal transformations. However, the Levi-Civita symbol is a [pseudotensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudotensor "Pseudotensor") because under an [orthogonal transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_matrix "Orthogonal matrix") of [Jacobian determinant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacobian_matrix_and_determinant "Jacobian matrix and determinant") −1, for example, a [reflection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflection_(mathematics) "Reflection (mathematics)") in an odd number of dimensions, it _should_ acquire a minus sign if it were a tensor. As it does not change at all, the Levi-Civita symbol is, by definition, a pseudotensor.

As the Levi-Civita symbol is a pseudotensor, the result of taking a cross product is a [pseudovector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudovector "Pseudovector"), not a vector.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_symbol#cite_note-Riley_et_al-5)

Under a general [coordinate change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_change "Coordinate change"), the components of the permutation tensor are multiplied by the [Jacobian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacobian_matrix_and_determinant "Jacobian matrix and determinant") of the [transformation matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transformation_matrix "Transformation matrix"). This implies that in coordinate frames different from the one in which the tensor was defined, its components can differ from those of the Levi-Civita symbol by an overall factor. If the frame is orthonormal, the factor will be ±1 depending on whether the orientation of the frame is the same or not.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_symbol#cite_note-Riley_et_al-5)

In index-free tensor notation, the Levi-Civita symbol is replaced by the concept of the [Hodge dual](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hodge_dual "Hodge dual").[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

For (**[1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_symbol#math_1)**), both sides are antisymmetric with respect of ij and mn. We therefore only need to consider the case _i_ ≠ _j_ and _m_ ≠ _n_. By substitution, we see that the equation holds for _ε_ 12 _ε_ 12, that is, for _i_ = _m_ = 1 and _j_ = _n_ = 2. (Both sides are then one). Since the equation is antisymmetric in ij and mn, any set of values for these can be reduced to the above case (which holds). The equation thus holds for all values of ij and mn.

Using (**[1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_symbol#math_1)**), we have for (**[2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_symbol#math_2)**)

![Image 36: {\displaystyle \varepsilon _{ij}\varepsilon ^{in}=\delta _{i}{}^{i}\delta _{j}{}^{n}-\delta _{i}{}^{n}\delta _{j}{}^{i}=2\delta _{j}{}^{n}-\delta _{j}{}^{n}=\delta _{j}{}^{n}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/30310b0d588a96f8a8efc22cf0bc78a864eca56a)
Here we used the [Einstein summation convention](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_summation_convention "Einstein summation convention") with i going from 1 to 2. Next, (**[3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_symbol#math_3)**) follows similarly from (**[2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_symbol#math_2)**).

To establish (**[5](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_symbol#math_5)**), notice that both sides vanish when _i_ ≠ _j_. Indeed, if _i_ ≠ _j_, then one can not choose m and n such that both permutation symbols on the left are nonzero. Then, with _i_ = _j_ fixed, there are only two ways to choose m and n from the remaining two indices. For any such indices, we have

![Image 37: {\displaystyle \varepsilon _{jmn}\varepsilon ^{imn}=\left(\varepsilon ^{imn}\right)^{2}=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4ebcbc59cb4dc2d3da520bf461e617b9f8d91021)
(no summation), and the result follows.

Then (**[6](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_symbol#math_6)**) follows since 3! = 6 and for any distinct indices _i_, _j_, _k_ taking values 1, 2, 3, we have

![Image 38: {\displaystyle \varepsilon _{ijk}\varepsilon ^{ijk}=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/33ba3b3478ae1012e5485c43d07cf7767c6aa5a3)(no summation, distinct _i_, _j_, _k_)

Applications and examples
-------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Levi-Civita_symbol&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: Applications and examples")]

In linear algebra, the [determinant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Determinant "Determinant") of a 3 × 3[square matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_matrix "Square matrix")**A** = [_a ij_] can be written[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_symbol#cite_note-6)

![Image 39: {\displaystyle \det(\mathbf {A} )=\sum _{i=1}^{3}\sum _{j=1}^{3}\sum _{k=1}^{3}\varepsilon _{ijk}a_{1i}a_{2j}a_{3k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/beb98573a2c43f7234607ca48ac0605e2bef26a3)
Similarly the determinant of an _n_ × _n_ matrix **A** = [_a ij_] can be written as[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_symbol#cite_note-Riley_et_al-5)

![Image 40: {\displaystyle \det(\mathbf {A} )=\varepsilon _{i_{1}\dots i_{n}}a_{1i_{1}}\dots a_{ni_{n}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/624037b5e0d67e88f75559b3d8b8eef62708d378)
where each i r should be summed over 1, ..., _n_, or equivalently:

![Image 41: {\displaystyle \det(\mathbf {A} )={\frac {1}{n!}}\varepsilon _{i_{1}\dots i_{n}}\varepsilon _{j_{1}\dots j_{n}}a_{i_{1}j_{1}}\dots a_{i_{n}j_{n}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ddb53f31327ebfd673ba1d3ebc7e85c15a1a9226)
where now each i r and each j r should be summed over 1, ..., _n_. More generally, we have the identity[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_symbol#cite_note-Riley_et_al-5)

![Image 42: {\displaystyle \sum _{i_{1},i_{2},\dots }\varepsilon _{i_{1}\dots i_{n}}a_{i_{1}\,j_{1}}\dots a_{i_{n}\,j_{n}}=\det(\mathbf {A} )\varepsilon _{j_{1}\dots j_{n}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7ca34c714ff51a545258d30ba70f5d7e90597dc6)

### Vector cross product

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Levi-Civita_symbol&action=edit&section=17 "Edit section: Vector cross product")]

#### Cross product (two vectors)

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Levi-Civita_symbol&action=edit&section=18 "Edit section: Cross product (two vectors)")]

Let ![Image 43: {\displaystyle (\mathbf {e_{1}} ,\mathbf {e_{2}} ,\mathbf {e_{3}} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d45b9a817716f203f184d855ed4c51207ec6e068) a [positively oriented](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orientation_(vector_space) "Orientation (vector space)") orthonormal basis of a vector space. If (_a_ 1, _a_ 2, _a_ 3) and (_b_ 1, _b_ 2, _b_ 3) are the coordinates of the [vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_(geometry) "Vector (geometry)")**a** and **b** in this basis, then their cross product can be written as a determinant:[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_symbol#cite_note-Riley_et_al-5)

![Image 44: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a\times b} ={\begin{vmatrix}\mathbf {e_{1}} &\mathbf {e_{2}} &\mathbf {e_{3}} \\a^{1}&a^{2}&a^{3}\\b^{1}&b^{2}&b^{3}\\\end{vmatrix}}=\sum _{i=1}^{3}\sum _{j=1}^{3}\sum _{k=1}^{3}\varepsilon _{ijk}\mathbf {e} _{i}a^{j}b^{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/889cde8a79a4213e00c27d6ae60d404f2de87f1c)
hence also using the Levi-Civita symbol, and more simply:

![Image 45: {\displaystyle (\mathbf {a\times b} )^{i}=\sum _{j=1}^{3}\sum _{k=1}^{3}\varepsilon _{ijk}a^{j}b^{k}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b798fa46e21c7f9d393a74a7c8abaf1de3d85f35)
In Einstein notation, the summation symbols may be omitted, and the i th component of their cross product equals[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_symbol#cite_note-Kay-4)

![Image 46: {\displaystyle (\mathbf {a\times b} )^{i}=\varepsilon _{ijk}a^{j}b^{k}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d753218e19a64d5d50e10ee54c8b0e4d970fb842)
The first component is

![Image 47: {\displaystyle (\mathbf {a\times b} )^{1}=a^{2}b^{3}-a^{3}b^{2}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0aa629f188edc5fc1a4dda7c8b7da5883e9a07eb)
then by cyclic permutations of 1, 2, 3 the others can be derived immediately, without explicitly calculating them from the above formulae:

![Image 48: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}(\mathbf {a\times b} )^{2}&=a^{3}b^{1}-a^{1}b^{3}\,,\\(\mathbf {a\times b} )^{3}&=a^{1}b^{2}-a^{2}b^{1}\,.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/030ec58e48a15541825567135d08a17784bc404d)

#### Triple scalar product (three vectors)

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Levi-Civita_symbol&action=edit&section=19 "Edit section: Triple scalar product (three vectors)")]

From the above expression for the cross product, we have:

![Image 49: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a\times b} =-\mathbf {b\times a} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8d9fd296c37cd456c261c10a6981ee312c7b96d4).
If **c** = (_c_ 1, _c_ 2, _c_ 3) is a third vector, then the [triple scalar product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triple_scalar_product "Triple scalar product") equals

![Image 50: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} \cdot (\mathbf {b\times c} )=\varepsilon _{ijk}a^{i}b^{j}c^{k}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5d059442d5d989e316bd62c383bd567249e8c1f3)
From this expression, it can be seen that the triple scalar product is antisymmetric when exchanging any pair of arguments. For example,

![Image 51: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} \cdot (\mathbf {b\times c} )=-\mathbf {b} \cdot (\mathbf {a\times c} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c51a7d073c282de6715a15845434e9212fcc8b5d).

#### Curl (one vector field)

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Levi-Civita_symbol&action=edit&section=20 "Edit section: Curl (one vector field)")]

If **F** = (_F_ 1, _F_ 2, _F_ 3) is a vector field defined on some [open set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_set "Open set") of ![Image 52: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5) as a [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)") of [position](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Position_vector "Position vector")**x** = (_x_ 1, _x_ 2, _x_ 3) (using [Cartesian coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinates "Cartesian coordinates")). Then the i th component of the [curl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curl_(mathematics) "Curl (mathematics)") of **F** equals[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_symbol#cite_note-Kay-4)

![Image 53: {\displaystyle (\nabla \times \mathbf {F} )^{i}(\mathbf {x} )=\varepsilon _{ijk}{\frac {\partial }{\partial x^{j}}}F^{k}(\mathbf {x} ),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/91b4a87f13cc5c475f19792c5daa72103a43b5e9)
which follows from the cross product expression above, substituting components of the [gradient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gradient "Gradient") vector [operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_operator "Linear operator") (nabla).

In any arbitrary [curvilinear coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvilinear_coordinate_system "Curvilinear coordinate system") and even in the absence of a [metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_tensor "Metric tensor") on the [manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manifold "Manifold"), the Levi-Civita symbol as defined above may be considered to be a [tensor density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_density "Tensor density") field in two different ways. It may be regarded as a [contravariant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covariance_and_contravariance_of_vectors "Covariance and contravariance of vectors") tensor density of weight +1 or as a covariant tensor density of weight −1. In _n_ dimensions using the generalized Kronecker delta,[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_symbol#cite_note-7)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_symbol#cite_note-8)

![Image 54: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\varepsilon ^{\mu _{1}\dots \mu _{n}}&=\delta _{\,1\,\dots \,n}^{\mu _{1}\dots \mu _{n}}\,\\\varepsilon _{\nu _{1}\dots \nu _{n}}&=\delta _{\nu _{1}\dots \nu _{n}}^{\,1\,\dots \,n}\,.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/31def93af1b3981aef59ed1713797d2e3b5f92b8)
Notice that these are numerically identical. In particular, the sign is the same.

Levi-Civita tensors
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Levi-Civita_symbol&action=edit&section=22 "Edit section: Levi-Civita tensors")]

On a [pseudo-Riemannian manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudo-Riemannian_manifold "Pseudo-Riemannian manifold"), one may define a coordinate-invariant covariant tensor field whose coordinate representation agrees with the Levi-Civita symbol wherever the coordinate system is such that the basis of the tangent space is orthonormal with respect to the metric and matches a selected orientation. This tensor should not be confused with the tensor density field mentioned above. The presentation in this section closely follows [Carroll 2004](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_symbol#CITEREFCarroll2004).

The covariant Levi-Civita tensor (also known as the [Riemannian volume form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_volume_form "Riemannian volume form")) in any coordinate system that matches the selected orientation is

![Image 55: {\displaystyle E_{a_{1}\dots a_{n}}={\sqrt {\left|\det[g_{ab}]\right|}}\,\varepsilon _{a_{1}\dots a_{n}}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/37e9a92e440539e0513ae569542bc390a4f154c1)
where g ab is the representation of the metric in that coordinate system. We can similarly consider a contravariant Levi-Civita tensor by raising the indices with the metric as usual,

![Image 56: {\displaystyle E^{a_{1}\dots a_{n}}=E_{b_{1}\dots b_{n}}\prod _{i=1}^{n}g^{a_{i}b_{i}}={\frac {1}{\sqrt {\left|\det[g_{ab}]\right|}}}\,\varepsilon ^{a_{1}\dots a_{n}},,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fa3492efae8d15837177a0c9515bd91395b17ef5)
but notice that if the [metric signature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_signature "Metric signature") contains an odd number of negative eigenvalues _q_, then the sign of the components of this tensor differ from the standard Levi-Civita symbol:[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_symbol#cite_note-9)

![Image 57: {\displaystyle E^{a_{1}\dots a_{n}}={\frac {\operatorname {sgn} \left(\det[g_{ab}]\right)}{\sqrt {\left|\det[g_{ab}]\right|}}}\,\varepsilon ^{a_{1}\dots a_{n}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6a560604067e888ef1a335e34f9e405519a8d818)
where sgn(det[g _ab_]) = (−1)_q_, ![Image 58: {\displaystyle \varepsilon _{a_{1}\dots a_{n}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/760a1af7c45078ae3218199d2f7abee8f7e63baf) is the usual Levi-Civita symbol discussed in the rest of this article, and we used the definition of the metric [determinant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Determinant "Determinant") in the derivation. More explicitly, when the tensor and basis orientation are chosen such that ![Image 59: {\textstyle E_{01\dots n}=+{\sqrt {\left|\det[g_{ab}]\right|}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/17ace6282f43d49b5451560f7ac626e66a7bc64e), we have that ![Image 60: {\displaystyle E^{01\dots n}={\frac {\operatorname {sgn}(\det[g_{ab}])}{\sqrt {\left|\det[g_{ab}]\right|}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c6b580c03f614708b26f6c3723071527fd82f130).

From this we can infer the identity,

![Image 61: {\displaystyle E^{\mu _{1}\dots \mu _{p}\alpha _{1}\dots \alpha _{n-p}}E_{\mu _{1}\dots \mu _{p}\beta _{1}\dots \beta _{n-p}}=(-1)^{q}p!\delta _{\beta _{1}\dots \beta _{n-p}}^{\alpha _{1}\dots \alpha _{n-p}}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/94b6c47b7e11fc7bf705827e38e10d07cf83380a)
where

![Image 62: {\displaystyle \delta _{\beta _{1}\dots \beta _{n-p}}^{\alpha _{1}\dots \alpha _{n-p}}=(n-p)!\delta _{\beta _{1}}^{\lbrack \alpha _{1}}\dots \delta _{\beta _{n-p}}^{\alpha _{n-p}\rbrack }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ca9ee2d97ed7e446a894fedeeb47897413691cde)
is the generalized Kronecker delta.

### Example: Minkowski space

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Levi-Civita_symbol&action=edit&section=23 "Edit section: Example: Minkowski space")]

In Minkowski space (the four-dimensional [spacetime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spacetime "Spacetime") of [special relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_relativity "Special relativity")), the covariant Levi-Civita tensor is

![Image 63: {\displaystyle E_{\alpha \beta \gamma \delta }=\pm {\sqrt {\left|\det[g_{\mu \nu }]\right|}}\,\varepsilon _{\alpha \beta \gamma \delta }\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/69cea2c0f47be8b11eed0db78ca4d4cb4cf5230d)
where the sign depends on the orientation of the basis. The contravariant Levi-Civita tensor is

![Image 64: {\displaystyle E^{\alpha \beta \gamma \delta }=g^{\alpha \zeta }g^{\beta \eta }g^{\gamma \theta }g^{\delta \iota }E_{\zeta \eta \theta \iota }\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b56d190e0afba6ce5313d59b47b0985e2cb3ba79)
The following are examples of the general identity above specialized to Minkowski space (with the negative sign arising from the odd number of negatives in the signature of the metric tensor in either sign convention):

![Image 65: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}E_{\alpha \beta \gamma \delta }E_{\rho \sigma \mu \nu }&=-g_{\alpha \zeta }g_{\beta \eta }g_{\gamma \theta }g_{\delta \iota }\delta _{\rho \sigma \mu \nu }^{\zeta \eta \theta \iota }\\E^{\alpha \beta \gamma \delta }E^{\rho \sigma \mu \nu }&=-g^{\alpha \zeta }g^{\beta \eta }g^{\gamma \theta }g^{\delta \iota }\delta _{\zeta \eta \theta \iota }^{\rho \sigma \mu \nu }\\E^{\alpha \beta \gamma \delta }E_{\alpha \beta \gamma \delta }&=-24\\E^{\alpha \beta \gamma \delta }E_{\rho \beta \gamma \delta }&=-6\delta _{\rho }^{\alpha }\\E^{\alpha \beta \gamma \delta }E_{\rho \sigma \gamma \delta }&=-2\delta _{\rho \sigma }^{\alpha \beta }\\E^{\alpha \beta \gamma \delta }E_{\rho \sigma \theta \delta }&=-\delta _{\rho \sigma \theta }^{\alpha \beta \gamma }\,.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/007860ac0ae48711cb274321e1728c9b9ddee770)
