**Christiaan Huygens**, [Lord of Zeelhem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halen "Halen"), [FRS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Society "Fellow of the Royal Society") ( [_HY-gənz_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key "Help:Pronunciation respelling key"),[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-2)[_HOY-gənz_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key "Help:Pronunciation respelling key");[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-3)Dutch:[[ˈkrɪstijaːn ˈɦœyɣə(n)s]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/Dutch "Help:IPA/Dutch")[](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/transcoded/f/f5/ChristianHuygensPronunciation.ogg/ChristianHuygensPronunciation.ogg.mp3 "Play audio")[ⓘ](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:ChristianHuygensPronunciation.ogg "File:ChristianHuygensPronunciation.ogg"); also spelled **Huyghens**; [Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin_language "Latin language"): _Hugenius_; 14 April 1629 – 8 July 1695) was a Dutch [mathematician](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematician "Mathematician"), [physicist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physicist "Physicist"), [engineer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineer "Engineer"), [astronomer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomer "Astronomer"), and [inventor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inventor "Inventor") who is regarded as a key figure in the [Scientific Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_Revolution "Scientific Revolution").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:24-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:14-5) In physics, Huygens made seminal contributions to [optics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optics "Optics") and [mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanics "Mechanics"), while as an astronomer he studied the [rings of Saturn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rings_of_Saturn "Rings of Saturn") and discovered its largest moon, [Titan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Titan_(moon) "Titan (moon)"). As an engineer and inventor, he improved the design of telescopes and invented the [pendulum clock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pendulum_clock "Pendulum clock"), the most accurate timekeeper for almost 300 years. A talented mathematician and physicist, his works contain the first idealization of a physical problem by a set of [mathematical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_model "Mathematical model")[parameters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parameter "Parameter"), and the first mathematical and mechanistic explanation of an [unobservable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unobservable "Unobservable") physical phenomenon.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:7-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:6-7)

Huygens first identified the correct laws of [elastic collision](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elastic_collision "Elastic collision") in his work _De Motu Corporum ex Percussione_, completed in 1656 but published posthumously in 1703.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:28-8) In 1659, Huygens derived geometrically the formula in [classical mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_mechanics "Classical mechanics") for the [centrifugal force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centrifugal_force "Centrifugal force") in his work _De vi Centrifuga_, a decade before [Isaac Newton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaac_Newton "Isaac Newton").[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-9) In optics, he is best known for his [wave theory of light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wave_theory_of_light "Wave theory of light"), which he described in his _[Traité de la Lumière](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treatise\_on\_Light "Treatise on Light")_ (1690). His theory of light was initially rejected in favour of Newton's [corpuscular theory of light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corpuscular_theory_of_light "Corpuscular theory of light"), until [Augustin-Jean Fresnel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Augustin-Jean_Fresnel "Augustin-Jean Fresnel") adapted Huygens's principle to give a complete explanation of the rectilinear propagation and diffraction effects of light in 1821. Today this principle is known as the [Huygens–Fresnel principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Huygens%E2%80%93Fresnel_principle "Huygens–Fresnel principle").

Huygens invented the pendulum clock in 1657, which he patented the same year. His [horological research](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horology "Horology") resulted in an extensive analysis of the [pendulum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pendulum "Pendulum") in _[Horologium Oscillatorium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horologium\_Oscillatorium "Horologium Oscillatorium")_ (1673), regarded as one of the most important 17th-century works on mechanics.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:7-6) While it contains descriptions of clock designs, most of the book is an analysis of pendular motion and a theory of [curves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curve "Curve"). In 1655, Huygens began grinding lenses with his brother Constantijn to build [refracting telescopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Refracting_telescope "Refracting telescope"). He discovered Saturn's biggest moon, Titan, and was the first to explain Saturn's strange appearance as due to "a thin, flat ring, nowhere touching, and inclined to the ecliptic."[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:3-10) In 1662, he developed what is now called the [Huygenian eyepiece](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Huygenian_eyepiece "Huygenian eyepiece"), a telescope with two lenses to diminish the amount of [dispersion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dispersion_(optics) "Dispersion (optics)").[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:2-11)

As a mathematician, Huygens developed the [theory of evolutes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evolute "Evolute") and wrote on [games of chance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Game_of_chance "Game of chance") and the [problem of points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Problem_of_points "Problem of points") in _Van Rekeningh in Spelen van Gluck_, which [Frans van Schooten](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frans_van_Schooten "Frans van Schooten") translated and published as _De Ratiociniis in Ludo Aleae_ (1657).[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:25-12) The use of [expected values](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expectation_value "Expectation value") by Huygens and others would later inspire [Jacob Bernoulli's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacob_Bernoulli "Jacob Bernoulli") work on [probability theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ars_Conjectandi "Ars Conjectandi").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-13)[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:5-14)

[Constantijn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constantijn_Huygens "Constantijn Huygens") surrounded by his five children (Christiaan, top right). [Mauritshuis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mauritshuis "Mauritshuis"), [The Hague](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Hague "The Hague").

Christiaan Huygens was born into a rich and influential Dutch family in [The Hague](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Hague "The Hague") on 14 April 1629, the second son of [Constantijn Huygens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constantijn_Huygens "Constantijn Huygens").[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-15)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-opendoor-16) Christiaan was named after his paternal grandfather.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-completedictionary-17)[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-18) His mother, [Suzanna van Baerle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suzanna_van_Baerle "Suzanna van Baerle"), died shortly after giving birth to Huygens's sister.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-19) The couple had five children: [Constantijn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constantijn_Huygens_Jr. "Constantijn Huygens Jr.") (1628), Christiaan (1629), [Lodewijk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lodewijck_Huygens "Lodewijck Huygens") (1631), Philips (1632) and Suzanna (1637).[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-father-20)

[Constantijn Huygens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constantijn_Huygens "Constantijn Huygens") was a diplomat and advisor to the [House of Orange](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/House_of_Orange-Nassau "House of Orange-Nassau"), in addition to being a poet and a musician. He [corresponded widely](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_Letters "Republic of Letters") with intellectuals across Europe, including [Galileo Galilei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galileo_Galilei "Galileo Galilei"), [Marin Mersenne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marin_Mersenne "Marin Mersenne"), and [René Descartes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Descartes "René Descartes").[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-21) Christiaan was educated at home until the age of sixteen, and from a young age liked to play with miniatures of [mills](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mill_(grinding) "Mill (grinding)") and other machines. He received a [liberal education](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liberal_education "Liberal education") from his father, studying languages, [music](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Music "Music"), [history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History "History"), [geography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography "Geography"), [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), [logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logic "Logic"), and [rhetoric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhetoric "Rhetoric"), alongside [dancing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dancing "Dancing"), [fencing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fencing "Fencing") and [horse riding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horse_riding "Horse riding").[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-completedictionary-17)[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-father-20)

In 1644, Huygens had as his mathematical tutor [Jan Jansz Stampioen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jan_Jansz_de_Jonge_Stampioen "Jan Jansz de Jonge Stampioen"), who assigned the 15-year-old a demanding reading list on contemporary science.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-22) Descartes was later impressed by his skills in geometry, as was Mersenne, who christened him the "new [Archimedes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archimedes "Archimedes")."[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:0-23)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-opendoor-16)[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:8-24)

At sixteen years of age, Constantijn sent Huygens to study law and mathematics at [Leiden University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leiden_University "Leiden University"), where he enrolled from May 1645 to March 1647.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-completedictionary-17)[Frans van Schooten Jr.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frans_van_Schooten "Frans van Schooten"), professor at Leiden's Engineering School, became private tutor to Huygens and his elder brother, Constantijn Jr., replacing Stampioen on the advice of Descartes.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-25)[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-26) Van Schooten brought Huygens's mathematical education up to date, particularly on the work of [Viète](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Vi%C3%A8te "François Viète"), Descartes, and [Fermat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre_de_Fermat "Pierre de Fermat").[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:32-27)

After two years, starting in March 1647, Huygens continued his studies at the newly founded [Orange College](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orange_College_of_Breda "Orange College of Breda"), in [Breda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Breda "Breda"), where his father was a [curator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curator "Curator"). Constantijn Huygens was closely involved in the new College, which lasted only to 1669; the rector was [André Rivet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Rivet "André Rivet").[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-28) Christiaan Huygens lived at the home of the jurist Johann Henryk Dauber while attending college, and had mathematics classes with the English lecturer [John Pell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Pell_(mathematician) "John Pell (mathematician)"). His time in Breda ended around the time when his brother Lodewijk, who was enrolled at the school, duelled with another student.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:14-5)[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-29) Huygens left Breda after completing his studies in August 1649 and had a stint as a diplomat on a mission with [Henry, Duke of Nassau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry,_Duke_of_Nassau "Henry, Duke of Nassau").[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-completedictionary-17) After stays at [Bentheim](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/County_of_Bentheim_(district) "County of Bentheim (district)") and [Flensburg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flensburg "Flensburg") in Germany, he visited [Copenhagen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Copenhagen "Copenhagen") and [Helsingør](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helsing%C3%B8r "Helsingør") in Denmark. Huygens hoped to cross the [Øresund](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%98resund "Øresund") to see Descartes in [Stockholm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stockholm "Stockholm") but Descartes died before he could do that.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:14-5)[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-30)

Although his father Constantijn had wished his son Christiaan to be a diplomat, circumstances kept him from becoming so. The [First Stadtholderless Period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_Stadtholderless_Period "First Stadtholderless Period") that began in 1650 meant that the House of Orange was no longer in power, removing Constantijn's influence. Further, he realized that his son had no interest in such a career.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-Dictionary,_p._469-31)

### Early correspondence

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Christiaan_Huygens&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Early correspondence")]

[![Image 4](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/bc/Ketting_Huygens.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Ketting_Huygens.jpg)

Picture of a hanging chain ([catenary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catenary "Catenary")) in a manuscript of Huygens

Huygens generally wrote in French or Latin.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-32) In 1646, while still a college student at Leiden, he began a correspondence with his father's friend, [Marin Mersenne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marin_Mersenne "Marin Mersenne"), who died soon afterwards in 1648.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-completedictionary-17) Mersenne wrote to Constantijn on his son's talent for mathematics, and flatteringly compared him to [Archimedes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archimedes "Archimedes") on 3 January 1647.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:10-33)

The letters show Huygens's early interest in mathematics. In October 1646 he wrote about the shape of a [suspension bridge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suspension_bridge "Suspension bridge"), demonstrating that a [hanging chain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catenary "Catenary") is not a [parabola](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parabola "Parabola"), as Galileo thought.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-34) Huygens would later label that curve the _catenaria_ ([catenary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catenary "Catenary")) in 1690 while corresponding with [Gottfried Leibniz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gottfried_Wilhelm_Leibniz "Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz").[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:16-35)

In the next two years (1647–48), Huygens's letters to Mersenne covered various topics, including a mathematical proof of the [law of free fall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_fall "Free fall"), the claim by [Grégoire de Saint-Vincent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gr%C3%A9goire_de_Saint-Vincent "Grégoire de Saint-Vincent") of [circle quadrature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Squaring_the_circle "Squaring the circle"), which Huygens showed to be wrong, the rectification of the ellipse, projectiles, and the [vibrating string](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vibrating_string "Vibrating string").[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-36) Some of Mersenne's concerns at the time, such as the [cycloid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cycloid "Cycloid") (he sent Huygens [Torricelli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evangelista_Torricelli "Evangelista Torricelli")'s treatise on the curve), the [centre of oscillation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Center_of_percussion "Center of percussion"), and the [gravitational constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_constant "Gravitational constant"), were matters Huygens only took seriously later in the 17th century.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:7-6) Mersenne had also written on musical theory. Huygens preferred [meantone temperament](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meantone_temperament "Meantone temperament"); he innovated in [31 equal temperament](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/31_equal_temperament "31 equal temperament") (which was not itself a new idea but known to [Francisco de Salinas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Francisco_de_Salinas "Francisco de Salinas")), using logarithms to investigate it further and show its close relation to the meantone system.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-Cohen1984-37)

In 1654, Huygens returned to his father's house in The Hague and was able to devote himself entirely to research.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-completedictionary-17) The family had another house, not far away at [Hofwijck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hofwijck "Hofwijck"), and he spent time there during the summer. Despite being very active, his scholarly life did not allow him to escape bouts of depression.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:9-38)

Subsequently, Huygens developed a broad range of correspondents, though with some difficulty after 1648 due to the five-year _[Fronde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fronde "Fronde")_ in France. Visiting Paris in 1655, Huygens called on [Ismael Boulliau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ismael_Boulliau "Ismael Boulliau") to introduce himself, who took him to see [Claude Mylon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Claude_Mylon "Claude Mylon").[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-39) The Parisian group of savants that had gathered around Mersenne held together into the 1650s, and Mylon, who had assumed the secretarial role, took some trouble to keep Huygens in touch.[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-40) Through [Pierre de Carcavi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre_de_Carcavi "Pierre de Carcavi") Huygens corresponded in 1656 with [Pierre de Fermat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre_de_Fermat "Pierre de Fermat"), whom he admired greatly. The experience was bittersweet and somewhat puzzling since it became clear that Fermat had dropped out of the research mainstream, and his priority claims could probably not be made good in some cases. Besides, Huygens was looking by then to [apply mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Applied_mathematics "Applied mathematics") to physics, while Fermat's concerns ran to purer topics.[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-41)

[![Image 5](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Christiaan_Huygens_by_Jaques_Clerion.jpg/250px-Christiaan_Huygens_by_Jaques_Clerion.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Christiaan_Huygens_by_Jaques_Clerion.jpg)

Christiaan Huygens, relief by [Jean-Jacques Clérion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean-Jacques_Cl%C3%A9rion "Jean-Jacques Clérion") (c. 1670)

Like some of his contemporaries, Huygens was often slow to commit his results and discoveries to print, preferring to disseminate his work through letters instead.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:12-42) In his early days, his mentor Frans van Schooten provided technical feedback and was cautious for the sake of his reputation.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-43)

Between 1651 and 1657, Huygens published a number of works that showed his talent for mathematics and his mastery of [classical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_geometry "Euclidean geometry") and [analytical geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytic_geometry "Analytic geometry"), increasing his reach and reputation among mathematicians.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:10-33) Around the same time, Huygens began to question Descartes's laws of [collision](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collision "Collision"), which were largely wrong, deriving the correct laws algebraically and later by way of geometry.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:13-44) He showed that, for any system of bodies, the [centre of gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Center_of_mass "Center of mass") of the system remains the same in velocity and direction, which Huygens called the [conservation of "quantity of movement"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservation_law "Conservation law"). While others at the time were studying impact, Huygens's theory of collisions was more general.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:14-5) These results became the main reference point and the focus for further debates through correspondence and in a short article in _[Journal des Sçavans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Journal\_des\_s%C3%A7avans "Journal des sçavans")_ but would remain unknown to a larger audience until the publication of _De Motu Corporum ex Percussione_ (_Concerning the motion of colliding bodies_) in 1703.[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-45)[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:13-44)

In addition to his mathematical and mechanical works, Huygens made important scientific discoveries: he was the first to identify [Titan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Titan_(moon) "Titan (moon)") as one of [Saturn's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saturn "Saturn") moons in 1655, invented the pendulum clock in 1657, and explained Saturn's strange appearance as due to a [ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rings_of_Saturn "Rings of Saturn") in 1659; all these discoveries brought him fame across Europe.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-completedictionary-17) On 3 May 1661, Huygens, together with astronomer [Thomas Streete](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Streete "Thomas Streete") and Richard Reeve, observed the planet [Mercury](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mercury_(planet) "Mercury (planet)") transit over the Sun using Reeve's telescope in London.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-46) Streete then debated the published record of [Hevelius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hevelius "Hevelius"), a controversy mediated by [Henry Oldenburg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry_Oldenburg "Henry Oldenburg").[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-47) Huygens passed to Hevelius a manuscript of [Jeremiah Horrocks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jeremiah_Horrocks "Jeremiah Horrocks") on the [transit of Venus in 1639](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transit_of_Venus,_1639 "Transit of Venus, 1639"), printed for the first time in 1662.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-48)

In that same year, [Sir Robert Moray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sir_Robert_Moray "Sir Robert Moray") sent Huygens [John Graunt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Graunt "John Graunt")'s [life table](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life_table "Life table"), and shortly after Huygens and his brother Lodewijk dabbled on [life expectancy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life_expectancy "Life expectancy").[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:12-42)[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-49) Huygens eventually created the first graph of a continuous distribution function under the assumption of a uniform [death rate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mortality_rate "Mortality rate"), and used it to solve problems in [joint annuities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Annuity "Annuity").[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-50) Contemporaneously, Huygens, who played the [harpsichord](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harpsichord "Harpsichord"), took an interest in [Simon Stevin's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simon_Stevin "Simon Stevin") theories on music; however, he showed very little concern to publish his theories on [consonance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Consonance "Consonance"), some of which were lost for centuries.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-51)[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-52) For his contributions to science, the [Royal Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Society "Royal Society") of London elected Huygens a Fellow in 1663, making him its first foreign member when he was just 34 years old.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-53)[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-54)

[![Image 6](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Christiaan-huygens2.jpg/250px-Christiaan-huygens2.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Christiaan-huygens2.jpg)

Huygens, right of centre, from _\_L'établissement de l'Académie des Sciences et fondation de l'observatoire\_, 1666_ by [Henri Testelin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Testelin "Henri Testelin") (c. 1675)

The [Montmor Academy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Montmor_Academy "Montmor Academy"), started in the mid-1650s, was the form the old Mersenne circle took after his death.[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-55) Huygens took part in its debates and supported those favouring experimental demonstration as a check on amateurish attitudes.[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-56) He visited Paris a third time in 1663; when the Montmor Academy closed down the next year, Huygens advocated for a more [Baconian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baconian_method "Baconian method") program in science. Two years later, in 1666, he moved to Paris on an invitation to fill a leadership position at [King Louis XIV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis_XIV "Louis XIV")'s new French [Académie des sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Academy_of_Sciences "French Academy of Sciences").[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-57)

While at the Académie in Paris, Huygens had an important patron and correspondent in [Jean-Baptiste Colbert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_Colbert "Jean-Baptiste Colbert"), First Minister to Louis XIV.[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-58) His relationship with the French Académie was not always easy, and in 1670 Huygens, seriously ill, chose [Francis Vernon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Francis_Vernon "Francis Vernon") to carry out a donation of his papers to the Royal Society in London should he die.[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-59) However, the aftermath of the [Franco-Dutch War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Franco-Dutch_War "Franco-Dutch War") (1672–78), and particularly England's role in it, may have damaged his later relationship with the Royal Society.[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-60)[Robert Hooke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Hooke "Robert Hooke"), as a Royal Society representative, lacked the finesse to handle the situation in 1673.[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-61)

The physicist and inventor [Denis Papin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Denis_Papin "Denis Papin") was an assistant to Huygens from 1671.[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-62) One of their projects, which did not bear fruit directly, was the [gunpowder engine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gunpowder_engine "Gunpowder engine"), a precursor of the [internal combustion engine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine "Internal combustion engine") that used [gunpowder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gunpowder "Gunpowder") as its fuel.[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-63)[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-64) Huygens made further astronomical observations at the Académie using the [observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paris_Observatory "Paris Observatory") recently completed in 1672. He introduced [Nicolaas Hartsoeker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nicolaas_Hartsoeker "Nicolaas Hartsoeker") to French scientists such as [Nicolas Malebranche](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nicolas_Malebranche "Nicolas Malebranche") and [Giovanni Cassini](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giovanni_Cassini "Giovanni Cassini") in 1678.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:14-5)[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-65)

The young diplomat Leibniz met Huygens while visiting Paris in 1672 on a vain mission to meet the French Foreign Minister [Arnauld de Pomponne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arnauld_de_Pomponne "Arnauld de Pomponne"). Leibniz was working on a [calculating machine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stepped_reckoner "Stepped reckoner") at the time and, after a short visit to London in early 1673, he was tutored in mathematics by Huygens until 1676.[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-Leibniz1996-66) An extensive correspondence ensued over the years, in which Huygens showed at first reluctance to accept the advantages of Leibniz's [infinitesimal calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinitesimal_calculus "Infinitesimal calculus").[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-67)

[![Image 7](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Hofwijck_%283%29.JPG/250px-Hofwijck_%283%29.JPG)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Hofwijck_(3).JPG)

[Hofwijck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hofwijck "Hofwijck"), Huygens's summer home; now a museum

Huygens moved back to The Hague in 1681 after suffering another bout of serious depressive illness. In 1684, he published _Astroscopia Compendiaria_ on his new tubeless [aerial telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aerial_telescope "Aerial telescope"). He attempted to return to France in 1685 but the [revocation of the Edict of Nantes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Revocation_of_the_Edict_of_Nantes "Revocation of the Edict of Nantes") precluded this move. His father died in 1687, and he inherited Hofwijck, which he made his home the following year.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-Dictionary,_p._469-31)

On his third visit to England, Huygens met Newton in person on 12 June 1689. They spoke about [Iceland spar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iceland_spar "Iceland spar"), and subsequently corresponded about resisted motion.[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-68)

Huygens returned to mathematical topics in his last years and observed the acoustical phenomenon now known as [flanging](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flanging "Flanging") in 1693.[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-69) Two years later, on 8 July 1695, Huygens died in The Hague and was buried, like his father before him, in an unmarked grave at the [Grote Kerk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grote_Kerk,_The_Hague "Grote Kerk, The Hague").[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-70)

Huygens never married.[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-71)

Huygens first became internationally known for his work in mathematics, publishing a number of important results that drew the attention of many European geometers.[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:15-72) Huygens's preferred method in his published works was that of Archimedes, though he made use of Descartes's analytic geometry and Fermat's [infinitesimal techniques](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adequality "Adequality") more extensively in his private notebooks.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-completedictionary-17)[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:32-27)

#### _Theoremata de Quadratura_

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Christiaan_Huygens&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Theoremata de Quadratura")]

[![Image 8](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Fig._2._The_second_stage_of_the_research_on_the_topic_of_circle_quadrature.tif/lossy-page1-230px-Fig._2._The_second_stage_of_the_research_on_the_topic_of_circle_quadrature.tif.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Fig._2._The_second_stage_of_the_research_on_the_topic_of_circle_quadrature.tif)

Huygens's first publication was in the field of [quadrature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrature_(geometry) "Quadrature (geometry)").

Huygens's first publication was _Theoremata de Quadratura Hyperboles, Ellipsis et Circuli_ (_Theorems on the quadrature of the hyperbola, ellipse, and circle_), published by the [Elzeviers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/House_of_Elzevir "House of Elzevir") in [Leiden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leiden "Leiden") in 1651.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:12-42) The first part of the work contained theorems for computing the areas of hyperbolas, ellipses, and circles that paralleled Archimedes's work on conic sections, particularly his _[Quadrature of the Parabola](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrature\_of\_the\_Parabola "Quadrature of the Parabola")_.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:10-33) The second part included a refutation to Grégoire de Saint-Vincent's claims on circle quadrature, which he had discussed with Mersenne earlier.

Huygens demonstrated that the centre of gravity of a segment of any [hyperbola](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbola "Hyperbola"), [ellipse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse "Ellipse"), or [circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle "Circle") was directly related to the area of that segment. He was then able to show the relationships between triangles inscribed in conic sections and the centre of gravity for those sections. By generalizing these theorems to cover all conic sections, Huygens extended classical methods to generate new results.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-completedictionary-17)

Quadrature and rectification were live issues in the 1650s and, through Mylon, Huygens participated in the controversy surrounding [Thomas Hobbes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Hobbes "Thomas Hobbes"). Persisting in highlighting his mathematical contributions, he made an international reputation.[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-73)

#### _De Circuli Magnitudine Inventa_

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Christiaan_Huygens&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: De Circuli Magnitudine Inventa")]

Huygens's next publication was _De Circuli Magnitudine Inventa_ (_New findings on the magnitude of the circle_), published in 1654. In this work, Huygens was able to narrow the gap between the circumscribed and inscribed polygons found in Archimedes's [_Measurement of the Circle_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Measurement_of_a_Circle "Measurement of a Circle"), showing that the ratio of the circumference to its diameter or [pi (π)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi "Pi") must lie in the first third of that interval.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:12-42)

Using a technique equivalent to [Richardson extrapolation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richardson_extrapolation "Richardson extrapolation"),[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-74) Huygens was able to shorten the inequalities used in Archimedes's method; in this case, by using the centre of the gravity of a segment of a parabola, he was able to approximate the centre of gravity of a segment of a circle, resulting in a faster and accurate approximation of the circle quadrature.[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-75) From these theorems, Huygens obtained two set of values for π: the first between 3.1415926 and 3.1415927, and the second between 3.1415926533 and 3.1415926538.[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-76)

Huygens also showed that, in the case of the [hyperbola](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbola "Hyperbola"), the same approximation with parabolic segments produces a quick and simple method to calculate [logarithms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logarithm "Logarithm").[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-77) He appended a collection of solutions to classical problems at the end of the work under the title _Illustrium Quorundam Problematum Constructiones_ (_Construction of some illustrious problems_).[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:12-42)

#### _De Ratiociniis in Ludo Aleae_

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Christiaan_Huygens&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: De Ratiociniis in Ludo Aleae")]

Huygens became interested in [games of chance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Game_of_chance "Game of chance") after he visited Paris in 1655 and encountered the work of Fermat, [Blaise Pascal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blaise_Pascal "Blaise Pascal") and [Girard Desargues](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Girard_Desargues "Girard Desargues") years earlier.[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-78) He eventually published what was, at the time, the most coherent presentation of a mathematical approach to games of chance in _De Ratiociniis in Ludo Aleae_ (_On reasoning in games of chance_).[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:4-79)[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-80) Frans van Schooten translated the original Dutch manuscript into Latin and published it in his _Exercitationum Mathematicarum_ (1657).[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-81)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:25-12)

The work contains early [game-theoretic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Game_theory "Game theory") ideas and deals in particular with the [problem of points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Problem_of_points "Problem of points").[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:5-14)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:25-12) Huygens took from Pascal the concepts of a "fair game" and equitable contract (i.e., equal division when the chances are equal), and extended the argument to set up a non-standard theory of expected values.[[82]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-82) His success in applying algebra to the realm of chance, which hitherto seemed inaccessible to mathematicians, demonstrated the power of combining Euclidean synthetic proofs with the symbolic reasoning found in the works of Viète and Descartes.[[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-83)

Huygens included five challenging problems at the end of the book that became the standard test for anyone wishing to display their mathematical skill in games of chance for the next sixty years.[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-84) People who worked on these problems included [Abraham de Moivre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abraham_de_Moivre "Abraham de Moivre"), Jacob Bernoulli, [Johannes Hudde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johannes_Hudde "Johannes Hudde"), [Baruch Spinoza](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baruch_Spinoza "Baruch Spinoza"), and Leibniz.

[![Image 9](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Huygens_floating_bodies.jpg/330px-Huygens_floating_bodies.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Huygens_floating_bodies.jpg)

Huygens's results for the stability of a floating rectangular [parallelepiped](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelepiped "Parallelepiped")

Huygens had earlier completed a manuscript in the manner of Archimedes's _[On Floating Bodies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/On\_Floating\_Bodies "On Floating Bodies")_ entitled _De Iis quae Liquido Supernatant_ (_About parts floating above liquids_). It was written around 1650 and was made up of three books. Although he sent the completed work to Frans van Schooten for feedback, in the end Huygens chose not to publish it, and at one point suggested it be burned.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:10-33)[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:11-85) Some of the results found here were not rediscovered until the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:28-8)

Huygens first re-derives Archimedes's solutions for the stability of the sphere and the paraboloid by a clever application of Torricelli's principle (i.e., that bodies in a system move only if their centre of gravity descends).[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-86) He then proves the general theorem that, for a floating body in equilibrium, the distance between its centre of gravity and its submerged portion is at a minimum.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:28-8) Huygens uses this theorem to arrive at original solutions for the stability of floating [cones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cone "Cone"), [parallelepipeds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelepiped "Parallelepiped"), and [cylinders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylinder "Cylinder"), in some cases through a full cycle of rotation.[[87]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-87) His approach was thus equivalent to the principle of [virtual work](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virtual_work "Virtual work"). Huygens was also the first to recognize that, for these homogeneous solids, their specific weight and their [aspect ratio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aspect_ratio "Aspect ratio") are the essentials parameters of [hydrostatic stability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrostatics "Hydrostatics").[[88]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-88)[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-89)

Huygens was the leading European natural philosopher between Descartes and Newton.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-completedictionary-17)[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-90) However, unlike many of his contemporaries, Huygens had no taste for grand theoretical or philosophical systems and generally avoided dealing with metaphysical issues (if pressed, he adhered to the [Cartesian philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesianism "Cartesianism") of his time).[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:6-7)[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:10-33) Instead, Huygens excelled in extending the work of his predecessors, such as Galileo, to derive solutions to unsolved physical problems that were amenable to mathematical analysis. In particular, he sought explanations that relied on contact between bodies and avoided [action at a distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_at_a_distance "Action at a distance").[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-completedictionary-17)[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-91)

In common with [Robert Boyle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Boyle "Robert Boyle") and [Jacques Rohault](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacques_Rohault "Jacques Rohault"), Huygens advocated an experimentally oriented, mechanical natural philosophy during his Paris years.[[92]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-92) Already in his first visit to England in 1661, Huygens had learnt about Boyle's [air pump](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Air_pump "Air pump") experiments during a meeting at [Gresham College](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gresham_College_and_the_formation_of_the_Royal_Society "Gresham College and the formation of the Royal Society"). Shortly afterwards, he reevaluated Boyle's experimental design and developed a series of experiments meant to test a new hypothesis.[[93]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:33-93) It proved a yearslong process that brought to the surface a number of experimental and theoretical issues, and which ended around the time he became a Fellow of the Royal Society.[[94]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-94) Despite the [replication of results](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reproducibility "Reproducibility") of Boyle's experiments trailing off messily, Huygens came to accept Boyle's view of the void against the Cartesian denial of it.[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-95)

Newton's influence on [John Locke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Locke "John Locke") was mediated by Huygens, who assured Locke that Newton's mathematics was sound, leading to Locke's acceptance of a corpuscular-mechanical physics.[[96]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-Redman1997-96)

### Laws of motion, impact, and gravitation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Christiaan_Huygens&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Laws of motion, impact, and gravitation")]

[![Image 10](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Collision_huygens.gif/250px-Collision_huygens.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Collision_huygens.gif)

A boating metaphor as a way to think about [relative motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relative_motion "Relative motion"), simplifying the theory of colliding bodies, from Huygens's _Oeuvres Complètes_

The general approach of the mechanical philosophers was to postulate theories of the kind now called "contact action." Huygens adopted this method but not without seeing its limitations,[[97]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-97) while Leibniz, his student in Paris, later abandoned it.[[98]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-98) Understanding the universe this way made the theory of collisions central to physics, as only explanations that involved matter in motion could be truly intelligible. While Huygens was influenced by the Cartesian approach, he was less doctrinaire.[[99]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-99) He studied [elastic collisions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elastic_collision "Elastic collision") in the 1650s but delayed publication for over a decade.[[100]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-Dict470-100)

Huygens concluded quite early that [Descartes's laws](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_laws_of_motion "Cartesian laws of motion") for elastic collisions were largely wrong, and he formulated the correct laws, including the conservation of the product of mass times the square of the speed for hard bodies, and the conservation of quantity of motion in one direction for all bodies.[[101]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-101) An important step was his recognition of the [Galilean invariance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galilean_invariance "Galilean invariance") of the problems.[[102]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-102) Huygens had worked out the laws of collision from 1652 to 1656 in a manuscript entitled _De Motu Corporum ex Percussione_, though his results took many years to be circulated. In 1661, he passed them on in person to [William Brouncker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Brouncker,_2nd_Viscount_Brouncker "William Brouncker, 2nd Viscount Brouncker") and [Christopher Wren](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christopher_Wren "Christopher Wren") in London.[[103]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-103) What Spinoza wrote to [Henry Oldenburg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry_Oldenburg "Henry Oldenburg") about them in 1666, during the [Second Anglo-Dutch War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_Anglo-Dutch_War "Second Anglo-Dutch War"), was guarded.[[104]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-Israel2001-104) The war ended in 1667, and Huygens announced his results to the Royal Society in 1668. He later published them in the _Journal des Sçavans_ in 1669.[[100]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-Dict470-100)

In 1659 Huygens found the constant of [gravitational acceleration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_acceleration "Gravitational acceleration") and stated what is now known as the second of [Newton's laws of motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_laws_of_motion "Newton's laws of motion") in quadratic form.[[105]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-105) He derived geometrically the now standard formula for the [centrifugal force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centrifugal_force "Centrifugal force"), exerted on an object when viewed in a rotating [frame of reference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frame_of_reference "Frame of reference"), for instance when driving around a curve. In modern notation:

![Image 11: {\displaystyle F_{c}={m\ \omega ^{2}}{r}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/261f606d386303686960f9dd98a047cb083caef6)
with _m_ the [mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass_(physics) "Mass (physics)") of the object, _ω_ the [angular velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_velocity "Angular velocity"), and _r_ the [radius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radius "Radius").[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:28-8) Huygens collected his results in a treatise under the title _De vi Centrifuga_, unpublished until 1703, where the kinematics of free fall were used to produce the first generalized conception of [force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Force "Force") prior to Newton.[[106]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-106)

The general idea for the centrifugal force, however, was published in 1673 and was a significant step in studying orbits in astronomy. It enabled the transition from [Kepler's third law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler%27s_third_law "Kepler's third law") of planetary motion to the [inverse square law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverse_square_law "Inverse square law") of gravitation.[[107]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-107) Yet, the interpretation of Newton's work on gravitation by Huygens differed from that of Newtonians such as [Roger Cotes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roger_Cotes "Roger Cotes"): he did not insist on the _a priori_ attitude of Descartes, but neither would he accept aspects of gravitational attractions that were not attributable in principle to contact between particles.[[108]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-108)

The approach used by Huygens also missed some central notions of mathematical physics, which were not lost on others. In his work on pendulums Huygens came very close to the theory of [simple harmonic motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simple_harmonic_motion "Simple harmonic motion"); the topic, however, was covered fully for the first time by Newton in Book II of the _[Principia Mathematica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophi%C3%A6\_Naturalis\_Principia\_Mathematica "Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica")_ (1687).[[109]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-Allen1999-109) In 1678 Leibniz picked out of Huygens's work on collisions the idea of [conservation law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservation_law_(physics) "Conservation law (physics)") that Huygens had left implicit.[[110]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-110)

[![Image 12](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Christiaan_Huygens_Clock_and_Horologii_Oscillatorii.jpg/330px-Christiaan_Huygens_Clock_and_Horologii_Oscillatorii.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Christiaan_Huygens_Clock_and_Horologii_Oscillatorii.jpg)

Spring-driven pendulum clock, designed by Huygens and built by [Salomon Coster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salomon_Coster "Salomon Coster") (1657),[[111]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-111) with a copy of the _Horologium Oscillatorium_ (1673),[[112]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-112) at [Museum Boerhaave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Museum_Boerhaave "Museum Boerhaave"), Leiden

In 1657, inspired by earlier research into pendulums as regulating mechanisms, Huygens invented the pendulum clock, which was a breakthrough in timekeeping and became the most accurate timekeeper for almost 300 years until the 1930s.[[113]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-113) The pendulum clock was much more accurate than the existing [verge and foliot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Verge_and_foliot "Verge and foliot") clocks and was immediately popular, quickly spreading over Europe. Clocks prior to this would lose about 15 minutes per day, whereas Huygens's clock would lose about 15 seconds per day.[[114]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-114) Although Huygens patented and contracted the construction of his clock designs to [Salomon Coster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salomon_Coster "Salomon Coster") in The Hague,[[115]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-115) he did not make much money from his invention. [Pierre Séguier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre_S%C3%A9guier "Pierre Séguier") refused him any French rights, while Simon Douw in [Rotterdam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotterdam "Rotterdam") and [Ahasuerus Fromanteel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ahasuerus_Fromanteel "Ahasuerus Fromanteel") in London copied his design in 1658.[[116]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-116) The oldest known Huygens-style pendulum clock is dated 1657 and can be seen at the [Museum Boerhaave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Museum_Boerhaave "Museum Boerhaave") in [Leiden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leiden "Leiden").[[117]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:17-117)[[118]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-118)[[119]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-119)[[120]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-120)

Part of the incentive for inventing the pendulum clock was to create an accurate [marine chronometer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marine_chronometer "Marine chronometer") that could be used to find [longitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Longitude "Longitude") by [celestial navigation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celestial_navigation "Celestial navigation") during sea voyages. However, the clock proved unsuccessful as a marine timekeeper because the rocking motion of the ship disturbed the motion of the pendulum. In 1660, Lodewijk Huygens made a trial on a voyage to Spain, and reported that heavy weather made the clock useless. [Alexander Bruce](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_Bruce,_2nd_Earl_of_Kincardine "Alexander Bruce, 2nd Earl of Kincardine") entered the field in 1662, and Huygens called in Sir Robert Moray and the Royal Society to mediate and preserve some of his rights.[[121]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-121)[[117]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:17-117) Trials continued into the 1660s, the best news coming from a Royal Navy captain [Robert Holmes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Holmes_(Royal_Navy_officer) "Robert Holmes (Royal Navy officer)") operating against the Dutch possessions in 1664.[[122]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-122)[Lisa Jardine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lisa_Jardine "Lisa Jardine") doubts that Holmes reported the results of the trial accurately, as [Samuel Pepys](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samuel_Pepys "Samuel Pepys") expressed his doubts at the time.[[123]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-123)

A trial for the French Academy on an expedition to [Cayenne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cayenne "Cayenne") ended badly. [Jean Richer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_Richer "Jean Richer") suggested correction for the [figure of the Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Figure_of_the_Earth "Figure of the Earth"). By the time of the [Dutch East India Company](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dutch_East_India_Company "Dutch East India Company") expedition of 1686 to the [Cape of Good Hope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cape_of_Good_Hope "Cape of Good Hope"), Huygens was able to supply the correction retrospectively.[[124]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-124)

#### _Horologium Oscillatorium_

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Christiaan_Huygens&action=edit&section=20 "Edit section: Horologium Oscillatorium")]

[![Image 13](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Evolute-parab-1-e.svg/250px-Evolute-parab-1-e.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Evolute-parab-1-e.svg)

Diagram showing the evolute of a curve

Sixteen years after the invention of the pendulum clock, in 1673, Huygens published his major work on horology entitled _[Horologium Oscillatorium: Sive de Motu Pendulorum ad Horologia Aptato Demonstrationes Geometricae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horologium\_Oscillatorium "Horologium Oscillatorium") (The Pendulum Clock: or Geometrical demonstrations concerning the motion of pendula as applied to clocks_). It is the first modern work on mechanics where a physical problem is idealized by a set of parameters then analysed mathematically.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:7-6)

Huygens's motivation came from the observation, made by Mersenne and others, that pendulums are not quite [isochronous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isochronous "Isochronous"): their period depends on their width of swing, with wide swings taking slightly longer than narrow swings.[[125]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:29-125) He tackled this problem by finding the curve down which a mass will slide under the influence of gravity in the same amount of time, regardless of its starting point; the so-called [tautochrone problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tautochrone_curve "Tautochrone curve"). By geometrical methods which anticipated the [calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus "Calculus"), Huygens showed it to be a [cycloid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cycloid "Cycloid"), rather than the circular arc of a pendulum's bob, and therefore that pendulums needed to move on a cycloid path in order to be isochronous. The mathematics necessary to solve this problem led Huygens to develop his theory of evolutes, which he presented in Part III of his _Horologium Oscillatorium_.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:7-6)[[126]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:21-126)

He also solved a problem posed by Mersenne earlier: how to calculate the period of a pendulum made of an arbitrarily-shaped swinging rigid body. This involved discovering the [centre of oscillation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Center_of_oscillation "Center of oscillation") and its reciprocal relationship with the pivot point. In the same work, he analysed the [conical pendulum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conical_pendulum "Conical pendulum"), consisting of a weight on a cord moving in a circle, using the concept of centrifugal force.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:7-6)[[127]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-127)

Huygens was the first to derive the formula for the [period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frequency "Frequency") of an ideal mathematical pendulum (with mass-less rod or cord and length much longer than its swing), in modern notation:

![Image 14: {\displaystyle T=2\pi {\sqrt {\frac {l}{g}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5413ebd719825801e48ca3b82cec455ad2cb9a6c)
with _T_ the period, _l_ the length of the pendulum and _g_ the [gravitational acceleration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_acceleration "Gravitational acceleration"). By his study of the oscillation period of compound pendulums Huygens made pivotal contributions to the development of the concept of [moment of inertia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_of_inertia "Moment of inertia").[[128]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-mach-128)

Huygens also observed [coupled oscillations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coupled_oscillation "Coupled oscillation"): two of his pendulum clocks mounted next to each other on the same support often became synchronized, swinging in opposite directions. He reported the results by letter to the Royal Society, and it is referred to as "[an odd kind of sympathy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Odd_sympathy "Odd sympathy")" in the Society's minutes.[[129]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-129) This concept is now known as [entrainment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entrainment_(physics) "Entrainment (physics)").[[130]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-130)

#### Balance spring watch

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Christiaan_Huygens&action=edit&section=21 "Edit section: Balance spring watch")]

[![Image 15](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Drawing_of_one_of_Huygens_first_balance_springs%2C_attached_to_a_balance_wheel.jpg/250px-Drawing_of_one_of_Huygens_first_balance_springs%2C_attached_to_a_balance_wheel.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Drawing_of_one_of_Huygens_first_balance_springs,_attached_to_a_balance_wheel.jpg)

Drawing of a balance spring invented by Huygens

In 1675, while investigating the oscillating properties of the cycloid, Huygens was able to transform a cycloidal pendulum into a vibrating spring through a combination of geometry and higher mathematics.[[131]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-131) In the same year, Huygens designed a spiral [balance spring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Balance_spring "Balance spring") and patented a [pocket watch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pocket_watch "Pocket watch"). These watches are notable for lacking a [fusee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fusee_(horology) "Fusee (horology)") for equalizing the mainspring torque. The implication is that Huygens thought his spiral spring would isochronize the balance in the same way that cycloid-shaped suspension curbs on his clocks would isochronize the pendulum.[[132]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:20-132)

He later used spiral springs in more conventional watches, made for him by [Thuret](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thuret_family "Thuret family") in Paris. Such springs are essential in modern watches with a detached [lever escapement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lever_escapement "Lever escapement") because they can be adjusted for [isochronism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isochronism "Isochronism"). Watches in Huygens's time, however, employed the very ineffective [verge escapement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Verge_escapement "Verge escapement"), which interfered with the isochronal properties of any form of balance spring, spiral or otherwise.[[133]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-133)

Huygens's design came around the same time as, though independently of, Robert Hooke's. Controversy over the priority of the balance spring persisted for centuries. In February 2006, a long-lost copy of Hooke's handwritten notes from several decades of Royal Society meetings was discovered in a cupboard in [Hampshire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hampshire "Hampshire"), England, presumably tipping the evidence in Hooke's favour.[[134]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-134)[[135]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-135)

[![Image 16](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Huygens_Aerial_telescope%2C_1684.jpg/250px-Huygens_Aerial_telescope%2C_1684.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Huygens_Aerial_telescope,_1684.jpg)

Huygens's [aerial telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aerial_telescope "Aerial telescope") from _Astroscopia Compendiaria_ (1684)

Huygens had a long-term interest in the study of [light refraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Refraction "Refraction") and lenses or [dioptrics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dioptrics "Dioptrics").[[136]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:27-136) From 1652 date the first drafts of a Latin treatise on the theory of dioptrics, known as the _Tractatus_, which contained a comprehensive and rigorous theory of the telescope. Huygens was one of the few to raise theoretical questions regarding the properties and working of the telescope, and almost the only one to direct his mathematical proficiency towards the actual instruments used in astronomy.[[137]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-137)

Huygens repeatedly announced its publication to his colleagues but ultimately postponed it in favor of a much more comprehensive treatment, now under the name of the _Dioptrica_.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:0-23) It consisted of three parts. The first part focused on the general principles of refraction, the second dealt with [spherical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_aberration "Spherical aberration") and [chromatic aberration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chromatic_aberration "Chromatic aberration"), while the third covered all aspects of the construction of telescopes and microscopes. In contrast to Descartes' dioptrics which treated only ideal (elliptical and hyperbolical) lenses, Huygens dealt exclusively with spherical lenses, which were the only kind that could really be made and incorporated in devices such as microscopes and telescopes.[[138]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:26-138)

Huygens also worked out practical ways to minimize the effects of spherical and chromatic aberration, such as long focal distances for the objective of a telescope, internal stops to reduce the aperture, and a new kind of ocular known as the [Huygenian eyepiece](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Huygenian_eyepiece "Huygenian eyepiece").[[138]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:26-138) The _Dioptrica_ was never published in Huygens’s lifetime and only appeared in press in 1703, when most of its contents were already familiar to the scientific world.

Together with his brother Constantijn, Huygens began grinding his own lenses in 1655 in an effort to improve telescopes.[[139]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-139) He designed in 1662 what is now called the Huygenian eyepiece, a set of two planoconvex lenses used as a telescope ocular.[[140]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-140)[[141]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-Dictionary-141) Huygens's lenses were known to be of superb quality and polished consistently according to his specifications; however, his telescopes did not produce very sharp images, leading some to speculate that he might have suffered from [near-sightedness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Near-sightedness "Near-sightedness").[[142]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:31-142)

Lenses were also a common interest through which Huygens could meet socially in the 1660s with [Spinoza](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baruch_Spinoza "Baruch Spinoza"), who ground them professionally. They had rather different outlooks on science, Spinoza being the more committed Cartesian, and some of their discussion survives in correspondence.[[143]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-143) He encountered the work of [Antoni van Leeuwenhoek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antoni_van_Leeuwenhoek "Antoni van Leeuwenhoek"), another lens grinder, in the field of [microscopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microscopy "Microscopy") which interested his father.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:7-6) Huygens also investigated the use of lenses in projectors. He is credited as the inventor of the [magic lantern](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magic_lantern "Magic lantern"), described in correspondence of 1659.[[144]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-144) There are others to whom such a lantern device has been attributed, such as [Giambattista della Porta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giambattista_della_Porta "Giambattista della Porta") and [Cornelis Drebbel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cornelis_Drebbel "Cornelis Drebbel"), though Huygens's design used lens for better projection ([Athanasius Kircher](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Athanasius_Kircher "Athanasius Kircher") has also been credited for that).[[145]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-145)

#### _Traité de la Lumière_

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Christiaan_Huygens&action=edit&section=25 "Edit section: Traité de la Lumière")]

[![Image 17](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Huyghens_-_Trait%C3%A9_de_la_lumi%C3%A8re_-_Fig._12-13.svg/250px-Huyghens_-_Trait%C3%A9_de_la_lumi%C3%A8re_-_Fig._12-13.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Huyghens_-_Trait%C3%A9_de_la_lumi%C3%A8re_-_Fig._12-13.svg)

Refraction of a plane wave, explained using Huygens's principle in _[Traité de la Lumière](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treatise\_on\_Light "Treatise on Light")_ (1690)

Huygens is especially remembered in optics for his [wave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wave "Wave") theory of light, which he first communicated in 1678 to the Académie des sciences in Paris. Originally a preliminary chapter of his _Dioptrica_, Huygens's theory was published in 1690 under the title _[Traité de la Lumière](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treatise\_on\_Light "Treatise on Light")_[[146]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-146) (_Treatise on light_), and contains the first fully mathematized, mechanistic explanation of an unobservable physical phenomenon (i.e., light propagation).[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:6-7)[[147]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-HuyThomp1912-147) Huygens refers to [Ignace-Gaston Pardies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ignace-Gaston_Pardies "Ignace-Gaston Pardies"), whose manuscript on optics helped him on his wave theory.[[148]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-148)

The challenge at the time was to explain [geometrical optics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometrical_optics "Geometrical optics"), as most [physical optics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_optics "Physical optics") phenomena (such as [diffraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffraction "Diffraction")) had not been observed or appreciated as issues. Huygens had experimented in 1672 with double refraction ([birefringence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Birefringence "Birefringence")) in the Iceland spar (a [calcite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calcite "Calcite")), a phenomenon discovered in 1669 by [Rasmus Bartholin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rasmus_Bartholin "Rasmus Bartholin"). At first, he could not elucidate what he found but was later able to explain it using his wavefront theory and concept of evolutes.[[147]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-HuyThomp1912-147) He also developed ideas on [caustics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caustic_(optics) "Caustic (optics)").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:7-6) Huygens assumes that the [speed of light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light "Speed of light") is finite, based on a report by [Ole Christensen Rømer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ole_R%C3%B8mer "Ole Rømer") in 1677 but which Huygens is presumed to have already believed.[[149]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-Smith1987-149) Huygens's theory posits light as radiating [wavefronts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wavefront "Wavefront"), with the common notion of light rays depicting propagation normal to those wavefronts. Propagation of the wavefronts is then explained as the result of [spherical waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_wave "Spherical wave") being emitted at every point along the wave front (known today as the [Huygens–Fresnel principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Huygens%E2%80%93Fresnel_principle "Huygens–Fresnel principle")).[[150]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-150) It assumed an omnipresent [ether](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luminiferous_aether "Luminiferous aether"), with transmission through perfectly elastic particles, a revision of the view of Descartes. The nature of light was therefore a [longitudinal wave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Longitudinal_wave "Longitudinal wave").[[149]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-Smith1987-149)

His theory of light was not widely accepted, while Newton's rival [corpuscular theory of light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corpuscular_theory_of_light "Corpuscular theory of light"), as found in his _[Opticks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Opticks "Opticks")_ (1704), gained more support. One strong objection to Huygens's theory was that longitudinal waves have only a single [polarization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polarization_(waves) "Polarization (waves)") which cannot explain the observed birefringence. However, [Thomas Young](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Young_(scientist) "Thomas Young (scientist)")'s [interference experiments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Young%27s_interference_experiment "Young's interference experiment") in 1801, and [François Arago](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Arago "François Arago")'s detection of the [Poisson spot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poisson_spot "Poisson spot") in 1819, could not be explained through Newton's or any other particle theory, reviving Huygens's ideas and wave models. [Fresnel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fresnel "Fresnel") became aware of Huygens's work and in 1821 was able to explain birefringence as a result of light being not a longitudinal (as had been assumed) but actually a [transverse wave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transverse_wave "Transverse wave").[[151]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-151) The thus-named Huygens–Fresnel principle was the basis for the advancement of physical optics, explaining all aspects of light propagation until [Maxwell's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Clerk_Maxwell "James Clerk Maxwell")[electromagnetic theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_electromagnetic_theory "History of electromagnetic theory") culminated in the development of [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics") and the discovery of the [photon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon "Photon").[[138]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:26-138)[[152]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-152)

[![Image 18](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Huygens_Systema_Saturnium.jpg/250px-Huygens_Systema_Saturnium.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Huygens_Systema_Saturnium.jpg)

Huygens's explanation for the aspects of Saturn, _Systema Saturnium_ (1659)

In 1655, Huygens discovered the first of Saturn's moons, [Titan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Titan_(moon) "Titan (moon)"), and observed and sketched the [Orion Nebula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_Nebula "Orion Nebula") using a [refracting telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Refracting_telescope "Refracting telescope") with a 43x magnification of his own design.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:2-11)[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:3-10) Huygens succeeded in subdividing the nebula into different stars (the brighter interior now bears the name of the _Huygenian region_ in his honour), and discovered several [interstellar nebulae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nebula "Nebula") and some [double stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double_star "Double star").[[153]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-153) He was also the first to propose that the [appearance of Saturn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rings_of_Saturn "Rings of Saturn"), which had baffled astronomers, was due to "a thin, flat ring, nowhere touching, and inclined to the ecliptic”.[[154]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-154)

More than three years later, in 1659, Huygens published his theory and findings in _Systema Saturnium_. It is considered the most important work on telescopic astronomy since Galileo's _[Sidereus Nuncius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sidereus\_Nuncius "Sidereus Nuncius")_ fifty years earlier.[[155]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:19-155) Much more than a report on Saturn, Huygens provided measurements for the relative distances of the planets from the Sun, introduced the concept of the [micrometer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Filar_micrometer "Filar micrometer"), and showed a method to measure angular diameters of planets, which finally allowed the telescope to be used as an instrument to measure (rather than just sighting) astronomical objects.[[156]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-156) He was also the first to question the authority of Galileo in telescopic matters, a sentiment that was to be common in the years following its publication.

In the same year, Huygens was able to observe [Syrtis Major](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syrtis_Major_Planum "Syrtis Major Planum"), a volcanic plain on [Mars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mars "Mars"). He used repeated observations of the movement of this feature over the course of a number of days to estimate the length of day on Mars, which he did quite accurately to 24 1/2 hours. This figure is only a few minutes off of the actual length of the Martian day of 24 hours, 37 minutes.[[157]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-157)

At the instigation of Jean-Baptiste Colbert, Huygens undertook the task of constructing a mechanical planetarium that could display all the planets and their moons then known circling around the Sun. Huygens completed his design in 1680 and had his clockmaker Johannes van Ceulen built it the following year. However, Colbert died in the interim and Huygens never got to deliver his planetarium to the [French Academy of Sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Academy_of_Sciences "French Academy of Sciences") as the new minister, [François-Michel le Tellier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois-Michel_le_Tellier,_Marquis_de_Louvois "François-Michel le Tellier, Marquis de Louvois"), decided not to renew Huygens's contract.[[158]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:1-158)[[159]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-159)

In his design, Huygens made an ingenious use of [continued fractions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continued_fraction "Continued fraction") to find the best rational approximations by which he could choose the gears with the correct number of teeth. The ratio between two gears determined the orbital periods of two planets. To move the planets around the Sun, Huygens used a clock-mechanism that could go forwards and backwards in time. Huygens claimed his planetarium was more accurate that a similar device constructed by [Ole Rømer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ole_R%C3%B8mer "Ole Rømer") around the same time, but his planetarium design was not published until after his death in the _Opuscula Posthuma_ (1703).[[158]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:1-158)

[![Image 19](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Christiaan_Huygens_Cosmotheoros_-_Relative_sizes_of_sun_and_planets%2C_1698.jpg/250px-Christiaan_Huygens_Cosmotheoros_-_Relative_sizes_of_sun_and_planets%2C_1698.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Christiaan_Huygens_Cosmotheoros_-_Relative_sizes_of_sun_and_planets,_1698.jpg)

Relative sizes of the Sun and planets in _Cosmotheoros_ (1698)

Shortly before his death in 1695, Huygens completed his most speculative work entitled _Cosmotheoros_. At his direction, it was to be published only posthumously by his brother, which Constantijn Jr. did in 1698.[[160]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-160) In this work, Huygens speculated on the existence of [extraterrestrial life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extraterrestrial_life "Extraterrestrial life"), which he imagined similar to that on Earth. Such speculations were not uncommon at the time, justified by [Copernicanism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Copernicanism "Copernicanism") or the [plenitude principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plenitude_principle "Plenitude principle"), but Huygens went into greater detail, though without acknowledging Newton's laws of gravitation or the fact that planetary atmospheres are composed of different gases.[[161]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-161)[[162]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-162)_Cosmotheoros,_ translated into English as _The celestial worlds discover’d_, is fundamentally a [utopian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Utopia "Utopia") work that owes some inspiration to the work of [Peter Heylin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Heylin "Peter Heylin"), and it was likely seen by contemporary readers as a piece of fiction in the tradition of [Francis Godwin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Francis_Godwin "Francis Godwin"), [John Wilkins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Wilkins "John Wilkins"), and [Cyrano de Bergerac](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyrano_de_Bergerac "Cyrano de Bergerac").[[163]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-163)[[164]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-164)[[165]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-165)

Huygens wrote that availability of water in liquid form was essential for life and that the properties of water must vary from planet to planet to suit the temperature range. He took his observations of dark and bright spots on the surfaces of Mars and Jupiter to be evidence of water and ice on those planets.[[166]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-166) He argued that extraterrestrial life is neither confirmed nor denied by the Bible, and questioned why God would create the other planets if they were not to serve a greater purpose than that of being admired from Earth. Huygens postulated that the great distance between the planets signified that God had not intended for beings on one to know about the beings on the others, and had not foreseen how much humans would advance in scientific knowledge.[[167]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:22-167)

It was also in this book that Huygens published his estimates for the relative sizes of the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System") and his method for calculating [stellar distances](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_distance "Stellar distance").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:14-5) He made a series of smaller holes in a screen facing the Sun, until he estimated the light was of the same intensity as that of the star [Sirius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sirius "Sirius"). He then calculated that the angle of this hole was 1/27,664th the diameter of the Sun, and thus it was about 30,000 times as far away, on the (incorrect) assumption that Sirius is as luminous as the Sun. The subject of [photometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photometry_(astronomy) "Photometry (astronomy)") remained in its infancy until the time of [Pierre Bouguer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre_Bouguer "Pierre Bouguer") and [Johann Heinrich Lambert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johann_Heinrich_Lambert "Johann Heinrich Lambert").[[168]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-Mccormmach2012-168)

Huygens has been called the first [theoretical physicist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theoretical_physics "Theoretical physics") and a founder of modern [mathematical physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_physics "Mathematical physics").[[169]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:30-169)[[170]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-170) Although his influence was considerable during his lifetime, it began to fade shortly after his death. His skills as a geometer and mechanical ingenuity elicited the admiration of many of his contemporaries, including Newton, Leibniz, [l'Hôpital](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guillaume_de_l%27H%C3%B4pital "Guillaume de l'Hôpital"), and the [Bernoullis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bernoulli_family "Bernoulli family").[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:12-42) For his work in physics, Huygens has been deemed one of the greatest scientists in the Scientific Revolution, rivaled only by Newton in both depth of insight and the number of results obtained.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:24-4)[[171]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-171) Huygens also helped develop the institutional frameworks for scientific research on the [European continent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continental_Europe "Continental Europe"), making him a leading actor in the establishment of modern science.[[172]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-172)

### Mathematics and physics

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Christiaan_Huygens&action=edit&section=31 "Edit section: Mathematics and physics")]

[![Image 20](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Christiaan-huygens4.jpg/250px-Christiaan-huygens4.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Christiaan-huygens4.jpg)

Portrait of Christiaan Huygens by [Bernard Vaillant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bernard_Vaillant "Bernard Vaillant") (1686)

In mathematics, Huygens mastered the methods of ancient [Greek geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_mathematics "Greek mathematics"), particularly the work of Archimedes, and was an adept user of the analytic geometry and infinitesimal techniques of Descartes and Fermat.[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:11-85) His mathematical style can be best described as geometrical infinitesimal analysis of curves and of motion. Drawing inspiration and imagery from mechanics, it remained pure mathematics in form.[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:15-72) Huygens brought this type of geometrical analysis to a close, as more mathematicians turned away from classical geometry to the [calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus "Calculus") for handling infinitesimals, limit processes, and motion.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:9-38)

Huygens was moreover able to fully employ mathematics to answer questions of physics. Often this entailed introducing a simple [model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_model "Mathematical model") for describing a complicated situation, then analyzing it starting from simple arguments to their logical consequences, developing the necessary mathematics along the way. As he wrote at the end of a draft of _De vi Centrifuga_:[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:10-33)

> Whatever you will have supposed not impossible either concerning gravity or motion or any other matter, if then you prove something concerning the magnitude of a line, surface, or body, it will be true; as for instance, Archimedes on the [quadrature of the parabola](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrature_of_the_parabola "Quadrature of the parabola"), where the tendency of heavy objects has been assumed to act through parallel lines.

Huygens favoured [axiomatic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiomatic_system "Axiomatic system") presentations of his results, which require rigorous methods of geometric demonstration: although he allowed levels of uncertainty in the selection of primary axioms and hypotheses, the proofs of theorems derived from these could never be in doubt.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:10-33) Huygens's style of publication exerted an influence in Newton's presentation of his own [major](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophi%C3%A6_Naturalis_Principia_Mathematica "Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica")[works](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Opticks "Opticks").[[173]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-173)[[174]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-174)

Besides the application of mathematics to physics and physics to mathematics, Huygens relied on mathematics as methodology, specifically its ability to generate new knowledge about the world.[[175]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:18-175) Unlike Galileo, who used mathematics primarily as rhetoric or synthesis, Huygens consistently employed mathematics as a way to discover and develop theories covering various phenomena and insisted that the reduction of the physical to the geometrical satisfy exacting standards of fit between the real and the ideal.[[125]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:29-125) In demanding such mathematical tractability and precision, Huygens set an example for eighteenth-century scientists such as [Johann Bernoulli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johann_Bernoulli "Johann Bernoulli"), [Jean le Rond d'Alembert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_le_Rond_d%27Alembert "Jean le Rond d'Alembert"), and [Charles-Augustin de Coulomb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles-Augustin_de_Coulomb "Charles-Augustin de Coulomb").[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:10-33)[[169]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:30-169)

Although never intended for publication, Huygens made use of algebraic expressions to represent physical entities in a handful of his manuscripts on collisions.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:13-44) This would make him one of the first to employ mathematical formulae to describe relationships in physics, as it is done today.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:14-5) Huygens also came close to the modern idea of [limit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limit_(mathematics) "Limit (mathematics)") while working on his _Dioptrica,_ though he never used the notion outside geometrical optics.[[176]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-176)

Huygens's standing as the greatest scientist in Europe was eclipsed by Newton's at the end of the seventeenth century, despite the fact that, as [Hugh Aldersey-Williams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hugh_Aldersey-Williams "Hugh Aldersey-Williams") notes, "Huygens's achievement exceeds that of Newton in some important respects".[[177]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-177) Although his journal publications anticipated the form of the modern [scientific article](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_literature "Scientific literature"),[[93]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:33-93) his persistent classicism and reluctance to publish his work did much to diminish his influence in the aftermath of the Scientific Revolution, as adherents of Leibniz’ calculus and Newton's physics took centre stage.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:9-38)[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:11-85)

Huygens's analyses of curves that satisfy certain physical properties, such as the [cycloid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cycloid "Cycloid"), led to later studies of many other such curves like the caustic, the [brachistochrone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brachistochrone_curve "Brachistochrone curve"), the sail curve, and the catenary.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:8-24)[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:16-35) His application of mathematics to physics, such as in his studies of impact and birefringence, would inspire new developments in mathematical physics and [rational mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_mechanics "Classical mechanics") in the following centuries (albeit in the new language of the calculus).[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:6-7) Additionally, Huygens developed the oscillating timekeeping mechanisms, the pendulum and the balance spring, that have been used ever since in mechanical [watches](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Watch "Watch") and [clocks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clock "Clock"). These were the first reliable timekeepers fit for [scientific use](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_instrument "Scientific instrument") (e.g., to make accurate measurements of the [inequality of the solar day](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synodic_day "Synodic day"), which was not possible before).[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:7-6)[[125]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:29-125) His work in this area foreshadowed the union of [applied mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Applied_mathematics "Applied mathematics") with [mechanical engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanical_engineering "Mechanical engineering") in the centuries that followed.[[132]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-:20-132)

The [European Space Agency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Space_Agency "European Space Agency")'s probe aboard the [Cassini spacecraft](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens "Cassini–Huygens") that landed on [Titan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Titan_(moon) "Titan (moon)"), [Saturn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saturn "Saturn")'s largest moon, in 2005 was [named after him](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Huygens_(spacecraft) "Huygens (spacecraft)").[[180]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens#cite_note-180)

A number of monuments to Christiaan Huygens can be found across important cities in the Netherlands, including [Rotterdam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotterdam "Rotterdam"), [Delft](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Delft "Delft"), and [Leiden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leiden "Leiden").
