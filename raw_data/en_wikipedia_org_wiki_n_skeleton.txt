This [hypercube graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypercube_graph "Hypercube graph") is the [1-skeleton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_of_a_polytope "Graph of a polytope") of the [tesseract](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tesseract "Tesseract").

This article is about mathematics. For the concept in computer graphics, see [topological skeleton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_skeleton "Topological skeleton").

In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), particularly in [algebraic topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_topology "Algebraic topology"), the **n-skeleton** of a [topological space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_space "Topological space")X presented as a [simplicial complex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simplicial_complex "Simplicial complex") (resp. [CW complex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CW_complex "CW complex")) refers to the [subspace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subspace_(topology) "Subspace (topology)")X n that is the [union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Union_(set_theory) "Union (set theory)") of the [simplices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simplex "Simplex") of X (resp. cells of X) of dimensions _m_ ≤ _n_. In other words, given an [inductive definition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inductive_definition "Inductive definition") of a complex, the n-skeleton is obtained by stopping at the n-th step.

These subspaces increase with n. The 0-skeleton is a [discrete space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discrete_space "Discrete space"), and the 1-skeleton a [topological graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_graph "Topological graph"). The skeletons of a space are used in [obstruction theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obstruction_theory "Obstruction theory"), to construct [spectral sequences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectral_sequence "Spectral sequence") by means of [filtrations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Filtration_(mathematics) "Filtration (mathematics)"), and generally to make [inductive arguments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_induction "Mathematical induction"). They are particularly important when X has infinite dimension, in the sense that the X n do not become constant as _n_ → ∞.

In [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry"), a _k_-skeleton of _n_-[polytope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polytope "Polytope") P (functionally represented as skel _k_(_P_)) consists of all _i_-polytope elements of dimension up to _k_.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/N-skeleton#cite_note-1)

For example:

skel 0(cube) = 8 vertices skel 1(cube) = 8 vertices, 12 edges skel 2(cube) = 8 vertices, 12 edges, 6 square faces
The 1-skeleton is also known as the [vertex-edge graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_of_a_polytope "Graph of a polytope") of the polytope.

For simplicial sets
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=N-skeleton&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: For simplicial sets")]

The above definition of the skeleton of a simplicial complex is a particular case of the notion of skeleton of a [simplicial set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simplicial_set "Simplicial set"). Briefly speaking, a simplicial set ![Image 2: {\displaystyle K_{*}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b186a1eb864cef54bd8c9c7e710c61f409d8181c) can be described by a collection of sets ![Image 3: {\displaystyle K_{i},\ i\geq 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3d27845a13955ff364359e525d39db944986787a), together with face and degeneracy maps between them satisfying a number of equations. The idea of the _n_-skeleton ![Image 4: {\displaystyle sk_{n}(K_{*})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8dd73fc7efaae161228e477b0023508aed0885ee) is to first discard the sets ![Image 5: {\displaystyle K_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/719736a1feb0bd7e73bb1425641a61229f55bb6d) with ![Image 6: {\displaystyle i>n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/88568023bcb42b163446ab1cc4b343f4ba079e95) and then to complete the collection of the ![Image 7: {\displaystyle K_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/719736a1feb0bd7e73bb1425641a61229f55bb6d) with ![Image 8: {\displaystyle i\leq n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1c372fbd81639c801a3397197b81a310bd207b1c) to the "smallest possible" simplicial set so that the resulting simplicial set contains no non-degenerate simplices in degrees ![Image 9: {\displaystyle i>n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/88568023bcb42b163446ab1cc4b343f4ba079e95).

has a left adjoint, denoted ![Image 11: {\displaystyle i^{*}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6290e0993b86a2f2dc5282e21d1087d9dd5aba49).[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/N-skeleton#cite_note-2) (The notations ![Image 12: {\displaystyle i^{*},i_{*}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2bb11485a02b4ff592786926123334e13210da36) are comparable with the one of [image functors for sheaves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Image_functors_for_sheaves "Image functors for sheaves").) For example, the 0-skeleton of _K_ is the constant simplicial set defined by ![Image 18: {\displaystyle K_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/44b0af6cafb690d3dbb0f3f30a032631338dc476). The above constructions work for more general categories (instead of sets) as well, provided that the category has [fiber products](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiber_product "Fiber product"). The coskeleton is needed to define the concept of [hypercovering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypercovering "Hypercovering") in [homotopical algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homotopical_algebra "Homotopical algebra") and [algebraic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_geometry "Algebraic geometry").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/N-skeleton#cite_note-3)
