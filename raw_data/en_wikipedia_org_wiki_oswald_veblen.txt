**Oswald Veblen** (June 24, 1880 – August 10, 1960) was an American [mathematician](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematician "Mathematician"), [geometer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometer "Geometer") and [topologist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topologist "Topologist"), whose work found application in [atomic physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_physics "Atomic physics") and the [theory of relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theory_of_relativity "Theory of relativity"). He proved the [Jordan curve theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jordan_curve_theorem "Jordan curve theorem") in 1905;[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oswald_Veblen#cite_note-1) while this was long considered the first rigorous proof of the theorem, many now also consider [Camille Jordan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camille_Jordan "Camille Jordan")'s original proof rigorous.

Veblen was born in [Decorah, Iowa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decorah,_Iowa "Decorah, Iowa"). His parents were Andrew Anderson Veblen (1848–1932), Professor of Physics at the [University of Iowa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Iowa "University of Iowa"), and Kirsti (Hougen) Veblen (1851–1908). Veblen's uncle was [Thorstein Veblen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thorstein_Veblen "Thorstein Veblen"), noted economist and sociologist.

Oswald went to school in [Iowa City](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iowa_City "Iowa City"). He did his undergraduate studies at the University of Iowa, where he received an [AB](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bachelor_of_Arts "Bachelor of Arts") in 1898, and [Harvard University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harvard_University "Harvard University"), where he was awarded a second [BA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bachelor_of_Arts "Bachelor of Arts") in 1900. For his graduate studies, he went to study mathematics at the [University of Chicago](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Chicago "University of Chicago"), where he obtained a [PhD](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PhD "PhD") in 1903. His dissertation, _A System of Axioms for Geometry_ was written under the supervision of [E. H. Moore](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/E._H._Moore "E. H. Moore"). During [World War I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_I "World War I"), Veblen served first as a captain, later as a major in the [United States Army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Army "United States Army").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oswald_Veblen#cite_note-2)

Veblen taught mathematics at [Princeton University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Princeton_University "Princeton University") from 1905 to 1932. In 1926, he was named [Henry B. Fine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry_Burchard_Fine "Henry Burchard Fine") Professor of Mathematics. In 1932, he helped organize the [Institute for Advanced Study](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Institute_for_Advanced_Study "Institute for Advanced Study") in Princeton, resigning his professorship to become the first professor at the Institute that same year. He kept his professorship at the Institute until he was made emeritus in 1950.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oswald_Veblen#cite_note-3)

During his years in Princeton, Veblen and his wife, Elizabeth M D Richardson, accumulated land along the [Princeton Ridge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rocky_Hill_Ridge "Rocky Hill Ridge"). In 1957 they donated 82 acres (33 ha) to establish the [Herrontown Woods Arboretum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herrontown_Woods_Arboretum "Herrontown Woods Arboretum"), the first and one of the largest nature preserves in [Princeton, New Jersey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Princeton,_New_Jersey "Princeton, New Jersey").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oswald_Veblen#cite_note-4)

Veblen was a Plenary Speaker of the [ICM](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Congress_of_Mathematicians "International Congress of Mathematicians") in 1928 in Bologna and in 1936 in Oslo.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oswald_Veblen#cite_note-5)

Veblen died in [Brooklin, Maine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brooklin,_Maine "Brooklin, Maine"), in 1960 at age 80. After his death the [American Mathematical Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Mathematical_Society "American Mathematical Society") created an award in his name, called the [Oswald Veblen Prize in Geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oswald_Veblen_Prize_in_Geometry "Oswald Veblen Prize in Geometry"). It is awarded every three years, and is the most prestigious award in recognition of outstanding research in geometry.

During his career, Veblen made important contributions in topology and in [projective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_geometry "Projective geometry") and [differential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry") geometries, including results important in [modern physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modern_physics "Modern physics"). He introduced the Veblen axioms for projective geometry and proved the [Veblen–Young theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Veblen%E2%80%93Young_theorem "Veblen–Young theorem"). He introduced the [Veblen functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Veblen_function "Veblen function") of ordinals and used an extension of them to define the small and large [Veblen ordinals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Large_countable_ordinal "Large countable ordinal"). In [World War II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II "World War II") he was involved in overseeing ballistics work at the [Aberdeen Proving Ground](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aberdeen_Proving_Ground "Aberdeen Proving Ground") that involved early modern [computing machines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computing_machine "Computing machine"), in particular supporting the proposal for creation of the pioneering [ENIAC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ENIAC "ENIAC") electronic [digital computer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digital_computer "Digital computer").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oswald_Veblen#cite_note-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oswald_Veblen#cite_note-7)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oswald_Veblen#cite_note-8) He also published a paper in 1912 on the [four-color conjecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Four-color_conjecture "Four-color conjecture").

Veblen was elected to the [American Philosophical Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Philosophical_Society "American Philosophical Society") in 1912, the United States [National Academy of Sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Academy_of_Sciences "National Academy of Sciences") in 1919, and the [American Academy of Arts and Sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Academy_of_Arts_and_Sciences "American Academy of Arts and Sciences") in 1923.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oswald_Veblen#cite_note-9)[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oswald_Veblen#cite_note-10)[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oswald_Veblen#cite_note-11)

In 1908, he married Elizabeth Richardson, the sister of British physicist [Owen Willans Richardson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Owen_Willans_Richardson "Owen Willans Richardson") and sister-in-law of American physicist [Clinton Joseph Davisson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clinton_Joseph_Davisson "Clinton Joseph Davisson").[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oswald_Veblen#cite_note-12)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oswald_Veblen#cite_note-13)[Steven G. Krantz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steven_G._Krantz "Steven G. Krantz") noted that the tradition of mathematical teas was started by Veblen at Princeton in the 1920s, as an outgrowth of enjoying his British wife Elizabeth's teas.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oswald_Veblen#cite_note-14)

Veblen Research Instructorship
------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Oswald_Veblen&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Veblen Research Instructorship")]

The Veblen Research Instructorship is a three-year position offered by the Department of Mathematics at [Princeton University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Princeton_University "Princeton University") and the [Institute for Advanced Study](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Institute_for_Advanced_Study "Institute for Advanced Study"). This position was established in 1998 and offered each year to outstanding candidates in pure and applied mathematics who have received their PhD within the last three years.

The Veblen instructors are Members of the Institute for Advanced Study and regular faculty members at Princeton University. The first and third year of the instructorship are spent at Princeton University and carry regular teaching responsibilities. The second year is spent at the Institute and dedicated to independent research of the instructor's choice.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oswald_Veblen#cite_note-15)
