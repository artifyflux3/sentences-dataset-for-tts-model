In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), and specifically [differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry"), a **connection form** is a manner of organizing the data of a [connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connection_(mathematics) "Connection (mathematics)") using the language of [moving frames](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moving_frame "Moving frame") and [differential forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_form "Differential form").

Historically, connection forms were introduced by [Élie Cartan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%89lie_Cartan "Élie Cartan") in the first half of the 20th century as part of, and one of the principal motivations for, his method of moving frames. The connection form generally depends on a choice of a [coordinate frame](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frame_bundle "Frame bundle"), and so is not a [tensorial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor "Tensor") object. Various generalizations and reinterpretations of the connection form were formulated subsequent to Cartan's initial work. In particular, on a [principal bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principal_bundle "Principal bundle"), a [principal connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connection_(principal_bundle) "Connection (principal bundle)") is a natural reinterpretation of the connection form as a tensorial object. On the other hand, the connection form has the advantage that it is a differential form defined on the [differentiable manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differentiable_manifold "Differentiable manifold"), rather than on an abstract principal bundle over it. Hence, despite their lack of tensoriality, connection forms continue to be used because of the relative ease of performing calculations with them.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connection_form#cite_note-1) In [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics"), connection forms are also used broadly in the context of [gauge theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauge_theory "Gauge theory"), through the [gauge covariant derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauge_covariant_derivative "Gauge covariant derivative").

A connection form associates to each [basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basis_of_a_vector_space "Basis of a vector space") of a [vector bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_bundle "Vector bundle") a [matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_(mathematics) "Matrix (mathematics)") of differential forms. The connection form is not tensorial because under a [change of basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Change_of_basis "Change of basis"), the connection form transforms in a manner that involves the [exterior derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_derivative "Exterior derivative") of the [transition functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atlas_(topology)#Transition_maps "Atlas (topology)"), in much the same way as the [Christoffel symbols](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christoffel_symbols "Christoffel symbols") for the [Levi-Civita connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_connection "Levi-Civita connection"). The main _tensorial_ invariant of a connection form is its [curvature form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvature_form "Curvature form"). In the presence of a [solder form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solder_form "Solder form") identifying the vector bundle with the [tangent bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_bundle "Tangent bundle"), there is an additional invariant: the [torsion form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torsion_(differential_geometry) "Torsion (differential geometry)"). In many cases, connection forms are considered on vector bundles with additional structure: that of a [fiber bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiber_bundle "Fiber bundle") with a [structure group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_group "Lie group").

### Frames on a vector bundle

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Connection_form&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Frames on a vector bundle")]

Let ![Image 1: {\displaystyle E}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4232c9de2ee3eec0a9c0a19b15ab92daa6223f9b) be a [vector bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_bundle "Vector bundle") of fibre dimension ![Image 2: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40) over a [differentiable manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differentiable_manifold "Differentiable manifold")![Image 3: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd). A **local frame** for ![Image 4: {\displaystyle E}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4232c9de2ee3eec0a9c0a19b15ab92daa6223f9b) is an ordered [basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basis_of_a_vector_space "Basis of a vector space") of [local sections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Section_(fiber_bundle) "Section (fiber bundle)") of ![Image 5: {\displaystyle E}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4232c9de2ee3eec0a9c0a19b15ab92daa6223f9b). It is always possible to construct a local frame, as vector bundles are always defined in terms of [local trivializations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_trivialization "Local trivialization"), in analogy to the [atlas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atlas_(topology) "Atlas (topology)") of a manifold. That is, given any point ![Image 6: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) on the base manifold ![Image 7: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd), there exists an open neighborhood ![Image 8: {\displaystyle U\subseteq M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a57ecb017c8458e5f519dbca5ddb7fffde7f1a16) of ![Image 9: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) for which the vector bundle over ![Image 10: {\displaystyle U}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/458a728f53b9a0274f059cd695e067c430956025) is locally trivial, that is isomorphic to ![Image 11: {\displaystyle U\times \mathbb {R} ^{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6b80843ab74234e43f73e372586de584aade0bab) projecting to ![Image 12: {\displaystyle U}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/458a728f53b9a0274f059cd695e067c430956025). The vector space structure on ![Image 13: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1bcd8908c9fa46eb979ef7b67d1bb65eb3692cbb) can thereby be extended to the entire local trivialization, and a basis on ![Image 14: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1bcd8908c9fa46eb979ef7b67d1bb65eb3692cbb) can be extended as well; this defines the local frame. (Here the real numbers are used, although much of the development can be extended to modules over rings in general, and to vector spaces over complex numbers ![Image 15: {\displaystyle \mathbb {C} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f9add4085095b9b6d28d045fd9c92c2c09f549a7) in particular.)

This frame can be used to express locally any section of ![Image 18: {\displaystyle E}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4232c9de2ee3eec0a9c0a19b15ab92daa6223f9b). Then

![Image 21: {\displaystyle \xi =\sum _{\alpha =1}^{k}e_{\alpha }\xi ^{\alpha }(\mathbf {e} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/148f7d78c3549f60e2cbe03fdadee106bfdd8e6b)
where ![Image 22: {\displaystyle \xi ^{\alpha }(\mathbf {e} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8bfc2d22f45f07f34a54f12e39d8dea906847184) denotes the _components_ of ![Image 23: {\displaystyle \xi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e0b461aaf61091abd5d2c808931c48b8ff9647db) in the frame ![Image 24: {\displaystyle \mathbf {e} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8b904155833429dc023056b5b9609c4a679d7064). As a matrix equation, this reads

![Image 25: {\displaystyle \xi ={\mathbf {e} }{\begin{bmatrix}\xi ^{1}(\mathbf {e} )\\\xi ^{2}(\mathbf {e} )\\\vdots \\\xi ^{k}(\mathbf {e} )\end{bmatrix}}={\mathbf {e} }\,\xi (\mathbf {e} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/018bd0534691468adffe09df57aaa3b4d3242b6a)
In [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity"), such frame fields are referred to as [tetrads](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrad_formalism "Tetrad formalism"). The tetrad specifically relates the local frame to an explicit coordinate system on the base manifold ![Image 26: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) (the coordinate system on ![Image 27: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) being established by the atlas).

### Exterior connections

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Connection_form&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Exterior connections")]

A [connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connection_(vector_bundle) "Connection (vector bundle)") in _E_ is a type of [differential operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_operator "Differential operator")

![Image 28: {\displaystyle D:\Gamma (E)\rightarrow \Gamma (E\otimes T^{*}M)=\Gamma (E)\otimes \Omega ^{1}M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8176ce9fc94549a29d20b7096af9c00b59b25664)
where Γ denotes the [sheaf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sheaf_(mathematics) "Sheaf (mathematics)") of local [sections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Section_(fibre_bundle) "Section (fibre bundle)") of a vector bundle, and Ω 1 _M_ is the bundle of differential 1-forms on _M_. For _D_ to be a connection, it must be correctly coupled to the [exterior derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_derivative "Exterior derivative"). Specifically, if _v_ is a local section of _E_, and _f_ is a smooth function, then

![Image 29: {\displaystyle D(fv)=v\otimes (df)+fDv}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/387e21060ef7f08e14f8265b8c6f4be342d8cd17)
where _df_ is the exterior derivative of _f_.

Sometimes it is convenient to extend the definition of _D_ to arbitrary [_E_-valued forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector-valued_differential_form "Vector-valued differential form"), thus regarding it as a differential operator on the tensor product of _E_ with the full [exterior algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_algebra "Exterior algebra") of differential forms. Given an exterior connection _D_ satisfying this compatibility property, there exists a unique extension of _D_:

![Image 30: {\displaystyle D:\Gamma (E\otimes \Lambda ^{*}T^{*}M)\rightarrow \Gamma (E\otimes \Lambda ^{*}T^{*}M)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9b485c490f2177542db2db1a35aa1d0de8abe6b1)
such that

![Image 31: {\displaystyle D(v\wedge \alpha )=(Dv)\wedge \alpha +(-1)^{{\text{deg}}\,v}v\wedge d\alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7f2cd966d5f2401ee9ddd1e60fec8061c8d808ab)
where _v_ is homogeneous of degree deg _v_. In other words, _D_ is a [derivation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivation_(abstract_algebra) "Derivation (abstract algebra)") on the sheaf of graded modules Γ(_E_ ⊗ Ω*_M_).

The **connection form** arises when applying the exterior connection to a particular frame **e**. Upon applying the exterior connection to the _e_ _α_, it is the unique _k_ × _k_ matrix (_ω_ _α_ _β_) of [one-forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One-form "One-form") on _M_ such that

![Image 32: {\displaystyle De_{\alpha }=\sum _{\beta =1}^{k}e_{\beta }\otimes \omega _{\alpha }^{\beta }.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffe6e5a6cec3e44c79df8a110af09150651a6618)
In terms of the connection form, the exterior connection of any section of _E_ can now be expressed. For example, suppose that _ξ_ = Σ _α_ _e_ _α_ _ξ_ _α_. Then

![Image 33: {\displaystyle D\xi =\sum _{\alpha =1}^{k}D(e_{\alpha }\xi ^{\alpha }(\mathbf {e} ))=\sum _{\alpha =1}^{k}e_{\alpha }\otimes d\xi ^{\alpha }(\mathbf {e} )+\sum _{\alpha =1}^{k}\sum _{\beta =1}^{k}e_{\beta }\otimes \omega _{\alpha }^{\beta }\xi ^{\alpha }(\mathbf {e} ).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1c3fef90847765435b49ca903e147eb74c39bab3)
Taking components on both sides,

![Image 34: {\displaystyle D\xi (\mathbf {e} )=d\xi (\mathbf {e} )+\omega \xi (\mathbf {e} )=(d+\omega )\xi (\mathbf {e} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1f518c6a934343338d4e3f910c832f1268d759ef)
where it is understood that _d_ and ω refer to the component-wise derivative with respect to the frame **e**, and a matrix of 1-forms, respectively, acting on the components of _ξ_. Conversely, a matrix of 1-forms _ω_ is _a priori_ sufficient to completely determine the connection locally on the open set over which the basis of sections **e** is defined.

In order to extend _ω_ to a suitable global object, it is necessary to examine how it behaves when a different choice of basic sections of _E_ is chosen. Write _ω_ _α_ _β_ = _ω_ _α_ _β_(**e**) to indicate the dependence on the choice of **e**.

Suppose that **e**′ is a different choice of local basis. Then there is an invertible _k_ × _k_ matrix of functions _g_ such that

![Image 35: {\displaystyle {\mathbf {e} }'={\mathbf {e} }\,g,\quad {\text{i.e., }}\,e'_{\alpha }=\sum _{\beta }e_{\beta }g_{\alpha }^{\beta }.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6a509f5421fad6cb6fddab923e693fda12c28f24)
Applying the exterior connection to both sides gives the transformation law for _ω_:

![Image 36: {\displaystyle \omega (\mathbf {e} \,g)=g^{-1}dg+g^{-1}\omega (\mathbf {e} )g.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9ae749c53f21e442fd810c0e7e4b336d58afcaa0)
Note in particular that _ω_ fails to transform in a [tensorial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor "Tensor") manner, since the rule for passing from one frame to another involves the derivatives of the transition matrix _g_.

#### Global connection forms

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Connection_form&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Global connection forms")]

If {_U_ _p_} is an open covering of _M_, and each _U_ _p_ is equipped with a trivialization **e**_p_ of _E_, then it is possible to define a global connection form in terms of the patching data between the local connection forms on the overlap regions. In detail, a **connection form** on _M_ is a system of matrices _ω_(**e**_p_) of 1-forms defined on each _U_ _p_ that satisfy the following compatibility condition

![Image 37: {\displaystyle \omega (\mathbf {e} _{q})=(\mathbf {e} _{p}^{-1}\mathbf {e} _{q})^{-1}d(\mathbf {e} _{p}^{-1}\mathbf {e} _{q})+(\mathbf {e} _{p}^{-1}\mathbf {e} _{q})^{-1}\omega (\mathbf {e} _{p})(\mathbf {e} _{p}^{-1}\mathbf {e} _{q}).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5c6b0d7654ede6f342158ab0da084901298c1ad0)
This _compatibility condition_ ensures in particular that the exterior connection of a section of _E_, when regarded abstractly as a section of _E_ ⊗ Ω 1 _M_, does not depend on the choice of basis section used to define the connection.

The **curvature two-form** of a connection form in _E_ is defined by

![Image 38: {\displaystyle \Omega (\mathbf {e} )=d\omega (\mathbf {e} )+\omega (\mathbf {e} )\wedge \omega (\mathbf {e} ).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/421e7bccb418de31539c6662d7ae1b4964f6d913)
Unlike the connection form, the curvature behaves tensorially under a change of frame, which can be checked directly by using the [Poincaré lemma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poincar%C3%A9_lemma "Poincaré lemma"). Specifically, if **e** → **e**_g_ is a change of frame, then the curvature two-form transforms by

![Image 39: {\displaystyle \Omega (\mathbf {e} \,g)=g^{-1}\Omega (\mathbf {e} )g.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3d91434148e28434b60f937e5ee03a76902a93d6)
One interpretation of this transformation law is as follows. Let **e*** be the [dual basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_basis "Dual basis") corresponding to the frame _e_. Then the 2-form

![Image 40: {\displaystyle \Omega ={\mathbf {e} }\Omega (\mathbf {e} ){\mathbf {e} }^{*}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d7e0bb925c9916d336b91c85c5a154d27c782097)
is independent of the choice of frame. In particular, Ω is a vector-valued two-form on _M_ with values in the [endomorphism ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endomorphism_ring "Endomorphism ring") Hom(_E_,_E_). Symbolically,

![Image 41: {\displaystyle \Omega \in \Gamma (\Lambda ^{2}T^{*}M\otimes {\text{Hom}}(E,E)).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b40833a0962e5b08958bd9f0ee641ba5c8ae7e2a)
In terms of the exterior connection _D_, the curvature endomorphism is given by

![Image 42: {\displaystyle \Omega (v)=D(Dv)=D^{2}v\,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b6c6a58b90572cf17110cb5591665ac3faaed93)
for _v_ ∈ _E_ (we can extend _v_ to a local section to define this expression). Thus the curvature measures the failure of the sequence

![Image 43: {\displaystyle \Gamma (E)\ {\stackrel {D}{\to }}\ \Gamma (E\otimes \Lambda ^{1}T^{*}M)\ {\stackrel {D}{\to }}\ \Gamma (E\otimes \Lambda ^{2}T^{*}M)\ {\stackrel {D}{\to }}\ \dots \ {\stackrel {D}{\to }}\ \Gamma (E\otimes \Lambda ^{n}T^{*}(M))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3ebb345a4086b8e0f100d4df8fe6d6c554d28d3c)
to be a [chain complex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chain_complex "Chain complex") (in the sense of [de Rham cohomology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_Rham_cohomology "De Rham cohomology")).

### Soldering and torsion

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Connection_form&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Soldering and torsion")]

Suppose that the fibre dimension _k_ of _E_ is equal to the dimension of the manifold _M_. In this case, the vector bundle _E_ is sometimes equipped with an additional piece of data besides its connection: a [solder form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solder_form "Solder form"). A **solder form** is a globally defined [vector-valued one-form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector-valued_form "Vector-valued form") θ ∈ Ω 1(_M_,_E_) such that the mapping

![Image 44: {\displaystyle \theta _{x}:T_{x}M\rightarrow E_{x}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8777b0bf773ccf12d7fc75882f746fcf3a1aaabe)
is a linear isomorphism for all _x_ ∈ _M_. If a solder form is given, then it is possible to define the **[torsion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torsion_(differential_geometry) "Torsion (differential geometry)")** of the connection (in terms of the exterior connection) as

![Image 45: {\displaystyle \Theta =D\theta .\,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1be1042b3e4984da2aeeefff7812dcd2273fc2c4)
The torsion Θ is an _E_-valued 2-form on _M_.

A solder form and the associated torsion may both be described in terms of a local frame **e** of _E_. If θ is a solder form, then it decomposes into the frame components

![Image 46: {\displaystyle \theta =\sum _{i}\theta ^{i}(\mathbf {e} )e_{i}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4a4c0b3a236829e71231303aeb07425c196a0a87)
The components of the torsion are then

![Image 47: {\displaystyle \Theta ^{i}(\mathbf {e} )=d\theta ^{i}(\mathbf {e} )+\sum _{j}\omega _{j}^{i}(\mathbf {e} )\wedge \theta ^{j}(\mathbf {e} ).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/edbfea8372b89c9996ad6219d28bf026331dfda5)
Much like the curvature, it can be shown that Θ behaves as a [contravariant tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covariance_and_contravariance_of_vectors "Covariance and contravariance of vectors") under a change in frame:

![Image 48: {\displaystyle \Theta ^{i}(\mathbf {e} \,g)=\sum _{j}g_{j}^{i}\Theta ^{j}(\mathbf {e} ).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c304d4476a0b6947d0ef0669d743a91cc3546287)
The frame-independent torsion may also be recovered from the frame components:

![Image 49: {\displaystyle \Theta =\sum _{i}e_{i}\Theta ^{i}(\mathbf {e} ).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/082dbcf217e55ea82c93ca083db8842651aa20bc)

### Example: the Levi-Civita connection

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Connection_form&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Example: the Levi-Civita connection")]

As an example, suppose that _M_ carries a [Riemannian metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_metric "Riemannian metric"). If one has a [vector bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_bundle "Vector bundle")_E_ over _M_, then the metric can be extended to the entire vector bundle, as the [bundle metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bundle_metric "Bundle metric"). One may then define a connection that is compatible with this bundle metric, this is the [metric connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_connection "Metric connection"). For the special case of _E_ being the [tangent bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_bundle "Tangent bundle")_TM_, the metric connection is called the [Riemannian connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_connection "Riemannian connection"). Given a Riemannian connection, one can always find a unique, equivalent connection that is [torsion-free](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torsion_tensor "Torsion tensor"). This is the [Levi-Civita connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_connection "Levi-Civita connection") on the tangent bundle _TM_ of _M_.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connection_form#cite_note-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connection_form#cite_note-3)

A local frame on the tangent bundle is an ordered list of vector fields **e** = (_e_ _i_ | _i_ = 1, 2, ..., _n_), where _n_ = dim _M_, defined on an open subset of _M_ that are linearly independent at every point of their domain. The [Christoffel symbols](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christoffel_symbols "Christoffel symbols") define the Levi-Civita connection by

![Image 52: {\displaystyle \nabla _{e_{i}}e_{j}=\sum _{k=1}^{n}\Gamma _{ij}^{k}(\mathbf {e} )e_{k}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ee4e719e14aa8ae3b0412b43c11228cff2d2682d)
If _θ_ = {_θ_ _i_ | _i_ = 1, 2, ..., _n_}, denotes the [dual basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_basis "Dual basis") of the [cotangent bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cotangent_bundle "Cotangent bundle"), such that _θ_ _i_(_e_ _j_) = _δ_ _i_ _j_ (the [Kronecker delta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kronecker_delta "Kronecker delta")), then the connection form is

![Image 53: {\displaystyle \omega _{i}^{j}(\mathbf {e} )=\sum _{k}\Gamma ^{j}{}_{ki}(\mathbf {e} )\theta ^{k}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/32d66e69e60d8ecb0e94a4273557706db91ddbc4)
In terms of the connection form, the exterior connection on a vector field _v_ = Σ _i_ _e_ _i_ _v_ _i_ is given by

![Image 54: {\displaystyle Dv=\sum _{k}e_{k}\otimes (dv^{k})+\sum _{j,k}e_{k}\otimes \omega _{j}^{k}(\mathbf {e} )v^{j}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8b5e39315bbf850975328ddb3c3dba4b91c7d73a)
One can recover the Levi-Civita connection, in the usual sense, from this by contracting with _e_ i:

![Image 55: {\displaystyle \nabla _{e_{i}}v=\langle Dv,e_{i}\rangle =\sum _{k}e_{k}\left(\nabla _{e_{i}}v^{k}+\sum _{j}\Gamma _{ij}^{k}(\mathbf {e} )v^{j}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bb3c6909172ac46e336f49c2fec809938639a153)
The curvature 2-form of the Levi-Civita connection is the matrix (Ω _i_ _j_) given by

![Image 56: {\displaystyle \Omega _{i}{}^{j}(\mathbf {e} )=d\omega _{i}{}^{j}(\mathbf {e} )+\sum _{k}\omega _{k}{}^{j}(\mathbf {e} )\wedge \omega _{i}{}^{k}(\mathbf {e} ).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/21836b30fe89a8ee494e61a27e0da29c13370925)
For simplicity, suppose that the frame **e** is [holonomic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holonomic_basis "Holonomic basis"), so that _dθ_ _i_ = 0.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connection_form#cite_note-4) Then, employing now the [summation convention](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Summation_convention "Summation convention") on repeated indices,

![Image 57: {\displaystyle {\begin{array}{ll}\Omega _{i}{}^{j}&=d(\Gamma ^{j}{}_{qi}\theta ^{q})+(\Gamma ^{j}{}_{pk}\theta ^{p})\wedge (\Gamma ^{k}{}_{qi}\theta ^{q})\\&\\&=\theta ^{p}\wedge \theta ^{q}\left(\partial _{p}\Gamma ^{j}{}_{qi}+\Gamma ^{j}{}_{pk}\Gamma ^{k}{}_{qi})\right)\\&\\&={\tfrac {1}{2}}\theta ^{p}\wedge \theta ^{q}R_{pqi}{}^{j}\end{array}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/60b6722039bba6143c53ea877d53b0f4b2f1133b)
where _R_ is the [Riemann curvature tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_curvature_tensor "Riemann curvature tensor").

The Levi-Civita connection is characterized as the unique [metric connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_connection "Metric connection") in the tangent bundle with zero torsion. To describe the torsion, note that the vector bundle _E_ is the tangent bundle. This carries a canonical solder form (sometimes called the [canonical one-form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canonical_one-form "Canonical one-form"), especially in the context of [classical mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_mechanics "Classical mechanics")) that is the section _θ_ of Hom(T _M_, T _M_) = T∗_M_ ⊗ T _M_ corresponding to the identity endomorphism of the tangent spaces. In the frame **e**, the solder form is _θ_ = Σ _i_ _e_ _i_ ⊗ _θ_ _i_, where again _θ_ _i_ is the dual basis.

The torsion of the connection is given by Θ = _Dθ_, or in terms of the frame components of the solder form by

![Image 58: {\displaystyle \Theta ^{i}(\mathbf {e} )=d\theta ^{i}+\sum _{j}\omega _{j}^{i}(\mathbf {e} )\wedge \theta ^{j}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9614416c62711f86fa4e18e4aad45f1377bb3236)
Assuming again for simplicity that **e** is holonomic, this expression reduces to

![Image 59: {\displaystyle \Theta ^{i}=\Gamma ^{i}{}_{kj}\theta ^{k}\wedge \theta ^{j}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ebc736af50305b0cba2d7c5778bb097cdd382668),
which vanishes if and only if Γ _i_ _kj_ is symmetric on its lower indices.

Given a metric connection with torsion, one can always find a single, unique connection that is torsion-free, this is the Levi-Civita connection. The difference between a Riemannian connection and its associated Levi-Civita connection is the [contorsion tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contorsion_tensor "Contorsion tensor").

A more specific type of connection form can be constructed when the vector bundle _E_ carries a [structure group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Associated_bundle "Associated bundle"). This amounts to a preferred class of frames **e** on _E_, which are related by a [Lie group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_group "Lie group")_G_. For example, in the presence of a [metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_(vector_bundle) "Metric (vector bundle)") in _E_, one works with frames that form an [orthonormal basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthonormal_basis "Orthonormal basis") at each point. The structure group is then the [orthogonal group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_group "Orthogonal group"), since this group preserves the orthonormality of frames. Other examples include:

*   The usual frames, considered in the preceding section, have structural group GL(_k_) where _k_ is the fibre dimension of _E_.
*   The holomorphic tangent bundle of a [complex manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_manifold "Complex manifold") (or [almost complex manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Almost_complex_manifold "Almost complex manifold")).[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connection_form#cite_note-Wells-5) Here the structure group is GL n(**C**) ⊂ GL 2n(**R**).[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connection_form#cite_note-6) In case a [hermitian metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermitian_metric "Hermitian metric") is given, then the structure group reduces to the [unitary group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unitary_group "Unitary group") acting on unitary frames.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connection_form#cite_note-Wells-5)
*   [Spinors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spinor "Spinor") on a manifold equipped with a [spin structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_structure "Spin structure"). The frames are unitary with respect to an invariant inner product on the spin space, and the group reduces to the [spin group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_group "Spin group").
*   Holomorphic tangent bundles on [CR manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CR_manifold "CR manifold").[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connection_form#cite_note-7)

In general, let _E_ be a given vector bundle of fibre dimension _k_ and _G_ ⊂ GL(_k_) a given Lie subgroup of the general linear group of **R**k. If (_e_ α) is a local frame of _E_, then a matrix-valued function (_g_ i j): _M_ → _G_ may act on the _e_ α to produce a new frame

![Image 60: {\displaystyle e_{\alpha }'=\sum _{\beta }e_{\beta }g_{\alpha }^{\beta }.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6be01a2129eca9efdd91c94ce4b81c7117c07a04)
Two such frames are **_G_-related**. Informally, the vector bundle _E_ has the **structure of a _G_-bundle** if a preferred class of frames is specified, all of which are locally _G_-related to each other. In formal terms, _E_ is a [fibre bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fibre_bundle "Fibre bundle") with structure group _G_ whose typical fibre is **R**k with the natural action of _G_ as a subgroup of GL(_k_).

### Compatible connections

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Connection_form&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Compatible connections")]

A connection is [compatible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_compatible "Metric compatible") with the structure of a _G_-bundle on _E_ provided that the associated [parallel transport](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_transport "Parallel transport") maps always send one _G_-frame to another. Formally, along a curve γ, the following must hold locally (that is, for sufficiently small values of _t_):

![Image 61: {\displaystyle \Gamma (\gamma )_{0}^{t}e_{\alpha }(\gamma (0))=\sum _{\beta }e_{\beta }(\gamma (t))g_{\alpha }^{\beta }(t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f80abdde7dddf58a3dbe5f462f6cdbb81c6fc2ae)
for some matrix _g_ α β (which may also depend on _t_). Differentiation at _t_=0 gives

![Image 62: {\displaystyle \nabla _{{\dot {\gamma }}(0)}e_{\alpha }=\sum _{\beta }e_{\beta }\omega _{\alpha }^{\beta }({\dot {\gamma }}(0))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/43a6e46c32fe73be0d8651587ce748c7b6f5d28f)
where the coefficients ω α β are in the [Lie algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_algebra "Lie algebra")**g** of the Lie group _G_.

With this observation, the connection form ω α β defined by

![Image 63: {\displaystyle De_{\alpha }=\sum _{\beta }e_{\beta }\otimes \omega _{\alpha }^{\beta }(\mathbf {e} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/08881c9eee66e3333f48f5c7ff199d391214c015)
is **compatible with the structure** if the matrix of one-forms ω α β(**e**) takes its values in **g**.

The curvature form of a compatible connection is, moreover, a **g**-valued two-form.

Under a change of frame

![Image 64: {\displaystyle e_{\alpha }'=\sum _{\beta }e_{\beta }g_{\alpha }^{\beta }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ccfafb4179c81364ea4871dc14004f1b8a6b495d)
where _g_ is a _G_-valued function defined on an open subset of _M_, the connection form transforms via

![Image 65: {\displaystyle \omega _{\alpha }^{\beta }(\mathbf {e} \cdot g)=(g^{-1})_{\gamma }^{\beta }dg_{\alpha }^{\gamma }+(g^{-1})_{\gamma }^{\beta }\omega _{\delta }^{\gamma }(\mathbf {e} )g_{\alpha }^{\delta }.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0de25959535fb9a00639ce80f873932d5372e201)
Or, using matrix products:

![Image 66: {\displaystyle \omega ({\mathbf {e} }\cdot g)=g^{-1}dg+g^{-1}\omega g.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4278f96c310d444e8ea6aa06805595ef88e42250)
To interpret each of these terms, recall that _g_: _M_ → _G_ is a _G_-valued (locally defined) function. With this in mind,

![Image 67: {\displaystyle \omega ({\mathbf {e} }\cdot g)=g^{*}\omega _{\mathfrak {g}}+{\text{Ad}}_{g^{-1}}\omega (\mathbf {e} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/34b3120b296f9d7cd7fbec1a8087bb383ce80ef1)
where ω**g** is the [Maurer-Cartan form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maurer-Cartan_form "Maurer-Cartan form") for the group _G_, here [pulled back](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pullback_(differential_geometry) "Pullback (differential geometry)") to _M_ along the function _g_, and Ad is the [adjoint representation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adjoint_representation "Adjoint representation") of _G_ on its Lie algebra.

The connection form, as introduced thus far, depends on a particular choice of frame. In the first definition, the frame is just a local basis of sections. To each frame, a connection form is given with a transformation law for passing from one frame to another. In the second definition, the frames themselves carry some additional structure provided by a Lie group, and changes of frame are constrained to those that take their values in it. The language of principal bundles, pioneered by [Charles Ehresmann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Ehresmann "Charles Ehresmann") in the 1940s, provides a manner of organizing these many connection forms and the transformation laws connecting them into a single intrinsic form with a single rule for transformation. The disadvantage to this approach is that the forms are no longer defined on the manifold itself, but rather on a larger principal bundle.

### The principal connection for a connection form

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Connection_form&action=edit&section=17 "Edit section: The principal connection for a connection form")]

Suppose that _E_ → _M_ is a vector bundle with structure group _G_. Let {_U_} be an open cover of _M_, along with _G_-frames on each _U_, denoted by **e**U. These are related on the intersections of overlapping open sets by

![Image 68: {\displaystyle {\mathbf {e} }_{V}={\mathbf {e} }_{U}\cdot h_{UV}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7bf29b126bbf8405a8128421f7641468cd8a95cf)
for some _G_-valued function _h_ UV defined on _U_ ∩ _V_.

Let F G _E_ be the set of all _G_-frames taken over each point of _M_. This is a principal _G_-bundle over _M_. In detail, using the fact that the _G_-frames are all _G_-related, F G _E_ can be realized in terms of gluing data among the sets of the open cover:

![Image 69: {\displaystyle F_{G}E=\left.\coprod _{U}U\times G\right/\sim }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e7b6bdbd55bd8a1419b53898120fd183d0abd20b)
where the [equivalence relation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equivalence_relation "Equivalence relation")![Image 70: {\displaystyle \sim }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/afcc42adfcfdc24d5c4c474869e5d8eaa78d1173) is defined by

![Image 71: {\displaystyle ((x,g_{U})\in U\times G)\sim ((x,g_{V})\in V\times G)\iff {\mathbf {e} }_{V}={\mathbf {e} }_{U}\cdot h_{UV}{\text{ and }}g_{U}=h_{UV}^{-1}(x)g_{V}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/936052b721b868d172eb9603b2750059d7c0babb)
On F G _E_, define a [principal _G_-connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connection_(principal_bundle) "Connection (principal bundle)") as follows, by specifying a **g**-valued one-form on each product _U_ × _G_, which respects the equivalence relation on the overlap regions. First let

![Image 72: {\displaystyle \pi _{1}:U\times G\to U,\quad \pi _{2}:U\times G\to G}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/242c4b81ead87284b3872667672765ecc7cefa91)
be the projection maps. Now, for a point (_x_,_g_) ∈ _U_ × _G_, set

![Image 73: {\displaystyle \omega _{(x,g)}=Ad_{g^{-1}}\pi _{1}^{*}\omega (\mathbf {e} _{U})+\pi _{2}^{*}\omega _{\mathbf {g} }.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d1a00185df248960c5f3a1cb6acfba3318aaf14d)
The 1-form ω constructed in this way respects the transitions between overlapping sets, and therefore descends to give a globally defined 1-form on the principal bundle F G _E_. It can be shown that ω is a principal connection in the sense that it reproduces the generators of the right _G_ action on F G _E_, and equivariantly intertwines the right action on T(F G _E_) with the adjoint representation of _G_.

### Connection forms associated to a principal connection

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Connection_form&action=edit&section=18 "Edit section: Connection forms associated to a principal connection")]

Conversely, a principal _G_-connection ω in a principal _G_-bundle _P_→_M_ gives rise to a collection of connection forms on _M_. Suppose that **e**: _M_ → _P_ is a local section of _P_. Then the pullback of ω along **e** defines a **g**-valued one-form on _M_:

![Image 74: {\displaystyle \omega ({\mathbf {e} })={\mathbf {e} }^{*}\omega .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/41538aa2e6b633fb3047bb1618144cbba51c1871)
Changing frames by a _G_-valued function _g_, one sees that ω(**e**) transforms in the required manner by using the Leibniz rule, and the adjunction:

![Image 75: {\displaystyle \langle X,({\mathbf {e} }\cdot g)^{*}\omega \rangle =\langle [d(\mathbf {e} \cdot g)](X),\omega \rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/202d3833f86f924435e77f63528e57b4f9de4c33)
where _X_ is a vector on _M_, and _d_ denotes the [pushforward](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pushforward_(differential) "Pushforward (differential)").
