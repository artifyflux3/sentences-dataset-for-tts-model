In [algebraic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_geometry "Algebraic geometry"), a **quotient stack** is a [stack](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stack_(mathematics) "Stack (mathematics)") that parametrizes equivariant objects. Geometrically, it generalizes a quotient of a [scheme](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scheme_(mathematics) "Scheme (mathematics)") or a [variety](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_variety "Algebraic variety") by a [group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_(mathematics) "Group (mathematics)"): a quotient variety, say, would be a coarse approximation of a quotient stack.

The notion is of fundamental importance in the study of stacks: a stack that arises in nature is often either a quotient stack itself or admits a stratification by quotient stacks (e.g., a [Deligne–Mumford stack](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deligne%E2%80%93Mumford_stack "Deligne–Mumford stack").) A quotient stack is also used to construct other stacks like **classifying stacks**.

A quotient stack is defined as follows. Let _G_ be an affine smooth [group scheme](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_scheme "Group scheme") over a scheme _S_ and _X_ an _S_-scheme on which _G_[acts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group-scheme_action "Group-scheme action"). Let the quotient stack ![Image 1: {\displaystyle [X/G]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6c10708eac95ed8fcce0dd132f18c6d844536ebf) be the [category over](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fibered_category "Fibered category") the category of _S_-schemes, where

Suppose the [quotient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Categorical_quotient "Categorical quotient")![Image 2: {\displaystyle X/G}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7cb103556c45e5f866285543c6b149b0f42f9121) exists as an [algebraic space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_space "Algebraic space") (for example, by the [Keel–Mori theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Keel%E2%80%93Mori_theorem "Keel–Mori theorem")). The canonical map

![Image 3: {\displaystyle [X/G]\to X/G}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/361d409e5d9f33aac9b9163f2cb4bbd87c68fc03),
that sends a bundle _P_ over _T_ to a corresponding _T_-point,[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quotient_stack#cite_note-1) need not be an isomorphism of stacks; that is, the space "X/G" is usually coarser. The canonical map is an isomorphism if and only if the stabilizers are trivial (in which case ![Image 4: {\displaystyle X/G}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7cb103556c45e5f866285543c6b149b0f42f9121) exists.)[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

In general, ![Image 5: {\displaystyle [X/G]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6c10708eac95ed8fcce0dd132f18c6d844536ebf) is an [Artin stack](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artin_stack "Artin stack") (also called algebraic stack). If the stabilizers of the [geometric points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_point "Geometric point") are finite and reduced, then it is a [Deligne–Mumford stack](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deligne%E2%80%93Mumford_stack "Deligne–Mumford stack").

[Burt Totaro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burt_Totaro "Burt Totaro")([2004](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quotient_stack#CITEREFTotaro2004)) has shown: let _X_ be a normal Noetherian algebraic stack whose stabilizer groups at closed points are affine. Then _X_ is a quotient stack if and only if it has the [resolution property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resolution_property "Resolution property"); i.e., every coherent sheaf is a quotient of a vector bundle. Earlier, [Robert Wayne Thomason](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Wayne_Thomason "Robert Wayne Thomason") proved that a quotient stack has the resolution property.

Remark: It is possible to approach the construction from the point of view of [simplicial sheaves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simplicial_sheaf "Simplicial sheaf").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quotient_stack#cite_note-2) See also: [simplicial diagram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simplicial_diagram "Simplicial diagram").

An effective quotient [orbifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbifold "Orbifold"), e.g., ![Image 6: {\displaystyle [M/G]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/375110835b60a898de6e63062ea75344f6de53ef) where the ![Image 7: {\displaystyle G}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5f3c8921a3b352de45446a6789b104458c9f90b) action has only finite stabilizers on the smooth space ![Image 8: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd), is an example of a quotient stack.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quotient_stack#cite_note-3)

If ![Image 9: {\displaystyle X=S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/172d09e1e067fe81720250a49f71e75a878cfee4) with trivial action of ![Image 10: {\displaystyle G}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5f3c8921a3b352de45446a6789b104458c9f90b) (often ![Image 11: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2) is a point), then ![Image 12: {\displaystyle [S/G]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/58495295cc8b13a63005a9e07ebd15ab143fb107) is called the **classifying stack** of ![Image 13: {\displaystyle G}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5f3c8921a3b352de45446a6789b104458c9f90b) (in analogy with the [classifying space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classifying_space "Classifying space") of ![Image 14: {\displaystyle G}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5f3c8921a3b352de45446a6789b104458c9f90b)) and is usually denoted by ![Image 15: {\displaystyle BG}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/773ca20b2080cb3766062a5451a01d2220e9b067). [Borel's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Borel%27s_theorem "Borel's theorem") describes the [cohomology ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cohomology_ring "Cohomology ring") of the classifying stack.

### Moduli of line bundles

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Quotient_stack&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Moduli of line bundles")]

One of the basic examples of quotient stacks comes from the moduli stack ![Image 16: {\displaystyle B\mathbb {G} _{m}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/85ebc4811253bfca5349df9577c2d3b0b23d9b58) of line bundles ![Image 17: {\displaystyle [*/\mathbb {G} _{m}]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3424e9b5b264bd4bb5ff8db067a682e06323baa) over ![Image 18: {\displaystyle {\text{Sch}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/361ab28a0bd8cfe0331fe93a226feb21d4fd7236), or ![Image 19: {\displaystyle [S/\mathbb {G} _{m}]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dbf618746df81249cee01a144c41a4aaab165e0b) over ![Image 20: {\displaystyle {\text{Sch}}/S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6f8f7f8883cd14e4dd3081f72183668412627c6b) for the trivial ![Image 21: {\displaystyle \mathbb {G} _{m}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/338f0e58c3bbaf1b4f67cd73a3fac1f29c40e39c)-action on ![Image 22: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2). For any scheme (or ![Image 23: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2)-scheme) ![Image 24: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab), the ![Image 25: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab)-points of the moduli stack are the groupoid of principal ![Image 26: {\displaystyle \mathbb {G} _{m}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/338f0e58c3bbaf1b4f67cd73a3fac1f29c40e39c)-bundles ![Image 27: {\displaystyle P\to X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/706af59e3c62c6fa253d5f87c0810d24703b99ff).

### Moduli of line bundles with n-sections

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Quotient_stack&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Moduli of line bundles with n-sections")]

There is another closely related moduli stack given by ![Image 28: {\displaystyle [\mathbb {A} ^{n}/\mathbb {G} _{m}]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/965da9456d85124750027716df5c549d6ca25715) which is the moduli stack of line bundles with ![Image 29: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)-sections. This follows directly from the definition of quotient stacks evaluated on points. For a scheme ![Image 30: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab), the ![Image 31: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab)-points are the groupoid whose objects are given by the set

> ![Image 32: {\displaystyle [\mathbb {A} ^{n}/\mathbb {G} _{m}](X)=\left\{{\begin{matrix}P&\to &\mathbb {A} ^{n}\\\downarrow &&\\X\end{matrix}}:{\begin{aligned}&P\to \mathbb {A} ^{n}{\text{ is }}\mathbb {G} _{m}{\text{ equivariant and}}\\&P\to X{\text{ is a principal }}\mathbb {G} _{m}{\text{-bundle}}\end{aligned}}\right\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e845f04daa50c893f43bf368a6eedada63e5c433)

The morphism in the top row corresponds to the ![Image 33: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)-sections of the associated line bundle over ![Image 34: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab). This can be found by noting giving a ![Image 35: {\displaystyle \mathbb {G} _{m}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/338f0e58c3bbaf1b4f67cd73a3fac1f29c40e39c)-equivariant map ![Image 36: {\displaystyle \phi :P\to \mathbb {A} ^{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ae44433ff7dd7179990f998a83904e41f3afe702) and restricting it to the fiber ![Image 37: {\displaystyle P|_{x}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7f7f4442a95c0ff3461c2c4b015691b4ca2c761b) gives the same data as a section ![Image 38: {\displaystyle \sigma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/59f59b7c3e6fdb1d0365a494b81fb9a696138c36) of the bundle. This can be checked by looking at a chart and sending a point ![Image 39: {\displaystyle x\in X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3e580967f68f36743e894aa7944f032dda6ea01d) to the map ![Image 40: {\displaystyle \phi _{x}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c6c310bb476ad5fc326d5e7153c205c0f4e9d94f), noting the set of ![Image 41: {\displaystyle \mathbb {G} _{m}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/338f0e58c3bbaf1b4f67cd73a3fac1f29c40e39c)-equivariant maps ![Image 42: {\displaystyle P|_{x}\to \mathbb {A} ^{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8a4f8ed5dd8a9ce10971abf683981d4476d626bc) is isomorphic to ![Image 43: {\displaystyle \mathbb {G} _{m}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/338f0e58c3bbaf1b4f67cd73a3fac1f29c40e39c). This construction then globalizes by gluing affine charts together, giving a global section of the bundle. Since ![Image 44: {\displaystyle \mathbb {G} _{m}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/338f0e58c3bbaf1b4f67cd73a3fac1f29c40e39c)-equivariant maps to ![Image 45: {\displaystyle \mathbb {A} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d64ccca0c6fbd18be5d4ec26e26efd89e9c0d76e) is equivalently an ![Image 46: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)-tuple of ![Image 47: {\displaystyle \mathbb {G} _{m}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/338f0e58c3bbaf1b4f67cd73a3fac1f29c40e39c)-equivariant maps to ![Image 48: {\displaystyle \mathbb {A} ^{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/67530a5bd8c23c0be226ac63ddf5f6b2619e682d), the result holds.
