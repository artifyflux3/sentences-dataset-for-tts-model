From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), particularly [linear algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_algebra "Linear algebra"), an **orthonormal basis** for an [inner product space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inner_product_space "Inner product space")![Image 1: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) with finite [dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension_(linear_algebra) "Dimension (linear algebra)") is a [basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basis_(linear_algebra) "Basis (linear algebra)") for ![Image 2: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) whose vectors are [orthonormal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthonormal "Orthonormal"), that is, they are all [unit vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_vector "Unit vector") and [orthogonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonality_(mathematics) "Orthogonality (mathematics)") to each other.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthonormal_basis#cite_note-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthonormal_basis#cite_note-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthonormal_basis#cite_note-3) For example, the [standard basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_basis "Standard basis") for a [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space")![Image 3: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d) is an orthonormal basis, where the relevant inner product is the [dot product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dot_product "Dot product") of vectors. The [image](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Image_(mathematics) "Image (mathematics)") of the standard basis under a [rotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation_(mathematics) "Rotation (mathematics)") or [reflection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflection_(mathematics) "Reflection (mathematics)") (or any [orthogonal transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_transformation "Orthogonal transformation")) is also orthonormal, and every orthonormal basis for ![Image 4: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d) arises in this fashion. An orthonormal basis can be derived from an [orthogonal basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_basis "Orthogonal basis") via [normalization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normalize_(linear_algebra) "Normalize (linear algebra)"). The choice of an [origin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Origin_(mathematics) "Origin (mathematics)") and an orthonormal basis forms a [coordinate frame](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_frame "Coordinate frame") known as an _**orthonormal frame**_.

For a general inner product space ![Image 5: {\displaystyle V,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ace9595e3ce66fdec7e9d30202626accd676b11e) an orthonormal basis can be used to define normalized [orthogonal coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_coordinates "Orthogonal coordinates") on ![Image 6: {\displaystyle V.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1b2661a49b86bd1a5548e527bbfb068aa9f59978) Under these coordinates, the inner product becomes a dot product of vectors. Thus the presence of an orthonormal basis reduces the study of a [finite-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension_(vector_space) "Dimension (vector space)") inner product space to the study of ![Image 7: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d) under the dot product. Every finite-dimensional inner product space has an orthonormal basis, which may be obtained from an arbitrary basis using the [Gram–Schmidt process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gram%E2%80%93Schmidt_process "Gram–Schmidt process").

In [functional analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functional_analysis "Functional analysis"), the concept of an orthonormal basis can be generalized to arbitrary (infinite-dimensional) [inner product spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inner_product_space "Inner product space").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthonormal_basis#cite_note-4) Given a pre-Hilbert space ![Image 8: {\displaystyle H,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ef601e1519093ba6c2944b945882c119f990e704) an _orthonormal basis_ for ![Image 9: {\displaystyle H}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/75a9edddcca2f782014371f75dca39d7e13a9c1b) is an orthonormal set of vectors with the property that every vector in ![Image 10: {\displaystyle H}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/75a9edddcca2f782014371f75dca39d7e13a9c1b) can be written as an [infinite linear combination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinite_linear_combination "Infinite linear combination") of the vectors in the basis. In this case, the orthonormal basis is sometimes called a **Hilbert basis** for ![Image 11: {\displaystyle H.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8933ae7244305ae7824aa18e077d1cf946e2ee9d) Note that an orthonormal basis in this sense is not generally a [Hamel basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamel_basis "Hamel basis"), since infinite linear combinations are required.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthonormal_basis#cite_note-FOOTNOTERoman2008218ch._9-5) Specifically, the [linear span](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_span "Linear span") of the basis must be [dense](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dense_set "Dense set") in ![Image 12: {\displaystyle H,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ef601e1519093ba6c2944b945882c119f990e704) although not necessarily the entire space.

If we go on to [Hilbert spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space "Hilbert space"), a non-orthonormal set of vectors having the same linear span as an orthonormal basis may not be a basis at all. For instance, any [square-integrable function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square-integrable_function "Square-integrable function") on the interval ![Image 13: {\displaystyle [-1,1]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/51e3b7f14a6f70e614728c583409a0b9a8b9de01) can be expressed ([almost everywhere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Almost_everywhere "Almost everywhere")) as an infinite sum of [Legendre polynomials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legendre_polynomials "Legendre polynomials") (an orthonormal basis), but not necessarily as an infinite sum of the [monomials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monomial "Monomial")![Image 14: {\displaystyle x^{n}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/db799005f97162ed8244bbded6d5bf67cda6f01c)

A different generalisation is to pseudo-inner product spaces, finite-dimensional vector spaces ![Image 15: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) equipped with a non-degenerate [symmetric bilinear form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetric_bilinear_form "Symmetric bilinear form") known as the [metric tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_tensor "Metric tensor"). In such a basis, the metric takes the form ![Image 16: {\displaystyle {\text{diag}}(+1,\cdots ,+1,-1,\cdots ,-1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8db0d60a6f5b0dc212604a4e9ad00eb0ec73af65) with ![Image 17: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) positive ones and ![Image 18: {\displaystyle q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/06809d64fa7c817ffc7e323f85997f783dbdf71d) negative ones.

*   For ![Image 19: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5), the set of vectors ![Image 20: {\displaystyle \left\{\mathbf {e_{1}} ={\begin{pmatrix}1&0&0\end{pmatrix}}\ ,\ \mathbf {e_{2}} ={\begin{pmatrix}0&1&0\end{pmatrix}}\ ,\ \mathbf {e_{3}} ={\begin{pmatrix}0&0&1\end{pmatrix}}\right\},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/074086d5577181b73d78c6222d134e4185484f0b) is called the **standard basis** and forms an orthonormal basis of ![Image 21: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5) with respect to the standard dot product. Note that both the standard basis and standard dot product rely on viewing ![Image 22: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5) as the [Cartesian product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_product "Cartesian product")![Image 23: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} \times \mathbb {R} \times \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/17007c98c10928212ba41ac10c3c2d494477a2de)**Proof:** A straightforward computation shows that the inner products of these vectors equals zero, ![Image 24: {\displaystyle \left\langle \mathbf {e_{1}} ,\mathbf {e_{2}} \right\rangle =\left\langle \mathbf {e_{1}} ,\mathbf {e_{3}} \right\rangle =\left\langle \mathbf {e_{2}} ,\mathbf {e_{3}} \right\rangle =0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7f908325daf38b02ff64d21971e18c53046ba420) and that each of their magnitudes equals one, ![Image 25: {\displaystyle \left\|\mathbf {e_{1}} \right\|=\left\|\mathbf {e_{2}} \right\|=\left\|\mathbf {e_{3}} \right\|=1.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/618d151bfd0c519ba693afa62b0dd2d8ee148707) This means that ![Image 26: {\displaystyle \left\{\mathbf {e_{1}} ,\mathbf {e_{2}} ,\mathbf {e_{3}} \right\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ed678a2e43297eecb06b85eb82e97030fc66d322) is an orthonormal set. All vectors ![Image 27: {\displaystyle (\mathbf {x} ,\mathbf {y} ,\mathbf {z} )\in \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0f21548a996456e795687909239a26731262cf55) can be expressed as a sum of the basis vectors scaled ![Image 28: {\displaystyle (\mathbf {x} ,\mathbf {y} ,\mathbf {z} )=\mathbf {xe_{1}} +\mathbf {ye_{2}} +\mathbf {ze_{3}} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f2a5b78c478b6bc9aac33b535be3deca1ceec754) so ![Image 29: {\displaystyle \left\{\mathbf {e_{1}} ,\mathbf {e_{2}} ,\mathbf {e_{3}} \right\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ed678a2e43297eecb06b85eb82e97030fc66d322) spans ![Image 30: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5) and hence must be a basis. It may also be shown that the standard basis rotated about an axis through the origin or reflected in a plane through the origin also forms an orthonormal basis of ![Image 31: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5).
*   For ![Image 32: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d), the standard basis and inner product are similarly defined. Any other orthonormal basis is related to the standard basis by an [orthogonal transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_transformation "Orthogonal transformation") in the group O(n).
*   For [pseudo-Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudo-Euclidean_space "Pseudo-Euclidean space")![Image 33: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{p,q},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2fcfda002aa3c715b50b639cd3e789213ebdccb0), an orthogonal basis ![Image 34: {\displaystyle \{e_{\mu }\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/93430449eb9d5fa3ecd91e11fcfabc0d9273c934) with metric ![Image 35: {\displaystyle \eta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e4d701857cf5fbec133eebaf94deadf722537f64) instead satisfies ![Image 36: {\displaystyle \eta (e_{\mu },e_{\nu })=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/15286260ecbfe2aa965b7a8ad76dc465f5099e72) if ![Image 37: {\displaystyle \mu \neq \nu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ee4379369da2c78ddd6bc9fe97117b575bb5eeb5), ![Image 38: {\displaystyle \eta (e_{\mu },e_{\mu })=+1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/263682c52fbab63f6d1fc64a8c72933eb45dcd59) if ![Image 39: {\displaystyle 1\leq \mu \leq p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87de4722d1f8be3830790191997044fa00670134), and ![Image 40: {\displaystyle \eta (e_{\mu },e_{\mu })=-1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ccd77f0a589de7c017ed61022853763b60860d50) if ![Image 41: {\displaystyle p+1\leq \mu \leq p+q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9711edfeced3556dbf5f3d9a820ccc390977ccdc). Any two orthonormal bases are related by a pseudo-orthogonal transformation. In the case ![Image 42: {\displaystyle (p,q)=(1,3)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1cea63006c0d34f045e07917d6bceb3312085c61), these are Lorentz transformations.
*   The set ![Image 43: {\displaystyle \left\{f_{n}:n\in \mathbb {Z} \right\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/29da3b6672f51115652865da3fd6b721eb2ecc47) with ![Image 44: {\displaystyle f_{n}(x)=\exp(2\pi inx),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/162253164b6d3a8698bdec951590185bb7e6413f) where ![Image 45: {\displaystyle \exp }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4d1185b570f67b4221307626254f64f9e619e769) denotes the [exponential function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponential_function "Exponential function"), forms an orthonormal basis of the space of functions with finite Lebesgue integrals, ![Image 46: {\displaystyle L^{2}([0,1]),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6da2a14151b01996b4df887f1ca1f3e8a044ab5c) with respect to the [2-norm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2-norm "2-norm"). This is fundamental to the study of [Fourier series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourier_series "Fourier series").
*   The set ![Image 47: {\displaystyle \left\{e_{b}:b\in B\right\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dde4734c4f80913a64ff714d7c39bd62d0938819) with ![Image 48: {\displaystyle e_{b}(c)=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/be29575802d2105d5c07b5f4a93b3beb4842e0b5) if ![Image 49: {\displaystyle b=c}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b133a00dc90e54130a96482c99750f845feb955e) and ![Image 50: {\displaystyle e_{b}(c)=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/680a18ed9dd945985682edebda8f66dfd127bd26) otherwise forms an orthonormal basis of ![Image 51: {\displaystyle \ell ^{2}(B).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c7f2b369701b870aadf6448df80da613823736be)
*   [Eigenfunctions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eigenfunction "Eigenfunction") of a [Sturm–Liouville eigenproblem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sturm%E2%80%93Liouville_eigenproblem "Sturm–Liouville eigenproblem").
*   The [column vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Column_vectors "Column vectors") of an [orthogonal matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_matrix "Orthogonal matrix") form an orthonormal set.

If ![Image 52: {\displaystyle B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47136aad860d145f75f3eed3022df827cee94d7a) is an orthogonal basis of ![Image 53: {\displaystyle H,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ef601e1519093ba6c2944b945882c119f990e704) then every element ![Image 54: {\displaystyle x\in H}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0957496d2596a81d84e50252c806c5ae488396) may be written as ![Image 55: {\displaystyle x=\sum _{b\in B}{\frac {\langle x,b\rangle }{\lVert b\rVert ^{2}}}b.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/17dcc7027fe88100517ed0027bd9a26d4de0f261)

When ![Image 56: {\displaystyle B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47136aad860d145f75f3eed3022df827cee94d7a) is orthonormal, this simplifies to ![Image 57: {\displaystyle x=\sum _{b\in B}\langle x,b\rangle b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c55e80b32d41762fb8b0da0da9bc701b33799a47) and the square of the [norm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norm_(mathematics) "Norm (mathematics)") of ![Image 58: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) can be given by ![Image 59: {\displaystyle \|x\|^{2}=\sum _{b\in B}|\langle x,b\rangle |^{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/081e8da0559e3ed0b9636274bb38020c96714538)

Even if ![Image 60: {\displaystyle B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47136aad860d145f75f3eed3022df827cee94d7a) is [uncountable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uncountable_set "Uncountable set"), only countably many terms in this sum will be non-zero, and the expression is therefore well-defined. This sum is also called the _[Fourier expansion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generalized\_Fourier\_series "Generalized Fourier series")_ of ![Image 61: {\displaystyle x,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/feff4d40084c7351bf57b11ba2427f6331f5bdbe) and the formula is usually known as [Parseval's identity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parseval%27s_identity "Parseval's identity").

If ![Image 62: {\displaystyle B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47136aad860d145f75f3eed3022df827cee94d7a) is an orthonormal basis of ![Image 63: {\displaystyle H,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ef601e1519093ba6c2944b945882c119f990e704) then ![Image 64: {\displaystyle H}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/75a9edddcca2f782014371f75dca39d7e13a9c1b) is _isomorphic_ to ![Image 65: {\displaystyle \ell ^{2}(B)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6ec10dbcecdcb7ede7aadbea8f75b99109e08705) in the following sense: there exists a [bijective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bijective "Bijective")[linear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_operator "Linear operator") map ![Image 66: {\displaystyle \Phi :H\to \ell ^{2}(B)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/431c308fb6fc9c22b7386b4d6b4a7cd02c78e113) such that ![Image 67: {\displaystyle \langle \Phi (x),\Phi (y)\rangle =\langle x,y\rangle \ \ \forall \ x,y\in H.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/603bf1dea55d74adce2c4206b56c08beaa5dae10)

A set ![Image 68: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2) of mutually orthonormal vectors in a Hilbert space ![Image 69: {\displaystyle H}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/75a9edddcca2f782014371f75dca39d7e13a9c1b) is called an orthonormal system. An orthonormal basis is an orthonormal system with the additional property that the linear span of ![Image 70: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2) is dense in ![Image 71: {\displaystyle H}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/75a9edddcca2f782014371f75dca39d7e13a9c1b).[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthonormal_basis#cite_note-FOOTNOTESteinwartChristmann2008503-6) Alternatively, the set ![Image 72: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2) can be regarded as either _complete_ or _incomplete_ with respect to ![Image 73: {\displaystyle H}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/75a9edddcca2f782014371f75dca39d7e13a9c1b). That is, we can take the smallest closed linear subspace ![Image 74: {\displaystyle V\subseteq H}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/183358eadafb771ef78f682bfa4feeb4b692f9fe) containing ![Image 75: {\displaystyle S.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/23bbb1f0f6ebdfa78b4fed06049640f7386bb44b) Then ![Image 76: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2) will be an orthonormal basis of ![Image 77: {\displaystyle V;}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8b8f359760e0256e606ef13df4b6faf0218752bc) which may of course be smaller than ![Image 78: {\displaystyle H}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/75a9edddcca2f782014371f75dca39d7e13a9c1b) itself, being an _incomplete_ orthonormal set, or be ![Image 79: {\displaystyle H,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ef601e1519093ba6c2944b945882c119f990e704) when it is a _complete_ orthonormal set.

Using [Zorn's lemma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zorn%27s_lemma "Zorn's lemma") and the [Gram–Schmidt process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gram%E2%80%93Schmidt_process "Gram–Schmidt process") (or more simply well-ordering and transfinite recursion), one can show that _every_ Hilbert space admits an orthonormal basis;[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthonormal_basis#cite_note-7) furthermore, any two orthonormal bases of the same space have the same [cardinality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardinal_number "Cardinal number") (this can be proven in a manner akin to that of the proof of the usual [dimension theorem for vector spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension_theorem_for_vector_spaces "Dimension theorem for vector spaces"), with separate cases depending on whether the larger basis candidate is countable or not). A Hilbert space is [separable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separable_metric_space "Separable metric space") if and only if it admits a [countable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Countable "Countable") orthonormal basis. (One can prove this last statement without using the [axiom of choice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiom_of_choice "Axiom of choice"). However, one would have to use the [axiom of countable choice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiom_of_countable_choice "Axiom of countable choice").)

Choice of basis as a choice of isomorphism
------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Orthonormal_basis&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Choice of basis as a choice of isomorphism")]

For concreteness we discuss orthonormal bases for a real, ![Image 80: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)-dimensional vector space ![Image 81: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) with a positive definite symmetric bilinear form ![Image 82: {\displaystyle \phi =\langle \cdot ,\cdot \rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cdf6668156b47e2191fb353ec4ac052abb4f7fec).

One way to view an orthonormal basis with respect to ![Image 83: {\displaystyle \phi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/72b1f30316670aee6270a28334bdf4f5072cdde4) is as a set of vectors ![Image 84: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {B}}=\{e_{i}\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b845337cc859ea4a6ffaafdefeeb9a94d0ec21c0), which allow us to write ![Image 85: {\displaystyle v=v^{i}e_{i}\ \ \forall \ v\in V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e25cbd4c37dd9bf487f8e07891f064a9c775a815) , and ![Image 86: {\displaystyle v^{i}\in \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a02e472b8f4db7b32a9d8aafb55f93b209b7c566) or ![Image 87: {\displaystyle (v^{i})\in \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/479ba7054ac353df9da28950d2ac66ea88065b2c). With respect to this basis, the components of ![Image 88: {\displaystyle \phi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/72b1f30316670aee6270a28334bdf4f5072cdde4) are particularly simple: ![Image 89: {\displaystyle \phi (e_{i},e_{j})=\delta _{ij}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/874ee29f8d6941ee49d7ccd53afa9d403ff63738) (where ![Image 90: {\displaystyle \delta _{ij}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fa75d04c11480d976e1396951e02cbb3c4f71568) is the [Kronecker delta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kronecker_delta "Kronecker delta")).

We can now view the basis as a map ![Image 91: {\displaystyle \psi _{\mathcal {B}}:V\rightarrow \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b366ebebc1842587a94271b06421abe9be564776) which is an isomorphism of inner product spaces: to make this more explicit we can write

![Image 92: {\displaystyle \psi _{\mathcal {B}}:(V,\phi )\rightarrow (\mathbb {R} ^{n},\delta _{ij}).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/617d5cc351cfa7a16e6c8efbd9936f2830d64fad)
Explicitly we can write ![Image 93: {\displaystyle (\psi _{\mathcal {B}}(v))^{i}=e^{i}(v)=\phi (e_{i},v)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4db0c57173de447aa161b47809ac839ffb2669db) where ![Image 94: {\displaystyle e^{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b410dd911800d0baf0c9bc3ab949d910cb216a4d) is the dual basis element to ![Image 95: {\displaystyle e_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ebdc3a9cb1583d3204eff8918b558c293e0d2cf3).

The inverse is a component map

![Image 96: {\displaystyle C_{\mathcal {B}}:\mathbb {R} ^{n}\rightarrow V,(v^{i})\mapsto \sum _{i=1}^{n}v^{i}e_{i}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5775dcae9064f67f0544aa3d87d0c5fe82ae0007)
These definitions make it manifest that there is a bijection

![Image 97: {\displaystyle \{{\text{Space of orthogonal bases }}{\mathcal {B}}\}\leftrightarrow \{{\text{Space of isomorphisms }}V\leftrightarrow \mathbb {R} ^{n}\}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bd9443ca81a6aa84fb5e03d84c47577d023de3c7)
The space of isomorphisms admits actions of orthogonal groups at either the ![Image 98: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) side or the ![Image 99: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d) side. For concreteness we fix the isomorphisms to point in the direction ![Image 100: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}\rightarrow V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e003aa2c76e38801750d89996ca568fc0a242511), and consider the space of such maps, ![Image 101: {\displaystyle {\text{Iso}}(\mathbb {R} ^{n}\rightarrow V)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/63120aa31b7e9a849670d158c39b5c3fcf6df13f).

This space admits a left action by the group of isometries of ![Image 102: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845), that is, ![Image 103: {\displaystyle R\in {\text{GL}}(V)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cc46db9ed1cd7892018cedddcedd6c883fd1fc44) such that ![Image 104: {\displaystyle \phi (\cdot ,\cdot )=\phi (R\cdot ,R\cdot )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/649e417897f85c5d2f275752379eebdda224c3e7), with the action given by composition: ![Image 105: {\displaystyle R*C=R\circ C.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f24b700d066a5af19289c9cd24e95399b3e98066)

This space also admits a right action by the group of isometries of ![Image 106: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d), that is, ![Image 107: {\displaystyle R_{ij}\in {\text{O}}(n)\subset {\text{Mat}}_{n\times n}(\mathbb {R} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35dc2c7c3e90ddb3d0273ae1585e8de8e490e8b0), with the action again given by composition: ![Image 108: {\displaystyle C*R_{ij}=C\circ R_{ij}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/00399d72c862ffda2de4eb414a5c29de15fc02e4).

As a principal homogeneous space
--------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Orthonormal_basis&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: As a principal homogeneous space")]

The set of orthonormal bases for ![Image 109: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d) with the standard inner product is a [principal homogeneous space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principal_homogeneous_space "Principal homogeneous space") or G-torsor for the [orthogonal group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_group "Orthogonal group")![Image 110: {\displaystyle G={\text{O}}(n),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7df8632696442815de17a13bbda7bf446d8cf6ca) and is called the [Stiefel manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stiefel_manifold "Stiefel manifold")![Image 111: {\displaystyle V_{n}(\mathbb {R} ^{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8c57f3d97a7881ba38c0d771864a8f6444bb2a9f) of orthonormal [![Image 112: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)-frames](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K-frame "K-frame").[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthonormal_basis#cite_note-8)

In other words, the space of orthonormal bases is like the orthogonal group, but without a choice of base point: given the space of orthonormal bases, there is no natural choice of orthonormal basis, but once one is given one, there is a one-to-one correspondence between bases and the orthogonal group. Concretely, a linear map is determined by where it sends a given basis: just as an invertible map can take any basis to any other basis, an orthogonal map can take any _orthogonal_ basis to any other _orthogonal_ basis.

The other Stiefel manifolds ![Image 113: {\displaystyle V_{k}(\mathbb {R} ^{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6b4afe29294bfe05c590fd01f03d584ef3b46f8d) for ![Image 114: {\displaystyle k<n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/48ab7000a6f47e3a09a79dcbe31b89272b0c1f1f) of _incomplete_ orthonormal bases (orthonormal ![Image 115: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40)-frames) are still homogeneous spaces for the orthogonal group, but not _principal_ homogeneous spaces: any ![Image 116: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40)-frame can be taken to any other ![Image 117: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40)-frame by an orthogonal map, but this map is not uniquely determined.
