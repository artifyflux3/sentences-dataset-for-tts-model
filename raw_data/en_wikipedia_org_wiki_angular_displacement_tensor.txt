With this assumption, the equations of continuum mechanics are considerably simplified. This approach may also be called small deformation theory , small displacement theory , or small displacement-gradient theory . It is contrasted with the finite strain theory where the opposite assumption is made.

The infinitesimal strain theory is commonly adopted in civil and mechanical engineering for the stress analysis of structures built from relatively stiff elastic materials like concrete and steel , since a common goal in the design of such structures is to minimize their deformation under typical loads . However, this approximation demands caution in the case of thin flexible bodies, such as rods, plates, and shells which are susceptible to significant rotations, thus making the results unreliable.

state in a continuum.

In real engineering components, stress (and strain) are 3-D tensors but in prismatic structures such as a long metal billet, the length of the structure is much greater than the other two dimensions. The strains associated with length, i.e., the normal strain ε ε 33 {\displaystyle \varepsilon _{33}} and the shear strains ε ε 13 {\displaystyle \varepsilon _{13}} and ε ε 23 {\displaystyle \varepsilon _{23}} (if the length is the 3-direction) are constrained by nearby material and are small compared to the cross-sectional strains . Plane strain is then an acceptable approximation. The strain tensor for plane strain is written as: ε ε _ _ _ _ = [ ε ε 11 ε ε 12 0 ε ε 21 ε ε 22 0 0 0 0 ] {\displaystyle {\underline {\underline {\boldsymbol {\varepsilon }}}}={\begin{bmatrix}\varepsilon _{11}&\varepsilon _{12}&0\\\varepsilon _{21}&\varepsilon _{22}&0\\0&0&0\end{bmatrix}}} in which the double underline indicates a second order tensor . This strain state is called plane strain . The corresponding stress tensor is: σ σ _ _ _ _ = [ σ σ 11 σ σ 12 0 σ σ 21 σ σ 22 0 0 0 σ σ 33 ] {\displaystyle {\underline {\underline {\boldsymbol {\sigma }}}}={\begin{bmatrix}\sigma _{11}&\sigma _{12}&0\\\sigma _{21}&\sigma _{22}&0\\0&0&\sigma _{33}\end{bmatrix}}} in which the non-zero σ σ 33 {\displaystyle \sigma _{33}} is needed to maintain the constraint ϵ ϵ 33 = 0 {\displaystyle \epsilon _{33}=0} . This stress term can be temporarily removed from the analysis to leave only the in-plane terms, effectively reducing the 3-D problem to a much simpler 2-D problem.
