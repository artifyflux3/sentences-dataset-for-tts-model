[Rectangular parallelepiped](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangular_parallelepiped "Rectangular parallelepiped"). The line through segment AD and the line through segment B 1 B are skew lines because they are not in the same plane.

In [three-dimensional geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_geometry "Three-dimensional geometry"), **skew lines** are two [lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_(geometry) "Line (geometry)") that do not [intersect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line-line_intersection "Line-line intersection") and are not [parallel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_(geometry) "Parallel (geometry)"). A simple example of a pair of skew lines is the pair of lines through opposite edges of a [regular tetrahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_tetrahedron "Regular tetrahedron"). Two lines that both lie in the same plane must either cross each other or be parallel, so skew lines can exist only in three or more [dimensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension "Dimension"). Two lines are skew if and only if they are not [coplanar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coplanar "Coplanar").

If four points are chosen at random [uniformly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uniform_distribution_(continuous) "Uniform distribution (continuous)") within a unit [cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube "Cube"), they will [almost surely](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Almost_surely "Almost surely") define a pair of skew lines. After the first three points have been chosen, the fourth point will define a non-skew line if, and only if, it is coplanar with the first three points. However, the plane through the first three points forms a subset of measure zero of the cube, and the probability that the fourth point lies on this plane is zero. If it does not, the lines defined by the points will be skew.

Similarly, in three-dimensional space a very small perturbation of any two parallel or intersecting lines will almost certainly turn them into skew lines. Therefore, any four points in [general position](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_position "General position") always form skew lines.

In this sense, skew lines are the "usual" case, and parallel or intersecting lines are special cases.

More than two lines
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Skew_lines&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: More than two lines")]

A _configuration_ of skew lines is a set of lines in which all pairs are skew. Two configurations are said to be _isotopic_ if it is possible to continuously transform one configuration into the other, maintaining throughout the transformation the invariant that all pairs of lines remain skew. Any two configurations of two lines are easily seen to be isotopic, and configurations of the same number of lines in dimensions higher than three are always isotopic, but there exist multiple non-isotopic configurations of three or more lines in three dimensions.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skew_lines#cite_note-viro-viro-2) The number of nonisotopic configurations of _n_ lines in **R**3, starting at _n_ = 1, is

1, 1, 2, 3, 7, 19, 74, ... (sequence [A110887](https://oeis.org/A110887 "oeis:A110887") in the [OEIS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/On-Line_Encyclopedia_of_Integer_Sequences "On-Line Encyclopedia of Integer Sequences")).[![Image 23](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Nested_hyperboloids.png/330px-Nested_hyperboloids.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Nested_hyperboloids.png)

A [fibration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiber_bundle "Fiber bundle") of [projective space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_space "Projective space") by skew lines on nested [hyperboloids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperboloid "Hyperboloid").

An [affine transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_transformation "Affine transformation") of this ruled surface produces a surface which in general has an elliptical cross-section rather than the circular cross-section produced by rotating L around L'; such surfaces are also called hyperboloids of one sheet, and again are ruled by two families of mutually skew lines. A third type of ruled surface is the [hyperbolic paraboloid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_paraboloid "Hyperbolic paraboloid"). Like the hyperboloid of one sheet, the hyperbolic paraboloid has two families of skew lines; in each of the two families the lines are parallel to a common plane although not to each other. Any three skew lines in **R**3 lie on exactly one ruled surface of one of these types.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skew_lines#cite_note-hilbert-cohn-vossen-3)

If three skew lines all meet three other skew lines, any transversal of the first set of three meets any transversal of the second set.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skew_lines#cite_note-coxeter-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skew_lines#cite_note-galluci-5)

Skew flats in higher dimensions
-------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Skew_lines&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Skew flats in higher dimensions")]

In higher-dimensional space, a [flat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flat_(geometry) "Flat (geometry)") of dimension _k_ is referred to as a _k_-flat. Thus, a line may also be called a 1-flat.

Generalizing the concept of _skew lines_ to _d_-dimensional space, an _i_-flat and a _j_-flat may be **skew** if _i_ + _j_<_d_.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skew_lines#cite_note-6) As with lines in 3-space, skew flats are those that are neither parallel nor intersect.

In [affine _d_-space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_geometry "Affine geometry"), two flats of any dimension may be parallel. However, in [projective space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_geometry "Projective geometry"), parallelism does not exist; two flats must either intersect or be skew. Let _I_ be the set of points on an _i_-flat, and let _J_ be the set of points on a _j_-flat. In projective _d_-space, if _i_ + _j_ ≥ _d_ then the intersection of _I_ and _J_ must contain a (_i_+_j_−_d_)-flat. (A _0_-flat is a point.)

In either geometry, if _I_ and _J_ intersect at a _k_-flat, for _k_ ≥ 0, then the points of _I_ ∪ _J_ determine a (_i_+_j_−_k_)-flat.
