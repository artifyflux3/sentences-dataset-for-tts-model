The **newton-metre** or **newton-meter** (also non-hyphenated, **newton metre** or **newton meter**; symbol **N⋅m**[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton-metre#cite_note-BIPM-SI-5.1-1) or **N m**[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton-metre#cite_note-BIPM-SI-5.1-1))[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton-metre#cite_note-2) is the unit of [torque](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torque "Torque") (also called _[moment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment\_(physics) "Moment (physics)")_) in the [International System of Units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Units "International System of Units") (SI). One newton-metre is equal to the torque resulting from a [force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Force "Force") of one [newton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton_(unit) "Newton (unit)") applied perpendicularly to the end of a [moment arm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_arm "Moment arm") that is one [metre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metre "Metre") long.

The unit is also used less commonly as a unit of [work](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Work_(physics) "Work (physics)"), or [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy "Energy"), in which case it is equivalent to the more common and standard SI unit of energy, the [joule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joule "Joule").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton-metre#cite_note-Eshbach-3) In this usage the metre term represents the distance travelled or displacement [in the direction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dot_product#Physics "Dot product") of the force, and not the [perpendicular distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product#Mechanics "Cross product") from a fulcrum (i.e. the lever arm length) as it does when used to express torque. This usage is generally discouraged,[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton-metre#cite_note-4) since it can lead to confusion as to whether a given quantity expressed in newton-metres is a torque or a quantity of energy.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton-metre#cite_note-bipm222-5) "Even though torque has the same dimension as energy (SI unit joule), the joule is never used for expressing torque".[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton-metre#cite_note-bipm222-5)

Newton-metres and joules are [dimensionally](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimensional_analysis "Dimensional analysis") equivalent in the sense that they have the same expression in [SI base units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SI_base_unit "SI base unit"),

1 N⋅m=1 kg⋅m 2 s 2,1 J=1 k g⋅m 2 s 2{\displaystyle 1\,{\text{N}}{\cdot }\mathrm {m} =1\,{\frac {{\text{kg}}{\cdot }{\text{m}}^{2}}{{\text{s}}^{2}}}\quad ,\quad 1\,\mathrm {J} =1\,{\frac {\mathrm {kg} {\cdot }\mathrm {m} ^{2}}{\mathrm {s} ^{2}}}}![Image 7: {\displaystyle 1\,{\text{N}}{\cdot }\mathrm {m} =1\,{\frac {{\text{kg}}{\cdot }{\text{m}}^{2}}{{\text{s}}^{2}}}\quad ,\quad 1\,\mathrm {J} =1\,{\frac {\mathrm {kg} {\cdot }\mathrm {m} ^{2}}{\mathrm {s} ^{2}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6dccc2ccc1dd1996e2184d14ecccbaa5143084f9)
but are distinguished in terms of applicable [kind of quantity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kind_of_quantity "Kind of quantity"), to avoid misunderstandings when a torque is mistaken for an energy or vice versa. Similar examples of dimensionally equivalent units include [Pa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pascal_(unit) "Pascal (unit)") versus J/m 3, [Bq](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Becquerel "Becquerel") versus [Hz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hertz "Hertz"), and [ohm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ohm "Ohm") versus [ohm per square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sheet_resistance#Units "Sheet resistance").

Conversion factors
------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Newton-metre&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Conversion factors")]

*   1 [kilogram-force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kilogram-force "Kilogram-force") metre = [9.80665](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_gravity "Standard gravity") N⋅m[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton-metre#cite_note-6)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton-metre#cite_note-7)
*   1 newton-metre ≈ 0.73756215 [pound-force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pound-foot_(torque) "Pound-foot (torque)")-feet
*   1 [pound-foot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pound-foot_(torque) "Pound-foot (torque)") ≡ 1 [pound-force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pound_(force) "Pound (force)")-foot ≈ 1.35581795 N⋅m
*   1 ounce-inch ≡ 1 [ounce-force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ounce-force "Ounce-force")-inch ≈ 7.06155181 mN⋅m (millinewton-metres)
*   1 [dyne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dyne "Dyne")-centimetre = 10−7 N⋅m
