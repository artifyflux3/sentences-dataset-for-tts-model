The cross-hatched plane is the linear span of **u** and **v** in both **R**2 and **R**3, here shown in [perspective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perspective_(graphical) "Perspective (graphical)").

In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), the **linear span** (also called the **linear hull**[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_span#cite_note-1) or just **span**) of a [set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_(mathematics) "Set (mathematics)")![Image 2: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2) of elements of a [vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space")![Image 3: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) is the smallest [linear subspace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_subspace "Linear subspace") of ![Image 4: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) that contains ![Image 5: {\displaystyle S.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/23bbb1f0f6ebdfa78b4fed06049640f7386bb44b) It is the set of all finite [linear combinations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_combination "Linear combination") of the elements of S,[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_span#cite_note-2) and the intersection of all linear subspaces that contain ![Image 6: {\displaystyle S.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/23bbb1f0f6ebdfa78b4fed06049640f7386bb44b) It is often denoted span(_S_)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_span#cite_note-:0-3) or ![Image 7: {\displaystyle \langle S\rangle .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3e4ebebb1ab0a24cd75f3c2cf013bb4a63ed6709)

For example, in [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry"), two [linearly independent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linearly_independent "Linearly independent")[vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_(geometry) "Vector (geometry)") span a [plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_(geometry) "Plane (geometry)").

To express that a vector space V is a linear span of a subset S, one commonly uses one of the following phrases: S spans V; S is a **spanning set** of V; V is spanned or [generated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generator_(mathematics) "Generator (mathematics)") by S; S is a generator set or a generating set of V.

Spans can be generalized to many [mathematical structures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_structure "Mathematical structure"), in which case, the smallest substructure containing ![Image 8: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2) is generally called the substructure _generated_ by ![Image 9: {\displaystyle S.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/23bbb1f0f6ebdfa78b4fed06049640f7386bb44b)

Given a [vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space")V over a [field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)")K, the span of a [set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_(mathematics) "Set (mathematics)")S of vectors (not necessarily finite) is defined to be the intersection W of all [subspaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_subspace "Linear subspace") of V that contain S. It is thus the smallest (for [set inclusion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_inclusion "Set inclusion")) subspace containing S. It is referred to as the subspace _spanned by_ S, or by the vectors in S. Conversely, S is called a _spanning set_ of W, and we say that S _spans_ W.

It follows from this definition that the span of S is the set of all finite [linear combinations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_combinations "Linear combinations") of elements (vectors) of S, and can be defined as such.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_span#cite_note-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_span#cite_note-:02-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_span#cite_note-6) That is, ![Image 10: {\displaystyle \operatorname {span} (S)={\biggl \{}\lambda _{1}\mathbf {v} _{1}+\lambda _{2}\mathbf {v} _{2}+\cdots +\lambda _{n}\mathbf {v} _{n}\mid n\in \mathbb {N} ,\;\mathbf {v} _{1},...\mathbf {v} _{n}\in S,\;\lambda _{1},...\lambda _{n}\in K{\biggr \}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a2e43654c3b22ce5c3ede260cfe383ca858030a9)

When S is [empty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empty_set "Empty set"), the only possibility is _n_ = 0, and the previous expression for ![Image 11: {\displaystyle \operatorname {span} (S)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/185bcd5416275cd32c9de5427eb20e1b55153f54) reduces to the [empty sum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empty_sum "Empty sum").[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_span#cite_note-7) The standard convention for the empty sum implies thus ![Image 12: {\displaystyle {\text{span}}(\emptyset )=\{\mathbf {0} \},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c12ffe204597126111e251ad8f609ff5ae326c84) a property that is immediate with the other definitions. However, many introductory textbooks simply include this fact as part of the definition.

When ![Image 13: {\displaystyle S=\{\mathbf {v} _{1},\ldots ,\mathbf {v} _{n}\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e0ac0bae489d45dac001f26c0c52c06b15f26050) is [finite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_set "Finite set"), one has ![Image 14: {\displaystyle \operatorname {span} (S)=\{\lambda _{1}\mathbf {v} _{1}+\lambda _{2}\mathbf {v} _{2}+\cdots +\lambda _{n}\mathbf {v} _{n}\mid \lambda _{1},...\lambda _{n}\in K\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f26ec80a76de02dfd4afa480939109354f55799e)

The [real](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number") vector space ![Image 15: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5) has {(−1, 0, 0), (0, 1, 0), (0, 0, 1)} as a spanning set. This particular spanning set is also a [basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basis_(linear_algebra) "Basis (linear algebra)"). If (−1, 0, 0) were replaced by (1, 0, 0), it would also form the [canonical basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_basis "Standard basis") of ![Image 16: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5).

Another spanning set for the same space is given by {(1, 2, 3), (0, 1, 2), (−1, 1⁄2, 3), (1, 1, 1)}, but this set is not a basis, because it is [linearly dependent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_dependency "Linear dependency").

The set {(1, 0, 0), (0, 1, 0), (1, 1, 0)} is not a spanning set of ![Image 17: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5), since its span is the space of all vectors in ![Image 18: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5) whose last component is zero. That space is also spanned by the set {(1, 0, 0), (0, 1, 0)}, as (1, 1, 0) is a linear combination of (1, 0, 0) and (0, 1, 0). Thus, the spanned space is not ![Image 19: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b00b2b4fd27c2cbffa02df568472f77b194a6db9) It can be identified with ![Image 20: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e150115ab9f63023215109595b76686a1ff890fd) by removing the third components equal to zero.

The empty set is a spanning set of {(0, 0, 0)}, since the empty set is a subset of all possible vector spaces in ![Image 21: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5), and {(0, 0, 0)} is the intersection of all of these vector spaces.

The set of [monomials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monomial "Monomial")x n, where n is a non-negative integer, spans the space of [polynomials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial "Polynomial").

### Equivalence of definitions

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Linear_span&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Equivalence of definitions")]

The set of all linear combinations of a subset S of V, a vector space over K, is the smallest linear subspace of V containing S.

_Proof._ We first prove that span _S_ is a subspace of V. Since S is a subset of V, we only need to prove the existence of a zero vector **0** in span _S_, that span _S_ is closed under addition, and that span _S_ is closed under scalar multiplication. Letting ![Image 22: {\displaystyle S=\{\mathbf {v} _{1},\mathbf {v} _{2},\ldots ,\mathbf {v} _{n}\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/14fe8d8f5069f14591e002c65c22e1d4acd21cc3), it is trivial that the zero vector of V exists in span _S_, since ![Image 23: {\displaystyle \mathbf {0} =0\mathbf {v} _{1}+0\mathbf {v} _{2}+\cdots +0\mathbf {v} _{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c12559ade30676d8f91f0ed5280081ab6397ddb2). Adding together two linear combinations of S also produces a linear combination of S: ![Image 24: {\displaystyle (\lambda _{1}\mathbf {v} _{1}+\cdots +\lambda _{n}\mathbf {v} _{n})+(\mu _{1}\mathbf {v} _{1}+\cdots +\mu _{n}\mathbf {v} _{n})=(\lambda _{1}+\mu _{1})\mathbf {v} _{1}+\cdots +(\lambda _{n}+\mu _{n})\mathbf {v} _{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ce23ab9c2b3f2015d13932c285c01b7ffd7df4dd), where all ![Image 25: {\displaystyle \lambda _{i},\mu _{i}\in K}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/488e9dbe508cac2327c2387f0c3760e95625fc3e), and multiplying a linear combination of S by a scalar ![Image 26: {\displaystyle c\in K}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a5e894da36b4db8c32e35962d3950750ad556f46) will produce another linear combination of S: ![Image 27: {\displaystyle c(\lambda _{1}\mathbf {v} _{1}+\cdots +\lambda _{n}\mathbf {v} _{n})=c\lambda _{1}\mathbf {v} _{1}+\cdots +c\lambda _{n}\mathbf {v} _{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7f898d3d824ab7a733ee5bfdffc81a4fe59c39b8). Thus span _S_ is a subspace of V.It follows that ![Image 28: {\displaystyle S\subseteq \operatorname {span} S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d1d99fbb3d10477160b46714a8d2186ef78ae923), since every **v**_i_ is a linear combination of S (trivially). Suppose that W is a linear subspace of V containing S. Since W is closed under addition and scalar multiplication, then every linear combination ![Image 29: {\displaystyle \lambda _{1}\mathbf {v} _{1}+\cdots +\lambda _{n}\mathbf {v} _{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d82e7f7354e332d33b0aa3ac6b4c9295b3232d72) must be contained in W. Thus, span _S_ is contained in every subspace of V containing S, and the intersection of all such subspaces, or the smallest such subspace, is equal to the set of all linear combinations of S.

### Size of spanning set is at least size of linearly independent set

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Linear_span&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Size of spanning set is at least size of linearly independent set")]

Every spanning set S of a vector space V must contain at least as many elements as any [linearly independent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_independence "Linear independence") set of vectors from V.

_Proof._ Let ![Image 30: {\displaystyle S=\{\mathbf {v} _{1},\ldots ,\mathbf {v} _{m}\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5f4aa1cb6fa7013a445c6c7a9f1df2d099a08e09) be a spanning set and ![Image 31: {\displaystyle W=\{\mathbf {w} _{1},\ldots ,\mathbf {w} _{n}\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81f9c642ba4d1fa1a15b87b90c97283802eb6e81) be a linearly independent set of vectors from V. We want to show that ![Image 32: {\displaystyle m\geq n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6b02f25e62da7fe3162ac80446437cdc1c0fd341).Since S spans V, then ![Image 33: {\displaystyle S\cup \{\mathbf {w} _{1}\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f391dc56ac54d7844cbecceef21af47d54c72f01) must also span V, and ![Image 34: {\displaystyle \mathbf {w} _{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/856752f8af2657e2d348dfd7f2d9d86d4abad771) must be a linear combination of S. Thus ![Image 35: {\displaystyle S\cup \{\mathbf {w} _{1}\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f391dc56ac54d7844cbecceef21af47d54c72f01) is linearly dependent, and we can remove one vector from S that is a linear combination of the other elements. This vector cannot be any of the **w**_i_, since W is linearly independent. The resulting set is ![Image 36: {\displaystyle \{\mathbf {w} _{1},\mathbf {v} _{1},\ldots ,\mathbf {v} _{i-1},\mathbf {v} _{i+1},\ldots ,\mathbf {v} _{m}\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d47eba76f1f2956766fa74fdd7c26046f02cb5d), which is a spanning set of V. We repeat this step n times, where the resulting set after the p th step is the union of ![Image 37: {\displaystyle \{\mathbf {w} _{1},\ldots ,\mathbf {w} _{p}\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/14f0eeb4c56fcc9b8d5793997d7a9447990bf330) and m - p vectors of S.It is ensured until the n th step that there will always be some **v**_i_ to remove out of S for every adjoint of **v**, and thus there are at least as many **v**_i_'s as there are **w**_i_'s—i.e. ![Image 38: {\displaystyle m\geq n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6b02f25e62da7fe3162ac80446437cdc1c0fd341). To verify this, we assume by way of contradiction that ![Image 39: {\displaystyle m<n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/490c01b0cb770144f28afd17bb5fef277daf6f38). Then, at the m th step, we have the set ![Image 40: {\displaystyle \{\mathbf {w} _{1},\ldots ,\mathbf {w} _{m}\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d540f5ca6b88d7f50b2a679f4f2b500c18d6fd99) and we can adjoin another vector ![Image 41: {\displaystyle \mathbf {w} _{m+1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/500f3633021d8cec54e78aa689136175b0c343d4). But, since ![Image 42: {\displaystyle \{\mathbf {w} _{1},\ldots ,\mathbf {w} _{m}\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d540f5ca6b88d7f50b2a679f4f2b500c18d6fd99) is a spanning set of V, ![Image 43: {\displaystyle \mathbf {w} _{m+1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/500f3633021d8cec54e78aa689136175b0c343d4) is a linear combination of ![Image 44: {\displaystyle \{\mathbf {w} _{1},\ldots ,\mathbf {w} _{m}\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d540f5ca6b88d7f50b2a679f4f2b500c18d6fd99). This is a contradiction, since W is linearly independent.

### Spanning set can be reduced to a basis

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Linear_span&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Spanning set can be reduced to a basis")]

Let V be a finite-dimensional vector space. Any set of vectors that spans V can be reduced to a [basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basis_(linear_algebra) "Basis (linear algebra)") for V, by discarding vectors if necessary (i.e. if there are linearly dependent vectors in the set). If the [axiom of choice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiom_of_choice "Axiom of choice") holds, this is true without the assumption that V has finite dimension. This also indicates that a basis is a minimal spanning set when V is finite-dimensional.

Generalizing the definition of the span of points in space, a subset X of the ground set of a [matroid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matroid "Matroid") is called a spanning set if the rank of X equals the rank of the entire ground set[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_span#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOxley201128-8)

The vector space definition can also be generalized to modules.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_span#cite_note-9)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_span#cite_note-10) Given an R-module A and a collection of elements _a_ 1, ..., _a n_ of A, the [submodule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Submodule "Submodule") of A spanned by _a_ 1, ..., _a n_ is the sum of [cyclic modules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclic_module "Cyclic module")![Image 45: {\displaystyle Ra_{1}+\cdots +Ra_{n}=\left\{\sum _{k=1}^{n}r_{k}a_{k}{\bigg |}r_{k}\in R\right\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/64242dc39bd9b91de6439a8c20dfdc3d7b747fd7) consisting of all _R_-linear combinations of the elements _a i_. As with the case of vector spaces, the submodule of _A_ spanned by any subset of _A_ is the intersection of all submodules containing that subset.

Closed linear span (functional analysis)
----------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Linear_span&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Closed linear span (functional analysis)")]

In [functional analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functional_analysis "Functional analysis"), a closed linear span of a [set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_(mathematics) "Set (mathematics)") of [vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space") is the minimal closed set which contains the linear span of that set.

Suppose that X is a normed vector space and let E be any non-empty subset of X. The **closed linear span** of E, denoted by ![Image 46: {\displaystyle {\overline {\operatorname {Sp} }}(E)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/751ef8a31871b2de0b7a3e29a68ad2a9f4f3280d) or ![Image 47: {\displaystyle {\overline {\operatorname {Span} }}(E)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/773d2a7eac545cd1fc0efc199a705713db4e224c), is the intersection of all the closed linear subspaces of X which contain E.

One mathematical formulation of this is

![Image 48: {\displaystyle {\overline {\operatorname {Sp} }}(E)=\{u\in X|\forall \varepsilon >0\,\exists x\in \operatorname {Sp} (E):\|x-u\|<\varepsilon \}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/395b2b3582d596bde6bd435c2a1e5e7e13691d0b)
The closed linear span of the set of functions _x n_ on the interval [0, 1], where _n_ is a non-negative integer, depends on the norm used. If the [_L_ 2 norm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lp_space#Lp_spaces_and_Lebesgue_integrals "Lp space") is used, then the closed linear span is the [Hilbert space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space "Hilbert space") of [square-integrable functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square-integrable_function "Square-integrable function") on the interval. But if the [maximum norm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maximum_norm "Maximum norm") is used, the closed linear span will be the space of continuous functions on the interval. In either case, the closed linear span contains functions that are not polynomials, and so are not in the linear span itself. However, the [cardinality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardinality "Cardinality") of the set of functions in the closed linear span is the [cardinality of the continuum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardinality_of_the_continuum "Cardinality of the continuum"), which is the same cardinality as for the set of polynomials.

The linear span of a set is dense in the closed linear span. Moreover, as stated in the lemma below, the closed linear span is indeed the [closure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closure_(mathematics) "Closure (mathematics)") of the linear span.

Closed linear spans are important when dealing with closed linear subspaces (which are themselves highly important, see [Riesz's lemma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riesz%27s_lemma "Riesz's lemma")).
