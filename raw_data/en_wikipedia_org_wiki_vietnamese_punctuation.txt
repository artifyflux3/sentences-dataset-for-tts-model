Historical punctuation
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Vietnamese_punctuation&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Historical punctuation")]

Historically, the Vietnamese language was written using [ch·ªØ N√¥m](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ch%E1%BB%AF_N%C3%B4m "Ch·ªØ N√¥m"), a script that incorporated both Chinese characters and locally invented characters to represent native Vietnamese words. *   Âè• (c√∫) - a mark that indicates a [full stop](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Full_stop "Full stop") („ÄÇ) at the end of a sentence. It is typically a circle or a dot in the bottom-right.
*   ËÆÄ (ƒë·∫≠u) - a mark that indicates a pause („ÄÅ) similar to a [comma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comma "Comma"). It is typically a dot or a circle in the bottom-right.

The usage of these two punctuation marks have some flexibility, varying between manuscripts. In some cases, both marks are positioned at the bottom right of the character, while in others, the mark Âè• (c√∫) appears at the bottom right, and ËÆÄ (ƒë·∫≠u) is placed at the bottom center. These usages may differ, there were also manuscripts that were not punctuated. Scholars had to either rely on line breaks in the text called Êñ∑Âè• (ƒëo·∫°n c√∫) or their mastery of Literary Chinese grammar.

*   Â°óÊäπ (d·∫•u ƒë·ªì m·∫°t) - If a single character is marked with three dots; it signifies an error or mistake and the character is deleted.
*   ñø∞ (d·∫•u c√°) and ñø± (d·∫•u nh√°y) - These marks were used in written Vietnamese to indicate that the character was being used a phonetic approximation thus should be read with a different reading.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vietnamese_punctuation#cite_note-4)

*   d·∫•u ch·∫•m ng∆∞∆°i - similar to the d·∫•u v√≤ng, it is used for common people. A dot is used between the characters.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vietnamese_punctuation#cite_note-:0-1)

After Vietnam switched to the [Vietnamese alphabet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vietnamese_alphabet "Vietnamese alphabet"), European punctuation was adopted in favor of traditional punctuation.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vietnamese_punctuation#cite_note-6)

*   d·∫•u ch·∫•m - [period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Full_stop "Full stop"), full stop (.); used to mark end of a sentence.
*   d·∫•u h·ªèi - [question mark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Question_mark "Question mark") (?); used at the end of an interrogative sentence.
*   d·∫•u ch·∫•m than (d·∫•u c·∫£m) - [exclamation mark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exclamation_mark "Exclamation mark") (!); used to express strong emotions, commands, or exclamations.
*   d·∫•u l·ª≠ng - [ellipsis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipsis "Ellipsis") (...); indicates an unfinished thought, hesitation, or omitted words.
*   d·∫•u ph·∫©y - [comma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comma "Comma") (,); Separates elements in a sentence, clarifies meaning, or indicates a pause.
*   d·∫•u ch·∫•m ph·∫©y - [semicolon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semicolon "Semicolon") (;); used to separate clauses in a complex sentence or list items that contain commas.
*   d·∫•u hai ch·∫•m - [colon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colon_(punctuation) "Colon (punctuation)") (:); introduces an explanation, a list, or a quotation.
*   d·∫•u (g·∫°ch) ngang - [dash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dash "Dash") (‚Äî); used to mark the boundary of a parenthetical element.
*   d·∫•u g·∫°ch n·ªëi - [hyphen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyphen "Hyphen") (-); used to link all syllable in polysyllabic loanwords e.g. [Niu-t∆°n (Newton)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaac_Newton "Isaac Newton"). 
    *   It was also used to link multiple syllables in compound words and names (including nicknames, art names, place names, and location names.), especially during the period when Latin Script (Ch·ªØ Qu·ªëc Ng·ªØ) started to be used. However, the usage of hyphens for linking compound words wasn't consistent, universal, and standardized, some writers or authors will use it consistently while others may use it relatively or partially. Nowadays, especially after 1975, Vietnamese texts in both Vietnam and overseas rarely use hyphens to connect compound words.
