A **unit of length** refers to any arbitrarily chosen and accepted reference standard for measurement of length. The most common units in modern use are the [metric units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_system "Metric system"), used in every country globally. In the United States the [U.S. customary units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U.S._customary_units "U.S. customary units") are also in use. British [Imperial units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imperial_units "Imperial units") are still used for some purposes in the United Kingdom and some other countries. The metric system is sub-divided into [SI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Units "International System of Units") and non-SI units.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_of_length#cite_note-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_of_length#cite_note-Hinkelman-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_of_length#cite_note-3)

The [base unit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SI_base_unit "SI base unit") in the International System of Units (SI) is the [meter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meter "Meter"), defined as "the length of the path travelled by [light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light "Speed of light") in vacuum during a time interval of 1‚ÅÑ299792458 seconds."[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_of_length#cite_note-Res1-9) It is approximately equal to 1.0936 yd. Other SI units are derived from the meter by adding [prefixes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_prefix "Metric prefix"), as in millimeter or kilometer, thus producing systematic decimal multiples and submultiples of the base unit that span many orders of magnitude. For example, a [kilometer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kilometer "Kilometer") is 1000 m.

In the [centimeter‚Äìgram‚Äìsecond system of units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centimetre%E2%80%93gram%E2%80%93second_system_of_units "Centimetre‚Äìgram‚Äìsecond system of units"), the basic unit of length is the [centimeter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centimeter "Centimeter"), or 1‚ÅÑ100 of a meter. Other non-SI units are derived from decimal multiples of the meter.

The basic unit of length in the imperial and U.S. customary systems is the [yard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yard "Yard"), defined as exactly 0.9144 m by international treaty in 1959.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_of_length#cite_note-Hinkelman-2)[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_of_length#cite_note-Fenna2002-10)

Common imperial units and U.S. customary units of length include:[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_of_length#cite_note-11)

*   [thou](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thou_(unit_of_length) "Thou (unit of length)") or mil (1‚ÅÑ1000 of an inch)
*   [inch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inch "Inch") (25.4 mm)
*   [foot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foot_(unit) "Foot (unit)") (12 inches, 0.3048 m)
*   [yard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yard "Yard") (3 feet, 0.9144 m)
*   (terrestrial) [mile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mile "Mile") (5280 feet, or 1760 yards 1609.344 m)
*   (land) [league](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/League_(unit) "League (unit)") 3 miles (4,800 m)

In addition, the following are used by [sailors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sailor "Sailor"):

*   [fathom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fathom "Fathom") (for depth; only in non-metric countries) (2 yards = 1.8288 m)
*   [nautical mile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nautical_mile "Nautical mile") (one minute of arc of latitude = 1852 m)

[Aviators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pilot_(aeronautics) "Pilot (aeronautics)") use feet for altitude worldwide (except in Russia and China) and nautical miles for distance.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

### Australian building trades

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Unit_of_length&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Australian building trades")]

The Australian building trades adopted the [metric system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_system "Metric system") in 1966 and the units used for measurement of length are [meters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meters "Meters") (m) and [millimeters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Millimeters "Millimeters") (mm). [Centimeters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centimeters "Centimeters") (cm) are avoided as they cause confusion when reading [plans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plans "Plans"). For example, the length two and a half meters is usually recorded as 2500 mm or 2.5 m; it would be considered non-standard to record this length as 250 cm.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_of_length#cite_note-12)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_of_length#cite_note-13)

American surveyors use a decimal-based system of measurement devised by [Edmund Gunter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edmund_Gunter "Edmund Gunter") in 1620. The base unit is [Gunter's chain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gunter%27s_chain "Gunter's chain") of 66 feet (20 m) which is subdivided into 4 rods, each of 16.5 ft or 100 links of 0.66 feet. A link is abbreviated "lk", and links "lks", in old deeds and land surveys done for the government.
