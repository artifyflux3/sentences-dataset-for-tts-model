This article is about the cross product of two vectors in three-dimensional Euclidean space. For other uses, see [Cross product (disambiguation)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product_(disambiguation) "Cross product (disambiguation)").

[![Image 1](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Cross_product_vector.svg/250px-Cross_product_vector.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Cross_product_vector.svg)

The cross product with respect to a right-handed coordinate system

In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), the **cross product** or **vector product** (occasionally **directed area product**, to emphasize its geometric significance) is a [binary operation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_operation "Binary operation") on two [vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_vector "Euclidean vector") in a three-dimensional [oriented](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orientation_(vector_space) "Orientation (vector space)")[Euclidean vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_vector_space "Euclidean vector space") (named here ![Image 2: {\displaystyle E}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4232c9de2ee3eec0a9c0a19b15ab92daa6223f9b)), and is denoted by the symbol ![Image 3: {\displaystyle \times }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0ffafff1ad26cbe49045f19a67ce532116a32703). Given two [linearly independent vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linearly_independent_vectors "Linearly independent vectors")**a** and **b**, the cross product, **a** × **b** (read "a cross b"), is a vector that is [perpendicular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular "Perpendicular") to both **a** and **b**,[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product#cite_note-:1-1) and thus [normal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_(geometry) "Normal (geometry)") to the plane containing them. It has many applications in mathematics, [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics"), [engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineering "Engineering"), and [computer programming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_programming "Computer programming"). It should not be confused with the [dot product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dot_product "Dot product") (projection product).

The magnitude of the cross product equals the area of a [parallelogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram "Parallelogram") with the vectors for sides; in particular, the magnitude of the product of two perpendicular vectors is the product of their lengths. The [units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_of_measurement "Unit of measurement") of the cross-product are the product of the units of each vector. If two vectors are [parallel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_vectors "Parallel vectors") or are [anti-parallel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiparallel_vectors "Antiparallel vectors") (that is, they are linearly dependent), or if either one has zero length, then their cross product is zero.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product#cite_note-:2-2)

The cross product is [anticommutative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anticommutativity "Anticommutativity") (that is, **a** × **b** = − **b** × **a**) and is [distributive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distributivity "Distributivity") over addition, that is, **a** × (**b** + **c**) = **a** × **b** + **a** × **c**.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product#cite_note-:1-1) The space ![Image 4: {\displaystyle E}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4232c9de2ee3eec0a9c0a19b15ab92daa6223f9b) together with the cross product is an [algebra over the real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra_over_a_field "Algebra over a field"), which is neither [commutative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative "Commutative") nor [associative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Associative "Associative"), but is a [Lie algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_algebra "Lie algebra") with the cross product being the [Lie bracket](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_bracket "Lie bracket").

Like the dot product, it depends on the [metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space "Metric space") of [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space"), but unlike the dot product, it also depends on a choice of [orientation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orientation_(mathematics) "Orientation (mathematics)") (or "[handedness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right-hand_rule "Right-hand rule")") of the space (it is why an oriented space is needed). The resultant vector is invariant of rotation of basis. Due to the dependence on [handedness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right-hand_rule "Right-hand rule"), the cross product is said to be a [pseudovector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudovector "Pseudovector").

In connection with the cross product, the [exterior product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_algebra "Exterior algebra") of vectors can be used in arbitrary dimensions (with a [bivector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bivector "Bivector") or [2-form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2-form "2-form") result) and is independent of the orientation of the space.

The product can be generalized in various ways, using the orientation and metric structure just as for the traditional 3-dimensional cross product; one can, in n dimensions, take the product of _n_ − 1 vectors to produce a vector perpendicular to all of them. But if the product is limited to non-trivial binary products with vector results, it exists only in three and seven dimensions.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product#cite_note-Massey2-3) The [cross-product in seven dimensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seven-dimensional_cross_product "Seven-dimensional cross product") has undesirable properties (e.g. it [fails](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seven-dimensional_cross_product#Relation_to_the_octonions "Seven-dimensional cross product") to satisfy the [Jacobi identity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacobi_identity "Jacobi identity")), so it is not used in mathematical physics to represent quantities such as multi-dimensional [space-time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space-time "Space-time").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product#cite_note-4) (See [§Generalizations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product#Generalizations) below for other dimensions.)

[![Image 5](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Right_hand_rule_cross_product.svg/250px-Right_hand_rule_cross_product.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Right_hand_rule_cross_product.svg)

Finding the direction of the cross product by the [right-hand rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right-hand_rule "Right-hand rule")

The cross product of two vectors **a** and **b** is defined only in three-dimensional space and is denoted by **a** × **b**. In [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics") and [applied mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Applied_mathematics "Applied mathematics"), the wedge notation **a** ∧ **b** is often used (in conjunction with the name _vector product_),[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product#cite_note-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product#cite_note-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product#cite_note-7) although in pure mathematics such notation is usually reserved for just the exterior product, an abstraction of the vector product to n dimensions.

The cross product **a** × **b** is defined as a vector **c** that is [perpendicular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular "Perpendicular") (orthogonal) to both **a** and **b**, with a direction given by the [right-hand rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right-hand_rule "Right-hand rule")[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product#cite_note-:1-1) and a magnitude equal to the area of the [parallelogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram "Parallelogram") that the vectors span.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product#cite_note-:2-2)

The cross product is defined by the formula[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product#cite_note-8)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product#cite_note-Cullen-9)

![Image 6: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} \times \mathbf {b} =\|\mathbf {a} \|\|\mathbf {b} \|\sin(\theta )\,\mathbf {n} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ed83632b34b99ffc6a4df41549013ab3835b8b5b)
where

_θ_ is the [angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle "Angle") between **a** and **b** in the plane containing them (hence, it is between 0° and 180°),‖**a**‖ and ‖**b**‖ are the [magnitudes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnitude_(vector) "Magnitude (vector)") of vectors **a** and **b**,**n** is a [unit vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_vector "Unit vector")[perpendicular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular "Perpendicular") to the plane containing **a** and **b**, with direction such that the ordered set (**a**, **b**, **n**) is [positively oriented](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orientation_(vector_space) "Orientation (vector space)").
If the vectors **a** and **b** are parallel (that is, the angle _θ_ between them is either 0° or 180°), by the above formula, the cross product of **a** and **b** is the [zero vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero_vector "Zero vector")**0**.

[![Image 7](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/Cross_product.gif/250px-Cross_product.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Cross_product.gif)

The cross product **a** × **b** (vertical, in purple) changes as the angle between the vectors **a** (blue) and **b** (red) changes. The cross product is always orthogonal to both vectors, and has magnitude zero when the vectors are parallel and maximum magnitude ‖**a**‖‖**b**‖ when they are orthogonal.

The direction of the vector **n** depends on the chosen orientation of the space. Conventionally, it is given by the right-hand rule, where one simply points the forefinger of the right hand in the direction of **a** and the middle finger in the direction of **b**. Then, the vector **n** is coming out of the thumb (see the adjacent picture). Using this rule implies that the cross product is [anti-commutative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anticommutativity "Anticommutativity"); that is, **b** × **a** = −(**a** × **b**). By pointing the forefinger toward **b** first, and then pointing the middle finger toward **a**, the thumb will be forced in the opposite direction, reversing the sign of the product vector.

As the cross product operator depends on the orientation of the space, in general the cross product of two vectors is not a "true" vector, but a _pseudovector_. See [§Handedness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product#Handedness) for more detail.

[![Image 8](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Sarrus_rule.svg/330px-Sarrus_rule.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Sarrus_rule.svg)

According to [Sarrus's rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sarrus%27s_rule "Sarrus's rule"), the [determinant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Determinant "Determinant") of a 3×3 matrix involves multiplications between matrix elements identified by crossed diagonals

In 1842, [William Rowan Hamilton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Rowan_Hamilton "William Rowan Hamilton") first described the algebra of [quaternions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quaternion "Quaternion") and the non-commutative Hamilton product. In particular, when the Hamilton product of two vectors (that is, pure quaternions with zero scalar part) is performed, it results in a quaternion with a scalar and vector part. The scalar and vector part of this Hamilton product corresponds to the negative of dot product and cross product of the two vectors.

In 1881, [Josiah Willard Gibbs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Josiah_Willard_Gibbs "Josiah Willard Gibbs"),[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product#cite_note-10) and independently [Oliver Heaviside](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oliver_Heaviside "Oliver Heaviside"), introduced the notation for both the dot product and the cross product using a period (**a** ⋅ **b**) and an "×" (**a** × **b**), respectively, to denote them.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product#cite_note-ucd-11)

In 1877, to emphasize the fact that the result of a dot product is a [scalar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_(mathematics) "Scalar (mathematics)") while the result of a cross product is a [vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_vector "Euclidean vector"), [William Kingdon Clifford](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Kingdon_Clifford "William Kingdon Clifford") coined the alternative names **scalar product** and **vector product** for the two operations.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product#cite_note-ucd-11) These alternative names are still widely used in the literature.

Both the cross notation (**a** × **b**) and the name _cross product_ were possibly inspired by the fact that each [scalar component](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_component "Scalar component") of **a** × **b** is computed by multiplying non-corresponding components of **a** and **b**. Conversely, a dot product **a** ⋅ **b** involves multiplications between corresponding components of **a** and **b**. As explained [below](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product#Matrix_notation), the cross product can be expressed in the form of a determinant of a special 3 × 3 matrix. According to [Sarrus's rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sarrus%27s_rule "Sarrus's rule"), this involves multiplications between matrix elements identified by crossed diagonals.

[![Image 23](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Parallelepiped_volume.svg/250px-Parallelepiped_volume.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Parallelepiped_volume.svg)

Figure 2. Three vectors defining a parallelepiped

The [magnitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_norm "Euclidean norm") of the cross product can be interpreted as the positive [area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area "Area") of the [parallelogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram "Parallelogram") having **a** and **b** as sides (see Figure 1):[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product#cite_note-:1-1)![Image 24: {\displaystyle \left\|\mathbf {a} \times \mathbf {b} \right\|=\left\|\mathbf {a} \right\|\left\|\mathbf {b} \right\|\left|\sin \theta \right|.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/da181ad0318bfdaf4e1ece58b4716249052a1cfd)

Indeed, one can also compute the volume _V_ of a [parallelepiped](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelepiped "Parallelepiped") having **a**, **b** and **c** as edges by using a combination of a cross product and a dot product, called [scalar triple product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_triple_product "Scalar triple product") (see Figure 2):

![Image 25: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} \cdot (\mathbf {b} \times \mathbf {c} )=\mathbf {b} \cdot (\mathbf {c} \times \mathbf {a} )=\mathbf {c} \cdot (\mathbf {a} \times \mathbf {b} ).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5bb8e0667620c09d9e39fbec1dab2002734af461)
Since the result of the scalar triple product may be negative, the volume of the parallelepiped is given by its absolute value:

![Image 26: {\displaystyle V=|\mathbf {a} \cdot (\mathbf {b} \times \mathbf {c} )|.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3f804e5b3ba3ed4b930443b736ba00eeb7f324e3)
Because the magnitude of the cross product goes by the sine of the angle between its arguments, the cross product can be thought of as a measure of _perpendicularity_ in the same way that the dot product is a measure of _parallelism_. Given two [unit vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_vectors "Unit vectors"), their cross product has a magnitude of 1 if the two are perpendicular and a magnitude of zero if the two are parallel. The dot product of two unit vectors behaves just oppositely: it is zero when the unit vectors are perpendicular and 1 if the unit vectors are parallel.

Unit vectors enable two convenient identities: the dot product of two unit vectors yields the cosine (which may be positive or negative) of the angle between the two unit vectors. The magnitude of the cross product of the two unit vectors yields the sine (which will always be positive).

### Algebraic properties

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cross_product&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Algebraic properties")]

[![Image 27](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Cross_product_scalar_multiplication.svg/500px-Cross_product_scalar_multiplication.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Cross_product_scalar_multiplication.svg)

Cross product [scalar multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_multiplication "Scalar multiplication"). **Left:** Decomposition of **b** into components parallel and perpendicular to **a**. Right: Scaling of the perpendicular components by a positive real number _r_ (if negative, **b** and the cross product are reversed).

[![Image 28](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Cross_product_distributivity.svg/500px-Cross_product_distributivity.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Cross_product_distributivity.svg)

Cross product distributivity over vector addition. **Left:** The vectors **b** and **c** are resolved into parallel and perpendicular components to **a**. **Right:** The parallel components vanish in the cross product, only the perpendicular components shown in the plane perpendicular to **a** remain.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product#cite_note-13)

[![Image 29](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Cross_product_triple.svg/500px-Cross_product_triple.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Cross_product_triple.svg)

The two nonequivalent triple cross products of three vectors **a**, **b**, **c**. In each case, two vectors define a plane, the other is out of the plane and can be split into parallel and perpendicular components to the cross product of the vectors defining the plane. These components can be found by [vector projection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_projection "Vector projection") and [rejection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_rejection "Vector rejection"). The triple product is in the plane and is rotated as shown.

If the cross product of two vectors is the zero vector (that is, **a** × **b** = **0**), then either one or both of the inputs is the zero vector, (**a** = **0** or **b** = **0**) or else they are parallel or antiparallel (**a** ∥ **b**) so that the sine of the angle between them is zero (_θ_ = 0° or _θ_ = 180° and sin _θ_ = 0).

The self cross product of a vector is the zero vector:

![Image 30: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} \times \mathbf {a} =\mathbf {0} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a30e3a67ced08f37b9b60b35acba30a88884879)
The cross product is [anticommutative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anticommutativity "Anticommutativity"),

![Image 31: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} \times \mathbf {b} =-(\mathbf {b} \times \mathbf {a} ),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d68d15a33fab12aa37a1edbf8d9415fe490d7c8e)
[distributive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distributive_property "Distributive property") over addition,

![Image 32: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} \times (\mathbf {b} +\mathbf {c} )=(\mathbf {a} \times \mathbf {b} )+(\mathbf {a} \times \mathbf {c} ),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4866b6c70d88a7298e16c6d03f2a1111cc713ac0)
and compatible with scalar multiplication so that

![Image 33: {\displaystyle (r\,\mathbf {a} )\times \mathbf {b} =\mathbf {a} \times (r\,\mathbf {b} )=r\,(\mathbf {a} \times \mathbf {b} ).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e857af91981df6a612dfcba261b568fa238b0d18)
It is not [associative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Associative "Associative"), but satisfies the [Jacobi identity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacobi_identity "Jacobi identity"):

![Image 34: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} \times (\mathbf {b} \times \mathbf {c} )+\mathbf {b} \times (\mathbf {c} \times \mathbf {a} )+\mathbf {c} \times (\mathbf {a} \times \mathbf {b} )=\mathbf {0} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9ed5e398f1b3a67085cf13b5e371f680cafd5184)
Distributivity, linearity and Jacobi identity show that the **R**3[vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_coordinate_space "Real coordinate space") together with vector addition and the cross product forms a [Lie algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_algebra "Lie algebra"), the Lie algebra of the real [orthogonal group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_group "Orthogonal group") in 3 dimensions, [SO(3)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SO(3) "SO(3)"). The cross product does not obey the [cancellation law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cancellation_law "Cancellation law"); that is, **a** × **b** = **a** × **c** with **a** ≠ **0** does not imply **b** = **c**, but only that:

![Image 35: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\mathbf {0} &=(\mathbf {a} \times \mathbf {b} )-(\mathbf {a} \times \mathbf {c} )\\&=\mathbf {a} \times (\mathbf {b} -\mathbf {c} ).\\\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d665ffc5a1eedd9785990194a6a3cbafad4c97c3)
This can be the case where **b** and **c** cancel, but additionally where **a** and **b** − **c** are parallel; that is, they are related by a scale factor _t_, leading to:

![Image 36: {\displaystyle \mathbf {c} =\mathbf {b} +t\,\mathbf {a} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1ab5e68ed7f6b180c4ef7a96a2b8040e3845d2db)
for some scalar _t_.

If, in addition to **a** × **b** = **a** × **c** and **a** ≠ **0** as above, it is the case that **a** ⋅ **b** = **a** ⋅ **c** then

![Image 37: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\mathbf {a} \times (\mathbf {b} -\mathbf {c} )&=\mathbf {0} \\\mathbf {a} \cdot (\mathbf {b} -\mathbf {c} )&=0,\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b3344e5eec330f3eac3a768868ff7a4c324eacf8)
As **b** − **c** cannot be simultaneously parallel (for the cross product to be **0**) and perpendicular (for the dot product to be 0) to **a**, it must be the case that **b** and **c** cancel: **b** = **c**.

From the geometrical definition, the cross product is invariant under proper [rotations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation_(mathematics) "Rotation (mathematics)") about the axis defined by **a** × **b**. In formulae:

![Image 38: {\displaystyle (R\mathbf {a} )\times (R\mathbf {b} )=R(\mathbf {a} \times \mathbf {b} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/506480ff74ac8093f4e4a1c9faccdbe3b8b5eb1c), where ![Image 39: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) is a [rotation matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation_matrix "Rotation matrix") with ![Image 40: {\displaystyle \det(R)=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5ef4e4006d672c548a3bd9975f097cb817e5a87c).
More generally, the cross product obeys the following identity under [matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_(mathematics) "Matrix (mathematics)") transformations:

![Image 41: {\displaystyle (M\mathbf {a} )\times (M\mathbf {b} )=(\det M)\left(M^{-1}\right)^{\mathrm {T} }(\mathbf {a} \times \mathbf {b} )=\operatorname {cof} M(\mathbf {a} \times \mathbf {b} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9446bfb084f66f153e944d16d8c4bbdb5a55deee)
where ![Image 42: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) is a 3-by-3 [matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_(mathematics) "Matrix (mathematics)") and ![Image 43: {\displaystyle \left(M^{-1}\right)^{\mathrm {T} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7847abafafce67e3b5ea0f6099a6fbd0799b61d0) is the [transpose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transpose "Transpose") of the [inverse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverse_matrix "Inverse matrix") and ![Image 44: {\displaystyle \operatorname {cof} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a0b51a0802a73401b300e807bf5ea0edc4d50e71) is the cofactor matrix. It can be readily seen how this formula reduces to the former one if ![Image 45: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) is a rotation matrix. If ![Image 46: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) is a 3-by-3 symmetric matrix applied to a generic cross product ![Image 47: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} \times \mathbf {b} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cf612179bab874c94c2ea2b4a541479534c3dacc), the following relation holds true:

![Image 48: {\displaystyle M(\mathbf {a} \times \mathbf {b} )=\operatorname {Tr} (M)(\mathbf {a} \times \mathbf {b} )-\mathbf {a} \times M\mathbf {b} +\mathbf {b} \times M\mathbf {a} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/567f71dac4e6244449c139965e6cd68817f5bbe9)
The cross product of two vectors lies in the [null space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Null_space "Null space") of the 2 × 3 matrix with the vectors as rows:

![Image 49: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} \times \mathbf {b} \in NS\left({\begin{bmatrix}\mathbf {a} \\\mathbf {b} \end{bmatrix}}\right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/86d907b7b79a65e2bb7e0f1fc0723714052555d8)
For the sum of two cross products, the following identity holds:

![Image 50: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} \times \mathbf {b} +\mathbf {c} \times \mathbf {d} =(\mathbf {a} -\mathbf {c} )\times (\mathbf {b} -\mathbf {d} )+\mathbf {a} \times \mathbf {d} +\mathbf {c} \times \mathbf {b} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5e2922975ff5e79c32d5eaf59faba3fd4a4cacf4)
The [product rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Product_rule "Product rule") of differential calculus applies to any bilinear operation, and therefore also to the cross product:

![Image 51: {\displaystyle {\frac {d}{dt}}(\mathbf {a} \times \mathbf {b} )={\frac {d\mathbf {a} }{dt}}\times \mathbf {b} +\mathbf {a} \times {\frac {d\mathbf {b} }{dt}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/97062e22177e79a60cab30370d02ffef91584a65)
where **a** and **b** are vectors that depend on the real variable _t_.

### Triple product expansion

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cross_product&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Triple product expansion")]

The cross product is used in both forms of the triple product. The [scalar triple product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_triple_product "Scalar triple product") of three vectors is defined as

![Image 52: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} \cdot (\mathbf {b} \times \mathbf {c} ),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af496fb6a4b0389bbc9a1005d6835f4b2399148f)
It is the signed volume of the [parallelepiped](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelepiped "Parallelepiped") with edges **a**, **b** and **c** and as such the vectors can be used in any order that's an [even permutation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Even_permutation "Even permutation") of the above ordering. The following therefore are equal:

![Image 53: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} \cdot (\mathbf {b} \times \mathbf {c} )=\mathbf {b} \cdot (\mathbf {c} \times \mathbf {a} )=\mathbf {c} \cdot (\mathbf {a} \times \mathbf {b} ),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/02e3d5e52a0f7d9a6682856f0a643b2896477a4a)
The [vector triple product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_triple_product "Vector triple product") is the cross product of a vector with the result of another cross product, and is related to the dot product by the following formula

![Image 54: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\mathbf {a} \times (\mathbf {b} \times \mathbf {c} )=\mathbf {b} (\mathbf {a} \cdot \mathbf {c} )-\mathbf {c} (\mathbf {a} \cdot \mathbf {b} )\\(\mathbf {a} \times \mathbf {b} )\times \mathbf {c} =\mathbf {b} (\mathbf {c} \cdot \mathbf {a} )-\mathbf {a} (\mathbf {b} \cdot \mathbf {c} )\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3e6fb95a2cfba6fe84a9b9ac182ff5c31bb691ab)
The [mnemonic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mnemonic "Mnemonic") "BAC minus CAB" is used to remember the order of the vectors in the right hand member. This formula is used in [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics") to simplify vector calculations. A special case, regarding [gradients](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gradient "Gradient") and useful in [vector calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_calculus "Vector calculus"), is

![Image 55: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\nabla \times (\nabla \times \mathbf {f} )&=\nabla (\nabla \cdot \mathbf {f} )-(\nabla \cdot \nabla )\mathbf {f} \\&=\nabla (\nabla \cdot \mathbf {f} )-\nabla ^{2}\mathbf {f} ,\\\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3a2678cadeebc8c95a1ef96cf104fd535855375c)
where ∇2 is the [vector Laplacian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_Laplacian "Vector Laplacian") operator.

Other identities relate the cross product to the scalar triple product:

![Image 56: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}(\mathbf {a} \times \mathbf {b} )\times (\mathbf {a} \times \mathbf {c} )&=(\mathbf {a} \cdot (\mathbf {b} \times \mathbf {c} ))\mathbf {a} \\(\mathbf {a} \times \mathbf {b} )\cdot (\mathbf {c} \times \mathbf {d} )&=\mathbf {b} ^{\mathrm {T} }\left(\left(\mathbf {c} ^{\mathrm {T} }\mathbf {a} \right)I-\mathbf {c} \mathbf {a} ^{\mathrm {T} }\right)\mathbf {d} \\&=(\mathbf {a} \cdot \mathbf {c} )(\mathbf {b} \cdot \mathbf {d} )-(\mathbf {a} \cdot \mathbf {d} )(\mathbf {b} \cdot \mathbf {c} )\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2847712b1d11ddec574c512e632f71326987e69a)
where _I_ is the [identity matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Identity_matrix "Identity matrix").

### Alternative formulation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cross_product&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Alternative formulation")]

The cross product and the dot product are related by:

![Image 57: {\displaystyle \left\|\mathbf {a} \times \mathbf {b} \right\|^{2}=\left\|\mathbf {a} \right\|^{2}\left\|\mathbf {b} \right\|^{2}-(\mathbf {a} \cdot \mathbf {b} )^{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8c74fbafd9fdaa1114e30e1e58299d726471af73)
The right-hand side is the [Gram determinant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gramian_matrix "Gramian matrix") of **a** and **b**, the square of the area of the parallelogram defined by the vectors. This condition determines the magnitude of the cross product. Namely, since the dot product is defined, in terms of the angle _θ_ between the two vectors, as:

![Image 58: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a\cdot b} =\left\|\mathbf {a} \right\|\left\|\mathbf {b} \right\|\cos \theta ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e5a7fe2718d1555a8b6d6f6a645875238b6ef1c8)
the above given relationship can be rewritten as follows:

![Image 59: {\displaystyle \left\|\mathbf {a\times b} \right\|^{2}=\left\|\mathbf {a} \right\|^{2}\left\|\mathbf {b} \right\|^{2}\left(1-\cos ^{2}\theta \right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1768e311ea3902ad24d072c9dc52383d4ce55e06)
Invoking the [Pythagorean trigonometric identity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagorean_trigonometric_identity "Pythagorean trigonometric identity") one obtains:

![Image 60: {\displaystyle \left\|\mathbf {a} \times \mathbf {b} \right\|=\left\|\mathbf {a} \right\|\left\|\mathbf {b} \right\|\left|\sin \theta \right|,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1bc236a94f6aa47d38d8ec0692f3d024fbb3c800)
which is the magnitude of the cross product expressed in terms of _θ_, equal to the area of the parallelogram defined by **a** and **b** (see [definition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product#Definition) above).

The combination of this requirement and the property that the cross product be orthogonal to its constituents **a** and **b** provides an alternative definition of the cross product.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product#cite_note-Massey-14)

### Cross product inverse

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cross_product&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Cross product inverse")]

Given two vectors **a** and **c** with **a**≠**0**, the equation **a** × **b** = **c** admits solutions for **b** if and only if **a** is orthogonal to **c** (that is, if **a** ⋅ **c** = 0). In that case, there exists an infinite family of solutions for **b**, which are

![Image 61: {\displaystyle \mathbf {b} ={\frac {\mathbf {c} \times \mathbf {a} }{\left\|\mathbf {a} \right\|^{2}}}+t\mathbf {a} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7edadfa855daa077baeaf31e33727c5bb86f442e)
where _t_ is an arbitrary constant.

This can be derived using the triple product expansion:

![Image 62: {\displaystyle \mathbf {c} \times \mathbf {a} =(\mathbf {a} \times \mathbf {b} )\times \mathbf {a} =\left\|\mathbf {a} \right\|^{2}\mathbf {b} -(\mathbf {a} \cdot \mathbf {b} )\mathbf {a} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/250e9fc21875646e7e181ccb85529234b33ae603)
Rearrange to solve for **b** to give

![Image 63: {\displaystyle \mathbf {b} ={\frac {\mathbf {c} \times \mathbf {a} }{\left\|\mathbf {a} \right\|^{2}}}+{\frac {\mathbf {a} \cdot \mathbf {b} }{\left\|\mathbf {a} \right\|^{2}}}\mathbf {a} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f92450575391e972ac806397b175149dc87792b4)
The coefficient of the last term can be simplified to just the arbitrary constant _t_ to yield the result shown above.

### Lagrange's identity

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cross_product&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: Lagrange's identity")]

The relation

![Image 64: {\displaystyle \left\|\mathbf {a} \times \mathbf {b} \right\|^{2}\equiv \det {\begin{bmatrix}\mathbf {a} \cdot \mathbf {a} &\mathbf {a} \cdot \mathbf {b} \\\mathbf {a} \cdot \mathbf {b} &\mathbf {b} \cdot \mathbf {b} \\\end{bmatrix}}\equiv \left\|\mathbf {a} \right\|^{2}\left\|\mathbf {b} \right\|^{2}-(\mathbf {a} \cdot \mathbf {b} )^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/59a25568aeaf60fa45307cb9e09b8816f0ef9f88)
can be compared with another relation involving the right-hand side, namely [Lagrange's identity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrange%27s_identity "Lagrange's identity") expressed as[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product#cite_note-Boichenko-15)

![Image 65: {\displaystyle \sum _{1\leq i<j\leq n}\left(a_{i}b_{j}-a_{j}b_{i}\right)^{2}\equiv \left\|\mathbf {a} \right\|^{2}\left\|\mathbf {b} \right\|^{2}-(\mathbf {a\cdot b} )^{2},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7b63da796f271026d4d88b8c54a037d2b2d1bee8)
where **a** and **b** may be _n_-dimensional vectors. This also shows that the [Riemannian volume form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_volume_form "Riemannian volume form") for surfaces is exactly the [surface element](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume_form "Volume form") from vector calculus. In the case where _n_ = 3, combining these two equations results in the expression for the magnitude of the cross product in terms of its components:[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product#cite_note-Lounesto1-16)

![Image 66: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\|\mathbf {a} \times \mathbf {b} \|^{2}&\equiv \sum _{1\leq i<j\leq 3}(a_{i}b_{j}-a_{j}b_{i})^{2}\\&\equiv (a_{1}b_{2}-b_{1}a_{2})^{2}+(a_{2}b_{3}-a_{3}b_{2})^{2}+(a_{3}b_{1}-a_{1}b_{3})^{2}.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/28750cad991d5798fc93c3b50b826f16aa2787d8)
The same result is found directly using the components of the cross product found from

![Image 67: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} \times \mathbf {b} \equiv \det {\begin{bmatrix}{\hat {\mathbf {i} }}&{\hat {\mathbf {j} }}&{\hat {\mathbf {k} }}\\a_{1}&a_{2}&a_{3}\\b_{1}&b_{2}&b_{3}\\\end{bmatrix}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/000971cad6116b209f59a4bd26b005fad02bf701)
In **R**3, Lagrange's equation is a special case of the multiplicativity |**vw**| = |**v**||**w**| of the norm in the [quaternion algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quaternion#Algebraic_properties "Quaternion").

It is a special case of another formula, also sometimes called Lagrange's identity, which is the three dimensional case of the [Binet–Cauchy identity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binet%E2%80%93Cauchy_identity "Binet–Cauchy identity"):[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product#cite_note-Liu-17)[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product#cite_note-Weisstein-18)

![Image 68: {\displaystyle (\mathbf {a} \times \mathbf {b} )\cdot (\mathbf {c} \times \mathbf {d} )\equiv (\mathbf {a} \cdot \mathbf {c} )(\mathbf {b} \cdot \mathbf {d} )-(\mathbf {a} \cdot \mathbf {d} )(\mathbf {b} \cdot \mathbf {c} ).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a221b772f040b8a79524612cbe046a6e79818286)
If **a** = **c** and **b** = **d**, this simplifies to the formula above.

### Infinitesimal generators of rotations

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cross_product&action=edit&section=16 "Edit section: Infinitesimal generators of rotations")]

The cross product conveniently describes the infinitesimal generators of [rotations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation_(mathematics) "Rotation (mathematics)") in **R**3. Specifically, if **n** is a unit vector in **R**3 and _R_(_φ_, **n**) denotes a rotation about the axis through the origin specified by **n**, with angle φ (measured in radians, counterclockwise when viewed from the tip of **n**), then

![Image 69: {\displaystyle \left.{d \over d\phi }\right|_{\phi =0}R(\phi ,{\boldsymbol {n}}){\boldsymbol {x}}={\boldsymbol {n}}\times {\boldsymbol {x}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a5c8854e35f51abe2f22cd4f46aee63a169894e2)
for every vector **x** in **R**3. The cross product with **n** therefore describes the infinitesimal generator of the rotations about **n**. These infinitesimal generators form the [Lie algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_algebra "Lie algebra")**so**(3) of the [rotation group SO(3)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation_group_SO(3) "Rotation group SO(3)"), and we obtain the result that the Lie algebra **R**3 with cross product is isomorphic to the Lie algebra **so**(3).

### Conversion to matrix multiplication

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cross_product&action=edit&section=18 "Edit section: Conversion to matrix multiplication")]

The vector cross product also can be expressed as the product of a [skew-symmetric matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skew-symmetric_matrix "Skew-symmetric matrix") and a vector:[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product#cite_note-Liu-17)![Image 70: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\mathbf {a} \times \mathbf {b} =[\mathbf {a} ]_{\times }\mathbf {b} &={\begin{bmatrix}\,0&\!-a_{3}&\,\,a_{2}\\\,\,a_{3}&0&\!-a_{1}\\-a_{2}&\,\,a_{1}&\,0\end{bmatrix}}{\begin{bmatrix}b_{1}\\b_{2}\\b_{3}\end{bmatrix}}\\\mathbf {a} \times \mathbf {b} ={[\mathbf {b} ]_{\times }}^{\mathrm {\!\!T} }\mathbf {a} &={\begin{bmatrix}\,0&\,\,b_{3}&\!-b_{2}\\-b_{3}&0&\,\,b_{1}\\\,\,b_{2}&\!-b_{1}&\,0\end{bmatrix}}{\begin{bmatrix}a_{1}\\a_{2}\\a_{3}\end{bmatrix}},\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/77eaf3e139944a22bc3543de85a65d2d280547c6) where the superscript T refers to the [transpose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transpose "Transpose") operation, and [**a**]× is defined by ![Image 71: {\displaystyle [\mathbf {a} ]_{\times }{\stackrel {\rm {def}}{=}}{\begin{bmatrix}\,\,0&\!-a_{3}&\,\,\,a_{2}\\\,\,\,a_{3}&0&\!-a_{1}\\\!-a_{2}&\,\,a_{1}&\,\,0\end{bmatrix}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/614cc7fd18f2f2e212803822f31acb2505c98c89)

The columns [**a**]×,i of the skew-symmetric matrix for a vector **a** can be also obtained by calculating the cross product with [unit vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_vectors "Unit vectors"). That is, ![Image 72: {\displaystyle [\mathbf {a} ]_{\times ,i}=\mathbf {a} \times \mathbf {{\hat {e}}_{i}} ,\;i\in \{1,2,3\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/74baaa1f6814e02fb133911b2bbab966485a3806) or ![Image 73: {\displaystyle [\mathbf {a} ]_{\times }=\sum _{i=1}^{3}\left(\mathbf {a} \times \mathbf {{\hat {e}}_{i}} \right)\otimes \mathbf {{\hat {e}}_{i}} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fb0381c1581881a166e2f4e9cefe0b236265eefd) where ![Image 74: {\displaystyle \otimes }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/de29098f5a34ee296a505681a0d5e875070f2aea) is the [outer product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_product "Outer product") operator.

Also, if **a** is itself expressed as a cross product: ![Image 75: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} =\mathbf {c} \times \mathbf {d} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bc201a9efafe594ecba74e68840b8c3af2bb880f) then ![Image 76: {\displaystyle [\mathbf {a} ]_{\times }=\mathbf {d} \mathbf {c} ^{\mathrm {T} }-\mathbf {c} \mathbf {d} ^{\mathrm {T} }.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d7bebe8181aeb49a3e8987339594fd7de7c454a9)

This result can be generalized to higher dimensions using [geometric algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_algebra "Geometric algebra"). In particular in any dimension bivectors can be identified with skew-symmetric matrices, so the product between a skew-symmetric matrix and vector is equivalent to the grade-1 part of the product of a bivector and vector.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product#cite_note-lounesto2001-19) In three dimensions bivectors are [dual](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hodge_dual "Hodge dual") to vectors so the product is equivalent to the cross product, with the bivector instead of its vector dual. In higher dimensions the product can still be calculated but bivectors have more degrees of freedom and are not equivalent to vectors.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product#cite_note-lounesto2001-19)

This notation is also often much easier to work with, for example, in [epipolar geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epipolar_geometry "Epipolar geometry").

From the general properties of the cross product follows immediately that![Image 77: {\displaystyle [\mathbf {a} ]_{\times }\,\mathbf {a} =\mathbf {0} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e918b623a3b34134199284e350a5a06f8fe0305) and ![Image 78: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} ^{\mathrm {T} }\,[\mathbf {a} ]_{\times }=\mathbf {0} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3cd1a98ffd5ab228553c458345bb26af8422bb43) and from fact that [**a**]× is skew-symmetric it follows that ![Image 79: {\displaystyle \mathbf {b} ^{\mathrm {T} }\,[\mathbf {a} ]_{\times }\,\mathbf {b} =0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c23cfb35b83ca69742e7da1381a7477a18d04e4d)

The above-mentioned triple product expansion (bac–cab rule) can be easily proven using this notation.

As mentioned above, the Lie algebra **R**3 with cross product is isomorphic to the Lie algebra **so(3)**, whose elements can be identified with the 3×3 skew-symmetric matrices. The map **a** → [**a**]× provides an isomorphism between **R**3 and **so(3)**. Under this map, the cross product of 3-vectors corresponds to the [commutator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutator "Commutator") of 3x3 skew-symmetric matrices.

### Index notation for tensors

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cross_product&action=edit&section=19 "Edit section: Index notation for tensors")]

The cross product can alternatively be defined in terms of the [Levi-Civita tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_symbol#Levi-Civita_tensors "Levi-Civita symbol")_E ijk_ and a dot product _η mi_, which are useful in converting [vector notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_notation "Vector notation") for tensor applications:

![Image 95: {\displaystyle \mathbf {c} =\mathbf {a\times b} \Leftrightarrow \ c^{m}=\sum _{i=1}^{3}\sum _{j=1}^{3}\sum _{k=1}^{3}\eta ^{mi}E_{ijk}a^{j}b^{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/45c39186d71340f05e95e3321e358c6070ed6ea2)
where the [indices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indexed_family "Indexed family")![Image 96: {\displaystyle i,j,k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9550e66fe7e601c4f58bbc9c19ba226301149cde) correspond to vector components. This characterization of the cross product is often expressed more compactly using the [Einstein summation convention](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_summation_convention "Einstein summation convention") as

![Image 97: {\displaystyle \mathbf {c} =\mathbf {a\times b} \Leftrightarrow \ c^{m}=\eta ^{mi}E_{ijk}a^{j}b^{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a8f4e1f828553a2a20d5f898f84cbf253ed9b656)
in which repeated indices are summed over the values 1 to 3.

In a positively-oriented orthonormal basis _η mi_ = δ _mi_ (the [Kronecker delta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kronecker_delta "Kronecker delta")) and ![Image 98: {\displaystyle E_{ijk}=\varepsilon _{ijk}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c2d9d932e4b8d60ae8f008b05f0cc44e707513ce) (the [Levi-Civita symbol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_symbol "Levi-Civita symbol")). In that case, this representation is another form of the skew-symmetric representation of the cross product:

![Image 99: {\displaystyle [\varepsilon _{ijk}a^{j}]=[\mathbf {a} ]_{\times }.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/063b837f18afcf9a012a49f73f4b4c2e350e99e9)
In [classical mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_mechanics "Classical mechanics"): representing the cross product by using the Levi-Civita symbol can cause mechanical symmetries to be obvious when physical systems are [isotropic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotropic "Isotropic"). (An example: consider a particle in a [Hooke's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hooke%27s_law "Hooke's law") potential in three-space, free to oscillate in three dimensions; none of these dimensions are "special" in any sense, so symmetries lie in the cross-product-represented angular momentum, which are made clear by the abovementioned Levi-Civita representation).[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

[![Image 100](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Cross_product_mnemonic.svg/250px-Cross_product_mnemonic.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Cross_product_mnemonic.svg)

Mnemonic to calculate a cross product in vector form

"Xyzzy (mnemonic)" redirects here. For other uses, see [Xyzzy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xyzzy_(disambiguation) "Xyzzy (disambiguation)").

The word "xyzzy" can be used to remember the definition of the cross product.

If

![Image 101: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} =\mathbf {b} \times \mathbf {c} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8e4d134fa6d725f5648e6dd39fb112a22a09d11f)
where:

![Image 102: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} ={\begin{bmatrix}a_{x}\\a_{y}\\a_{z}\end{bmatrix}},\ \mathbf {b} ={\begin{bmatrix}b_{x}\\b_{y}\\b_{z}\end{bmatrix}},\ \mathbf {c} ={\begin{bmatrix}c_{x}\\c_{y}\\c_{z}\end{bmatrix}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2d41a7bf21aff94bee70e4eb269dc8e716e6c4bc)
then:

![Image 103: {\displaystyle a_{x}=b_{y}c_{z}-b_{z}c_{y}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d37e6a72deeb92305976e28978611cc764efd2bd)![Image 104: {\displaystyle a_{y}=b_{z}c_{x}-b_{x}c_{z}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c71d9a7826acac7affc19e813f6a8744a7d227b3)![Image 105: {\displaystyle a_{z}=b_{x}c_{y}-b_{y}c_{x}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/df01e0774c7c3da6b1421f2d05d9041f7bd9e547)
The second and third equations can be obtained from the first by simply vertically rotating the subscripts, _x_ → _y_ → _z_ → _x_. The problem, of course, is how to remember the first equation, and two options are available for this purpose: either to remember the relevant two diagonals of Sarrus's scheme (those containing _**i**_), or to remember the xyzzy sequence.

Since the first diagonal in Sarrus's scheme is just the [main diagonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Main_diagonal "Main diagonal") of the [above](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product#Matrix_notation)-mentioned 3×3 matrix, the first three letters of the word xyzzy can be very easily remembered.

### Cross visualization

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cross_product&action=edit&section=21 "Edit section: Cross visualization")]

Similarly to the mnemonic device above, a "cross" or X can be visualized between the two vectors in the equation. This may be helpful for remembering the correct cross product formula.

If

![Image 106: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} =\mathbf {b} \times \mathbf {c} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8e4d134fa6d725f5648e6dd39fb112a22a09d11f)
then:

![Image 107: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} ={\begin{bmatrix}b_{x}\\b_{y}\\b_{z}\end{bmatrix}}\times {\begin{bmatrix}c_{x}\\c_{y}\\c_{z}\end{bmatrix}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/542d9f675a022492ecc144e2c7c22c96c35f166e)
If we want to obtain the formula for ![Image 108: {\displaystyle a_{x}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/339ff13ca52000e5467b829dfd008f6846820b57) we simply drop the ![Image 109: {\displaystyle b_{x}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c18f5bec576cc285434ce8439080b6ec5b0acd47) and ![Image 110: {\displaystyle c_{x}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fb6e0397e797e2cde37718a8e2b2e0fad6252c8a) from the formula, and take the next two components down:

![Image 111: {\displaystyle a_{x}={\begin{bmatrix}b_{y}\\b_{z}\end{bmatrix}}\times {\begin{bmatrix}c_{y}\\c_{z}\end{bmatrix}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4d27d183776ad72f859224dfed728eb30dce5ff9)
When doing this for ![Image 112: {\displaystyle a_{y}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4332752b07ec00b533637753ef567a2730cf335f) the next two elements down should "wrap around" the matrix so that after the z component comes the x component. For clarity, when performing this operation for ![Image 113: {\displaystyle a_{y}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4332752b07ec00b533637753ef567a2730cf335f), the next two components should be z and x (in that order). While for ![Image 114: {\displaystyle a_{z}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/491e51e74fbe03e2af58caa2275d8e5763f58215) the next two components should be taken as x and y.

![Image 115: {\displaystyle a_{y}={\begin{bmatrix}b_{z}\\b_{x}\end{bmatrix}}\times {\begin{bmatrix}c_{z}\\c_{x}\end{bmatrix}},\ a_{z}={\begin{bmatrix}b_{x}\\b_{y}\end{bmatrix}}\times {\begin{bmatrix}c_{x}\\c_{y}\end{bmatrix}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/389e7225cc9ae6bf38edebfae230026f19e63bcc)
For ![Image 116: {\displaystyle a_{x}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/339ff13ca52000e5467b829dfd008f6846820b57) then, if we visualize the cross operator as pointing from an element on the left to an element on the right, we can take the first element on the left and simply multiply by the element that the cross points to in the right-hand matrix. We then subtract the next element down on the left, multiplied by the element that the cross points to here as well. This results in our ![Image 117: {\displaystyle a_{x}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/339ff13ca52000e5467b829dfd008f6846820b57) formula –

![Image 118: {\displaystyle a_{x}=b_{y}c_{z}-b_{z}c_{y}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9a6365cbec22fd34d17a3b7652cef7d8bab45e3c)
We can do this in the same way for ![Image 119: {\displaystyle a_{y}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4332752b07ec00b533637753ef567a2730cf335f) and ![Image 120: {\displaystyle a_{z}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/491e51e74fbe03e2af58caa2275d8e5763f58215) to construct their associated formulas.

The cross product has applications in various contexts. For example, it is used in computational geometry, physics and engineering. A non-exhaustive list of examples follows.

### Computational geometry

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cross_product&action=edit&section=23 "Edit section: Computational geometry")]

The cross product appears in the calculation of the distance of two [skew lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skew_lines#Distance "Skew lines") (lines not in the same plane) from each other in three-dimensional space.

The cross product can be used to calculate the normal for a triangle or polygon, an operation frequently performed in [computer graphics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_graphics "Computer graphics"). For example, the winding of a polygon (clockwise or anticlockwise) about a point within the polygon can be calculated by triangulating the polygon (like spoking a wheel) and summing the angles (between the spokes) using the cross product to keep track of the sign of each angle.

In [computational geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computational_geometry "Computational geometry") of [the plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_(geometry) "Plane (geometry)"), the cross product is used to determine the sign of the [acute angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acute_angle "Acute angle") defined by three points ![Image 121: {\displaystyle p_{1}=(x_{1},y_{1}),p_{2}=(x_{2},y_{2})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cc24706313458008bbc514288a5d11cf6691475f) and ![Image 122: {\displaystyle p_{3}=(x_{3},y_{3})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7e4192c622d48ec1f28d8947f3cda3a67aadab2d). It corresponds to the direction (upward or downward) of the cross product of the two coplanar [vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_(geometry) "Vector (geometry)") defined by the two pairs of points ![Image 123: {\displaystyle (p_{1},p_{2})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/aaa988e111a7acf37bf178e4a067e7db92a7a9fe) and ![Image 124: {\displaystyle (p_{1},p_{3})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/73535c3ec930bf7a5d166793c43ac5f2096d2c65). The sign of the acute angle is the sign of the expression

![Image 125: {\displaystyle P=(x_{2}-x_{1})(y_{3}-y_{1})-(y_{2}-y_{1})(x_{3}-x_{1}),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/210dc8f99c4badca5678b33d515bb4135ed4e34e)
which is the signed length of the cross product of the two vectors. To use the cross product, simply extend the 2D vectors ![Image 126: {\displaystyle p_{1},p_{2},p_{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/baa74f1b302485d081777988b0eb6579d144c853) to co-planar 3D vectors by setting ![Image 127: {\displaystyle z_{k}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9be50688d4b8ea6cec09c155f290b0de7d44749a) for each of them.

In the "right-handed" coordinate system, if the result is 0, the points are [collinear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collinear "Collinear"); if it is positive, the three points constitute a positive angle of rotation around ![Image 128: {\displaystyle p_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b9b58f22283ca46dd5da309cc34303b06a797783) from ![Image 129: {\displaystyle p_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/43f1b08d7d69712872e051c2b33fdfa9f5d42319) to ![Image 130: {\displaystyle p_{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2a79626b787857474daa665c953bbc6725e7c345), otherwise a negative angle. From another point of view, the sign of ![Image 131: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a) tells whether ![Image 132: {\displaystyle p_{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2a79626b787857474daa665c953bbc6725e7c345) lies to the left or to the right of line ![Image 133: {\displaystyle p_{1},p_{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/28c123768aa69c852d0177697ff2dae4d78d72b7)

The cross product is used in calculating the volume of a [polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyhedron "Polyhedron") such as a [tetrahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron#Volume "Tetrahedron") or [parallelepiped](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelepiped#Volume "Parallelepiped").

### Angular momentum and torque

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cross_product&action=edit&section=24 "Edit section: Angular momentum and torque")]

The [angular momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum "Angular momentum")**L** of a particle about a given origin is defined as:

![Image 134: {\displaystyle \mathbf {L} =\mathbf {r} \times \mathbf {p} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d6f9c1f2e0915e90da8390594af31f2904fd553b)
where **r** is the position vector of the particle relative to the origin, **p** is the linear momentum of the particle.

In the same way, the [moment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_(physics) "Moment (physics)")**M** of a force **F**B applied at point B around point A is given as:

![Image 135: {\displaystyle \mathbf {M} _{\mathrm {A} }=\mathbf {r} _{\mathrm {AB} }\times \mathbf {F} _{\mathrm {B} }\,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e1f9cd728aa36c77caccf3a5f20de29eb4d5652d)
In mechanics the _moment of a force_ is also called _[torque](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torque "Torque")_ and written as ![Image 136: {\displaystyle \mathbf {\tau } }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fcc46f8785eec5db11fc73af8c5f02473cd0fd71)

Since position **r**, linear momentum **p** and force **F** are all _true_ vectors, both the angular momentum **L** and the moment of a force **M** are _pseudovectors_ or _axial vectors_.

The cross product frequently appears in the description of rigid motions. Two points _P_ and _Q_ on a [rigid body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rigid_body "Rigid body") can be related by:

![Image 137: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} _{P}-\mathbf {v} _{Q}={\boldsymbol {\omega }}\times \left(\mathbf {r} _{P}-\mathbf {r} _{Q}\right)\,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c5b34474b510afd445f4606874dea1ab7dbc9141)
where ![Image 138: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eca0f46511c4c986c48b254073732c0bd98ae0c1) is the point's position, ![Image 139: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35c1866e359fbfd2e0f606c725ba5cc37a5195d6) is its velocity and ![Image 140: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {\omega }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7cb8af7a2f64af348e559652b6b1f0d2415ba444) is the body's [angular velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_velocity "Angular velocity").

Since position ![Image 141: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eca0f46511c4c986c48b254073732c0bd98ae0c1) and velocity ![Image 142: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35c1866e359fbfd2e0f606c725ba5cc37a5195d6) are _true_ vectors, the angular velocity ![Image 143: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {\omega }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7cb8af7a2f64af348e559652b6b1f0d2415ba444) is a _pseudovector_ or _axial vector_.

The cross product is used to describe the [Lorentz force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentz_force "Lorentz force") experienced by a moving electric charge _q e_:

![Image 144: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} =q_{e}\left(\mathbf {E} +\mathbf {v} \times \mathbf {B} \right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/799b9238a8f2183426d69bcff68f643a5039af90)
Since velocity **v**, force **F** and electric field **E** are all _true_ vectors, the magnetic field **B** is a _pseudovector_.

In [vector calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_calculus "Vector calculus"), the cross product is used to define the formula for the [vector operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_operator "Vector operator")[curl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curl_(mathematics) "Curl (mathematics)").

The trick of rewriting a cross product in terms of a matrix multiplication appears frequently in [epipolar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epipolar_geometry "Epipolar geometry") and multi-view geometry, in particular when deriving matching constraints.

As an external product
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cross_product&action=edit&section=28 "Edit section: As an external product")]

[![Image 145](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Exterior_calc_cross_product.svg/250px-Exterior_calc_cross_product.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Exterior_calc_cross_product.svg)

The cross product in relation to the exterior product. In red are the orthogonal [unit vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_vector "Unit vector"), and the "parallel" unit bivector.

The cross product can be defined in terms of the exterior product. It can be generalized to an [external product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product#External_product) in other than three dimensions.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product#cite_note-20) This generalization allows a natural geometric interpretation of the cross product. In [exterior algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_algebra "Exterior algebra") the exterior product of two vectors is a bivector. A bivector is an oriented plane element, in much the same way that a vector is an oriented line element. Given two vectors _a_ and _b_, one can view the bivector _a_ ∧ _b_ as the oriented parallelogram spanned by _a_ and _b_. The cross product is then obtained by taking the [Hodge star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hodge_star "Hodge star") of the bivector _a_ ∧ _b_, mapping [2-vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/P-vector "P-vector") to vectors:

![Image 146: {\displaystyle a\times b=\star (a\wedge b).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e1d5ac0b57cac1a3209637a9195e25a5119a7b34)
This can be thought of as the oriented multi-dimensional element "perpendicular" to the bivector. In a _d-_ dimensional space, Hodge star takes a _k_-vector to a (_d–k_)-vector; thus only in _d =_ 3 dimensions is the result an element of dimension one (3–2 = 1), i.e. a vector. For example, in _d =_ 4 dimensions, the cross product of two vectors has dimension 4–2 = 2, giving a bivector. Thus, only in three dimensions does cross product define an algebra structure to multiply vectors.

There are several ways to generalize the cross product to higher dimensions.

The cross product can be seen as one of the simplest Lie products, and is thus generalized by [Lie algebras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_algebra "Lie algebra"), which are axiomatized as binary products satisfying the axioms of multilinearity, skew-symmetry, and the Jacobi identity. Many Lie algebras exist, and their study is a major field of mathematics, called [Lie theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_theory "Lie theory").

For example, the [Heisenberg algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heisenberg_algebra "Heisenberg algebra") gives another Lie algebra structure on ![Image 147: {\displaystyle \mathbf {R} ^{3}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/621a5f78791e33a0d47e7e344046f77523425d9f) In the basis ![Image 148: {\displaystyle \{x,y,z\},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5738613a098266a2028c572b89d028f5f40a9736) the product is ![Image 149: {\displaystyle [x,y]=z,[x,z]=[y,z]=0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf27588f0b4586bd6a22e7e3d7c9d8513219e3ea)

The cross product can also be described in terms of [quaternions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quaternion "Quaternion"). In general, if a vector [_a_ 1, _a_ 2, _a_ 3] is represented as the quaternion _a_ 1 _i_ + _a_ 2 _j_ + _a_ 3 _k_, the cross product of two vectors can be obtained by taking their product as quaternions and deleting the real part of the result. The real part will be the negative of the dot product of the two vectors.

A cross product for 7-dimensional vectors can be obtained in the same way by using the [octonions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octonion "Octonion") instead of the quaternions. The nonexistence of nontrivial vector-valued cross products of two vectors in other dimensions is related to the result from [Hurwitz's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hurwitz%27s_theorem_(normed_division_algebras) "Hurwitz's theorem (normed division algebras)") that the only [normed division algebras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normed_division_algebra "Normed division algebra") are the ones with dimension 1, 2, 4, and 8.

In general dimension, there is no direct analogue of the binary cross product that yields specifically a vector. There is however the exterior product, which has similar properties, except that the exterior product of two vectors is now a [2-vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/P-vector "P-vector") instead of an ordinary vector. As mentioned above, the cross product can be interpreted as the exterior product in three dimensions by using the [Hodge star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hodge_star "Hodge star") operator to map 2-vectors to vectors. The Hodge dual of the exterior product yields an (_n_ − 2)-vector, which is a natural generalization of the cross product in any number of dimensions.

The exterior product and dot product can be combined (through summation) to form the [geometric product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_algebra "Geometric algebra") in geometric algebra.

As mentioned above, the cross product can be interpreted in three dimensions as the Hodge dual of the exterior product. In any finite _n_ dimensions, the Hodge dual of the exterior product of _n_ − 1 vectors is a vector. So, instead of a binary operation, in arbitrary finite dimensions, the cross product is generalized as the Hodge dual of the exterior product of some given _n_ − 1 vectors. This generalization is called **external product**.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product#cite_note-21)

Interpreting the three-dimensional [vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space") of the algebra as the [2-vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bivector "Bivector") (not the 1-vector) [subalgebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graded_vector_space "Graded vector space") of the three-dimensional geometric algebra, where ![Image 150: {\displaystyle \mathbf {i} =\mathbf {e_{2}} \mathbf {e_{3}} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4bc41789935f9bb10217849a63ae7603efd51548), ![Image 151: {\displaystyle \mathbf {j} =\mathbf {e_{1}} \mathbf {e_{3}} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b9d434d81dc1ab28da683fc7be160935b613a18d), and ![Image 152: {\displaystyle \mathbf {k} =\mathbf {e_{1}} \mathbf {e_{2}} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d956a585cd57b1f6f3765580f78d2abf3833eb1f), the cross product corresponds exactly to the [commutator product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_algebra#Extensions_of_the_inner_and_exterior_products "Geometric algebra") in geometric algebra and both use the same symbol ![Image 153: {\displaystyle \times }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0ffafff1ad26cbe49045f19a67ce532116a32703). The commutator product is defined for 2-vectors ![Image 154: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3) and ![Image 155: {\displaystyle B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47136aad860d145f75f3eed3022df827cee94d7a) in geometric algebra as:

![Image 156: {\displaystyle A\times B={\tfrac {1}{2}}(AB-BA),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0e58d1da5ae34764872368ce27065b8e3e346fd8)
where ![Image 157: {\displaystyle AB}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b04153f9681e5b06066357774475c04aaef3a8bd) is the geometric product.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product#cite_note-22)

The commutator product could be generalised to arbitrary [multivectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multivector#Geometric_algebra "Multivector") in three dimensions, which results in a multivector consisting of only elements of [grades](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graded_vector_space "Graded vector space") 1 (1-vectors/[true vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product#Handedness)) and 2 (2-vectors/pseudovectors). While the commutator product of two 1-vectors is indeed the same as the exterior product and yields a 2-vector, the commutator of a 1-vector and a 2-vector yields a true vector, corresponding instead to the [left and right contractions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_algebra#Extensions_of_the_inner_and_exterior_products "Geometric algebra") in geometric algebra. The commutator product of two 2-vectors has no corresponding equivalent product, which is why the commutator product is defined in the first place for 2-vectors. Furthermore, the commutator triple product of three 2-vectors is the same as the [vector triple product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_triple_product "Vector triple product") of the same three pseudovectors in vector algebra. However, the commutator triple product of three 1-vectors in geometric algebra is instead the [negative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sign_(mathematics)#Sign_of_a_direction "Sign (mathematics)") of the [vector triple product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_triple_product "Vector triple product") of the same three true vectors in vector algebra.

Generalizations to higher dimensions is provided by the same commutator product of 2-vectors in higher-dimensional geometric algebras, but the 2-vectors are no longer pseudovectors. Just as the commutator product/cross product of 2-vectors in three dimensions [correspond to the simplest Lie algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product#Lie_algebra), the 2-vector subalgebras of higher dimensional geometric algebra equipped with the commutator product also correspond to the Lie algebras.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product#cite_note-23) Also as in three dimensions, the commutator product could be further generalised to arbitrary multivectors.

### Multilinear algebra

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cross_product&action=edit&section=36 "Edit section: Multilinear algebra")]

In the context of [multilinear algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multilinear_algebra "Multilinear algebra"), the cross product can be seen as the (1,2)-tensor (a [mixed tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mixed_tensor "Mixed tensor"), specifically a [bilinear map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bilinear_map "Bilinear map")) obtained from the 3-dimensional [volume form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume_form "Volume form"),[[note 2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product#cite_note-24) a (0,3)-tensor, by [raising an index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raising_and_lowering_indices "Raising and lowering indices").

In detail, the 3-dimensional volume form defines a product ![Image 158: {\displaystyle V\times V\times V\to \mathbf {R} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e1d8d4b9e1dd451129b07c62494a9fb9188dca78) by taking the determinant of the matrix given by these 3 vectors. By [duality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_space "Dual space"), this is equivalent to a function ![Image 159: {\displaystyle V\times V\to V^{*},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/65c478b9b5d0e101dd541954435d6a49ffd4493e) (fixing any two inputs gives a function ![Image 160: {\displaystyle V\to \mathbf {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/30534e02fed26a26f80c4b15146efd1d141038a1) by evaluating on the third input) and in the presence of an [inner product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inner_product "Inner product") (such as the dot product; more generally, a non-degenerate [bilinear form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bilinear_form "Bilinear form")), we have an isomorphism ![Image 161: {\displaystyle V\to V^{*},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/db276e2672d2bd1926cdae1ce5368a8ca01fd685) and thus this yields a map ![Image 162: {\displaystyle V\times V\to V,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6f1f6ccf08a4e3c583e80259351b6188ab96e3b8) which is the cross product: a (0,3)-tensor (3 vector inputs, scalar output) has been transformed into a (1,2)-tensor (2 vector inputs, 1 vector output) by "raising an index".

Translating the above algebra into geometry, the function "volume of the parallelepiped defined by ![Image 163: {\displaystyle (a,b,-)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dc21a1d4c65d0fc51564a9640351d92c5048f973)" (where the first two vectors are fixed and the last is an input), which defines a function ![Image 164: {\displaystyle V\to \mathbf {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/30534e02fed26a26f80c4b15146efd1d141038a1), can be _represented_ uniquely as the dot product with a vector: this vector is the cross product ![Image 165: {\displaystyle a\times b.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/951b3d74659ad389120ff04c748c1dd8de0f8530) From this perspective, the cross product is _defined_ by the [scalar triple product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_triple_product "Scalar triple product"), ![Image 166: {\displaystyle \mathrm {Vol} (a,b,c)=(a\times b)\cdot c.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cc8bf49b45055c8c8c4b1dc6ec01d16e0b7837bc)

In the same way, in higher dimensions one may define generalized cross products by raising indices of the _n_-dimensional volume form, which is a ![Image 167: {\displaystyle (0,n)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fda9516a58dc3bd99e060e9ec8565620a57a3a9e)-tensor. These products are all multilinear and skew-symmetric, and can be defined in terms of the determinant and [parity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parity_(physics) "Parity (physics)").

This is the unique multilinear, alternating product which evaluates to ![Image 173: {\displaystyle e_{1}\times \cdots \times e_{n-1}=e_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9e047ff00009e196bcdb21a66a6730c2407448bf), ![Image 174: {\displaystyle e_{2}\times \cdots \times e_{n}=e_{1},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4a7b8c1fb738081be9f6cf9dee31cb65376b4d96) and so forth for cyclic permutations of indices.

In coordinates, one can give a formula for this ![Image 175: {\displaystyle (n-1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/df88c6333caaf6471cf277f24b802ff9931b133e)-ary analogue of the cross product in **R**_n_ by:

In the case that _n_ is even, however, the distinction must be kept. And just like the vector cross product, it can be defined in a coordinate independent way as the Hodge dual of the wedge product of the arguments. Moreover, the product ![Image 178: {\displaystyle [v_{1},\ldots ,v_{n}]:=\bigwedge _{i=0}^{n}v_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a3ded7786514805c536cc7c4063b827b634303f2) satisfies the Filippov identity,

![Image 179: {\displaystyle [[x_{1},\ldots ,x_{n}],y_{2},\ldots ,y_{n}]]=\sum _{i=1}^{n}[x_{1},\ldots ,x_{i-1},[x_{i},y_{2},\ldots ,y_{n}],x_{i+1},\ldots ,x_{n}],}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8cec8eccbfa6d7d839058f46452d3339623469e9)
and so it endows **R**n+1 with a structure of n-Lie algebra (see Proposition 1 of [[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product#cite_note-25)).

In 1773, [Joseph-Louis Lagrange](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph-Louis_Lagrange "Joseph-Louis Lagrange") used the component form of both the dot and cross products in order to study the [tetrahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron "Tetrahedron") in three dimensions.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product#cite_note-26)[[note 3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product#cite_note-27)

In 1843, [William Rowan Hamilton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Rowan_Hamilton "William Rowan Hamilton") introduced the [quaternion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quaternion "Quaternion") product, and with it the terms _vector_ and _scalar_. Given two quaternions [0, **u**] and [0, **v**], where **u** and **v** are vectors in **R**3, their quaternion product can be summarized as [−**u** ⋅ **v**, **u** × **v**]. [James Clerk Maxwell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Clerk_Maxwell "James Clerk Maxwell") used Hamilton's quaternion tools to develop his famous [electromagnetism equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxwell%27s_equations "Maxwell's equations"), and for this and other reasons quaternions for a time were an essential part of physics education.

In 1844, [Hermann Grassmann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Grassmann "Hermann Grassmann") published a geometric algebra not tied to dimension two or three. Grassmann developed several products, including a cross product represented then by [uv].[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product#cite_note-FOOTNOTECajori1929[httpsarchiveorgdetailshistoryofmathema00cajo_0pages134_134]-28) (_See also: [exterior algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior\_algebra "Exterior algebra")._)

In 1853, [Augustin-Louis Cauchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Augustin-Louis_Cauchy "Augustin-Louis Cauchy"), a contemporary of Grassmann, published a paper on algebraic keys which were used to solve equations and had the same multiplication properties as the cross product.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrowe1994[httpsarchiveorgdetailshistoryofvectora0000crowpage83_83]-29)[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product#cite_note-30)

In 1878, [William Kingdon Clifford](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Kingdon_Clifford "William Kingdon Clifford"), known for a [precursor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_algebra "Geometric algebra") to the [Clifford algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_algebra "Clifford algebra") named in his honor, published _[Elements of Dynamic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elements\_of\_Dynamic "Elements of Dynamic")_, in which the term _vector product_ is attested. In the book, this product of two vectors is defined to have magnitude equal to the [area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area "Area") of the [parallelogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram "Parallelogram") of which they are two sides, and direction perpendicular to their plane.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product#cite_note-31)

In lecture notes from 1881, [Gibbs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Josiah_Willard_Gibbs "Josiah Willard Gibbs") represented the cross product by ![Image 180: {\displaystyle u\times v}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dedbd3ae14284142498f3ff0777c08d2501df031) and called it the _skew product_.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product#cite_note-32)[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrowe1994[httpsarchiveorgdetailshistoryofvectora0000crowpage154_154]-33) In 1901, Gibb's student [Edwin Bidwell Wilson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edwin_Bidwell_Wilson "Edwin Bidwell Wilson") edited and extended these lecture notes into the [textbook](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Textbook "Textbook")_[Vector Analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector\_Analysis "Vector Analysis")_. Wilson kept the term _skew product_, but observed that the alternative terms _cross product_[[note 4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product#cite_note-34) and _vector product_ were more frequent.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWilson1901[httpsarchiveorgdetails117714283page61_61]-35)

In 1908, [Cesare Burali-Forti](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cesare_Burali-Forti "Cesare Burali-Forti") and [Roberto Marcolongo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roberto_Marcolongo "Roberto Marcolongo") introduced the vector product notation u ∧ v.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product#cite_note-FOOTNOTECajori1929[httpsarchiveorgdetailshistoryofmathema00cajo_0pages134_134]-28) This is used in [France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/France "France") and other areas until this day, as the symbol ![Image 181: {\displaystyle \times }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0ffafff1ad26cbe49045f19a67ce532116a32703) is already used to denote [multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication "Multiplication") and the [Cartesian product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_product "Cartesian product").[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

2.   **[^](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product#cite_ref-24)**By a volume form one means a function that takes in _n_ vectors and gives out a scalar, the volume of the [parallelotope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelepiped#Parallelotope "Parallelepiped") defined by the vectors: ![Image 182: {\displaystyle V\times \cdots \times V\to \mathbf {R} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eae9b5cc99f9f9a1d5b19be63d5e29b1017aa570) This is an _n_-ary multilinear skew-symmetric form. In the presence of a basis, such as on ![Image 183: {\displaystyle \mathbf {R} ^{n},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ab2b659e3536671c46ccafcb75d6be9dec6899ce) this is given by the determinant, but in an abstract vector space, this is added structure. In terms of [_G_-structures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G-structure "G-structure"), a volume form is an [![Image 184: {\displaystyle SL}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/61c38625f8201fc63c5b85fe08aee863ba84b509)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_linear_group "Special linear group")-structure.
3.   **[^](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product#cite_ref-27)**In modern notation, Lagrange defines ![Image 185: {\displaystyle \mathbf {\xi } =\mathbf {y} \times \mathbf {z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d9cb1af100b44e2fea483c930da2833d10c867d9), ![Image 186: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {\eta }}=\mathbf {z} \times \mathbf {x} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/499128d381ede615e0b3b7917741b4e50ea2cff9), and ![Image 187: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {\zeta }}=\mathbf {x} \times {\boldsymbol {y}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f1b0286092be3398b5098f1f041055181f0a9a1e). Thereby, the modern ![Image 188: {\displaystyle \mathbf {x} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/32adf004df5eb0a8c7fd8c0b6b7405183c5a5ef2) corresponds to the three variables ![Image 189: {\displaystyle (x,x',x'')}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a8261457d91534189aa15cb51cb7f5eba370c8e9) in Lagrange's notation.
