Galaxy filaments, walls and voids form web-like structures. Computer simulation.

In [cosmology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmology "Cosmology"), **galaxy filaments** are the largest known structures in the [universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe "Universe"), consisting of walls of galactic [superclusters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supercluster "Supercluster"). These massive, thread-like formations can commonly reach 50 to 80 [megaparsecs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parsec#Megaparsecs_and_gigaparsecs "Parsec") (160 to 260 megalight-years)—with the largest found to date being [Quipu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quipu_(supercluster) "Quipu (supercluster)") (400 megaparsecs),[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_filament#cite_note-gough2025-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_filament#cite_note-boehringer2025-2) and possibly the still unconfirmed [Hercules-Corona Borealis Great Wall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hercules%E2%80%93Corona_Borealis_Great_Wall "Hercules–Corona Borealis Great Wall") at around 3 gigaparsecs (9.8 Gly) in length—and form the boundaries between [voids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Void_(astronomy) "Void (astronomy)").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_filament#cite_note-3) Due to the accelerating expansion of the universe, the individual clusters of gravitationally bound galaxies that make up galaxy filaments are moving away from each other at an accelerated rate; in the far future they will dissolve.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_filament#cite_note-4)

Galaxy filaments form the [cosmic web](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_Web "Cosmic Web") and define the overall structure of the observable universe.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_filament#cite_note-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_filament#cite_note-astro-ph/9602090-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_filament#cite_note-2001ASPC..232..108C-7)

Discovery of structures larger than superclusters began in the late 1980s. In 1987, astronomer [R. Brent Tully](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/R._Brent_Tully "R. Brent Tully") of the [University of Hawaii](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Hawaii "University of Hawaii")'s Institute of Astronomy identified what he called the [Pisces–Cetus Supercluster Complex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pisces%E2%80%93Cetus_Supercluster_Complex "Pisces–Cetus Supercluster Complex"). The [CfA2 Great Wall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CfA2_Great_Wall "CfA2 Great Wall") was discovered in 1989,[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_filament#cite_note-8) followed by the [Sloan Great Wall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sloan_Great_Wall "Sloan Great Wall") in 2003.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_filament#cite_note-9)

In January 2013, researchers led by Roger Clowes of the [University of Central Lancashire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Central_Lancashire "University of Central Lancashire") announced the discovery of a [large quasar group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Large_quasar_group "Large quasar group"), the [Huge-LQG](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Huge-LQG "Huge-LQG"), which dwarfs previously discovered galaxy filaments in size.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_filament#cite_note-10) In November 2013, using [gamma-ray bursts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamma-ray_burst "Gamma-ray burst") as reference points, astronomers discovered the [Hercules–Corona Borealis Great Wall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hercules%E2%80%93Corona_Borealis_Great_Wall "Hercules–Corona Borealis Great Wall"), an extremely large filament measuring more than 10 billion light-years across.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_filament#cite_note-2014paper-11)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_filament#cite_note-original-12)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_filament#cite_note-conundrum-13)

The filament subtype of filaments have roughly similar major and minor axes in cross-section, along the lengthwise axis.

*   A short filament was proposed by Adi Zitrin and [Noah Brosch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noah_Brosch "Noah Brosch")—detected by identifying an alignment of star-forming galaxies—in the neighborhood of the [Milky Way](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milky_Way "Milky Way") and the [Local Group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_Group "Local Group").[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_filament#cite_note-22) The proposal of this filament, and of a similar but shorter filament, were the result of a study by McQuinn _et al._ (2014) based on distance measurements using the [TRGB](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tip_of_the_red-giant_branch "Tip of the red-giant branch") method.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_filament#cite_note-23)

The **galaxy wall** subtype of filaments have a significantly greater major axis than minor axis in cross-section, along the lengthwise axis.

*   A "Centaurus Great Wall" (or "Fornax Great Wall" or "Virgo Great Wall") has been proposed, which would include the [Fornax Wall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fornax_Wall "Fornax Wall") as a portion of it (visually created by the [Zone of Avoidance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zone_of_Avoidance "Zone of Avoidance")) along with the [Centaurus Supercluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centaurus_Supercluster "Centaurus Supercluster") and the [Virgo Supercluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgo_Supercluster "Virgo Supercluster"), also known as the Local Supercluster, within which the Milky Way galaxy is located (implying this to be the Local Great Wall).[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_filament#cite_note-LzLSS-26)[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_filament#cite_note-LSSg-27)
*   A wall was proposed to be the physical embodiment of the [Great Attractor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Attractor "Great Attractor"), with the [Norma Cluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norma_Cluster "Norma Cluster") as part of it. It is sometimes referred to as the [Great Attractor Wall](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Great_Attractor_Wall&action=edit&redlink=1 "Great Attractor Wall (page does not exist)") or [Norma Wall](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Norma_Wall&action=edit&redlink=1 "Norma Wall (page does not exist)").[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_filament#cite_note-28) This suggestion was superseded by the proposal of a supercluster, [Laniakea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laniakea_Supercluster "Laniakea Supercluster"), that would encompass the Great Attractor, Virgo Supercluster, [Hydra–Centaurus Superclusters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydra%E2%80%93Centaurus_Supercluster "Hydra–Centaurus Supercluster").[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_filament#cite_note-2014arXiv1409.0880T-29)
*   A wall was proposed in 2000 to lie at z=1.47 in the vicinity of radio galaxy [B3 0003+387](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=B3_0003%2B387&action=edit&redlink=1 "B3 0003+387 (page does not exist)").[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_filament#cite_note-30)
*   A wall was proposed in 2000 to lie at z=0.559 in the northern [Hubble Deep Field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble_Deep_Field "Hubble Deep Field") (HDF North).[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_filament#cite_note-31)[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_filament#cite_note-32)

### Large quasar groups

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Galaxy_filament&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Large quasar groups")]

[Large quasar groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Large_quasar_group "Large quasar group") (LQGs) are some of the largest structures known.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_filament#cite_note-ScienceDaily-2013-01-11-33) They are theorized to be protohyperclusters/proto-supercluster-complexes/galaxy filament precursors.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_filament#cite_note-10.1093/mnras/sts497-34)
