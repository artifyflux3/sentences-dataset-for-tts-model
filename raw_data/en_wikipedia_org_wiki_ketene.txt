From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

This article is about the generic structure class. For the specific chemical of this name, see [ethenone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethenone "Ethenone").

[![Image 1](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Ketenes_nonsymmetric.png/250px-Ketenes_nonsymmetric.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Ketenes_nonsymmetric.png)

General formula for a ketene

In [organic chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_chemistry "Organic chemistry"), a **ketene** is an [organic compound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_compound "Organic compound") of the form RR'C=C=O, where R and R' are two arbitrary [monovalent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Valence_(chemistry) "Valence (chemistry)")[chemical groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functional_group "Functional group") (or two separate [substitution sites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Substituent "Substituent") in the same molecule).[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ketene#cite_note-Ullmann-1) The name may also refer to the specific compound [ethenone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethenone "Ethenone")H 2 C=C=O, the simplest ketene.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ketene#cite_note-2)

Although they are highly useful, most ketenes are [unstable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_stability "Chemical stability"). When used as [reagents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reagent "Reagent") in a chemical procedure, they are typically generated when needed, and consumed as soon as (or while) they are produced.

Ketenes were first studied as a class by [Hermann Staudinger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Staudinger "Hermann Staudinger") before 1905.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ketene#cite_note-3)

Ketenes were systematically investigated by Hermann Staudinger in 1905 in the form of diphenylketene (conversion of ![Image 2: {\displaystyle \alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b79333175c8b3f0840bfb4ec41b8072c83ea88d3)-chlorodiphenyl acetyl chloride with zinc). Staudinger was inspired by the first examples of reactive organic intermediates and stable radicals discovered by [Moses Gomberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moses_Gomberg "Moses Gomberg") in 1900 (compounds with triphenylmethyl group).[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ketene#cite_note-4)

Ketenes are highly electrophilic at the carbon atom bonded with the heteroatom, due to its _sp_ character. Ketenes can be formed with different heteroatoms bonded to the _sp_ carbon atom, such as [O](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen "Oxygen"), [S](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulphur "Sulphur") or [Se](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Selenium "Selenium"), respectively called ketenes, [thioketenes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thioketene "Thioketene") and selenoketenes.

[Ethenone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethenone "Ethenone"), the simplest ketene, has different experimental lengths for each of its double bonds; the C=O bond is 1.160 [Å](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angstrom "Angstrom") and the C=C bond is 1.314 Å. The angle between the two [H](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen") atoms is 121.5°, similar to the theoretically ideal angle in [alkenes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkene "Alkene") between _sp 2_ carbon atoms and H substituents.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ketene#cite_note-5)

Ketenes are unstable and cannot be stored. Absent nucleophiles with which to react, they dimerise (see [§Reactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ketene#Reactions)).

Ethenone is produced on a commercial scale by thermal dehydration of [acetic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid "Acetic acid"). Substituted ketenes can be prepared from [acyl chlorides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acyl_chloride "Acyl chloride") by an [elimination reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elimination_reaction "Elimination reaction") in which [HCl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_chloride "Hydrogen chloride") is lost:

[![Image 3: Formation of a ketene from an acyl chloride.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e0/Mecanisme-de-la-formation-des-cetenes.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Mecanisme-de-la-formation-des-cetenes.png "Formation of a ketene from an acyl chloride.")

Formation of a ketene from an acyl chloride.

In this reaction, a base, usually [triethylamine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triethylamine "Triethylamine"), removes the [acidic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acid "Acid")[proton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton "Proton") alpha to the [carbonyl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonyl "Carbonyl") group, inducing the formation of the carbon-carbon double bond and the loss of a [chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chloride "Chloride") ion:

[![Image 4: Synthesis of Ketene](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Ketene_Synthesis.png/500px-Ketene_Synthesis.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Ketene_Synthesis.png "Synthesis of Ketene")

Ketenes can also be formed from α-[diazoketones](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Diazoketone&action=edit&redlink=1 "Diazoketone (page does not exist)") by the [Wolff rearrangement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolff_rearrangement "Wolff rearrangement"), and from [vinylene carbonate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vinylene_carbonate "Vinylene carbonate") by [phosphorus(V) sulfide](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Phosphorus(V)_sulfide&action=edit&redlink=1 "Phosphorus(V) sulfide (page does not exist)") and irradiation.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ketene#cite_note-6)

Another way to generate ketenes is through [flash vacuum thermolysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flash_vacuum_thermolysis "Flash vacuum thermolysis") (FVT) with 2-[pyridylamines](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Pyridylamine&action=edit&redlink=1 "Pyridylamine (page does not exist)"). Plüg and Wentrup developed a method in 1997 that improved on FVT reactions to produce ketenes with a stable FVT that is moisture insensitive, using mild conditions (480°C). The N-pyridylamines are prepared via a condensation with R-[malonates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malonate "Malonate") with N-amino([pyridene](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Pyridene&action=edit&redlink=1 "Pyridene (page does not exist)")) and [DCC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/N,N%27-Dicyclohexylcarbodiimide "N,N'-Dicyclohexylcarbodiimide") as the solvent.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ketene#cite_note-7)

A more robust method for preparing ketenes is the [carbonylation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonylation "Carbonylation") of [metal-carbenes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transition_metal_carbene_complex "Transition metal carbene complex"), and _in situ_ reaction of the thus produced highly reactive ketenes with suitable reagents such as [imines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imines "Imines"), [amines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amines "Amines"), or [alcohols](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alcohols "Alcohols").[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ketene#cite_note-8) This method is an efficient [one‐pot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One-pot_synthesis "One-pot synthesis") tandem protocol of the carbonylation of α‐diazocarbonyl compounds and a variety of _N_‐tosylhydrazones catalysed by Co(II)–[porphyrin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Porphyrin "Porphyrin") metalloradicals leading to the formation of ketenes, which subsequently react with a variety of [nucleophiles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleophile "Nucleophile") and imines to form [esters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Esters "Esters"), [amides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amides "Amides") and [β‐lactams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beta-lactam "Beta-lactam"). This system has a broad substrate scope and can be applied to various combinations of [carbene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbene "Carbene") precursors, nucleophiles and imines.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ketene#cite_note-9)

[Ethenone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethenone "Ethenone") can be produced through [pyrolysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrolysis "Pyrolysis") of [acetone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone "Acetone") vapours over a hot filament in an apparatus that was eventually developed into the "ketene lamp" or "Hurd lamp" (named for Charles D. Hurd).[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ketene#cite_note-10)

Due to their [cumulated double bonds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cumulated_double_bond "Cumulated double bond"), ketenes are very reactive.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ketene#cite_note-:1-11) The [free energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gibbs_free_energy "Gibbs free energy") released in their saturation can power the formation of relatively strained rings.

Ketenes are strong [acylating agents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acylating_agent "Acylating agent"). They react with carboxylic acids to form [carboxylic acid anhydrides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carboxylic_acid_anhydride "Carboxylic acid anhydride")...

[![Image 5](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Ketene_Reaktion2_V1.svg/500px-Ketene_Reaktion2_V1.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Ketene_Reaktion2_V1.svg)

...with [alcohols](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alcohol_(chemistry) "Alcohol (chemistry)") to form [carboxylic acid esters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carboxylic_acid_ester "Carboxylic acid ester")...

[![Image 6](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Ketene_Reaktion1_V1.svg/500px-Ketene_Reaktion1_V1.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Ketene_Reaktion1_V1.svg)

...with [amines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amine "Amine") to give [amides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amide "Amide")...

[![Image 7](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Ketene_Reaktion4_V1.svg/500px-Ketene_Reaktion4_V1.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Ketene_Reaktion4_V1.svg)

...with [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxidane "Oxidane") to give carboxylic acids...

[![Image 8](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Ketene_Reaktion6_V1.svg/500px-Ketene_Reaktion6_V1.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Ketene_Reaktion6_V1.svg)

...and with [enolisable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enol "Enol")[carbonyl compounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonyl_compounds "Carbonyl compounds") to give [enol esters](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Enol_ester&action=edit&redlink=1 "Enol ester (page does not exist)"). For example, [ethenone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethenone "Ethenone") reacts with [acetone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone "Acetone") to form a propen-2-yl acetate:[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ketene#cite_note-Ullmann-1)

[![Image 9](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Ketene_Reaktion7_V3_unlabeled.svg/500px-Ketene_Reaktion7_V3_unlabeled.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Ketene_Reaktion7_V3_unlabeled.svg)

As first observed in 1908,[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ketene#cite_note-12) ketenes react with virtually any [electron-rich](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron-rich "Electron-rich")[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ketene#cite_note-:0-13) π bond to form 4-membered rings.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ketene#cite_note-Ullmann-1) For example, in the [Staudinger synthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Staudinger_synthesis "Staudinger synthesis"),[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ketene#cite_note-:2-14)[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ketene#cite_note-:3-15) a ketene attacks an [imine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imine "Imine") to form a [β-lactam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%CE%92-lactam "Β-lactam"):

[![Image 10](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Staudinger-Synthese_%C3%9CV6.svg/500px-Staudinger-Synthese_%C3%9CV6.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Staudinger-Synthese_%C3%9CV6.svg)

Ketenes also cyclize onto [enolic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enol "Enol") and [enaminic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enamine "Enamine") alkenes, [carbodiimides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbodiimides "Carbodiimides"), and electron-rich [alkynes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkyne "Alkyne") (the latter forming [cyclobutenones](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cyclobutenone&action=edit&redlink=1 "Cyclobutenone (page does not exist)")). _cis_ Alkenes react more easily than _trans_ alkenes.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ketene#cite_note-16) Electron-withdrawing substituents on the ketene accelerate the reaction,[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ketene#cite_note-:0-13) but disubstituted ketenes react slowly due to steric hindrance.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ketene#cite_note-17)

Ketenes attack [ketones and aldehydes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonyl_group "Carbonyl group") to give β-lactones, but only under Lewis acid catalysis or when the carbonyl is electron-impoverished:[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ketene#cite_note-18)

[![Image 11](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e3/KetScope4.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:KetScope4.png)

[Dienes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diene "Diene") generally react as two separate alkenes, and [fulvenes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fulvenes "Fulvenes") typically react in the ring, leaving the exocyclic double bond intact:[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ketene#cite_note-19)

[![Image 12](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/3d/KetScope3.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:KetScope3.png)

[2+2] cycloadditions proceed by a concerted, thermal mechanism, which [requires](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Woodward%E2%80%93Hoffmann_rules "Woodward–Hoffmann rules") suprafacial- antarafacial alignment. Ketenes, unlike most alkenes, can align [antarafacially](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antarafacial_and_suprafacial "Antarafacial and suprafacial") with respect to other alkenes.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ketene#cite_note-20) The unique [transition state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transition_state "Transition state") geometry has the interesting consequence that the bulkier substituent on the ketene will tend to end up on the more sterically hindered face of the cyclobutanone ring. In the transition state for cyclization, the small substituent points toward the alkene.

[![Image 13](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/ca/KetMech.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:KetMech.png)

Ketenes place the larger substituent in the _endo_ position when attacking cyclic alkenes.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ketene#cite_note-21)

The use of chiral amine catalysts has allowed access to cycloaddition products in high enantiomeric excess.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ketene#cite_note-22)

[![Image 14](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/25/KetStereo.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:KetStereo.png)

#### Higher-length cycloadditions

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ketene&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Higher-length cycloadditions")]

In rarer cases, ketenes may undergo [3+2], and [4+2] cycloadditions.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ketene#cite_note-23)

[![Image 15](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f0/KetGen.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:KetGen.png)

[3+2] Cycloadditions may take place with [1,3-dipoles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1,3-dipole "1,3-dipole"). This process appears to be concerted, but either ketenic double-bond can react.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ketene#cite_note-24)

[![Image 16](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/6e/KetScope5.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:KetScope5.png)

[Michael acceptors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michael_acceptor "Michael acceptor") often react in a [4+2] fashion:[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ketene#cite_note-25)

[![Image 17](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b5/KetScope6.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:KetScope6.png)

Conjugated ketenes may act as 4π partners in [4+2] cycloadditions as well.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ketene#cite_note-26) Examples in which a vinylketene serves as the 4π partner are rare, but occur with some ketene-conjugated heterodienes:[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ketene#cite_note-27)

[![Image 18](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/80/KetScope7.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:KetScope7.png)

Ketenes autodimerize to give various products. The parent reacts acylates itself to form [diketene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diketene "Diketene"), a [β-lactone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beta-Lactone "Beta-Lactone"), whereas disubstituted ketenes undergo [2+2] cycloaddition to a substituted cyclobutadione:[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ketene#cite_note-28)

[![Image 19: Dimerisation of ketene](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Dimerisation_of_ketene.png/500px-Dimerisation_of_ketene.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Dimerisation_of_ketene.png "Dimerisation of ketene")
Monosubstituted ketenes can afford either the ester or diketone dimer.

Although many polar solvents and catalysts accelerate many reactions using ketene, such reactions are normally performed in nonpolar media to prevent dimerization.

Dimerization of [stearic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stearic "Stearic") ketene affords [alkyl ketene dimers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkyl_ketene_dimer "Alkyl ketene dimer"), widely used in the paper industry.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ketene#cite_note-Ullmann-1) AKD's react with the hydroxyl groups on the cellulose via [esterification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Esterification "Esterification") reaction.

Likewise, [diols](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diol "Diol") (HO−R−OH) and bis-ketenes (O=C=CH−R'−CH=C=O) react to yield [polyesters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyester "Polyester") with a repeat unit of (−O−R−O−CO−R'−CO).

The [Staudinger synthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Staudinger_synthesis "Staudinger synthesis") is used to synthesize [β-lactam antibiotics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%CE%92-Lactam_antibiotic "Β-Lactam antibiotic").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ketene#cite_note-Ullmann-1)

[Ethyl acetoacetate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethyl_acetoacetate "Ethyl acetoacetate"), an organic synthesis feedstock, is prepared industrially from [diketene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diketene "Diketene") in [ethanol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethanol "Ethanol").[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]
