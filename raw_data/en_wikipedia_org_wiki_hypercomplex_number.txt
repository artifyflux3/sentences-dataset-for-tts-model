In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), **hypercomplex number** is a traditional term for an [element](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Element_(mathematics) "Element (mathematics)") of a finite-dimensional [unital](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra_over_a_field#Unital_algebra "Algebra over a field")[algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra_over_a_field "Algebra over a field") over the [field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)") of [real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number"). The study of hypercomplex numbers in the late 19th century forms the basis of modern [group representation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_representation "Group representation") theory.

[![Image 1](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Common_associative_algebras_over_the_reals.svg/330px-Common_associative_algebras_over_the_reals.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Common_associative_algebras_over_the_reals.svg)

A [lattice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lattice_(order) "Lattice (order)") of some hypercomplex number systems and the corresponding lattice of [groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_(mathematics) "Group (mathematics)") generated by the basis elements

In the nineteenth century, [number systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_system "Number system") called [quaternions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quaternion "Quaternion"), [tessarines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tessarine "Tessarine"), [coquaternions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coquaternion "Coquaternion"), [biquaternions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biquaternion "Biquaternion"), and [octonions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octonion "Octonion") became established concepts in mathematical literature, extending the real and [complex numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number"). The concept of a hypercomplex number covered them all, and called for a discipline to explain and classify them.

The cataloguing project began in 1872 when [Benjamin Peirce](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benjamin_Peirce "Benjamin Peirce") first published his _Linear Associative Algebra_, and was carried forward by his son [Charles Sanders Peirce](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Sanders_Peirce "Charles Sanders Peirce").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypercomplex_number#cite_note-1) Most significantly, they identified the [nilpotent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nilpotent "Nilpotent") and the [idempotent elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Idempotent_element_(ring_theory) "Idempotent element (ring theory)") as useful hypercomplex numbers for classifications. The [Cayley–Dickson construction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cayley%E2%80%93Dickson_construction "Cayley–Dickson construction") used [involutions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Involution_(mathematics) "Involution (mathematics)") to generate complex numbers, quaternions, and octonions out of the real number system. Hurwitz and Frobenius proved theorems that put limits on hypercomplexity: [Hurwitz's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hurwitz%27s_theorem_(normed_division_algebras) "Hurwitz's theorem (normed division algebras)") says finite-dimensional real [composition algebras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Composition_algebra "Composition algebra") are the reals ![Image 2: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc), the complexes ![Image 3: {\displaystyle \mathbb {C} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f9add4085095b9b6d28d045fd9c92c2c09f549a7), the quaternions ![Image 4: {\displaystyle \mathbb {H} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e050965453c42bcc6bd544546703c836bdafeac9), and the octonions ![Image 5: {\displaystyle \mathbb {O} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c1ed2664a4fe515e6fbed25a7193ce663b82920c), and the [Frobenius theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frobenius_theorem_(real_division_algebras) "Frobenius theorem (real division algebras)") says the only real [associative division algebras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Associative_division_algebra "Associative division algebra") are ![Image 6: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc), ![Image 7: {\displaystyle \mathbb {C} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f9add4085095b9b6d28d045fd9c92c2c09f549a7), and ![Image 8: {\displaystyle \mathbb {H} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e050965453c42bcc6bd544546703c836bdafeac9). In 1958 [J. Frank Adams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frank_Adams "Frank Adams") published a further generalization in terms of Hopf invariants on _H_-spaces which still limits the dimension to 1, 2, 4, or 8.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypercomplex_number#cite_note-Adams1958-2)

It was [matrix algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_(mathematics) "Matrix (mathematics)") that harnessed the hypercomplex systems. For instance, 2 x 2 [real matrices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_matrix "Real matrix") were found [isomorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isomorphism "Isomorphism") to [coquaternions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coquaternion "Coquaternion"). Soon the matrix paradigm began to explain several others as they were represented by matrices and their operations. In 1907 [Joseph Wedderburn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Wedderburn "Joseph Wedderburn") showed that associative hypercomplex systems could be represented by [square matrices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_matrices "Square matrices"), or [direct products](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direct_product "Direct product") of algebras of square matrices.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypercomplex_number#cite_note-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypercomplex_number#cite_note-4) From that date the preferred term for a _hypercomplex system_ became _[associative algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Associative\_algebra "Associative algebra")_, as seen in the title of Wedderburn's thesis at [University of Edinburgh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Edinburgh "University of Edinburgh"). Note however, that non-associative systems like octonions and [hyperbolic quaternions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_quaternion "Hyperbolic quaternion") represent another type of hypercomplex number.

As [Thomas Hawkins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_W._Hawkins_Jr. "Thomas W. Hawkins Jr.")[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypercomplex_number#cite_note-5) explains, the hypercomplex numbers are stepping stones to learning about [Lie groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_group "Lie group") and [group representation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_representation "Group representation") theory. For instance, in 1929 [Emmy Noether](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emmy_Noether "Emmy Noether") wrote on "hypercomplex quantities and representation theory".[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypercomplex_number#cite_note-6) In 1973 [Kantor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaiah_Kantor "Isaiah Kantor") and Solodovnikov published a textbook on hypercomplex numbers which was translated in 1989.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypercomplex_number#cite_note-KS78-7)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypercomplex_number#cite_note-8)

[Karen Parshall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karen_Parshall "Karen Parshall") has written a detailed exposition of the heyday of hypercomplex numbers,[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypercomplex_number#cite_note-9) including the role of mathematicians including [Theodor Molien](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theodor_Molien "Theodor Molien")[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypercomplex_number#cite_note-10) and [Eduard Study](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eduard_Study "Eduard Study").[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypercomplex_number#cite_note-11) For the transition to [modern algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abstract_algebra "Abstract algebra"), [Bartel van der Waerden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bartel_van_der_Waerden "Bartel van der Waerden") devotes thirty pages to hypercomplex numbers in his _History of Algebra_.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypercomplex_number#cite_note-12)

A definition of a **hypercomplex number** is given by [Kantor & Solodovnikov (1989)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypercomplex_number#CITEREFKantorSolodovnikov1989) as an element of a [unital](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unital_algebra "Unital algebra"), but not necessarily [associative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Associative_property "Associative property") or [commutative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_property "Commutative property"), finite-dimensional algebra over the real numbers. Elements are generated with real number coefficients ![Image 9: {\displaystyle (a_{0},\dots ,a_{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/10af1a71845fb2562c8b6b5c67e9def323aac046) for a basis ![Image 10: {\displaystyle \{1,i_{1},\dots ,i_{n}\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5c0100b3d781a103c464ab675454dc4f04a67a3). Where possible, it is conventional to choose the basis so that ![Image 11: {\displaystyle i_{k}^{2}\in \{-1,0,+1\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/49e6a39edd8668a01530e035b0991ee116a360fa). A technical approach to hypercomplex numbers directs attention first to those of [dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension "Dimension") two.

Two-dimensional real algebras
-----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hypercomplex_number&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Two-dimensional real algebras")]

**Theorem:**[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypercomplex_number#cite_note-KS78-7): 14, 15 [[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypercomplex_number#cite_note-13)[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypercomplex_number#cite_note-14) Up to isomorphism, there are exactly three 2-dimensional unital algebras over the reals: the ordinary [complex numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number"), the [split-complex numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Split-complex_number "Split-complex number"), and the [dual numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_number "Dual number"). In particular, every 2-dimensional unital algebra over the reals is associative and commutative.

Proof: Since the algebra is 2-dimensional, we can pick a basis {1, _u_}. Since the algebra is [closed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closure_(mathematics) "Closure (mathematics)") under squaring, the non-real basis element _u_ squares to a linear combination of 1 and _u_:

![Image 12: {\displaystyle u^{2}=a_{0}+a_{1}u}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f017e410c7fe7c10c2ad064e92e4a1f81ceedd4f)
for some real numbers _a_ 0 and _a_ 1.

Using the common method of [completing the square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Completing_the_square "Completing the square") by subtracting _a_ 1 _u_ and adding the quadratic complement _a_ 2

1 / 4 to both sides yields

![Image 13: {\displaystyle u^{2}-a_{1}u+{\frac {1}{4}}a_{1}^{2}=a_{0}+{\frac {1}{4}}a_{1}^{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/de65cdb785b9549d47a2a835d23115f23ae42b55)
Thus ![Image 14: {\textstyle \left(u-{\frac {1}{2}}a_{1}\right)^{2}={\tilde {u}}^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cc6850bed1bd0614b858bba8ce240b8f747cbd3e) where ![Image 15: {\textstyle {\tilde {u}}^{2}~=a_{0}+{\frac {1}{4}}a_{1}^{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ef3ebb738bd8577e0387a916803f9ae30fa98abd) The three cases depend on this real value:

The complex numbers are the only 2-dimensional hypercomplex algebra that is a [field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)"). [Split algebras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Split_algebra "Split algebra") such as the split-complex numbers that include non-real roots of 1 also contain [idempotents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Idempotent_element "Idempotent element")![Image 16: {\textstyle {\frac {1}{2}}(1\pm j)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/12264bf91753bd912de59eb3b9a22feaa07a09ed) and [zero divisors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero_divisor "Zero divisor")![Image 17: {\displaystyle (1+j)(1-j)=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8cd660269db0311632a7c6dd31e7368b6a488b84), so such algebras cannot be [division algebras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_algebra "Division algebra"). However, these properties can turn out to be very meaningful, for instance in representing a [light cone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_cone "Light cone") with a [null cone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Null_cone "Null cone").

In a 2004 edition of _[Mathematics Magazine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics\_Magazine "Mathematics Magazine")_ the 2-dimensional real algebras have been styled the "generalized complex numbers".[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypercomplex_number#cite_note-15) The idea of [cross-ratio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross-ratio "Cross-ratio") of four complex numbers can be extended to the 2-dimensional real algebras.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypercomplex_number#cite_note-16)

Higher-dimensional examples (more than one non-real axis)
---------------------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hypercomplex_number&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Higher-dimensional examples (more than one non-real axis)")]

A [Clifford algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_algebra "Clifford algebra") is the unital associative algebra generated over an underlying vector space equipped with a [quadratic form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadratic_form "Quadratic form"). Over the real numbers this is equivalent to being able to define a symmetric scalar product, _u_ ⋅ _v_ = ⁠1/2⁠(_uv_ + _vu_) that can be used to [orthogonalise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonalization "Orthogonalization") the quadratic form, to give a basis {_e_ 1, ..., _e_ _k_} such that: ![Image 18: {\displaystyle {\frac {1}{2}}\left(e_{i}e_{j}+e_{j}e_{i}\right)={\begin{cases}-1,0,+1&i=j,\\0&i\not =j.\end{cases}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b191245361f9c0454fd7b3103ff0289ce2439f06)

Imposing closure under multiplication generates a multivector space spanned by a basis of 2 _k_ elements, {1, _e_ 1, _e_ 2, _e_ 3, ..., _e_ 1 _e_ 2, ..., _e_ 1 _e_ 2 _e_ 3, ...}. These can be interpreted as the basis of a hypercomplex number system. Unlike the basis {_e_ 1, ..., _e_ _k_}, the remaining basis elements need not [anti-commute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anticommutative_property "Anticommutative property"), depending on how many simple exchanges must be carried out to swap the two factors. So _e_ 1 _e_ 2 = −_e_ 2 _e_ 1, but _e_ 1(_e_ 2 _e_ 3) = +(_e_ 2 _e_ 3)_e_ 1.

Putting aside the bases which contain an element _e_ _i_ such that _e_ _i_ 2 = 0 (i.e. directions in the original space over which the quadratic form was [degenerate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degenerate_form "Degenerate form")), the remaining Clifford algebras can be identified by the label Cl _p_,_q_(![Image 19: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc)), indicating that the algebra is constructed from _p_ simple basis elements with _e_ _i_ 2 = +1, _q_ with _e_ _i_ 2 = −1, and where ![Image 20: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc) indicates that this is to be a Clifford algebra over the reals—i.e. coefficients of elements of the algebra are to be real numbers.

These algebras, called [geometric algebras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_algebra "Geometric algebra"), form a systematic set, which turn out to be very useful in physics problems which involve [rotations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation "Rotation"), [phases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phase_(waves) "Phase (waves)"), or [spins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_(physics) "Spin (physics)"), notably in [classical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_mechanics "Classical mechanics") and [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics"), [electromagnetic theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_theory "Electromagnetic theory") and [relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theory_of_relativity "Theory of relativity").

Examples include: the [complex numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number") Cl 0,1(![Image 21: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc)), [split-complex numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Split-complex_number "Split-complex number") Cl 1,0(![Image 22: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc)), [quaternions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quaternion "Quaternion") Cl 0,2(![Image 23: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc)), [split-biquaternions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Split-biquaternion "Split-biquaternion") Cl 0,3(![Image 24: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc)), [split-quaternions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Split-quaternion "Split-quaternion")Cl 1,1(![Image 25: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc)) ≈ Cl 2,0(![Image 26: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc)) (the natural algebra of two-dimensional space); Cl 3,0(![Image 27: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc)) (the natural algebra of three-dimensional space, and the algebra of the [Pauli matrices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pauli_matrices "Pauli matrices")); and the [spacetime algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spacetime_algebra "Spacetime algebra") Cl 1,3(![Image 28: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc)).

The elements of the algebra Cl _p_,_q_(![Image 29: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc)) form an even subalgebra Cl[0]

_q_+1,_p_(![Image 30: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc)) of the algebra Cl _q_+1,_p_(![Image 31: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc)), which can be used to parametrise rotations in the larger algebra. There is thus a close connection between complex numbers and rotations in two-dimensional space; between quaternions and rotations in three-dimensional space; between split-complex numbers and (hyperbolic) rotations ([Lorentz transformations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentz_transformations "Lorentz transformations")) in 1+1-dimensional space, and so on.

Whereas Cayley–Dickson and split-complex constructs with eight or more dimensions are not associative with respect to multiplication, Clifford algebras retain associativity at any number of dimensions.

In 1995 [Ian R. Porteous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ian_R._Porteous "Ian R. Porteous") wrote on "The recognition of subalgebras" in his book on Clifford algebras. His Proposition 11.4 summarizes the hypercomplex cases:[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypercomplex_number#cite_note-17)

Let _A_ be a real associative algebra with unit element 1. Then 
*   1 generates ![Image 32: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc) ([algebra of real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number")),
*   any two-dimensional subalgebra generated by an element _e_ 0 of _A_ such that _e_ 0 2 = −1 is isomorphic to ![Image 33: {\displaystyle \mathbb {C} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f9add4085095b9b6d28d045fd9c92c2c09f549a7) ([algebra of complex numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number")),
*   any two-dimensional subalgebra generated by an element _e_ 0 of _A_ such that _e_ 0 2 = 1 is isomorphic to ![Image 34: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc)2 (pairs of real numbers with component-wise product, isomorphic to the [algebra of split-complex numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Split-complex_number "Split-complex number")),
*   any four-dimensional subalgebra generated by a set {_e_ 0, _e_ 1} of mutually anti-commuting elements of _A_ such that ![Image 35: {\displaystyle e_{0}^{2}=e_{1}^{2}=-1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/236fbf38822fef7fa28fb6f0ff60abea9cde4212) is isomorphic to ![Image 36: {\displaystyle \mathbb {H} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e050965453c42bcc6bd544546703c836bdafeac9) ([algebra of quaternions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quaternion "Quaternion")),
*   any four-dimensional subalgebra generated by a set {_e_ 0, _e_ 1} of mutually anti-commuting elements of _A_ such that ![Image 37: {\displaystyle e_{0}^{2}=e_{1}^{2}=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/25d082ef0fd6997ad7a3151d4ccc016073754a07) is isomorphic to M 2(![Image 38: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc)) (2 × 2 [real matrices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_matrices "Real matrices"), [coquaternions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coquaternion "Coquaternion")),
*   any eight-dimensional subalgebra generated by a set {_e_ 0, _e_ 1, _e_ 2} of mutually anti-commuting elements of _A_ such that ![Image 39: {\displaystyle e_{0}^{2}=e_{1}^{2}=e_{2}^{2}=-1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/398100dbc25e8c0d102eec361d9aee632e5c30a4) is isomorphic to 2![Image 40: {\displaystyle \mathbb {H} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e050965453c42bcc6bd544546703c836bdafeac9) ([split-biquaternions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Split-biquaternion "Split-biquaternion")),
*   any eight-dimensional subalgebra generated by a set {_e_ 0, _e_ 1, _e_ 2} of mutually anti-commuting elements of _A_ such that ![Image 41: {\displaystyle e_{0}^{2}=e_{1}^{2}=e_{2}^{2}=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f62e623406a5f320cc18753e9fa7f8134435653a) is isomorphic to M 2(![Image 42: {\displaystyle \mathbb {C} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f9add4085095b9b6d28d045fd9c92c2c09f549a7)) (2 × 2 complex matrices, [biquaternions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biquaternion "Biquaternion"), [Pauli algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pauli_algebra "Pauli algebra")).

### Cayley–Dickson construction

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hypercomplex_number&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Cayley–Dickson construction")]

[![Image 43](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Cayley_Q8_multiplication_graph.svg/250px-Cayley_Q8_multiplication_graph.svg.png)](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a5/Cayley_Q8_multiplication_graph.svg)

Cayley Q8 graph of quaternion multiplication showing cycles of multiplication of _i_ (red), _j_ (green) and _k_ (blue). In [the SVG file,](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/04/Cayley_Q8_quaternion_multiplication_graph.svg) hover over or click a path to highlight it.

All of the Clifford algebras Cl _p_,_q_(![Image 44: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc)) apart from the real numbers, complex numbers and the quaternions contain non-real elements that square to +1; and so cannot be division algebras. A different approach to extending the complex numbers is taken by the [Cayley–Dickson construction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cayley%E2%80%93Dickson_construction "Cayley–Dickson construction"). This generates number systems of dimension 2 _n_, _n_ = 2, 3, 4, ..., with bases ![Image 45: {\displaystyle \left\{1,i_{1},\dots ,i_{2^{n}-1}\right\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a4373677f9738051dc189049641e235bc534f389), where all the non-real basis elements anti-commute and satisfy ![Image 46: {\displaystyle i_{m}^{2}=-1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a97c991ba408bf70a02af2ed352a0e7f8b67167d). In 8 or more dimensions (_n_ ≥ 3) these algebras are non-associative. In 16 or more dimensions (_n_ ≥ 4) these algebras also have [zero-divisors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero-divisor "Zero-divisor").

The first algebras in this sequence include the 4-dimensional [quaternions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quaternion "Quaternion"), 8-dimensional [octonions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octonion "Octonion"), and 16-dimensional [sedenions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sedenion "Sedenion"). An algebraic symmetry is lost with each increase in dimensionality: quaternion multiplication is not [commutative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative "Commutative"), octonion multiplication is non-[associative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Associative "Associative"), and the [norm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norm_(mathematics) "Norm (mathematics)") of [sedenions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sedenion "Sedenion") is not multiplicative. After the sedenions are the 32-dimensional [trigintaduonions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trigintaduonion "Trigintaduonion") (or 32-nions), the 64-dimensional sexagintaquatronions (or 64-nions), the 128-dimensional centumduodetrigintanions (or 128-nions), the 256-dimensional ducentiquinquagintasexions (or 256-nions), and _[ad infinitum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ad\_infinitum "Ad infinitum")_, as summarized in the table below.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypercomplex_number#cite_note-18)

The Cayley–Dickson construction can be modified by inserting an extra sign at some stages. It then generates the "split algebras" in the collection of [composition algebras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Composition_algebra "Composition algebra") instead of the division algebras:

[split-complex numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Split-complex_number "Split-complex number") with basis ![Image 53: {\displaystyle \{1,\,i_{1}\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dbbb864e4558bf3cb14c5542ac510fb6ba427c4c) satisfying ![Image 54: {\displaystyle \ i_{1}^{2}=+1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ff34b6d6ac32050f37a146d48732231ddd9e7bad),[split-quaternions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Split-quaternion "Split-quaternion") with basis ![Image 55: {\displaystyle \{1,\,i_{1},\,i_{2},\,i_{3}\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b3a78b1216022bd4fc73c34461ea5cafeeac9d1) satisfying ![Image 56: {\displaystyle \ i_{1}^{2}=-1,\,i_{2}^{2}=i_{3}^{2}=+1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5efd498bb1dcee4a92f9bd9d07f86bdb2f1277c6), and[split-octonions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Split-octonion "Split-octonion") with basis ![Image 57: {\displaystyle \{1,\,i_{1},\,\dots ,\,i_{7}\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/297cf2eb51e4428ca36a999d03b991a781444037) satisfying ![Image 58: {\displaystyle \ i_{1}^{2}=i_{2}^{2}=i_{3}^{2}=-1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87078379d36ab514017e2a3074994b7c8240e1f8), ![Image 59: {\displaystyle \ i_{4}^{2}=i_{5}^{2}=i_{6}^{2}=i_{7}^{2}=+1.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f432bd1086c7db119dd3a284343c9da3043bf05b)
Unlike the complex numbers, the split-complex numbers are not [algebraically closed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraically_closed_field "Algebraically closed field"), and further contain nontrivial [zero divisors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero_divisor "Zero divisor") and nontrivial [idempotents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Idempotent "Idempotent"). As with the quaternions, split-quaternions are not commutative, but further contain [nilpotents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nilpotent "Nilpotent"); they are isomorphic to the [square matrices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_matrices "Square matrices") of dimension two. Split-octonions are non-associative and contain nilpotents.

The [tensor product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_product "Tensor product") of any two algebras is another algebra, which can be used to produce many more examples of hypercomplex number systems.

In particular taking tensor products with the complex numbers (considered as algebras over the reals) leads to four-dimensional [bicomplex numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bicomplex_number "Bicomplex number")![Image 60: {\displaystyle \mathbb {C} \otimes _{\mathbb {R} }\mathbb {C} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/092ae5d54639c6ad95b99a7cfc1a3b6c1f77f858) (isomorphic to tessarines ![Image 61: {\displaystyle \mathbb {C} \otimes _{\mathbb {R} }D}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e0fc6f8ee776b7c0b248fb71fe30f47563758c2f)), eight-dimensional [biquaternions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biquaternion "Biquaternion")![Image 62: {\displaystyle \mathbb {C} \otimes _{\mathbb {R} }\mathbb {H} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c4f3310a16eca1ee4b1601c0b8a33f75f3971747), and 16-dimensional [complex octonions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octonion "Octonion")![Image 63: {\displaystyle \mathbb {C} \otimes _{\mathbb {R} }\mathbb {O} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/adf9953ccba22a0c26363910727c3a5a7ebe9e08).

*   [bicomplex numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bicomplex_number "Bicomplex number"): a 4-dimensional vector space over the reals, 2-dimensional over the complex numbers, isomorphic to tessarines.
*   [multicomplex numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multicomplex_number "Multicomplex number"): 2 _n_-dimensional vector spaces over the reals, 2 _n_−1-dimensional over the complex numbers
*   [composition algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Composition_algebra "Composition algebra"): algebra with a [quadratic form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadratic_form "Quadratic form") that composes with the product
