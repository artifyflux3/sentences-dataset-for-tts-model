The [owl of Athena](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Owl_of_Athena "Owl of Athena"), a symbol of knowledge in the Western world

**Knowledge** is an [awareness of facts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Declarative_knowledge "Declarative knowledge"), a [familiarity with individuals and situations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge_by_acquaintance "Knowledge by acquaintance"), or a [practical skill](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Procedural_knowledge "Procedural knowledge"). Knowledge of facts, also called [propositional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proposition "Proposition") knowledge, is often characterized as [true](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Truth "Truth")[belief](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belief "Belief") that is distinct from opinion or guesswork by virtue of [justification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Justification_(epistemology) "Justification (epistemology)"). While there is wide agreement among philosophers that propositional knowledge is a form of true belief, many controversies focus on justification. This includes questions like how to understand justification, whether it is needed at all, and whether something else besides it is needed. These controversies intensified in the latter half of the 20th century due to a series of thought experiments called _[Gettier cases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gettier\_cases "Gettier cases")_ that provoked alternative definitions.

Knowledge can be produced in many ways. The main source of [empirical knowledge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empirical_knowledge "Empirical knowledge") is [perception](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perception "Perception"), which involves the usage of the [senses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Senses "Senses") to learn about the external world. [Introspection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Introspection "Introspection") allows people to learn about their internal [mental states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mental_states "Mental states") and processes. Other sources of knowledge include [memory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Memory "Memory"), [rational intuition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_intuition "Rational intuition"), [inference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inference "Inference"), and [testimony](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Testimony "Testimony").[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-1) According to [foundationalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foundationalism "Foundationalism"), some of these sources are basic in that they can justify beliefs, without depending on other mental states. [Coherentists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coherentism "Coherentism") reject this claim and contend that a sufficient degree of coherence among all the mental states of the believer is necessary for knowledge. According to infinitism, an infinite chain of beliefs is needed.

The main discipline investigating knowledge is [epistemology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epistemology "Epistemology"), which studies what people know, how they come to know it, and what it means to know something. It discusses the value of knowledge and the thesis of [philosophical skepticism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophical_skepticism "Philosophical skepticism"), which questions the possibility of knowledge. Knowledge is relevant to many fields like the [sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Science "Science"), which aim to acquire knowledge using the [scientific method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_method "Scientific method") based on repeatable [experimentation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Experimentation "Experimentation"), [observation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observation "Observation"), and [measurement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Measurement "Measurement"). Various religions hold that humans should seek knowledge and that God or the divine is the source of knowledge. The anthropology of knowledge studies how knowledge is acquired, stored, retrieved, and communicated in different cultures. The [sociology of knowledge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sociology_of_knowledge "Sociology of knowledge") examines under what sociohistorical circumstances knowledge arises, and what sociological consequences it has. The [history of knowledge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_knowledge "History of knowledge") investigates how knowledge in different fields has developed, and evolved, in the course of history.

Definitions
-----------

Knowledge is a form of familiarity, [awareness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Awareness "Awareness"), [understanding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Understanding "Understanding"), or acquaintance. It often involves the possession of information learned through [experience](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Experience "Experience")[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-2) and can be understood as a [cognitive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cognition "Cognition") success or an epistemic contact with reality, like making a discovery.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-3) Many academic definitions focus on [propositional knowledge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propositional_knowledge "Propositional knowledge") in the form of believing certain facts, as in "I know that Dave is at home".[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-4) Other types of knowledge include knowledge-how in the form of practical [competence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Competence_(human_resources) "Competence (human resources)"), as in "she knows how to swim", and [knowledge by acquaintance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge_by_acquaintance "Knowledge by acquaintance") as a familiarity with the known object based on previous direct experience, like knowing someone personally.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-5)

Knowledge is often understood as a state of an individual person, but it can also refer to a characteristic of a group of people as group knowledge, social knowledge, or collective knowledge.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-6) Some social sciences understand knowledge as a broad social phenomenon that is similar to culture.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-7) The term may further denote knowledge stored in documents like the "knowledge housed in the library"[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-8) or the [knowledge base](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge_base "Knowledge base") of an [expert system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expert_system "Expert system").[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-9) Knowledge is closely related to [intelligence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intelligence "Intelligence"), but intelligence is more about the ability to acquire, process, and apply information, while knowledge concerns information and skills that a person already possesses.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-10)

The word _knowledge_ has its roots in the 12th-century [Old English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Old_English "Old English") word _cnawan_, which comes from the [Old High German](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Old_High_German "Old High German") word _gecnawan_.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-11) The English word includes various meanings that some other languages distinguish using several words.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-12) In ancient Greek, for example, four important terms for knowledge were used: _[epistēmē](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Episteme "Episteme")_ (unchanging theoretical knowledge), _[technē](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Techne "Techne")_ (expert technical knowledge), _[mētis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metis\_(mythology) "Metis (mythology)")_ (strategic knowledge), and _[gnōsis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gnosis "Gnosis")_ (personal intellectual knowledge).[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-13) The main discipline studying knowledge is called [epistemology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epistemology "Epistemology") or the theory of knowledge. It examines the nature of knowledge and justification, how knowledge arises, and what value it has. Further topics include the different types of knowledge and the limits of what can be known.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-14)

Despite agreements about the general characteristics of knowledge, its exact definition is disputed. Some definitions only focus on the most salient features of knowledge to give a practically useful characterization.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-15) Another approach, termed _analysis of knowledge_, tries to provide a theoretically precise definition by listing the conditions that are [individually necessary and jointly sufficient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Necessity_and_sufficiency "Necessity and sufficiency"),[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-16) similar to how [chemists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemistry "Chemistry") analyze a sample by seeking a list of all the chemical elements composing it.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-17) According to a different view, knowledge is a unique state that cannot be analyzed in terms of other phenomena.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-18) Some scholars base their definition on abstract intuitions while others focus on concrete cases[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-19) or rely on how the term is used in [ordinary language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordinary_language "Ordinary language").[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-20) There is also disagreement about whether knowledge is a rare phenomenon that requires high standards or a common phenomenon found in many everyday situations.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-21)

### Analysis of knowledge

[![Image 2: Venn diagram of justified true belief](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Justified_true_belief.png/250px-Justified_true_belief.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Justified_true_belief.png)

The definition of knowledge as justified true belief is often discussed in the academic literature.

An often-discussed definition characterizes knowledge as justified true belief. This definition identifies three essential features: it is (1) a [belief](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belief "Belief") that is (2) [true](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Truth "Truth") and (3) [justified](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Justification_(epistemology) "Justification (epistemology)").[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-22)[[b]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-24) Truth is a widely accepted feature of knowledge. It implies that, while it may be possible to believe something false, one cannot know something false.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-25)[[c]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-27) That knowledge is a form of belief implies that one cannot know something if one does not believe it. Some everyday expressions seem to violate this principle, like the claim that "I do not believe it, I know it!" But the point of such expressions is usually to emphasize one's confidence rather than denying that a belief is involved.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-28)

The main controversy surrounding this definition concerns its third feature: justification.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-29) This component is often included because of the impression that some true beliefs are not forms of knowledge, such as beliefs based on [superstition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superstition "Superstition"), [lucky](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luck "Luck") guesses, or erroneous [reasoning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reasoning "Reasoning"). For example, a person who guesses that a coin flip will land heads usually does not know that even if their belief turns out to be true. This indicates that there is more to knowledge than just being right about something.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-30) These cases are excluded by requiring that beliefs have justification for them to count as knowledge.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-31) Some [philosophers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophy "Philosophy") hold that a belief is justified if it is [based on evidence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evidentialism "Evidentialism"), which can take the form of [mental states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mental_states "Mental states") like experience, [memory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Memory "Memory"), and other beliefs. Others state that beliefs are justified if they are produced by [reliable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reliabilism "Reliabilism") processes, like sensory perception or logical reasoning.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-32)

[![Image 3: Venn diagram of justified true belief that does not amount to knowledge](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Justified_True_Belief_model_of_knowledge.svg/330px-Justified_True_Belief_model_of_knowledge.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Justified_True_Belief_model_of_knowledge.svg)

The Gettier problem is grounded in the idea that some justified true beliefs do not amount to knowledge.

The definition of knowledge as justified true belief came under severe criticism in the 20th century, when epistemologist [Edmund Gettier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edmund_Gettier "Edmund Gettier") formulated a series of counterexamples.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-33) They purport to present concrete cases of justified true beliefs that fail to constitute knowledge. The reason for their failure is usually a form of epistemic luck: the beliefs are justified but their justification is not relevant to the truth.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-34) In a well-known example, someone drives along a country road with many [barn facades](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potemkin_village "Potemkin village") and only one real barn. The person is not aware of this, stops in front of the real barn by a lucky coincidence, and forms the justified true belief that they are in front of a barn. This example aims to establish that the person does not know that they are in front of a real barn, since they would not have been able to tell the difference.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-35) This means that it is a lucky coincidence that this justified belief is also true.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-36)

According to some philosophers, these counterexamples show that justification is not required for knowledge[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-37) and that knowledge should instead be characterized in terms of reliability or the manifestation of [cognitive virtues](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intellectual_virtue "Intellectual virtue"). Another approach defines knowledge in regard to the function it plays in cognitive processes as that which provides reasons for thinking or doing something.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-38) A different response accepts justification as an aspect of knowledge and include additional criteria.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-39) Many candidates have been suggested, like the requirements that the justified true belief does not depend on any false beliefs, that no [defeaters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Defeater "Defeater")[[d]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-41) are present, or that the person would not have the belief if it was false.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-42) Another view states that beliefs have to be infallible to amount to knowledge.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-43)[[e]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-45) A further approach, associated with [pragmatism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pragmatism "Pragmatism"), focuses on the aspect of inquiry and characterizes knowledge in terms of what works as a practice that aims to produce habits of action.[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-46) There is still very little consensus in the academic discourse as to which of the proposed modifications or reconceptualizations is correct, and there are various [alternative definitions of knowledge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Definitions_of_knowledge#Other_definitions "Definitions of knowledge").[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-47)

Types
-----

A common distinction among types of knowledge is between propositional knowledge, or knowledge-that, and non-propositional knowledge in the form of practical skills or acquaintance.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-48)[[f]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-50) Other distinctions focus on how the knowledge is acquired and on the content of the known information.[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-51)

### Propositional

[![Image 4: Photo of the Totius Latinitatis Lexicon by Egidio Forcellini, a multi-volume Latin dictionary](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/Latin_dictionary.jpg/250px-Latin_dictionary.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Latin_dictionary.jpg)

Declarative knowledge can be stored in books.

Propositional knowledge, also referred to as declarative and descriptive knowledge, is a form of theoretical knowledge about facts, like knowing that "2 + 2 = 4". It is the paradigmatic type of knowledge in [analytic philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytic_philosophy "Analytic philosophy").[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-52) Propositional knowledge is _propositional_ in the sense that it involves a relation to a proposition. Since propositions are often expressed through that-clauses, it is also referred to as _knowledge-that_, as in "Akari knows that kangaroos hop".[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-53) In this case, Akari stands in the [relation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relation_(philosophy) "Relation (philosophy)") of knowing to the proposition "kangaroos hop". Closely related types of knowledge are _know-wh_, for example, _knowing who_ is coming to dinner and _knowing why_ they are coming.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-54) These expressions are normally understood as types of propositional knowledge since they can be paraphrased using a that-clause.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-55)[[g]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-57)

Propositional knowledge takes the form of mental representations involving concepts, ideas, theories, and general rules. These representations connect the knower to certain parts of reality by showing what they are like. They are often context-independent, meaning that they are not restricted to a specific use or purpose.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-58) Propositional knowledge encompasses both knowledge of specific facts, like that the atomic mass of gold is 196.97 [u](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dalton_(unit) "Dalton (unit)"), and generalities, like that the color of leaves of some trees changes in autumn.[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-59) Because of the dependence on mental representations, it is often held that the capacity for propositional knowledge is exclusive to relatively sophisticated creatures, such as humans. This is based on the claim that advanced intellectual capacities are needed to believe a proposition that expresses what the world is like.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-auto6-60)

### Non-propositional

[![Image 5: Photograph of someone riding a bicycle](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Bicycle_ride.jpg/250px-Bicycle_ride.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Bicycle_ride.jpg)

Knowing how to ride a bicycle is one form of non-propositional knowledge.

Non-propositional knowledge is knowledge in which no essential relation to a proposition is involved. The two most well-known forms are knowledge-how (know-how or [procedural knowledge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Procedural_knowledge "Procedural knowledge")) and knowledge by acquaintance.[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-61) To possess knowledge-how means to have some form of practical [ability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ability "Ability"), skill, or [competence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Competence_(human_resources) "Competence (human resources)"),[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-62) like knowing how to ride a bicycle or knowing how to swim. Some of the abilities responsible for knowledge-how involve forms of knowledge-that, as in knowing how to prove a [mathematical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics") theorem, but this is not generally the case.[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-63) Some types of knowledge-how do not require a highly developed mind, in contrast to propositional knowledge, and are more common in the animal kingdom. For example, an [ant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ant "Ant") knows how to walk even though it presumably lacks a mind sufficiently developed to represent the corresponding proposition.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-auto6-60)[[h]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-65)

Knowledge by acquaintance is familiarity with something that results from direct experiential contact.[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-66) The object of knowledge can be a person, a thing, or a place. For example, by eating chocolate, one becomes acquainted with the taste of chocolate, and visiting [Lake Taupō](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lake_Taup%C5%8D "Lake Taupō") leads to the formation of knowledge by acquaintance of Lake Taupō. In these cases, the person forms non-inferential knowledge based on first-hand experience without necessarily acquiring factual information about the object. By contrast, it is also possible to indirectly learn a lot of propositional knowledge about chocolate or Lake Taupō by reading books without having the direct experiential contact required for knowledge by acquaintance.[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-67) The concept of knowledge by acquaintance was first introduced by [Bertrand Russell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bertrand_Russell "Bertrand Russell"). He holds that knowledge by acquaintance is more basic than propositional knowledge since to understand a proposition, one has to be acquainted with its constituents.[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-68)

### _A priori_ and _a posteriori_

The distinction between _a priori_ and _a posteriori_ knowledge depends on the role of experience in the processes of formation and justification.[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-69) To know something _a posteriori_ means to know it based on experience.[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-70) For example, by seeing that it rains outside or hearing that the baby is crying, one acquires _a posteriori_ knowledge of these facts.[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-71)_A priori_ knowledge is possible without any experience to justify or support the known proposition.[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-72) Mathematical knowledge, such as that 2 + 2 = 4, is traditionally taken to be _a priori_ knowledge since no empirical investigation is necessary to confirm this fact. In this regard, _a posteriori_ knowledge is empirical knowledge while _a priori_ knowledge is non-empirical knowledge.[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-73)

The relevant experience in question is primarily identified with [sensory experience](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Experience#Perception "Experience"). Some non-sensory experiences, like memory and introspection, are often included as well. Some conscious phenomena are excluded from the relevant experience, like rational insight. For example, conscious thought processes may be required to arrive at _a priori_ knowledge regarding the solution of mathematical problems, like when performing [mental arithmetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mental_arithmetic "Mental arithmetic") to multiply two numbers.[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-74) The same is the case for the experience needed to learn the words through which the claim is expressed. For example, knowing that "all bachelors are unmarried" is _a priori_ knowledge because no sensory experience is necessary to confirm this fact even though experience was needed to learn the meanings of the words "bachelor" and "unmarried".[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-75)

It is difficult to explain how _a priori_ knowledge is possible and some empiricists deny it exists. It is usually seen as unproblematic that one can come to know things through experience, but it is not clear how knowledge is possible without experience. One of the earliest solutions to this problem comes from [Plato](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plato "Plato"), who argues that the soul already possesses the knowledge and just needs to recollect, or remember, it to access it again.[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-76) A similar explanation is given by [Descartes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Descartes "Descartes"), who holds that _a priori_ knowledge exists as [innate knowledge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Innate_knowledge "Innate knowledge") present in the [mind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mind "Mind") of each human.[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-77) A further approach posits a special mental faculty responsible for this type of knowledge, often referred to as [rational intuition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_intuition "Rational intuition") or rational insight.[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-78)

### Others

Various other types of knowledge are discussed in the academic literature. In philosophy, "self-knowledge" refers to a person's knowledge of their own [sensations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sense "Sense"), [thoughts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thought "Thought"), beliefs, and other mental states. A common view is that self-knowledge is more direct than knowledge of the external world, which relies on the interpretation of sense data. Because of this, it is traditionally claimed that self-knowledge is indubitable, like the claim that a person cannot be wrong about whether they are in pain. However, this position is not universally accepted in the contemporary discourse and an alternative view states that self-knowledge also depends on interpretations that could be false.[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-79) In a slightly different sense, [self-knowledge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-knowledge_(psychology) "Self-knowledge (psychology)") can also refer to knowledge of the [self](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self "Self") as a persisting entity with certain [personality traits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Personality_traits "Personality traits"), [preferences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Preference "Preference"), physical attributes, relationships, goals, and [social identities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Identity_(social_science) "Identity (social science)").[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-80)[[i]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-82)

[Metaknowledge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metaknowledge "Metaknowledge") is knowledge about knowledge. It can arise in the form of self-knowledge but includes other types as well, such as knowing what someone else knows or what information is contained in a scientific article. Other aspects of metaknowledge include knowing how knowledge can be acquired, stored, distributed, and used.[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-83)

[Common knowledge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Common_knowledge "Common knowledge") is knowledge that is publicly known and shared by most individuals within a community. It establishes a common ground for communication, understanding, social cohesion, and cooperation.[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-84)[General knowledge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_knowledge "General knowledge") encompasses common knowledge but also includes knowledge that many people have been exposed to but may not be able to immediately recall.[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-85) Common knowledge contrasts with [domain knowledge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_knowledge "Domain knowledge") or specialized knowledge, which belongs to a specific domain and is only possessed by experts.[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-86)

**Situated knowledge** is knowledge specific to a particular situation.[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-auto4-87) It is closely related to practical or tacit knowledge, which is learned and applied in specific circumstances. This especially concerns certain forms of acquiring knowledge, such as [trial and error](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trial_and_error "Trial and error") or learning from experience.[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-88) In this regard, situated knowledge usually lacks a more explicit structure and is not articulated in terms of universal ideas.[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-89) The term is often used in [feminism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feminism "Feminism") and [postmodernism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism "Postmodernism") to argue that many forms of knowledge are not absolute but depend on the concrete historical, cultural, and linguistic context.[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-auto4-87)

[Explicit knowledge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Explicit_knowledge "Explicit knowledge") is knowledge that can be fully articulated, shared, and explained, like the knowledge of historical dates and mathematical formulas. It can be acquired through traditional learning methods, such as reading books and attending lectures. It contrasts with [tacit knowledge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tacit_knowledge "Tacit knowledge"), which is not easily articulated or explained to others, like the ability to recognize someone's face and the practical expertise of a master craftsman. Tacit knowledge is often learned through first-hand experience or direct practice.[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-90)

[Cognitive load theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cognitive_load_theory "Cognitive load theory") distinguishes between biologically primary and secondary knowledge. Biologically primary knowledge is knowledge that humans have as part of their evolutionary heritage, such as knowing how to recognize faces and speech and many general problem-solving capacities. Biologically secondary knowledge is knowledge acquired because of specific social and cultural circumstances, such as knowing how to read and write.[[82]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-91)

Knowledge can be [occurrent or dispositional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belief#Occurrent_and_dispositional "Belief"). Occurrent knowledge is knowledge that is actively involved in cognitive processes. Dispositional knowledge, by contrast, lies dormant in the back of a person's mind and is given by the mere ability to access the relevant information. For example, if a person knows that cats have [whiskers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whiskers "Whiskers") then this knowledge is dispositional most of the time and becomes occurrent while they are thinking about it.[[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-92)

Many forms of Eastern spirituality and religion distinguish between higher and lower knowledge. They are also referred to as [para vidya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Para_Vidya "Para Vidya") and [apara vidya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vidya_(philosophy)#Hierarchy_of_knowledge "Vidya (philosophy)") in [Hinduism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hinduism "Hinduism") or the [two truths doctrine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two_truths_doctrine "Two truths doctrine") in [Buddhism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buddhism "Buddhism"). Lower knowledge is based on the senses and the intellect. It encompasses both mundane or conventional truths as well as discoveries of the empirical sciences.[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-93) Higher knowledge is understood as knowledge of God, [the absolute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_(philosophy) "Absolute (philosophy)"), the [true self](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/True_self "True self"), or the [ultimate reality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultimate_reality "Ultimate reality"). It belongs neither to the external world of physical objects nor to the internal world of the experience of emotions and concepts. Many spiritual teachings stress the importance of higher knowledge to progress on the spiritual path and to see reality as it truly is beyond the [veil of appearances](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maya_(religion) "Maya (religion)").[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-94)

Sources
-------

[![Image 6: Photos of the five senses](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Five_Senses.jpg/250px-Five_Senses.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Five_Senses.jpg)

Perception relies on the [senses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Senses "Senses") to acquire knowledge.

Sources of knowledge are ways in which people come to know things. They can be understood as cognitive capacities that are exercised when a person acquires new knowledge.[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-95) Various sources of knowledge are discussed in the academic literature, often in terms of the mental faculties responsible. They include perception, introspection, memory, inference, and testimony. However, not everyone agrees that all of them actually lead to knowledge. Usually, [perception](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perception "Perception") or observation, i.e. using one of the [senses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Senses "Senses"), is identified as the most important source of empirical knowledge.[[87]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-96) Knowing that a baby is sleeping is observational knowledge if it was caused by a perception of the snoring baby. However, this would not be the case if one learned about this fact through a telephone conversation with one's spouse. Perception comes in different modalities, including [vision](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visual_perception "Visual perception"), [sound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Auditory_perception "Auditory perception"), [touch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Touch "Touch"), [smell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Olfactory_perception "Olfactory perception"), and [taste](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taste "Taste"), which correspond to different [physical stimuli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_stimulation "Physical stimulation").[[88]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-97) It is an active process in which sensory signals are selected, organized, and interpreted to form a representation of the environment. This leads in some cases to [illusions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Illusion "Illusion") that misrepresent certain aspects of reality, like the [Müller-Lyer illusion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M%C3%BCller-Lyer_illusion "Müller-Lyer illusion") and the [Ponzo illusion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ponzo_illusion "Ponzo illusion").[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-98)

[Introspection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Introspection "Introspection") is often seen in analogy to perception as a source of knowledge, not of external physical objects, but of internal [mental states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mental_states "Mental states"). A traditionally common view is that introspection has a special epistemic status by being infallible. According to this position, it is not possible to be mistaken about introspective facts, like whether one is in pain, because there is no difference between appearance and reality. However, this claim has been contested in the contemporary discourse and critics argue that it may be possible, for example, to mistake an unpleasant itch for a pain or to confuse the experience of a slight ellipse for the experience of a circle.[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-99) Perceptual and introspective knowledge often act as a form of fundamental or basic knowledge. According to some [empiricists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empiricism "Empiricism"), they are the only sources of basic knowledge and provide the foundation for all other knowledge.[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-100)

Memory differs from perception and introspection in that it is not as independent or basic as they are since it depends on other previous experiences.[[92]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-101) The faculty of memory retains knowledge acquired in the past and makes it accessible in the present, as when remembering a past event or a friend's phone number.[[93]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-102) It is generally seen as a reliable source of knowledge. However, it can be deceptive at times nonetheless, either because the original experience was unreliable or because the memory degraded and does not accurately represent the original experience anymore.[[94]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-103)[[j]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-105)

Knowledge based on perception, introspection, and memory may give rise to inferential knowledge, which comes about when reasoning is applied to draw inferences from other known facts.[[96]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-106) For example, the perceptual knowledge of a Czech stamp on a postcard may give rise to the inferential knowledge that one's friend is visiting the Czech Republic. This type of knowledge depends on other sources of knowledge responsible for the premises. Some [rationalists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rationalism "Rationalism") argue for rational intuition as a further source of knowledge that does not rely on observation and introspection. They hold for example that some beliefs, like the mathematical belief that 2 + 2 = 4, are justified through pure reason alone.[[97]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-107)

[![Image 7: Photograph of a person giving testimony](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Borochov-testimony.jpg/250px-Borochov-testimony.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Borochov-testimony.jpg)

Knowledge by testimony relies on statements given by other people, like the testimony given at a trial.

[Testimony](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Testimony "Testimony") is often included as an additional source of knowledge that, unlike the other sources, is not tied to one specific cognitive faculty. Instead, it is based on the idea that one person can come to know a fact because another person talks about this fact. Testimony can happen in numerous ways, like regular speech, a letter, a newspaper, or a [blog](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blog "Blog"). The [problem of testimony](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophy_of_testimony "Philosophy of testimony") consists in clarifying why and under what circumstances testimony can lead to knowledge. A common response is that it depends on the reliability of the person pronouncing the testimony: only testimony from reliable sources can lead to knowledge.[[98]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-108)

Limits
------

The problem of the limits of knowledge concerns the question of which facts are [unknowable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unknowability "Unknowability").[[99]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-109) These limits constitute a form of inevitable [ignorance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ignorance "Ignorance") that can affect both what is knowable about the external world as well as what one can know about oneself and about what is good.[[100]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-110) Some limits of knowledge only apply to particular people in specific situations while others pertain to humanity at large.[[101]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-111) A fact is unknowable to a person if this person lacks access to the relevant information, like facts in the past that did not leave any significant traces. For example, it may be unknowable to people today what [Caesar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesar "Caesar")'s breakfast was the [day he was assassinated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ides_of_March "Ides of March") but it was knowable to him and some contemporaries.[[102]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-112) Another factor restricting knowledge is given by the limitations of the human [cognitive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cognition "Cognition") faculties. Some people may lack the cognitive ability to understand highly abstract mathematical truths and some facts cannot be known by any human because they are too complex for the human mind to conceive.[[103]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-113) A further limit of knowledge arises due to certain logical [paradoxes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paradox "Paradox"). For instance, there are some ideas that will never occur to anyone. It is not possible to know them because if a person knew about such an idea then this idea would have occurred at least to them.[[104]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-114)[[k]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-116)

There are many disputes about what can or cannot be known in certain fields. [Religious skepticism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Religious_skepticism "Religious skepticism") is the view that beliefs about God or other religious doctrines do not amount to knowledge.[[106]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-117)[Moral skepticism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moral_skepticism "Moral skepticism") encompasses a variety of views, including the claim that [moral knowledge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethics#Moral_knowledge "Ethics") is impossible, meaning that one cannot know what is morally good or whether a certain behavior is morally right.[[107]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-118) An influential theory about the limits of [metaphysical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metaphysical "Metaphysical") knowledge was proposed by [Immanuel Kant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immanuel_Kant "Immanuel Kant"). For him, knowledge is restricted to the field of [appearances](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phenomenon "Phenomenon") and does not reach the [things in themselves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thing_in_itself "Thing in itself"), which exist independently of humans and lie beyond the realm of appearances. Based on the observation that metaphysics aims to characterize the things in themselves, he concludes that no metaphysical knowledge is possible, like knowing whether [the world has a beginning or is infinite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kant%27s_antinomies#The_first_antinomy_(of_space_and_time) "Kant's antinomies").[[108]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-119)

There are also limits to knowledge in the empirical sciences, such as the [uncertainty principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uncertainty_principle "Uncertainty principle"), which states that it is impossible to know the exact magnitudes of certain certain pairs of physical properties, like the position and [momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum "Momentum") of a particle, at the same time.[[109]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-120) Other examples are physical systems studied by [chaos theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chaos_theory "Chaos theory"), for which it is not practically possible to predict how they will behave since they are so sensitive to [initial conditions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Initial_conditions "Initial conditions") that even the slightest of variations may produce a completely different behavior. This phenomenon is known as the [butterfly effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Butterfly_effect "Butterfly effect").[[110]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-121)

[![Image 8: Bust of Pyrrho of Elis](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Philosopher%2C_marble_head%2C_Roman_copy%2C_AM_Corfu%2C_Krfm22.jpg/250px-Philosopher%2C_marble_head%2C_Roman_copy%2C_AM_Corfu%2C_Krfm22.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Philosopher,_marble_head,_Roman_copy,_AM_Corfu,_Krfm22.jpg)

[Pyrrho](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrrho "Pyrrho") was one of the first philosophical skeptics.

The strongest position about the limits of knowledge is [radical or global skepticism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radical_skepticism "Radical skepticism"), which holds that humans lack any form of knowledge or that knowledge is impossible. For example, the [dream argument](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dream_argument "Dream argument") states that perceptual experience is not a source of knowledge since dreaming provides unreliable information and a person could be dreaming without knowing it. Because of this inability to discriminate between dream and perception, it is argued that there is no perceptual knowledge of the external world.[[111]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-122)[[l]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-124) This thought experiment is based on the problem of [underdetermination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Underdetermination "Underdetermination"), which arises when the available evidence is not sufficient to make a rational decision between competing theories. In such cases, a person is not justified in believing one theory rather than the other. If this is always the case then global skepticism follows.[[112]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-auto3-123) Another skeptical argument assumes that knowledge requires absolute certainty and aims to show that all human cognition is fallible since it fails to meet this standard.[[113]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-125)

An influential argument against radical skepticism states that radical skepticism is [self-contradictory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-refuting_idea "Self-refuting idea") since denying the existence of knowledge is itself a knowledge-claim.[[114]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-126) Other arguments rely on [common sense](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Common_sense "Common sense")[[115]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-127) or deny that infallibility is required for knowledge.[[116]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-128) Very few philosophers have explicitly defended radical skepticism but this position has been influential nonetheless, usually in a negative sense: many see it as a serious challenge to any epistemological theory and often try to show how their preferred theory overcomes it.[[117]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-129)[Another form of philosophical skepticism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrrhonism "Pyrrhonism") advocates the [suspension of judgment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epoche "Epoche") as a form of attaining [tranquility](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ataraxia "Ataraxia") while remaining [humble](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Humility "Humility") and [open-minded](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open-minded "Open-minded").[[118]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-130)

A less radical limit of knowledge is identified by [fallibilists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fallibilism "Fallibilism"), who argue that the possibility of error can never be fully excluded. This means that even the best-researched scientific theories and the most fundamental common-sense views could still be subject to error. Further research may reduce the possibility of being wrong, but it can never fully exclude it. Some fallibilists reach the skeptical conclusion from this observation that there is no knowledge but the more common view is that knowledge exists but is fallible.[[119]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-131)[Pragmatists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pragmatists "Pragmatists") argue that one consequence of fallibilism is that inquiry should not aim for truth or absolute certainty but for well-supported and justified beliefs while remaining open to the possibility that one's beliefs may need to be [revised](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belief_revision "Belief revision") later.[[120]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-132)

Structure
---------

The structure of knowledge is the way in which the mental states of a person need to be related to each other for knowledge to arise.[[121]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-133) A common view is that a person has to have good reasons for holding a belief if this belief is to amount to knowledge. When the belief is challenged, the person may justify it by referring to their reason for holding it. In many cases, this reason depends itself on another belief that may as well be challenged. An example is a person who believes that Ford cars are cheaper than BMWs. When their belief is challenged, they may justify it by claiming that they heard it from a reliable source. This justification depends on the assumption that their source is reliable, which may itself be challenged. The same may apply to any subsequent reason they cite.[[122]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-auto2-134) This threatens to lead to an [infinite regress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinite_regress "Infinite regress") since the epistemic status at each step depends on the epistemic status of the previous step.[[123]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-135) Theories of the structure of knowledge offer responses for how to solve this problem.[[122]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-auto2-134)

[![Image 9: Diagram showing the differences between foundationalism, coherentism, and infinitism](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Foundationalism%2C_coherentism%2C_infinitism.svg/500px-Foundationalism%2C_coherentism%2C_infinitism.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Foundationalism,_coherentism,_infinitism.svg)

Foundationalism, coherentism, and infinitism are theories of the structure of knowledge. The black arrows symbolize how one belief supports another belief.

Three traditional theories are [foundationalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foundationalism "Foundationalism"), [coherentism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coherentism "Coherentism"), and [infinitism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinitism "Infinitism"). Foundationalists and coherentists deny the existence of an infinite regress, in contrast to infinitists.[[122]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-auto2-134) According to foundationalists, some basic reasons have their epistemic status independent of other reasons and thereby constitute the endpoint of the regress.[[124]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-136) Some foundationalists hold that certain sources of knowledge, like perception, provide basic reasons. Another view is that this role is played by certain self-evident truths, like the [knowledge of one's own existence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cogito_ergo_sum "Cogito ergo sum") and the content of one's ideas.[[125]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-137) The view that basic reasons exist is not universally accepted. One criticism states that there should be a reason why some reasons are basic while others are not. According to this view, the putative basic reasons are not actually basic since their status would depend on other reasons. Another criticism is based on [hermeneutics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermeneutics "Hermeneutics") and argues that all [understanding is circular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermeneutic_circle "Hermeneutic circle") and requires interpretation, which implies that knowledge does not need a secure foundation.[[126]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-138)

Coherentists and infinitists avoid these problems by denying the contrast between basic and non-basic reasons. Coherentists argue that there is only a finite number of reasons, which mutually support and justify one another. This is based on the intuition that beliefs do not exist in isolation but form a complex web of interconnected ideas that is justified by its coherence rather than by a few privileged foundational beliefs.[[127]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-auto-139) One difficulty for this view is how to demonstrate that it does not involve the fallacy of [circular reasoning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circular_reasoning "Circular reasoning").[[128]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-140) If two beliefs mutually support each other then a person has a reason for accepting one belief if they already have the other. However, mutual support alone is not a good reason for newly accepting both beliefs at once. A closely related issue is that there can be distinct sets of coherent beliefs. Coherentists face the problem of explaining why someone should accept one coherent set rather than another.[[127]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-auto-139) For infinitists, in contrast to foundationalists and coherentists, there is an infinite number of reasons. This view embraces the idea that there is a regress since each reason depends on another reason. One difficulty for this view is that the human mind is limited and may not be able to possess an infinite number of reasons. This raises the question of whether, according to infinitism, human knowledge is possible at all.[[129]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-141)

Value
-----

[![Image 10: Sculpture showing a torch being passed form one person to another](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Los_portadores_de_la_antorcha.jpg/250px-Los_portadores_de_la_antorcha.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Los_portadores_de_la_antorcha.jpg)

_Los portadores de la antorcha (The Torch-Bearers)_ – sculpture by [Anna Hyatt Huntington](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anna_Hyatt_Huntington "Anna Hyatt Huntington") symbolizing the transmission of knowledge from one generation to the next ([Ciudad Universitaria, Madrid, Spain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complutense_University_of_Madrid "Complutense University of Madrid"))

Knowledge may be valuable either because it is useful or because it is good in itself. Knowledge can be useful by helping a person achieve their goals. For example, if one knows the answers to questions in an exam one is able to pass that exam or by knowing which horse is the fastest, one can earn money from bets. In these cases, knowledge has [instrumental value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Instrumental_value "Instrumental value").[[130]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-142) Not all forms of knowledge are useful and many beliefs about trivial matters have no instrumental value. This concerns, for example, knowing how many grains of sand are on a specific beach or memorizing phone numbers one never intends to call. In a few cases, knowledge may even have a negative value. For example, if a person's life depends on gathering the courage to jump over a ravine, then having a true belief about the involved dangers may hinder them from doing so.[[131]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-143)

[![Image 11: Photo of early childhood education in Ziway, Ethiopia](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/Early_Childhood_Education_USAID_Africa.jpg/250px-Early_Childhood_Education_USAID_Africa.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Early_Childhood_Education_USAID_Africa.jpg)

The value of knowledge plays a key role in education for deciding which knowledge to pass on to the students.

Besides having instrumental value, knowledge may also have [intrinsic value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiology#Intrinsic_value "Axiology"). This means that some forms of knowledge are good in themselves even if they do not provide any practical benefits. According to philosopher [Duncan Pritchard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duncan_Pritchard "Duncan Pritchard"), this applies to forms of knowledge linked to [wisdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wisdom "Wisdom").[[132]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-144) It is controversial whether all knowledge has intrinsic value, including knowledge about trivial facts like knowing whether the biggest apple tree had an even number of leaves yesterday morning. One view in favor of the intrinsic value of knowledge states that having no belief about a matter is a neutral state and knowledge is always better than this neutral state, even if the value difference is only minimal.[[133]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-145)

A more specific issue in epistemology concerns the question of whether or why knowledge is more valuable than mere true belief.[[134]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-146) There is wide agreement that knowledge is usually good in some sense but the thesis that knowledge is better than true belief is controversial. An early discussion of this problem is found in Plato's _[Meno](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meno "Meno")_ in relation to the claim that both knowledge and true belief can successfully guide [action](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_(philosophy) "Action (philosophy)") and, therefore, have apparently the same value. For example, it seems that mere true belief is as effective as knowledge when trying to find the way to [Larissa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Larissa "Larissa").[[135]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-147) According to Plato, knowledge is better because it is more stable.[[136]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-148) Another suggestion is that knowledge gets its additional value from justification. One difficulty for this view is that while justification makes it more probable that a belief is true, it is not clear what additional value it provides in comparison to an unjustified belief that is already true.[[137]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-149)

The problem of the value of knowledge is often discussed in relation to [reliabilism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reliabilism "Reliabilism") and [virtue epistemology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virtue_epistemology "Virtue epistemology").[[138]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-150) Reliabilism can be defined as the thesis that knowledge is reliably formed true belief. This view has difficulties in explaining why knowledge is valuable or how a reliable belief-forming process adds additional value.[[139]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-151) According to an analogy by philosopher [Linda Zagzebski](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linda_Zagzebski "Linda Zagzebski"), a cup of coffee made by a reliable coffee machine has the same value as an equally good cup of coffee made by an unreliable coffee machine.[[140]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-152) This difficulty in solving the value problem is sometimes used as an argument against reliabilism.[[141]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-153) Virtue epistemology, by contrast, offers a unique solution to the value problem. Virtue epistemologists see knowledge as the manifestation of cognitive virtues. They hold that knowledge has additional value due to its association with virtue. This is based on the idea that cognitive success in the form of the manifestation of virtues is inherently valuable independent of whether the resulting states are instrumentally useful.[[142]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-154)

Acquiring and transmitting knowledge often comes with certain costs, such as the material resources required to obtain new information and the time and energy needed to understand it. For this reason, an awareness of the value of knowledge is crucial to many fields that have to make decisions about whether to seek knowledge about a specific matter. On a political level, this concerns the problem of identifying the most promising research programs to allocate funds.[[143]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-155) Similar concerns affect businesses, where stakeholders have to decide whether the cost of acquiring knowledge is justified by the economic benefits that this knowledge may provide, and the military, which relies on [intelligence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Military_intelligence "Military intelligence") to identify and prevent threats.[[144]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-156) In the field of education, the value of knowledge can be used to choose which knowledge should be passed on to the students.[[145]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-157)

Science
-------

The scientific approach is usually regarded as an exemplary process of how to gain knowledge about empirical facts.[[146]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-auto5-158) Scientific knowledge includes mundane knowledge about easily observable facts, for example, [chemical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical "Chemical") knowledge that certain reactants become hot when mixed together. It also encompasses knowledge of less tangible issues, like claims about the behavior of [genes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gene "Gene"), [neutrinos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutrino "Neutrino"), and [black holes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_hole "Black hole").[[147]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-159)

A key aspect of most forms of science is that they seek natural laws that explain empirical observations.[[146]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-auto5-158) Scientific knowledge is discovered and tested using the [scientific method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_method "Scientific method").[[m]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-161) This method aims to arrive at reliable knowledge by formulating the problem in a clear way and by ensuring that the evidence used to support or refute a specific theory is public, reliable, and replicable. This way, other researchers can repeat the experiments and observations in the initial study to confirm or disconfirm it.[[149]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-162) The scientific method is often analyzed as a series of steps that begins with regular observation and data collection. Based on these insights, scientists then try to find a [hypothesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypothesis "Hypothesis") that explains the observations. The hypothesis is then tested using a controlled experiment to compare whether predictions based on the hypothesis match the observed results. As a last step, the results are interpreted and a conclusion is reached whether and to what degree the findings confirm or disconfirm the hypothesis.[[150]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-163)

The empirical sciences are usually divided into [natural](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_science "Natural science") and [social sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_science "Social science"). The natural sciences, like [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics"), [biology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology "Biology"), and [chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemistry "Chemistry"), focus on [quantitative research](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantitative_research "Quantitative research") methods to arrive at knowledge about natural phenomena.[[151]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-164) Quantitative research happens by making precise numerical [measurements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Measurements "Measurements") and the natural sciences often rely on advanced technological [instruments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Instrumentation "Instrumentation") to perform these measurements and to setup experiments. Another common feature of their approach is to use mathematical tools to [analyze the measured data](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_analysis "Data analysis") and formulate [exact and general laws](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_law "Scientific law") to describe the observed phenomena.[[152]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-165)

The social sciences, like [sociology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sociology "Sociology"), [anthropology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anthropology "Anthropology"), and [communication studies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communication_studies "Communication studies"), examine social phenomena on the level of human behavior, relationships, and society at large.[[153]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-166) While they also make use of quantitative research, they usually give more emphasis to [qualitative methods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qualitative_research "Qualitative research"). Qualitative research gathers non-numerical data, often with the goal of arriving at a deeper understanding of the meaning and interpretation of social phenomena from the perspective of those involved.[[154]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-167) This approach can take various forms, such as [interviews](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interview_(research) "Interview (research)"), [focus groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Focus_groups "Focus groups"), and [case studies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Case_studies "Case studies").[[155]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-168)[Mixed-method research](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mixed-method_research "Mixed-method research") combines quantitative and qualitative methods to explore the same phenomena from a variety of perspectives to get a more comprehensive understanding.[[156]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-169)

The progress of scientific knowledge is traditionally seen as a gradual and continuous process in which the existing body of knowledge is increased at each step. This view has been challenged by some philosophers of science, such as [Thomas Kuhn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Kuhn "Thomas Kuhn"), who holds that between phases of incremental progress, there are so-called scientific revolutions in which a [paradigm shift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paradigm_shift "Paradigm shift") occurs. According to this view, some basic assumptions are changed due to the paradigm shift, resulting in a radically new perspective on the body of scientific knowledge that is [incommensurable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commensurability_(philosophy_of_science) "Commensurability (philosophy of science)") with the previous outlook.[[157]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-170)[[n]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-172)

[Scientism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientism "Scientism") refers to a group of views that privilege the sciences and the scientific method over other forms of inquiry and knowledge acquisition. In its strongest formulation, it is the claim that there is no other knowledge besides scientific knowledge.[[159]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-173) A common critique of scientism, made by philosophers such as [Hans-Georg Gadamer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hans-Georg_Gadamer "Hans-Georg Gadamer") and [Paul Feyerabend](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Feyerabend "Paul Feyerabend"), is that the fixed requirement of following the scientific method is too rigid and results in a misleading picture of reality by excluding various relevant phenomena from the scope of knowledge.[[160]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-174)

History
-------

The history of knowledge is the field of inquiry that studies how knowledge in different fields has developed and evolved in the course of history. It is closely related to the [history of science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_science "History of science"), but covers a wider area that includes knowledge from fields like [philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_philosophy "History of philosophy"), [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_mathematics "History of mathematics"), [education](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_education "History of education"), [literature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_literature "History of literature"), [art](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_art "History of art"), and [religion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_religion "History of religion"). It further covers practical knowledge of specific [crafts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Craft "Craft"), [medicine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_medicine "History of medicine"), and everyday practices. It investigates not only how knowledge is created and employed, but also how it is disseminated and preserved.[[161]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-175)

[Before the ancient period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prehistory "Prehistory"), knowledge about social conduct and survival skills was passed down orally and in the form of customs from one generation to the next.[[162]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-176) The [ancient period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_period "Ancient period") saw the rise of major civilizations starting about 3000 BCE in [Mesopotamia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mesopotamia "Mesopotamia"), [Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Egypt "Ancient Egypt"), [India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_India "Ancient India"), and [China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_China "Ancient China"). The [invention of writing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invention_of_writing "Invention of writing") in this period significantly increased the amount of stable knowledge within society since it could be stored and shared without being limited by [imperfect human memory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Memory#Failures "Memory").[[163]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-177) During this time, the first developments in scientific fields like mathematics, astronomy, and medicine were made. They were later formalized and greatly expanded by the [ancient Greeks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greece "Ancient Greece") starting in the 6th century BCE. Other ancient advancements concerned knowledge in the fields of agriculture, law, and politics.[[164]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-178)

[![Image 12: Photo of a replica of the printing press created by Johannes Gutenberg](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Printing_machine_of_Johanes_Gutenbrg1.jpg/250px-Printing_machine_of_Johanes_Gutenbrg1.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Printing_machine_of_Johanes_Gutenbrg1.jpg)

The invention of the printing press in the 15th century greatly expanded access to written materials.

In the [medieval period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval_period "Medieval period"), religious knowledge was a central concern, and religious institutions, like the [Catholic Church](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catholic_Church "Catholic Church") in Europe, influenced intellectual activity.[[165]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-179) Jewish communities set up [yeshivas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yeshiva "Yeshiva") as centers for studying religious texts and [Jewish law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halakha "Halakha").[[166]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-180) In the [Muslim world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muslim_world "Muslim world"), [madrasa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Madrasa "Madrasa") schools were established and focused on [Islamic law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamic_law "Islamic law") and [Islamic philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamic_philosophy "Islamic philosophy").[[167]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-181) Many intellectual achievements of the ancient period were preserved, refined, and expanded during the [Islamic Golden Age](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamic_Golden_Age "Islamic Golden Age") from the 8th to 13th centuries.[[168]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-182) Centers of higher learning were established in this period in various regions, like [Al-Qarawiyyin University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Qarawiyyin_University "Al-Qarawiyyin University") in Morocco,[[169]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-183) the [Al-Azhar University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Azhar_University "Al-Azhar University") in Egypt,[[170]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-184) the [House of Wisdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/House_of_Wisdom "House of Wisdom") in Iraq,[[171]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-185) and the first universities in Europe.[[172]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-186) This period also saw the formation of [guilds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guilds "Guilds"), which preserved and advanced technical and craft knowledge.[[173]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-187)

In the [Renaissance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Renaissance "Renaissance") period, starting in the 14th century, there was a renewed interest in the [humanities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Humanities "Humanities") and sciences.[[174]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-188) The [printing press](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Printing_press "Printing press") was invented in the 15th century and significantly increased the availability of written media and general [literacy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Literacy "Literacy") of the population.[[175]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-189) These developments served as the foundation of the [Scientific Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_Revolution "Scientific Revolution") in the [Age of Enlightenment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment "Age of Enlightenment") starting in the 16th and 17th centuries. It led to an explosion of knowledge in fields such as physics, chemistry, biology, and the social sciences.[[176]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-190) The technological advancements that accompanied this development made possible the [Industrial Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Industrial_Revolution "Industrial Revolution") in the 18th and 19th centuries.[[177]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-191) In the 20th century, the development of computers and the Internet led to a vast expansion of knowledge by revolutionizing how knowledge is stored, shared, and created.[[178]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-192)[[o]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-194)

In various disciplines
----------------------

### Religion

Knowledge plays a central role in many religions. Knowledge claims about the [existence of God](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence_of_God "Existence of God") or [religious doctrines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Religious_doctrine "Religious doctrine") about how each one should live their lives are found in almost every culture.[[180]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-195) However, such knowledge claims are often controversial and are commonly rejected by [religious skeptics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Religious_skepticism "Religious skepticism") and [atheists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atheists "Atheists").[[181]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-Penelhum1971-196) The [epistemology of religion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Religious_epistemology "Religious epistemology") is the field of inquiry studying whether [belief in God](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belief_in_God "Belief in God") and in other religious doctrines is [rational](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational "Rational") and amounts to knowledge.[[182]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-197) One important view in this field is [evidentialism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evidentialism "Evidentialism"), which states that belief in religious doctrines is justified if it is supported by sufficient evidence. Suggested examples of evidence for religious doctrines include [religious experiences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Religious_experience "Religious experience") such as direct contact with the divine or inner testimony when hearing God's voice.[[183]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-198) Evidentialists often reject that belief in religious doctrines amounts to knowledge based on the claim that there is not sufficient evidence.[[184]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-199) A famous saying in this regard is due to Bertrand Russell. When asked how he would justify his lack of belief in God when facing his judgment after death, he replied "Not enough evidence, God! Not enough evidence."[[185]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-200)

However, religious teachings about the existence and nature of God are not always seen as knowledge claims by their defenders. Some explicitly state that the proper attitude towards such doctrines is not knowledge but [faith](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Faith "Faith"). This is often combined with the assumption that these doctrines are true but cannot be fully understood by reason or verified through rational inquiry. For this reason, it is claimed that one should accept them even though they do not amount to knowledge.[[181]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-Penelhum1971-196) Such a view is reflected in a famous saying by Immanuel Kant where he claims that he "had to deny knowledge in order to make room for faith."[[186]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-201)

Distinct religions often differ from each other concerning the doctrines they proclaim as well as their understanding of the role of knowledge in religious practice.[[187]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-202) In both the Jewish and the Christian traditions, knowledge plays a role in the [fall of man](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fall_of_man "Fall of man"), in which Adam and Eve were expelled from the [Garden of Eden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Garden_of_Eden "Garden of Eden"). Responsible for this fall was that they ignored God's command and ate from the [tree of knowledge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tree_of_the_knowledge_of_good_and_evil "Tree of the knowledge of good and evil"), which gave them the knowledge of good and evil. This is seen as a rebellion against God since this knowledge belongs to God and it is not for humans to decide what is right or wrong.[[188]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-203) In the Christian literature, knowledge is seen as one of the [seven gifts of the Holy Spirit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seven_gifts_of_the_Holy_Spirit "Seven gifts of the Holy Spirit").[[189]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-204) In [Islam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islam "Islam"), "the Knowing" (_al-ʿAlīm_) is one of the [99 names](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Names_of_God_in_the_Qur%27an "Names of God in the Qur'an") reflecting distinct attributes of [God](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/God_in_Islam "God in Islam"). The [Qur'an](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qur%27an "Qur'an") asserts that knowledge comes from [Allah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allah "Allah") and the acquisition of knowledge is encouraged in the [teachings of Muhammad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hadith "Hadith").[[190]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-205)

[![Image 13: Oil painting showing Saraswati](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Saraswati_-_Raja_Ravi_Varma.jpg/250px-Saraswati_-_Raja_Ravi_Varma.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Saraswati_-_Raja_Ravi_Varma.jpg)

[Saraswati](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saraswati "Saraswati") is the goddess of knowledge and the arts in Hinduism.

In Buddhism, knowledge that leads to [liberation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enlightenment_in_Buddhism "Enlightenment in Buddhism") is called _vijjā_. It contrasts with _[avijjā](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Avijj%C4%81 "Avijjā")_ or ignorance, which is understood as the root of all [suffering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Du%E1%B8%A5kha "Duḥkha"). This is often explained in relation to the claim that humans suffer because they crave things that are [impermanent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Impermanence#Buddhism "Impermanence"). The ignorance of the impermanent nature of things is seen as the factor responsible for this craving.[[191]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-206) The central goal of Buddhist practice is to stop suffering. This aim is to be achieved by understanding and practicing the teaching known as the [Four Noble Truths](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Four_Noble_Truths "Four Noble Truths") and thereby overcoming ignorance.[[192]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-207) Knowledge plays a key role in the classical path of Hinduism known as _[jñāna yoga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jnana\_yoga "Jnana yoga")_ or "path of knowledge". It aims to achieve oneness with the divine by fostering an understanding of the self and its relation to [Brahman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brahman "Brahman") or ultimate reality.[[193]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-208)

### Anthropology

The _anthropology of knowledge_ is a multi-disciplinary field of inquiry.[[194]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-209) It studies how knowledge is acquired, stored, retrieved, and communicated.[[195]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-210) Special interest is given to how knowledge is reproduced and changes in relation to social and cultural circumstances.[[196]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-Allwood2013-211) In this context, the term _knowledge_ is used in a very broad sense, roughly equivalent to terms like _understanding_ and _culture_.[[197]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-212) This means that the forms and reproduction of understanding are studied irrespective of their [truth value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Truth_value "Truth value"). In epistemology, by contrast, knowledge is usually restricted to forms of true belief. The main focus in [anthropology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anthropology "Anthropology") is on [empirical observations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empirical_evidence "Empirical evidence") of how people ascribe truth values to meaning contents, like when affirming an assertion, even if these contents are false.[[196]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-Allwood2013-211) This also includes practical components: knowledge is what is employed when interpreting and acting on the world and involves diverse phenomena, such as feelings, embodied skills, information, and concepts. It is used to understand and anticipate events to prepare and react accordingly.[[198]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-213)

The reproduction of knowledge and its changes often happen through some form of communication used to [transfer knowledge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge_transfer "Knowledge transfer").[[199]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-214) This includes face-to-face discussions and online communications as well as seminars and rituals. An important role in this context falls to institutions, like university departments or scientific journals in the academic context.[[196]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-Allwood2013-211) Anthropologists of knowledge understand traditions as knowledge that has been reproduced within a society or geographic region over several generations. They are interested in how this reproduction is affected by external influences. For example, societies tend to interpret knowledge claims found in other societies and incorporate them in a modified form.[[200]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-215)

Within a society, people belonging to the same social group usually understand things and organize knowledge in similar ways to one another. In this regard, social identities play a significant role: people who associate themselves with similar identities, like age-influenced, professional, religious, and ethnic identities, tend to embody similar forms of knowledge. Such identities concern both how a person sees themselves, for example, in terms of the ideals they pursue, as well as how other people see them, such as the expectations they have toward the person.[[201]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-216)

### Sociology

The sociology of knowledge is the subfield of sociology that studies how thought and society are related to each other.[[202]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-auto1-217) Like the anthropology of knowledge, it understands "knowledge" in a wide sense that encompasses philosophical and political ideas, religious and ideological doctrines, folklore, law, and technology. The sociology of knowledge studies in what sociohistorical circumstances knowledge arises, what consequences it has, and on what existential conditions it depends. The examined conditions include physical, demographic, economic, and sociocultural factors. For instance, philosopher [Karl Marx](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karl_Marx "Karl Marx") claimed that the dominant ideology in a society is a product of and changes with the underlying socioeconomic conditions.[[202]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-auto1-217) Another example is found in forms of decolonial scholarship that claim that colonial powers are responsible for the [hegemony](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hegemony "Hegemony") of Western knowledge systems. They seek a [decolonization of knowledge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decolonization_of_knowledge "Decolonization of knowledge") to undermine this hegemony.[[203]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-218) A related issue concerns the link between knowledge and [power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_(social_and_political) "Power (social and political)"), in particular, the extent to which knowledge is power. The philosopher [Michel Foucault](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michel_Foucault "Michel Foucault") explored this issue and examined how knowledge and the institutions responsible for it control people through what he termed [biopower](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biopower "Biopower") by shaping societal norms, values, and regulatory mechanisms in fields like [psychiatry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Psychiatry "Psychiatry"), medicine, and the [penal system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Penal_system "Penal system").[[204]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-219)

A central subfield is the [sociology of scientific knowledge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sociology_of_scientific_knowledge "Sociology of scientific knowledge"), which investigates the social factors involved in the production and validation of scientific knowledge. This encompasses examining the impact of the [distribution of resources](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Funding_of_science "Funding of science") and rewards on the scientific process, which leads some areas of research to flourish while others languish. Further topics focus on selection processes, such as [how academic journals decide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scholarly_peer_review "Scholarly peer review") whether to publish an article and how academic institutions recruit researchers, and the general values and norms characteristic of the [scientific profession](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientist "Scientist").[[205]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-220)

### Others

[Formal epistemology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formal_epistemology "Formal epistemology") studies knowledge using formal tools found in mathematics and logic.[[206]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-221) An important issue in this field concerns the epistemic principles of knowledge. These are rules governing how knowledge and related states behave and in what relations they stand to each other. The transparency principle, also referred to as the _luminosity of knowledge_, states that it is impossible for someone to know something without knowing that they know it.[[p]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-222)[[207]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-223) According to the conjunction principle, if a person has justified beliefs in two separate propositions, then they are also justified in believing the [conjunction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logical_conjunction "Logical conjunction") of these two propositions. In this regard, if Bob has a justified belief that dogs are animals and another justified belief that cats are animals, then he is justified to believe the conjunction that both dogs and cats are animals. Other commonly discussed principles are the closure principle and the evidence transfer principle.[[208]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-224)

[Knowledge management](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge_management "Knowledge management") is the process of creating, gathering, storing, and sharing knowledge. It involves the management of information assets that can take the form of [documents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Document "Document"), [databases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Database "Database"), policies, and procedures. It is of particular interest in the field of business and [organizational development](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organizational_development "Organizational development"), as it directly impacts [decision-making](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decision-making "Decision-making") and [strategic planning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strategic_planning "Strategic planning"). Knowledge management efforts are often employed to increase [operational efficiency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operational_efficiency "Operational efficiency") in attempts to gain a [competitive advantage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Competitive_advantage "Competitive advantage").[[209]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-225) Key processes in the field of knowledge management are knowledge creation, [knowledge storage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_storage "Data storage"), [knowledge sharing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge_sharing "Knowledge sharing"), and knowledge application. Knowledge creation is the first step and involves the production of new information. Knowledge storage can happen through media like books, audio recordings, film, and digital databases. Secure storage facilitates knowledge sharing, which involves the transmission of information from one person to another. For the knowledge to be beneficial, it has to be put into practice, meaning that its insights should be used to either improve existing practices or implement new ones.[[210]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-226)

[Knowledge representation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge_representation "Knowledge representation") is the process of storing organized information, which may happen using various forms of media and also includes information stored in the mind.[[211]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-227) It plays a key role in the [artificial intelligence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artificial_intelligence "Artificial intelligence"), where the term is used for the field of inquiry that studies how computer systems can efficiently represent information. This field investigates how different [data structures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_structure "Data structure") and interpretative procedures can be combined to achieve this goal and which formal languages can be used to express knowledge items. Some efforts in this field are directed at developing general languages and systems that can be employed in a great variety of domains while others focus on an optimized representation method within one specific domain. Knowledge representation is closely linked to [automatic reasoning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Automatic_reasoning "Automatic reasoning") because the purpose of knowledge representation formalisms is usually to construct a [knowledge base](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge_base "Knowledge base") from which [inferences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inference "Inference") are drawn.[[212]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-228) Influential knowledge base formalisms include logic-based systems, [rule-based systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rule-based_system "Rule-based system"), [semantic networks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semantic_networks "Semantic networks"), and [frames](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frame_(artificial_intelligence) "Frame (artificial intelligence)"). Logic-based systems rely on [formal languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formal_language "Formal language") employed in [logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logic "Logic") to represent knowledge. They use linguistic devices like individual terms, [predicates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Predicate_(mathematical_logic) "Predicate (mathematical logic)"), and [quantifiers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantifier_(logic) "Quantifier (logic)"). For rule-based systems, each unit of information is expressed using a conditional production rule of the form "if A then B". Semantic nets model knowledge as a [graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_(discrete_mathematics) "Graph (discrete mathematics)") consisting of [vertices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_(graph_theory) "Vertex (graph theory)") to represent facts or concepts and edges to represent the relations between them. Frames provide complex taxonomies to group items into classes, subclasses, and instances.[[213]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-229)

[Pedagogy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pedagogy "Pedagogy") is the study of [teaching methods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teaching_methods "Teaching methods") or the art of teaching.[[q]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-231) It explores [how learning takes place](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Learning_theory_(education) "Learning theory (education)") and which techniques teachers may employ to transmit knowledge to students and improve their learning experience while keeping them motivated.[[215]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-232) There is a great variety of teaching methods and the most effective approach often depends on factors like the subject matter and the age and proficiency level of the learner.[[216]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-233) In teacher-centered education, the teacher acts as the authority figure imparting information and directing the learning process. [Student-centered approaches](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Student-centered_learning "Student-centered learning") give a more active role to students with the teacher acting as a coach to facilitate the process.[[217]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-234) Further methodological considerations encompass the difference between group work and individual learning and the use of instructional media and other forms of [educational technology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Educational_technology "Educational technology").[[218]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge#cite_note-235)
