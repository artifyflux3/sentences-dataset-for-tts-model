From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Calendar with lunar month, solar year

Not to be confused with [Lunarsolar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunarsolar "Lunarsolar").

[![Image 6](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Chinese_Calendar%28Daoguang_15%29.jpg/250px-Chinese_Calendar%28Daoguang_15%29.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Chinese_Calendar(Daoguang_15).jpg)

Record of the [Chinese lunisolar calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_lunisolar_calendar "Chinese lunisolar calendar") for 1834, 1835, and 1836 during the [Qing dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qing_dynasty "Qing dynasty") under the [Daoguang Emperor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Daoguang_Emperor "Daoguang Emperor")'s Reign (道光十四年，道光十五年，道光十六年)

A **lunisolar calendar** is a [calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calendar "Calendar") in many [cultures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Culture "Culture"), that combines monthly [lunar cycles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunar_cycle "Lunar cycle") with the [solar year](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_year "Solar year"). As with all calendars which divide the year into months, there is an additional requirement that the year have a whole number of months (Moon cycles). The majority of years have twelve months but every second or third year is an [embolismic year](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Embolismic_month "Embolismic month"), which adds a thirteenth [intercalary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intercalation_(timekeeping) "Intercalation (timekeeping)"), embolismic, or leap month.

Lunisolar calendars are [lunar calendars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunar_calendar "Lunar calendar") but, in contrast to purely lunar calendars such as the [Islamic calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamic_calendar "Islamic calendar"), have additional intercalation rules that reset them periodically into a rough agreement with the solar year and thus with the seasons.

Examples
--------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Lunisolar_calendar&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Examples")]

The [Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_calendar "Chinese calendar"), [Buddhist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buddhist_calendar "Buddhist calendar"), [Burmese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burmese_calendar "Burmese calendar"), [Assyrian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Assyrian_calendar "Assyrian calendar"), [Hebrew](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hebrew_calendar "Hebrew calendar"), [Jain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jain_calendar "Jain calendar"), traditional Nepali, [Hindu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hindu_calendar "Hindu calendar"), [Japanese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japanese_calendar "Japanese calendar"), [Korean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_calendar "Korean calendar"), [Mongolian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mongolian_calendar "Mongolian calendar"), [Tibetan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tibetan_calendar "Tibetan calendar"), and [Vietnamese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vietnamese_calendar "Vietnamese calendar") calendars (in the [East Asian Chinese cultural sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_Asian_cultural_sphere "East Asian cultural sphere")), plus the ancient [Hellenic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hellenic_calendars "Hellenic calendars"), [Coligny](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coligny_calendar "Coligny calendar"), and [Babylonian calendars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Babylonian_calendar "Babylonian calendar") are all lunisolar. Also, some of the ancient [pre-Islamic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamic_calendar#Pre-Islamic_calendar "Islamic calendar") calendars in [south Arabia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Arabia "South Arabia") followed a lunisolar system.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunisolar_calendar#cite_note-EI2-Tarikh-1) The Chinese, Coligny and Hebrew[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunisolar_calendar#cite_note-2) lunisolar calendars track more or less the tropical year whereas the Buddhist and Hindu lunisolar calendars track the sidereal year. Therefore, the first three give an idea of the seasons whereas the last two give an idea of the position among the constellations of the full moon.

### Chinese lunisolar calendar

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Lunisolar_calendar&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Chinese lunisolar calendar")]

Main article: [Chinese calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_calendar "Chinese calendar")

[![Image 7](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Five_Phases_and_Four_Seasons_Calendar.png/250px-Five_Phases_and_Four_Seasons_Calendar.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Five_Phases_and_Four_Seasons_Calendar.png)

The Five Phases and Four Seasons of the traditional [Chinese lunisolar calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_lunisolar_calendar "Chinese lunisolar calendar"), with English translation.

The [Chinese calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_calendar "Chinese calendar") (華夏曆法) or [Chinese lunisolar calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_lunisolar_calendar "Chinese lunisolar calendar") is also called Agricultural Calendar [農曆; 农历; Nónglì; 'farming calendar'], or Yin Calendar [陰曆; 阴历; Yīnlì; 'yin calendar']), as movements of the sun (representing [Yang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yin_Yang "Yin Yang")) and moon (representing Yin) are the references for the Chinese lunisolar calendar calculations.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] The Chinese lunisolar calendar is the origin of some variant calendars adopted by other neighboring countries, such as Vietnam and Korea.

Together with astronomical, horological, and [phenological](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/phenology "wikt:phenology") observations, definitions, measurements, and predictions of years, months, and days were refined. Astronomical phenomena and calculations emphasized especially the efforts to mathematically correlate the solar and lunar cycles from the perspective of the earth,[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] which however are known to require some degree of numeric approximation or compromise.

The earliest record of the Chinese lunisolar calendar was in the [Zhou dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zhou_dynasty "Zhou dynasty") (1050 BC – 771 BC, around 3000 years ago.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunisolar_calendar#cite_note-3) Throughout history, the Chinese lunisolar calendar had many variations and evolved with different dynasties with increasing accuracy, including the "six ancient calendars" in the [Warring States period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Warring_States_period "Warring States period"), the Qin calendar in the [Qin dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qin_dynasty "Qin dynasty"), the Han calendar or the Taichu calendar in the [Han dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Han_dynasty "Han dynasty") and [Tang dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tang_dynasty "Tang dynasty"), the Shoushi calendar in the [Yuan dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yuan_dynasty "Yuan dynasty"), and the Daming calendar in the [Ming dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ming_dynasty "Ming dynasty"), etc. Starting in 1912, the western solar calendar is used together with the lunisolar calendar in China.

The most celebrated Chinese holidays, such as the [Chinese New Year](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_New_Year "Chinese New Year") (華夏新年), [Lantern Festival](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lantern_Festival "Lantern Festival") (元宵節), [Mid-Autumn Festival](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mid-Autumn_Festival "Mid-Autumn Festival") (中秋節), and [Dragon Boat Festival](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dragon_Boat_Festival "Dragon Boat Festival") (端午節) are all based upon the [Chinese lunisolar calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_lunisolar_calendar "Chinese lunisolar calendar"). In addition, the popular [Chinese zodiac](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_zodiac "Chinese zodiac") is a classification scheme based on the [Chinese calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_calendar "Chinese calendar") that assigns an animal and its reputed attributes to each year in a repeating twelve-year cycle. The traditional calendar used the [sexagenary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sexagenary_cycle "Sexagenary cycle") cycle-based [_ganzhi_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_calendar_correspondence_table "Chinese calendar correspondence table") system's mathematically repeating cycles of [Heavenly Stems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heavenly_Stems "Heavenly Stems") and [Earthly Branches](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earthly_Branches "Earthly Branches").[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

Movable feasts in the Christian calendars, related to the lunar cycle
---------------------------------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Lunisolar_calendar&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Movable feasts in the Christian calendars, related to the lunar cycle")]

The [Gregorian calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gregorian_calendar "Gregorian calendar") (the world's most commonly used) is a solar one but the [Western Christian churches](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_Christianity "Western Christianity") use a lunar-based algorithm to determine the [date of Easter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Date_of_Easter "Date of Easter") and consequent [movable feasts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Movable_feast "Movable feast").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunisolar_calendar#cite_note-4) Briefly, the date is determined with respect to the [ecclesiastical full moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecclesiastical_full_moon "Ecclesiastical full moon") that follows the [ecclesiastical equinox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecclesiastical_equinox "Ecclesiastical equinox") in March. (These events are almost, but not quite, the same as the actual astronomical observations.) The [Eastern Christian churches](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastern_Christianity "Eastern Christianity") have a similar algorithm that is based on the [Julian calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Julian_calendar "Julian calendar").

### Determining leap months

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Lunisolar_calendar&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Determining leap months")]

[![Image 8](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/60px-Question_book-new.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg)This section **needs additional citations for [verification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability "Wikipedia:Verifiability")**. _Find sources:_["Lunisolar calendar"](https://www.google.com/search?as_eq=wikipedia&q=%22Lunisolar+calendar%22)–[news](https://www.google.com/search?tbm=nws&q=%22Lunisolar+calendar%22+-wikipedia&tbs=ar:1)**·**[newspapers](https://www.google.com/search?&q=%22Lunisolar+calendar%22&tbs=bkt:s&tbm=bks)**·**[books](https://www.google.com/search?tbs=bks:1&q=%22Lunisolar+calendar%22+-wikipedia)**·**[scholar](https://scholar.google.com/scholar?q=%22Lunisolar+calendar%22)**·**[JSTOR](https://www.jstor.org/action/doBasicSearch?Query=%22Lunisolar+calendar%22&acc=on&wc=on)_(July 2021)_ _([Learn how and when to remove this message](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:Maintenance\_template\_removal "Help:Maintenance template removal"))_

A [tropical year](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tropical_year "Tropical year") is approximately 365.2422 [days](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mean_solar_day "Mean solar day") long and a [synodic month](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synodic_month "Synodic month") is approximately 29.5306 days long,[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunisolar_calendar#cite_note-5) so a tropical year is approximately 365.2422 / 29.5306 ≈ 12.36826 months long. Because 0.36826 is between 1⁄3 and 1⁄2, a typical year of 12 months needs to be supplemented with one intercalary or leap month every 2 to 3 years. More precisely, 0.36826 is quite close to 7⁄19 (about 0.3684211): several lunisolar calendars have 7 leap months in every cycle of 19 years (called a '[Metonic cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metonic_cycle "Metonic cycle")'). The [Babylonians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Babylonians "Babylonians") applied the 19-year cycle in the late sixth century BCE.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunisolar_calendar#cite_note-6)

Intercalation of leap months is frequently controlled by the "[epact](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epact "Epact")", which is the difference between the lunar and solar years (approximately 11 days). The classic Metonic cycle can be reproduced by assigning an initial epact value of 1 to the last year of the cycle and incrementing by 11 each year. Between the last year of one cycle and the first year of the next the increment is 12– the _**saltus lunae**_ ([Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin_language "Latin language") for 'leap of the moon')– which causes the epacts to repeat every 19 years. When the epact reaches 30 or higher, an intercalary month is added and 30 is subtracted. The Metonic cycle states that 7 of 19 years will contain an additional intercalary month and those years are numbered: 3, 6, 8, 11, 14, 17 and 19. Both the Hebrew calendar and the Julian calendar use this sequence.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

The Buddhist and Hebrew calendars restrict the leap month to a single month of the year;[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] the number of common months between leap months is, therefore, usually 36, but occasionally only 24 months. Because the Chinese and Hindu lunisolar calendars allow the leap month to occur after or before (respectively) any month but use the true apparent motion of the [Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun "Sun"),[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] their leap months do not usually occur within a couple of months of [perihelion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perihelion "Perihelion"), when the apparent speed of the Sun along the [ecliptic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecliptic "Ecliptic") is fastest (now about 3 January). This increases the usual number of common months between leap months to roughly 34 months when a doublet of common years occurs, while reducing the number to about 29 months when only a common singleton occurs.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

### With uncounted time

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Lunisolar_calendar&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: With uncounted time")]

An alternative way of dealing with the fact that a solar year does not contain an integer number of [lunar months](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunar_month "Lunar month") is by including uncounted time in a period of the year that is not assigned to a named month.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunisolar_calendar#cite_note-7) Some [Coast Salish peoples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coast_Salish_peoples "Coast Salish peoples") used a calendar of this kind. For instance, the [Chehalis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sts%27Ailes "Sts'Ailes") began their count of lunar months from the arrival of spawning [chinook salmon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinook_salmon "Chinook salmon") (in Gregorian calendar October), and counted 10 months, leaving an uncounted period until the next chinook [salmon run](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salmon_run "Salmon run").[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunisolar_calendar#cite_note-8)
