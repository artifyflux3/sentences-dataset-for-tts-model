An **ellipsoid** is a surface that can be obtained from a [sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere "Sphere") by deforming it by means of directional [scalings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scaling_(geometry) "Scaling (geometry)"), or more generally, of an [affine transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_transformation "Affine transformation").

An ellipsoid is a [quadric surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadric_surface "Quadric surface"); that is, a [surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_(mathematics) "Surface (mathematics)") that may be defined as the [zero set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero_set "Zero set") of a [polynomial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial "Polynomial") of degree two in three variables. Among quadric surfaces, an ellipsoid is characterized by either of the two following properties. Every planar [cross section](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_section_(geometry) "Cross section (geometry)") is either an [ellipse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse "Ellipse"), or is empty, or is reduced to a single point (this explains the name, meaning "ellipse-like"). It is [bounded](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bounded_set "Bounded set"), which means that it may be enclosed in a sufficiently large sphere.

An ellipsoid has three pairwise [perpendicular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular "Perpendicular")[axes of symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_symmetry "Rotational symmetry") which intersect at a [center of symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_symmetry "Central symmetry"), called the center of the ellipsoid. The [line segments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_segment "Line segment") that are delimited on the axes of symmetry by the ellipsoid are called the _principal axes_, or simply axes of the ellipsoid. If the three axes have different lengths, the figure is a **triaxial ellipsoid** (rarely **scalene ellipsoid**), and the axes are uniquely defined.

If two of the axes have the same length, then the ellipsoid is an _ellipsoid of [revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface\_of\_revolution "Surface of revolution")_, also called a _[spheroid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spheroid "Spheroid")_. In this case, the ellipsoid is invariant under a [rotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation "Rotation") around the third axis, and there are thus infinitely many ways of choosing the two perpendicular axes of the same length. In the case of two axes being the same length:

*   If the third axis is shorter, the ellipsoid is a sphere that has been flattened (called an _[oblate spheroid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oblate\_spheroid "Oblate spheroid")_).
*   If the third axis is longer, it is a sphere that has been lengthened (called a _[prolate spheroid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prolate\_spheroid "Prolate spheroid")_).

If the three axes have the same length, the ellipsoid is a sphere.

The general ellipsoid, also known as triaxial ellipsoid, is a quadratic surface which is defined in [Cartesian coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system "Cartesian coordinate system") as:

![Image 2: {\displaystyle {\frac {x^{2}}{a^{2}}}+{\frac {y^{2}}{b^{2}}}+{\frac {z^{2}}{c^{2}}}=1,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e827840d891557041e332ae4a1e951827616b911)
where ![Image 3: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc), ![Image 4: {\displaystyle b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f11423fbb2e967f986e36804a8ae4271734917c3) and ![Image 5: {\displaystyle c}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/86a67b81c2de995bd608d5b2df50cd8cd7d92455) are the length of the semi-axes.

The points ![Image 6: {\displaystyle (a,0,0)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f996b14331c3f572f564c075c08379f5260407fc), ![Image 7: {\displaystyle (0,b,0)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2f0cc4529c22456d00199991960eb7ebf2b0da41) and ![Image 8: {\displaystyle (0,0,c)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4c7bb79ff570e90cece9f1d58fce362c3612904a) lie on the surface. The line segments from the origin to these points are called the principal semi-axes of the ellipsoid, because _a_, _b_, _c_ are half the length of the principal axes. They correspond to the [semi-major axis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semi-major_axis "Semi-major axis") and [semi-minor axis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semi-minor_axis "Semi-minor axis") of an [ellipse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse "Ellipse").

In [spherical coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_coordinate_system "Spherical coordinate system") for which ![Image 9: {\displaystyle (x,y,z)=(r\sin \theta \cos \varphi ,r\sin \theta \sin \varphi ,r\cos \theta )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fa4f4e242ce3c28c0c4c340c0413e8243f19080e), the general ellipsoid is defined as:

![Image 10: {\displaystyle {r^{2}\sin ^{2}\theta \cos ^{2}\varphi  \over a^{2}}+{r^{2}\sin ^{2}\theta \sin ^{2}\varphi  \over b^{2}}+{r^{2}\cos ^{2}\theta  \over c^{2}}=1,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4fadff709e811fabe0fdf5129331c556c681a41a)
where ![Image 11: {\displaystyle \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e5ab2664b422d53eb0c7df3b87e1360d75ad9af) is the polar angle and ![Image 12: {\displaystyle \varphi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/33ee699558d09cf9d653f6351f9fda0b2f4aaa3e) is the azimuthal angle.

When ![Image 13: {\displaystyle a=b=c}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/41dfe5f5f74cb7a2c79093352406fd1d19123882), the ellipsoid is a sphere.

When ![Image 14: {\displaystyle a=b\neq c}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/da1c1c2dfce05b8675c08f5f77dea6fccd5ff3e7), the ellipsoid is a spheroid or ellipsoid of revolution. In particular, if ![Image 15: {\displaystyle a=b>c}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ff4e2c770be564fcc272802932f2139280fb5720), it is an [oblate spheroid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oblate_spheroid "Oblate spheroid"); if ![Image 16: {\displaystyle a=b<c}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d70a96ad4f65ae8f76c297346246f2c0a128db5c), it is a [prolate spheroid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prolate_spheroid "Prolate spheroid").

where

![Image 18: {\displaystyle 0\leq \theta \leq \pi ,\qquad 0\leq \varphi <2\pi .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/94cdc4460a601f26bf2523823b90e409ab812505)

### Approximate formula

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ellipsoid&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Approximate formula")]

![Image 35: {\displaystyle S\approx 4\pi {\sqrt[{p}]{\frac {a^{p}b^{p}+a^{p}c^{p}+b^{p}c^{p}}{3}}}.\,\!}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a3ba7c5165e42013df15a6129b6c703ac3a57416)
Here _p_ ≈ 1.6075 yields a relative error of at most 1.061%;[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipsoid#cite_note-7) a value of _p_ = ⁠8/5⁠ = 1.6 is optimal for nearly spherical ellipsoids, with a relative error of at most 1.178%.

In the "flat" limit of c much smaller than a and b, the area is approximately 2π _ab_, equivalent to _p_ = log 2 3 ≈ 1.5849625007.

[![Image 36](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Ellipsoid-ebener-Schnitt.svg/330px-Ellipsoid-ebener-Schnitt.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Ellipsoid-ebener-Schnitt.svg)

Plane section of an ellipsoid

The intersection of a plane and a sphere is a circle (or is reduced to a single point, or is empty). Any ellipsoid is the image of the unit sphere under some affine transformation, and any plane is the image of some other plane under the same transformation. So, because affine transformations map circles to ellipses, the intersection of a plane with an ellipsoid is an ellipse or a single point, or is empty.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipsoid#cite_note-8) Obviously, spheroids contain circles. This is also true, but less obvious, for triaxial ellipsoids (see [Circular section](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circular_section "Circular section")).

### Determining the ellipse of a plane section

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ellipsoid&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Determining the ellipse of a plane section")]

[![Image 37](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Ellipso-eb-beisp.svg/250px-Ellipso-eb-beisp.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Ellipso-eb-beisp.svg)

Plane section of an ellipsoid (see example)

**Given:** Ellipsoid ⁠_x_ 2/_a_ 2⁠ + ⁠_y_ 2/_b_ 2⁠ + ⁠_z_ 2/_c_ 2⁠ = 1 and the plane with equation _n x x_ + _n y y_ + _n z z_ = _d_, which have an ellipse in common.

**Wanted:** Three vectors **f**0 (center) and **f**1, **f**2 (conjugate vectors), such that the ellipse can be represented by the parametric equation

![Image 38: {\displaystyle \mathbf {x} =\mathbf {f} _{0}+\mathbf {f} _{1}\cos t+\mathbf {f} _{2}\sin t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/96e0874b1311f497ed8c40b824ec394da27a54bf)
(see [ellipse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse#Ellipse_as_an_affine_image_of_the_unit_circle_x%C2%B2+y%C2%B2=1 "Ellipse")).

[![Image 39](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Ellipso-eb-ku.svg/330px-Ellipso-eb-ku.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Ellipso-eb-ku.svg)

Plane section of the unit sphere (see example)

**Solution:** The scaling _u_ = ⁠_x_/_a_⁠, _v_ = ⁠_y_/_b_⁠, _w_ = ⁠_z_/_c_⁠ transforms the ellipsoid onto the unit sphere _u_ 2 + _v_ 2 + _w_ 2 = 1 and the given plane onto the plane with equation

![Image 40: {\displaystyle \ n_{x}au+n_{y}bv+n_{z}cw=d.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bee01f13ffe285d314192d70ad31be9c4dea8c6d)
Let _m u u_ + _m v v_ + _m w w_ = _δ_ be the [Hesse normal form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hesse_normal_form "Hesse normal form") of the new plane and

![Image 41: {\displaystyle \;\mathbf {m} ={\begin{bmatrix}m_{u}\\m_{v}\\m_{w}\end{bmatrix}}\;}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c0070bd6ea4c56b2cf322d5d1d687ebe648dca3e)
its unit normal vector. Hence

![Image 42: {\displaystyle \mathbf {e} _{0}=\delta \mathbf {m} \;}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1fab51b46eb622ef9d7d0644eb245e643c7ff984)
is the _center_ of the intersection circle and

![Image 43: {\displaystyle \;\rho ={\sqrt {1-\delta ^{2}}}\;}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3cafa3cb9abe355b62914c43b6bc16738fa790b5)
its radius (see diagram).

Where _m w_ = ±1 (i.e. the plane is horizontal), let

![Image 44: {\displaystyle \ \mathbf {e} _{1}={\begin{bmatrix}\rho \\0\\0\end{bmatrix}},\qquad \mathbf {e} _{2}={\begin{bmatrix}0\\\rho \\0\end{bmatrix}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f87d9a5fa0347a564630cdee680bca5f5b0a4b03)
Where _m w_ ≠ ±1, let

![Image 45: {\displaystyle \mathbf {e} _{1}={\frac {\rho }{\sqrt {m_{u}^{2}+m_{v}^{2}}}}\,{\begin{bmatrix}m_{v}\\-m_{u}\\0\end{bmatrix}}\,,\qquad \mathbf {e} _{2}=\mathbf {m} \times \mathbf {e} _{1}\ .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/694e74514ba1341c42a0c90df3da66235386519b)
In any case, the vectors **e**1, **e**2 are orthogonal, parallel to the intersection plane and have length ρ (radius of the circle). Hence the intersection circle can be described by the parametric equation

![Image 46: {\displaystyle \;\mathbf {u} =\mathbf {e} _{0}+\mathbf {e} _{1}\cos t+\mathbf {e} _{2}\sin t\;.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/42379862edff840b96f8c5f621c23a31d54e88a9)
The reverse scaling (see above) transforms the unit sphere back to the ellipsoid and the vectors **e**0, **e**1, **e**2 are mapped onto vectors **f**0, **f**1, **f**2, which were wanted for the parametric representation of the intersection ellipse.

How to find the vertices and semi-axes of the ellipse is described in [ellipse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse#Ellipse_as_an_affine_image_of_the_unit_circle_x%C2%B2+y%C2%B2=1 "Ellipse").

Determination of the semi axis of the ellipsoid

The pins-and-string construction of an ellipsoid is a transfer of the idea constructing an ellipse using two [pins and a string](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse#Pins-and-string_method "Ellipse") (see diagram).

A pins-and-string construction of an [ellipsoid of revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipsoid_of_revolution "Ellipsoid of revolution") is given by the pins-and-string construction of the rotated ellipse.

The construction of points of a _triaxial ellipsoid_ is more complicated. First ideas are due to the Scottish physicist [J. C. Maxwell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Clerk_Maxwell "James Clerk Maxwell") (1868).[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipsoid#cite_note-9) Main investigations and the extension to quadrics was done by the German mathematician O. Staude in 1882, 1886 and 1898.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipsoid#cite_note-10)[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipsoid#cite_note-11)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipsoid#cite_note-12) A description of the pins-and-string construction of ellipsoids and hyperboloids is contained in the book _[Geometry and the Imagination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry\_and\_the\_Imagination "Geometry and the Imagination")_ by [Hilbert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Hilbert "David Hilbert")&[Cohn-Vossen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stefan_Cohn-Vossen "Stefan Cohn-Vossen").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipsoid#cite_note-13)

### Steps of the construction

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ellipsoid&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Steps of the construction")]

1.   Choose an _ellipse_ E and a _hyperbola_ H, which are a pair of [focal conics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Focal_conics "Focal conics"): ![Image 50: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}E(\varphi )&=(a\cos \varphi ,b\sin \varphi ,0)\\H(\psi )&=(c\cosh \psi ,0,b\sinh \psi ),\quad c^{2}=a^{2}-b^{2}\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fdf0235890bbdd3a6227e218aebe9344f16f1fd1) with the vertices and foci of the ellipse ![Image 51: {\displaystyle S_{1}=(a,0,0),\quad F_{1}=(c,0,0),\quad F_{2}=(-c,0,0),\quad S_{2}=(-a,0,0)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0c1899073fbd99f0859fbd3a36accfb3c1a0691f) and a _string_ (in diagram red) of length l.
2.   Pin one end of the string to [vertex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_(curve) "Vertex (curve)")_S_ 1 and the other to focus _F_ 2. The string is kept tight at a point P with positive y- and z-coordinates, such that the string runs from _S_ 1 to P behind the upper part of the hyperbola (see diagram) and is free to slide on the hyperbola. The part of the string from P to _F_ 2 runs and slides in front of the ellipse. The string runs through that point of the hyperbola, for which the distance |_S_ 1 _P_| over any hyperbola point is at a minimum. The analogous statement on the second part of the string and the ellipse has to be true, too.
3.   Then: P is a point of the ellipsoid with equation ![Image 52: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}&{\frac {x^{2}}{r_{x}^{2}}}+{\frac {y^{2}}{r_{y}^{2}}}+{\frac {z^{2}}{r_{z}^{2}}}=1\\&r_{x}={\tfrac {1}{2}}(l-a+c),\quad r_{y}={\textstyle {\sqrt {r_{x}^{2}-c^{2}}}},\quad r_{z}={\textstyle {\sqrt {r_{x}^{2}-a^{2}}}}.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/77c2b731f482e8ad171f5154847e0f7887d39cc3)
4.   The remaining points of the ellipsoid can be constructed by suitable changes of the string at the focal conics.

Equations for the semi-axes of the generated ellipsoid can be derived by special choices for point P:

![Image 53: {\displaystyle Y=(0,r_{y},0),\quad Z=(0,0,r_{z}).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f870d72ec0e622fb55f6efdc1ccc2b05fcbcf964)
The lower part of the diagram shows that _F_ 1 and _F_ 2 are the foci of the ellipse in the xy-plane, too. Hence, it is [confocal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confocal_conic_sections "Confocal conic sections") to the given ellipse and the length of the string is _l_ = 2 _r x_ + (_a_ − _c_). Solving for r x yields _r x_ = ⁠1/2⁠(_l_ − _a_ + _c_); furthermore _r_ 2

_y_ = _r_ 2

_x_ − _c_ 2.

From the upper diagram we see that _S_ 1 and _S_ 2 are the foci of the ellipse section of the ellipsoid in the xz-plane and that _r_ 2

_z_ = _r_ 2

_x_ − _a_ 2.

If, conversely, a triaxial ellipsoid is given by its equation, then from the equations in step 3 one can derive the parameters a, b, l for a pins-and-string construction.

### Confocal ellipsoids

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ellipsoid&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Confocal ellipsoids")]

If E is an ellipsoid [confocal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confocal_quadrics "Confocal quadrics") to E with the squares of its semi-axes

![Image 54: {\displaystyle {\overline {r}}_{x}^{2}=r_{x}^{2}-\lambda ,\quad {\overline {r}}_{y}^{2}=r_{y}^{2}-\lambda ,\quad {\overline {r}}_{z}^{2}=r_{z}^{2}-\lambda }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a3896fcf3a6fb64b3a1c156b12d2b9d02eeedd8e)
then from the equations of E

![Image 55: {\displaystyle r_{x}^{2}-r_{y}^{2}=c^{2},\quad r_{x}^{2}-r_{z}^{2}=a^{2},\quad r_{y}^{2}-r_{z}^{2}=a^{2}-c^{2}=b^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5909d3e3d84b5ccdfbffaeed4babc006a714d091)
one finds, that the corresponding focal conics used for the pins-and-string construction have the same semi-axes _a_, _b_, _c_ as ellipsoid E. Therefore (analogously to the foci of an ellipse) one considers the focal conics of a triaxial ellipsoid as the (infinite many) foci and calls them the **focal curves** of the ellipsoid.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipsoid#cite_note-14)

The converse statement is true, too: if one chooses a second string of length _l_ and defines

![Image 56: {\displaystyle \lambda =r_{x}^{2}-{\overline {r}}_{x}^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6a65ecfcd4f2649ea29fd40dc6a471e1c32d873f)
then the equations

![Image 57: {\displaystyle {\overline {r}}_{y}^{2}=r_{y}^{2}-\lambda ,\quad {\overline {r}}_{z}^{2}=r_{z}^{2}-\lambda }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/05e8b703d74aa6f94500fe335fe6789e8cc9a3ab)
are valid, which means the two ellipsoids are confocal.

### Limit case, ellipsoid of revolution

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ellipsoid&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Limit case, ellipsoid of revolution")]

In case of _a_ = _c_ (a [spheroid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spheroid "Spheroid")) one gets _S_ 1 = _F_ 1 and _S_ 2 = _F_ 2, which means that the focal ellipse degenerates to a line segment and the focal hyperbola collapses to two infinite line segments on the x-axis. The ellipsoid is [rotationally symmetric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_symmetry "Rotational symmetry") around the x-axis and

![Image 58: {\displaystyle r_{x}={\tfrac {1}{2}}l,\quad r_{y}=r_{z}={\textstyle {\sqrt {r_{x}^{2}-c^{2}}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fcd540c1dd56a3701f1ddb7167620f7dc1c985e0).

### Properties of the focal hyperbola

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ellipsoid&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Properties of the focal hyperbola")]

[![Image 59](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Ellipsoid-pk-zk.svg/500px-Ellipsoid-pk-zk.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Ellipsoid-pk-zk.svg)

**Top:** 3-axial Ellipsoid with its focal hyperbola.

**Bottom:** parallel and central projection of the ellipsoid such that it looks like a sphere, i.e. its apparent shape is a circle

True curve If one views an ellipsoid from an external point V of its focal hyperbola, then it seems to be a sphere, that is its apparent shape is a circle. Equivalently, the tangents of the ellipsoid containing point V are the lines of a circular [cone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cone "Cone"), whose axis of rotation is the [tangent line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_(geometry) "Tangent (geometry)") of the hyperbola at V.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipsoid#cite_note-15)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipsoid#cite_note-16) If one allows the center V to disappear into infinity, one gets an [orthogonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_projection "Orthogonal projection")[parallel projection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_projection "Parallel projection") with the corresponding [asymptote](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asymptote "Asymptote") of the focal hyperbola as its direction. The _true curve of shape_ (tangent points) on the ellipsoid is not a circle. The lower part of the diagram shows on the left a parallel projection of an ellipsoid (with semi-axes 60, 40, 30) along an asymptote and on the right a central projection with center V and main point H on the tangent of the hyperbola at point V. (H is the foot of the perpendicular from V onto the image plane.) For both projections the apparent shape is a circle. In the parallel case the image of the origin O is the circle's center; in the central case main point H is the center.Umbilical points The focal hyperbola intersects the ellipsoid at its four [umbilical points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Umbilical_point "Umbilical point").[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipsoid#cite_note-17)

In higher dimensions and general position
-----------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ellipsoid&action=edit&section=16 "Edit section: In higher dimensions and general position")]

A **hyperellipsoid**, or ellipsoid of dimension ![Image 61: {\displaystyle n-1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fbd0b0f32b28f51962943ee9ede4fb34198a2521) in a [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space") of dimension ![Image 62: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b), is a [quadric hypersurface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadric_hypersurface "Quadric hypersurface") defined by a polynomial of degree two that has a [homogeneous part](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homogeneous_polynomial "Homogeneous polynomial") of degree two which is a [positive definite quadratic form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positive_definite_quadratic_form "Positive definite quadratic form").

One can also define a hyperellipsoid as the image of a sphere under an invertible [affine transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_transformation "Affine transformation"). The spectral theorem can again be used to obtain a standard equation of the form

![Image 63: {\displaystyle {\frac {x_{1}^{2}}{a_{1}^{2}}}+{\frac {x_{2}^{2}}{a_{2}^{2}}}+\cdots +{\frac {x_{n}^{2}}{a_{n}^{2}}}=1.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/774920df7a8e92ad8bd951112e2d193f9391057c)
The volume of an n-dimensional _hyperellipsoid_ can be obtained by replacing R n by the product of the semi-axes _a_ 1 _a_ 2..._a n_ in the formula for the [volume of a hypersphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume_of_an_n-ball#The_volume "Volume of an n-ball"):

![Image 64: {\displaystyle V={\frac {\pi ^{\frac {n}{2}}}{\Gamma {\left({\frac {n}{2}}+1\right)}}}a_{1}a_{2}\cdots a_{n}\approx {\frac {1}{\sqrt {\pi n}}}\cdot \left({\frac {2e\pi }{n}}\right)^{n/2}a_{1}a_{2}\cdots a_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4f9dbf3d2146e2eb28f367a3fab7d47992c1771d)
(where Γ is the [gamma function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamma_function "Gamma function")).

If **A** is a real, symmetric, n-by-n[positive-definite matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positive-definite_matrix "Positive-definite matrix"), and **v** is a vector in ![Image 65: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d7035fcb9fe3ebecc6bc9f372f82d0352202c8bf) then the set of points **x** that satisfy the equation

![Image 66: {\displaystyle (\mathbf {x} -\mathbf {v} )^{\mathsf {T}}\!{\boldsymbol {A}}\,(\mathbf {x} -\mathbf {v} )=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9fa3f8b053299460247484615cd96e39393938dc)
is an _n_-dimensional ellipsoid centered at **v**. The expression ![Image 67: {\displaystyle (\mathbf {x} -\mathbf {v} )^{\mathsf {T}}\!{\boldsymbol {A}}\,(\mathbf {x} -\mathbf {v} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ac3c5bbaeb46bffa813130f0a7dbaef0837eee09) is also called the **ellipsoidal norm** of **x** − **v**. For every ellipsoid, there are unique **A** and **v** that satisfy the above equation.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipsoid#cite_note-:0-18): 67

The [eigenvectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eigenvector "Eigenvector") of **A** are the principal axes of the ellipsoid, and the [eigenvalues](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eigenvalue "Eigenvalue") of **A** are the reciprocals of the squares of the semi-axes (in three dimensions these are _a_−2, _b_−2 and _c_−2).[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipsoid#cite_note-19) In particular:

*   The [diameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diameter "Diameter") of the ellipsoid is twice the longest semi-axis, which is twice the square-root of the reciprocal of the largest eigenvalue of **A**.
*   The [width](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Width "Width") of the ellipsoid is twice the shortest semi-axis, which is twice the square-root of the reciprocal of the smallest eigenvalue of **A**.

An invertible [linear transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_transformation "Linear transformation") applied to a sphere produces an ellipsoid, which can be brought into the above standard form by a suitable [rotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation "Rotation"), a consequence of the [polar decomposition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_decomposition "Polar decomposition") (also, see [spectral theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectral_theorem "Spectral theorem")). If the linear transformation is represented by a [symmetric 3×3 matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetric_matrix "Symmetric matrix"), then the eigenvectors of the matrix are orthogonal (due to the [spectral theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectral_theorem "Spectral theorem")) and represent the directions of the axes of the ellipsoid; the lengths of the semi-axes are computed from the eigenvalues. The [singular value decomposition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Singular_value_decomposition "Singular value decomposition") and [polar decomposition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_decomposition "Polar decomposition") are matrix decompositions closely related to these geometric observations.

For every positive definite matrix ![Image 68: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {A}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5a8b5a6d1dbadead8b1dc48719c888e6cac5f861), there exists a unique positive definite matrix denoted _**A**_ 1/2, such that ![Image 69: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {A}}={\boldsymbol {A}}^{1/2}{\boldsymbol {A}}^{1/2};}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fd264a712b9268cc3f01a8ad41838a31f10a7bc0) this notation is motivated by the fact that this matrix can be seen as the "positive square root" of ![Image 70: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {A}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c66e20ae7faf5a23b00241e8aa7f84f9df9b86bd) The ellipsoid defined by ![Image 71: {\displaystyle (\mathbf {x} -\mathbf {v} )^{\mathsf {T}}\!{\boldsymbol {A}}\,(\mathbf {x} -\mathbf {v} )=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9fa3f8b053299460247484615cd96e39393938dc) can also be presented as[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipsoid#cite_note-:0-18): 67

> ![Image 72: {\displaystyle A^{-1/2}\cdot S(\mathbf {0} ,1)+\mathbf {v} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/19c6327f81b1a32aea9c95dc9a57cb6b617b5379)

where S(**0**,1) is the [unit sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_sphere "Unit sphere") around the origin.

### Parametric representation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ellipsoid&action=edit&section=18 "Edit section: Parametric representation")]

[![Image 73](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Ellipsoid-affin.svg/330px-Ellipsoid-affin.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Ellipsoid-affin.svg)

ellipsoid as an affine image of the unit sphere

The key to a parametric representation of an ellipsoid in general position is the alternative definition:

_An ellipsoid is an affine image of the unit sphere._
An [affine transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_transformation "Affine transformation") can be represented by a translation with a vector **f**0 and a regular 3×3 matrix _**A**_:

![Image 74: {\displaystyle \mathbf {x} \mapsto \mathbf {f} _{0}+{\boldsymbol {A}}\mathbf {x} =\mathbf {f} _{0}+x\mathbf {f} _{1}+y\mathbf {f} _{2}+z\mathbf {f} _{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a0a3a6b42a120b369119efbc1d46507a6743e37c)
where **f**1, **f**2, **f**3 are the column vectors of matrix _**A**_.

A parametric representation of an ellipsoid in general position can be obtained by the parametric representation of a unit sphere (see above) and an affine transformation:

The ellipsoidal shape finds many practical applications:

[Geodesy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesy "Geodesy")
*   [Earth ellipsoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth_ellipsoid "Earth ellipsoid"), a mathematical figure approximating the shape of the [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth "Earth").
*   [Reference ellipsoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reference_ellipsoid "Reference ellipsoid"), a mathematical figure approximating the shape of [planetary bodies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_body "Planetary body") in general.

[Mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanics "Mechanics")
*   [Poinsot's ellipsoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poinsot%27s_ellipsoid "Poinsot's ellipsoid"), a geometrical method for visualizing the [torque-free motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torque-free_precession "Torque-free precession") of a rotating [rigid body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rigid_body "Rigid body").
*   [Lamé's stress ellipsoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lam%C3%A9%27s_stress_ellipsoid "Lamé's stress ellipsoid"), an alternative to [Mohr's circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mohr%27s_circle "Mohr's circle") for the graphical representation of the [stress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stress_(mechanics) "Stress (mechanics)") state at a point.
*   [Manipulability ellipsoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manipulability_ellipsoid "Manipulability ellipsoid"), used to describe a robot's freedom of motion.
*   [Jacobi ellipsoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacobi_ellipsoid "Jacobi ellipsoid"), a triaxial ellipsoid formed by a rotating fluid

[Crystallography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystallography "Crystallography")
*   [Index ellipsoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Index_ellipsoid "Index ellipsoid"), a diagram of an ellipsoid that depicts the orientation and relative magnitude of [refractive indices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Refractive_index "Refractive index") in a [crystal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystal "Crystal").
*   [Thermal ellipsoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermal_ellipsoid "Thermal ellipsoid"), ellipsoids used in crystallography to indicate the magnitudes and directions of the [thermal vibration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermal_vibration "Thermal vibration") of atoms in [crystal structures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystal_structure "Crystal structure").

*   [Ellipsoid method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipsoid_method "Ellipsoid method"), a [convex optimization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_optimization "Convex optimization") algorithm of theoretical significance

Lighting
*   [Ellipsoidal reflector floodlight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipsoidal_reflector_floodlight "Ellipsoidal reflector floodlight")
*   [Ellipsoidal reflector spotlight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipsoidal_reflector_spotlight "Ellipsoidal reflector spotlight")

Medicine
*   Measurements obtained from [MRI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MRI "MRI") imaging of the [prostate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prostate "Prostate") can be used to determine the volume of the gland using the approximation _L_ × _W_ × _H_ × 0.52 (where 0.52 is an approximation for ⁠π/6⁠)[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipsoid#cite_note-21)

The [moments of inertia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_of_Inertia "Moment of Inertia") of an ellipsoid of uniform density are

For _a_ = _b_ = _c_ these moments of inertia reduce to those for a sphere of uniform density.

[![Image 80](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/2003EL61art.jpg/250px-2003EL61art.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:2003EL61art.jpg)

Artist's conception of [Haumea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haumea "Haumea"), a Jacobi-ellipsoid [dwarf planet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dwarf_planet "Dwarf planet"), with its two moons

Ellipsoids and [cuboids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cuboid "Cuboid") rotate stably along their major or minor axes, but not along their median axis. This can be seen experimentally by throwing an eraser with some spin. In addition, [moment of inertia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_of_inertia "Moment of inertia") considerations mean that rotation along the major axis is more easily perturbed than rotation along the minor axis.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipsoid#cite_note-22)

One practical effect of this is that scalene astronomical bodies such as [Haumea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haumea "Haumea") generally rotate along their minor axes (as does Earth, which is merely [oblate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oblate_spheroid "Oblate spheroid")); in addition, because of [tidal locking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tidal_locking "Tidal locking"), moons in [synchronous orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synchronous_orbit "Synchronous orbit") such as [Mimas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mimas_(moon) "Mimas (moon)") orbit with their major axis aligned radially to their planet.

A spinning body of homogeneous self-gravitating fluid will assume the form of either a [Maclaurin spheroid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maclaurin_spheroid "Maclaurin spheroid") (oblate spheroid) or [Jacobi ellipsoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacobi_ellipsoid "Jacobi ellipsoid") (scalene ellipsoid) when in [hydrostatic equilibrium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrostatic_equilibrium "Hydrostatic equilibrium"), and for moderate rates of rotation. At faster rotations, non-ellipsoidal [piriform](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/pyriform "wikt:pyriform") or [oviform](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oviform "Oviform") shapes can be expected, but these are not stable.

The ellipsoid is the most general shape for which it has been possible to calculate the [creeping flow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Creeping_flow "Creeping flow") of fluid around the solid shape. The calculations include the force required to translate through a fluid and to rotate within it. Applications include determining the size and shape of large molecules, the sinking rate of small particles, and the swimming abilities of [microorganisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganisms "Microorganisms").[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipsoid#cite_note-23)

### In probability and statistics

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ellipsoid&action=edit&section=23 "Edit section: In probability and statistics")]

The [elliptical distributions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptical_distribution "Elliptical distribution"), which generalize the [multivariate normal distribution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multivariate_normal_distribution "Multivariate normal distribution") and are used in [finance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finance "Finance"), can be defined in terms of their [density functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Density_function "Density function"). When they exist, the density functions f have the structure:

![Image 81: {\displaystyle f(x)=k\cdot g\left((\mathbf {x} -{\boldsymbol {\mu }}){\boldsymbol {\Sigma }}^{-1}(\mathbf {x} -{\boldsymbol {\mu }})^{\mathsf {T}}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/82ed22687be6d372e2366fcc4c892d4dc120049f)
where k is a scale factor, **x** is an n-dimensional [random row vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Random_vector "Random vector") with median vector **μ** (which is also the mean vector if the latter exists), **Σ** is a [positive definite matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positive_definite_matrix "Positive definite matrix") which is proportional to the [covariance matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covariance_matrix "Covariance matrix") if the latter exists, and g is a function mapping from the non-negative reals to the non-negative reals giving a finite area under the curve.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipsoid#cite_note-24) The multivariate normal distribution is the special case in which _g_(_z_) = exp(−⁠_z_/2⁠) for quadratic form z.

Thus the density function is a scalar-to-scalar transformation of a quadric expression. Moreover, the equation for any [iso-density surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contour_line "Contour line") states that the quadric expression equals some constant specific to that value of the density, and the iso-density surface is an ellipsoid.
