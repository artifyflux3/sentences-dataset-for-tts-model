The **metre per second** is the unit of both [speed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed "Speed") (a [scalar quantity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_(physics) "Scalar (physics)")) and [velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity "Velocity") (a [vector quantity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_(mathematics_and_physics) "Vector (mathematics and physics)"), which has direction and magnitude) in the [International System of Units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Units "International System of Units") (SI), equal to the speed of a body covering a [distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distance "Distance") of one [metre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metre "Metre") in a time of one [second](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second "Second"). As the base unit for speed in the SI, it is commonly used in physics, mechanics, and engineering contexts. It represents both scalar speed and vector velocity, depending on context. According to the definition of metre,[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metre_per_second#cite_note-nist-definitions-1)1 m/s is exactly ![Image 1: {\textstyle {\frac {1}{299792458}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2f424cc04ee5055bc6bc36f8a46969a40cc15a9d) of the [speed of light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light "Speed of light").

History and Standardization
---------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Metre_per_second&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: History and Standardization")]

The metre per second became the official SI derived unit for both speed and velocity with the establishment of the International System of Units (SI) in 1960 by the **[General Conference on Weights and Measures (CGPM)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Conference_on_Weights_and_Measures "General Conference on Weights and Measures")**.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metre_per_second#cite_note-10) Prior to this, various units such as **[feet per second](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foot_per_second "Foot per second")**, **[miles per hour](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Miles_per_hour "Miles per hour")**, and **[knots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knot_(unit) "Knot (unit)")** were more commonly used, depending on the region and application.

The unit derives from the SI base units of **metre (length)** and **second (time)**, both of which were defined more precisely in the 20th century. The **metre** was originally based on the dimensions of the Earth, but is now defined by the distance light travels in a vacuum in 1/299,792,458 of a second. The **second** is defined using the vibration frequency of cesium atoms (9,192,631,770 oscillations per second).[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metre_per_second#cite_note-11) Because of its accuracy, simplicity and preciseness, this unit is adopted as the official unit of speed and velocity and is almost always used as the unit of speed and velocity in scientific occasions.

Relation to other measures
--------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Metre_per_second&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Relation to other measures")]

The **benz**, named in honour of [Karl Benz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karl_Benz "Karl Benz"), has been proposed as a name for one metre per second.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metre_per_second#cite_note-Klein2011-12) Although it has seen some support as a practical unit,[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metre_per_second#cite_note-Heijungs2005-13) primarily from German sources,[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metre_per_second#cite_note-Klein2011-12) it was rejected as the SI unit of velocity[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metre_per_second#cite_note-Cardarelli2004-14) and has not seen widespread use or acceptance.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metre_per_second#cite_note-Dresner1974-15)

The square of metres per second, or [square metre per square second](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_metre_per_square_second "Square metre per square second"), is used as a unit of [gravitational potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_potential "Gravitational potential").

The "metre per second" symbol is encoded by [Unicode](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unicode "Unicode") at code point U+33A7㎧SQUARE M OVER S.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metre_per_second#cite_note-Unicode-U3300-16)
