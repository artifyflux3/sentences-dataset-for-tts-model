In [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry"), a **4-polytope** (sometimes also called a **polychoron**,[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polychoron#cite_note-1)**polycell**, or **polyhedroid**) is a [four-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Four-dimensional "Four-dimensional")[polytope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polytope "Polytope").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polychoron#cite_note-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polychoron#cite_note-3) It is a connected and closed figure, composed of lower-dimensional polytopal elements: [vertices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_(geometry) "Vertex (geometry)"), [edges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edge_(geometry) "Edge (geometry)"), [faces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Face_(geometry) "Face (geometry)") ([polygons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon "Polygon")), and [cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_(mathematics) "Cell (mathematics)") ([polyhedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyhedron "Polyhedron")). Each face is shared by exactly two cells. The 4-polytopes were discovered by the Swiss mathematician [Ludwig Schl√§fli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ludwig_Schl%C3%A4fli "Ludwig Schl√§fli") before 1853.

The two-dimensional analogue of a 4-polytope is a [polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon "Polygon"), and the three-dimensional analogue is a [polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyhedron "Polyhedron").

Topologically 4-polytopes are closely related to the [uniform honeycombs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_uniform_honeycomb "Convex uniform honeycomb"), such as the [cubic honeycomb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cubic_honeycomb "Cubic honeycomb"), which tessellate 3-space; similarly the 3D [cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube "Cube") is related to the infinite 2D [square tiling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_tiling "Square tiling"). Convex 4-polytopes can be _cut and unfolded_ as [nets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyhedral_net "Polyhedral net") in 3-space.

A 4-polytope is a closed [four-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Four-dimensional_space "Four-dimensional space") figure. It comprises [vertices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_(geometry) "Vertex (geometry)") (corner points), [edges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edge_(geometry) "Edge (geometry)"), [faces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Face_(geometry) "Face (geometry)") and [cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_(mathematics) "Cell (mathematics)"). A cell is the three-dimensional analogue of a face, and is therefore a [polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyhedron "Polyhedron"). Each face must join exactly two cells, analogous to the way in which each edge of a polyhedron joins just two faces. Like any [polytope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polytope "Polytope"), the elements of a 4-polytope cannot be subdivided into two or more sets which are also 4-polytopes, i.e. it is not a compound.

The convex [regular 4-polytopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_4-polytopes "Regular 4-polytopes") are the four-dimensional analogues of the [Platonic solids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platonic_solids "Platonic solids"). The most familiar 4-polytope is the [tesseract](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tesseract "Tesseract") or hypercube, the 4D analogue of the cube.

The convex regular 4-polytopes can be ordered by size as a measure of 4-dimensional content (hypervolume) for the same radius. Each greater polytope in the sequence is _rounder_ than its predecessor, enclosing more content[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polychoron#cite_note-FOOTNOTECoxeter1973292%E2%80%93293Table_I(ii):_The_sixteen_regular_polytopes_{''p,q,r''}_in_four_dimensions-5) within the same radius. The 4-simplex (5-cell) is the limit smallest case, and the 120-cell is the largest. Complexity (as measured by comparing [configuration matrices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_4-polytope#As_configurations "Regular 4-polytope") or simply the number of vertices) follows the same ordering.

4-polytopes cannot be seen in three-dimensional space due to their extra dimension. Several techniques are used to help visualise them.

Orthogonal projection
[Orthogonal projections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_projection "Orthogonal projection") can be used to show various symmetry orientations of a 4-polytope. They can be drawn in 2D as vertex-edge graphs, and can be shown in 3D with solid faces as visible [projective envelopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_envelope "Projective envelope").

Perspective projection
Just as a 3D shape can be projected onto a flat sheet, so a 4-D shape can be projected onto 3-space or even onto a flat sheet. One common projection is a [Schlegel diagram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schlegel_diagram "Schlegel diagram") which uses [stereographic projection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stereographic_projection "Stereographic projection") of points on the surface of a [3-sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3-sphere "3-sphere") into three dimensions, connected by straight edges, faces, and cells drawn in 3-space.

Sectioning
Just as a slice through a polyhedron reveals a cut surface, so a slice through a 4-polytope reveals a cut "hypersurface" in three dimensions. A sequence of such sections can be used to build up an understanding of the overall shape. The extra dimension can be equated with time to produce a smooth animation of these cross sections.

Nets
A [net](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Net_(polytope) "Net (polytope)") of a 4-polytope is composed of polyhedral [cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_(geometry) "Cell (geometry)") that are connected by their faces and all occupy the same three-dimensional space, just as the polygon faces of a [net of a polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Net_(polyhedron) "Net (polyhedron)") are connected by their edges and all occupy the same plane.

Topological characteristics
---------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=4-polytope&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Topological characteristics")]

[![Image 96](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Br%C3%BCckner_Achtzelle_2.jpg/250px-Br%C3%BCckner_Achtzelle_2.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Br%C3%BCckner_Achtzelle_2.jpg)

4-polytopes with 8 cells by [Max Br√ºckner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Max_Br%C3%BCckner "Max Br√ºckner") (1909), including a [Schlegel diagram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schlegel_diagram "Schlegel diagram") of the [tesseract](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tesseract "Tesseract").

The topology of any given 4-polytope is defined by its [Betti numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Betti_number "Betti number") and [torsion coefficients](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torsion_coefficient_(topology) "Torsion coefficient (topology)").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polychoron#cite_note-richeson-6)

The value of the [Euler characteristic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler_characteristic "Euler characteristic") used to characterise polyhedra does not generalize usefully to higher dimensions, and is zero for all 4-polytopes, whatever their underlying topology. This inadequacy of the Euler characteristic to reliably distinguish between different topologies in higher dimensions led to the discovery of the more sophisticated Betti numbers.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polychoron#cite_note-richeson-6)

Similarly, the notion of orientability of a polyhedron is insufficient to characterise the surface twistings of toroidal 4-polytopes, and this led to the use of torsion coefficients.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polychoron#cite_note-richeson-6)

Like all polytopes, 4-polytopes may be classified based on properties like "[convexity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_set "Convex set")" and "[symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry "Symmetry")".

*   A 4-polytope is _[convex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex\_polytope "Convex polytope")_ if its boundary (including its cells, faces and edges) does not intersect itself and the line segment joining any two points of the 4-polytope is contained in the 4-polytope or its interior; otherwise, it is _non-convex_. Self-intersecting 4-polytopes are also known as [star 4-polytopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_4-polytope "Star 4-polytope"), from analogy with the star-like shapes of the non-convex [star polygons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_polygon "Star polygon") and [Kepler‚ÄìPoinsot polyhedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler%E2%80%93Poinsot_polyhedra "Kepler‚ÄìPoinsot polyhedra").
*   A 4-polytope is _[regular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular\_polytope "Regular polytope")_ if it is [transitive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transitive_group_action "Transitive group action") on its [flags](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flag_(geometry) "Flag (geometry)"). This means that its cells are all [congruent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congruence_(geometry) "Congruence (geometry)")[regular polyhedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polyhedra "Regular polyhedra"), and similarly its [vertex figures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_figures "Vertex figures") are congruent and of another kind of regular polyhedron.
*   A convex 4-polytope is _[semi-regular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semiregular\_polytope "Semiregular polytope")_ if it has a [symmetry group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry_group "Symmetry group") under which all vertices are equivalent ([vertex-transitive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex-transitive "Vertex-transitive")) and its cells are [regular polyhedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polyhedron "Regular polyhedron"). The cells may be of two or more kinds, provided that they have the same kind of face. There are only 3 cases identified by [Thorold Gosset](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thorold_Gosset "Thorold Gosset") in 1900: the [rectified 5-cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectified_5-cell "Rectified 5-cell"), [rectified 600-cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectified_600-cell "Rectified 600-cell"), and [snub 24-cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snub_24-cell "Snub 24-cell").
*   A 4-polytope is _[uniform](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uniform\_polytope "Uniform polytope")_ if it has a [symmetry group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry_group "Symmetry group") under which all vertices are equivalent, and its cells are [uniform polyhedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uniform_polyhedron "Uniform polyhedron"). The faces of a uniform 4-polytope must be [regular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polygon "Regular polygon").
*   A 4-polytope is _[scaliform](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scaliform\_polytope "Scaliform polytope")_ if it is vertex-transitive, and has all equal length edges. This allows cells which are not uniform, such as the regular-faced convex [Johnson solids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johnson_solid "Johnson solid").
*   A regular 4-polytope which is also [convex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_polytope "Convex polytope") is said to be a [convex regular 4-polytope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_regular_4-polytope "Convex regular 4-polytope").
*   A 4-polytope is _prismatic_ if it is the [Cartesian product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_product "Cartesian product") of two or more lower-dimensional polytopes. A prismatic 4-polytope is uniform if its factors are uniform. The [hypercube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tesseract "Tesseract") is prismatic (product of two [squares](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_(geometry) "Square (geometry)"), or of a [cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube "Cube") and [line segment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_segment "Line segment")), but is considered separately because it has symmetries other than those inherited from its factors.
*   A _[tiling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tessellation "Tessellation") or [honeycomb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Honeycomb\_(geometry) "Honeycomb (geometry)") of 3-space_ is the division of three-dimensional [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space") into a repetitive [grid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grid_(spatial_index) "Grid (spatial index)") of polyhedral cells. Such tilings or tessellations are infinite and do not bound a "4D" volume, and are examples of infinite 4-polytopes. A _uniform tiling of 3-space_ is one whose vertices are congruent and related by a [space group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_group "Space group") and whose cells are [uniform polyhedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uniform_polyhedron "Uniform polyhedron").
