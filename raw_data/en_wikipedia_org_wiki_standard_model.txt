Title: Standard Model

URL Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model

Published Time: 2002-04-04T13:57:29Z

Markdown Content:
The **Standard Model** of [particle physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_physics "Particle physics") is the [theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_theory "Scientific theory") describing three of the four known [fundamental forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_force "Fundamental force") ([electromagnetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetism "Electromagnetism"), [weak](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weak_interaction "Weak interaction") and [strong interactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strong_interaction "Strong interaction") – excluding [gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity "Gravity")) in the [universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe "Universe") and classifying all known [elementary particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementary_particle "Elementary particle"). It was developed in stages throughout the latter half of the 20th century, through the work of many scientists worldwide,[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-1) with the current formulation being finalized in the mid-1970s upon [experimental confirmation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Experimental_confirmation "Experimental confirmation") of the existence of [quarks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quark "Quark"). Since then, proof of the [top quark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Top_quark "Top quark") (1995), the [tau neutrino](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tau_neutrino "Tau neutrino") (2000), and the [Higgs boson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Higgs_boson "Higgs boson") (2012) have added further credence to the Standard Model. In addition, the Standard Model has predicted various properties of [weak neutral currents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weak_neutral_current "Weak neutral current") and the [W and Z bosons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/W_and_Z_bosons "W and Z bosons") with great accuracy.

Although the Standard Model is believed to be theoretically self-consistent[[note 1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-3) and has demonstrated some success in providing [experimental predictions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Experimental_prediction "Experimental prediction"), it leaves some [physical phenomena unexplained](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics_beyond_the_standard_model "Physics beyond the standard model") and so falls short of being a [complete theory of fundamental interactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theory_of_everything "Theory of everything").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-NYT-20230911-4) For example, it does not fully explain [why there is more matter than anti-matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baryon_asymmetry "Baryon asymmetry"), incorporate the full [theory of gravitation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theory_of_gravitation "Theory of gravitation")[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-5) as described by [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity"), or account for the [universe's accelerating expansion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe "Accelerating expansion of the universe") as possibly described by [dark energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_energy "Dark energy"). The model does not contain any viable [dark matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter "Dark matter") particle that possesses all of the required properties deduced from observational [cosmology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_cosmology "Physical cosmology"). It also does not incorporate [neutrino oscillations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutrino_oscillation "Neutrino oscillation") and their non-zero masses.

The development of the Standard Model was driven by [theoretical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theoretical_physics "Theoretical physics") and [experimental](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Experimental_physics "Experimental physics") particle physicists alike. The Standard Model is a paradigm of a [quantum field theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_field_theory "Quantum field theory") for theorists, exhibiting a wide range of phenomena, including [spontaneous symmetry breaking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spontaneous_symmetry_breaking "Spontaneous symmetry breaking"), [anomalies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anomaly_(physics) "Anomaly (physics)"), and non-perturbative behavior. It is used as a basis for building more exotic models that incorporate [hypothetical particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypothetical_particle "Hypothetical particle"), [extra dimensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extra_dimensions "Extra dimensions"), and elaborate symmetries (such as [supersymmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supersymmetry "Supersymmetry")) to explain experimental results at variance with the Standard Model, such as the existence of dark matter and neutrino oscillations.

Historical background
---------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Standard_Model&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Historical background")]

In 1928, [Paul Dirac](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Dirac "Paul Dirac") introduced the [Dirac equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dirac_equation "Dirac equation"), which implied the existence of [antimatter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antimatter "Antimatter").

In 1954, [Yang Chen-Ning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yang_Chen-Ning "Yang Chen-Ning") and [Robert Mills](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Mills_(physicist) "Robert Mills (physicist)") extended the concept of [gauge theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauge_theory "Gauge theory") for [abelian groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abelian_group "Abelian group"), e.g. [quantum electrodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_electrodynamics "Quantum electrodynamics"), to [nonabelian groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nonabelian_group "Nonabelian group") to provide an explanation for [strong interactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strong_interaction "Strong interaction").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-YM-6) In 1957, [Chien-Shiung Wu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chien-Shiung_Wu "Chien-Shiung Wu") demonstrated [parity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parity_(physics) "Parity (physics)") was not conserved in the [weak interaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weak_interaction "Weak interaction").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-7)

In 1961, [Sheldon Glashow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sheldon_Glashow "Sheldon Glashow") combined the [electromagnetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetism "Electromagnetism") and [weak interactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weak_interaction "Weak interaction").[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-8) In 1964, [Murray Gell-Mann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Murray_Gell-Mann "Murray Gell-Mann") and [George Zweig](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_Zweig "George Zweig") introduced quarks and that same year [Oscar W. Greenberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oscar_W._Greenberg "Oscar W. Greenberg") implicitly introduced color charge of quarks.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-9) In 1967 [Steven Weinberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steven_Weinberg "Steven Weinberg")[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-10) and [Abdus Salam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abdus_Salam "Abdus Salam")[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-11) incorporated the [Higgs mechanism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Higgs_mechanism "Higgs mechanism")[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-Englert1964-12)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-Peter_W._Higgs_1964_508%E2%80%93509-13)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-G.S._Guralnik,_C.R._Hagen,_T.W.B._Kibble_1964_585%E2%80%93587-14) into Glashow's [electroweak interaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electroweak_interaction "Electroweak interaction"), giving it its modern form.

In 1970, Sheldon Glashow, John Iliopoulos, and Luciano Maiani introduced the [GIM mechanism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GIM_mechanism "GIM mechanism"), predicting the [charm quark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charm_quark "Charm quark").[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-:2-15) In 1973 Gross and Wilczek and Politzer independently discovered that non-Abelian gauge theories, like the color theory of the strong force, have [asymptotic freedom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asymptotic_freedom "Asymptotic freedom").[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-:2-15) In 1976, Martin Perl discovered the [tau lepton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tau_lepton "Tau lepton") at the [SLAC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SLAC_National_Accelerator_Laboratory "SLAC National Accelerator Laboratory").[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-16)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-17) In 1977, a team led by Leon Lederman at Fermilab discovered the bottom quark.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-18)

The Higgs mechanism is believed to give rise to the [masses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass "Mass") of all the [elementary particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementary_particle "Elementary particle") in the Standard Model. This includes the masses of the [W and Z bosons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/W_and_Z_bosons "W and Z bosons"), and the masses of the [fermions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermion "Fermion"), i.e. the [quarks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quark "Quark") and [leptons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lepton "Lepton").

After the [neutral weak currents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutral_current "Neutral current") caused by [Z boson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subatomic_particle "Subatomic particle") exchange [were discovered](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gargamelle "Gargamelle") at [CERN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CERN "CERN") in 1973,[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-19)[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-20)[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-21)[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-22) the electroweak theory became widely accepted and Glashow, Salam, and Weinberg shared the 1979 [Nobel Prize in Physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physics "Nobel Prize in Physics") for discovering it. The W± and Z 0[bosons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boson "Boson") were discovered experimentally in 1983; and the ratio of their masses was found to be as the Standard Model predicted.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-23)

The theory of the [strong interaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strong_interaction "Strong interaction") (i.e. [quantum chromodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_chromodynamics "Quantum chromodynamics"), QCD), to which many contributed, acquired its modern form in 1973–74 when [asymptotic freedom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asymptotic_freedom "Asymptotic freedom") was proposed[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-24)[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-25) (a development that made QCD the main focus of theoretical research)[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-26) and experiments confirmed that the [hadrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hadron "Hadron") were composed of fractionally charged quarks.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-27)[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-28)

The term "Standard Model" was introduced by [Abraham Pais](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abraham_Pais "Abraham Pais") and [Sam Treiman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sam_Treiman "Sam Treiman") in 1975,[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-29) with reference to the electroweak theory with four quarks.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-30)[Steven Weinberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steven_Weinberg "Steven Weinberg") has since claimed priority, explaining that he chose the term Standard [Model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Model "Model") out of a sense of modesty[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-31)[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-32)[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-33)[_[better source needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTRS "Wikipedia:NOTRS")_] and used it in 1973 during a talk in Aix-en-Provence in France.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-34)

The Standard Model includes members of several classes of elementary particles, which in turn can be distinguished by other characteristics, such as [color charge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Color_charge "Color charge").

The Standard Model includes 12 [elementary particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementary_particle "Elementary particle") of [spin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_(physics) "Spin (physics)")⁠1/2⁠, known as [fermions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermion "Fermion").[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-:0-35) Fermions respect the [Pauli exclusion principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pauli_exclusion_principle "Pauli exclusion principle"), meaning that two [identical fermions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indistinguishable_particles "Indistinguishable particles") cannot simultaneously occupy the same quantum state in the same atom.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-36) Each fermion has a corresponding [antiparticle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiparticle "Antiparticle"), which are particles that have corresponding properties with the exception of [opposite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Additive_inverse "Additive inverse")[charges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charge_(physics) "Charge (physics)").[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-37) Fermions are classified based on how they interact, which is determined by the charges they carry, into two groups: [quarks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quark "Quark") and [leptons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lepton "Lepton"). Within each group, pairs of particles that exhibit similar physical behaviors are then grouped into [generations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generation_(particle_physics) "Generation (particle physics)") (see the table). Each member of a generation has a greater mass than the corresponding particle of generations prior. Thus, there are three generations of quarks and leptons.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-:1-38) As first-generation particles do not decay, they comprise all of ordinary ([baryonic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baryon "Baryon")) matter. Specifically, all atoms consist of electrons orbiting around the [atomic nucleus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_nucleus "Atomic nucleus"), ultimately constituted of up and down quarks. On the other hand, second- and third-generation charged particles decay with very short [half-lives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Half-life "Half-life") and can only be observed in high-energy environments. Neutrinos of all generations also do not decay, and pervade the universe, but rarely interact with baryonic matter.

There are six quarks: [up](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Up_quark "Up quark"), [down](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Down_quark "Down quark"), [charm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charm_quark "Charm quark"), [strange](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strange_quark "Strange quark"), [top](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Top_quark "Top quark"), and [bottom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bottom_quark "Bottom quark").[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-:0-35)[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-:1-38) Quarks carry [color charge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Color_charge "Color charge"), and hence interact via the [strong interaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strong_interaction "Strong interaction"). The [color confinement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Color_confinement "Color confinement") phenomenon results in quarks being strongly bound together such that they form color-neutral composite particles called [hadrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hadron "Hadron"); quarks cannot individually exist and must always bind with other quarks. Hadrons can contain either a quark-antiquark pair ([mesons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meson "Meson")) or three quarks ([baryons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baryon "Baryon")).[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-39) The lightest baryons are the [nucleons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleon "Nucleon"): the [proton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton "Proton") and [neutron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron "Neutron"). Quarks also carry [electric charge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_charge "Electric charge") and [weak isospin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weak_isospin "Weak isospin"), and thus interact with other fermions through [electromagnetism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetism "Electromagnetism") and [weak interaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weak_interaction "Weak interaction"). The six leptons consist of the [electron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron "Electron"), [electron neutrino](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_neutrino "Electron neutrino"), [muon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muon "Muon"), [muon neutrino](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muon_neutrino "Muon neutrino"), [tau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tau_(particle) "Tau (particle)"), and [tau neutrino](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tau_neutrino "Tau neutrino"). The leptons do not carry color charge, and do not respond to strong interaction. The charged leptons carry an [electric charge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_charge "Electric charge") of −1 _[e](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementary\_charge "Elementary charge")_, while the three [neutrinos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutrino "Neutrino") carry zero electric charge. Thus, the neutrinos' motions are influenced by only the [weak interaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weak_interaction "Weak interaction") and [gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity "Gravity"), making them difficult to observe.

Interactions in the Standard Model. All Feynman diagrams in the model are built from combinations of these vertices. _q_ is any quark, _g_ is a gluon, _X_ is any charged particle, γ is a photon, _f_ is any fermion, _m_ is any particle with mass (with the possible exception of the neutrinos), _m_ B is any boson with mass. In diagrams with multiple particle labels separated by '/', one particle label is chosen. In diagrams with particle labels separated by '|', the labels must be chosen in the same order. For example, in the four boson electroweak case the valid diagrams are WWWW, WWZZ, WWγγ, WWZγ. The conjugate of each listed vertex (reversing the direction of arrows) is also allowed.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-40)

The Standard Model includes 4 kinds of [gauge bosons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauge_boson "Gauge boson") of [spin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_(physics) "Spin (physics)") 1,[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-:0-35) with bosons being quantum particles containing an integer spin. The gauge bosons are defined as [force carriers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Force_carrier "Force carrier"), as they are responsible for mediating the [fundamental interactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_interaction "Fundamental interaction"). The Standard Model explains the four fundamental forces as arising from the interactions, with fermions [exchanging](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Static_forces_and_virtual-particle_exchange "Static forces and virtual-particle exchange")[virtual](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virtual_particle "Virtual particle") force carrier particles, thus mediating the forces. At a macroscopic scale, this manifests as a [force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Force "Force").[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-41) As a result, they do not follow the Pauli exclusion principle that constrains fermions; bosons do not have a theoretical limit on their [spatial density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume_number_density "Volume number density"). The types of gauge bosons are described below.

*   [Electromagnetism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetism "Electromagnetism"): [Photons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon "Photon") mediate the electromagnetic force, responsible for interactions between electrically charged particles. The photon is massless and is described by the theory of [quantum electrodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_electrodynamics "Quantum electrodynamics") (QED).
*   [Strong interaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strong_interaction "Strong interaction"): [Gluons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gluon "Gluon") mediate the strong interactions, which binds quarks to each other by influencing the [color charge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Color_charge "Color charge"), with the interactions being described in the theory of [quantum chromodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_chromodynamics "Quantum chromodynamics") (QCD). They have no mass, and there are eight distinct gluons, with each being denoted through a color-anticolor charge combination (e.g. red–antigreen).[[note 2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-43) As gluons have an effective color charge, they can also interact amongst themselves.
*   [Weak interaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weak_interaction "Weak interaction"): The [W+ , W− , and Z](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/W_and_Z_bosons "W and Z bosons") gauge bosons mediate the weak interactions between all fermions, being responsible for [radioactivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radioactive_decay "Radioactive decay"). They contain mass, with the Z having more mass than the W±

. The weak interactions involving the W±

 act only on [_left-handed_ particles and _right-handed_ antiparticles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chirality_(physics) "Chirality (physics)") respectively. The W±

 carries an electric charge of +1 and −1 and couples to the electromagnetic interaction. The electrically neutral Z boson interacts with both left-handed particles and right-handed antiparticles. These three gauge bosons along with the photons are grouped together, as collectively mediating the [electroweak](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electroweak "Electroweak") interaction.
*   [Gravitation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitation "Gravitation"): It is currently unexplained in the Standard Model, as the hypothetical mediating particle [graviton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graviton "Graviton") has been proposed, but not observed.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-44) This is due to the incompatibility of quantum mechanics and [Einstein's theory of general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity"), regarded as being the best explanation for gravity. In general relativity, gravity is explained as being the geometric curving of spacetime.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-45)

The [Feynman diagram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feynman_diagram "Feynman diagram") calculations, which are a graphical representation of the [perturbation theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perturbation_theory_(quantum_mechanics) "Perturbation theory (quantum mechanics)") approximation, invoke "force mediating particles", and when applied to analyze [high-energy scattering experiments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_accelerator "Particle accelerator") are in reasonable agreement with the data. However, perturbation theory (and with it the concept of a "force-mediating particle") fails in other situations. These include low-energy quantum chromodynamics, [bound states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bound_state "Bound state"), and [solitons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soliton "Soliton"). The interactions between all the particles described by the Standard Model are summarized by the diagrams on the right of this section.

The Higgs particle is a massive [scalar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_field_theory "Scalar field theory") elementary particle theorized by [Peter Higgs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Higgs "Peter Higgs") ([and others](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1964_PRL_symmetry_breaking_papers "1964 PRL symmetry breaking papers")) in 1964, when he showed that Goldstone's 1962 theorem (generic continuous symmetry, which is spontaneously broken) provides a third polarisation of a massive vector field. Hence, Goldstone's original scalar doublet, the massive spin-zero particle, was [proposed as the Higgs boson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1964_PRL_symmetry_breaking_papers "1964 PRL symmetry breaking papers"), and is a key building block in the Standard Model.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-46) It has no intrinsic [spin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_(physics) "Spin (physics)"), and for that reason is classified as a [boson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boson "Boson") with spin-0.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-:0-35)

The Higgs boson plays a unique role in the Standard Model, by explaining why the other elementary particles, except the [photon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon "Photon") and [gluon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gluon "Gluon"), are massive. In particular, the Higgs boson explains why the photon has no mass, while the [W and Z bosons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/W_and_Z_bosons "W and Z bosons") are very heavy. Elementary-particle masses and the differences between [electromagnetism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetism "Electromagnetism") (mediated by the photon) and the [weak force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weak_force "Weak force") (mediated by the W and Z bosons) are critical to many aspects of the structure of microscopic (and hence macroscopic) matter. In [electroweak theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electroweak_interaction "Electroweak interaction"), the Higgs boson generates the masses of the leptons (electron, muon, and tau) and quarks. As the Higgs boson is massive, it must interact with itself.

Because the Higgs boson is a very massive particle and also decays almost immediately when created, only a very high-energy [particle accelerator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_accelerator "Particle accelerator") can observe and record it. Experiments to confirm and determine the nature of the Higgs boson using the [Large Hadron Collider](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Large_Hadron_Collider "Large Hadron Collider") (LHC) at [CERN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CERN "CERN") began in early 2010 and were performed at [Fermilab](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermilab "Fermilab")'s [Tevatron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tevatron "Tevatron") until its closure in late 2011. Mathematical consistency of the Standard Model requires that any mechanism capable of generating the masses of elementary particles must become visible[_[clarification needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Please\_clarify "Wikipedia:Please clarify")_] at energies above 1.4[TeV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronvolt "Electronvolt");[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-47) therefore, the LHC (designed to collide two 7 TeV proton beams) was built to answer the question of whether the Higgs boson actually exists.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-48)

On 4 July 2012, two of the experiments at the LHC ([ATLAS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ATLAS_experiment "ATLAS experiment") and [CMS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compact_Muon_Solenoid "Compact Muon Solenoid")) both reported independently that they had found a new particle with a mass of about 125[GeV/_c_ 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronvolt#Mass "Electronvolt") (about 133 proton masses, on the order of 10−25 kg), which is "consistent with the Higgs boson".[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-49)[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-NYT-20120704-50) On 13 March 2013, it was confirmed to be the searched-for Higgs boson.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-CERN_20130314-51)[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-CERN_EPS2017-52)

Technically, [quantum field theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_field_theory "Quantum field theory") provides the mathematical framework for the Standard Model, in which a [Lagrangian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrangian_(field_theory) "Lagrangian (field theory)") controls the dynamics and kinematics of the theory. Each kind of particle is described in terms of a dynamical [field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(physics) "Field (physics)") that pervades [space-time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space-time "Space-time").[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-53) The construction of the Standard Model proceeds following the modern method of constructing most field theories: by first postulating a set of symmetries of the system, and then by writing down the most general [renormalizable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Renormalization "Renormalization") Lagrangian from its particle (field) content that observes these symmetries.

The [global](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_symmetry "Global symmetry")[Poincaré symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poincar%C3%A9_group "Poincaré group") is postulated for all relativistic quantum field theories. It consists of the familiar [translational symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Translational_symmetry "Translational symmetry"), [rotational symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_symmetry "Rotational symmetry") and the inertial reference frame invariance central to the theory of [special relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_relativity "Special relativity"). The [local](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_symmetry "Local symmetry") SU(3) × SU(2) × U(1) [gauge symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauge_symmetry "Gauge symmetry") is an [internal symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internal_symmetries "Internal symmetries") that essentially defines the Standard Model. Roughly, the three factors of the gauge symmetry give rise to the three fundamental interactions. The fields fall into different [representations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Representation_of_a_Lie_group "Representation of a Lie group") of the various symmetry groups of the Standard Model (see table). Upon writing the most general Lagrangian, one finds that the dynamics depends on 19 parameters, whose numerical values are established by experiment. The parameters are summarized in the table (made visible by clicking "show") above.

#### Quantum chromodynamics sector

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Standard_Model&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Quantum chromodynamics sector")]

The quantum chromodynamics (QCD) sector defines the interactions between quarks and gluons, which is a [Yang–Mills gauge theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yang%E2%80%93Mills_theory "Yang–Mills theory") with SU(3) symmetry, generated by ![Image 2: {\displaystyle T^{a}=\lambda ^{a}/2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f290eaca89deceb27e294b45c5d7eef69f368919). Since leptons do not interact with gluons, they are not affected by this sector. The Dirac Lagrangian of the quarks coupled to the gluon fields is given by ![Image 3: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {L}}_{\text{QCD}}={\overline {\psi }}i\gamma ^{\mu }D_{\mu }\psi -{\frac {1}{4}}G_{\mu \nu }^{a}G_{a}^{\mu \nu },}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3b0b7887d9441531a621c36261e44d6c83363d3f) where ![Image 4: {\displaystyle \psi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/45e5789e5d9c8f7c79744f43ecaaf8ba42a8553a) is a three component column vector of [Dirac spinors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dirac_spinor "Dirac spinor"), each element of which refers to a quark field with a specific [color charge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Color_charge "Color charge") (i.e. red, blue, and green) and summation over [flavor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flavour_(particle_physics) "Flavour (particle physics)") (i.e. up, down, strange, etc.) is implied.

The gauge covariant derivative of QCD is defined by ![Image 5: {\displaystyle D_{\mu }\equiv \partial _{\mu }-ig_{\text{s}}{\frac {1}{2}}\lambda ^{a}G_{\mu }^{a}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f3d271db1f8e1916751bec41d2e7c582e9a4acde), where

*   _γ_ _μ_ are the [Dirac matrices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dirac_matrices "Dirac matrices"),
*   _G_ _a_

_μ_ is the 8-component (![Image 6: {\displaystyle a=1,2,\dots ,8}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/584f686125120403ab10cfd5b783fefe8e6d7121)) SU(3) gauge field,
*   _λ_ _a_

 are the 3 × 3 [Gell-Mann matrices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gell-Mann_matrices "Gell-Mann matrices"), generators of the SU(3) color group,
*   _G_ _a_

_μν_ represents the [gluon field strength tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gluon_field_strength_tensor "Gluon field strength tensor"), and
*   _g_ s is the strong coupling constant.

The QCD Lagrangian is invariant under local SU(3) gauge transformations; i.e., transformations of the form ![Image 7: {\displaystyle \psi \rightarrow \psi '=U\psi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/84a9551de66e75e4d79772d14265223ff7b05ab8), where ![Image 8: {\displaystyle U=e^{-ig_{\text{s}}\lambda ^{a}\phi ^{a}(x)}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2f24649acc9e38e770acd841e78dc40dc6ada4a6) is 3 × 3 unitary matrix with determinant 1, making it a member of the group SU(3), and ![Image 9: {\displaystyle \phi ^{a}(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/37df4c1c86a7b6f8812c30cfcd93bbd4783cf3a6) is an arbitrary function of spacetime.

The electroweak sector is a [Yang–Mills gauge theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yang%E2%80%93Mills_theory "Yang–Mills theory") with the symmetry group U(1) × SU(2)L, ![Image 10: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {L}}_{\text{EW}}={\overline {Q}}_{{\text{L}}j}i\gamma ^{\mu }D_{\mu }Q_{{\text{L}}j}+{\overline {u}}_{{\text{R}}j}i\gamma ^{\mu }D_{\mu }u_{{\text{R}}j}+{\overline {d}}_{{\text{R}}j}i\gamma ^{\mu }D_{\mu }d_{{\text{R}}j}+{\overline {\ell }}_{{\text{L}}j}i\gamma ^{\mu }D_{\mu }\ell _{{\text{L}}j}+{\overline {e}}_{{\text{R}}j}i\gamma ^{\mu }D_{\mu }e_{{\text{R}}j}-{\tfrac {1}{4}}W_{a}^{\mu \nu }W_{\mu \nu }^{a}-{\tfrac {1}{4}}B^{\mu \nu }B_{\mu \nu },}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a8405d3f87bdc5754272ef29657e80adbcfb7da) where the subscript ![Image 11: {\displaystyle j}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2f461e54f5c093e92a55547b9764291390f0b5d0) sums over the three generations of fermions; ![Image 12: {\displaystyle Q_{\text{L}},u_{\text{R}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/34d3e4d698d88b0ea96f7016a3be3e2552ca1b3e), and ![Image 13: {\displaystyle d_{\text{R}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1fd94931576e4b4fa23236106ad2e5e6d74c029c) are the left-handed doublet, right-handed singlet up type, and right handed singlet down type quark fields; and ![Image 14: {\displaystyle \ell _{\text{L}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7fd2bf7c575e8ba912406fdff1557d6620f49bba) and ![Image 15: {\displaystyle e_{\text{R}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2898f8fed9bc6d9cde28f60f7af1883eede490bb) are the left-handed doublet and right-handed singlet lepton fields.

The electroweak [gauge covariant derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauge_covariant_derivative "Gauge covariant derivative") is defined as ![Image 16: {\displaystyle D_{\mu }\equiv \partial _{\mu }-ig'{\tfrac {1}{2}}Y_{\text{W}}B_{\mu }-ig{\tfrac {1}{2}}{\vec {\tau }}_{\text{L}}{\vec {W}}_{\mu }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/78e05c60adee36a88046b5a6f44606aef696000c), where

Notice that the addition of fermion mass terms into the electroweak Lagrangian is forbidden, since terms of the form ![Image 17: {\displaystyle m{\overline {\psi }}\psi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b96616c4307954df9f951c501fd9c5f35a94973c) do not respect U(1) × SU(2)L gauge invariance. Neither is it possible to add explicit mass terms for the U(1) and SU(2) gauge fields. The Higgs mechanism is responsible for the generation of the gauge boson masses, and the fermion masses result from Yukawa-type interactions with the Higgs field.

In the Standard Model, the [Higgs field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Higgs_field "Higgs field") is an SU(2)L doublet of complex [scalar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_(physics) "Scalar (physics)") fields with four degrees of freedom: ![Image 18: {\displaystyle \varphi ={\begin{pmatrix}\varphi ^{+}\\\varphi ^{0}\end{pmatrix}}={\frac {1}{\sqrt {2}}}{\begin{pmatrix}\varphi _{1}+i\varphi _{2}\\\varphi _{3}+i\varphi _{4}\end{pmatrix}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/86d706e3335d1e731c4d82501fbd8c862ff03bd2) where the superscripts + and 0 indicate the electric charge ![Image 19: {\displaystyle Q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8752c7023b4b3286800fe3238271bbca681219ed) of the components. The weak hypercharge ![Image 20: {\displaystyle Y_{\text{W}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/832ea281734f232f063dc72cdaf2b16a7defb76a) of both components is 1. Before symmetry breaking, the Higgs Lagrangian is ![Image 21: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {L}}_{\text{H}}=\left(D_{\mu }\varphi \right)^{\dagger }\left(D^{\mu }\varphi \right)-V(\varphi ),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1121d1b03dd17bdfb3fcda4d20348f0aa3afc07c) where ![Image 22: {\displaystyle D_{\mu }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/267983ed233945e70f42935df28e623cfce15c12) is the electroweak gauge covariant derivative defined above and ![Image 23: {\displaystyle V(\varphi )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35516139da223f6fe5129764734d9932abe27673) is the potential of the Higgs field. The square of the covariant derivative leads to three and four point interactions between the electroweak gauge fields ![Image 24: {\displaystyle W_{\mu }^{a}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/900a6ee8c880be1e7089aa8bef2aa29b02de241e) and ![Image 25: {\displaystyle B_{\mu }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0fe5f2581724819fecf73ea084522e1278fcde4a) and the scalar field ![Image 26: {\displaystyle \varphi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/33ee699558d09cf9d653f6351f9fda0b2f4aaa3e). The scalar potential is given by ![Image 27: {\displaystyle V(\varphi )=-\mu ^{2}\varphi ^{\dagger }\varphi +\lambda \left(\varphi ^{\dagger }\varphi \right)^{2},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f704fe82b0ec9fade63b6781ff21326c042778a6) where ![Image 28: {\displaystyle \mu ^{2}>0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f0772c3f1368864f3d8d1eb4ec89927740b52f88), so that ![Image 29: {\displaystyle \varphi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/33ee699558d09cf9d653f6351f9fda0b2f4aaa3e) acquires a non-zero [Vacuum expectation value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vacuum_expectation_value "Vacuum expectation value"), which generates masses for the Electroweak gauge fields (the Higgs mechanism), and ![Image 30: {\displaystyle \lambda >0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eea25afc0351140f919cf791c49c1964b8b081de), so that the potential is bounded from below. The quartic term describes self-interactions of the scalar field ![Image 31: {\displaystyle \varphi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/33ee699558d09cf9d653f6351f9fda0b2f4aaa3e).

The minimum of the potential is degenerate with an infinite number of equivalent [ground state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ground_state "Ground state") solutions, which occurs when ![Image 32: {\displaystyle \varphi ^{\dagger }\varphi ={\tfrac {\mu ^{2}}{2\lambda }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c4086c2d9f31abc2c4793611a551ae4600494cb9). It is possible to perform a [gauge transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unitary_gauge "Unitary gauge") on ![Image 33: {\displaystyle \varphi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/33ee699558d09cf9d653f6351f9fda0b2f4aaa3e) such that the ground state is transformed to a basis where ![Image 34: {\displaystyle \varphi _{1}=\varphi _{2}=\varphi _{4}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ceff904baec83e124b9e1a3e4cc7ecd1075cfb5e) and ![Image 35: {\displaystyle \varphi _{3}={\tfrac {\mu }{\sqrt {\lambda }}}\equiv v}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2eab2210a4ec31a287f7052e682748591360368b). This breaks the symmetry of the ground state. The expectation value of ![Image 36: {\displaystyle \varphi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/33ee699558d09cf9d653f6351f9fda0b2f4aaa3e) now becomes ![Image 37: {\displaystyle \langle \varphi \rangle ={\frac {1}{\sqrt {2}}}{\begin{pmatrix}0\\v\end{pmatrix}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/969ca007ecefec4d8cabe897f7856626278d32c4) where ![Image 38: {\displaystyle v}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e07b00e7fc0847fbd16391c778d65bc25c452597) has units of mass and sets the scale of electroweak physics. This is the only dimensional parameter of the Standard Model and has a measured value of ~246 GeV/_c_ 2.

After symmetry breaking, the masses of the W and Z are given by ![Image 39: {\displaystyle m_{\text{W}}={\frac {1}{2}}gv}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a28416839d0fba95d0a40b9b712d4522c1205016) and ![Image 40: {\displaystyle m_{\text{Z}}={\frac {1}{2}}{\sqrt {g^{2}+g'^{2}}}v}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/843bb536945512340849f536ba9b9fb5e5301ad8), which can be viewed as predictions of the theory. The photon remains massless. The mass of the [Higgs boson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Higgs_boson "Higgs boson") is ![Image 41: {\displaystyle m_{\text{H}}={\sqrt {2\mu ^{2}}}={\sqrt {2\lambda }}v}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/10b856dffa86ff43b2cdfab4c632525604e590bd). Since ![Image 42: {\displaystyle \mu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9fd47b2a39f7a7856952afec1f1db72c67af6161) and ![Image 43: {\displaystyle \lambda }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b43d0ea3c9c025af1be9128e62a18fa74bedda2a) are free parameters, the Higgs's mass could not be predicted beforehand and had to be determined experimentally.

The [Yukawa interaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yukawa_interaction "Yukawa interaction") terms are: ![Image 44: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {L}}_{\text{Yukawa}}=(Y_{\text{u}})_{mn}({\bar {Q}}_{\text{L}})_{m}{\tilde {\varphi }}(u_{\text{R}})_{n}+(Y_{\text{d}})_{mn}({\bar {Q}}_{\text{L}})_{m}\varphi (d_{\text{R}})_{n}+(Y_{\text{e}})_{mn}({\bar {\ell }}_{\text{L}})_{m}{\varphi }(e_{\text{R}})_{n}+\mathrm {h.c.} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/631781a4722c53e78373c44dc84b75dbe69f0a26) where ![Image 45: {\displaystyle Y_{\text{u}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/981c3edfdac996c9d21da2440a206e692692cf74), ![Image 46: {\displaystyle Y_{\text{d}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3e39cac0d67e06243c7e65ffc0a78b748eb0455a), and ![Image 47: {\displaystyle Y_{\text{e}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6edaf2398a78c9c4ad6924a848d3438fcd430ddc) are 3 × 3 matrices of Yukawa couplings, with the mn term giving the coupling of the generations m and n, and h.c. means Hermitian conjugate of preceding terms. The fields ![Image 48: {\displaystyle Q_{\text{L}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9d5eb41c53113e98d2d4a6b0d8f3f993a7579e06) and ![Image 49: {\displaystyle \ell _{\text{L}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7fd2bf7c575e8ba912406fdff1557d6620f49bba) are left-handed quark and lepton doublets. Likewise, ![Image 50: {\displaystyle u_{\text{R}},d_{\text{R}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5c7cc85cecf632eb69375061b163e8c5eb18f613) and ![Image 51: {\displaystyle e_{\text{R}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2898f8fed9bc6d9cde28f60f7af1883eede490bb) are right-handed up-type quark, down-type quark, and lepton singlets. Finally ![Image 52: {\displaystyle \varphi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/33ee699558d09cf9d653f6351f9fda0b2f4aaa3e) is the Higgs doublet and ![Image 53: {\displaystyle {\tilde {\varphi }}=i\tau _{2}\varphi ^{*}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/70704c70119d005759715c450a1f1d6813def007) is its charge conjugate state.

The Yukawa terms are invariant under the SU(2)L × U(1)Y gauge symmetry of the Standard Model and generate masses for all fermions after spontaneous symmetry breaking.

Fundamental interactions
------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Standard_Model&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Fundamental interactions")]

The Standard Model describes three of the four fundamental interactions in nature; only gravity remains unexplained. In the Standard Model, such an interaction is described as an exchange of [bosons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boson "Boson") between the objects affected, such as a [photon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon "Photon") for the electromagnetic force and a [gluon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gluon "Gluon") for the strong interaction. Those particles are called [force carriers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Force_carrier "Force carrier") or messenger [particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particles "Particles").[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-54)

Fundamental Interactions of the Standard Model including the hypothetical graviton

Despite being perhaps the most familiar fundamental interaction, gravity is not described by the Standard Model, due to contradictions that arise when combining general relativity, the modern theory of gravity, and quantum mechanics.[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-56)[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-57) However, gravity is so weak at microscopic scales, that it is essentially unmeasurable. The [graviton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graviton "Graviton") is postulated to be the mediating particle, but has not yet been proved to exist.[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-58)

Electromagnetism is the only long-range force in the Standard Model. It is mediated by photons and couples to electric charge.[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-59) Electromagnetism is responsible for a wide range of phenomena including [atomic electron shell structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_shell "Electron shell"), [chemical bonds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_bonds "Chemical bonds"), [electric circuits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_circuit "Electric circuit") and [electronics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronics "Electronics"). Electromagnetic interactions in the Standard Model are described by quantum electrodynamics.

The weak interaction is responsible for various forms of [particle decay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_decay "Particle decay"), such as [beta decay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beta_decay "Beta decay"). It is weak and short-range, due to the fact that the weak mediating particles, W and Z bosons, have mass. W bosons have electric charge and mediate interactions that change the particle type (referred to as flavor) and charge. Interactions mediated by W bosons are [charged current interactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charged_current_interaction "Charged current interaction"). Z bosons are neutral and mediate neutral current interactions, which do not change particle flavor. Thus Z bosons are similar to the photon, aside from them being massive and interacting with the neutrino. The weak interaction is also the only interaction to violate [parity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parity_violation "Parity violation") and [CP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CP_violation "CP violation"). Parity violation is maximal for charged current interactions, since the W boson interacts exclusively with left-handed fermions and right-handed antifermions.

In the Standard Model, the weak force is understood in terms of the electroweak theory, which states that the weak and electromagnetic interactions become united into a single _electroweak_ interaction at high energies.

The strong interaction is responsible for hadronic and [nuclear binding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_binding_energy "Nuclear binding energy"). It is mediated by gluons, which couple to color charge. Since gluons themselves have color charge, the strong force exhibits [confinement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Color_confinement "Color confinement") and [asymptotic freedom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asymptotic_freedom "Asymptotic freedom"). Confinement means that only color-neutral particles can exist in isolation, therefore quarks can only exist in hadrons and never in isolation, at low energies. Asymptotic freedom means that the strong force becomes weaker, as the energy scale increases. The strong force overpowers the [electrostatic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrostatic "Electrostatic") repulsion of protons and quarks in nuclei and hadrons respectively, at their respective scales.

While quarks are bound in hadrons by the fundamental strong interaction, which is mediated by gluons, nucleons are bound by an emergent phenomenon termed the _residual strong force_ or _[nuclear force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear\_force "Nuclear force")_. This interaction is mediated by mesons, such as the [pion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pion "Pion"). The color charges inside the nucleon cancel out, meaning most of the gluon and quark fields cancel out outside of the nucleon. However, some residue is "leaked", which appears as the exchange of virtual mesons, which result in an effective attractive force between nucleons. The (fundamental) strong interaction is described by quantum chromodynamics, which is a component of the Standard Model.

Tests and predictions
---------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Standard_Model&action=edit&section=17 "Edit section: Tests and predictions")]

The Standard Model predicted the existence of the [W and Z bosons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/W_and_Z_bosons "W and Z bosons"), [gluon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gluon "Gluon"), [top quark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Top_quark "Top quark") and [charm quark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charm_quark "Charm quark"), and predicted many of their properties before these particles were observed. The predictions were experimentally confirmed with good precision.[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-60)

The Standard Model also predicted the existence of the [Higgs boson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Higgs_boson "Higgs boson"), which was found in 2012 at the [Large Hadron Collider](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Large_Hadron_Collider "Large Hadron Collider"), the final fundamental particle predicted by the Standard Model to be experimentally confirmed.[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-61)

Unsolved problem in physics

*   What gives rise to the Standard Model of particle physics?
*   Why do particle masses and [coupling constants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coupling_constant "Coupling constant") have the values that we measure?
*   Why are there three [generations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generation_(particle_physics) "Generation (particle physics)") of particles?
*   Why is there more matter than [antimatter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antimatter "Antimatter") in the universe?
*   Where does [dark matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter "Dark matter") fit into the model? Does it even consist of one or more new particles?

Self-consistency of the Standard Model (currently formulated as a non-[abelian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abelian_group "Abelian group") gauge theory quantized through path-integrals) has not been mathematically proved. While regularized versions useful for approximate computations (for example [lattice gauge theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lattice_gauge_theory "Lattice gauge theory")) exist, it is not known whether they converge (in the sense of S-matrix elements) in the limit that the regulator is removed. A key question related to the consistency is the [Yang–Mills existence and mass gap](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yang%E2%80%93Mills_existence_and_mass_gap "Yang–Mills existence and mass gap") problem.

Experiments indicate that [neutrinos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutrinos "Neutrinos") have [mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass "Mass"), which the classic Standard Model did not allow.[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-62) To accommodate this finding, the classic Standard Model can be modified to include neutrino mass, although it is not obvious exactly how this should be done.

If one insists on using only Standard Model particles, this can be achieved by adding a non-renormalizable interaction of leptons with the Higgs boson.[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-63) On a fundamental level, such an interaction emerges in the [seesaw mechanism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seesaw_mechanism "Seesaw mechanism") where heavy right-handed neutrinos are added to the theory. This is natural in the [left-right symmetric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Left-right_symmetry "Left-right symmetry") extension of the Standard Model[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-Minkowski1977-64)[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-MohapatraSenjanovic1980-65) and in certain [grand unified theories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grand_unified_theory "Grand unified theory").[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-Gell-Mann1979-66) As long as new physics appears below or around 10 14[GeV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronvolt "Electronvolt"), the neutrino masses can be of the right order of magnitude.

Theoretical and experimental research has attempted to extend the Standard Model into a [unified field theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unified_field_theory "Unified field theory") or a [theory of everything](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theory_of_everything "Theory of everything"), a complete theory explaining all physical phenomena including constants. Inadequacies of the Standard Model that motivate such research include:

*   The model does not explain [gravitation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitation "Gravitation"), although physical confirmation of a theoretical particle known as a [graviton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graviton "Graviton") would account for it to a degree. Though it addresses strong and electroweak interactions, the Standard Model does not consistently explain the canonical theory of gravitation, [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity"), in terms of [quantum field theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_field_theory "Quantum field theory"). The reason for this is, among other things, that quantum field theories of gravity generally break down before reaching the [Planck scale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_scale "Planck scale"). As a consequence, we have no reliable theory for the very early universe.
*   Some physicists consider it to be _ad hoc_ and inelegant, requiring 19 numerical constants whose values are unrelated and arbitrary.[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-BlumhoferHutter1997-67) Although the Standard Model, as it now stands, can explain why neutrinos have masses, the specifics of neutrino mass are still unclear. It is believed that explaining neutrino mass will require an additional 7 or 8 constants, which are also arbitrary parameters.[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-68)
*   The Higgs mechanism gives rise to the [hierarchy problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hierarchy_problem "Hierarchy problem") if some new physics (coupled to the Higgs) is present at high energy scales. In these cases, in order for the weak scale to be much smaller than the [Planck scale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_scale "Planck scale"), severe fine tuning of the parameters is required; there are, however, other scenarios that include [quantum gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asymptotic_safety_in_quantum_gravity "Asymptotic safety in quantum gravity") in which such fine tuning can be avoided.[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-69) There are also issues of [quantum triviality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_triviality "Quantum triviality"), which suggests that it may not be possible to create a consistent quantum field theory involving elementary scalar particles.[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model#cite_note-TrivPurs-70)
*   The model is inconsistent with the emerging [Lambda-CDM model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lambda-CDM_model "Lambda-CDM model") of cosmology. Contentions include the absence of an explanation in the Standard Model of particle physics for the observed amount of [cold dark matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cold_dark_matter "Cold dark matter") (CDM) and its contributions to [dark energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_energy "Dark energy"), which are many orders of magnitude too large. It is also difficult to accommodate the observed predominance of matter over antimatter ([matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matter "Matter")/[antimatter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antimatter "Antimatter")[asymmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baryon_asymmetry "Baryon asymmetry")). The [isotropy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotropic "Isotropic") and [homogeneity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homogeneity_(physics) "Homogeneity (physics)") of the visible universe over large distances seems to require a mechanism like cosmic [inflation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inflation_(cosmology) "Inflation (cosmology)"), which would also constitute an extension of the Standard Model.

Currently, no proposed [theory of everything](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theory_of_everything "Theory of everything") has been widely accepted or verified.
