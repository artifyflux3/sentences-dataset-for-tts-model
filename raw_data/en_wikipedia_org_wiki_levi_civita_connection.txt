From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

In [Riemannian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_manifold "Riemannian manifold") or [pseudo-Riemannian geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudo-Riemannian_manifold "Pseudo-Riemannian manifold") (in particular the [Lorentzian geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentzian_manifold "Lorentzian manifold") of [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity")), the **Levi-Civita connection** is the unique [affine connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_connection "Affine connection") on the [tangent bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_bundle "Tangent bundle") of a [manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manifold "Manifold") that [preserves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_connection "Metric connection") the ([pseudo-](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudo-Riemannian_manifold "Pseudo-Riemannian manifold"))[Riemannian metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_metric "Riemannian metric") and is [torsion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torsion_(differential_geometry) "Torsion (differential geometry)")-free.

The [fundamental theorem of Riemannian geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_theorem_of_Riemannian_geometry "Fundamental theorem of Riemannian geometry") states that there is a unique connection that satisfies these properties.

In the theory of [Riemannian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_manifold "Riemannian manifold") and [pseudo-Riemannian manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudo-Riemannian_manifold "Pseudo-Riemannian manifold") the term [covariant derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covariant_derivative "Covariant derivative") is often used for the Levi-Civita connection. The components (structure coefficients) of this connection with respect to a system of local coordinates are called [Christoffel symbols](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christoffel_symbols "Christoffel symbols").

The Levi-Civita connection is named after [Tullio Levi-Civita](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tullio_Levi-Civita "Tullio Levi-Civita"), although originally "discovered" by [Elwin Bruno Christoffel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elwin_Bruno_Christoffel "Elwin Bruno Christoffel"). Levi-Civita,[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_connection#cite_note-Levi-Civita1917-1) along with [Gregorio Ricci-Curbastro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gregorio_Ricci-Curbastro "Gregorio Ricci-Curbastro"), used Christoffel's symbols[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_connection#cite_note-2) to define the notion of [parallel transport](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_transport "Parallel transport") and explore the relationship of parallel transport with the [curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_curvature_tensor "Riemann curvature tensor"), thus developing the modern notion of [holonomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holonomy "Holonomy").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_connection#cite_note-3)

In 1869, Christoffel discovered that the components of the intrinsic derivative of a vector field, upon changing the coordinate system, transform as the components of a contravariant vector. This discovery was the real beginning of tensor analysis.

In 1906, [L. E. J. Brouwer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L._E._J._Brouwer "L. E. J. Brouwer") was the first [mathematician](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematician "Mathematician") to consider the [parallel transport](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_transport "Parallel transport") of a [vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_vector "Euclidean vector") for the case of a space of [constant curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constant_curvature "Constant curvature").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_connection#cite_note-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_connection#cite_note-5)

In 1917, [Tullio Levi-Civita](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tullio_Levi-Civita "Tullio Levi-Civita") pointed out its importance for the case of a [hypersurface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypersurface "Hypersurface") immersed in a [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space"), i.e., for the case of a [Riemannian manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_manifold "Riemannian manifold") embedded in a "larger" ambient space.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_connection#cite_note-Levi-Civita1917-1) He interpreted the intrinsic derivative in the case of an embedded surface as the tangential component of the usual derivative in the ambient affine space. The Levi-Civita notions of [intrinsic derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intrinsic_derivative "Intrinsic derivative") and parallel displacement of a vector along a curve make sense on an abstract Riemannian manifold, even though the original motivation relied on a specific embedding ![Image 1: {\displaystyle M^{n}\subset \mathbf {R} ^{n(n+1)/2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/170ea057f2d37e0bc75d8d0d55599225e9c7e655)

In 1918, independently of Levi-Civita, [Jan Arnoldus Schouten](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jan_Arnoldus_Schouten "Jan Arnoldus Schouten") obtained analogous results.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_connection#cite_note-6) In the same year, [Hermann Weyl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Weyl "Hermann Weyl") generalized Levi-Civita's results.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_connection#cite_note-7)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_connection#cite_note-8)

*   (_M_, _g_) denotes a [pseudo-Riemannian manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudo-Riemannian_manifold "Pseudo-Riemannian manifold").
*   _TM_ is the [tangent bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_bundle "Tangent bundle") of _M_.
*   _g_ is the [pseudo-Riemannian metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudo-Riemannian_metric "Pseudo-Riemannian metric") of _M_.
*   _X_, _Y_, _Z_ are smooth vector fields on _M_, i. e. smooth [sections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Section_(fiber_bundle) "Section (fiber bundle)") of _TM_.
*   [_X_, _Y_] is the [Lie bracket](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_bracket_of_vector_fields "Lie bracket of vector fields") of _X_ and _Y_. It is again a smooth vector field.

The metric _g_ can take up to two vectors or vector fields _X_, _Y_ as arguments. In the former case the output is a number, the (pseudo-)[inner product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inner_product "Inner product") of _X_ and _Y_. In the latter case, the inner product of _X_ _p_, _Y_ _p_ is taken at all points _p_ on the manifold so that _g_(_X_, _Y_) defines a smooth function on _M_. Vector fields act (by definition) as differential operators on smooth functions. In local coordinates ![Image 2: {\displaystyle (x_{1},\ldots ,x_{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7935f7983d8a5ae59fea84efe65415235fa7c47b), the action reads

![Image 3: {\displaystyle X(f)=X^{i}{\frac {\partial }{\partial x^{i}}}f=X^{i}\partial _{i}f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/45de47b3c1d47624319f187638687c6b33df9d43)
where [Einstein's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Einstein "Albert Einstein")[summation convention](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_summation_convention "Einstein summation convention") is used.

An [affine connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_connection "Affine connection")![Image 4: {\displaystyle \nabla }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a3d0e93b78c50237f9ea83d027e4ebbdaef354b2) is called a Levi-Civita connection if

1.   _it preserves the metric_, i.e., ![Image 5: {\displaystyle \nabla g=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3df8f74c4a479667a19d8ac51c2c726766cf7b66).
2.   _it is [torsion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torsion\_of\_connection "Torsion of connection")-free_, i.e., for any vector fields ![Image 6: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) and ![Image 7: {\displaystyle Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/961d67d6b454b4df2301ac571808a3538b3a6d3f) we have ![Image 8: {\displaystyle \nabla _{X}Y-\nabla _{Y}X=[X,Y]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8c6e5703b32d7cbf681d8c5c7304eb681147024d), where ![Image 9: {\displaystyle [X,Y]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/94470b44d283fde62130212956058ca6b727da37) is the [Lie bracket](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_bracket_of_vector_fields "Lie bracket of vector fields") of the [vector fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_field "Vector field")![Image 10: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) and ![Image 11: {\displaystyle Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/961d67d6b454b4df2301ac571808a3538b3a6d3f).

Condition 1 above is sometimes referred to as [compatibility with the metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compatibility_with_the_metric "Compatibility with the metric"), and condition 2 is sometimes called symmetry, cf. Do Carmo's text.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_connection#cite_note-9)

Fundamental theorem of (pseudo-)Riemannian geometry
---------------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Levi-Civita_connection&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Fundamental theorem of (pseudo-)Riemannian geometry")]

**Theorem** Every pseudo-Riemannian manifold ![Image 12: {\displaystyle (M,g)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68e27d2e539fd0c3a9a7efab6257abd17de7fc57) has a unique Levi Civita connection ![Image 13: {\displaystyle \nabla }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a3d0e93b78c50237f9ea83d027e4ebbdaef354b2).

_Proof_:[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_connection#cite_note-10)[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_connection#cite_note-11) To prove uniqueness, unravel the definition of the action of a connection on tensors to find

![Image 14: {\displaystyle X{\bigl (}g(Y,Z){\bigr )}=(\nabla _{X}g)(Y,Z)+g(\nabla _{X}Y,Z)+g(Y,\nabla _{X}Z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/911f5a9a3b4fa0dfe16238ab3f84a3a01db24df3).
Hence one can write the condition that ![Image 15: {\displaystyle \nabla }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a3d0e93b78c50237f9ea83d027e4ebbdaef354b2) preserves the metric as

![Image 16: {\displaystyle X{\bigl (}g(Y,Z){\bigr )}=g(\nabla _{X}Y,Z)+g(Y,\nabla _{X}Z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d650fd7725582252867497d8c9b1b695ea313088).
By the symmetry of ![Image 17: {\displaystyle g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3556280e66fe2c0d0140df20935a6f057381d77),

![Image 18: {\displaystyle X{\bigl (}g(Y,Z){\bigr )}+Y{\bigl (}g(Z,X){\bigr )}-Z{\bigl (}g(Y,X){\bigr )}=g(\nabla _{X}Y+\nabla _{Y}X,Z)+g(\nabla _{X}Z-\nabla _{Z}X,Y)+g(\nabla _{Y}Z-\nabla _{Z}Y,X)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fed59893ceb695036cbf74d3fa0ba5c01cac903d).
By torsion-freeness, the right hand side is therefore equal to

![Image 19: {\displaystyle 2g(\nabla _{X}Y,Z)-g([X,Y],Z)+g([X,Z],Y)+g([Y,Z],X)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3f3a61ee78fda33dae9fa5064282d8a98ef1fe09).
Thus, the [Koszul](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean-Louis_Koszul "Jean-Louis Koszul") formula

![Image 20: {\displaystyle g(\nabla _{X}Y,Z)={\tfrac {1}{2}}{\Big \{}X{\bigl (}g(Y,Z){\bigr )}+Y{\bigl (}g(Z,X){\bigr )}-Z{\bigl (}g(X,Y){\bigr )}+g([X,Y],Z)-g([Y,Z],X)-g([X,Z],Y){\Big \}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b2f3e458da5800721c33fd9f1ee153fc6aebda10)
holds. Hence, if a Levi-Civita connection exists, it must be unique, because ![Image 21: {\displaystyle Z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1cc6b75e09a8aa3f04d8584b11db534f88fb56bd) is arbitrary, ![Image 22: {\displaystyle g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3556280e66fe2c0d0140df20935a6f057381d77) is non degenerate, and the right hand side does not depend on ![Image 23: {\displaystyle \nabla }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a3d0e93b78c50237f9ea83d027e4ebbdaef354b2).

To prove existence, note that for given vector field ![Image 24: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) and ![Image 25: {\displaystyle Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/961d67d6b454b4df2301ac571808a3538b3a6d3f), the right hand side of the Koszul expression is linear over smooth functions in the vector field ![Image 26: {\displaystyle Z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1cc6b75e09a8aa3f04d8584b11db534f88fb56bd), not just real-linear. Hence by the non degeneracy of ![Image 27: {\displaystyle g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3556280e66fe2c0d0140df20935a6f057381d77), the right hand side uniquely defines some new vector field, which is suggestively denoted ![Image 28: {\displaystyle \nabla _{X}Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/27a07889f225ba5b4eae1cd7c00283c33c2ff1a2) as in the left hand side. By substituting the Koszul formula, one now checks that for all vector fields ![Image 29: {\displaystyle X,Y,Z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bcf4a8b48db1a32d24aabe164b07744069093225) and all functions ![Image 30: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61),

![Image 31: {\displaystyle g(\nabla _{X}(Y_{1}+Y_{2}),Z)=g(\nabla _{X}Y_{1},Z)+g(\nabla _{X}Y_{2},Z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/158d082fa39cb81ccd49f2d1a28c6d7a9a725440)![Image 32: {\displaystyle g(\nabla _{X}(fY),Z)=X(f)g(Y,Z)+fg(\nabla _{X}Y,Z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c056d162bc56e2a652179f1115b211c8793707c2)![Image 33: {\displaystyle g(\nabla _{X}Y,Z)+g(\nabla _{X}Z,Y)=X{\bigl (}g(Y,Z){\bigr )}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/225b8786bd673c829d9e9d199e28f0181baeb0d4)![Image 34: {\displaystyle g(\nabla _{X}Y,Z)-g(\nabla _{Y}X,Z)=g([X,Y],Z).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/be13fa3e3a7e44da1a7cfab5bf1d6fc95eb8e43a)
Hence the Koszul expression does, in fact, define a connection, and this connection is compatible with the metric and is torsion free, i.e. is a Levi-Civita connection.

With minor variation, the same proof shows that there is a unique connection that is compatible with the metric and has prescribed torsion.

Christoffel symbols
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Levi-Civita_connection&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Christoffel symbols")]

Let ![Image 35: {\displaystyle \nabla }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a3d0e93b78c50237f9ea83d027e4ebbdaef354b2) be an affine connection on the tangent bundle. Choose local coordinates ![Image 36: {\displaystyle x^{1},\ldots ,x^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d7ba4766c77e384fcf3fa5a11063d993cd794355) with coordinate basis vector fields ![Image 37: {\displaystyle \partial _{1},\ldots ,\partial _{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/369f99744d794427b0f686443a2750d45484ad15) and write ![Image 38: {\displaystyle \nabla _{j}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a9e252d1dd7ab8513a5f83b0d03380d8ee63d64d) for ![Image 39: {\displaystyle \nabla _{\partial _{j}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/08196e5c9f831ac77f115e50ba7915c9c626b638). The [Christoffel symbols](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christoffel_symbols "Christoffel symbols")![Image 40: {\displaystyle \Gamma _{jk}^{l}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bb2154ebd776c31fa82dfe732eb8f6ae384ced93) of ![Image 41: {\displaystyle \nabla }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a3d0e93b78c50237f9ea83d027e4ebbdaef354b2) with respect to these coordinates are defined as

![Image 42: {\displaystyle \nabla _{j}\partial _{k}=\Gamma _{jk}^{l}\partial _{l}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/90c49cc9d05e3f4ab03066e64a06e82f60a897e8)
The Christoffel symbols conversely define the connection ![Image 43: {\displaystyle \nabla }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a3d0e93b78c50237f9ea83d027e4ebbdaef354b2) on the coordinate neighbourhood because

![Image 44: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\nabla _{X}Y&=\nabla _{X^{j}\partial _{j}}(Y^{k}\partial _{k})\\&=X^{j}\nabla _{j}(Y^{k}\partial _{k})\\&=X^{j}{\bigl (}\partial _{j}(Y^{k})\partial _{k}+Y^{k}\nabla _{j}\partial _{k}{\bigr )}\\&=X^{j}{\bigl (}\partial _{j}(Y^{k})\partial _{k}+Y^{k}\Gamma _{jk}^{l}\partial _{l}{\bigr )}\\&=X^{j}{\bigl (}\partial _{j}(Y^{l})+Y^{k}\Gamma _{jk}^{l}{\bigr )}\partial _{l}\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/814512ee27f768b879bfecec6af40c466c78cea9)
that is,

![Image 45: {\displaystyle (\nabla _{j}Y)^{l}=\partial _{j}Y^{l}+\Gamma _{jk}^{l}Y^{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3937617ec9dcbe59562e58480f45afb0e2677b7e)
An affine connection ![Image 46: {\displaystyle \nabla }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a3d0e93b78c50237f9ea83d027e4ebbdaef354b2) is compatible with a metric iff

![Image 47: {\displaystyle \partial _{i}{\bigl (}g(\partial _{j},\partial _{k}){\bigr )}=g(\nabla _{i}\partial _{j},\partial _{k})+g(\partial _{j},\nabla _{i}\partial _{k})=g(\Gamma _{ij}^{l}\partial _{l},\partial _{k})+g(\partial _{j},\Gamma _{ik}^{l}\partial _{l})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0be89a65c7897dd8653929ed88a40fa24d6353f5)
i.e., if and only if

![Image 48: {\displaystyle \partial _{i}g_{jk}=\Gamma _{ij}^{l}g_{lk}+\Gamma _{ik}^{l}g_{jl}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/237fa59904d0e7b94dd06d5c2e1befb5de8e1d3d)
An affine connection ∇ is torsion free iff

![Image 49: {\displaystyle \nabla _{j}\partial _{k}-\nabla _{k}\partial _{j}=(\Gamma _{jk}^{l}-\Gamma _{kj}^{l})\partial _{l}=[\partial _{j},\partial _{k}]=0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5cc38e27961b55dc1833512cd7aab82913446d4)
i.e., if and only if

![Image 50: {\displaystyle \Gamma _{jk}^{l}=\Gamma _{kj}^{l}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a0640c41177591edef9189c26f31a05fabdbc27a)
is symmetric in its lower two indices.

As one checks by taking for ![Image 51: {\displaystyle X,Y,Z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bcf4a8b48db1a32d24aabe164b07744069093225), coordinate vector fields ![Image 52: {\displaystyle \partial _{j},\partial _{k},\partial _{l}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/294e151b4d1c254a3744dc12ed912e0ab380d050) (or computes directly), the Koszul expression of the Levi-Civita connection derived above is equivalent to a definition of the Christoffel symbols in terms of the metric as

![Image 53: {\displaystyle \Gamma _{jk}^{l}={\tfrac {1}{2}}g^{lr}\left(\partial _{k}g_{rj}+\partial _{j}g_{rk}-\partial _{r}g_{jk}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fc0d094e98c8dfb0d38d28aecd77f12d4159bcc7)
where as usual ![Image 54: {\displaystyle g^{ij}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b14a4aa3b277a89268fd9026b8f16a749199cb10) are the coefficients of the dual metric tensor, i.e. the entries of the inverse of the matrix ![Image 55: {\displaystyle g_{kl}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/91faf4f3be32fcf819a519779d71b27ce87ae7a8).

Derivative along curve
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Levi-Civita_connection&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Derivative along curve")]

The Levi-Civita connection (like any affine connection) also defines a derivative along [curves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curve "Curve"), sometimes denoted by _D_.

Given a smooth curve _γ_ on (_M_, _g_) and a [vector field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_field "Vector field")_V_ along _γ_ its derivative is defined by

![Image 56: {\displaystyle D_{t}V=\nabla _{{\dot {\gamma }}(t)}V.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/92ef6a2bd64813fcb21e2e50d6e17e3428b24f2c)
Formally, _D_ is the [pullback connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pullback_(differential_geometry) "Pullback (differential geometry)")_γ_*∇ on the [pullback bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pullback_bundle "Pullback bundle")_γ_*_TM_.

In particular, ![Image 57: {\displaystyle {\dot {\gamma }}(t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9dc803b9b734185548ca3c18bb50e78bc44c1b35) is a vector field along the curve _γ_ itself. If ![Image 58: {\displaystyle \nabla _{{\dot {\gamma }}(t)}{\dot {\gamma }}(t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c8ffd33a5ccb2dbc006858fb4aa0249531392466) vanishes, the curve is called a geodesic of the covariant derivative. Formally, the condition can be restated as the vanishing of the pullback connection applied to ![Image 59: {\displaystyle {\dot {\gamma }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c250a2cd0b68db26e9663686345f6cee8074d5f0):

![Image 60: {\displaystyle \left(\gamma ^{*}\nabla \right){\dot {\gamma }}\equiv 0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3776b0905562b1faa9198015cf81e08b36fed2d8)
If the covariant derivative is the Levi-Civita connection of a certain metric, then the geodesics for the connection are precisely those [geodesics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesics "Geodesics") of the [metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_tensor "Metric tensor") that are parametrised proportionally to their arc length.

In general, [parallel transport](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_transport "Parallel transport") along a curve with respect to a connection defines [isomorphisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isomorphism "Isomorphism") between the tangent spaces at the points of the curve. If the connection is a Levi-Civita connection, then these isomorphisms are [orthogonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_group "Orthogonal group") – that is, they preserve the inner products on the various tangent spaces.

The images below show parallel transport induced by the Levi-Civita connection associated to two different Riemannian metrics on the [punctured plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Punctured_plane "Punctured plane")![Image 61: {\displaystyle \mathbf {R} ^{2}\backslash \{0,0\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/05c7ffbcd631a5e420f746d5733b4eb67ddc11d1). The curve the parallel transport is done along is the unit circle. Example: the unit sphere in R 3
-------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Levi-Civita_connection&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Example: the unit sphere in R3")]

Let ⟨ , ⟩ be the usual [scalar product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_product "Scalar product") on **R**3. Let **S**2 be the [unit sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_sphere "Unit sphere") in **R**3. The tangent space to **S**2 at a point _m_ is naturally identified with the vector subspace of **R**3 consisting of all vectors orthogonal to _m_. It follows that a vector field _Y_ on **S**2 can be seen as a map _Y_: **S**2 → **R**3, which satisfies ![Image 71: {\bigl \langle }Y(m),m{\bigr \rangle }=0,\qquad \forall m\in \mathbf {S} ^{2}.](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/74fc1f68249c760b66f0930b8e523d03c49f2bfb)

Denote as _d m Y_ the differential of the map _Y_ at the point _m_. Then we have:

**Lemma**—The formula ![Image 72: {\displaystyle \left(\nabla _{X}Y\right)(m)=d_{m}Y(X(m))+\langle X(m),Y(m)\rangle m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81c6314e9e73db30011b7d9b22e7e56dc3fbd8e8) defines an affine connection on **S**2 with vanishing torsion.

**Proof**

It is straightforward to prove that ∇ satisfies the Leibniz identity and is _C_∞(**S**2) linear in the first variable. It is also a straightforward computation to show that this connection is torsion free. So all that needs to be proved here is that the formula above produces a vector field tangent to **S**2. That is, we need to prove that for all _m_ in **S**2![Image 73: {\displaystyle {\bigl \langle }\left(\nabla _{X}Y\right)(m),m{\bigr \rangle }=0\qquad (1).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7c4b4d8d1cae980ae11f587b81fd32a5593d5dfa) Consider the map _f_ that sends every _m_ in **S**2 to ⟨_Y_(_m_), _m_⟩, which is always 0. The map _f_ is constant, hence its differential vanishes. In particular ![Image 74: {\displaystyle d_{m}f(X)={\bigl \langle }d_{m}Y(X),m{\bigr \rangle }+{\bigl \langle }Y(m),X(m){\bigr \rangle }=0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6733babd854360f9eb6e84eb6518551ba9e3673f) The equation (1) above follows. [Q.E.D.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Q.E.D. "Q.E.D.")

In fact, this connection is the Levi-Civita connection for the metric on **S**2 inherited from **R**3. Indeed, one can check that this connection preserves the metric.

Behaviour under conformal rescaling
-----------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Levi-Civita_connection&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Behaviour under conformal rescaling")]

If the metric ![Image 75: {\displaystyle g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3556280e66fe2c0d0140df20935a6f057381d77) in a [conformal class](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conformal_geometry "Conformal geometry") is replaced by the conformally rescaled metric of the same class ![Image 76: {\displaystyle {\hat {g}}=e^{2\gamma }g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dd88bf55e744f198596026470a32771686889744), then the Levi-Civita connection transforms according to the rule[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_connection#cite_note-12)![Image 77: {\displaystyle {\widehat {\nabla }}_{X}Y=\nabla _{X}Y+X(\gamma )Y+Y(\gamma )X-g(X,Y)\mathrm {grad} _{g}(\gamma ).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6f43ccc72cb4395230272d1e6a35481309ab2035) where ![Image 78: {\displaystyle \mathrm {grad} _{g}(\gamma )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3f25d589d29d8565404e65e8577424f75516e7f9) is the gradient vector field of ![Image 79: {\displaystyle \gamma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a223c880b0ce3da8f64ee33c4f0010beee400b1a) i.e. the vector field ![Image 80: {\displaystyle g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3556280e66fe2c0d0140df20935a6f057381d77)-dual to ![Image 81: {\displaystyle d\gamma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/30ebc8770ffa7125766625968871f6371aabc77b), in local coordinates given by ![Image 82: {\displaystyle g^{ik}(\partial _{i}\gamma )\partial _{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b7d0ae3f08dd08b37146ae6ae71835321105b5a4). Indeed, it is trivial to verify that ![Image 83: {\displaystyle {\widehat {\nabla }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b2c96a8b66e596d9ed8e3eef400b1000034cfd14) is torsion-free. To verify metricity, assume that ![Image 84: {\displaystyle g(Y,Y)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/df38041498c4de9c4f08d33d316b6bd7420d000a) is constant. In that case, ![Image 85: {\displaystyle {\hat {g}}({\widehat {\nabla }}_{X}Y,Y)=X(\gamma ){\hat {g}}(Y,Y)={\frac {1}{2}}X({\hat {g}}(Y,Y)).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ab315c9c04911785dfd946d84765d130a09db516)

As an application, consider again the unit sphere, but this time under [stereographic projection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stereographic_projection "Stereographic projection"), so that the metric (in complex [Fubini–Study coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fubini%E2%80%93Study_metric "Fubini–Study metric")![Image 86: {\displaystyle z,{\bar {z}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/44e11845770b7dd62ec0028d9d4cb0bcea1add27)) is: ![Image 87: {\displaystyle g={\frac {4\,dz\,d{\bar {z}}}{(1+z{\bar {z}})^{2}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ed3df41f36fb036a955a335490189ab31cc7b455) This exhibits the metric of the sphere as conformally flat, with the Euclidean metric ![Image 88: {\displaystyle dz\,d{\bar {z}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/739d06dcf921c7f97b1993757907ca708407cbf9), with ![Image 89: {\displaystyle \gamma =\ln(2)-\ln(1+z{\bar {z}})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c5f451302fafaa1274dbf30aa2e8d5359f32453d). We have ![Image 90: {\displaystyle d\gamma =-(1+z{\bar {z}})^{-1}({\bar {z}}\,dz+z\,d{\bar {z}})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d6cb488fd39223517d63f38279a9a63922b0db17), and so ![Image 91: {\displaystyle {\widehat {\nabla }}_{\partial _{z}}\partial _{z}=-{\frac {2{\bar {z}}\partial _{z}}{1+z{\bar {z}}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9d7cd7051b2e8b7cd8e41b0f6ea39a23161ef842) With the Euclidean gradient ![Image 92: {\displaystyle \mathrm {grad} _{Euc}(\gamma )=-(1+z{\bar {z}})^{-1}({\bar {z}}\partial _{z}+z\partial _{\bar {z}})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8dc654313638cc3dc8b55effb660d195965cda0b), we have ![Image 93: {\displaystyle {\widehat {\nabla }}_{\partial _{z}}\partial _{\bar {z}}=0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cf61a52a6f674c82ed56226df1732670490db060) These relations, together with their complex conjugates, define the Christoffel symbols for the two-sphere.
