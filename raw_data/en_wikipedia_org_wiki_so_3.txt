Title: 3D rotation group

URL Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SO(3)

Published Time: 2003-01-24T21:03:22Z

Markdown Content:
In [mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_mechanics "Classical mechanics") and [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry"), the **3D rotation group**, often denoted **[SO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_orthogonal_group "Special orthogonal group")(3)**, is the [group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_(mathematics) "Group (mathematics)") of all [rotations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation "Rotation") about the [origin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Origin_(mathematics) "Origin (mathematics)") of [three-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_space "Three-dimensional space")[Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space")![Image 1: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5) under the operation of [composition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_composition "Function composition").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SO(3)#cite_note-1)

By definition, a rotation about the origin is a transformation that preserves the origin, [Euclidean distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_distance "Euclidean distance") (so it is an [isometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isometry "Isometry")), and [orientation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orientation_(vector_space) "Orientation (vector space)") (i.e., _handedness_ of space). Composing two rotations results in another rotation, every rotation has a unique [inverse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverse_function "Inverse function") rotation, and the [identity map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Identity_map "Identity map") satisfies the definition of a rotation. Owing to the above properties (along composite rotations' [associative property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Associative_property "Associative property")), the set of all rotations is a [group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_(mathematics) "Group (mathematics)") under composition.

Every non-trivial rotation is determined by its axis of rotation (a line through the origin) and its angle of rotation. Rotations are not commutative (for example, rotating _R_ 90Â° in the x-y plane followed by _S_ 90Â° in the y-z plane is not the same as _S_ followed by _R_), making the 3D rotation group a [nonabelian group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-abelian_group "Non-abelian group"). Moreover, the rotation group has a natural structure as a [manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manifold "Manifold") for which the group operations are [smoothly differentiable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smooth_function "Smooth function"), so it is in fact a [Lie group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_group "Lie group"). It is [compact](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compact_space "Compact space") and has dimension 3.

Rotations are [linear transformations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_transformation "Linear transformation") of ![Image 2: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5) and can therefore be represented by [matrices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_(mathematics) "Matrix (mathematics)") once a [basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basis_of_a_vector_space "Basis of a vector space") of ![Image 3: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5) has been chosen. Specifically, if we choose an [orthonormal basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthonormal_basis "Orthonormal basis") of ![Image 4: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5), every rotation is described by an [orthogonal 3 Ã— 3 matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_matrix "Orthogonal matrix") (i.e., a 3 Ã— 3 matrix with real entries which, when multiplied by its [transpose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transpose "Transpose"), results in the [identity matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Identity_matrix "Identity matrix")) with [determinant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Determinant "Determinant") 1. The group SO(3) can therefore be identified with the group of these matrices under [matrix multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_multiplication "Matrix multiplication"). These matrices are known as "special orthogonal matrices", explaining the notation SO(3).

The group SO(3) is used to describe the possible rotational symmetries of an object, as well as the possible orientations of an object in space. Its [representations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_representation "Group representation") are important in physics, where they give rise to the [elementary particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementary_particle "Elementary particle") of integer [spin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_(physics) "Spin (physics)").

Besides just preserving length, rotations also preserve the [angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle "Angle") between vectors. This follows from the fact that the standard [dot product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dot_product "Dot product") between two vectors **u** and **v** can be written purely in terms of length (see the [law of cosines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law_of_cosines "Law of cosines")): ![Image 5: {\displaystyle \mathbf {u} \cdot \mathbf {v} ={\frac {1}{2}}\left(\|\mathbf {u} +\mathbf {v} \|^{2}-\|\mathbf {u} \|^{2}-\|\mathbf {v} \|^{2}\right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2be32bc3925318102e0a1d5f45a696051b5198d2)

It follows that every length-preserving linear transformation in ![Image 6: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5) preserves the dot product, and thus the angle between vectors. Rotations are often defined as linear transformations that preserve the inner product on ![Image 7: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5), which is equivalent to requiring them to preserve length. See [classical group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_group "Classical group") for a treatment of this more general approach, where SO(3) appears as a special case.

Orthogonal and rotation matrices
--------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=3D_rotation_group&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Orthogonal and rotation matrices")]

Every rotation maps an [orthonormal basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthonormal_basis "Orthonormal basis") of ![Image 8: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5) to another orthonormal basis. Like any linear transformation of [finite-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite-dimensional "Finite-dimensional") vector spaces, a rotation can always be represented by a [matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_(mathematics) "Matrix (mathematics)"). Let _R_ be a given rotation. With respect to the [standard basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_basis "Standard basis")**e**1, **e**2, **e**3 of ![Image 9: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5) the columns of _R_ are given by (_R_**e**1, _R_**e**2, _R_**e**3). Since the standard basis is orthonormal, and since _R_ preserves angles and length, the columns of _R_ form another orthonormal basis. This [orthonormality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthonormality "Orthonormality") condition can be expressed in the form

![Image 10: {\displaystyle R^{\mathsf {T}}R=RR^{\mathsf {T}}=I,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4f00b1d1fa4a159fc813f017112c4b3bc45b7635)
where _R_ T denotes the [transpose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transpose "Transpose") of _R_ and I is the 3 Ã— 3[identity matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Identity_matrix "Identity matrix"). Matrices for which this property holds are called [orthogonal matrices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_matrix "Orthogonal matrix"). The group of all 3 Ã— 3 orthogonal matrices is denoted O(3), and consists of all proper and improper rotations.

In addition to preserving length, proper rotations must also preserve orientation. A matrix will preserve or reverse orientation according to whether the [determinant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Determinant "Determinant") of the matrix is positive or negative. For an orthogonal matrix _R_, note that det _R_ T = det _R_ implies (det _R_)2 = 1, so that det _R_ = Â±1. The [subgroup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subgroup "Subgroup") of orthogonal matrices with determinant +1 is called the _special [orthogonal group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal\_group "Orthogonal group")_, denoted SO(3).

Thus every rotation can be represented uniquely by an orthogonal matrix with unit determinant. Moreover, since composition of rotations corresponds to [matrix multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_multiplication "Matrix multiplication"), the rotation group is [isomorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isomorphic "Isomorphic") to the special orthogonal group SO(3).

[Improper rotations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Improper_rotation "Improper rotation") correspond to orthogonal matrices with determinant âˆ’1, and they do not form a group because the product of two improper rotations is a proper rotation.

The rotation group is a [group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_(mathematics) "Group (mathematics)") under [function composition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_composition "Function composition") (or equivalently the [product of linear transformations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_product "Matrix product")). It is a [subgroup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subgroup "Subgroup") of the [general linear group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_linear_group "General linear group") consisting of all [invertible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invertible_matrix "Invertible matrix") linear transformations of the [real 3-space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_coordinate_space "Real coordinate space")![Image 11: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5).[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SO(3)#cite_note-2)

Furthermore, the rotation group is [nonabelian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nonabelian_group "Nonabelian group"). That is, the order in which rotations are composed makes a difference. For example, a quarter turn around the positive _x_-axis followed by a quarter turn around the positive _y_-axis is a different rotation than the one obtained by first rotating around _y_ and then _x_.

The orthogonal group, consisting of all proper and improper rotations, is generated by reflections. Every proper rotation is the composition of two reflections, a special case of the [Cartanâ€“DieudonnÃ© theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartan%E2%80%93Dieudonn%C3%A9_theorem "Cartanâ€“DieudonnÃ© theorem").

### Complete classification of finite subgroups

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=3D_rotation_group&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Complete classification of finite subgroups")]

The finite subgroups of ![Image 12: {\displaystyle \mathrm {SO} (3)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8366fc6e92660ba077b87b745b305a4176b1d1ef) are completely [classified](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classification_theorem "Classification theorem").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SO(3)#cite_note-coxeter-3)

Every finite subgroup is isomorphic to either an element of one of two [countably infinite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Countably_infinite "Countably infinite") families of planar isometries: the [cyclic groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclic_group "Cyclic group")![Image 13: {\displaystyle C_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0301812adb392070d834ca2df4ed97f1cf132f33) or the [dihedral groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihedral_group "Dihedral group")![Image 14: {\displaystyle D_{2n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/317005e1541e49becbb38da665de9d667b8863b2), or to one of three other groups: the [tetrahedral group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedral_group "Tetrahedral group")![Image 15: {\displaystyle \cong A_{4}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b9c787947b23c62f7360e3fdd060af896e32c6bc), the [octahedral group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octahedral_group "Octahedral group")![Image 16: {\displaystyle \cong S_{4}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a9037c415dceccb174f6bd8e2cb6d777053b5122), or the [icosahedral group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Icosahedral_group "Icosahedral group")![Image 17: {\displaystyle \cong A_{5}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3dea51f976c1fc6e55ca425cbf639c25f1530a1f).

Every nontrivial proper rotation in 3 dimensions fixes a unique 1-dimensional [linear subspace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_subspace "Linear subspace") of ![Image 18: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5) which is called the _axis of rotation_ (this is [Euler's rotation theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler%27s_rotation_theorem "Euler's rotation theorem")). Each such rotation acts as an ordinary 2-dimensional rotation in the plane [orthogonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal "Orthogonal") to this axis. Since every 2-dimensional rotation can be represented by an angle _Ï†_, an arbitrary 3-dimensional rotation can be specified by an axis of rotation together with an [angle of rotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle_of_rotation "Angle of rotation") about this axis. (Technically, one needs to specify an orientation for the axis and whether the rotation is taken to be [clockwise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clockwise_and_counterclockwise "Clockwise and counterclockwise") or [counterclockwise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Counterclockwise "Counterclockwise") with respect to this orientation).

For example, counterclockwise rotation about the positive _z_-axis by angle _Ï†_ is given by

![Image 19: {\displaystyle R_{z}(\phi )={\begin{bmatrix}\cos \phi &-\sin \phi &0\\\sin \phi &\cos \phi &0\\0&0&1\end{bmatrix}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f62c196069d2c1a2ce655d79fa0e48e7fc977255)
Given a [unit vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_vector "Unit vector")**n** in ![Image 20: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5) and an angle _Ï†_, let _R_(_Ï†_, **n**) represent a counterclockwise rotation about the axis through **n** (with orientation determined by **n**). Then

*   _R_(0, **n**) is the identity transformation for any **n**
*   _R_(_Ï†_, **n**) = _R_(âˆ’_Ï†_, âˆ’**n**)
*   _R_(Ï€ + _Ï†_, **n**) = _R_(Ï€ âˆ’ _Ï†_, âˆ’**n**).

Using these properties one can show that any rotation can be represented by a unique angle _Ï†_ in the range 0 â‰¤ Ï† â‰¤ Ï€ and a unit vector **n** such that

*   **n** is arbitrary if _Ï†_ = 0
*   **n** is unique if 0 <_Ï†_<Ï€
*   **n** is unique up to a [sign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sign_(mathematics) "Sign (mathematics)") if _Ï†_ = Ï€ (that is, the rotations _R_(Ï€, Â±**n**) are identical).

In the next section, this representation of rotations is used to identify SO(3) topologically with three-dimensional real projective space.

The Lie group SO(3) is [diffeomorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffeomorphism "Diffeomorphism") to the [real projective space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_projective_space "Real projective space")![Image 21: {\displaystyle \mathbb {P} ^{3}(\mathbb {R} ).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8f5ea9bdc7df9ead8e852c208b16ebdbb1a8f5ae)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SO(3)#cite_note-4)

Consider the solid ball in ![Image 22: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5) of radius Ï€ (that is, all points of ![Image 23: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5) of distance Ï€ or less from the origin). Given the above, for every point in this ball there is a rotation, with axis through the point and the origin, and rotation angle equal to the distance of the point from the origin. The identity rotation corresponds to the point at the center of the ball. Rotations through an angle ðœƒ between 0 and Ï€ (not including either) are on the same axis at the same distance. Rotation through angles between 0 and âˆ’Ï€ correspond to the point on the same axis and distance from the origin but on the opposite side of the origin. The one remaining issue is that the two rotations through Ï€ and through âˆ’Ï€ are the same. So we [identify](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quotient_space_(topology) "Quotient space (topology)") (or "glue together") [antipodal points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antipodal_point "Antipodal point") on the surface of the ball. After this identification, we arrive at a [topological space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_space "Topological space")[homeomorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homeomorphic "Homeomorphic") to the rotation group.

Indeed, the ball with antipodal surface points identified is a [smooth manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smooth_manifold "Smooth manifold"), and this manifold is [diffeomorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffeomorphism "Diffeomorphism") to the rotation group. It is also diffeomorphic to the [real 3-dimensional projective space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_projective_space "Real projective space")![Image 24: {\displaystyle \mathbb {P} ^{3}(\mathbb {R} ),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eacc73ad667eb9da5d2c9532fc4287673081c21a) so the latter can also serve as a topological model for the rotation group.

These identifications illustrate that SO(3) is [connected](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connected_space "Connected space") but not [simply connected](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simply_connected "Simply connected"). As to the latter, in the ball with antipodal surface points identified, consider the path running from the "north pole" straight through the interior down to the south pole. This is a closed loop, since the north pole and the south pole are identified. This loop cannot be shrunk to a point, since no matter how it is deformed, the start and end point have to remain antipodal, or else the loop will "break open". In terms of rotations, this loop represents a continuous sequence of rotations about the _z_-axis starting (by example) at the identity (center of the ball), through the south pole, jumping to the north pole and ending again at the identity rotation (i.e., a series of rotation through an angle _Ï†_ where _Ï†_ runs from 0 to [2 Ï€](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turn_(geometry) "Turn (geometry)")).

Surprisingly, running through the path twice, i.e., running from the north pole down to the south pole, jumping back to the north pole (using the fact that north and south poles are identified), and then again running from the north pole down to the south pole, so that _Ï†_ runs from 0 to 4 Ï€, gives a closed loop which _can_ be shrunk to a single point: first move the paths continuously to the ball's surface, still connecting north pole to south pole twice. The second path can then be mirrored over to the antipodal side without changing the path at all. Now we have an ordinary closed loop on the surface of the ball, connecting the north pole to itself along a great circle. This circle can be shrunk to the north pole without problems. The [plate trick](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plate_trick "Plate trick") and similar tricks demonstrate this practically.

The same argument can be performed in general, and it shows that the [fundamental group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_group "Fundamental group") of SO(3) is the [cyclic group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclic_group "Cyclic group") of order 2 (a fundamental group with two elements). In [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics") applications, the non-triviality (more than one element) of the fundamental group allows for the existence of objects known as [spinors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spinor "Spinor"), and is an important tool in the development of the [spinâ€“statistics theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin%E2%80%93statistics_theorem "Spinâ€“statistics theorem").

The [universal cover](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universal_cover "Universal cover") of SO(3) is a [Lie group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_group "Lie group") called [Spin(3)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin(3) "Spin(3)"). The group Spin(3) is isomorphic to the [special unitary group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_unitary_group "Special unitary group") SU(2); it is also diffeomorphic to the unit [3-sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3-sphere "3-sphere")_S_ 3 and can be understood as the group of [versors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Versor "Versor") ([quaternions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quaternion "Quaternion") with [absolute value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_value "Absolute value") 1). The connection between quaternions and rotations, commonly exploited in [computer graphics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_graphics "Computer graphics"), is explained in [quaternions and spatial rotations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quaternions_and_spatial_rotation "Quaternions and spatial rotation"). The map from _S_ 3 onto SO(3) that identifies antipodal points of _S_ 3 is a [surjective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surjective "Surjective")[homomorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homomorphism "Homomorphism") of Lie groups, with [kernel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kernel_(algebra) "Kernel (algebra)") {Â±1}. Topologically, this map is a two-to-one [covering map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covering_map "Covering map"). (See the [plate trick](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plate_trick "Plate trick").)

Connection between SO(3) and SU(2)
----------------------------------

### A note on Lie algebras

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=3D_rotation_group&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: A note on Lie algebras")]

In [Lie algebra representations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_algebra_representation "Lie algebra representation"), the group SO(3) is compact and simple of rank 1, and so it has a single independent [Casimir element](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Casimir_element "Casimir element"), a quadratic invariant function of the three generators which commutes with all of them. The Killing form for the rotation group is just the [Kronecker delta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kronecker_delta "Kronecker delta"), and so this Casimir invariant is simply the sum of the squares of the generators, ![Image 99: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {J}}_{x},{\boldsymbol {J}}_{y},{\boldsymbol {J}}_{z},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a70f7cc60947545d505aa58dc0d48603d5240abc) of the algebra

![Image 100: {\displaystyle [{\boldsymbol {J}}_{x},{\boldsymbol {J}}_{y}]={\boldsymbol {J}}_{z},\quad [{\boldsymbol {J}}_{z},{\boldsymbol {J}}_{x}]={\boldsymbol {J}}_{y},\quad [{\boldsymbol {J}}_{y},{\boldsymbol {J}}_{z}]={\boldsymbol {J}}_{x}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6eb35b6ad6e2c39742ca6423fac8bf75ad4a2c9b)
That is, the Casimir invariant is given by

![Image 101: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {J}}^{2}\equiv {\boldsymbol {J}}\cdot {\boldsymbol {J}}={\boldsymbol {J}}_{x}^{2}+{\boldsymbol {J}}_{y}^{2}+{\boldsymbol {J}}_{z}^{2}\propto {\boldsymbol {I}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dec64044c017e6a267c63737f0bc1d4a38042dc9)
For unitary irreducible [representations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_algebra_representation "Lie algebra representation")D j, the eigenvalues of this invariant are real and discrete, and characterize each representation, which is finite dimensional, of dimensionality ![Image 102: {\displaystyle 2j+1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a0a258cc33d54429124aedc154d9b3a968c4d99b). That is, the eigenvalues of this Casimir operator are

![Image 103: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {J}}^{2}=-j(j+1){\boldsymbol {I}}_{2j+1},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/955a6240a4be70673e13d7b7492d72ea95df4fb8)
where j is integer or half-integer, and referred to as the [spin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_(physics) "Spin (physics)") or [angular momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum "Angular momentum").

So, the 3 Ã— 3 generators _**L**_ displayed above act on the triplet (spin 1) representation, while the 2 Ã— 2 generators below, _**t**_, act on the [doublet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spinor "Spinor") ([spin-1/2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin-1/2 "Spin-1/2")) representation. That is, the resulting generators for higher spin systems in three spatial dimensions, for arbitrarily large j, can be calculated using these [spin operators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_operator "Spin operator") and [ladder operators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ladder_operator "Ladder operator").

-----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=3D_rotation_group&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: Uniform random sampling")]

![Image 139: {\displaystyle SO(3)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/16c677fee782e584fd417726201ce27c567f1e11) is doubly covered by the group of unit quaternions, which is isomorphic to the 3-sphere. Since the [Haar measure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haar_measure "Haar measure") on the unit quaternions is just the 3-area measure in 4 dimensions, the Haar measure on ![Image 140: {\displaystyle SO(3)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/16c677fee782e584fd417726201ce27c567f1e11) is just the pushforward of the 3-area measure.

Consequently, generating a uniformly random rotation in ![Image 141: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5) is equivalent to generating a uniformly random point on the 3-sphere. This can be accomplished by the following![Image 142: {\displaystyle ({\sqrt {1-u_{1}}}\sin(2\pi u_{2}),{\sqrt {1-u_{1}}}\cos(2\pi u_{2}),{\sqrt {u_{1}}}\sin(2\pi u_{3}),{\sqrt {u_{1}}}\cos(2\pi u_{3}))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/05e19e1ebfc8ab0113fa2904819021088392909c)

where ![Image 143: {\displaystyle u_{1},u_{2},u_{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4f5a52b84973e274c62eef0f3afce7c377f11d2a) are uniformly random samples of ![Image 144: {\displaystyle [0,1]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/738f7d23bb2d9642bab520020873cccbef49768d).[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SO(3)#cite_note-17)

Bakerâ€“Campbellâ€“Hausdorff formula
--------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=3D_rotation_group&action=edit&section=16 "Edit section: Bakerâ€“Campbellâ€“Hausdorff formula")]

Suppose X and Y in the Lie algebra are given. Their exponentials, exp(_X_) and exp(_Y_), are rotation matrices, which can be multiplied. Since the exponential map is a surjection, for some Z in the Lie algebra, exp(_Z_) = exp(_X_) exp(_Y_), and one may tentatively write

![Image 145: {\displaystyle Z=C(X,Y),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a2ba1bf19a610b8c4f4b7e7e82439b2d0219b76f)
for C some expression in _X_ and _Y_. When exp(_X_) and exp(_Y_) commute, then _Z_ = _X_ + _Y_, mimicking the behavior of complex exponentiation.

The general case is given by the more elaborate [BCH formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BCH_formula "BCH formula"), a series expansion of nested Lie brackets.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SO(3)#cite_note-18) For matrices, the Lie bracket is the same operation as the [commutator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutator "Commutator"), which monitors lack of commutativity in multiplication. This general expansion unfolds as follows,[[nb 4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SO(3)#cite_note-19)

![Image 146: {\displaystyle Z=C(X,Y)=X+Y+{\frac {1}{2}}[X,Y]+{\tfrac {1}{12}}[X,[X,Y]]-{\frac {1}{12}}[Y,[X,Y]]+\cdots .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/deb50254cdee1dcfdbb89e3c9994d201051418bd)
The infinite expansion in the BCH formula for SO(3) reduces to a compact form,

![Image 147: {\displaystyle Z=\alpha X+\beta Y+\gamma [X,Y],}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1cdd2c4da063f9ec30359beaacd3767b962f3875)
for suitable trigonometric function coefficients (_Î±_, _Î²_, _Î³_).

It is worthwhile to write this composite rotation generator as

![Image 148: {\displaystyle \alpha X+\beta Y+\gamma [X,Y]{\underset {{\mathfrak {so}}(3)}{=}}X+Y+{\frac {1}{2}}[X,Y]+{\frac {1}{12}}[X,[X,Y]]-{\frac {1}{12}}[Y,[X,Y]]+\cdots ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/11e64023ced100d20590d64041bc325a08fba789)
to emphasize that this is a _Lie algebra identity_.

The above identity holds for all [faithful representations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Faithful_representation "Faithful representation") of ð–˜ð–”(3). The [kernel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kernel_(algebra) "Kernel (algebra)") of a Lie algebra homomorphism is an [ideal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ideal_(Lie_algebra) "Ideal (Lie algebra)"), but ð–˜ð–”(3), being [simple](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simple_(abstract_algebra) "Simple (abstract algebra)"), has no nontrivial ideals and all nontrivial representations are hence faithful. It holds in particular in the doublet or spinor representation. The same explicit formula thus follows in a simpler way through Pauli matrices, cf. the [2Ã—2 derivation for SU(2)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pauli_matrices#Exponential_of_a_Pauli_vector "Pauli matrices").

For the general _n_ Ã— _n_ case, one might use Ref.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SO(3)#cite_note-20)

Infinitesimal rotations
-----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=3D_rotation_group&action=edit&section=17 "Edit section: Infinitesimal rotations")]

An [infinitesimal rotation matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinitesimal_rotation_matrix "Infinitesimal rotation matrix") or differential rotation matrix is a [matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_(mathematics) "Matrix (mathematics)") representing an [infinitely](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinitesimal "Infinitesimal") small [rotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation "Rotation").

While a [rotation matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation_matrix "Rotation matrix") is an [orthogonal matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_matrix "Orthogonal matrix")![Image 149: {\displaystyle R^{\mathsf {T}}=R^{-1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/85439c87cf2430c0c01e9eb25f6d6cee89ee3238) representing an element of ![Image 150: {\displaystyle \mathrm {SO} (n)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9fa71842f19b6810b4bfa9eb282e92fbf285094e) (the [special orthogonal group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_orthogonal_group "Special orthogonal group")), the [differential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_(mathematics) "Differential (mathematics)") of a rotation is a [skew-symmetric matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skew-symmetric_matrix "Skew-symmetric matrix")![Image 151: {\displaystyle A^{\mathsf {T}}=-A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8f020a5cc94f0fd27929041f589affa0e575a083) in the [tangent space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_space "Tangent space")![Image 152: {\displaystyle {\mathfrak {so}}(n)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/57c18bd074c7bdc79ea650563edde2e2bc080321) (the [special orthogonal Lie algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_orthogonal_Lie_algebra "Special orthogonal Lie algebra")), which is not itself a rotation matrix.

An infinitesimal rotation matrix has the form

![Image 153: {\displaystyle I+d\theta \,A,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e3da72e5ca97ba81447afc60fae1a089f4c1a20f)
where ![Image 154: {\displaystyle I}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/535ea7fc4134a31cbe2251d9d3511374bc41be9f) is the identity matrix, ![Image 155: {\displaystyle d\theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/75ae6ca1248d081ef3fcfdd3e17ba0e3f6c02ee9) is vanishingly small, and â ![Image 156: {\displaystyle A\in {\mathfrak {so}}(n)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/890bc58f0448c24e5a51eb996e6d024b9f3d40db)â .

For example, if â ![Image 157: {\displaystyle A=L_{x}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4eaa5e8d5cb15b03dfa763d36362a272c1c7fd83)â , representing an infinitesimal three-dimensional rotation about the x-axis, a basis element of â ![Image 158: {\displaystyle {\mathfrak {so}}(3)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fb4f1d3d3bf3da64b92af1a1018ce00545808b9d)â , then

![Image 159: {\displaystyle L_{x}={\begin{bmatrix}0&0&0\\0&0&-1\\0&1&0\end{bmatrix}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/17735dab574393e5b3fd014038a70e5767006575)
and

![Image 160: {\displaystyle I+d\theta L_{x}={\begin{bmatrix}1&0&0\\0&1&-d\theta \\0&d\theta &1\end{bmatrix}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35760f4d048001b7b2793b130aa3c2c8dc843458)
The computation rules for infinitesimal rotation matrices are the usual ones except that infinitesimals of second order are dropped. With these rules, these matrices do not satisfy all the same properties as ordinary finite rotation matrices under the usual treatment of infinitesimals.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SO(3)#cite_note-22) It turns out that _the order in which infinitesimal rotations are applied is irrelevant_.

Realizations of rotations
-------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=3D_rotation_group&action=edit&section=18 "Edit section: Realizations of rotations")]

We have seen that there are a variety of ways to represent rotations:

*   as orthogonal matrices with determinant 1,
*   by axis and rotation angle
*   in [quaternion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quaternion "Quaternion") algebra with [versors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Versor "Versor") and the map [3-sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3-sphere "3-sphere")_S_ 3 â†’ SO(3) (see [quaternions and spatial rotations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quaternions_and_spatial_rotation "Quaternions and spatial rotation"))
*   in [geometric algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_algebra "Geometric algebra") as a [rotor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotor_(mathematics) "Rotor (mathematics)")
*   as a sequence of three rotations about three fixed axes; see [Euler angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler_angle "Euler angle").

Spherical harmonics
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=3D_rotation_group&action=edit&section=19 "Edit section: Spherical harmonics")]

The group SO(3) of three-dimensional Euclidean rotations has an infinite-dimensional representation on the Hilbert space

![Image 161: {\displaystyle L^{2}\left(\mathbf {S} ^{2}\right)=\operatorname {span} \left\{Y_{m}^{\ell },\ell \in \mathbb {N} ^{+},-\ell \leq m\leq \ell \right\},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e3bc85e3aa871572201b562763217a9b989a84f8)
where ![Image 162: {\displaystyle Y_{m}^{\ell }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2eec221a8066161cb86ec6711256ef71c3cf5791) are [spherical harmonics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_harmonics "Spherical harmonics"). Its elements are square integrable complex-valued functions[[nb 5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SO(3)#cite_note-23) on the sphere. The inner product on this space is given by

![Image 163: {\displaystyle \langle f,g\rangle =\int _{\mathbf {S} ^{2}}{\overline {f}}g\,d\Omega =\int _{0}^{2\pi }\int _{0}^{\pi }{\overline {f}}g\sin \theta \,d\theta \,d\phi .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2088f1508e232caad82c50f0cb9c3dac8e27a3c6)H1

If f is an arbitrary square integrable function defined on the unit sphere **S**2, then it can be expressed as[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SO(3)#cite_note-Gelfand_M_S-24)

![Image 164: {\displaystyle |f\rangle =\sum _{\ell =1}^{\infty }\sum _{m=-\ell }^{m=\ell }\left|Y_{m}^{\ell }\right\rangle \left\langle Y_{m}^{\ell }|f\right\rangle ,\qquad f(\theta ,\phi )=\sum _{\ell =1}^{\infty }\sum _{m=-\ell }^{m=\ell }f_{\ell m}Y_{m}^{\ell }(\theta ,\phi ),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e84afefce7669a38b9d21715f3277a0ced631d5a)H2

where the expansion coefficients are given by

![Image 165: {\displaystyle f_{\ell m}=\left\langle Y_{m}^{\ell },f\right\rangle =\int _{\mathbf {S} ^{2}}{\overline {Y_{m}^{\ell }}}f\,d\Omega =\int _{0}^{2\pi }\int _{0}^{\pi }{\overline {Y_{m}^{\ell }}}(\theta ,\phi )f(\theta ,\phi )\sin \theta \,d\theta \,d\phi .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5ce2a69e69fff86e2f243bfb7514a82d5f64c350)H3

The Lorentz group action restricts to that of SO(3) and is expressed as

![Image 166: {\displaystyle (\Pi (R)f)(\theta (x),\phi (x))=\sum _{\ell =1}^{\infty }\sum _{m=-\ell }^{m=\ell }\sum _{m'=-\ell }^{m'=\ell }D_{mm'}^{(\ell )}(R)f_{\ell m'}Y_{m}^{\ell }\left(\theta \left(R^{-1}x\right),\phi \left(R^{-1}x\right)\right),\qquad R\in \operatorname {SO} (3),\quad x\in \mathbf {S} ^{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dc3368a8ffafb5960be67166351209e979b6569d)H4

This action is unitary, meaning that

![Image 167: {\displaystyle \langle \Pi (R)f,\Pi (R)g\rangle =\langle f,g\rangle \qquad \forall f,g\in \mathbf {S} ^{2},\quad \forall R\in \operatorname {SO} (3).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e6bcbe64a890bd54a4b6bedb81b97cec938975ce)H5

The _D_(_â„“_) can be obtained from the _D_(_m_, _n_) of above using [Clebschâ€“Gordan decomposition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clebsch%E2%80%93Gordan_coefficients "Clebschâ€“Gordan coefficients"), but they are more easily directly expressed as an exponential of an odd-dimensional **su**(2)-representation (the 3-dimensional one is exactly ð–˜ð–”(3)).[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SO(3)#cite_note-25)[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SO(3)#cite_note-26) In this case the space _L_ 2(**S**2) decomposes neatly into an infinite direct sum of irreducible odd finite-dimensional representations _V_ 2 _i_ + 1, _i_ = 0, 1, ... according to[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SO(3)#cite_note-27)

![Image 168: {\displaystyle L^{2}\left(\mathbf {S} ^{2}\right)=\sum _{i=0}^{\infty }V_{2i+1}\equiv \bigoplus _{i=0}^{\infty }\operatorname {span} \left\{Y_{m}^{2i+1}\right\}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5ae5af5894e798d0b87223ab1c3db3b16bb292ae)H6

This is characteristic of infinite-dimensional unitary representations of SO(3). If Î  is an infinite-dimensional unitary representation on a [separable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separable_space "Separable space")[[nb 6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SO(3)#cite_note-28) Hilbert space, then it decomposes as a direct sum of finite-dimensional unitary representations.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SO(3)#cite_note-Gelfand_M_S-24) Such a representation is thus never irreducible. All irreducible finite-dimensional representations (Î , _V_) can be made unitary by an appropriate choice of inner product,[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SO(3)#cite_note-Gelfand_M_S-24)

![Image 169: {\displaystyle \langle f,g\rangle _{U}\equiv \int _{\operatorname {SO} (3)}\langle \Pi (R)f,\Pi (R)g\rangle \,dg={\frac {1}{8\pi ^{2}}}\int _{0}^{2\pi }\int _{0}^{\pi }\int _{0}^{2\pi }\langle \Pi (R)f,\Pi (R)g\rangle \sin \theta \,d\phi \,d\theta \,d\psi ,\quad f,g\in V,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/45dbd7a26dd69a84707bfdbe4bf50e407b117bbb)
where the integral is the unique invariant integral over SO(3) normalized to 1, here expressed using the [Euler angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler_angles "Euler angles") parametrization. The inner product inside the integral is any inner product on _V_.

The rotation group generalizes quite naturally to _n_-dimensional [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space"), ![Image 170: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d) with its standard Euclidean structure. The group of all proper and improper rotations in _n_ dimensions is called the [orthogonal group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_group "Orthogonal group") O(_n_), and the subgroup of proper rotations is called the [special orthogonal group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_orthogonal_group "Special orthogonal group") SO(_n_), which is a [Lie group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_group "Lie group") of dimension _n_(_n_ âˆ’ 1)/2.

In [special relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_relativity "Special relativity"), one works in a 4-dimensional vector space, known as [Minkowski space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minkowski_space "Minkowski space") rather than 3-dimensional Euclidean space. Unlike Euclidean space, Minkowski space has an inner product with an indefinite [signature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_signature "Metric signature"). However, one can still define _generalized rotations_ which preserve this inner product. Such generalized rotations are known as [Lorentz transformations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentz_transformation "Lorentz transformation") and the group of all such transformations is called the [Lorentz group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentz_group "Lorentz group").

The rotation group SO(3) can be described as a subgroup of [E+(3)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SE(3) "SE(3)"), the [Euclidean group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_group "Euclidean group") of [direct isometries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_group#Direct_and_indirect_isometries "Euclidean group") of Euclidean ![Image 171: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b00b2b4fd27c2cbffa02df568472f77b194a6db9) This larger group is the group of all motions of a [rigid body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rigid_body "Rigid body"): each of these is a combination of a rotation about an arbitrary axis and a translation, or put differently, a combination of an element of SO(3) and an arbitrary translation.

In general, the rotation group of an object is the [symmetry group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry_group "Symmetry group") within the group of direct isometries; in other words, the intersection of the full symmetry group and the group of direct isometries. For [chiral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chirality_(mathematics) "Chirality (mathematics)") objects it is the same as the full symmetry group.
