In [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics"), **action** is a [scalar quantity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_(physics) "Scalar (physics)") that describes how the balance of kinetic versus potential energy of a [physical system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_system "Physical system") changes with trajectory. Action is significant because it is an input to the [principle of stationary action](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principle_of_stationary_action "Principle of stationary action"), an approach to classical mechanics that is simpler for multiple objects.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_(physics)#cite_note-pubs.aip.org-1) Action and the variational principle are used in [Feynman's formulation of quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Path_integral_formulation "Path integral formulation")[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_(physics)#cite_note-2) and in general relativity.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_(physics)#cite_note-3) For systems with small values of action close to the [Planck constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant "Planck constant"), quantum effects are significant.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_(physics)#cite_note-FeynmanII-4)

In the simple case of a single particle moving with a constant velocity (thereby undergoing [uniform linear motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uniform_linear_motion "Uniform linear motion")), the action is the [momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum "Momentum") of the particle times the distance it moves, [added up](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral_(mathematics) "Integral (mathematics)") along its path; equivalently, action is the difference between the particle's [kinetic energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_energy "Kinetic energy") and its [potential energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potential_energy "Potential energy"), times the duration for which it has that amount of energy.

More formally, action is a [mathematical functional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functional_(mathematics) "Functional (mathematics)") which takes the [trajectory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trajectory "Trajectory") (also called path or history) of the system as its argument and has a [real number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number") as its result. Generally, the action takes different values for different paths.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_(physics)#cite_note-mcgraw1-5) Action has [dimensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimensional_analysis "Dimensional analysis") of [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy "Energy")×[time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time "Time") or [momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum "Momentum")×[length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Length "Length"), and its [SI unit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SI_unit "SI unit") is [joule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joule "Joule")-second (like the [Planck constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant "Planck constant")_h_).[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_(physics)#cite_note-6)

Introductory physics often begins with [Newton's laws of motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_laws_of_motion "Newton's laws of motion"), relating force and motion; action is part of a completely equivalent alternative approach with practical and educational advantages.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_(physics)#cite_note-pubs.aip.org-1) However, the concept took many decades to supplant Newtonian approaches and remains a challenge to introduce to students.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_(physics)#cite_note-Fee1942-7)

For a trajectory of a ball moving in the air on Earth the **action** is defined between two points in time, ![Image 2: {\displaystyle t_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cb0768c0bd659f2f84fb5ef9f4b74f336123d915) and ![Image 3: {\displaystyle t_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/749fee708b41e7079eabd50d61c8bf3e965db16f) as the kinetic energy (KE) minus the potential energy (PE), integrated over time.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_(physics)#cite_note-FeynmanII-4)

![Image 4: {\displaystyle S=\int _{t_{1}}^{t_{2}}\left(KE(t)-PE(t)\right)dt}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/244b77def981c679f02f45732028c9304fe6a53c)
The action balances kinetic against potential energy.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_(physics)#cite_note-FeynmanII-4) The kinetic energy of a ball of mass ![Image 5: {\displaystyle m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a07d98bb302f3856cbabc47b2b9016692e3f7bc) is ![Image 6: {\displaystyle (1/2)mv^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fb3e4b7409cafc428f92f816827c3b6556002be4) where ![Image 7: {\displaystyle v}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e07b00e7fc0847fbd16391c778d65bc25c452597) is the velocity of the ball; the potential energy is ![Image 8: {\displaystyle mgx}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a189c2c1b22d88e543a2201c1c98b793a5dfea85) where ![Image 9: {\displaystyle g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3556280e66fe2c0d0140df20935a6f057381d77) is the acceleration due to gravity. Then the action between ![Image 10: {\displaystyle t_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cb0768c0bd659f2f84fb5ef9f4b74f336123d915) and ![Image 11: {\displaystyle t_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/749fee708b41e7079eabd50d61c8bf3e965db16f) is

![Image 12: {\displaystyle S=\int _{t_{1}}^{t_{2}}\left({\frac {1}{2}}mv^{2}(t)-mgx(t)\right)dt}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fc0aba5ba753ac286c4877f8341e3b9e57eb23d6)
The action value depends upon the trajectory taken by the ball through ![Image 13: {\displaystyle x(t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d54c275db3a1e620737b58e143b0818107fa5f5c) and ![Image 14: {\displaystyle v(t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/243a0bf98a12f48552ba6a70302122d81b237b3d). This makes the action an input to the powerful [stationary-action principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stationary-action_principle "Stationary-action principle") for [classical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_mechanics "Classical mechanics") and for [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics"). Newton's equations of motion for the ball can be derived from the action using the stationary-action principle, but the advantages of action-based mechanics only begin to appear in cases where Newton's laws are difficult to apply. Replace the ball with an electron: classical mechanics fails but stationary action continues to work.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_(physics)#cite_note-FeynmanII-4) The energy difference in the simple action definition, kinetic minus potential energy, is generalized and called [the Lagrangian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrangian_(physics)#The_Lagrangian "Lagrangian (physics)") for more complex cases.

### Planck's quantum of action

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Action_(physics)&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Planck's quantum of action")]

The [Planck constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant "Planck constant"), written as ![Image 15: {\displaystyle h}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b26be3e694314bc90c3215047e4a2010c6ee184a) is the quantum of action.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_(physics)#cite_note-8) The quantum of [angular momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum "Angular momentum") is ![Image 16: {\displaystyle \hbar ={\frac {h}{2\pi }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/df05180652a87fe1af83af4bba3402117bd18466). These constants have units of energy times time. They appear in all significant quantum equations, like the [uncertainty principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uncertainty_principle "Uncertainty principle") and the [de Broglie wavelength](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_Broglie_wavelength "De Broglie wavelength"). Whenever the value of the action approaches the Planck constant, quantum effects are significant.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_(physics)#cite_note-FeynmanII-4)

[Pierre Louis Maupertuis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre_Louis_Maupertuis "Pierre Louis Maupertuis") and [Leonhard Euler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leonhard_Euler "Leonhard Euler") working in the 1740s developed early versions of the action principle. [Joseph Louis Lagrange](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Louis_Lagrange "Joseph Louis Lagrange") clarified the mathematics when he invented the [calculus of variations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus_of_variations "Calculus of variations"). [William Rowan Hamilton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Rowan_Hamilton "William Rowan Hamilton") made the next big breakthrough, formulating Hamilton's principle in 1853.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_(physics)#cite_note-Kline1972-9): 740  Hamilton's principle became the cornerstone for classical work with different forms of action until [Richard Feynman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Feynman "Richard Feynman") and [Julian Schwinger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Julian_Schwinger "Julian Schwinger") developed quantum action principles.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_(physics)#cite_note-10): 127

Expressed in mathematical language, using the [calculus of variations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus_of_variations "Calculus of variations"), the [evolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_evolution "Time evolution") of a physical system (i.e., how the system actually progresses from one state to another) corresponds to a [stationary point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stationary_point "Stationary point") (usually, a minimum) of the action. Action has the [dimensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimensional_analysis "Dimensional analysis") of [[energy]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy "Energy")×[[time]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time "Time"), and its [SI unit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SI_unit "SI unit") is [joule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joule "Joule")-second, which is identical to the unit of [angular momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum "Angular momentum").

Several different definitions of "the action" are in common use in physics.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_(physics)#cite_note-handfinch-11)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_(physics)#cite_note-12) The action is usually an [integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral "Integral") over time. However, when the action pertains to [fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(physics) "Field (physics)"), it may be integrated over spatial variables as well. In some cases, the action is integrated along the path followed by the physical system.

The action is typically represented as an [integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral "Integral") over time, taken along the path of the system between the initial time and the final time of the development of the system:[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_(physics)#cite_note-handfinch-11)![Image 17: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {S}}=\int _{t_{1}}^{t_{2}}L\,dt,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/96ab468bfe1e444d4354d9a8df4d1fee81524718) where the integrand _L_ is called the [Lagrangian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrangian_mechanics "Lagrangian mechanics"). For the action integral to be well-defined, the trajectory has to be bounded in time and space.

### Action (functional)

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Action_(physics)&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Action (functional)")]

Most commonly, the term is used for a [functional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functional_(mathematics) "Functional (mathematics)")![Image 18: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {S}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2302a18e269dbecc43c57c0c2aced3bfae15278d) which takes a [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)") of time and (for [fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(physics) "Field (physics)")) space as input and returns a [scalar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_(physics) "Scalar (physics)").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_(physics)#cite_note-penrose-13)[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_(physics)#cite_note-kibble-14) In [classical mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_mechanics "Classical mechanics"), the input function is the evolution **q**(_t_) of the system between two times _t_ 1 and _t_ 2, where **q** represents the [generalized coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generalized_coordinate "Generalized coordinate"). The action ![Image 19: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {S}}[\mathbf {q} (t)]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6d6bc32eb3214884af1b99e757d7f6481963852a) is defined as the [integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral "Integral") of the [Lagrangian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrangian_mechanics "Lagrangian mechanics")_L_ for an input evolution between the two times: ![Image 20: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {S}}[\mathbf {q} (t)]=\int _{t_{1}}^{t_{2}}L(\mathbf {q} (t),{\dot {\mathbf {q} }}(t),t)\,dt,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5d938fdeb7af50b8ae7fa7ba90444788e903385e) where the endpoints of the evolution are fixed and defined as ![Image 21: {\displaystyle \mathbf {q} _{1}=\mathbf {q} (t_{1})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38816b7d68c1bc3cf5b79e6429f82f9dcdfcf007) and ![Image 22: {\displaystyle \mathbf {q} _{2}=\mathbf {q} (t_{2})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dd30a043517c28fc096956dc09b8ffbbcb536964). According to [Hamilton's principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamilton%27s_principle "Hamilton's principle"), the true evolution **q**true(_t_) is an evolution for which the action ![Image 23: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {S}}[\mathbf {q} (t)]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6d6bc32eb3214884af1b99e757d7f6481963852a) is [stationary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stationary_point "Stationary point") (a minimum, maximum, or a [saddle point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saddle_point "Saddle point")). This principle results in the equations of motion in [Lagrangian mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrangian_mechanics "Lagrangian mechanics").

### Abbreviated action (functional)

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Action_(physics)&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Abbreviated action (functional)")]

In addition to the action functional, there is another functional called the _abbreviated action_. In the abbreviated action, the input function is the _path_ followed by the physical system without regard to its parameterization by time. For example, the path of a planetary orbit is an ellipse, and the path of a particle in a uniform gravitational field is a parabola; in both cases, the path does not depend on how fast the particle traverses the path.

The abbreviated action ![Image 24: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {S}}_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a7e11723561e4933eca17b886ec6df62f60c6a6b) (sometime written as ![Image 25: {\displaystyle W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54a9c4c547f4d6111f81946cad242b18298d70b7)) is defined as the integral of the generalized momenta, ![Image 26: {\displaystyle p_{i}={\frac {\partial L(q,t)}{\partial {\dot {q}}_{i}}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fb6913b776853e8aa5f84b42458b09c4811586b6) for a system Lagrangian ![Image 27: {\displaystyle L}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/103168b86f781fe6e9a4a87b8ea1cebe0ad4ede8) along a path in the [generalized coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generalized_coordinates "Generalized coordinates")![Image 28: {\displaystyle q_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2752dcbff884354069fe332b8e51eb0a70a531b6): ![Image 29: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {S}}_{0}=\int _{q_{1}}^{q_{2}}\mathbf {p} \cdot d\mathbf {q} =\int _{q_{1}}^{q_{2}}\Sigma _{i}p_{i}\,dq_{i}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3bf1e458ab0c712bd6588f0e18d7836aaac7753d) where ![Image 30: {\displaystyle q_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9daa41f6e8f78ea6bb5711d7ac97901ce564b94e) and ![Image 31: {\displaystyle q_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fd2d05084feb02b8ba29b0673440fb673b102589) are the starting and ending coordinates. According to [Maupertuis's principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maupertuis%27s_principle "Maupertuis's principle"), the true path of the system is a path for which the abbreviated action is [stationary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stationary_point "Stationary point").

The physical significance of this function is understood by taking its total time derivative

This can be integrated to give

### Action of a generalized coordinate

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Action_(physics)&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Action of a generalized coordinate")]

A variable _J k_ in the [action-angle coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action-angle_coordinates "Action-angle coordinates"), called the "action" of the generalized coordinate _q k_, is defined by integrating a single generalized momentum around a closed path in [phase space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phase_space "Phase space"), corresponding to rotating or oscillating motion:[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_(physics)#cite_note-Goldestein3-15): 454

![Image 35: {\displaystyle J_{k}=\oint p_{k}\,dq_{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d964b9a5b6bf195540ab0b724900d09fde769910)

The corresponding canonical variable conjugate to _J k_ is its "angle" _w k_, for reasons described more fully under [action-angle coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action-angle_coordinates "Action-angle coordinates"). The integration is only over a single variable _q k_ and, therefore, unlike the integrated [dot product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dot_product "Dot product") in the abbreviated action integral above. The _J k_ variable equals the change in _S k_(_q k_) as _q k_ is varied around the closed path. For several physical systems of interest, J k is either a constant or varies very slowly; hence, the variable _J k_ is often used in perturbation calculations and in determining [adiabatic invariants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adiabatic_invariant "Adiabatic invariant"). For example, they are used in the calculation of planetary and satellite orbits.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_(physics)#cite_note-Goldestein3-15): 477

### Single relativistic particle

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Action_(physics)&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Single relativistic particle")]

When relativistic effects are significant, the action of a point particle of mass _m_ travelling a [world line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_line "World line")_C_ parametrized by the [proper time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proper_time "Proper time")![Image 36: {\displaystyle \tau }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38a7dcde9730ef0853809fefc18d88771f95206c) is ![Image 37: {\displaystyle S=-mc^{2}\int _{C}\,d\tau .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/12a8b94210c6d9d5edb107d8d66ed4fff37c30c8)

If instead, the particle is parametrized by the coordinate time _t_ of the particle and the coordinate time ranges from _t_ 1 to _t_ 2, then the action becomes ![Image 38: {\displaystyle S=\int _{t1}^{t2}L\,dt,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9cabd36b94cf649b3f35421f067b73094ed92cc5) where the [Lagrangian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrangian_mechanics "Lagrangian mechanics") is[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_(physics)#cite_note-16)![Image 39: {\displaystyle L=-mc^{2}{\sqrt {1-{\frac {v^{2}}{c^{2}}}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7e0a041256c639e58e98af55cb0ab50320da5643)

Physical laws are frequently expressed as [differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_equation "Differential equation"), which describe how physical quantities such as [position](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Position_vector "Position vector") and [momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum "Momentum") change [continuously](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_function "Continuous function") with [time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_in_physics "Time in physics"), [space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space "Space") or a generalization thereof. Given the [initial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Initial_value_problem "Initial value problem") and [boundary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boundary_value_problem "Boundary value problem") conditions for the situation, the "solution" to these empirical equations is one or more [functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)") that describe the behavior of the system and are called _[equations of motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equations\_of\_motion "Equations of motion")_.

_Action_ is a part of an alternative approach to finding such equations of motion. Classical mechanics postulates that the path actually followed by a physical system is that for which the _action is minimized_, or more generally, is [stationary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stationary_point "Stationary point"). In other words, the action satisfies a [variational](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus_of_variations "Calculus of variations") principle: the [principle of stationary action](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principle_of_stationary_action "Principle of stationary action") (see also below). The action is defined by an [integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral_(calculus) "Integral (calculus)"), and the classical equations of motion of a system can be derived by minimizing the value of that integral.

The action principle provides deep insights into physics, and is an important concept in modern [theoretical physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theoretical_physics "Theoretical physics"). Various action principles and related concepts are summarized below.

### Maupertuis's principle

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Action_(physics)&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Maupertuis's principle")]

In classical mechanics, [Maupertuis's principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maupertuis%27s_principle "Maupertuis's principle") (named after Pierre Louis Maupertuis) states that the path followed by a physical system is the one of least length (with a suitable interpretation of path and length). Maupertuis's principle uses the [abbreviated action](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_(physics)#abbreviated_action) between two generalized points on a path.

### Hamilton's principal function

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Action_(physics)&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Hamilton's principal function")]

[Hamilton's principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamilton%27s_principle "Hamilton's principle") states that the differential equations of motion for _any_ physical system can be re-formulated as an equivalent [integral equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral_equation "Integral equation"). Thus, there are two distinct approaches for formulating dynamical models.

Hamilton's principle applies not only to the [classical mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_mechanics "Classical mechanics") of a single particle, but also to [classical fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_field "Classical field") such as the [electromagnetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetism "Electromagnetism") and [gravitational](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity "Gravity")[fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(physics) "Field (physics)"). Hamilton's principle has also been extended to [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics") and [quantum field theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_field_theory "Quantum field theory")—in particular the [path integral formulation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Path_integral_formulation "Path integral formulation") of quantum mechanics makes use of the concept—where a physical system explores all possible paths, with the phase of the probability amplitude for each path being determined by the action for the path; the final probability amplitude adds all paths using their complex amplitude and phase.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_(physics)#cite_note-abers1-17)

### Hamilton–Jacobi equation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Action_(physics)&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Hamilton–Jacobi equation")]

Hamilton's principal function ![Image 40: {\displaystyle S=S(q,t;q_{0},t_{0})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1581ed9c5bf6d1fce3e9ef31e7f576aedff40139) is obtained from the action functional ![Image 41: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {S}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2302a18e269dbecc43c57c0c2aced3bfae15278d) by fixing the initial time ![Image 42: {\displaystyle t_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/02d3006c4190b1939b04d9b9bb21006fb4e6fa4a) and the initial endpoint ![Image 43: {\displaystyle q_{0},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2d82d824827d2308348c6f0d67e576517a957022) while allowing the upper time limit ![Image 44: {\displaystyle t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/65658b7b223af9e1acc877d848888ecdb4466560) and the second endpoint ![Image 45: {\displaystyle q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/06809d64fa7c817ffc7e323f85997f783dbdf71d) to vary. The Hamilton's principal function satisfies the Hamilton–Jacobi equation, a formulation of [classical mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_mechanics "Classical mechanics"). Due to a similarity with the [Schrödinger equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schr%C3%B6dinger_equation "Schrödinger equation"), the Hamilton–Jacobi equation provides, arguably, the most direct link with [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics").

### Euler–Lagrange equations

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Action_(physics)&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: Euler–Lagrange equations")]

In Lagrangian mechanics, the requirement that the action integral be [stationary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stationary_point "Stationary point") under small perturbations is equivalent to a set of [differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_equation "Differential equation") (called the Euler–Lagrange equations) that may be obtained using the [calculus of variations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus_of_variations "Calculus of variations").

The **action principle** can be extended to obtain the [equations of motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equations_of_motion "Equations of motion") for fields, such as the [electromagnetic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_field "Electromagnetic field") or [gravitational field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_field "Gravitational field"). [Maxwell's equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxwell%27s_equations "Maxwell's equations") can [be derived as conditions of stationary action](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_tensor#Lagrangian_formulation_of_classical_electromagnetism "Electromagnetic tensor").

The [Einstein equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_equation "Einstein equation") utilizes the _[Einstein–Hilbert action](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein%E2%80%93Hilbert\_action "Einstein–Hilbert action")_ as constrained by a [variational principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variational_principle "Variational principle"). The [trajectory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trajectory "Trajectory") (path in [spacetime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spacetime "Spacetime")) of a body in a gravitational field can be found using the action principle. For a free falling body, this trajectory is a [geodesic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic "Geodesic").

Implications of symmetries in a physical situation can be found with the action principle, together with the [Euler–Lagrange equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler%E2%80%93Lagrange_equations "Euler–Lagrange equations"), which are derived from the action principle. An example is [Noether's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noether%27s_theorem "Noether's theorem"), which states that to every [continuous symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_symmetry "Continuous symmetry") in a physical situation there corresponds a [conservation law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservation_law_(physics) "Conservation law (physics)") (and conversely). This deep connection requires that the action principle be assumed.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_(physics)#cite_note-abers1-17)

### Path integral formulation of quantum field theory

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Action_(physics)&action=edit&section=18 "Edit section: Path integral formulation of quantum field theory")]

In [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics"), the system does not follow a single path whose action is stationary, but the behavior of the system depends on all permitted paths and the value of their action. The action corresponding to the various paths is used to calculate the [path integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Path_integral_formulation "Path integral formulation"), which gives the [probability amplitudes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability_amplitude "Probability amplitude") of the various outcomes.

Although equivalent in classical mechanics with [Newton's laws](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_laws "Newton's laws"), the **action principle** is better suited for generalizations and plays an important role in modern physics. Indeed, this principle is one of the great generalizations in physical science. It is best understood within quantum mechanics, particularly in [Richard Feynman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Feynman "Richard Feynman")'s [path integral formulation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Path_integral_formulation "Path integral formulation"), where it arises out of [destructive interference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Destructive_interference "Destructive interference") of quantum amplitudes.

The action principle can be generalized still further. For example, the action need not be an integral, because [nonlocal actions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nonlocal_Lagrangian "Nonlocal Lagrangian") are possible. The configuration space need not even be a [functional space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functional_space "Functional space"), given certain features such as [noncommutative geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noncommutative_geometry "Noncommutative geometry"). However, a physical basis for these mathematical extensions remains to be established experimentally.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_(physics)#cite_note-penrose-13)
