In [Euclidean geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_geometry "Euclidean geometry"), a **parallelogram** is a [simple](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simple_polygon "Simple polygon") (non-[self-intersecting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_self-intersecting_polygons "List of self-intersecting polygons")) [quadrilateral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral "Quadrilateral") with two pairs of [parallel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_(geometry) "Parallel (geometry)") sides. The opposite or facing sides of a parallelogram are of equal length and the opposite angles of a parallelogram are of equal measure. The [congruence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congruence_(geometry) "Congruence (geometry)") of opposite sides and opposite angles is a direct consequence of the Euclidean [parallel postulate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_postulate "Parallel postulate") and neither condition can be proven without appealing to the Euclidean parallel postulate or one of its equivalent formulations.

By comparison, a quadrilateral with at least one pair of parallel sides is a [trapezoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid "Trapezoid") in American English or a trapezium in British English.

The three-dimensional counterpart of a parallelogram is a [parallelepiped](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelepiped "Parallelepiped").

The word "parallelogram" comes from the Greek παραλληλό-γραμμον, _parallēló-grammon_, which means "a shape of parallel lines".

*   [Rectangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle "Rectangle") – A parallelogram with four right angles.
*   [Rhombus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhombus "Rhombus") – A parallelogram with four sides of equal length. Any parallelogram that is a rectangle or a rhombus was traditionally called a [rhomboid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhomboid "Rhomboid") but this term is not used in modern mathematics.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram#cite_note-1)
*   [Square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_(geometry) "Square (geometry)") – A parallelogram with four sides of equal length and four right angles.

A [simple](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simple_polygon "Simple polygon") (non-self-intersecting) [quadrilateral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral "Quadrilateral") is a parallelogram [if and only if](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/If_and_only_if "If and only if") any one of the following statements is true:[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram#cite_note-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram#cite_note-3)

*   Two pairs of opposite sides are parallel (by definition).
*   Two pairs of opposite sides are equal in length.
*   Two pairs of opposite angles are equal in measure.
*   The [diagonals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diagonal "Diagonal") bisect each other.
*   One pair of opposite sides is [parallel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_(geometry) "Parallel (geometry)") and equal in length.
*   [Adjacent angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adjacent_angles "Adjacent angles") are [supplementary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supplementary_angles "Supplementary angles").
*   Each diagonal divides the quadrilateral into two [congruent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congruence_(geometry) "Congruence (geometry)")[triangles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle "Triangle").
*   The sum of the [squares](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_number "Square number") of the sides equals the sum of the squares of the diagonals. (This is the [parallelogram law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram_law "Parallelogram law").)
*   It has [rotational symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_symmetry "Rotational symmetry") of order 2.
*   The sum of the distances from any interior point to the sides is independent of the location of the point.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram#cite_note-4) (This is an extension of [Viviani's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viviani%27s_theorem "Viviani's theorem").)
*   There is a point _X_ in the plane of the quadrilateral with the property that every straight line through _X_ divides the quadrilateral into two regions of equal area.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram#cite_note-5)

Thus, all parallelograms have all the properties listed above, and [conversely](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Converse_(logic) "Converse (logic)"), if just any one of these statements is true in a simple quadrilateral, then it is considered a parallelogram.

*   Opposite sides of a parallelogram are parallel (by definition) and so will never intersect.
*   The area of a parallelogram is twice the area of a triangle created by one of its diagonals.
*   The area of a parallelogram is also equal to the magnitude of the [vector cross product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_cross_product "Vector cross product") of two [adjacent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adjacent_side_(polygon) "Adjacent side (polygon)") sides.
*   Any line through the midpoint of a parallelogram bisects the area.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram#cite_note-6)
*   Any non-degenerate [affine transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_transformation "Affine transformation") takes a parallelogram to another parallelogram.
*   A parallelogram has [rotational symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_symmetry "Rotational symmetry") of order 2 (through 180°) (or order 4 if a square). If it also has exactly two lines of [reflectional symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflectional_symmetry "Reflectional symmetry") then it must be a rhombus or an oblong (a non-square rectangle). If it has four lines of reflectional symmetry, it is a [square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square "Square").
*   The perimeter of a parallelogram is 2(_a_ + _b_) where _a_ and _b_ are the lengths of adjacent sides.
*   Unlike any other convex polygon, a parallelogram cannot be inscribed in any triangle with less than twice its area.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram#cite_note-7)
*   The centers of four squares all constructed either internally or externally on the sides of a parallelogram are the vertices of a square.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram#cite_note-Weisstein-8)
*   If two lines parallel to sides of a parallelogram are constructed [concurrent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concurrent_lines "Concurrent lines") to a diagonal, then the parallelograms formed on opposite sides of that diagonal are equal in area.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram#cite_note-Weisstein-8)
*   The diagonals of a parallelogram divide it into four triangles of equal area.

All of the [area formulas for general convex quadrilaterals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#Area_of_a_convex_quadrilateral "Quadrilateral") apply to parallelograms. Further formulas are specific to parallelograms:

A parallelogram with base _b_ and height _h_ can be divided into a [trapezoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid "Trapezoid") and a [right triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_triangle "Right triangle"), and rearranged into a [rectangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle "Rectangle"), as shown in the figure to the left. This means that the [area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area "Area") of a parallelogram is the same as that of a rectangle with the same base and height:

![Image 5: {\displaystyle K=bh.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0aafb35db413cd4e1e428c61e0564f097692f1a9)[![Image 6](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Parallelogram_area.svg/250px-Parallelogram_area.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Parallelogram_area.svg)

The area of the parallelogram is the area of the blue region, which is the interior of the parallelogram

The base × height area formula can also be derived using the figure to the right. The area _K_ of the parallelogram to the right (the blue area) is the total area of the rectangle less the area of the two orange triangles. The area of the rectangle is

![Image 7: {\displaystyle K_{\text{rect}}=(B+A)\times H\,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/624d8b3c282546b2da21eebbf5230221831ae316)
and the area of a single triangle is

![Image 8: {\displaystyle K_{\text{tri}}={\frac {A}{2}}\times H.\,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a207b19540ff507ebf5aea92b2216b934baa939d)
Therefore, the area of the parallelogram is

![Image 9: {\displaystyle K=K_{\text{rect}}-2\times K_{\text{tri}}=((B+A)\times H)-(A\times H)=B\times H.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/74aba8c98bfea059c67c19e4c6bedd1f50b135d3)
Another area formula, for two sides _B_ and _C_ and angle θ, is

![Image 10: {\displaystyle K=B\cdot C\cdot \sin \theta .\,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f761ff8f6540b6a058436e66ee822609c8f7d9)
Provided that the parallelogram is a rhombus, the area can be expressed using sides _B_ and _C_ and angle ![Image 11: {\displaystyle \gamma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a223c880b0ce3da8f64ee33c4f0010beee400b1a) at the intersection of the diagonals:[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram#cite_note-9)

Proof that diagonals bisect each other
--------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Parallelogram&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Proof that diagonals bisect each other")]

[![Image 23: Parallelogram ABCD](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Parallelogram1.svg/200px-Parallelogram1.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Parallelogram1.svg "Parallelogram ABCD")

Parallelogram ABCD

To prove that the diagonals of a parallelogram bisect each other, we will use [congruent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congruence_(geometry) "Congruence (geometry)")[triangles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle#Basic_facts "Triangle"):

![Image 24: {\displaystyle \angle ABE\cong \angle CDE}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fab9e09a52dea0bf091179212defbbf6035ff086)_(alternate interior angles are equal in measure)_![Image 25: {\displaystyle \angle BAE\cong \angle DCE}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f6807434bee15ed8506eb916def45f584f398fb0)_(alternate interior angles are equal in measure)_.
(since these are angles that a transversal makes with [parallel lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_(geometry) "Parallel (geometry)")_AB_ and _DC_).

Also, side _AB_ is equal in length to side _DC_, since opposite sides of a parallelogram are equal in length.

Therefore, triangles _ABE_ and _CDE_ are congruent (ASA postulate, _two corresponding angles and the included side_).

Therefore,

![Image 26: {\displaystyle AE=CE}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f6d80254dff1b2250ab27448227c0257efc43fe1)![Image 27: {\displaystyle BE=DE.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fff2cf29d8b7a94f92079ccca0cfbf611369228c)
Since the diagonals _AC_ and _BD_ divide each other into segments of equal length, the diagonals bisect each other.

Separately, since the diagonals _AC_ and _BD_ bisect each other at point _E_, point _E_ is the midpoint of each diagonal.

Lattice of parallelograms
-------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Parallelogram&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Lattice of parallelograms")]

Parallelograms can tile the plane by translation. If edges are equal, or angles are right, the symmetry of the lattice is higher. These represent the four [Bravais lattices in 2 dimensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bravais_lattice#In_2_dimensions "Bravais lattice").

### Automedian triangle

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Parallelogram&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Automedian triangle")]

An [automedian triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Automedian_triangle "Automedian triangle") is one whose [medians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Median_(geometry) "Median (geometry)") are in the same proportions as its sides (though in a different order). If _ABC_ is an automedian triangle in which vertex _A_ stands opposite the side _a_, _G_ is the [centroid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centroid "Centroid") (where the three medians of _ABC_ intersect), and _AL_ is one of the extended medians of _ABC_ with _L_ lying on the circumcircle of _ABC_, then _BGCL_ is a parallelogram.

### Varignon parallelogram

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Parallelogram&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Varignon parallelogram")]

[![Image 32](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Varignon_parallelogram.svg/250px-Varignon_parallelogram.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Varignon_parallelogram.svg)

Proof without words of Varignon's theorem 

[Varignon's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Varignon%27s_theorem "Varignon's theorem") holds that the [midpoints](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Midpoint "Midpoint") of the sides of an arbitrary quadrilateral are the vertices of a parallelogram, called its _Varignon parallelogram_. If the quadrilateral is [convex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_polygon "Convex polygon") or [concave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concave_polygon "Concave polygon") (that is, not self-intersecting), then the area of the Varignon parallelogram is half the area of the quadrilateral.

[Proof without words](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proof_without_words "Proof without words") (see figure):

1.   An arbitrary quadrilateral and its diagonals.
2.   Bases of similar triangles are parallel to the blue diagonal.
3.   Ditto for the red diagonal.
4.   The base pairs form a parallelogram with half the area of the quadrilateral, _A q_, as the sum of the areas of the four large triangles, _A l_ is 2 _A q_ (each of the two pairs reconstructs the quadrilateral) while that of the small triangles, _A s_ is a quarter of _A l_ (half linear dimensions yields quarter area), and the area of the parallelogram is _A q_ minus _A s_.

### Tangent parallelogram of an ellipse

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Parallelogram&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Tangent parallelogram of an ellipse")]

For an [ellipse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse "Ellipse"), two diameters are said to be [conjugate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conjugate_diameters "Conjugate diameters") if and only if the [tangent line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_line "Tangent line") to the ellipse at an endpoint of one diameter is parallel to the other diameter. Each pair of conjugate diameters of an ellipse has a corresponding [tangent parallelogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_parallelogram "Tangent parallelogram"), sometimes called a bounding parallelogram, formed by the tangent lines to the ellipse at the four endpoints of the conjugate diameters. All tangent parallelograms for a given ellipse have the same area.

It is possible to [reconstruct](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compass_and_straightedge_constructions "Compass and straightedge constructions") an ellipse from any pair of conjugate diameters, or from any tangent parallelogram.

### Faces of a parallelepiped

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Parallelogram&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Faces of a parallelepiped")]

A [parallelepiped](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelepiped "Parallelepiped") is a three-dimensional figure whose six [faces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Face_(geometry) "Face (geometry)") are parallelograms.

*   [parallelogon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogon "Parallelogon"), generalisation encompassing hexagons as well as quadrilaterals
*   [zonogon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zonogon "Zonogon"), generalisation to polygons with any even number of sides
*   [Antiparallelogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiparallelogram "Antiparallelogram")
*   [Levi-Civita parallelogramoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_parallelogramoid "Levi-Civita parallelogramoid")
