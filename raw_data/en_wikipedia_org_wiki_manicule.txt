The **manicule**, ‚òõ, is a [typographic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Typography "Typography") mark with the appearance of a hand with its [index finger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Index_finger "Index finger") extending in a [pointing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pointing "Pointing") gesture. Originally used for handwritten [marginal notes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marginal_note "Marginal note"), it later came to be used in printed works to draw the reader's attention to important text. Though once widespread, it is rarely used today, except as an occasional archaic novelty or on informal directional signs.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manicule#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHouston2013-2)

A manicule from the "Specimen Book of the Cincinnati Type Foundry" (1882) 

For most of its history, the mark has been inconsistently referred to by a variety of names. William H. Sherman, in the first dedicated study of the mark, uses the term _manicule_ (from the Latin root _manicula_, meaning "little hand"), but also identifies 14[[b]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manicule#cite_note-3) further names which he records as having been used:[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manicule#cite_note-FOOTNOTESherman20059%E2%80%9310-4)

*   hand
*   pointing hand
*   hand director
*   pointer
*   digit
*   fist
*   mutton fist
*   bishop's fist
*   index
*   _indicationum_
*   indicator
*   _[indicule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikt "Wikt")_[[c]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manicule#cite_note-5)
*   [maniple](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maniple_(vestment) "Maniple (vestment)")[[d]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manicule#cite_note-6)
*   [pilcrow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pilcrow "Pilcrow")[[e]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manicule#cite_note-7)

Sherman calls the last three terms erroneous: _indicule_ and _maniple_ as mishearings or conflations, and _[pilcrow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pilcrow "Pilcrow")_ as properly referring to the paragraph mark, ¬∂.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manicule#cite_note-FOOTNOTESherman20059%E2%80%9310-4)

### Handwritten manicules

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Manicule&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Handwritten manicules")]

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Manicule_hand_drawn_from_15th_century.png/250px-Manicule_hand_drawn_from_15th_century.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Manicule_hand_drawn_from_15th_century.png)

Manicule from the fifteenth century

The symbol originates in scribal tradition of the medieval and Renaissance period, appearing in the margin of manuscripts to mark corrections or notes. The earliest book known to include manicules is the 1086 [Domesday Book](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domesday_Book "Domesday Book"), where they are used for marginal annotations alongside other marks such as [daggers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dagger_(mark) "Dagger (mark)"). The age of the annotations is not known, and they may date to later than the 11th century.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manicule#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcPharlin194247%E2%80%9348-8)

Manicules are first known to appear in the 12th century in handwritten [manuscripts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manuscript "Manuscript") in Spain,[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manicule#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGlaister2001141-9) and became common in the 14th and 15th centuries in Italy with some very elaborate with shading and artful cuffs.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manicule#cite_note-FOOTNOTESherman200511-10) Some were playful and elaborate, but others were as simple as "two squiggly strokes suggesting the barest sketch of a pointing hand" and thus quick to draw.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manicule#cite_note-FOOTNOTESherman200512-11)

After the popularization of the [printing press](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Printing_press "Printing press") starting in the 1450s, the handwritten version continued in handwritten form as a means to [annotate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Annotate "Annotate") printed documents, eventually falling out of popularity by the nineteenth century.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manicule#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHouston2013-2)

Early printers using a type representing the manicule included Mathias Huss and Johannes Schabeler in Lyons in their 1484 edition of Paulus Florentinus's _Breviarum totius juris canonici_.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manicule#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGlaister2001141-9) Writer John Boardley identifies the first appearance of a manicule in a printed book as an earlier 1479 edition of the same work, _Breviarum totius juris canonici_, printed in Milan by Leonhard Pachel and Ulrich Scinzenzeller.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manicule#cite_note-Boardley-12)

In contrast with their handwritten use, early printed manicules appeared in the main text, pointing outward toward corresponding printed margin notes. Later, beginning in the sixteenth century,[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manicule#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcPharlin194251-13) the manicule came to be used as a decorative element on the title pages of books, alongside other so-called "[dingbats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dingbat "Dingbat")" such as the [fleuron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fleuron_(typography) "Fleuron (typography)") (‚ù¶).[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manicule#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHouston2013-2)

The manicule attained a great degree of popularity in the nineteenth century, particularly in advertisements. At this time, they also became more visually diverse, with larger and more complex fists being created.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manicule#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHouston2013-2) They were also widely used in signage, with some [fingerposts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fingerpost "Fingerpost") having relief-printed or even fully three-dimensional physical manifestations of pointing hands.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manicule#cite_note-FOOTNOTESherman200513-14) The [United States Postal Service](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Postal_Service "United States Postal Service") has also used a pointing hand as a graphical indicator for its "[Return to Sender](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Return_address "Return address")" stamp.

Its popularity declined toward the end of the nineteenth century, perhaps due to its oversaturation in advertising. By the 1890s, it was rarely used unless for ironic effect.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manicule#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHouston2013-2) Sherman (2005) argues that as the symbols became standardized, they were no longer reflective of individuality in comparison to other writing, and this explains their diminished popularity.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manicule#cite_note-FOOTNOTESherman200520%E2%80%9321-15)

[![Image 6](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/John_Wilkes_Booth_wanted_poster_new.jpg/250px-John_Wilkes_Booth_wanted_poster_new.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:John_Wilkes_Booth_wanted_poster_new.jpg)

1865 wanted poster of [John Wilkes Booth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Wilkes_Booth "John Wilkes Booth") using index-fist character.

The typical use of the pointing hand is as a [bullet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bullet_(typography) "Bullet (typography)")-like symbol to direct the reader's attention to important text, having roughly the same meaning as the word "attention" or "[note](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nota_bene "Nota bene")". It is used this way both by annotators and printers. Even in the first few centuries of use, it can be seen used to draw attention to specific text, such as a title (in some cases in the form of a row of manicules), inserted text, noteworthy passage, or [sententiae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sententiae "Sententiae"). In some cases, [flower marks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fleuron_(typography) "Fleuron (typography)") and [asterisks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asterisks "Asterisks") were used for similar purposes. Less commonly, in earlier centuries the pointing hand acted as a [section divider](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Section_divider "Section divider") with a [pilcrow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pilcrow "Pilcrow") as paragraph divider; or more rarely as the paragraph divider itself.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manicule#cite_note-FOOTNOTESherman200514%E2%80%9318-16)

Some encyclopedias use it in articles to [cross-reference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross-reference "Cross-reference"), as in ‚òû other articles. It occasionally sees use in magazines and comic books to indicate to the reader that a story on the right-hand page continues onto the next.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

In modern printing, it was used as a standard typographical symbol marking notes. The American _Dictionary of Printing and Bookmaking_ (1894) treats it as the seventh in the standard sequence of footnote markers, following the paragraph sign ([pilcrow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pilcrow "Pilcrow")).[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manicule#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHasler1953-17)

In linguistics, the symbol is used in [optimality theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optimality_theory "Optimality theory") tableaux to identify the optimal output in a candidate of generated possibilities from a given input.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manicule#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPrinceSmolensky200419-18)

American science fiction writer [Kurt Vonnegut](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kurt_Vonnegut "Kurt Vonnegut") used the symbol as a form of margin on the first line of every paragraph in his novel _[Breakfast of Champions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Breakfast\_of\_Champions "Breakfast of Champions")_. The literary effect of this was to create separation between each paragraph, reinforcing the [stream of consciousness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stream_of_consciousness_(narrative_mode) "Stream of consciousness (narrative mode)") style of the text.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

American essayist and cultural critic [H.L. Mencken](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/H.L._Mencken "H.L. Mencken"), often credited with having first coined the aphorism, "When you point one finger, there are three fingers pointing back to you," is also reported to have used this symbol to convey this sentiment in shorthand, seen first in his telegrams as early as the 1920s.

[Thomas Pynchon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Pynchon "Thomas Pynchon") parodies this punctuation mark in his novel _[Gravity's Rainbow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity%27s\_Rainbow "Gravity's Rainbow")_ by depicting a [middle finger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_finger "Middle finger"), rather than an index finger, pointing at a line of text.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manicule#cite_note-19)

An upward pointing hand is often used in the [mouse cursor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cursor_(user_interface)#Pointer "Cursor (user interface)") in [graphical user interfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graphical_user_interface "Graphical user interface") (such as those in [Adobe Acrobat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adobe_Acrobat "Adobe Acrobat") and [Photoshop](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoshop "Photoshop")) to indicate an object that can be manipulated. The first is believed to be the [Xerox Star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xerox_Star "Xerox Star").[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manicule#cite_note-FOOTNOTESherman200513-14) Many web browsers use an upward pointing hand cursor to indicate a clickable [hyperlink](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperlink "Hyperlink"). [CSS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CSS "CSS") 2.0 allows the "cursor" property to be set to "hand" or "pointer" to intentionally change the mouse cursor to this symbol when hovering over an object; "move" may produce a closed fisted hand. Many [video games](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Video_game "Video game") made in the 1980s and '90s, primarily text-based [adventure games](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adventure_game "Adventure game"), also used these cursors.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]
