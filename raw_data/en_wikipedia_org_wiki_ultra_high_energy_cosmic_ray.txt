In [astroparticle physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astroparticle_physics "Astroparticle physics"), an **ultra-high-energy cosmic ray** (**UHECR**) is a [cosmic ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_ray "Cosmic ray") with an energy greater than 1 EeV (10 18[electronvolts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronvolts "Electronvolts"), approximately 0.16 [joules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joule "Joule")),[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultra-high-energy_cosmic_ray#cite_note-1) far beyond both the [rest mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rest_mass "Rest mass") and energies typical of other cosmic ray particles. The origin of these highest energy cosmic rays is not known.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultra-high-energy_cosmic_ray#cite_note-Kotera-2011-2)

These particles are extremely rare; between 2004 and 2007, the initial runs of the [Pierre Auger Observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre_Auger_Observatory "Pierre Auger Observatory") (PAO) detected 27 events with estimated arrival energies above 5.7×10 19 eV, that is, about one such event every four weeks in the 3,000 km 2 (1,200 sq mi) area surveyed by the observatory.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultra-high-energy_cosmic_ray#cite_note-3)

Observational history
---------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ultra-high-energy_cosmic_ray&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Observational history")]

The first observation of a cosmic ray particle with an energy exceeding 1.0×10 20 eV (16 J) was made by [John Linsley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Linsley "John Linsley") and Livio Scarsi at the [Volcano Ranch experiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volcano_Ranch_experiment "Volcano Ranch experiment") in New Mexico in 1962.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultra-high-energy_cosmic_ray#cite_note-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultra-high-energy_cosmic_ray#cite_note-5)

Cosmic ray particles with even higher energies have since been observed. Among them was the [Oh-My-God particle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oh-My-God_particle "Oh-My-God particle") observed by the University of Utah's Fly's Eye experiment on the evening of 15 October 1991 over [Dugway Proving Ground](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dugway_Proving_Ground "Dugway Proving Ground"), Utah. Its observation was shocking to [astrophysicists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrophysics "Astrophysics"), who estimated its energy at approximately 3.2×10 20 eV (50 J)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultra-high-energy_cosmic_ray#cite_note-6)—essentially an [atomic nucleus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_nucleus "Atomic nucleus") with kinetic energy equal to a [baseball](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baseball_(ball) "Baseball (ball)") (5 ounces or 142 grams) traveling at about 100 kilometers per hour (60 mph).

The energy of this particle is some 40 million times that of the highest energy protons that have been produced in any terrestrial [particle accelerator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_accelerator "Particle accelerator"). However, only a small fraction of this energy would be available for an interaction with a proton or neutron on Earth, with most of the energy remaining in the form of kinetic energy of the products of the interaction (see [Collider §Explanation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collider#Explanation "Collider")). The effective energy available for such a collision is the square root of double the product of the particle's energy and the mass energy of the proton, which for this particle gives 7.5×10 14 eV, roughly 50 times the collision energy of the [Large Hadron Collider](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Large_Hadron_Collider "Large Hadron Collider").

Since the first observation, by the [University of Utah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Utah "University of Utah")'s [Fly's Eye Cosmic Ray Detector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High_Resolution_Fly%27s_Eye_Cosmic_Ray_Detector "High Resolution Fly's Eye Cosmic Ray Detector"), at least fifteen similar events have been recorded, confirming the phenomenon. These very high energy cosmic ray particles are very rare; the energy of most cosmic ray particles is between 10 MeV and 10 GeV.

### Pierre Auger Observatory

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ultra-high-energy_cosmic_ray&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Pierre Auger Observatory")]

Pierre Auger Observatory is an international cosmic ray observatory designed to detect ultra-high-energy cosmic ray particles (with energies beyond 10 20 eV). These high-energy particles have an estimated arrival rate of just 1 per square kilometer per century, therefore, in order to record a large number of these events, the Auger Observatory has created a detection area of 3,000 km 2 (the size of [Rhode Island](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhode_Island "Rhode Island")) in [Mendoza Province](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mendoza_Province "Mendoza Province"), western [Argentina](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Argentina "Argentina"). The Pierre Auger Observatory, in addition to obtaining directional information from the cluster of water-Cherenkov tanks used to observe the cosmic-ray-shower components, also has four telescopes trained on the night sky to observe [fluorescence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluorescence "Fluorescence") of the [nitrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen "Nitrogen") molecules as the shower particles traverse the sky, giving further directional information on the original cosmic ray particle.

In September 2017, data from 12 years of observations from PAO supported an extragalactic source (outside of Earth's galaxy) for the origin of extremely high energy cosmic rays.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultra-high-energy_cosmic_ray#cite_note-7)

The origin of these rare highest energy cosmic rays is not known. Since observations find no correlation with the Galactic plane and Galactic magnetic fields are not strong enough to accelerate particles to these energies, these cosmic rays are believed to have extra-galactic origin.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultra-high-energy_cosmic_ray#cite_note-Kotera-2011-2)

One suggested source of UHECR particles is their origination from [neutron stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron_star "Neutron star"). In young neutron stars with spin periods of <10 ms, the [magnetohydrodynamic (MHD) forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamics "Magnetohydrodynamics") from the quasi-neutral fluid of superconducting protons and electrons existing in a neutron [superfluid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superfluid "Superfluid") accelerate iron nuclei to UHECR velocities. The neutron superfluid in rapidly rotating stars creates a magnetic field of 10 8 to 10 11 teslas, at which point the neutron star is classified as a [magnetar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetar "Magnetar"). This magnetic field is the strongest stable field in the observed universe and creates the relativistic MHD wind believed to accelerate iron nuclei remaining from the supernova to the necessary energy.

Another hypothesized source of UHECRs from neutron stars is during neutron star to [strange star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strange_star "Strange star") combustion. This hypothesis relies on the assumption that [strange matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strange_matter "Strange matter") is the [ground state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ground_state "Ground state") of matter which has no experimental or observational data to support it. Due to the immense gravitational pressures from the neutron star, it is believed that small pockets of matter consisting of _up_, _down_, and _strange_ quarks in equilibrium acting as a single hadron (as opposed to a number of [Σ 0 baryons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sigma_baryon "Sigma baryon")). This will then combust the entire star to strange matter, at which point the neutron star becomes a strange star and its magnetic field breaks down, which occurs because the protons and neutrons in the quasi-neutral fluid have become [strangelets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strangelets "Strangelets"). This magnetic field breakdown releases large amplitude electromagnetic waves (LAEMWs). The LAEMWs accelerate light ion remnants from the supernova to UHECR energies.

"Ultra-high-energy cosmic ray _electrons_" (defined as [electrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrons "Electrons") with energies of ≥10 14[eV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronvolt "Electronvolt")) might be explained by the [Centrifugal mechanism of acceleration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centrifugal_mechanism_of_acceleration "Centrifugal mechanism of acceleration") in the magnetospheres of the [Crab](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crab_Nebula#Crab_Pulsar "Crab Nebula")-like [pulsars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulsar "Pulsar").[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultra-high-energy_cosmic_ray#cite_note-8) The feasibility of electron acceleration to this energy scale in the [Crab Pulsar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crab_Pulsar "Crab Pulsar") magnetosphere is supported by the 2019 observation of [ultra-high-energy gamma rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultra-high-energy_gamma_ray "Ultra-high-energy gamma ray") coming from the [Crab Nebula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crab_Nebula#Crab_Pulsar "Crab Nebula"), a young pulsar with a spin period of 33 ms.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultra-high-energy_cosmic_ray#cite_note-9)

### Active galactic cores

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ultra-high-energy_cosmic_ray&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Active galactic cores")]

Interactions with [blue-shifted](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blue_shift "Blue shift")[cosmic microwave background radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation "Cosmic microwave background radiation") limit the distance that these particles can travel before losing energy; this is known as the [Greisen–Zatsepin–Kuzmin limit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greisen%E2%80%93Zatsepin%E2%80%93Kuzmin_limit "Greisen–Zatsepin–Kuzmin limit") or GZK limit.

The source of such high energy particles has been a mystery for many years. Recent results from the Pierre Auger Observatory show that ultra-high-energy cosmic ray arrival directions appear to be correlated with extragalactic supermassive black holes at the center of nearby galaxies called [active galactic nuclei (AGN)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Active_galactic_nucleus "Active galactic nucleus").[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultra-high-energy_cosmic_ray#cite_note-PAO2007-10) However, since the angular correlation scale used is fairly large (3.1°) these results do not unambiguously identify the origins of such cosmic ray particles. The AGN could merely be closely associated with the actual sources, for example in galaxies or other astrophysical objects that are clumped with matter on large scales within 100 [megaparsecs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Megaparsec "Megaparsec").[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultra-high-energy_cosmic_ray#cite_note-11)

Some of the [supermassive black holes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supermassive_black_hole "Supermassive black hole") in AGN are known to be rotating, as in the [Seyfert galaxy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seyfert_galaxy "Seyfert galaxy") MCG 6-30-15[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultra-high-energy_cosmic_ray#cite_note-12) with time-variability in their inner accretion disks.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultra-high-energy_cosmic_ray#cite_note-13) Black hole spin is a potentially effective agent to drive UHECR production,[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultra-high-energy_cosmic_ray#cite_note-14) provided ions are suitably launched to circumvent limiting factors deep within the galactic nucleus, notably curvature radiation[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultra-high-energy_cosmic_ray#cite_note-15) and inelastic scattering with radiation from the inner disk. Low-luminosity, intermittent Seyfert galaxies may meet the requirements with the formation of a linear accelerator several light years away from the nucleus, yet within their extended ion tori whose UV radiation ensures a supply of ionic contaminants.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultra-high-energy_cosmic_ray#cite_note-16) The corresponding electric fields are small, on the order of 10 V/cm, whereby the observed UHECRs are indicative for the astronomical size of the source. Improved statistics by the Pierre Auger Observatory will be instrumental in identifying the presently tentative association of UHECRs (from the Local Universe) with Seyferts and [LINERs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Low-ionization_nuclear_emission-line_region "Low-ionization nuclear emission-line region").[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultra-high-energy_cosmic_ray#cite_note-17)

### Other possible sources of the particles

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ultra-high-energy_cosmic_ray&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Other possible sources of the particles")]

In addition to neutron stars and active galactic nuclei, the best candidate sources of the UHECR are:[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultra-high-energy_cosmic_ray#cite_note-Kotera-2011-2)

*   [Supernova remnants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supernova_remnant "Supernova remnant")
*   intergalactic shocks created during the epoch of galaxy formation
*   [gamma-ray bursts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamma-ray_burst "Gamma-ray burst")[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultra-high-energy_cosmic_ray#cite_note-1995PhRvL..75..386W-18)[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultra-high-energy_cosmic_ray#cite_note-1995ApJ...449L..37M-19)
*   relativistic [supernovae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supernova "Supernova")[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultra-high-energy_cosmic_ray#cite_note-2011NatCo...2E.175C-20)

### Relation with dark matter

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ultra-high-energy_cosmic_ray&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Relation with dark matter")]

It is hypothesized that active galactic nuclei are capable of converting dark matter into high energy protons. Yuri Pavlov and Andrey Grib at the Alexander Friedmann Laboratory for Theoretical Physics in Saint Petersburg hypothesize that dark matter particles are about 15 times heavier than protons, and that they can decay into pairs of heavier virtual particles of a type that interacts with ordinary matter.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultra-high-energy_cosmic_ray#cite_note-21) Near an active galactic nucleus, one of these particles can fall into the black hole, while the other escapes, as described by the [Penrose process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Penrose_process "Penrose process"). Some of those particles will collide with incoming particles; these are very high energy collisions which, according to Pavlov, can form ordinary visible protons with very high energy. Pavlov then claims that evidence of such processes are ultra-high-energy cosmic ray particles.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultra-high-energy_cosmic_ray#cite_note-22)

Ultra-high-energy particles can interact with the photons in the [cosmic microwave background](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background "Cosmic microwave background") while traveling over cosmic distances.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultra-high-energy_cosmic_ray#cite_note-23) This led to a predicted high energy cutoff for those cosmic rays known as the [Greisen–Zatsepin–Kuzmin limit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greisen%E2%80%93Zatsepin%E2%80%93Kuzmin_limit "Greisen–Zatsepin–Kuzmin limit") (GZK limit) which matches observed cosmic ray spectra.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultra-high-energy_cosmic_ray#cite_note-Kotera-2011-2): 6

The propagation of particles can also be affected by cosmic magnetic fields. While there is some studies of galactic magnetic fields, the origin and scale of extragalactic magnetic fields are poorly understood.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultra-high-energy_cosmic_ray#cite_note-Kotera-2011-2): 15

*   [Extragalactic cosmic ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extragalactic_cosmic_ray "Extragalactic cosmic ray")
*   [HZE ions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HZE_ions "HZE ions")– High-energy, heavy ions of cosmic origin
*   [Solar energetic particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_energetic_particles "Solar energetic particles")– High-energy particles from the Sun
*   [Oh-My-God particle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oh-My-God_particle "Oh-My-God particle")– Ultra-high-energy cosmic ray detected in 1991
