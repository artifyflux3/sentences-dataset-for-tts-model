In [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry"), a **solid angle** (symbol: Ω) is a measure of the amount of the [field of view](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_of_view "Field of view") from some particular point that a given object covers. That is, it is a measure of how large the object appears to an observer looking from that point. The point from which the object is viewed is called the _apex_ of the solid angle, and the object is said to _[subtend](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subtended\_angle "Subtended angle")_ its solid angle at that point.

In the [International System of Units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Units "International System of Units") (SI), a solid angle is expressed in a [dimensionless](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimensionless_quantity "Dimensionless quantity")[unit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_of_measurement "Unit of measurement") called a _[steradian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steradian "Steradian")_ (symbol: sr), which is equal to one square [radian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radian "Radian"), sr = rad 2. One steradian corresponds to one unit of area (of any shape) on the [unit sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_sphere "Unit sphere") surrounding the apex, so an object that blocks all rays from the apex would cover a number of steradians equal to the total [surface area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_area "Surface area") of the unit sphere, ![Image 4: {\displaystyle 4\pi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/057444bf35a0c22b19bcae1ef06e06ecdf8abe56). Solid angles can also be measured in squares of angular measures such as [degrees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_degree "Square degree"), minutes, and seconds.

A small object nearby may subtend the same solid angle as a larger object farther away. For example, although the [Moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moon "Moon") is much smaller than the [Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun "Sun"), it is also much closer to [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth "Earth"). Indeed, as viewed from any point on Earth, both objects have approximately the same solid angle (and therefore apparent size). This is evident during a [solar eclipse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_eclipse "Solar eclipse").

Definition and properties
-------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Solid_angle&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Definition and properties")]

The magnitude of an object's solid angle in [steradians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steradian "Steradian") is equal to the [area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area_(geometry) "Area (geometry)") of the segment of a [unit sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_sphere "Unit sphere"), centered at the apex, that the object covers. Giving the area of a segment of a unit sphere in steradians is analogous to giving the length of an arc of a [unit circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_circle "Unit circle") in radians. Just as the magnitude of a plane angle in radians at the vertex of a circular sector is the ratio of the length of its arc to its radius, the magnitude of a solid angle in steradians is the ratio of the area covered on a sphere by an object to the square of the radius of the sphere. The formula for the magnitude of the solid angle in steradians is

![Image 5: {\displaystyle \Omega ={\frac {A}{r^{2}}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cf6149c6dcd69ff789a370d91f138a4ff1e0fda2)

where ![Image 6: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3) is the area (of any shape) on the surface of the sphere and ![Image 7: {\displaystyle r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d1ecb613aa2984f0576f70f86650b7c2a132538) is the radius of the sphere.

Solid angles are often used in [astronomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy "Astronomy"), [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics"), and in particular [astrophysics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrophysics "Astrophysics"). The solid angle of an object that is very far away is roughly proportional to the ratio of area to squared distance. Here "area" means the area of the object when projected along the viewing direction.

[![Image 8](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Solid_Angle%2C_1_Steradian.svg/250px-Solid_Angle%2C_1_Steradian.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Solid_Angle,_1_Steradian.svg)

Any area on a sphere which is equal in area to the square of its radius, when observed from its center, subtends precisely one [steradian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steradian "Steradian").

The solid angle of a sphere measured from any point in its interior is 4[π](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi "Pi")sr. The solid angle subtended at the center of a cube by one of its faces is one-sixth of that, or 2 π/3 sr. The solid angle subtended at the corner of a cube (an [octant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octant_(geometry) "Octant (geometry)")) or spanned by a [spherical octant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_octant "Spherical octant") is π/2 sr, one-eighth of the solid angle of a sphere.

Solid angles can also be measured in [square degrees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_degree "Square degree") (1 sr = (180/π)2 square degrees), in square [arc-minutes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arc-minutes "Arc-minutes") and square [arc-seconds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arc-seconds "Arc-seconds").[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_angle#cite_note-1) It can also be expressed in fractions of the sphere (1 sr = ⁠1/4 π⁠ fractional area), also known as [spat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spat_(angular_unit) "Spat (angular unit)") (1 sp = 4 π sr).

In [spherical coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_coordinates#Integration_and_differentiation_in_spherical_coordinates "Spherical coordinates") there is a formula for the [differential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_of_a_function "Differential of a function"),

![Image 9: {\displaystyle d\Omega =\sin \theta \,d\theta \,d\varphi ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/95edc40d7613aabd033e626131afdec63461db94)

where θ is the [colatitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colatitude "Colatitude") (angle from the North Pole) and φ is the longitude.

The solid angle for an arbitrary [oriented surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oriented_surface "Oriented surface")S subtended at a point P is equal to the solid angle of the projection of the surface S to the unit sphere with center P, which can be calculated as the [surface integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_integral "Surface integral"):

![Image 10: {\displaystyle \Omega =\iint _{S}{\frac {{\hat {r}}\cdot {\hat {n}}}{r^{2}}}\,dS\ =\iint _{S}\sin \theta \,d\theta \,d\varphi ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/813a8c78d2cde1bd83ae64245c0840975dc83e88)

where ![Image 11: {\displaystyle {\hat {r}}={\vec {r}}/r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/256efb7ce45460857353617c7a5863cf2168d1dc) is the [unit vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_vector "Unit vector") corresponding to ![Image 12: {\displaystyle {\vec {r}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6aec3c9ce13b53e9e24c98e7cce4212627884c91), the [position vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Position_vector "Position vector") of an infinitesimal area of surface _dS_ with respect to point P, and where ![Image 13: {\displaystyle {\hat {n}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d125dccc556f5c8b0bf98a4f3847590b3f353bd4) represents the unit [normal vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_vector "Normal vector") to _dS_. Even if the projection on the unit sphere to the surface S is not [isomorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isomorphic "Isomorphic"), the multiple folds are correctly considered according to the surface orientation described by the sign of the [scalar product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_product "Scalar product")![Image 14: {\displaystyle {\hat {r}}\cdot {\hat {n}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/753adeed649105d37314c368b0b635b0175b6917).

Thus one can approximate the solid angle subtended by a small [facet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Facet "Facet") having flat surface area _dS_, orientation ![Image 15: {\displaystyle {\hat {n}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d125dccc556f5c8b0bf98a4f3847590b3f353bd4), and distance _r_ from the viewer as:

![Image 16: {\displaystyle d\Omega =4\pi \left({\frac {dS}{A}}\right)\,({\hat {r}}\cdot {\hat {n}}),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/40afe9d9c270c0f4ceaa0cb234d3a73d5000c796)

where the [surface area of a sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_area_of_a_sphere "Surface area of a sphere") is _A_ = 4 π _r_ 2.

Practical applications
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Solid_angle&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Practical applications")]

*   Defining [luminous intensity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luminous_intensity "Luminous intensity") and [luminance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luminance "Luminance"), and the correspondent radiometric quantities [radiant intensity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiant_intensity "Radiant intensity") and [radiance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiance "Radiance")
*   Calculating [spherical excess](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_trigonometry#Area_and_spherical_excess "Spherical trigonometry")_E_ of a [spherical triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_triangle "Spherical triangle")
*   The calculation of potentials by using the [boundary element method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boundary_element_method "Boundary element method") (BEM)
*   Evaluating the size of [ligands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ligand "Ligand") in metal complexes, see [ligand cone angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ligand_cone_angle "Ligand cone angle")
*   Calculating the [electric field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_field "Electric field") and [magnetic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_field "Magnetic field") strength around charge distributions
*   Deriving [Gauss's Law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauss%27s_Law "Gauss's Law")
*   Calculating emissive power and irradiation in heat transfer
*   Calculating cross sections in [Rutherford scattering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rutherford_scattering "Rutherford scattering")
*   Calculating cross sections in [Raman scattering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raman_scattering "Raman scattering")
*   The solid angle of the [acceptance cone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acceptance_cone "Acceptance cone") of the [optical fiber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optical_fiber "Optical fiber")
*   The computation of nodal densities in meshes.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_angle#cite_note-2)

### Cone, spherical cap, hemisphere

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Solid_angle&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Cone, spherical cap, hemisphere")]

[![Image 17](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Steradian_cone_and_cap.svg/250px-Steradian_cone_and_cap.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Steradian_cone_and_cap.svg)

Diagram showing a section through the centre of a cone (1) subtending a solid angle of 1 steradian in a sphere of radius r, along with the spherical "cap" (2). The external surface area A of the cap equals ![Image 18: {\displaystyle r^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a363a15442d031416d1eb62254a9c726e3f6c66c) only if solid angle of the cone is exactly 1 steradian. Hence, in this figure _θ_ = _A_/2 and _r_ = 1.

The solid angle of a [cone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cone_(geometry) "Cone (geometry)") with its apex at the apex of the solid angle, and with [apex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apex_(geometry) "Apex (geometry)") angle 2 _θ_, is the area of a [spherical cap](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_cap "Spherical cap") on a [unit sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_sphere "Unit sphere")

![Image 19: {\displaystyle \Omega =2\pi \left(1-\cos \theta \right)\ =4\pi \sin ^{2}{\frac {\theta }{2}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/91f481f6c05e1785cfdedb6700ce01abfc69e359)

For small _θ_ such that cos _θ_ ≈ 1 − ⁠_θ_ 2/2⁠ this reduces to π _θ_ 2 ≈ π _r_ 2, the area of a circle. (As h → 0, _θ_ → r.)

The above is found by computing the following [double integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double_integral "Double integral") using the unit [surface element in spherical coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_coordinate_system#Integration_and_differentiation_in_spherical_coordinates "Spherical coordinate system"):

![Image 20: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\int _{0}^{2\pi }\int _{0}^{\theta }\sin \theta '\,d\theta '\,d\phi &=\int _{0}^{2\pi }d\phi \int _{0}^{\theta }\sin \theta '\,d\theta '\\&=2\pi \int _{0}^{\theta }\sin \theta '\,d\theta '\\&=2\pi \left[-\cos \theta '\right]_{0}^{\theta }\\&=2\pi \left(1-\cos \theta \right).\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/553bfb7326b3102cfc2e03f81cf7b34da431296b)

This formula can also be derived without the use of [calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus "Calculus").

Over 2200 years ago [Archimedes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archimedes "Archimedes") proved that the surface area of a spherical cap is always equal to the area of a circle whose radius equals the distance from the rim of the spherical cap to the point where the cap's axis of symmetry intersects the cap.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_angle#cite_note-3)

[![Image 21](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Archimedes-spherical-cap.png/250px-Archimedes-spherical-cap.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Archimedes-spherical-cap.png)

Archimedes' theorem that surface area of the region of sphere below horizontal plane H in given diagram is equal to area of a circle of radius t.

In the above coloured diagram this radius is given as

![Image 22: {\displaystyle 2r\sin {\frac {\theta }{2}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/45476a3732d427d758c4f3f86215caf6c8eb6537) In the adjacent black & white diagram this radius is given as "t".

Hence for a unit sphere the solid angle of the spherical cap is given as

![Image 23: {\displaystyle \Omega =4\pi \sin ^{2}{\frac {\theta }{2}}=2\pi \left(1-\cos \theta \right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/74412aa3558d931cce1b1178e41e8613b8e23f80)

When _θ_ = ⁠π/2⁠, the spherical cap becomes a [hemisphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere "Sphere") having a solid angle 2 π.

The solid angle of the complement of the cone is

![Image 24: {\displaystyle 4\pi -\Omega =2\pi \left(1+\cos \theta \right)=4\pi \cos ^{2}{\frac {\theta }{2}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ee979e8fb9d6fb6b93c64f576fa8a33be8723ad7)

This is also the solid angle of the part of the [celestial sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celestial_sphere "Celestial sphere") that an astronomical observer positioned at latitude _θ_ can see as the Earth rotates. At the equator all of the celestial sphere is visible; at either pole, only one half.

The solid angle subtended by a segment of a spherical cap cut by a plane at angle _γ_ from the cone's axis and passing through the cone's apex can be calculated by the formula[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_angle#cite_note-Mazonka-4)

![Image 25: {\displaystyle \Omega =2\left[\arccos \left({\frac {\sin \gamma }{\sin \theta }}\right)-\cos \theta \arccos \left({\frac {\tan \gamma }{\tan \theta }}\right)\right].}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6801ed323a0f13ac7a1460c6549fa0b15002269d)

For example, if _γ_ = −_θ_, then the formula reduces to the spherical cap formula above: the first term becomes π, and the second π cos _θ_.

where ![Image 34: {\displaystyle \theta _{s}={\frac {\theta _{a}+\theta _{b}+\theta _{c}}{2}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8bb9b92701a270b613790400284b0d7089d56523)

Another interesting formula involves expressing the vertices as vectors in 3 dimensional space. Let ![Image 35: {\displaystyle {\vec {a}}\ ,\,{\vec {b}}\ ,\,{\vec {c}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/72f41e23bc49994564f046f8b266f685ab97ea8e) be the vector positions of the vertices A, B and C, and let a, b, and c be the magnitude of each vector (the origin-point distance). The solid angle Ω subtended by the triangular surface ABC is:[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_angle#cite_note-8)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_angle#cite_note-9)

![Image 36: {\displaystyle \tan \left({\frac {1}{2}}\Omega \right)={\frac {\left|{\vec {a}}\ {\vec {b}}\ {\vec {c}}\right|}{abc+\left({\vec {a}}\cdot {\vec {b}}\right)c+\left({\vec {a}}\cdot {\vec {c}}\right)b+\left({\vec {b}}\cdot {\vec {c}}\right)a}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3f1c5eeb500549deecf720c7ab47d513225e3e39)

where ![Image 37: {\displaystyle \left|{\vec {a}}\ {\vec {b}}\ {\vec {c}}\right|={\vec {a}}\cdot ({\vec {b}}\times {\vec {c}})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/60256bc77b0620a46ba98e799d4388aa0a56289c)

denotes the [scalar triple product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triple_product "Triple product") of the three vectors and ![Image 38: {\displaystyle {\vec {a}}\cdot {\vec {b}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b00c5274227b517684c55880c88f45414fe44936) denotes the [scalar product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_product "Scalar product").

Care must be taken here to avoid negative or incorrect solid angles. One source of potential errors is that the scalar triple product can be negative if a, b, c have the wrong [winding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Determinant "Determinant"). Computing the absolute value is a sufficient solution since no other portion of the equation depends on the winding. The other pitfall arises when the scalar triple product is positive but the divisor is negative. In this case returns a negative value that must be increased by π.

If both the side lengths (_α_ and _β_) of the base of the pyramid and the distance (_d_) from the center of the base rectangle to the apex of the pyramid (the center of the sphere) are known, then the above equation can be manipulated to give

The solid angle of an arbitrary pyramid with an _n_-sided base defined by the sequence of unit vectors representing edges {_s_ 1, _s_ 2}, ... _s_ _n_ can be efficiently computed by:[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_angle#cite_note-Mazonka-4)

![Image 42: {\displaystyle \Omega =2\pi -\arg \prod _{j=1}^{n}\left(\left(s_{j-1}s_{j}\right)\left(s_{j}s_{j+1}\right)-\left(s_{j-1}s_{j+1}\right)+i\left[s_{j-1}s_{j}s_{j+1}\right]\right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ba0474c9860232795ae0d57fb0ee32f2979b7c8b)

where parentheses (* *) is a [scalar product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_product "Scalar product") and square brackets [* * *] is a [scalar triple product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_triple_product "Scalar triple product"), and i is an [imaginary unit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imaginary_unit "Imaginary unit"). Indices are cycled: _s_ 0 = _s_ _n_ and _s_ 1 = _s_ _n_ + 1. The complex products add the phase associated with each vertex angle of the polygon. However, a multiple of ![Image 43: {\displaystyle 2\pi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/73efd1f6493490b058097060a572606d2c550a06) is lost in the branch cut of ![Image 44: {\displaystyle \arg }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ec03a9c123925f400a40064ca491d268f9312956) and must be kept track of separately. Also, the running product of complex phases must scaled occasionally to avoid underflow in the limit of nearly parallel segments.

### Latitude-longitude rectangle

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Solid_angle&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Latitude-longitude rectangle")]

The solid angle of a latitude-longitude rectangle on a [globe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Globe "Globe") is ![Image 45: {\displaystyle \left(\sin \phi _{\mathrm {N} }-\sin \phi _{\mathrm {S} }\right)\left(\theta _{\mathrm {E} }-\theta _{\mathrm {W} }\,\!\right)\;\mathrm {sr} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cf840153d418e1e33277c845f9d81a0af3fc349f) where _φ_ N and _φ_ S are north and south lines of [latitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latitude "Latitude") (measured from the [equator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equator "Equator") in [radians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radian "Radian") with angle increasing northward), and _θ_ E and _θ_ W are east and west lines of [longitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Longitude "Longitude") (where the angle in radians increases eastward).[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_angle#cite_note-10) Mathematically, this represents an arc of angle _ϕ_ N − _ϕ_ S swept around a sphere by _θ_ E − _θ_ W radians. When longitude spans 2 π radians and latitude spans π radians, the solid angle is that of a sphere.

A latitude-longitude rectangle should not be confused with the solid angle of a rectangular pyramid. All four sides of a rectangular pyramid intersect the sphere's surface in [great circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_circle "Great circle") arcs. With a latitude-longitude rectangle, only lines of longitude are great circle arcs; lines of latitude are not.

By using the definition of [angular diameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_diameter "Angular diameter"), the formula for the solid angle of a celestial object can be defined in terms of the radius of the object, ![Image 46: {\textstyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/197e66194eb64577670e2a100026bff6fb15d236), and the distance from the observer to the object, ![Image 47: {\displaystyle d}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e85ff03cbe0c7341af6b982e47e9f90d235c66ab):

![Image 48: {\displaystyle \Omega =2\pi \left(1-{\frac {\sqrt {d^{2}-R^{2}}}{d}}\right):d\geq R.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/782cd672742ad3a2e964c1d470b741e2b89136f9)

By inputting the appropriate average values for the [Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun "Sun") and the [Moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moon "Moon") (in relation to Earth), the average solid angle of the Sun is 6.794×10−5 steradians and the average solid angle of the [Moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moon "Moon") is 6.418×10−5 steradians. In terms of the total celestial sphere, the [Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun "Sun") and the [Moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moon "Moon") subtend average _fractional areas_ of 0.000 5406% (5.406[ppm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Part_per_million "Part per million")) and 0.000 5107% (5.107 ppm), respectively. As these solid angles are about the same size, the Moon can cause both total and annular solar [eclipses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_eclipse "Solar eclipse") depending on the distance between the Earth and the Moon during the eclipse.

Solid angles in arbitrary dimensions
------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Solid_angle&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Solid angles in arbitrary dimensions")]

The solid angle subtended by the complete (d − 1)-dimensional spherical surface of the unit sphere in [_d_-dimensional Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space") can be defined in any number of dimensions _d_. One often needs this solid angle factor in calculations with spherical symmetry. It is given by the formula ![Image 49: {\displaystyle \Omega _{d}={\frac {2\pi ^{\frac {d}{2}}}{\Gamma \left({\frac {d}{2}}\right)}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/016b623cebc313b2747225f50571a4046ceaed69) where Γ is the [gamma function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamma_function "Gamma function"). When _d_ is an integer, the gamma function can be computed explicitly.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_angle#cite_note-11) It follows that ![Image 50: {\displaystyle \Omega _{d}={\begin{cases}{\frac {1}{\left({\frac {d}{2}}-1\right)!}}2\pi ^{\frac {d}{2}}\ &d{\text{ even}}\\{\frac {\left({\frac {1}{2}}\left(d-1\right)\right)!}{(d-1)!}}2^{d}\pi ^{{\frac {1}{2}}(d-1)}\ &d{\text{ odd}}.\end{cases}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bbaf6b586f4b6d5cc3b1ab81e9aa071863e1eb1d)

This gives the expected results of 4 π steradians for the 3D sphere bounded by a surface of area 4π _r_ 2 and 2 π radians for the 2D circle bounded by a circumference of length 2π _r_. It also gives the slightly less obvious 2 for the 1D case, in which the origin-centered 1D "sphere" is the interval [−_r_, _r_] and this is bounded by two limiting points.

The counterpart to the vector formula in arbitrary dimension was derived by Aomoto[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_angle#cite_note-12)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_angle#cite_note-13) and independently by Ribando.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_angle#cite_note-14) It expresses them as an infinite multivariate [Taylor series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taylor_series "Taylor series"): ![Image 51: {\displaystyle \Omega =\Omega _{d}{\frac {\left|\det(V)\right|}{(4\pi )^{d/2}}}\sum _{{\vec {a}}\in \mathbb {N} _{0}^{\binom {d}{2}}}\left[{\frac {(-2)^{\sum _{i<j}a_{ij}}}{\prod _{i<j}a_{ij}!}}\prod _{i}\Gamma \left({\frac {1+\sum _{m\neq i}a_{im}}{2}}\right)\right]{\vec {\alpha }}^{\vec {a}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0fc134d3a32a327f1989c92b95bb5fc82e4c9f1e) Given d unit vectors ![Image 52: {\displaystyle {\vec {v}}_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4c1e99e843fffeae2c302f24e96edb76b2cbf915) defining the angle, let V denote the matrix formed by combining them so the i th column is ![Image 53: {\displaystyle {\vec {v}}_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4c1e99e843fffeae2c302f24e96edb76b2cbf915), and ![Image 54: {\displaystyle \alpha _{ij}={\vec {v}}_{i}\cdot {\vec {v}}_{j}=\alpha _{ji},\alpha _{ii}=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6064b350e803731788f670a974b58c27ea14fc45). The variables ![Image 55: {\displaystyle \alpha _{ij},1\leq i<j\leq d}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6cae56896d751558a6bbc2732b87795de7df7610) form a multivariable ![Image 56: {\displaystyle {\vec {\alpha }}=(\alpha _{12},\dotsc ,\alpha _{1d},\alpha _{23},\dotsc ,\alpha _{d-1,d})\in \mathbb {R} ^{\binom {d}{2}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b38f81d240d4b1ef53e31c0d607a6be7e4285e06). For a "congruent" integer multiexponent ![Image 57: {\displaystyle {\vec {a}}=(a_{12},\dotsc ,a_{1d},a_{23},\dotsc ,a_{d-1,d})\in \mathbb {N} _{0}^{\binom {d}{2}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a8b48423679647d36859f501c212335743a37206) define ![Image 58: {\textstyle {\vec {\alpha }}^{\vec {a}}=\prod \alpha _{ij}^{a_{ij}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b9f668fcdbe9ea4fb5e3cff34203ced75be7b12f). Note that here ![Image 59: {\displaystyle \mathbb {N} _{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/77ab7e98123f0def29a1cd3df96a0b7a58f4202c) = non-negative integers, or natural numbers beginning with 0. The notation ![Image 60: {\displaystyle \alpha _{ji}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/df57a9e94d996a97766949dbcfb2050b5557493a) for ![Image 61: {\displaystyle j>i}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fb45e6bd25f2486ca8b3052e74e27c11fa0d1761) means the variable ![Image 62: {\displaystyle \alpha _{ij}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/768573314617898450b38de70be02c6341de6235), similarly for the exponents ![Image 63: {\displaystyle a_{ji}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3630693f8dd03d56a80ad9b4fde40e3e14020962). Hence, the term ![Image 64: {\textstyle \sum _{m\neq l}a_{lm}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/67771520a15636ac4aae3dd9ac9560ad4dd9bdd6) means the sum over all terms in ![Image 65: {\displaystyle {\vec {a}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/546e6615827e17295718741fd0b86f639a947f16) in which l appears as either the first or second index. Where this series converges, it converges to the solid angle defined by the vectors.
