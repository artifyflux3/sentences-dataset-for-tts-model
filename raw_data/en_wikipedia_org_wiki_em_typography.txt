From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

"1Em" redirects here; not to be confused with [IEM (disambiguation)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IEM_(disambiguation) "IEM (disambiguation)") or [lem (disambiguation)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lem_(disambiguation) "Lem (disambiguation)").

Typographic unit equal to the point size

An **em** (from _[em quadrat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quad\_(typography) "Quad (typography)")_) is a [unit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Typographic_unit "Typographic unit") in the field of [typography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Typography "Typography"), equal to the currently specified [point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_(typography) "Point (typography)") size. It corresponds to the [body height](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Body_height_(typography) "Body height (typography)") of the [typeface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Typeface "Typeface").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Em_(typography)#cite_note-stokes-1) For example, one em in a 16-point typeface is 16 points. Therefore, this unit is the same for all typefaces at a given point size.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Em_(typography)#cite_note-Bringhurst1992-2)

The [em space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whitespace_character#Unicode "Whitespace character") is one _em_ wide.

Typographic measurements using this unit are frequently expressed in decimal notation (e.g., 0.7 em) or as fractions of 100 or 1000 (e.g., 70⁄100 em or 700⁄1000 em). The number of [pixels per em](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pixels_per_em "Pixels per em") varies depending on system.

History
-------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Em_(typography)&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: History")]

[![Image 18](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Metal_type.svg/250px-Metal_type.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Metal_type.svg)

A metal [sort](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sort_(typesetting) "Sort (typesetting)"). The line height, _c_, is the precursor of the em.

In [metal type](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sort_(typesetting) "Sort (typesetting)"), the point size (and hence the _em_, from _[em quadrat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Em\_quad "Em quad")_) was equal to the line height of the metal body from which the letter rises. In metal type, the physical size of a letter could not normally exceed the em.

A digital font's design space in digital type is called the em, which is a grid with arbitrary resolution. Scaling the em to a particular point size is how imaging systems—whether for screen or print—work.

In digital type, the relationship of the height of particular letters to the em is arbitrarily set by the typeface designer. However, as a very rough guideline, an "average" font might have a [cap height](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cap_height "Cap height") of 70% of the em, and an [x-height](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-height "X-height") of 48% of the em.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Em_(typography)#cite_note-Phinney2011-3)

Obsolete alternative definition
-------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Em_(typography)&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Obsolete alternative definition")]

[![Image 19](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/M_versus_em.svg/250px-M_versus_em.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:M_versus_em.svg)

The letter _M_, on the left in [Perpetua](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpetua_(typeface) "Perpetua (typeface)") and on the right in [Calisto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calisto_(typeface) "Calisto (typeface)"), inside squares of one em on each side. The _M_ in Perpetua does not match the point size of the em.

In some older texts, but not all, the em is defined, or said to have been defined, as the width of the capital 'M' in the current typeface and point size.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Em_(typography)#cite_note-adobe-4) Possibly, this is because the 'M' (or 'm') [sort](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sort_(typesetting) "Sort (typesetting)") in such cases cast the full-width of the [quad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quad_(typography) "Quad (typography)") (also known as _em quad_, _mutton quad_, or _m quadrat_); and thus, the width of the sort would equal its point size.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Em_(typography)#cite_note-mckerrow-5)

Note however, that in the oldest attested English text from 1683 mentioning em (as _m_ or [m quadrat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quad_(typography)#History "Quad (typography)")), this alternative definition is not used, and also not in many other older texts.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Em_(typography)#cite_note-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Em_(typography)#cite_note-7)

CSS
---

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Em_(typography)&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: CSS")]

In [Cascading Style Sheets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cascading_Style_Sheets "Cascading Style Sheets"), the _em_ unit is the height of the font in nominal points or inches. The actual, physical height of any given portion of the font depends on [the user-defined DPI setting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dots_per_inch#Computer_monitor_DPI_standards "Dots per inch"), current element font-size, and the particular font being used.

To make style rules that depend only on the default font size, another unit was developed: the rem. The **rem**_«rem unit»_, or **root-em**, is the font size of the root element of the document. Unlike the em, which may be different for each element, the rem is constant throughout the document.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Em_(typography)#cite_note-8)
