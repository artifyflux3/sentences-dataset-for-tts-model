[![Image 1](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/3d_two_bases_same_vector.svg/130px-3d_two_bases_same_vector.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:3d_two_bases_same_vector.svg)

The same vector can be represented in two different bases (purple and red arrows).

In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), a [set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_(mathematics) "Set (mathematics)")B of elements of a [vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space")_V_ is called a **basis** (pl.: **bases**) if every element of _V_ can be written in a unique way as a finite [linear combination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_combination "Linear combination") of elements of B. The coefficients of this linear combination are referred to as **components** or **coordinates** of the vector with respect to B. The elements of a basis are called **basis vectors**.

Equivalently, a set B is a basis if its elements are [linearly independent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linearly_independent "Linearly independent") and every element of V is a [linear combination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_combination "Linear combination") of elements of B.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basis_(linear_algebra)#cite_note-1) In other words, a basis is a linearly independent [spanning set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spanning_set "Spanning set").

A vector space can have several bases; however all the bases have the same number of elements, called the [dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension_(vector_space) "Dimension (vector space)") of the vector space.

This article deals mainly with finite-dimensional vector spaces. However, many of the principles are also valid for infinite-dimensional vector spaces.

Basis vectors find applications in the study of [crystal structures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystal_structure "Crystal structure") and [frames of reference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frame_of_reference "Frame of reference").

A **basis**_B_ of a [vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space")_V_ over a [field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)")_F_ (such as the [real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_numbers "Real numbers")**R** or the [complex numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number")**C**) is a linearly independent [subset](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subset "Subset") of _V_ that [spans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_span "Linear span")_V_. This means that a subset B of _V_ is a basis if it satisfies the two following conditions:

_linear independence_ for every [finite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_set "Finite set") subset ![Image 2: {\displaystyle \{\mathbf {v} _{1},\dotsc ,\mathbf {v} _{m}\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0ad18556ba1c09b4618b99d131f2618012465f2c) of B, if ![Image 3: {\displaystyle c_{1}\mathbf {v} _{1}+\cdots +c_{m}\mathbf {v} _{m}=\mathbf {0} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/84f5bee016da09d35f5c3d73e0f59d7073bf3cd6) for some ![Image 4: {\displaystyle c_{1},\dotsc ,c_{m}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/003be6910b8bba989a0216ea5096726e7439de4c) in _F_, then ![Image 5: {\displaystyle c_{1}=\cdots =c_{m}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3cd6594f137d7dfddc99a449c8fdfd7779538189);_spanning property_ for every vector **v** in _V_, one can choose ![Image 6: {\displaystyle a_{1},\dotsc ,a_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/74bda393afa1b6471d6a48ff74e876d1d02f4415) in _F_ and ![Image 7: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} _{1},\dotsc ,\mathbf {v} _{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/457ec28cda46a37875dbe3da6a489991b4021870) in B such that ![Image 8: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} =a_{1}\mathbf {v} _{1}+\cdots +a_{n}\mathbf {v} _{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/24b9a893609b1cdd54482caac917801308074e6a).
The [scalars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_(mathematics) "Scalar (mathematics)")![Image 9: {\displaystyle a_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0bc77764b2e74e64a63341054fa90f3e07db275f) are called the coordinates of the vector **v** with respect to the basis _B_, and by the first property they are uniquely determined.

A vector space that has a [finite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_set "Finite set") basis is called [finite-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension_(vector_space) "Dimension (vector space)"). In this case, the finite subset can be taken as _B_ itself to check for linear independence in the above definition.

It is often convenient or even necessary to have an [ordering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Total_order "Total order") on the basis vectors, for example, when discussing [orientation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orientation_(vector_space) "Orientation (vector space)"), or when one considers the scalar coefficients of a vector with respect to a basis without referring explicitly to the basis elements. In this case, the ordering is necessary for associating each coefficient to the corresponding basis element. This ordering can be done by numbering the basis elements. In order to emphasize that an order has been chosen, one speaks of an **ordered basis**, which is therefore not simply an unstructured [set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_(mathematics) "Set (mathematics)"), but a [sequence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sequence "Sequence"), an [indexed family](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indexed_family "Indexed family"), or similar; see [§Ordered bases and coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basis_(linear_algebra)#Ordered_bases_and_coordinates) below.

The blue and orange vectors are the elements of the basis; the green vector can be given in terms of the basis vectors, and so is [linearly dependent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linearly_dependent "Linearly dependent") upon them.

The set **R**2 of the [ordered pairs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordered_pair "Ordered pair") of [real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number") is a vector space under the operations of component-wise addition ![Image 11: {\displaystyle (a,b)+(c,d)=(a+c,b+d)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a427192a7477dd41aa915f664310d2189676175e) and scalar multiplication ![Image 12: {\displaystyle \lambda (a,b)=(\lambda a,\lambda b),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1ea46e36cbef12a8c67fdb027be1cf000b155e59) where ![Image 13: {\displaystyle \lambda }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b43d0ea3c9c025af1be9128e62a18fa74bedda2a) is any real number. A simple basis of this vector space consists of the two vectors **e**1 = (1, 0) and **e**2 = (0, 1). These vectors form a basis (called the [standard basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_basis "Standard basis")) because any vector **v** = (_a_, _b_) of **R**2 may be uniquely written as ![Image 14: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} =a\mathbf {e} _{1}+b\mathbf {e} _{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3702d660fbde16c5bd8125bcf12770a6b0f59b20) Any other pair of linearly independent vectors of **R**2, such as (1, 1) and (−1, 2), forms also a basis of **R**2.

More generally, if F is a [field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)"), the set ![Image 15: {\displaystyle F^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/caeaf2cf3b94c8a21721478bcd52964068489df9) of [n-tuples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tuple "Tuple") of elements of F is a vector space for similarly defined addition and scalar multiplication. Let ![Image 16: {\displaystyle \mathbf {e} _{i}=(0,\ldots ,0,1,0,\ldots ,0)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c34432fa7edbb792800be99f17ace56869f8f317) be the n-tuple with all components equal to 0, except the i th, which is 1. Then ![Image 17: {\displaystyle \mathbf {e} _{1},\ldots ,\mathbf {e} _{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8dd3ab5553c66d4056925d9ce0271000a2ad895a) is a basis of ![Image 18: {\displaystyle F^{n},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c347aed27814fbd4ec67161fec29354645c77583) which is called the _standard basis_ of ![Image 19: {\displaystyle F^{n}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/578693038b78b82ed7b58aae279a18e800ee47f1)

A different flavor of example is given by [polynomial rings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial_ring "Polynomial ring"). If F is a field, the collection _F_[_X_] of all [polynomials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial "Polynomial") in one [indeterminate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indeterminate_(variable) "Indeterminate (variable)")X with coefficients in F is an F-vector space. One basis for this space is the [monomial basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monomial_basis "Monomial basis")B, consisting of all [monomials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monomial "Monomial"): ![Image 20: {\displaystyle B=\{1,X,X^{2},\ldots \}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/80eef87c6d1f93185519d1990076994619a8d755) Any set of polynomials such that there is exactly one polynomial of each degree (such as the [Bernstein basis polynomials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bernstein_polynomial "Bernstein polynomial") or [Chebyshev polynomials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chebyshev_polynomials "Chebyshev polynomials")) is also a basis. (Such a set of polynomials is called a [polynomial sequence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial_sequence "Polynomial sequence").) But there are also many bases for _F_[_X_] that are not of this form.

Many properties of finite bases result from the [Steinitz exchange lemma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steinitz_exchange_lemma "Steinitz exchange lemma"), which states that, for any vector space V, given a finite [spanning set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spanning_set "Spanning set")S and a [linearly independent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linearly_independent "Linearly independent") set L of n elements of V, one may replace n well-chosen elements of S by the elements of L to get a spanning set containing L, having its other elements in S, and having the same number of elements as S.

Most properties resulting from the Steinitz exchange lemma remain true when there is no finite spanning set, but their proofs in the infinite case generally require the [axiom of choice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiom_of_choice "Axiom of choice") or a weaker form of it, such as the [ultrafilter lemma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultrafilter_lemma "Ultrafilter lemma").

If V is a vector space over a field F, then:

*   If L is a linearly independent subset of a spanning set _S_ ⊆ _V_, then there is a basis B such that ![Image 21: {\displaystyle L\subseteq B\subseteq S.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2ac934311145d64d062562652ad92bdc9720c824)
*   V has a basis (this is the preceding property with L being the [empty set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empty_set "Empty set"), and _S_ = _V_).
*   All bases of V have the same [cardinality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardinality "Cardinality"), which is called the [dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension_(vector_space) "Dimension (vector space)") of V. This is the [dimension theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension_theorem_for_vector_spaces "Dimension theorem for vector spaces").
*   A generating set S is a basis of V if and only if it is minimal, that is, no [proper subset](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subset "Subset") of S is also a generating set of V.
*   A linearly independent set L is a basis if and only if it is maximal, that is, it is not a proper subset of any linearly independent set.

If V is a vector space of dimension n, then:

*   A subset of V with n elements is a basis if and only if it is linearly independent.
*   A subset of V with n elements is a basis if and only if it is a spanning set of V.

Let V be a vector space of finite dimension n over a field F, and ![Image 22: {\displaystyle B=\{\mathbf {b} _{1},\ldots ,\mathbf {b} _{n}\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/335d9fcf1cc801fdb7d050b713483206e3afda95) be a basis of V. By definition of a basis, every **v** in V may be written, in a unique way, as ![Image 23: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} =\lambda _{1}\mathbf {b} _{1}+\cdots +\lambda _{n}\mathbf {b} _{n},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4a2036aebfca3efbc2dadc2030f72fdc8c113976) where the coefficients ![Image 24: {\displaystyle \lambda _{1},\ldots ,\lambda _{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a896f82d292f2489a979c7a2c7a52561df77dd4d) are scalars (that is, elements of F), which are called the _coordinates_ of **v** over B. However, if one talks of the _set_ of the coefficients, one loses the correspondence between coefficients and basis elements, and several vectors may have the same _set_ of coefficients. For example, ![Image 25: {\displaystyle 3\mathbf {b} _{1}+2\mathbf {b} _{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6bf6852546e2d9794c100c5d1ee2d3da51d9bfc3) and ![Image 26: {\displaystyle 2\mathbf {b} _{1}+3\mathbf {b} _{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0b243b51863956401503b3f323ebcef401b89753) have the same set of coefficients {2, 3}, and are different. It is therefore often convenient to work with an **ordered basis**; this is typically done by [indexing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Index_set "Index set") the basis elements by the first natural numbers. Then, the coordinates of a vector form a [sequence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sequence_(mathematics) "Sequence (mathematics)") similarly indexed, and a vector is completely characterized by the sequence of coordinates. An ordered basis, especially when used in conjunction with an [origin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Origin_(mathematics) "Origin (mathematics)"), is also called a _**coordinate frame**_ or simply a _frame_ (for example, a [Cartesian frame](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_frame "Cartesian frame") or an [affine frame](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_frame "Affine frame")).

Let, as usual, ![Image 27: {\displaystyle F^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/caeaf2cf3b94c8a21721478bcd52964068489df9) be the set of the [n-tuples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tuple "Tuple") of elements of F. This set is an F-vector space, with addition and scalar multiplication defined component-wise. The map ![Image 28: {\displaystyle \varphi :(\lambda _{1},\ldots ,\lambda _{n})\mapsto \lambda _{1}\mathbf {b} _{1}+\cdots +\lambda _{n}\mathbf {b} _{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a400b36db356268aa434ec8875213e41ccdab371) is a [linear isomorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_isomorphism "Linear isomorphism") from the vector space ![Image 29: {\displaystyle F^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/caeaf2cf3b94c8a21721478bcd52964068489df9) onto V. In other words, ![Image 30: {\displaystyle F^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/caeaf2cf3b94c8a21721478bcd52964068489df9) is the [coordinate space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_space "Coordinate space") of V, and the n-tuple ![Image 31: {\displaystyle \varphi ^{-1}(\mathbf {v} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3cb1c3345e302de8a36d2e6c042ee8f03046cfda) is the [coordinate vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_vector "Coordinate vector") of **v**.

The [inverse image](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverse_image "Inverse image") by ![Image 32: {\displaystyle \varphi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/33ee699558d09cf9d653f6351f9fda0b2f4aaa3e) of ![Image 33: {\displaystyle \mathbf {b} _{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/465522bf597a945d1099bcd61062206186528638) is the n-tuple ![Image 34: {\displaystyle \mathbf {e} _{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ba77c5a75ef9e230d1a36183785477a2eb3c5c1e) all of whose components are 0, except the i th that is 1. The ![Image 35: {\displaystyle \mathbf {e} _{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ba77c5a75ef9e230d1a36183785477a2eb3c5c1e) form an ordered basis of ![Image 36: {\displaystyle F^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/caeaf2cf3b94c8a21721478bcd52964068489df9), which is called its [standard basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_basis "Standard basis") or [canonical basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canonical_basis "Canonical basis"). The ordered basis B is the image by ![Image 37: {\displaystyle \varphi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/33ee699558d09cf9d653f6351f9fda0b2f4aaa3e) of the canonical basis of ![Image 38: {\displaystyle F^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/caeaf2cf3b94c8a21721478bcd52964068489df9).

It follows from what precedes that every ordered basis is the image by a linear isomorphism of the canonical basis of ![Image 39: {\displaystyle F^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/caeaf2cf3b94c8a21721478bcd52964068489df9), and that every linear isomorphism from ![Image 40: {\displaystyle F^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/caeaf2cf3b94c8a21721478bcd52964068489df9) onto V may be defined as the isomorphism that maps the canonical basis of ![Image 41: {\displaystyle F^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/caeaf2cf3b94c8a21721478bcd52964068489df9) onto a given ordered basis of V. In other words, it is equivalent to define an ordered basis of V, or a linear isomorphism from ![Image 42: {\displaystyle F^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/caeaf2cf3b94c8a21721478bcd52964068489df9) onto V.

Let _V_ be a vector space of dimension n over a field _F_. Given two (ordered) bases ![Image 43: {\displaystyle B_{\text{old}}=(\mathbf {v} _{1},\ldots ,\mathbf {v} _{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fb6f0e44c74ba5089939bb6f7ae580a19d9945b3) and ![Image 44: {\displaystyle B_{\text{new}}=(\mathbf {w} _{1},\ldots ,\mathbf {w} _{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3313cff7068a4ff2e50e753a7c09696980124e1e) of _V_, it is often useful to express the coordinates of a vector x with respect to ![Image 45: {\displaystyle B_{\mathrm {old} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b94f7b54590df6ba30fd78f41ad4ef91521352b0) in terms of the coordinates with respect to ![Image 46: {\displaystyle B_{\mathrm {new} }.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/717467166ce3a4e1b76aeecfb8b20b008af6e438) This can be done by the _change-of-basis formula_, that is described below. The subscripts "old" and "new" have been chosen because it is customary to refer to ![Image 47: {\displaystyle B_{\mathrm {old} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b94f7b54590df6ba30fd78f41ad4ef91521352b0) and ![Image 48: {\displaystyle B_{\mathrm {new} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2d22f1f11164e2e53c9f09e4ca2f0aaf815abae8) as the _old basis_ and the _new basis_, respectively. It is useful to describe the old coordinates in terms of the new ones, because, in general, one has [expressions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expression_(mathematics) "Expression (mathematics)") involving the old coordinates, and if one wants to obtain equivalent expressions in terms of the new coordinates; this is obtained by replacing the old coordinates by their expressions in terms of the new coordinates.

Typically, the new basis vectors are given by their coordinates over the old basis, that is, ![Image 49: {\displaystyle \mathbf {w} _{j}=\sum _{i=1}^{n}a_{i,j}\mathbf {v} _{i}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/765f139ab8f17f7a7bd2238e997b9296aa8f5a9e) If ![Image 50: {\displaystyle (x_{1},\ldots ,x_{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7935f7983d8a5ae59fea84efe65415235fa7c47b) and ![Image 51: {\displaystyle (y_{1},\ldots ,y_{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/44e34dc59edcc955dcaf524b13843536063ef6a0) are the coordinates of a vector **x** over the old and the new basis respectively, the change-of-basis formula is ![Image 52: {\displaystyle x_{i}=\sum _{j=1}^{n}a_{i,j}y_{j},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8be92986488b0d64e01d0f2f71b9bee1e2bcb438) for _i_ = 1, ..., _n_.

This formula may be concisely written in [matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_(mathematics) "Matrix (mathematics)") notation. Let A be the matrix of the ![Image 53: {\displaystyle a_{i,j}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4bb5a346f58c6568306a02596dd318d1b7e6b2c2), and ![Image 54: {\displaystyle X={\begin{bmatrix}x_{1}\\\vdots \\x_{n}\end{bmatrix}}\quad {\text{and}}\quad Y={\begin{bmatrix}y_{1}\\\vdots \\y_{n}\end{bmatrix}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3f6432dcdd623a0d797e77cbb393ae5f7b187609) be the [column vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Column_vector "Column vector") of the coordinates of **v** in the old and the new basis respectively, then the formula for changing coordinates is ![Image 55: {\displaystyle X=AY.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/88beba537b02787fb3f0b0b4bbdb078064bb55a2)

The formula can be proven by considering the decomposition of the vector **x** on the two bases: one has ![Image 56: {\displaystyle \mathbf {x} =\sum _{i=1}^{n}x_{i}\mathbf {v} _{i},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/441f6bb34ed725f449cc4d9cce20ba0aa1f1cc93) and ![Image 57: {\displaystyle \mathbf {x} =\sum _{j=1}^{n}y_{j}\mathbf {w} _{j}=\sum _{j=1}^{n}y_{j}\sum _{i=1}^{n}a_{i,j}\mathbf {v} _{i}=\sum _{i=1}^{n}{\biggl (}\sum _{j=1}^{n}a_{i,j}y_{j}{\biggr )}\mathbf {v} _{i}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6c958d9835900e8c9e91ef2e89c26993ae623d0a)

The change-of-basis formula results then from the uniqueness of the decomposition of a vector over a basis, here ![Image 58: {\displaystyle B_{\text{old}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d50aa92e9489f126c7214f2ede61abce2b1457aa); that is ![Image 59: {\displaystyle x_{i}=\sum _{j=1}^{n}a_{i,j}y_{j},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8be92986488b0d64e01d0f2f71b9bee1e2bcb438) for _i_ = 1, ..., _n_.

If one replaces the field occurring in the definition of a vector space by a [ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ring_(mathematics) "Ring (mathematics)"), one gets the definition of a [module](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Module_(mathematics) "Module (mathematics)"). For modules, [linear independence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_independence "Linear independence") and [spanning sets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spanning_set "Spanning set") are defined exactly as for vector spaces, although "[generating set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generating_set_of_a_module "Generating set of a module")" is more commonly used than that of "spanning set".

Like for vector spaces, a _basis_ of a module is a linearly independent subset that is also a generating set. A major difference with the theory of vector spaces is that not every module has a basis. A module that has a basis is called a _free module_. Free modules play a fundamental role in module theory, as they may be used for describing the structure of non-free modules through [free resolutions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_resolution "Free resolution").

A module over the integers is exactly the same thing as an [abelian group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abelian_group "Abelian group"). Thus a free module over the integers is also a free abelian group. Free abelian groups have specific properties that are not shared by modules over other rings. Specifically, every subgroup of a free abelian group is a free abelian group, and, if G is a subgroup of a finitely generated free abelian group H (that is an abelian group that has a finite basis), then there is a basis ![Image 60: {\displaystyle \mathbf {e} _{1},\ldots ,\mathbf {e} _{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8dd3ab5553c66d4056925d9ce0271000a2ad895a) of H and an integer 0 ≤ _k_ ≤ _n_ such that ![Image 61: {\displaystyle a_{1}\mathbf {e} _{1},\ldots ,a_{k}\mathbf {e} _{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7cb90cb4df7d183ad387dfad03499acbdc21bb91) is a basis of G, for some nonzero integers ![Image 62: {\displaystyle a_{1},\ldots ,a_{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fe9c0425acf7d429b00d87e9a650d8f277dfe7b0). For details, see [Free abelian group §Subgroups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_abelian_group#Subgroups "Free abelian group").

In the context of infinite-dimensional vector spaces over the real or complex numbers, the term **Hamel basis** (named after [Georg Hamel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georg_Hamel "Georg Hamel")[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basis_(linear_algebra)#cite_note-2)) or **algebraic basis** can be used to refer to a basis as defined in this article. This is to make a distinction with other notions of "basis" that exist when infinite-dimensional vector spaces are endowed with extra structure. The most important alternatives are [orthogonal bases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_basis "Orthogonal basis") on [Hilbert spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space "Hilbert space"), [Schauder bases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schauder_basis "Schauder basis"), and [Markushevich bases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Markushevich_basis "Markushevich basis") on [normed linear spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normed_linear_space "Normed linear space"). In the case of the real numbers **R** viewed as a vector space over the field **Q** of rational numbers, Hamel bases are uncountable, and have specifically the [cardinality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardinality "Cardinality") of the continuum, which is the [cardinal number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardinal_number "Cardinal number")![Image 63: {\displaystyle 2^{\aleph _{0}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/779da5db4ed54fa334dd92089cdf1c284e45febb), where ![Image 64: {\displaystyle \aleph _{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/721cd7f8c15a2e72ad162bdfa5baea8eef98aab1) ([aleph-nought](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aleph-nought "Aleph-nought")) is the smallest infinite cardinal, the cardinal of the integers.

The common feature of the other notions is that they permit the taking of infinite linear combinations of the basis vectors in order to generate the space. This, of course, requires that infinite sums are meaningfully defined on these spaces, as is the case for [topological vector spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_vector_space "Topological vector space") – a large class of vector spaces including e.g. [Hilbert spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space "Hilbert space"), [Banach spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Banach_space "Banach space"), or [Fréchet spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fr%C3%A9chet_space "Fréchet space").

The preference of other types of bases for infinite-dimensional spaces is justified by the fact that the Hamel basis becomes "too big" in Banach spaces: If _X_ is an infinite-dimensional normed vector space that is [complete](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complete_space "Complete space") (i.e. _X_ is a [Banach space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Banach_space "Banach space")), then any Hamel basis of _X_ is necessarily [uncountable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uncountable "Uncountable"). This is a consequence of the [Baire category theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baire_category_theorem "Baire category theorem"). The completeness as well as infinite dimension are crucial assumptions in the previous claim. Indeed, finite-dimensional spaces have by definition finite bases and there are infinite-dimensional (_non-complete_) normed spaces that have countable Hamel bases. Consider ![Image 65: {\displaystyle c_{00}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e27c5ab99b38e2a5159eaac0f6849f1b6ceb8130), the space of the [sequences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sequence "Sequence")![Image 66: {\displaystyle x=(x_{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/175a3c8754ea61452e450ad1b02246073c38ee3b) of real numbers that have only finitely many non-zero elements, with the norm ![Image 67: {\textstyle \|x\|=\sup _{n}|x_{n}|}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d6fbc2b1e870c034af37f2410c09dd2772b64f5a). Its [standard basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_basis "Standard basis"), consisting of the sequences having only one non-zero element, which is equal to 1, is a countable Hamel basis.

In the study of [Fourier series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourier_series "Fourier series"), one learns that the functions {1} ∪ { sin(_nx_), cos(_nx_): _n_ = 1, 2, 3, ... } are an "orthogonal basis" of the (real or complex) vector space of all (real or complex valued) functions on the interval [0, 2π] that are square-integrable on this interval, i.e., functions _f_ satisfying ![Image 68: {\displaystyle \int _{0}^{2\pi }\left|f(x)\right|^{2}\,dx<\infty .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bbdd774542bad1a0dcd834ac22b4968143a0c031)

The functions {1} ∪ { sin(_nx_), cos(_nx_): _n_ = 1, 2, 3, ... } are linearly independent, and every function _f_ that is square-integrable on [0, 2π] is an "infinite linear combination" of them, in the sense that ![Image 69: {\displaystyle \lim _{n\to \infty }\int _{0}^{2\pi }{\biggl |}a_{0}+\sum _{k=1}^{n}\left(a_{k}\cos \left(kx\right)+b_{k}\sin \left(kx\right)\right)-f(x){\biggr |}^{2}dx=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3bb7fa477762463f22fb6c29b2ac5827da76a1b)

for suitable (real or complex) coefficients _a_ _k_, _b_ _k_. But many[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basis_(linear_algebra)#cite_note-3) square-integrable functions cannot be represented as _finite_ linear combinations of these basis functions, which therefore _do not_ comprise a Hamel basis. Every Hamel basis of this space is much bigger than this merely countably infinite set of functions. Hamel bases of spaces of this kind are typically not useful, whereas [orthonormal bases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthonormal_bases "Orthonormal bases") of these spaces are essential in [Fourier analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourier_analysis "Fourier analysis").

The geometric notions of an [affine space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_space "Affine space"), [projective space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_space "Projective space"), [convex set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_set "Convex set"), and [cone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cone_(linear_algebra) "Cone (linear algebra)") have related notions of _basis_.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basis_(linear_algebra)#cite_note-4) An **affine basis** for an _n_-dimensional affine space is ![Image 70: {\displaystyle n+1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2a135e65a42f2d73cccbfc4569523996ca0036f1) points in [general linear position](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_linear_position "General linear position"). A **projective basis** is ![Image 71: {\displaystyle n+2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/aaf4207e44c00a9312fe3e8710efbefcd6eafdc0) points in general position, in a projective space of dimension _n_. A **convex basis** of a [polytope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polytope "Polytope") is the set of the vertices of its [convex hull](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_hull "Convex hull"). A **cone basis**[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basis_(linear_algebra)#cite_note-5) consists of one point by edge of a polygonal cone. See also a [Hilbert basis (linear programming)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_basis_(linear_programming) "Hilbert basis (linear programming)").

For a [probability distribution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability_distribution "Probability distribution") in **R**_n_ with a [probability density function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability_density_function "Probability density function"), such as the equidistribution in an _n_-dimensional ball with respect to Lebesgue measure, it can be shown that n randomly and independently chosen vectors will form a basis [with probability one](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/With_probability_one "With probability one"), which is due to the fact that n linearly dependent vectors **x**1, ..., **x**_n_ in **R**_n_ should satisfy the equation det[**x**1 ⋯ **x**_n_] = 0 (zero determinant of the matrix with columns **x**_i_), and the set of zeros of a non-trivial polynomial has zero measure. This observation has led to techniques for approximating random bases.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basis_(linear_algebra)#cite_note-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basis_(linear_algebra)#cite_note-GorbanTyukin2016-7)

It is difficult to check numerically the linear dependence or exact orthogonality. Therefore, the notion of ε-orthogonality is used. For [spaces with inner product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inner_product_space "Inner product space"), _x_ is ε-orthogonal to _y_ if ![Image 73: {\displaystyle \left|\left\langle x,y\right\rangle \right|/\left(\left\|x\right\|\left\|y\right\|\right)<\varepsilon }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4e568d5440d18dd8fef635db2f45d33e6aaf4c9b) (that is, cosine of the angle between x and y is less than ε).

Choose _N_ independent random vectors from a ball (they are [independent and identically distributed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Independent_and_identically_distributed_random_variables "Independent and identically distributed random variables")). Let _θ_ be a small positive number. Then for

Proof that every vector space has a basis
-----------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Basis_(linear_algebra)&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Proof that every vector space has a basis")]

Let **V** be any vector space over some field **F**. Let **X** be the set of all linearly independent subsets of **V**.

The set **X** is nonempty since the empty set is an independent subset of **V**, and it is [partially ordered](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_order "Partial order") by inclusion, which is denoted, as usual, by ⊆.

Let **Y** be a subset of **X** that is totally ordered by ⊆, and let L**Y** be the union of all the elements of **Y** (which are themselves certain subsets of **V**).

Since (**Y**, ⊆) is totally ordered, every finite subset of L**Y** is a subset of an element of **Y**, which is a linearly independent subset of **V**, and hence L**Y** is linearly independent. Thus L**Y** is an element of **X**. Therefore, L**Y** is an upper bound for **Y** in (**X**, ⊆): it is an element of **X**, that contains every element of **Y**.

As **X** is nonempty, and every totally ordered subset of (**X**, ⊆) has an upper bound in **X**, [Zorn's lemma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zorn%27s_lemma "Zorn's lemma") asserts that **X** has a maximal element. In other words, there exists some element L**max** of **X** satisfying the condition that whenever L**max** ⊆ L for some element L of **X**, then L = L**max**.

It remains to prove that L**max** is a basis of **V**. Since L**max** belongs to **X**, we already know that L**max** is a linearly independent subset of **V**.

If there were some vector **w** of **V** that is not in the span of L**max**, then **w** would not be an element of L**max** either. Let L**w** = L**max** ∪ {**w**}. This set is an element of **X**, that is, it is a linearly independent subset of **V** (because **w** is not in the span of L**max**, and L**max** is independent). As L**max** ⊆ L**w**, and L**max** ≠ L**w** (because L**w** contains the vector **w** that is not contained in L**max**), this contradicts the maximality of L**max**. Thus this shows that L**max** spans **V**.

Hence L**max** is linearly independent and spans **V**. It is thus a basis of **V**, and this proves that every vector space has a basis.

This proof relies on Zorn's lemma, which is equivalent to the [axiom of choice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiom_of_choice "Axiom of choice"). Conversely, it has been proved that if every vector space has a basis, then the axiom of choice is true.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basis_(linear_algebra)#cite_note-9) Thus the two assertions are equivalent.
