In [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_geometry "Euclidean geometry") a **quadrilateral** is a four-sided [polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon "Polygon"), having four [edges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edge_(geometry) "Edge (geometry)") (sides) and four [corners](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_(geometry) "Vertex (geometry)") (vertices). The word is derived from the Latin words _quadri_, a variant of four, and _latus_, meaning "side". It is also called a **tetragon**, derived from Greek "tetra" meaning "four" and "gon" meaning "corner" or "angle", in analogy to other polygons (e.g. [pentagon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentagon "Pentagon")). Since "gon" means "angle", it is analogously called a **quadrangle**, or 4-angle. A quadrilateral with vertices ![Image 2: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3), ![Image 3: {\displaystyle B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47136aad860d145f75f3eed3022df827cee94d7a), ![Image 4: {\displaystyle C}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4fc55753007cd3c18576f7933f6f089196732029) and ![Image 5: {\displaystyle D}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f34a0c600395e5d4345287e21fb26efd386990e6) is sometimes denoted as ![Image 6: {\displaystyle \square ABCD}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/83f54153304ec3963272dedf0701611bf6633aeb).[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-:0-1)

Quadrilaterals are either [simple](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simple_polygon "Simple polygon") (not self-intersecting), or [complex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_polygon "Complex polygon") (self-intersecting, or crossed). Simple quadrilaterals are either [convex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_polygon "Convex polygon") or [concave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concave_polygon "Concave polygon").

The [interior angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internal_and_external_angle "Internal and external angle") of a simple (and [planar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_(geometry) "Plane (geometry)")) quadrilateral _ABCD_ add up to 360 [degrees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_(angle) "Degree (angle)"), that is[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-:0-1)

![Image 7: {\displaystyle \angle A+\angle B+\angle C+\angle D=360^{\circ }.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3ea3c18f6a825eb8a3f7e2ccc2c347a7e6c977c4)
This is a special case of the _n_-gon interior angle sum formula: _S_ = (_n_ − 2) × 180° (here, n=4).[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-2)

All non-self-crossing quadrilaterals [tile the plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tessellation "Tessellation"), by repeated rotation around the midpoints of their edges.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-3)

Simple quadrilaterals
---------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Quadrilateral&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Simple quadrilaterals")]

Any quadrilateral that is not self-intersecting is a simple quadrilateral.

### Convex quadrilateral

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Quadrilateral&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Convex quadrilateral")]

[![Image 8](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Euler_diagram_of_quadrilateral_types.svg/500px-Euler_diagram_of_quadrilateral_types.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Euler_diagram_of_quadrilateral_types.svg)

[Euler diagram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler_diagram "Euler diagram") of some types of simple quadrilaterals. (UK) denotes British English and (US) denotes American English.

[![Image 9](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Symmetries_of_square.svg/330px-Symmetries_of_square.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Symmetries_of_square.svg)

Convex quadrilaterals by symmetry, represented with a [Hasse diagram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hasse_diagram "Hasse diagram")

In a convex quadrilateral all interior angles are less than 180°, and the two diagonals both lie inside the quadrilateral.

*   Irregular quadrilateral ([British English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_English "British English")) or trapezium ([North American English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_English "North American English")): no sides are parallel. (In British English, this was once called a _trapezoid_. For more, see [Trapezoid §Trapezium vs Trapezoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#Trapezium_vs_Trapezoid "Trapezoid").)
*   [Trapezium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid "Trapezoid") (UK) or [trapezoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid "Trapezoid") (US): at least one pair of opposite sides are [parallel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_(geometry) "Parallel (geometry)"). Trapezia (UK) and trapezoids (US) include parallelograms.
*   [Isosceles trapezium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isosceles_trapezium "Isosceles trapezium") (UK) or [isosceles trapezoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isosceles_trapezoid "Isosceles trapezoid") (US): one pair of opposite sides are parallel and the base [angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle "Angle") are equal in measure. Alternative definitions are a quadrilateral with an axis of symmetry bisecting one pair of opposite sides, or a trapezoid with diagonals of equal length.
*   [Parallelogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram "Parallelogram"): a quadrilateral with two pairs of parallel sides. Equivalent conditions are that opposite sides are of equal length; that opposite angles are equal; or that the diagonals bisect each other. Parallelograms include rhombi (including those rectangles called squares) and rhomboids (including those rectangles called oblongs). In other words, parallelograms include all rhombi and all rhomboids, and thus also include all rectangles.
*   [Rhombus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhombus "Rhombus"), rhomb:[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-:0-1) all four sides are of equal length (equilateral). An equivalent condition is that the diagonals perpendicularly bisect each other. Informally: "a pushed-over square" (but strictly including a square, too).
*   [Rhomboid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhomboid "Rhomboid"): a parallelogram in which adjacent sides are of unequal lengths, and some angles are [oblique](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle#Types_of_angles "Angle") (equiv., having no right angles). Informally: "a pushed-over oblong". Not all references agree; some define a rhomboid as a parallelogram that is not a rhombus.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-4)
*   [Rectangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle "Rectangle"): all four angles are right angles (equiangular). An equivalent condition is that the diagonals bisect each other, and are equal in length. Rectangles include squares and oblongs. Informally: "a box or oblong" (including a square).
*   [Square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_(geometry) "Square (geometry)") (regular quadrilateral): all four sides are of equal length (equilateral), and all four angles are right angles. An equivalent condition is that opposite sides are parallel (a square is a parallelogram), and that the diagonals perpendicularly bisect each other and are of equal length. A quadrilateral is a square if and only if it is both a rhombus and a rectangle (i.e., four equal sides and four equal angles).
*   [Oblong](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/oblong "wikt:oblong"): longer than wide, or wider than long (i.e., a rectangle that is not a square).[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-5)
*   [Kite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kite_(geometry) "Kite (geometry)"): two pairs of adjacent sides are of equal length. This implies that one diagonal divides the kite into [congruent triangles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congruent_triangles "Congruent triangles"), and so the angles between the two pairs of equal sides are equal in measure. It also implies that the diagonals are perpendicular. Kites include rhombi.

[![Image 10](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Quadrilaterals.svg/661px-Quadrilaterals.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Quadrilaterals.svg)

*   [Tangential quadrilateral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangential_quadrilateral "Tangential quadrilateral"): the four sides are tangents to an inscribed circle. A convex quadrilateral is tangential if and only if opposite sides have equal sums.
*   [Tangential trapezoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangential_trapezoid "Tangential trapezoid"): a trapezoid where the four sides are [tangents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent "Tangent") to an [inscribed circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inscribed_circle "Inscribed circle").
*   [Cyclic quadrilateral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclic_quadrilateral "Cyclic quadrilateral"): the four vertices lie on a [circumscribed circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumscribed_circle "Circumscribed circle"). A convex quadrilateral is cyclic if and only if opposite angles sum to 180°.
*   [Right kite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_kite "Right kite"): a kite with two opposite right angles. It is a type of cyclic quadrilateral.
*   [Harmonic quadrilateral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harmonic_quadrilateral "Harmonic quadrilateral"): a cyclic quadrilateral such that the products of the lengths of the opposing sides are equal.
*   [Bicentric quadrilateral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bicentric_quadrilateral "Bicentric quadrilateral"): it is both tangential and cyclic.
*   [Orthodiagonal quadrilateral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthodiagonal_quadrilateral "Orthodiagonal quadrilateral"): the diagonals cross at [right angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_angle "Right angle").
*   [Equidiagonal quadrilateral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equidiagonal_quadrilateral "Equidiagonal quadrilateral"): the diagonals are of equal length.
*   Bisect-diagonal quadrilateral: one diagonal bisects the other into equal lengths. Every dart and kite is bisect-diagonal. When both diagonals bisect another, it's a parallelogram.
*   [Ex-tangential quadrilateral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ex-tangential_quadrilateral "Ex-tangential quadrilateral"): the four extensions of the sides are tangent to an [excircle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Excircle "Excircle").
*   An _equilic quadrilateral_ has two opposite equal sides that when extended, meet at 60°.
*   A _Watt quadrilateral_ is a quadrilateral with a pair of opposite sides of equal length.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-6)
*   A _quadric quadrilateral_ is a convex quadrilateral whose four vertices all lie on the perimeter of a square.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-7)
*   A _diametric quadrilateral_ is a cyclic quadrilateral having one of its sides as a diameter of the circumcircle.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-8)
*   A _Hjelmslev quadrilateral_ is a quadrilateral with two right angles at opposite vertices.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-9)

### Concave quadrilaterals

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Quadrilateral&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Concave quadrilaterals")]

In a concave quadrilateral, one interior angle is bigger than 180°, and one of the two diagonals lies outside the quadrilateral.

*   A _dart_ (or arrowhead) is a [concave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concave_polygon "Concave polygon") quadrilateral with bilateral symmetry like a kite, but where one interior angle is reflex. See [Kite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kite_(geometry) "Kite (geometry)").

Complex quadrilaterals
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Quadrilateral&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Complex quadrilaterals")]

[![Image 11](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/DU21_facets.png/250px-DU21_facets.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:DU21_facets.png)

An antiparallelogram

A [self-intersecting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_self-intersecting_polygons "List of self-intersecting polygons") quadrilateral is called variously a **cross-quadrilateral**, **crossed quadrilateral**, **[butterfly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Butterfly "Butterfly") quadrilateral** or **[bow-tie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bow-tie "Bow-tie") quadrilateral**. In a crossed quadrilateral, the four "interior" angles on either side of the crossing (two [acute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acute_angle "Acute angle") and two [reflex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflex_angle "Reflex angle"), all on the left or all on the right as the figure is traced out) add up to 720°.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-10)

*   [Crossed trapezoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isosceles_trapezoid#Self-intersections "Isosceles trapezoid") (US) or trapezium (Commonwealth):[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-11) a crossed quadrilateral in which one pair of nonadjacent sides is parallel (like a [trapezoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid "Trapezoid")).
*   [Antiparallelogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiparallelogram "Antiparallelogram"): a crossed quadrilateral in which each pair of nonadjacent sides have equal lengths (like a [parallelogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram "Parallelogram")).
*   [Crossed rectangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crossed_rectangle "Crossed rectangle"): an antiparallelogram whose sides are two opposite sides and the two diagonals of a [rectangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle "Rectangle"), hence having one pair of parallel opposite sides.
*   [Crossed square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square#Crossed_square "Square"): a special case of a crossed rectangle where two of the sides intersect at right angles.

Special line segments
---------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Quadrilateral&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Special line segments")]

The two [diagonals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diagonal "Diagonal") of a convex quadrilateral are the [line segments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_segment "Line segment") that connect opposite vertices.

The two **bimedians** of a convex quadrilateral are the line segments that connect the midpoints of opposite sides.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-12) They intersect at the "vertex centroid" of the quadrilateral (see [§Remarkable points and lines in a convex quadrilateral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#Remarkable_points_and_lines_in_a_convex_quadrilateral) below).

The four **maltitudes** of a convex quadrilateral are the perpendiculars to a side—through the midpoint of the opposite side.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-13)

Area of a convex quadrilateral
------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Quadrilateral&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Area of a convex quadrilateral")]

There are various general formulas for the [area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area "Area")_K_ of a convex quadrilateral _ABCD_ with sides _a_ = _AB_, _b_ = _BC_, _c_ = _CD_ and _d_ = _DA_.

### Trigonometric formulas

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Quadrilateral&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Trigonometric formulas")]

The area can be expressed in trigonometric terms as[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-:1-14)

![Image 12: {\displaystyle K={\tfrac {1}{2}}pq\sin \theta ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2eb422f3244c36997bed91a3574fe0db7ce0b8f1)
where the lengths of the diagonals are _p_ and _q_ and the angle between them is _θ_.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-15) In the case of an orthodiagonal quadrilateral (e.g. rhombus, square, and kite), this formula reduces to ![Image 13: {\displaystyle K={\tfrac {pq}{2}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9f1650f1d3797c628d94e5a092ff3851b730c979) since _θ_ is 90°.

The area can be also expressed in terms of bimedians as[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-Josefsson4-16)

![Image 14: {\displaystyle K=mn\sin \varphi ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b1974d756d5a624225461818948e149c577ad29)
where the lengths of the bimedians are _m_ and _n_ and the angle between them is _φ_.

[Bretschneider's formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bretschneider%27s_formula "Bretschneider's formula")[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-17)[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-:1-14) expresses the area in terms of the sides and two opposite angles:

![Image 15: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}K&={\sqrt {(s-a)(s-b)(s-c)(s-d)-{\tfrac {1}{2}}abcd\;[1+\cos(A+C)]}}\\&={\sqrt {(s-a)(s-b)(s-c)(s-d)-abcd\,\cos ^{2}{\tfrac {1}{2}}(A+C)}}\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6195867b271ce1ef16fc42e8371f1fcf1657f0cc)
where the sides in sequence are _a_, _b_, _c_, _d_, where _s_ is the semiperimeter, and _A_ and _C_ are two (in fact, any two) opposite angles. This reduces to [Brahmagupta's formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brahmagupta%27s_formula "Brahmagupta's formula") for the area of a cyclic quadrilateral—when _A_ + _C_ = 180°.

Another area formula in terms of the sides and angles, with angle _C_ being between sides _b_ and _c_, and _A_ being between sides _a_ and _d_, is

![Image 16: {\displaystyle K={\tfrac {1}{2}}ad\sin {A}+{\tfrac {1}{2}}bc\sin {C}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/02a70bf585f6eb899d823ab9c028808f95243196)
In the case of a cyclic quadrilateral, the latter formula becomes ![Image 17: {\displaystyle K={\tfrac {1}{2}}(ad+bc)\sin {A}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d88698617b7222ea84fb512999f74d1f7bd09086)

In a parallelogram, where both pairs of opposite sides and angles are equal, this formula reduces to ![Image 18: {\displaystyle K=ab\cdot \sin {A}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/70722d5cac90f9bbfbbff084058a295f8db48638)

Alternatively, we can write the area in terms of the sides and the intersection angle _θ_ of the diagonals, as long as _θ_ is not 90°:[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-Mitchell-18)

![Image 19: {\displaystyle K={\tfrac {1}{4}}\left|\tan \theta \right|\cdot \left|a^{2}+c^{2}-b^{2}-d^{2}\right|.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/aa7a981cb95357bd5d2af46dd35cf8d616439723)
In the case of a parallelogram, the latter formula becomes ![Image 20: {\displaystyle K={\tfrac {1}{2}}\left|\tan \theta \right|\cdot \left|a^{2}-b^{2}\right|.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/24a83bc9f432b80bfc1e7690bdaf3bb525ddbd0d)

Another area formula including the sides _a_, _b_, _c_, _d_ is[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-Josefsson4-16)

![Image 21: {\displaystyle K={\tfrac {1}{2}}{\sqrt {{\bigl (}(a^{2}+c^{2})-2x^{2}{\bigr )}{\bigl (}(b^{2}+d^{2})-2x^{2}{\bigr )}}}\sin {\varphi }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3de13c339159849d2703d5675a63b2a767c045e0)
where _x_ is the distance between the midpoints of the diagonals, and _φ_ is the angle between the [bimedians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#Special_line_segments).

The last trigonometric area formula including the sides _a_, _b_, _c_, _d_ and the angle _α_ (between _a_ and _b_) is:[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-19)

![Image 22: {\displaystyle K={\tfrac {1}{2}}ab\sin {\alpha }+{\tfrac {1}{4}}{\sqrt {4c^{2}d^{2}-(c^{2}+d^{2}-a^{2}-b^{2}+2ab\cos {\alpha })^{2}}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af62c77b929550016c0e5f2172236254b38ab1b5)
which can also be used for the area of a concave quadrilateral (having the concave part opposite to angle _α_), by just changing the first sign + to -.

### Non-trigonometric formulas

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Quadrilateral&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Non-trigonometric formulas")]

The following two formulas express the area in terms of the sides _a_, _b_, _c_ and _d_, the [semiperimeter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semiperimeter#Quadrilaterals "Semiperimeter")_s_, and the diagonals _p_, _q_:

![Image 23: {\displaystyle K={\sqrt {(s-a)(s-b)(s-c)(s-d)-{\tfrac {1}{4}}(ac+bd+pq)(ac+bd-pq)}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ebcbb01d08917fc3edbaf23e36a08ba376462043)[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-20)![Image 24: {\displaystyle K={\tfrac {1}{4}}{\sqrt {4p^{2}q^{2}-\left(a^{2}+c^{2}-b^{2}-d^{2}\right)^{2}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a497c04c22e1ad983d44c4a1444520a2bda67610)[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-21)
The first reduces to Brahmagupta's formula in the cyclic quadrilateral case, since then _pq_ = _ac_ + _bd_.

The area can also be expressed in terms of the bimedians _m_, _n_ and the diagonals _p_, _q_:

![Image 25: {\displaystyle K={\tfrac {1}{2}}{\sqrt {(m+n+p)(m+n-p)(m+n+q)(m+n-q)}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fd6160ff5c0893c088302ca5e1ed1ed6dbc6234b)[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-22)![Image 26: {\displaystyle K={\tfrac {1}{2}}{\sqrt {p^{2}q^{2}-(m^{2}-n^{2})^{2}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6c2dc8282ed3d6e1fd84870cc3d34eecebc358d1)[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-Josefsson3-23): Thm. 7 
In fact, any three of the four values _m_, _n_, _p_, and _q_ suffice for determination of the area, since in any quadrilateral the four values are related by ![Image 27: {\displaystyle p^{2}+q^{2}=2(m^{2}+n^{2}).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/48ffcfca88c30dfa5f35dd88ce8c062d62bf367e)[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-Altshiller-Court-24): p. 126  The corresponding expressions are:[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-Josefsson6-25)

![Image 28: {\displaystyle K={\tfrac {1}{2}}{\sqrt {[(m+n)^{2}-p^{2}]\cdot [p^{2}-(m-n)^{2}]}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/46e6f895931ea57ab71ca3ec4d03175e3d73bef8)
if the lengths of two bimedians and one diagonal are given, and[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-Josefsson6-25)

![Image 29: {\displaystyle K={\tfrac {1}{4}}{\sqrt {[(p+q)^{2}-4m^{2}]\cdot [4m^{2}-(p-q)^{2}]}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/da2f523551c427056fa7199b6ad0d093b770d4b6)
if the lengths of two diagonals and one bimedian are given.

The area of a quadrilateral _ABCD_ can be calculated using [vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_(geometric) "Vector (geometric)"). Let vectors **AC** and **BD** form the diagonals from _A_ to _C_ and from _B_ to _D_. The area of the quadrilateral is then

![Image 30: {\displaystyle K={\tfrac {1}{2}}|\mathbf {AC} \times \mathbf {BD} |,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e48a73a24dc41afdcd2b345c0ae2a5b0b6489e56)

### Generalizations of the parallelogram law and Ptolemy's theorem

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Quadrilateral&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Generalizations of the parallelogram law and Ptolemy's theorem")]

In any convex quadrilateral _ABCD_, the sum of the squares of the four sides is equal to the sum of the squares of the two diagonals plus four times the square of the line segment connecting the midpoints of the diagonals. Thus

![Image 36: {\displaystyle a^{2}+b^{2}+c^{2}+d^{2}=p^{2}+q^{2}+4x^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/52d207f329643a75a3c3c856d040896474e262a1)
where x is the distance between the midpoints of the diagonals.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-Altshiller-Court-24): p.126  This is sometimes known as [Euler's quadrilateral theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler%27s_quadrilateral_theorem "Euler's quadrilateral theorem") and is a generalization of the [parallelogram law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram_law "Parallelogram law").

The German mathematician [Carl Anton Bretschneider](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Anton_Bretschneider "Carl Anton Bretschneider") derived in 1842 the following generalization of [Ptolemy's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ptolemy%27s_theorem "Ptolemy's theorem"), regarding the product of the diagonals in a convex quadrilateral[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-28)

![Image 37: {\displaystyle p^{2}q^{2}=a^{2}c^{2}+b^{2}d^{2}-2abcd\cos {(A+C)}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ae09985fb3f93afa18f4c32a5a0fcc36ba072351)
This relation can be considered to be a [law of cosines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law_of_cosines "Law of cosines") for a quadrilateral. In a [cyclic quadrilateral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclic_quadrilateral "Cyclic quadrilateral"), where _A_ + _C_ = 180°, it reduces to _pq_ = _ac_ + _bd_. Since cos(_A_ + _C_) ≥ −1, it also gives a proof of Ptolemy's inequality.

The shape and size of a convex quadrilateral are fully determined by the lengths of its sides in sequence and of one diagonal between two specified vertices. The two diagonals _p_, _q_ and the four side lengths _a_, _b_, _c_, _d_ of a quadrilateral are related[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-:1-14) by the [Cayley-Menger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distance_geometry#Cayley.E2.80.93Menger_determinants "Distance geometry")[determinant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Determinant "Determinant"), as follows:

The internal [angle bisectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle_bisector "Angle bisector") of a convex quadrilateral either form a [cyclic quadrilateral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclic_quadrilateral "Cyclic quadrilateral")[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-Altshiller-Court-24): p.127  (that is, the four intersection points of adjacent angle bisectors are [concyclic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concyclic_points "Concyclic points")) or they are [concurrent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concurrent_lines "Concurrent lines"). In the latter case the quadrilateral is a [tangential quadrilateral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangential_quadrilateral "Tangential quadrilateral").

In quadrilateral _ABCD_, if the [angle bisectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bisection#Of_angles "Bisection") of A and C meet on diagonal BD, then the angle bisectors of B and D meet on diagonal AC.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-31)

[![Image 41](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Varignon_theorem_convex.png/330px-Varignon_theorem_convex.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Varignon_theorem_convex.png)

The Varignon parallelogram _EFGH_

The [bimedians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#Special_line_segments) of a quadrilateral are the line segments connecting the [midpoints](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Midpoint "Midpoint") of the opposite sides. The intersection of the bimedians is the [centroid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centroid "Centroid") of the vertices of the quadrilateral.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-:1-14)

The midpoints of the sides of any quadrilateral (convex, concave or crossed) are the vertices of a [parallelogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram "Parallelogram") called the [Varignon parallelogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Varignon%27s_theorem "Varignon's theorem"). It has the following properties:

*   Each pair of opposite sides of the Varignon parallelogram are parallel to a diagonal in the original quadrilateral.
*   A side of the Varignon parallelogram is half as long as the diagonal in the original quadrilateral it is parallel to.
*   The area of the Varignon parallelogram equals half the area of the original quadrilateral. This is true in convex, concave and crossed quadrilaterals provided the area of the latter is defined to be the difference of the areas of the two triangles it is composed of.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-32)
*   The [perimeter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perimeter "Perimeter") of the Varignon parallelogram equals the sum of the diagonals of the original quadrilateral.
*   The diagonals of the Varignon parallelogram are the bimedians of the original quadrilateral.

The two bimedians in a quadrilateral and the line segment joining the midpoints of the diagonals in that quadrilateral are [concurrent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concurrent_lines "Concurrent lines") and are all bisected by their point of intersection.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-Altshiller-Court-24): p.125

In a convex quadrilateral with sides a, b, c and d, the length of the bimedian that connects the midpoints of the sides a and c is

![Image 42: {\displaystyle m={\tfrac {1}{2}}{\sqrt {-a^{2}+b^{2}-c^{2}+d^{2}+p^{2}+q^{2}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ca1cb9942329f76eb2454b706ec6c63ce8337272)
where p and q are the length of the diagonals.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-33) The length of the bimedian that connects the midpoints of the sides b and d is

![Image 43: {\displaystyle n={\tfrac {1}{2}}{\sqrt {a^{2}-b^{2}+c^{2}-d^{2}+p^{2}+q^{2}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/71beb3c187aab28cf32df989830aff9041280d6c)
Hence[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-Altshiller-Court-24): p.126

![Image 44: {\displaystyle \displaystyle p^{2}+q^{2}=2(m^{2}+n^{2}).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0f0b98b4334dd6400016e189f7a1d151ce92c1e0)
This is also a [corollary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corollary "Corollary") to the [parallelogram law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram_law "Parallelogram law") applied in the Varignon parallelogram.

The lengths of the bimedians can also be expressed in terms of two opposite sides and the distance x between the midpoints of the diagonals. This is possible when using Euler's quadrilateral theorem in the above formulas. Whence[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-Josefsson3-23)

![Image 45: {\displaystyle m={\tfrac {1}{2}}{\sqrt {2(b^{2}+d^{2})-4x^{2}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9903d87f5dc01b783a55324c992bfe6771ff37d2)
and

![Image 46: {\displaystyle n={\tfrac {1}{2}}{\sqrt {2(a^{2}+c^{2})-4x^{2}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e3e358f396a3695ce1f75f9f1c10cc5119b9d864)
Note that the two opposite sides in these formulas are not the two that the bimedian connects.

In a convex quadrilateral, there is the following [dual](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duality_(mathematics) "Duality (mathematics)") connection between the bimedians and the diagonals:[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-Josefsson-29)

*   The two bimedians have equal length [if and only if](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/If_and_only_if "If and only if") the two diagonals are [perpendicular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular "Perpendicular").
*   The two bimedians are perpendicular if and only if the two diagonals have equal length.

### Diagonals and bimedians

[Euler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leonhard_Euler "Leonhard Euler") also generalized [Ptolemy's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ptolemy%27s_theorem "Ptolemy's theorem"), which is an equality in a [cyclic quadrilateral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclic_quadrilateral "Cyclic quadrilateral"), into an inequality for a convex quadrilateral. It states that

![Image 71: {\displaystyle pq\leq ac+bd}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/270efed7debd13222f4c73bad75713339edb9476)
where there is equality [if and only if](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/If_and_only_if "If and only if") the quadrilateral is cyclic.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-Altshiller-Court-24): p.128–129  This is often called [Ptolemy's inequality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ptolemy%27s_inequality "Ptolemy's inequality").

Maximum and minimum properties
------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Quadrilateral&action=edit&section=22 "Edit section: Maximum and minimum properties")]

Among all quadrilaterals with a given [perimeter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perimeter "Perimeter"), the one with the largest area is the [square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_(geometry) "Square (geometry)"). This is called the _[isoperimetric theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isoperimetric\_inequality "Isoperimetric inequality") for quadrilaterals_. It is a direct consequence of the area inequality[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-Alsina-38): p.114

![Image 76: {\displaystyle K\leq {\tfrac {1}{16}}L^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6587904ece9061f95aaded3a026ac50ca8949edc)
where _K_ is the area of a convex quadrilateral with perimeter _L_. Equality holds [if and only if](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/If_and_only_if "If and only if") the quadrilateral is a square. The dual theorem states that of all quadrilaterals with a given area, the square has the shortest perimeter.

The quadrilateral with given side lengths that has the [maximum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxima_and_minima "Maxima and minima") area is the [cyclic quadrilateral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclic_quadrilateral "Cyclic quadrilateral").[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-Peter-43)

Of all convex quadrilaterals with given diagonals, the [orthodiagonal quadrilateral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthodiagonal_quadrilateral "Orthodiagonal quadrilateral") has the largest area.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-Alsina-38): p.119  This is a direct consequence of the fact that the area of a convex quadrilateral satisfies

![Image 77: {\displaystyle K={\tfrac {1}{2}}pq\sin {\theta }\leq {\tfrac {1}{2}}pq,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5198c0272ce42ab760d0fdd72ec64d9c84abbadf)
where _θ_ is the angle between the diagonals _p_ and _q_. Equality holds if and only if _θ_ = 90°.

If _P_ is an interior point in a convex quadrilateral _ABCD_, then

![Image 78: {\displaystyle AP+BP+CP+DP\geq AC+BD.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/52215f60c8de53fd7e51f58610f47efde1fa7b72)
From this inequality it follows that the point inside a quadrilateral that [minimizes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxima_and_minima "Maxima and minima") the sum of distances to the [vertices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_(geometry) "Vertex (geometry)") is the intersection of the diagonals. Hence that point is the [Fermat point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermat_point "Fermat point") of a convex quadrilateral.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-autogenerated1-44): p.120

Remarkable points and lines in a convex quadrilateral
-----------------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Quadrilateral&action=edit&section=23 "Edit section: Remarkable points and lines in a convex quadrilateral")]

The centre of a quadrilateral can be defined in several different ways. The "vertex centroid" comes from considering the quadrilateral as being empty but having equal masses at its vertices. The "side centroid" comes from considering the sides to have constant mass per unit length. The usual centre, called just [centroid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centroid "Centroid") (centre of area) comes from considering the surface of the quadrilateral as having constant density. These three points are in general not all the same point.[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-45)

The "vertex centroid" is the intersection of the two [bimedians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#Special_line_segments).[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-46) As with any polygon, the _x_ and _y_ coordinates of the vertex centroid are the [arithmetic means](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic_mean "Arithmetic mean") of the _x_ and _y_ coordinates of the vertices.

The "area centroid" of quadrilateral _ABCD_ can be constructed in the following way. Let _G a_, _G b_, _G c_, _G d_ be the centroids of triangles _BCD_, _ACD_, _ABD_, _ABC_ respectively. Then the "area centroid" is the intersection of the lines _G a G c_ and _G b G d_.[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-Myakishev-47)

In a general convex quadrilateral _ABCD_, there are no natural analogies to the [circumcenter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumcenter "Circumcenter") and [orthocenter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthocenter "Orthocenter") of a [triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle "Triangle"). But two such points can be constructed in the following way. Let _O a_, _O b_, _O c_, _O d_ be the circumcenters of triangles _BCD_, _ACD_, _ABD_, _ABC_ respectively; and denote by _H a_, _H b_, _H c_, _H d_ the orthocenters in the same triangles. Then the intersection of the lines _O a O c_ and _O b O d_ is called the [quasicircumcenter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumcenter_of_mass "Circumcenter of mass"), and the intersection of the lines _H a H c_ and _H b H d_ is called the _quasiorthocenter_ of the convex quadrilateral.[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-Myakishev-47) These points can be used to define an [Euler line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler_line "Euler line") of a quadrilateral. In a convex quadrilateral, the quasiorthocenter _H_, the "area centroid" _G_, and the quasicircumcenter _O_ are [collinear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collinear "Collinear") in this order, and _HG_ = 2 _GO_.[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-Myakishev-47)

There can also be defined a _quasinine-point center_ _E_ as the intersection of the lines _E a E c_ and _E b E d_, where _E a_, _E b_, _E c_, _E d_ are the [nine-point centers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nine-point_circle "Nine-point circle") of triangles _BCD_, _ACD_, _ABD_, _ABC_ respectively. Then _E_ is the [midpoint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Midpoint "Midpoint") of _OH_.[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-Myakishev-47)

Another remarkable line in a convex non-parallelogram quadrilateral is the [Newton line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton_line "Newton line"), which connects the midpoints of the diagonals, the segment connecting these points being bisected by the vertex centroid. One more interesting line (in some sense dual to the [Newton's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton_line "Newton line") one) is the line connecting the point of intersection of diagonals with the vertex centroid. The line is remarkable by the fact that it contains the (area) centroid. The vertex centroid divides the segment connecting the intersection of diagonals and the (area) centroid in the ratio 3:1.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-48)

For any quadrilateral _ABCD_ with points _P_ and _Q_ the intersections of _AD_ and _BC_ and _AB_ and _CD_, respectively, the circles _(PAB), (PCD), (QAD),_ and _(QBC)_ pass through a common point _M_, called a Miquel point.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-49)

For a convex quadrilateral _ABCD_ in which _E_ is the point of intersection of the diagonals and _F_ is the point of intersection of the extensions of sides _BC_ and _AD_, let ω be a circle through _E_ and _F_ which meets _CB_ internally at _M_ and _DA_ internally at _N_. Let _CA_ meet ω again at _L_ and let _DB_ meet ω again at _K_. Then, applying [Pascal's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pascal%27s_theorem "Pascal's theorem") to the hexagons _EKNFML_ and _EKMFNL_ inscribed in ω, there holds: the straight lines _NK_ and _ML_ intersect at point _P_ that is located on the side _AB_; the straight lines _NL_ and _KM_ intersect at point _Q_ that is located on the side _CD_. Points _P_ and _Q_ are called "Pascal points" formed by circle ω on sides _AB_ and _CD_. [[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-Fraivert-50)[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-Fraivert2-51)[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-Fraivert3-52)

Skew quadrilaterals
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Quadrilateral&action=edit&section=26 "Edit section: Skew quadrilaterals")]

[![Image 80](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Disphenoid_tetrahedron.png/250px-Disphenoid_tetrahedron.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Disphenoid_tetrahedron.png)

The (red) side edges of [tetragonal disphenoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetragonal_disphenoid "Tetragonal disphenoid") represent a regular zig-zag skew quadrilateral.

A non-planar quadrilateral is called a **skew quadrilateral**. Formulas to compute its dihedral angles from the edge lengths and the angle between two adjacent edges were derived for work on the properties of molecules such as [cyclobutane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclobutane "Cyclobutane") that contain a "puckered" ring of four atoms.[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-55) Historically the term **gauche quadrilateral** was also used to mean a skew quadrilateral.[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#cite_note-56) A skew quadrilateral together with its diagonals form a (possibly non-regular) [tetrahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron "Tetrahedron"), and conversely every skew quadrilateral comes from a tetrahedron where a pair of opposite [edges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edge_(geometry) "Edge (geometry)") is removed.
