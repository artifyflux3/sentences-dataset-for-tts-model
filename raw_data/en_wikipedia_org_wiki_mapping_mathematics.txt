From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

(Redirected from [Mapping (mathematics)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Mapping_(mathematics)&redirect=no "Mapping (mathematics)"))

Function, homomorphism, or morphism

For other uses, see [map (disambiguation)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Map_(disambiguation) "Map (disambiguation)").

[![Image 16](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Function_color_example_3.svg/250px-Function_color_example_3.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Function_color_example_3.svg)

A map is a function, as in the association of any of the four colored shapes in X to its color in Y

In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), a **map** or **mapping** is a [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)") in its general sense.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mapping_(mathematics)#cite_note-1) These terms may have originated as from the process of making a [geographical map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Map "Map"): _mapping_ the Earth surface to a sheet of paper.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mapping_(mathematics)#cite_note-:1-2)

The term _map_ may be used to distinguish some special types of functions, such as [homomorphisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homomorphism "Homomorphism"). For example, a [linear map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_map "Linear map") is a homomorphism of [vector spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space"), while the term [linear function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_function "Linear function") may have this meaning or it may mean a [linear polynomial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_polynomial "Linear polynomial").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mapping_(mathematics)#cite_note-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mapping_(mathematics)#cite_note-4) In [category theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category_theory "Category theory"), a map may refer to a [morphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morphism "Morphism").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mapping_(mathematics)#cite_note-:1-2) The term _transformation_ can be used interchangeably,[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mapping_(mathematics)#cite_note-:1-2) but _[transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transformation\_(function) "Transformation (function)")_ often refers to a function from a set to itself. There are also a few less common uses in [logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logic "Logic") and [graph theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_theory "Graph theory").

Maps as functions
-----------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Map_(mathematics)&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Maps as functions")]

Main article: [Function (mathematics)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)")

In many branches of mathematics, the term _map_ is used to mean a [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)"),[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mapping_(mathematics)#cite_note-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mapping_(mathematics)#cite_note-:0-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mapping_(mathematics)#cite_note-7) sometimes with a specific property of particular importance to that branch. For instance, a "map" is a "[continuous function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_function "Continuous function")" in [topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology "Topology"), a "[linear transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_map "Linear map")" in [linear algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_algebra "Linear algebra"), etc.

Some authors, such as [Serge Lang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Serge_Lang "Serge Lang"),[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mapping_(mathematics)#cite_note-8) use "function" only to refer to maps in which the [codomain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Codomain "Codomain") is a set of numbers (i.e. a subset of [**R**](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_numbers "Real numbers") or [**C**](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_numbers "Complex numbers")), and reserve the term _mapping_ for more general functions.

Maps of certain kinds have been given specific names. These include [homomorphisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homomorphism "Homomorphism") in [algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra "Algebra"), [isometries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isometries "Isometries") in [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry"), [operators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operator_(mathematics) "Operator (mathematics)") in [analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_analysis "Mathematical analysis") and [representations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_representation "Group representation") in [group theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_theory "Group theory").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mapping_(mathematics)#cite_note-:1-2)

In the theory of [dynamical systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynamical_system "Dynamical system"), a map denotes an [evolution function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discrete-time_dynamical_system "Discrete-time dynamical system") used to create [discrete dynamical systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynamical_system#Maps "Dynamical system").

A _partial map_ is a _[partial function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial\_function "Partial function")_. Related terminology such as _[domain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain\_of\_a\_function "Domain of a function")_, _[codomain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Codomain "Codomain")_, _[injective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Injective\_function "Injective function")_, and _[continuous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous\_function "Continuous function")_ can be applied equally to maps and functions, with the same meaning. All these usages can be applied to "maps" as general functions or as functions with special properties.

As morphisms
------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Map_(mathematics)&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: As morphisms")]

Main article: [Morphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morphism "Morphism")

In category theory, "map" is often used as a synonym for "[morphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morphism "Morphism")" or "arrow", which is a structure-respecting function and thus may imply more structure than "function" does.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mapping_(mathematics)#cite_note-9) For example, a morphism f:X→Y{\displaystyle f:\,X\to Y}![Image 17: {\displaystyle f:\,X\to Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9eb71802731dc4e3a1776064219d59b1a58dc276) in a [concrete category](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concrete_category "Concrete category") (i.e. a morphism that can be viewed as a function) carries with it the information of its domain (the source X{\displaystyle X}![Image 18: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) of the morphism) and its codomain (the target Y{\displaystyle Y}![Image 19: {\displaystyle Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/961d67d6b454b4df2301ac571808a3538b3a6d3f)). In the widely used definition of a function f:X→Y{\displaystyle f:X\to Y}![Image 20: {\displaystyle f:X\to Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/abd1e080abef4bbdab67b43819c6431e7561361c), f{\displaystyle f}![Image 21: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) is a subset of X×Y{\displaystyle X\times Y}![Image 22: {\displaystyle X\times Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1613c1ff4b6fbfb6c80a8da83e90ad28f0ab3483) consisting of all the pairs (x,f(x)){\displaystyle (x,f(x))}![Image 23: {\displaystyle (x,f(x))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b21dd0c5c5815bc0516f679f631fd588ceb458d6) for x∈X{\displaystyle x\in X}![Image 24: {\displaystyle x\in X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3e580967f68f36743e894aa7944f032dda6ea01d). In this sense, the function does not capture the set Y{\displaystyle Y}![Image 25: {\displaystyle Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/961d67d6b454b4df2301ac571808a3538b3a6d3f) that is used as the codomain; only the range f(X){\displaystyle f(X)}![Image 26: {\displaystyle f(X)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b884e2d65b3356219702968b6751485fb8f38570) is determined by the function.
