From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Corrosion of materials occurring in outer space

**Corrosion in space** is the [corrosion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corrosion "Corrosion") of materials occurring in [outer space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_space "Outer space"). Instead of [moisture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moisture "Moisture") and [oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen "Oxygen") acting as the primary corrosion causes, the materials exposed to outer space are subjected to [vacuum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vacuum "Vacuum"), bombardment by [ultraviolet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultraviolet "Ultraviolet") and [X-rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray "X-ray"), [solar energetic particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_energetic_particles "Solar energetic particles") (mostly [electrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron "Electron") and [protons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton "Proton") from [solar wind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_wind "Solar wind")), and electromagnetic radiation.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corrosion_in_space#cite_note-:0-1) In the upper layers of the atmosphere (between 90â€“800 km), the atmospheric atoms, ions, and [free radicals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radical_(chemistry) "Radical (chemistry)"), most notably [atomic oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_oxygen "Atomic oxygen"), play a major role. The concentration of atomic oxygen depends on altitude and [solar activity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_weather "Space weather"), as the bursts of ultraviolet radiation cause [photodissociation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photodissociation "Photodissociation") of molecular oxygen.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corrosion_in_space#cite_note-2) Between 160 and 560 km, the atmosphere consists of about 90% atomic oxygen.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corrosion_in_space#cite_note-3)

Materials
---------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Corrosion_in_space&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Materials")]

Corrosion in space has the highest impact on spacecraft with moving parts. Early satellites tended to develop problems with seizing bearings. Now the bearings are coated with a thin layer of [gold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gold "Gold").

Different materials resist corrosion in space differently. Electrolytes in batteries or cooling loops can cause galvanic corrosion, general corrosion, and stress corrosion.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corrosion_in_space#cite_note-:0-1)[Aluminium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aluminium "Aluminium") is slowly eroded by atomic oxygen, while [gold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gold "Gold") and [platinum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platinum "Platinum") are highly corrosion-resistant. Gold-coated foils and thin layers of gold on exposed surfaces are therefore used to protect the spacecraft from the harsh environment. Thin layers of [silicon dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_dioxide "Silicon dioxide") deposited on the surfaces can also protect metals from the effects of atomic oxygen; e.g., the [Starshine 3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Starshine_(satellite) "Starshine (satellite)") satellite aluminium front mirrors were protected that way. However, the protective layers are subject to erosion by [micrometeorites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Micrometeorites "Micrometeorites").

Silver builds up a layer of silver oxide, which tends to flake off and has no protective function; such gradual erosion of silver interconnects of solar cells was found to be the cause of some observed in-orbit failures.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corrosion_in_space#cite_note-envdeg-4)

Many [plastics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plastic "Plastic") are considerably sensitive to atomic oxygen and ionizing radiation. Coatings resistant to atomic oxygen are a common protection method, especially for plastics. [Silicone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicone "Silicone")-based [paints](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paint "Paint") and coatings are frequently employed, due to their excellent resistance to radiation and atomic oxygen.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corrosion_in_space#cite_note-5) However, the silicone durability is somewhat limited, as the surface exposed to atomic oxygen is converted to [silica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silica "Silica") which is brittle and tends to crack.

Solving corrosion
-----------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Corrosion_in_space&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Solving corrosion")]

The process of space corrosion is being actively investigated. One of the efforts aims to design a sensor based on [zinc oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zinc_oxide "Zinc oxide"), able to measure the amount of atomic oxygen in the vicinity of the spacecraft; the sensor relies on drop of electrical conductivity of zinc oxide as it absorbs further oxygen. [_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

Other problems
--------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Corrosion_in_space&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Other problems")]

The [outgassing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outgassing "Outgassing") of volatile silicones on [low Earth orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit "Low Earth orbit") devices leads to presence of a cloud of contaminants around the spacecraft. Together with atomic oxygen bombardment, this may lead to gradual deposition of thin layers of carbon-containing silicon dioxide. Their poor transparency is a concern in case of optical systems and [solar panels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photovoltaic_module "Photovoltaic module"). Deposits of up to several micrometers were observed after 10 years of service on the solar panels of the [Mir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mir "Mir") space station.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corrosion_in_space#cite_note-6)

Other sources of problems for structures subjected to outer space are erosion and redeposition of the materials by [sputtering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sputtering "Sputtering") caused by fast atoms and [micrometeoroids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Micrometeoroid "Micrometeoroid"). Another major concern, though of non-corrosive kind, is [material fatigue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Material_fatigue "Material fatigue") caused by cyclical heating and cooling and associated thermal expansion mechanical stresses.
