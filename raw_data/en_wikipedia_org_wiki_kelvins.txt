The **kelvin** (symbol: **K**) is the [base unit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SI_base_unit "SI base unit") for [temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperature "Temperature") in the [International System of Units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Units "International System of Units") (SI). The **Kelvin scale** is an [absolute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_scale "Absolute scale")[temperature scale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperature_scale "Temperature scale") that starts at the lowest possible temperature ([absolute zero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_zero "Absolute zero")), taken to be 0 K.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-MEP_for_kelvin_2019-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-SI_Brochure_9-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-BIPM_web_page_for_kelvin-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-NIST_SI_redefinition-4) By definition, the [Celsius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celsius "Celsius") scale (symbol °C) and the Kelvin scale have the exact same magnitude; that is, a rise of 1 K is equal to a rise of 1°C and vice versa, and any temperature in degrees Celsius can be converted to kelvin by adding 273.15.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-MEP_for_kelvin_2019-1)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-nist_intro-5)

The 19th century British scientist [Lord Kelvin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lord_Kelvin "Lord Kelvin") first developed and proposed the scale.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-nist_intro-5) It was often called the "absolute Celsius" scale in the early 20th century.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-absoluteC-6) The kelvin was formally added to the International System of Units in 1954, defining 273.16 K to be the [triple point of water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triple_point_of_water "Triple point of water"). The Celsius, [Fahrenheit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fahrenheit "Fahrenheit"), and [Rankine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rankine_scale "Rankine scale") scales were redefined in terms of the Kelvin scale using this definition.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-SI_Brochure_9-2)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-Busting_Myths_about_the_Metric_System-7)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-NIST_HB44_Appendix_C-8) The [2019 revision of the SI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2019_revision_of_the_SI "2019 revision of the SI") now defines the kelvin in terms of energy by setting the [Boltzmann constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boltzmann_constant "Boltzmann constant"); every 1 K change of [thermodynamic temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamic_temperature "Thermodynamic temperature") corresponds to a change in the [thermal energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermal_energy "Thermal energy"), _k_ B _T_, of _exactly_ 1.380 649×10−23[joules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joules "Joules").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-SI_Brochure_9-2)

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Melting_ice_thermometer.jpg/250px-Melting_ice_thermometer.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Melting_ice_thermometer.jpg)

An ice water bath offered a practical [calibration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calibration "Calibration") point for [thermometers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermometers "Thermometers") (shown here in Celsius) before the physical nature of heat was well understood.

During the 18th century, [multiple temperature scales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conversion_of_scales_of_temperature "Conversion of scales of temperature") were developed,[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-NIST_kelvin_history-9) notably [Fahrenheit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fahrenheit "Fahrenheit") and centigrade (later Celsius). These scales predated much of the modern science of [thermodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamics "Thermodynamics"), including [atomic theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_theory "Atomic theory") and the [kinetic theory of gases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases "Kinetic theory of gases") which underpin the concept of absolute zero. Instead, they chose defining points within the range of human experience that could be reproduced easily and with reasonable accuracy, but lacked any deep significance in thermal physics. In the case of the Celsius scale (and the long defunct [Newton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton_scale "Newton scale") and [Réaumur scales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/R%C3%A9aumur_scale "Réaumur scale")) the melting point of ice served as such a starting point, with Celsius being defined (from the [1740s](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1740s "1740s") to the [1940s](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1940s "1940s")) by calibrating a thermometer such that:

*   Water's [freezing point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freezing_point "Freezing point") is 0°C.
*   Water's [boiling point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boiling_point "Boiling point") is 100°C.

This definition assumes pure water at a specific [pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pressure "Pressure") chosen to approximate the natural air pressure at sea level. Thus, an increment of 1°C equals ⁠1/100⁠ of the temperature difference between the melting and boiling points. The same temperature interval was later used for the Kelvin scale.

From 1787 to 1802, it was determined by [Jacques Charles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacques_Charles "Jacques Charles") (unpublished), [John Dalton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Dalton "John Dalton"),[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-Dalton_1801_1-10)[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-Dalton_1801_2-11) and [Joseph Louis Gay-Lussac](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Louis_Gay-Lussac "Joseph Louis Gay-Lussac")[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-Gay-Lussac_1802-12) that, at constant pressure, ideal gases expanded or contracted their volume linearly ([Charles's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles%27s_law "Charles's law")) by about 1/273 parts per degree Celsius of temperature's change up or down, between 0°C and 100°C. Extrapolation of this law suggested that a gas cooled to about −273°C would occupy zero volume.

[Lord Kelvin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Thomson,_1st_Baron_Kelvin "William Thomson, 1st Baron Kelvin"), the namesake of the unit of measure.

#### First absolute scale

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Kelvin&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: First absolute scale")]

In 1848, William Thomson, who was later [ennobled](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nobility "Nobility") as [Lord Kelvin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lord_Kelvin "Lord Kelvin"), published a paper _On an Absolute Thermometric Scale_.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomson1882100%E2%80%93106-13) The scale proposed in the paper turned out to be unsatisfactory, but the principles and formulas upon which the scale was based were correct.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-Magie-14) For example, in a footnote, Thomson derived the value of −273°C for absolute zero by calculating the negative reciprocal of 0.00366—the [coefficient of thermal expansion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermal_expansion#Isobaric_expansion_in_ideal_gases "Thermal expansion") of an ideal gas per degree Celsius relative to the ice point.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-15) This derived value agrees with the currently accepted value of −273.15°C, allowing for the precision and uncertainty involved in the calculation.

The scale was designed on the principle that "a unit of heat descending from a body A at the temperature T° of this scale, to a body B at the temperature (T − 1)°, would give out the same mechanical effect, whatever be the number T."[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomson1882104-16) Specifically, Thomson expressed the amount of work necessary to produce a unit of #### Modern absolute scale

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Kelvin&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Modern absolute scale")]

Thomson understood that with Joule's proposed formula for ![Image 18: {\displaystyle \mu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9fd47b2a39f7a7856952afec1f1db72c67af6161), the relationship between work and heat for a perfect thermodynamic engine was simply the constant ![Image 19: {\displaystyle J}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/359e4f407b49910e02c27c2f52e87a36cd74c053).[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomson1882190formula_(7)-28) In 1854, Thomson and Joule thus formulated a second absolute scale that was more practical and convenient, agreeing with air thermometers for most purposes.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomson1882106,_232%E2%80%93236-29) Specifically, "the numerical measure of temperature shall be simply the mechanical equivalent of the thermal unit divided by Carnot's function."[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomson1882234-30)

To explain this definition, consider a reversible [Carnot cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carnot_cycle "Carnot cycle") engine, where ![Image 20: {\displaystyle Q_{\mathrm {H} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/89aab8d5e46fb046fd7f93776a0cc23f3a80017b) is the amount of heat energy transferred into the system, ![Image 21: {\displaystyle Q_{\mathrm {C} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a2c0b0806296d506d1929d6a0f62df2ee52d988d) is the heat leaving the system, ![Image 22: {\displaystyle W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54a9c4c547f4d6111f81946cad242b18298d70b7) is the work done by the system (![Image 23: {\displaystyle Q_{\mathrm {H} }-Q_{\mathrm {C} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3defdb11f688d4c956729b0383891f4a5e09f7c8)), ![Image 24: {\displaystyle t_{\mathrm {H} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d02f1ab8631d4042dd1bb5b84c20faf24b2e6e3e) is the temperature of the hot reservoir in degrees Celsius, and ![Image 25: {\displaystyle t_{\mathrm {C} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9d004fedcf510815d79eb8e6f58353c6c2f0f35e) is the temperature of the cold reservoir in Celsius. The Carnot function is defined as ![Image 26: {\displaystyle \mu =W/Q_{\mathrm {H} }/(t_{\mathrm {H} }-t_{\mathrm {C} })}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/13441ff8a1117e3e2ba298f74d3759862a8b6041), and the absolute temperature as ![Image 27: {\displaystyle T_{\mathrm {H} }=J/\mu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6f24de45d7ef0d5b0c276fe918781cb8cd5c8503). One finds the relationship ![Image 28: {\displaystyle T_{\mathrm {H} }=JQ_{\mathrm {H} }(t_{\mathrm {H} }-t_{\mathrm {C} })/W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ae79c4af9e8e576d2128fc543eab84d66672000c). By supposing ![Image 29: {\displaystyle T_{\mathrm {H} }-T_{\mathrm {C} }=J(t_{\mathrm {H} }-t_{\mathrm {C} })}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bb56fec0635fef38a4f5af39dcf9b85c9826bec1), one obtains the general principle of an absolute thermodynamic temperature scale for the Carnot engine, ![Image 30: {\displaystyle Q_{\mathrm {H} }/T_{\mathrm {H} }=Q_{\mathrm {C} }/T_{\mathrm {C} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bd88dbbcf9c41800117450bf5d0dfc225f9f0f4f). The definition can be shown to correspond to the thermometric temperature of the [ideal gas laws](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ideal_gas_law "Ideal gas law").[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-31)

This definition by itself is not sufficient. Thomson specified that the scale should have two properties:[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomson1882235-32)

*   The absolute values of two temperatures are to one another in the proportion of the heat taken in to the heat rejected in a perfect thermodynamic engine working with a source and refrigerator at the higher and lower of the temperatures respectively.
*   The difference of temperatures between the freezing- and boiling-points of water under standard atmospheric pressure shall be called 100 degrees. (The same increment as the Celsius scale) Thomson's best estimates at the time were that the temperature of freezing water was 273.7 K and the temperature of boiling water was 373.7 K.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThomson1882236-33)

These two properties would be featured in all future versions of the Kelvin scale, although it was not yet known by that name. In the early decades of the 20th century, the Kelvin scale was often called the "absolute [Celsius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celsius_scale "Celsius scale")" scale, indicating Celsius degrees counted from absolute zero rather than the freezing point of water, and using the same symbol for regular Celsius degrees, °C.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-absoluteC-6)

### Triple point standard

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Kelvin&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Triple point standard")]

[![Image 31](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Phase-diag2.svg/500px-Phase-diag2.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Phase-diag2.svg)

A typical [phase diagram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phase_diagram "Phase diagram"). The solid green line applies to most substances; the dashed green line gives the anomalous behavior of water. The boiling line (solid blue) runs from the triple point to the [critical point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Critical_point_(thermodynamics) "Critical point (thermodynamics)"), beyond which further increases in temperature and pressure produce a [supercritical fluid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supercritical_fluid "Supercritical fluid").

In 1873, William Thomson's older brother [James](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Thomson_(engineer) "James Thomson (engineer)") coined the term _[triple point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triple\_point "Triple point")_[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-34) to describe the combination of temperature and [pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pressure "Pressure") at which the solid, liquid, and gas [phases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phase_(matter) "Phase (matter)") of a substance were capable of coexisting in [thermodynamic equilibrium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamic_equilibrium "Thermodynamic equilibrium"). While any two phases could coexist along a range of temperature-pressure combinations (e.g. the [boiling point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boiling_point "Boiling point") of water can be affected quite dramatically by raising or lowering the pressure), the triple point condition for a given substance can occur only at a single pressure and only at a single temperature. By the 1940s, the triple point of water had been experimentally measured to be about 0.6% of [standard atmospheric pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_atmosphere_(unit) "Standard atmosphere (unit)") and very close to 0.01°C per the historical definition of Celsius then in use.

In 1948, the Celsius scale was recalibrated by assigning the triple point temperature of water the value of 0.01°C exactly[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-:0-35) and allowing the [melting point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Melting_point "Melting point") at standard atmospheric pressure to have an empirically determined value (and the actual melting point at ambient pressure to have a [fluctuating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Low-pressure_area "Low-pressure area") value) close to 0°C. This was justified on the grounds that the triple point was judged to give a more accurately reproducible reference temperature than the melting point.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-CGPM_9_res_3_(1948)-36) The triple point could be measured with ±0.0001°C accuracy, while the melting point just to ±0.001°C.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-:0-35)

In 1954, with absolute zero having been experimentally determined to be about −273.15°C per the definition of °C then in use, Resolution 3 of the 10th [General Conference on Weights and Measures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Conference_on_Weights_and_Measures "General Conference on Weights and Measures") (CGPM) introduced a new internationally standardized Kelvin scale which defined the triple point as exactly 273.15 + 0.01 = 273.16 degrees Kelvin.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-CGPM_10_res_3_(1954)-37)[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-res310-38)

In 1967/1968, Resolution 3 of the 13th CGPM renamed the unit increment of thermodynamic temperature "kelvin", symbol K, replacing "degree Kelvin", symbol °K.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-CGPM_13_res_3_(1967)-39)[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-res313-40)[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-Westphal_1952-41) The 13th CGPM also held in Resolution 4 that "The kelvin, unit of thermodynamic temperature, is equal to the fraction ⁠1/273.16⁠ of the thermodynamic temperature of the triple point of water."[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-NIST_SI_redefinition-4)[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-CGPM_13_res_4_(1967)-42)[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-res413-43)

After [the 1983 redefinition of the metre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metre#Speed_of_light_definition "Metre"), this left the kelvin, the second, and the kilogram as the only SI units not defined with reference to any other unit.

In 2005, noting that the triple point could be influenced by the isotopic ratio of the hydrogen and oxygen making up a water sample and that this was "now one of the major sources of the observed variability between different realizations of the water triple point", the [International Committee for Weights and Measures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Committee_for_Weights_and_Measures "International Committee for Weights and Measures") (CIPM), a committee of the CGPM, affirmed that for the purposes of delineating the temperature of the triple point of water, the definition of the kelvin would refer to water having the isotopic composition specified for [Vienna Standard Mean Ocean Water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vienna_Standard_Mean_Ocean_Water "Vienna Standard Mean Ocean Water").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-NIST_SI_redefinition-4)[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-CGPM_23_res_10_(2007)-44)[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-sib2115-45)

[![Image 32](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Unit_relations_in_the_new_SI_black_arrows_to_K.svg/330px-Unit_relations_in_the_new_SI_black_arrows_to_K.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Unit_relations_in_the_new_SI_black_arrows_to_K.svg)

2019 SI unit dependencies. The kelvin (K) is now fixed in terms of the Boltzmann constant (_k_ B) and the [joule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joule "Joule"). The joule is not shown because it is a [derived unit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SI_derived_unit "SI derived unit") defined by the metre (m), second (s), and kilogram (kg). Those SI base units are themselves defined by the [universal constants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_constant "Physical constant") of the [speed of light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light "Speed of light") (_c_), the [caesium-133 hyperfine transition frequency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium_standard "Caesium standard") (Δ _ν_ Cs) and the [Planck constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant "Planck constant") (_h_). Black arrows trace the dependencies from these constants to the kelvin.

In 2005, the [CIPM](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Committee_for_Weights_and_Measures "International Committee for Weights and Measures") began a programme to redefine the kelvin (along with other [SI base units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SI_base_units "SI base units")) using a more experimentally rigorous method. In particular, the committee proposed [redefining the kelvin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2019_revision_of_the_SI#Kelvin "2019 revision of the SI") such that the [Boltzmann constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boltzmann_constant "Boltzmann constant") (_k_ B) would take the exact value 1.380 6505×10−23 J/K.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-draft-46) The committee hoped the program would be completed in time for its adoption by the CGPM at its 2011 meeting, but at the 2011 meeting the decision was postponed to the 2014 meeting when it would be considered part of a [larger program](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2019_revision_of_the_SI "2019 revision of the SI").[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-47) A challenge was to avoid degrading the accuracy of measurements close to the triple point. The redefinition was further postponed in 2014, pending more accurate measurements of the Boltzmann constant in terms of the current definition,[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-48) but was finally adopted at the 26th CGPM in late 2018, with a value of _k_ B=1.380 649×10−23 J⋅K−1.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-physconst-k-49)[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-draft-46)[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-MEP_for_kelvin_2019-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-SI_Brochure_9-2)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-NIST_SI_redefinition-4)[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-CGPM_26_res_1_(2018)-50)

In fundamental physics, the mapping _E_ = _k_ B _T_ which converts between the characteristic microscopic energy and the macroscopic temperature scale is often simplified by using [natural units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_units "Natural units") which set the Boltzmann constant to [unity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1 "1"). This convention means that temperature and energy quantities have the same [dimensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension_(physics) "Dimension (physics)").[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-Kalinin-51)[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-52) In particular, the SI unit kelvin becomes superfluous, being defined in terms of joules as 1 K = 1.380 649×10−23 J.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-53) With this convention, temperature is always given in units of energy, and the kelvin unit is not explicitly needed in formulas.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-Kalinin-51)

For scientific purposes, the redefinition's main advantage is in allowing more accurate measurements at very low and very high temperatures, as the techniques used depend on the Boltzmann constant. Independence from any particular substance or measurement is also a philosophical advantage. The kelvin now only depends on the Boltzmann constant and [universal constants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_constant "Physical constant") (see 2019 SI unit dependencies diagram), allowing the kelvin to be expressed as:[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-SI_Brochure_9-2)

1 kelvin = ⁠1.380 649×10−23/(6.626 070 15×10−34)(9 192 631 770)⁠⁠[_h_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant "Planck constant")[Δ _ν_ Cs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium_standard "Caesium standard")/[_k_ B](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boltzmann_constant "Boltzmann constant")⁠ ≈ 2.266 6653⁠_h_ Δ _ν_ Cs/_k_ B⁠.
For practical purposes, the redefinition was unnoticed; enough digits were used for the Boltzmann constant to ensure that 273.16 K has enough [significant digits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Significant_figures "Significant figures") to contain the uncertainty of water's triple point[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-codata2017-54) and water still normally freezes at 0°C[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-55) to a high degree of precision. But before the redefinition, the triple point of water was exact and the Boltzmann constant had a measured value of 1.380 649 03(51)×10−23 J/K, with a relative standard uncertainty of 3.7×10−7.[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-codata2017-54) Afterward, the Boltzmann constant is exact and the uncertainty is transferred to the triple point of water, which is now 273.1600(1)K.[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-uncertainty-57)

The new definition officially came into force on 20 May 2019, the 144th anniversary of the [Metre Convention](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metre_Convention "Metre Convention").[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-CGPM_26_res_1_(2018)-50)[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-MEP_for_kelvin_2019-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-SI_Brochure_9-2)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-NIST_SI_redefinition-4)

[![Image 33](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Kelvin_Temperature_Chart_Vertical_tightened.svg/120px-Kelvin_Temperature_Chart_Vertical_tightened.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Kelvin_Temperature_Chart_Vertical_tightened.svg)

[Colour temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colour_temperature "Colour temperature") (right) of various light sources (left)

The kelvin is often used as a measure of the [colour temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colour_temperature "Colour temperature") of light sources. Colour temperature is based upon the principle that a [black body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_body "Black body") radiator emits light with a frequency distribution characteristic of its temperature. Black bodies at temperatures below about 4000 K appear reddish, whereas those above about 7500 K appear bluish. Colour temperature is important in the fields of image projection and photography, where a colour temperature of approximately 5600 K is required to match "daylight" film emulsions.

In [astronomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy "Astronomy"), the [stellar classification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_classification "Stellar classification") of stars and their place on the [Hertzsprung–Russell diagram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hertzsprung%E2%80%93Russell_diagram "Hertzsprung–Russell diagram") are based, in part, upon their surface temperature, known as [effective temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Effective_temperature "Effective temperature"). The photosphere of the [Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun "Sun"), for instance, has an effective temperature of 5772 K[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-58)[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-59)[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-60)[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-61) as adopted by IAU 2015 Resolution B3.

Digital cameras and photographic software often use colour temperature in K in edit and setup menus. The simple guide is that higher colour temperature produces an image with enhanced white and blue hues. The reduction in colour temperature produces an image more dominated by reddish, ["warmer" colours](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Color_theory#Warm_vs._cool_colors "Color theory").

### Kelvin as a unit of noise temperature

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Kelvin&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Kelvin as a unit of noise temperature")]

For [electronics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronics "Electronics"), the kelvin is used as an indicator of how [noisy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronic_noise "Electronic noise") a circuit is in relation to an ultimate [noise floor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noise_floor "Noise floor"), i.e. the [noise temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noise_temperature "Noise temperature"). The [Johnson–Nyquist noise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johnson%E2%80%93Nyquist_noise "Johnson–Nyquist noise") of [resistors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resistors "Resistors") (which produces an associated [_kTC_ noise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johnson%E2%80%93Nyquist_noise#Thermal_noise_on_capacitors "Johnson–Nyquist noise") when combined with [capacitors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capacitors "Capacitors")) is a type of thermal noise derived from the [Boltzmann constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boltzmann_constant "Boltzmann constant") and can be used to determine the noise temperature of a [circuit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrical_network "Electrical network") using the [Friis formulas for noise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friis_formulas_for_noise "Friis formulas for noise").

Derived units and SI multiples
------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Kelvin&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Derived units and SI multiples")]

The only [SI derived unit with a special name](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SI_derived_unit#Derived_units_with_special_names "SI derived unit") derived from the kelvin is the degree Celsius. Like other SI units, the kelvin can also be modified by adding a [metric prefix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_prefix "Metric prefix") that multiplies it by a [power of 10](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_of_10 "Power of 10"):

According to SI convention, the kelvin is never referred to nor written as a [_degree_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_(temperature) "Degree (temperature)"). The word "kelvin" is not capitalized when used as a unit. It may be in plural form as appropriate (for example, "it is 283 kelvins outside", as for "it is 50 degrees Fahrenheit" and "10 degrees Celsius").[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-plurals-62)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-nist_intro-5)[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-63)[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-64) The unit's symbol K is a capital letter,[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-CGPM_13_res_3_(1967)-39) per the SI convention to capitalize symbols of units derived from the name of a person.[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-65) It is common convention to capitalize Kelvin when referring to Lord Kelvin[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-nist_intro-5) or the Kelvin scale.[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-66)

The unit symbol K is encoded in [Unicode](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unicode "Unicode") at code point U+212A K KELVIN SIGN. However, this is a [compatibility character](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unicode_compatibility_characters "Unicode compatibility characters") provided for compatibility with legacy encodings. The Unicode standard recommends using U+004B K LATIN CAPITAL LETTER K instead; that is, a normal capital [K](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K "K"). "Three letterlike symbols have been given canonical equivalence to regular letters: U+2126 Ω OHM SIGN, U+212A K KELVIN SIGN, and U+212B Å ANGSTROM SIGN. In all three instances, the regular letter should be used."[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins#cite_note-67)

If in such a single-unit case the number is less than one, the unit is always singular when spelled out; for example, 0.5 kPa is spelled out as 'five-tenths kilopascal'.
