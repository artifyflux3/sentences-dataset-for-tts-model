In [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics"), an **electronvolt** (symbol **eV**), also written **electron-volt** and **electron volt**, is the measure of an amount of [kinetic energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_energy "Kinetic energy") gained by a single [electron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron "Electron") accelerating through an [electric potential difference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voltage "Voltage") of one [volt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volt "Volt") in [vacuum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vacuum "Vacuum"). When used as a [unit of energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Units_of_energy "Units of energy"), the numerical value of 1 eV in [joules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joule "Joule") (symbol J) is equal to the numerical value of the [charge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_charge "Electric charge") of an electron in [coulombs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coulomb "Coulomb") (symbol C). Under the [2019 revision of the SI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2019_revision_of_the_SI "2019 revision of the SI"), this sets 1 eV equal to the exact value 1.602 176 634×10−19 J.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronvolt#cite_note-physconst-eV-1)

Historically, the electronvolt was devised as a standard [unit of measure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_of_measure "Unit of measure") through its usefulness in [electrostatic particle accelerator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_accelerator#Electrostatic_particle_accelerators "Particle accelerator") sciences, because a particle with [electric charge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_charge "Electric charge")_q_ gains an energy _E_ = _qV_ after passing through a voltage of _V_.

An electronvolt is the amount of energy gained or lost by a single [electron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron "Electron") when it moves through an [electric potential difference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voltage "Voltage") of one [volt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volt "Volt"). Hence, it has a value of one [volt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volt "Volt"), which is 1 J/C, multiplied by the [elementary charge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementary_charge "Elementary charge")_e_=1.602 176 634×10−19 C.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronvolt#cite_note-physconst-e-2) Therefore, one electronvolt is equal to 1.602 176 634×10−19 J.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronvolt#cite_note-physconst-eV-1)

The electronvolt (eV) is a unit of energy, but is not an [SI unit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SI_unit "SI unit"). It is a commonly used [unit of energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_of_energy "Unit of energy") within physics, widely used in [solid state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid-state_physics "Solid-state physics"), [atomic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_physics "Atomic physics"), [nuclear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_physics "Nuclear physics") and [particle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_physics "Particle physics") physics, and [high-energy astrophysics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-energy_astronomy "High-energy astronomy"). It is commonly used with [SI prefixes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SI_prefix "SI prefix")_milli-_ (10−3), _kilo-_ (10 3), _mega-_ (10 6), _giga-_ (10 9), _tera-_ (10 12), _peta-_ (10 15), _exa-_ (10 18), _zetta-_ (10 21), _yotta-_ (10 24), _ronna-_ (10 27), or _quetta-_ (10 30), the respective symbols being meV, keV, MeV, GeV, TeV, PeV, EeV, ZeV, YeV, ReV, and QeV. The SI unit of energy is the joule (J).

In some older documents, and in the name _[Bevatron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bevatron "Bevatron")_, the symbol _BeV_ is used, where the _B_ stands for _[billion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Billion "Billion")_. The symbol _BeV_ is therefore equivalent to _GeV_, though neither is an SI unit.

In the fields of physics in which the electronvolt is used, other quantities are typically measured using units derived from it; products with fundamental constants of importance in the theory are often used.

By [mass–energy equivalence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass%E2%80%93energy_equivalence "Mass–energy equivalence"), the electronvolt corresponds to a unit of [mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass "Mass"). It is common in [particle physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_physics "Particle physics"), where units of mass and energy are often interchanged, to express mass in units of eV/_c_ 2, where _c_ is the [speed of light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light "Speed of light") in vacuum (from [_E_ = _mc_ 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass%E2%80%93energy_equivalence "Mass–energy equivalence")). It is common to informally express mass in terms of eV as a [unit of mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_of_mass "Unit of mass"), effectively using a system of [natural units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_units "Natural units") with _c_ set to 1.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronvolt#cite_note-3) The [kilogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kilogram "Kilogram") equivalent of 1 eV/_c_ 2 is:

![Image 1: {\displaystyle 1\;{\text{eV}}/c^{2}={\frac {(1.602\ 176\ 634\times 10^{-19}\,{\text{C}})\times 1\,{\text{V}}}{(299\ 792\ 458\;\mathrm {m/s} )^{2}}}=1.782\ 661\ 92\times 10^{-36}\;{\text{kg}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d521426c1c50df8608d6e7e168c878e55f4b750)

For example, an electron and a [positron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positron "Positron"), each with a mass of 0.511 MeV/_c_ 2, can [annihilate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Annihilation "Annihilation") to yield 1.022 MeV of energy. A [proton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton "Proton") has a mass of 0.938 GeV/_c_ 2. In general, the masses of all [hadrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hadron "Hadron") are of the order of 1 GeV/_c_ 2, which makes the GeV/_c_ 2 a convenient unit of mass for particle physics:[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronvolt#cite_note-4)

1 GeV/_c_ 2 = 1.782 661 92×10−27 kg.

The [atomic mass constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_mass_constant "Atomic mass constant") (_m_ u), one twelfth of the mass a carbon-12 atom, is close to the mass of a proton. To convert to electronvolt mass-equivalent, use the formula:

_m_ u = 1 Da = 931.4941 MeV/_c_ 2 = 0.931 4941 GeV/_c_ 2.

By dividing a particle's kinetic energy in electronvolts by the fundamental constant _c_ (the speed of light), one can describe the particle's [momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum "Momentum") in units of eV/_c_.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronvolt#cite_note-FNALunits-5) In natural units in which the fundamental velocity constant _c_ is numerically 1, the _c_ may informally be omitted to express momentum using the unit electronvolt.

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f7/Einstein-triangle-in-natural-units.svg/250px-Einstein-triangle-in-natural-units.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Einstein-triangle-in-natural-units.svg)

The [energy–momentum relation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy%E2%80%93momentum_relation "Energy–momentum relation") in [natural units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_units "Natural units"), ![Image 3: {\displaystyle E^{2}=p^{2}+m_{0}^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/527f323b9d0f683f723b66915c78cc05f670b110), is a [Pythagorean equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagorean_theorem "Pythagorean theorem") that can be visualized as a [right triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_triangle "Right triangle") where the total [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy "Energy")![Image 4: {\displaystyle E}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4232c9de2ee3eec0a9c0a19b15ab92daa6223f9b) is the [hypotenuse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypotenuse "Hypotenuse") and the [momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum "Momentum")![Image 5: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) and [rest mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invariant_mass "Invariant mass")![Image 6: {\displaystyle m_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3a6ff51ee949104fe6fae553cfbdfba29d5fac1e) are the two [legs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cathetus "Cathetus").

The [energy–momentum relation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy%E2%80%93momentum_relation "Energy–momentum relation")![Image 7: {\displaystyle E^{2}=p^{2}c^{2}+m_{0}^{2}c^{4}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3ff33cff7f554bf4de95c58e7a4e9b8e1f1b522c) in natural units (with ![Image 8: {\displaystyle c=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3e3467f9e219a5ea38a30da5c3a02c2c23f61a79)) ![Image 9: {\displaystyle E^{2}=p^{2}+m_{0}^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/527f323b9d0f683f723b66915c78cc05f670b110) is a [Pythagorean equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagorean_equation "Pythagorean equation"). When a relatively high energy is applied to a particle with relatively low [rest mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rest_mass "Rest mass"), it can be approximated as ![Image 10: {\displaystyle E\simeq p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8a2e9add2ddf41f349abe7bfe5ec4579e68e602a) in [high-energy physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_physics "Particle physics") such that an applied energy with expressed in the unit eV conveniently results in a numerically approximately equivalent change of momentum when expressed with the unit eV/_c_.

The dimension of momentum is T−1 L M. The dimension of energy is T−2 L 2 M. Dividing a unit of energy (such as eV) by a fundamental constant (such as the speed of light) that has the dimension of velocity (T−1 L) facilitates the required conversion for using a unit of energy to quantify momentum.

For example, if the momentum _p_ of an electron is 1 GeV/_c_, then the conversion to [MKS system of units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MKS_system_of_units "MKS system of units") can be achieved by: ![Image 11: {\displaystyle p=1\;{\text{GeV}}/c={\frac {(1\times 10^{9})\times (1.602\ 176\ 634\times 10^{-19}\;{\text{C}})\times (1\;{\text{V}})}{2.99\ 792\ 458\times 10^{8}\;{\text{m}}/{\text{s}}}}=5.344\ 286\times 10^{-19}\;{\text{kg}}{\cdot }{\text{m}}/{\text{s}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/28c52ab078b1ee53834417c06c25e39591305168)

In [particle physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_physics "Particle physics"), a system of natural units in which the speed of light in vacuum _c_ and the [reduced Planck constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant "Planck constant")_ħ_ are dimensionless and equal to unity is widely used: _c_ = _ħ_ = 1. In these units, both distances and times are expressed in inverse energy units (while energy and mass are expressed in the same units, see [mass–energy equivalence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass%E2%80%93energy_equivalence "Mass–energy equivalence")). In particular, particle [scattering lengths](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scattering_length "Scattering length") are often presented using a unit of inverse particle mass.

Outside this system of units, the conversion factors between electronvolt, second, and nanometer are the following: ![Image 12: {\displaystyle \hbar =1.054\ 571\ 817\ 646\times 10^{-34}\ \mathrm {J{\cdot }s} =6.582\ 119\ 569\ 509\times 10^{-16}\ \mathrm {eV{\cdot }s} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/63f5f1b4b732f6dd8bab435640b1c54762075421)

The above relations also allow expressing the [mean lifetime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mean_lifetime "Mean lifetime")_τ_ of an unstable particle (in seconds) in terms of its [decay width](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decay_width "Decay width") Γ (in eV) via Γ = _ħ_/_τ_. For example, the [B 0 meson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/B_meson "B meson") has a lifetime of 1.530(9)[picoseconds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Picosecond "Picosecond"), mean decay length is _cτ_ = 459.7 μm, or a decay width of 4.302(25)×10−4 eV.

Conversely, the tiny meson mass differences responsible for [meson oscillations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutral_particle_oscillation "Neutral particle oscillation") are often expressed in the more convenient inverse picoseconds.

Energy in electronvolts is sometimes expressed through the wavelength of light with photons of the same energy: ![Image 13: {\displaystyle {\frac {1\;{\text{eV}}}{hc}}={\frac {1.602\ 176\ 634\times 10^{-19}\;{\text{J}}}{(6.62\ 607\ 015\times 10^{-34}\;{\text{J}}{\cdot }{\text{s}})\times (2.99\ 792\ 458\times 10^{11}\;{\text{mm}}/{\text{s}})}}\thickapprox 806.55439\;{\text{mm}}^{-1}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e646ae54f4274f507eac9dbdf72406953d31c054)

In certain fields, such as [plasma physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasma_physics "Plasma physics"), it is convenient to use the electronvolt to express temperature. The electronvolt is divided by the [Boltzmann constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boltzmann_constant "Boltzmann constant") to convert to the [Kelvin scale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvin_scale "Kelvin scale"): ![Image 14: {\displaystyle {1\,\mathrm {eV} /k_{\text{B}}}={1.602\ 176\ 634\times 10^{-19}{\text{ J}} \over 1.380\ 649\times 10^{-23}{\text{ J/K}}}=11\ 604.518\ 12{\text{ K}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c374ea306dafd70c6a284daf8f4e8c37b1e24a5b) where _k_ B is the [Boltzmann constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boltzmann_constant "Boltzmann constant").

The _k_ B is assumed when using the electronvolt to express temperature, for example, a typical [magnetic confinement fusion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_confinement_fusion "Magnetic confinement fusion") plasma is 15 keV (kiloelectronvolt), which corresponds to 174 MK (megakelvin).

As an approximation: at a temperature of _T_ = 20°C, _k_ B _T_ is about 0.025 eV (≈ ⁠290 K/11604 K/eV⁠).

The energy _E_, frequency _ν_, and wavelength _λ_ of a photon are related by ![Image 17: {\displaystyle E=h\nu ={\frac {hc}{\lambda }}={\frac {\mathrm {4.135\ 667\ 696\times 10^{-15}\;eV/Hz} \times \mathrm {299\,792\,458\;m/s} }{\lambda }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/40cd13fc0f79bdeeb58feb79cf4f37dfc2ae95fe) where _h_ is the [Planck constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant "Planck constant"), _c_ is the [speed of light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light "Speed of light"). This reduces to[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronvolt#cite_note-physconst-heV-6)![Image 18: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}E&=4.135\ 667\ 696\times 10^{-15}\;\mathrm {eV/Hz} \times \nu \\[4pt]&={\frac {1\ 239.841\ 98\;\mathrm {eV{\cdot }nm} }{\lambda }}.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/00656cf6edf1e4f4d8a4169e26b71a5ffc3d8e74) A photon with a wavelength of 532 nm (green light) would have an energy of approximately 2.33 eV. Similarly, 1 eV would correspond to an infrared photon of wavelength 1240 nm or frequency 241.8 THz.

Scattering experiments
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Electronvolt&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Scattering experiments")]

In a low-energy nuclear scattering experiment, it is conventional to refer to the nuclear recoil energy in units of eVr, keVr, etc. This distinguishes the nuclear recoil energy from the "electron equivalent" recoil energy (eVee, keVee, etc.) measured by [scintillation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scintillation_(physics) "Scintillation (physics)") light. For example, the yield of a [phototube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phototube "Phototube") is measured in phe/keVee ([photoelectrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectron "Photoelectron") per keV electron-equivalent energy). The relationship between eV, eVr, and eVee depends on the medium the scattering takes place in, and must be established empirically for each material.

**Photon frequency vs. energy particle in electronvolts**. The [energy of a photon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon_energy "Photon energy") varies only with the frequency of the photon, related by the speed of light.
