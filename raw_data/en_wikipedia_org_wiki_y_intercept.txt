Graph ![Image 2: {\displaystyle y=f(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2311a6a75c54b0ea085a381ba472c31d59321514) with the ![Image 3: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4)-axis as the horizontal axis and the ![Image 4: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d)-axis as the vertical axis. The ![Image 5: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d)-intercept of ![Image 6: {\displaystyle f(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/202945cce41ecebb6f643f31d119c514bec7a074) is indicated by the red dot at ![Image 7: {\displaystyle (x=0,y=1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/72a0eb87fe3dcb1e6ac4d681ea55392073171335).

In [analytic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytic_geometry "Analytic geometry"), using the common convention that the horizontal axis represents a variable ![Image 8: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) and the vertical axis represents a variable ![Image 9: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d), a **![Image 10: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d)-intercept** or **vertical intercept** is a point where the [graph of a function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_of_a_function "Graph of a function") or [relation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relation_(mathematics) "Relation (mathematics)") intersects the ![Image 11: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d)-axis of the [coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_system "Coordinate system").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Y-intercept#cite_note-1) As such, these points satisfy ![Image 12: {\displaystyle x=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/953917eaf52f2e1baad54c8c9e3d6f9bb3710cdc).

If the curve in question is given as ![Image 13: {\displaystyle y=f(x),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c9867a6ecb3cc19e19e0af39fb46523e69e616c1) the ![Image 14: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d)-coordinate of the ![Image 15: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d)-intercept is found by calculating ![Image 16: {\displaystyle f(0)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bc54adf96acf7e2466de00b7739144d36840108f). Functions which are undefined at ![Image 17: {\displaystyle x=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/953917eaf52f2e1baad54c8c9e3d6f9bb3710cdc) have no ![Image 18: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d)-intercept.

If the function is [linear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_function "Linear function") and is expressed in [slope-intercept form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slope-intercept_form "Slope-intercept form") as ![Image 19: {\displaystyle f(x)=a+bx}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a69108540d50b00d90abcd7c1009a2458047a50e), the constant term ![Image 20: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc) is the ![Image 21: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d)-coordinate of the ![Image 22: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d)-intercept.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Y-intercept#cite_note-2)

Multiple y-intercepts
---------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Y-intercept&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Multiple y-intercepts")]

Some 2-dimensional mathematical relationships such as [circles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle "Circle"), [ellipses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse "Ellipse"), and [hyperbolas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbola "Hyperbola") can have more than one ![Image 23: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d)-intercept. Because [functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)") associate ![Image 24: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4)-values to no more than one ![Image 25: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d)-value as part of their definition, they can have at most one ![Image 26: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d)-intercept.

Analogously, an [![Image 27: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4)-intercept](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-intercept "X-intercept") is a point where the [graph of a function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_of_a_function "Graph of a function") or [relation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relation_(mathematics) "Relation (mathematics)") intersects with the ![Image 28: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4)-axis. As such, these points satisfy ![Image 29: {\displaystyle y=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/094f824655138f6b11d96a0da32e7f0716ba6959). The zeros, or roots, of such a function or relation are the ![Image 30: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4)-coordinates of these ![Image 31: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4)-intercepts.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Y-intercept#cite_note-3)

Functions of the form ![Image 32: {\displaystyle y=f(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2311a6a75c54b0ea085a381ba472c31d59321514) have at most one ![Image 33: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d)-intercept, but may contain multiple ![Image 34: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4)-intercepts. The ![Image 35: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4)-intercepts of functions, if any exist, are often more difficult to locate than the ![Image 36: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d)-intercept, as finding the ![Image 37: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d)-intercept involves simply evaluating the function at ![Image 38: {\displaystyle x=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/953917eaf52f2e1baad54c8c9e3d6f9bb3710cdc).

In higher dimensions
--------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Y-intercept&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: In higher dimensions")]

The notion may be extended for 3-dimensional space and higher dimensions, as well as for other coordinate axes, possibly with other names. For example, one may speak of the ![Image 39: {\displaystyle I}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/535ea7fc4134a31cbe2251d9d3511374bc41be9f)-intercept of the [current–voltage characteristic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Current%E2%80%93voltage_characteristic "Current–voltage characteristic") of, say, a [diode](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diode "Diode"). (In [electrical engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrical_engineering "Electrical engineering"), ![Image 40: {\displaystyle I}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/535ea7fc4134a31cbe2251d9d3511374bc41be9f) is the symbol used for [electric current](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Current_(electricity) "Current (electricity)").)
