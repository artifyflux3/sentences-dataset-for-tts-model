The name cirrus fibratus is derived from Latin , meaning "fibrous".

[ 3 ] These clouds are similar to cirrus uncinus , commonly known as "mares' tails," yet different in that fibratus clouds do not have tufts or hooks at the end.

[ 4 ] The filaments are usually separate from one another.

[ 5 ] Like other cirrus clouds , cirrus fibratus occurs at high altitudes . They can indicate an approaching warm front [ 6 ] and can also be an indication that fair weather will follow.
