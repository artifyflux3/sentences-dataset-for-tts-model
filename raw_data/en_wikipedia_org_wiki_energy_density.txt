In [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics"), **energy density** is the quotient between the amount of [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy "Energy") stored in a given system or contained in a given region of space and the [volume](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume "Volume") of the system or region considered. Often only the _useful_ or extractable energy is measured. It is sometimes confused with stored energy per unit [mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass "Mass"), which is called _[specific energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Specific\_energy "Specific energy")_ or _gravimetric energy density_.

There are different types of energy stored, corresponding to a particular type of reaction. In order of the typical magnitude of the energy stored, examples of reactions are: [nuclear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power "Nuclear power"), [chemical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_energy "Chemical energy") (including [electrochemical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrochemistry "Electrochemistry")), [electrical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrical "Electrical"), [pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pressure "Pressure"), [material deformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deformation_(engineering) "Deformation (engineering)") or in [electromagnetic fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_field "Electromagnetic field"). [Nuclear reactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_reaction "Nuclear reaction") take place in stars and nuclear power plants, both of which derive energy from the binding energy of nuclei. [Chemical reactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_reaction "Chemical reaction") are used by organisms to derive energy from food and by automobiles from the [combustion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Combustion "Combustion") of gasoline. Liquid hydrocarbons (fuels such as gasoline, diesel and kerosene) are today the densest way known to economically store and transport chemical energy at a large scale (1 kg of diesel fuel burns with the oxygen contained in ≈15 kg of air). Burning local [biomass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biomass "Biomass") fuels supplies household energy needs ([cooking fires](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Improved_cookstove "Improved cookstove"), [oil lamps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oil_lamp "Oil lamp"), etc.) worldwide. [Electrochemical reactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrochemical_reaction "Electrochemical reaction") are used by devices such as laptop computers and mobile phones to release energy from batteries.

Energy per unit volume has the same physical units as pressure, and in many situations is [synonymous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synonym "Synonym"). For example, the energy density of a magnetic field may be expressed as and behaves like a physical pressure. The energy required to compress a gas to a certain volume may be determined by multiplying the difference between the gas pressure and the external pressure by the change in volume. A [pressure gradient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pressure_gradient "Pressure gradient") describes the [potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potential_energy "Potential energy") to perform [work](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Work_(physics) "Work (physics)") on the surroundings by converting [internal energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internal_energy "Internal energy") to work until equilibrium is reached.

In [cosmological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_cosmology "Physical cosmology") and other contexts in [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity"), the energy densities considered relate to the elements of the [stress–energy tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stress%E2%80%93energy_tensor "Stress–energy tensor") and therefore do include the [rest mass energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rest_mass_energy "Rest mass energy") as well as energy densities associated with [pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pressure "Pressure").

When discussing the chemical energy contained, there are different types which can be quantified depending on the intended purpose. One is the theoretical total amount of [thermodynamic work](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamic_work "Thermodynamic work") that can be derived from a system, at a given temperature and pressure imposed by the surroundings, called [exergy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exergy "Exergy"). Another is the theoretical amount of electrical energy that can be derived from [reactants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reactant "Reactant") that are at room temperature and atmospheric pressure. This is given by the change in standard [Gibbs free energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gibbs_free_energy "Gibbs free energy"). But as a source of [heat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat "Heat") or for use in a [heat engine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat_engine "Heat engine"), the relevant quantity is the change in standard [enthalpy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enthalpy "Enthalpy") or the [heat of combustion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat_of_combustion "Heat of combustion").

There are two kinds of heat of combustion:

*   The higher value (HHV), or gross heat of combustion, includes all the heat released as the products cool to room temperature and whatever water vapor is present condenses.
*   The lower value (LHV), or net heat of combustion, does not include the heat which could be released by condensing water vapor, and may not include the heat released on cooling all the way down to room temperature.

A convenient table of HHV and LHV of some fuels can be found in the references.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_density#cite_note-engineeringtoolbox-1)

### In energy storage and fuels

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Energy_density&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: In energy storage and fuels")]

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Energy_density.svg/500px-Energy_density.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Energy_density.svg)

Selected energy densities plot[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_density#cite_note-tzar2-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_density#cite_note-tzar3-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_density#cite_note-tzar4-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_density#cite_note-fisher-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_density#cite_note-tzar6-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_density#cite_note-tzar7-7)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_density#cite_note-tzar8-8)

For [energy storage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_storage "Energy storage"), the energy density relates the stored [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy "Energy") to the volume of the storage equipment, e.g. the [fuel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fuel "Fuel") tank. The higher the energy density of the fuel, the more energy may be stored or transported for the same amount of volume. The energy of a fuel per unit mass is called its [specific energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Specific_energy "Specific energy").

The adjacent figure shows the [gravimetric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravimetric "Gravimetric") and [volumetric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volumetric "Volumetric") energy density of some fuels and storage technologies (modified from the [Gasoline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gasoline "Gasoline") article). Some values may not be precise because of [isomers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isomers "Isomers") or other irregularities. The [heating values](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heating_value "Heating value") of the fuel describe their specific energies more comprehensively.

The density values for chemical fuels do not include the weight of the oxygen required for combustion. The [atomic weights](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_weight "Atomic weight") of carbon and oxygen are similar, while hydrogen is much lighter. Figures are presented in this way for those fuels where in practice air would only be drawn in locally to the burner. This explains the apparently lower energy density of materials that contain their own oxidizer (such as gunpowder and TNT), where the mass of the oxidizer in effect adds weight, and absorbs some of the energy of combustion to dissociate and liberate oxygen to continue the reaction. This also explains some apparent anomalies, such as the energy density of a sandwich appearing to be higher than that of a stick of dynamite.

Given the high energy density of gasoline, the exploration of alternative media to store the energy of powering a car, such as hydrogen or battery, is strongly limited by the energy density of the alternative medium. The same mass of lithium-ion storage, for example, would result in a car with only 2% the range of its gasoline counterpart. If sacrificing the range is undesirable, much more storage volume is necessary. Alternative options are discussed for energy storage to increase energy density and decrease charging time, such as [supercapacitors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supercapacitor "Supercapacitor").[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_density#cite_note-9)[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_density#cite_note-10)[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_density#cite_note-11)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_density#cite_note-12)

No single energy storage method boasts the best in [specific power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power-to-weight_ratio "Power-to-weight ratio"), [specific energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Specific_energy "Specific energy"), and energy density. [Peukert's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peukert%27s_law "Peukert's law") describes how the amount of useful energy that can be obtained (for a lead-acid cell) depends on how quickly it is pulled out.

In general an [engine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engine "Engine") will generate less [kinetic energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_energy "Kinetic energy") due to [inefficiencies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inefficiency "Inefficiency") and [thermodynamic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamics "Thermodynamics") considerations—hence the [specific fuel consumption](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thrust_specific_fuel_consumption "Thrust specific fuel consumption") of an engine will always be greater than its rate of production of the kinetic energy of motion.

Energy density differs from [energy conversion efficiency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_conversion_efficiency "Energy conversion efficiency") (net output per input) or [embodied energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Embodied_energy "Embodied energy") (the energy output costs to provide, as [harvesting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_industry "Energy industry"), [refining](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oil_refinery "Oil refinery"), distributing, and dealing with [pollution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pollution "Pollution") all use energy). Large scale, intensive energy use impacts and is impacted by [climate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate "Climate"), [waste storage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_waste "Nuclear waste"), and [environmental consequences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deforestation "Deforestation").

The greatest energy source by far is matter itself, according to the [mass–energy equivalence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass%E2%80%93energy_equivalence "Mass–energy equivalence"). This energy is described by _E_ = _mc_ 2, where _c_ is the speed of light. In terms of density, _m_ = _ρV_, where _ρ_ is the volumetric mass density, _V_ is the volume occupied by the mass. This energy can be released by the processes of [nuclear fission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_fission "Nuclear fission") (~0.1%), [nuclear fusion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_fusion "Nuclear fusion") (~1%), or the [annihilation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Annihilation "Annihilation") of some or all of the matter in the volume _V_ by matter–[antimatter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antimatter "Antimatter") collisions (100%).[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

The most effective ways of accessing this energy, aside from antimatter, are [fusion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_fusion "Nuclear fusion") and [fission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_fission "Nuclear fission"). Fusion is the process by which the sun produces energy which will be available for billions of years (in the form of sunlight and heat). However as of 2024, sustained [fusion power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fusion_power "Fusion power") production continues to be elusive. Power from fission in [nuclear power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power "Nuclear power") plants (using uranium and thorium) will be available for at least many decades or even centuries because of the plentiful supply of the elements on earth,[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_density#cite_note-13) though the full potential of this source can only be realized through [breeder reactors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Breeder_reactor "Breeder reactor"), which are, apart from the [BN-600 reactor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BN-600_reactor "BN-600 reactor"), not yet used commercially.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_density#cite_note-cohen-14)

Nuclear fuels typically have volumetric energy densities at least tens of thousands of times higher than chemical fuels. A 1 inch tall uranium fuel pellet is equivalent to about 1 ton of coal, 120 gallons of crude oil, or 17,000 cubic feet of natural gas.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_density#cite_note-w827-15) In [light-water reactors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light-water_reactor "Light-water reactor"), 1 kg of natural uranium – following a corresponding enrichment and used for power generation– is equivalent to the energy content of nearly 10,000 kg of mineral oil or 14,000 kg of coal.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_density#cite_note-r402-16) Comparatively, [coal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coal "Coal"), [gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas "Gas"), and [petroleum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petroleum "Petroleum") are the current primary energy sources in the U.S.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_density#cite_note-17) but have a much lower energy density.

The density of thermal energy contained in the core of a [light-water reactor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light-water_reactor "Light-water reactor") ([pressurized water reactor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pressurized_water_reactor "Pressurized water reactor") (PWR) or [boiling water reactor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boiling_water_reactor "Boiling water reactor") (BWR)) of typically 1 GW (1000 MW electrical corresponding to ≈3000 MW thermal) is in the range of 10 to 100 MW of thermal energy per cubic meter of cooling water depending on the location considered in the system (the core itself (≈30 m 3), the reactor pressure vessel (≈50 m 3), or the whole primary circuit (≈300 m 3)). This represents a considerable density of energy that requires a continuous water flow at high velocity at all times in order to remove [heat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat "Heat") from the core, even after an emergency shutdown of the reactor.

The incapacity to cool the cores of three BWRs at [Fukushima](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fukushima_nuclear_accident "Fukushima nuclear accident") after the 2011 [tsunami](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tsunami "Tsunami") and the resulting loss of external electrical power and cold source caused the meltdown of the three cores in only a few hours, even though the three reactors were correctly shut down just after the [Tōhoku earthquake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2011_T%C5%8Dhoku_earthquake_and_tsunami "2011 Tōhoku earthquake and tsunami"). This extremely high power density distinguishes nuclear power plants (NPP's) from any thermal power plants (burning coal, fuel or gas) or any chemical plants and explains the large redundancy required to permanently control the [neutron reactivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_chain_reaction "Nuclear chain reaction") and to remove the residual heat from the core of NPP's.

### Antimatter–matter annihilation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Energy_density&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Antimatter–matter annihilation")]

Because antimatter–matter interactions result in complete conversion of the rest mass to radiant energy, the energy density of this reaction depends on the density of the matter and antimatter used. A [neutron star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron_star "Neutron star") would approximate the most dense system capable of matter-antimatter annihilation. A [black hole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_hole "Black hole"), although denser than a neutron star, does not have an equivalent anti-particle form, but would offer the same 100% conversion rate of mass to energy in the form of [Hawking radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hawking_radiation "Hawking radiation"). Even in the case of relatively small black holes (smaller than astronomical objects) the power output would be tremendous.

Electric and magnetic fields
----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Energy_density&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Electric and magnetic fields")]

[Electric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_field "Electric field") and [magnetic fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_field "Magnetic field") can store energy and its density relates to the strength of the fields within a given volume. This (volumetric) energy density is given by ![Image 3: {\displaystyle u={\frac {\varepsilon }{2}}\mathbf {E} ^{2}+{\frac {1}{2\mu }}\mathbf {B} ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f783cf6738d1f64e6b63b04d36769e1d903adeec) where **E** is the [electric field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_field "Electric field"), **B** is the [magnetic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_field "Magnetic field"), and ε and µ are the permittivity and permeability of the surroundings respectively. The SI unit is the joule per cubic metre.

In ideal (linear and nondispersive) substances, the energy density is ![Image 4: {\displaystyle u={\frac {1}{2}}(\mathbf {E} \cdot \mathbf {D} +\mathbf {H} \cdot \mathbf {B} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/27216a0f4c6b8fa436f1c3ceaa91d892396f4303) where **D** is the [electric displacement field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_displacement_field "Electric displacement field") and **H** is the [magnetizing field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Effective_magnetic_field "Effective magnetic field"). In the case of absence of magnetic fields, by exploiting [Fröhlich's relationships](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herbert_Fr%C3%B6hlich "Herbert Fröhlich") it is also possible to extend these equations to [anisotropic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anisotropic "Anisotropic") and [nonlinear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nonlinear "Nonlinear") dielectrics, as well as to calculate the correlated [Helmholtz free energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helmholtz_free_energy "Helmholtz free energy") and [entropy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy "Entropy") densities.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_density#cite_note-18)

In the context of [magnetohydrodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamics "Magnetohydrodynamics"), the physics of conductive fluids, the magnetic energy density behaves like an additional [pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pressure "Pressure") that adds to the [gas pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gas "Kinetic theory of gas") of a [plasma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasma_(physics) "Plasma (physics)").

When a pulsed [laser](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laser "Laser") impacts a surface, the [radiant exposure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiant_exposure "Radiant exposure"), i.e. the energy deposited per unit of surface, may also be called **energy density** or fluence.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_density#cite_note-19)
