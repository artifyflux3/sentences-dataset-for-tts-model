**Euclid** (; [Ancient Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language "Ancient Greek language"): Εὐκλείδης; fl. 300 BC) was an [ancient Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greece "Ancient Greece")[mathematician](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematician "Mathematician") active as a [geometer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometer "Geometer") and [logician](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logician "Logician").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBruno2003[httpsarchiveorgdetailsmathmathematicia00brunpage125_125]-2) Considered the "father of geometry",[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTESialaros2021%C2%A7_%22Summary%22-3) he is chiefly known for the _[Elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid%27s\_Elements "Euclid's Elements")_ treatise, which established the foundations of [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry") that largely dominated the field until the early 19th century. His system, now referred to as [Euclidean geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_geometry "Euclidean geometry"), involved innovations in combination with a synthesis of theories from earlier Greek mathematicians, including [Eudoxus of Cnidus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eudoxus_of_Cnidus "Eudoxus of Cnidus"), [Hippocrates of Chios](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hippocrates_of_Chios "Hippocrates of Chios"), [Thales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thales "Thales") and [Theaetetus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theaetetus_(mathematician) "Theaetetus (mathematician)"). With [Archimedes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archimedes "Archimedes") and [Apollonius of Perga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apollonius_of_Perga "Apollonius of Perga"), Euclid is generally considered among the greatest mathematicians of antiquity, and one of the most influential in the [history of mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_mathematics "History of mathematics").

Very little is known of Euclid's life, and most information comes from the scholars [Proclus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proclus "Proclus") and [Pappus of Alexandria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pappus_of_Alexandria "Pappus of Alexandria") many centuries later. [Medieval Islamic mathematicians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics_in_the_medieval_Islamic_world "Mathematics in the medieval Islamic world") invented a fanciful biography, and medieval [Byzantine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantine_Empire "Byzantine Empire") and early [Renaissance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Renaissance "Renaissance") scholars mistook him for the earlier philosopher [Euclid of Megara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid_of_Megara "Euclid of Megara"). It is now generally accepted that he spent his career in [Alexandria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexandria "Alexandria") and lived around 300 BC, after [Plato](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plato "Plato")'s students and before Archimedes. There is some speculation that Euclid studied at the [Platonic Academy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platonic_Academy "Platonic Academy") and later taught at the [Musaeum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Musaeum "Musaeum"); he is regarded as bridging the earlier Platonic tradition in [Athens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Athens "Athens") with the later tradition of Alexandria.

In the _Elements_, Euclid deduced the [theorems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theorem "Theorem") from a small set of [axioms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiom "Axiom"). He also wrote works on [perspective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perspective_(visual) "Perspective (visual)"), [conic sections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conic_section "Conic section"), [spherical geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_geometry "Spherical geometry"), [number theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_theory "Number theory"), and [mathematical rigour](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rigour#Mathematical_proof "Rigour"). In addition to the _Elements_, Euclid wrote a central early text in the [optics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optics "Optics") field, _[Optics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid%27s\_Optics "Euclid's Optics")_, and lesser-known works including _[Data](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid%27s\_Data "Euclid's Data")_ and _Phaenomena_. Euclid's authorship of _On Divisions of Figures_ and _Catoptrics_ has been questioned. He is thought to have written many [lost works](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#Lost_works).

### Traditional narrative

[![Image 4](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/0_Chambre_de_Rapha%C3%ABl_-_%C3%89cole_d%27Ath%C3%A8nes_-_Mus%C3%A9es_du_Vatican.JPG/500px-0_Chambre_de_Rapha%C3%ABl_-_%C3%89cole_d%27Ath%C3%A8nes_-_Mus%C3%A9es_du_Vatican.JPG)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:0_Chambre_de_Rapha%C3%ABl_-_%C3%89cole_d%27Ath%C3%A8nes_-_Mus%C3%A9es_du_Vatican.JPG)

Detail of [Raphael](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raphael "Raphael")'s impression of Euclid, teaching students in _[The School of Athens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The\_School\_of\_Athens "The School of Athens")_ (1509–1511)

The English name 'Euclid' is the anglicized version of the [Ancient Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek "Ancient Greek") name _Eukleídes_ (Εὐκλείδης).[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTESialaros2021%C2%A7_%22Life%22-4)[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-6) It is derived from '[eu-](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/eu-#English "wikt:eu-")' ([εὖ](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%B5%E1%BD%96#Ancient_Greek "wikt:εὖ"); 'well') and 'klês' ([-κλῆς](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/#-%CE%BA%CE%BB%E1%BF%86%CF%82 "wikt:"); 'fame'), meaning "renowned, glorious".[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTE''OED''b-7) In English, by [metonymy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metonymy "Metonymy"), 'Euclid' can mean his most well-known work, [Euclid's _Elements_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid%27s_Elements "Euclid's Elements"), or a copy thereof,[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTE''OED''a-5) and is sometimes synonymous with 'geometry'.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBruno2003[httpsarchiveorgdetailsmathmathematicia00brunpage125_125]-2)

As with many [ancient Greek mathematicians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek_mathematicians "Ancient Greek mathematicians"), the details of Euclid's life are mostly unknown.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHeath1981354-8) He is accepted as the author of four mostly extant treatises—the _Elements_, _[Optics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid%27s\_Optics "Euclid's Optics")_, _[Data](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid%27s\_Data "Euclid's Data")_, _[Phaenomena](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid%27s\_Phaenomena "Euclid's Phaenomena")_—but besides this, there is nothing known for certain of him.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAsper2010%C2%A7_para._1-9)[[b]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-11) The traditional narrative mainly follows the 5th century AD account by [Proclus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proclus "Proclus") in his _Commentary on the First Book of Euclid's Elements_, as well as a few anecdotes from [Pappus of Alexandria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pappus_of_Alexandria "Pappus of Alexandria") in the early 4th century.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTESialaros2021%C2%A7_%22Life%22-4)[[c]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-13)

According to Proclus, Euclid lived shortly after several of [Plato](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plato "Plato")'s (d. 347 BC) followers and before the mathematician [Archimedes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archimedes "Archimedes") (c. 287– c. 212 BC);[[d]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-14) specifically, Proclus placed Euclid during the rule of [Ptolemy I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ptolemy_I_Soter "Ptolemy I Soter") (r. 305/304–282 BC).[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHeath1981354-8)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAsper2010%C2%A7_para._1-9)[[e]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-15) Euclid's birthdate is unknown; some scholars estimate around 330[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBall1960[httpsarchiveorgdetailsshortaccountofhi0000ballpage52mode2up_52]-16)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTESialaros2020141-17) or 325 BC,[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBruno2003[httpsarchiveorgdetailsmathmathematicia00brunpage125_125]-2)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoulding2010125-18) but others refrain from speculating.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmorynski20082-19) It is presumed that he was of Greek descent,[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBall1960[httpsarchiveorgdetailsshortaccountofhi0000ballpage52mode2up_52]-16) but his birthplace is unknown.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoyer1991[httpsarchiveorgdetailshistoryofmathema00boyepage100mode2upviewtheater_100]-20)[[f]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-22) Proclus held that Euclid followed the [Platonic tradition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platonism "Platonism"), but there is no definitive confirmation for this.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHeath1981355-23) It is unlikely he was a contemporary of Plato, so it is often presumed that he was educated by Plato's disciples at the [Platonic Academy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platonic_Academy "Platonic Academy") in Athens.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoulding2010126-24) Historian [Thomas Heath](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Heath_(classicist) "Thomas Heath (classicist)") supported this theory, noting that most capable geometers lived in Athens, including many of those whose work Euclid built on;[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHeath1908[httpsarchiveorgdetailsbub_gb_UhgPAAAAIAAJpagen13mode2up_2]-25) historian Michalis Sialaros considers this a mere conjecture.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTESialaros2021%C2%A7_%22Life%22-4)[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTESialaros2020147%E2%80%93148-26) In any event, the contents of Euclid's work demonstrate familiarity with the Platonic geometry tradition.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBall1960[httpsarchiveorgdetailsshortaccountofhi0000ballpage52mode2up_52]-16)

In his _Collection_, Pappus mentions that [Apollonius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apollonius_of_Perga "Apollonius of Perga") studied with Euclid's students in [Alexandria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexandria "Alexandria"), and this has been taken to imply that Euclid worked and founded a [mathematical tradition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/School_of_thought "School of thought") there.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAsper2010%C2%A7_para._1-9)[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTESialaros2020142-27)[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHeath1908[httpsarchiveorgdetailsbub_gb_UhgPAAAAIAAJpagen13mode2up_2]-25) The city was founded by [Alexander the Great](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_the_Great "Alexander the Great") in 331 BC,[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBruno2003[httpsarchiveorgdetailsmathmathematicia00brunpage126_126]-28) and the rule of Ptolemy I from 306 BC onwards gave it a stability which was relatively unique amid the chaotic [wars over dividing Alexander's empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wars_of_the_Diadochi "Wars of the Diadochi").[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBall1960[httpsarchiveorgdetailsshortaccountofhi0000ballpage50mode2up_51]-29) Ptolemy began a process of [hellenization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hellenization "Hellenization") and commissioned numerous constructions, building the massive [Musaeum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Musaeum "Musaeum") institution, which was a leading center of education.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoyer1991[httpsarchiveorgdetailshistoryofmathema00boyepage100mode2upviewtheater_100]-20)[[g]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-31) Euclid is speculated to have been among the Musaeum's first scholars.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBruno2003[httpsarchiveorgdetailsmathmathematicia00brunpage126_126]-28) Euclid's date of death is unknown; it has been speculated that he died c. 270 BC.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBruno2003[httpsarchiveorgdetailsmathmathematicia00brunpage126_126]-28)

### Identity and historicity

[![Image 5](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Domenico_Marol%C3%AC_-_Euclid_of_Megara.jpg/500px-Domenico_Marol%C3%AC_-_Euclid_of_Megara.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Domenico_Marol%C3%AC_-_Euclid_of_Megara.jpg)

[Domenico Maroli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domenico_Maroli "Domenico Maroli")'s 1650s painting _Euclide di Megara si traveste da donna per recarsi ad Atene a seguire le lezioni di Socrate_ [_Euclid of Megara Dressing as a Woman to Hear Socrates Teach in Athens_]. At the time, Euclid the philosopher and Euclid the mathematician were wrongly considered the same person, so this painting includes mathematical objects on the table.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTESialaros2021%C2%A7_%22Life%22_and_Note_5-32)

Euclid is often referred to as 'Euclid of Alexandria' to differentiate him from the earlier philosopher [Euclid of Megara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid_of_Megara "Euclid of Megara"), a pupil of [Socrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socrates "Socrates") included in [dialogues of Plato](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_speakers_in_Plato%27s_dialogues "List of speakers in Plato's dialogues") with whom he was historically conflated.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTESialaros2021%C2%A7_%22Life%22-4)[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmorynski20082-19)[Valerius Maximus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Valerius_Maximus "Valerius Maximus"), the 1st century AD Roman compiler of anecdotes, mistakenly substituted Euclid's name for [Eudoxus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eudoxus_of_Cnidus "Eudoxus of Cnidus") (4th century BC) as the mathematician to whom Plato sent those asking how to [double the cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doubling_the_cube "Doubling the cube").[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJones2005-33) Perhaps on the basis of this mention of a mathematical Euclid roughly a century early, Euclid became mixed up with Euclid of Megara in medieval [Byzantine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantine_Empire "Byzantine Empire") sources (now lost),[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoulding2010120-34) eventually leading Euclid the mathematician to be ascribed details of both men's biographies and described as _Megarensis_ (lit.'of Megara').[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTESialaros2021%C2%A7_%22Life%22-4)[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTETaisbakVan_der_Waerden2021%C2%A7_%22Life%22-35) The Byzantine scholar [Theodore Metochites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theodore_Metochites "Theodore Metochites") (c. 1300) explicitly conflated the two Euclids, as did printer [Erhard Ratdolt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erhard_Ratdolt "Erhard Ratdolt")'s 1482 _[editio princeps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Editio\_princeps "Editio princeps")_ of [Campanus of Novara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Campanus_of_Novara "Campanus of Novara")'s Latin translation of the _Elements_.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoulding2010120-34) After the mathematician [Bartolomeo Zamberti](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bartolomeo_Zamberti&action=edit&redlink=1 "Bartolomeo Zamberti (page does not exist)")[[fr](https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bartolomeo_Zamberti "fr:Bartolomeo Zamberti"); [de](https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bartolomeo_Zamberti "de:Bartolomeo Zamberti")] appended most of the extant biographical fragments about either Euclid to the preface of his 1505 translation of the _Elements_, subsequent publications passed on this identification.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoulding2010120-34) A further confusion, which gives the birthplace of Euclid as [Gela, Sicily](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gela "Gela"), arises from the fact that [Euclid of Megara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid_of_Megara "Euclid of Megara") is sometimes said to have been born in Gela.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-:0-36) Later Renaissance scholars, particularly [Peter Ramus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Ramus "Peter Ramus"), reevaluated this claim, proving it false via issues in chronology and contradiction in early sources.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoulding2010120-34)

[Medieval Arabic sources](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics_in_the_medieval_Islamic_world "Mathematics in the medieval Islamic world") give vast amounts of information concerning Euclid's life, but are completely unverifiable.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTESialaros2021%C2%A7_%22Life%22-4) Euclid, who was supposedly a [Tyre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tyre,_Lebanon "Tyre, Lebanon")-born Greek domiciled at [Damascus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Damascus "Damascus"), was claimed to have been the son of Naucrates.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-:0-36) Most scholars consider them of dubious authenticity.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAsper2010%C2%A7_para._1-9) Heath in particular contends that the fictionalization was done to strengthen the connection between a revered mathematician and the Arab world.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHeath1981355-23) There are also numerous anecdotal stories concerning to Euclid, all of uncertain historicity, which "picture him as a kindly and gentle old man".[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoyer1991[httpsarchiveorgdetailshistoryofmathema00boyepage100mode2upviewtheater_101]-37) The best known of these is Proclus' story about Ptolemy asking Euclid if there was a quicker path to learning geometry than reading his _Elements_, which Euclid replied with "there is no royal road to geometry".[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoyer1991[httpsarchiveorgdetailshistoryofmathema00boyepage100mode2upviewtheater_101]-37) This anecdote is questionable since a very similar interaction between [Menaechmus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Menaechmus "Menaechmus") and Alexander the Great is recorded from [Stobaeus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stobaeus "Stobaeus").[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoyer199196-38) Both accounts were written in the 5th century AD, neither indicates its source, and neither appears in ancient Greek literature.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTESialaros201890-39)

Any firm dating of Euclid's activity c. 300 BC is called into question by a lack of contemporary references.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTESialaros2021%C2%A7_%22Life%22-4) The earliest original reference to Euclid is in Apollonius' [prefatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prefatory "Prefatory") letter to the _[Conics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conics "Conics")_ (early 2nd century BC): "The third book of the _Conics_ contains many astonishing theorems that are useful for both the syntheses and the determinations of number of solutions of solid [loci](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Locus_(mathematics) "Locus (mathematics)"). Most of these, and the finest of them, are novel. And when we discovered them we realized that Euclid had not made the synthesis of the locus on three and four lines but only an accidental fragment of it, and even that was not felicitously done."[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJones2005-33) The _Elements_ is speculated to have been at least partly in circulation by the 3rd century BC, as Archimedes and Apollonius take several of its propositions for granted;[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTESialaros2021%C2%A7_%22Life%22-4) however, Archimedes employs an older variant of the [theory of proportions](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Theory_of_proportions&action=edit&redlink=1 "Theory of proportions (page does not exist)") than the one found in the _Elements_.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAsper2010%C2%A7_para._1-9) The oldest physical copies of material included in the _Elements_, dating from roughly 100 AD, can be found on [papyrus fragments unearthed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxyrhynchus_Papyri "Oxyrhynchus Papyri") in an ancient rubbish heap from [Oxyrhynchus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxyrhynchus "Oxyrhynchus"), [Roman Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Egypt "Roman Egypt"). The oldest extant direct citations to the _Elements_ in works whose dates are firmly known are not until the 2nd century AD, by [Galen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galen "Galen") and [Alexander of Aphrodisias](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_of_Aphrodisias "Alexander of Aphrodisias"); by this time it was a standard school text.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJones2005-33) Some ancient Greek mathematicians mention Euclid by name, but he is usually referred to as "ὁ στοιχειώτης" ("the author of _Elements_").[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHeath1981357-40) In the Middle Ages, some scholars contended Euclid was not a historical personage and that his name arose from a [corruption](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Language_change "Language change") of Greek mathematical terms.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBall1960[httpsarchiveorgdetailsshortaccountofhi0000ballpage52mode2up_52%E2%80%9353]-41)

Works
-----

### _Elements_

[![Image 6](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/P._Oxy._I_29.jpg/500px-P._Oxy._I_29.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:P._Oxy._I_29.jpg)

A [papyrus fragment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Papyrus_Oxyrhynchus_29 "Papyrus Oxyrhynchus 29") of Euclid's _Elements_ dated to c. 75–125 AD. Found at [Oxyrhynchus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxyrhynchus "Oxyrhynchus"), the diagram accompanies Book II, Proposition 5.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFowler1999210%E2%80%93211-42)

Euclid is best known for his thirteen-book treatise, the _Elements_ ([Ancient Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language "Ancient Greek language"): [Στοιχεῖα](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CF%83%CF%84%CE%BF%CE%B9%CF%87%CE%B5%CE%AF%CE%B1 "wikt:στοιχεία"); _Stoicheia_), considered his _[magnum opus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnum\_opus "Magnum opus")_.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTESialaros2021%C2%A7_%22Summary%22-3)[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAsper2010%C2%A7_para._2-43) Much of its content originates from earlier mathematicians, including [Eudoxus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eudoxus_of_Cnidus "Eudoxus of Cnidus"), [Hippocrates of Chios](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hippocrates_of_Chios "Hippocrates of Chios"), [Thales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thales "Thales") and [Theaetetus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theaetetus_(mathematician) "Theaetetus (mathematician)"), while other theorems are mentioned by Plato and Aristotle.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAsper2010%C2%A7_para._6-44) It is difficult to differentiate the work of Euclid from that of his predecessors, especially because the _Elements_ essentially superseded much earlier and now-lost Greek mathematics.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTETaisbakVan_der_Waerden2021%C2%A7_%22Sources_and_contents_of_the_''Elements''%22-45)[[h]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-46) The classicist Markus Asper concludes that "apparently Euclid's achievement consists of assembling accepted mathematical knowledge into a cogent order and adding new proofs to fill in the gaps" and the historian [Serafina Cuomo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Serafina_Cuomo "Serafina Cuomo") described it as a "reservoir of results".[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTECuomo2005131-47)[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAsper2010%C2%A7_para._6-44) Despite this, Sialaros furthers that "the remarkably tight structure of the _Elements_ reveals authorial control beyond the limits of a mere editor".[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTESialaros2021%C2%A7_%22Works%22-10)

The _Elements_ does not exclusively discuss geometry as is sometimes believed.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTETaisbakVan_der_Waerden2021%C2%A7_%22Sources_and_contents_of_the_''Elements''%22-45) It is traditionally divided into three topics: [plane geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_geometry "Plane geometry") (books 1–6), basic [number theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_theory "Number theory") (books 7–10) and [solid geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_geometry "Solid geometry") (books 11–13)—though book 5 (on proportions) and 10 (on [irrational](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irrational_number "Irrational number") lines) do not exactly fit this scheme.[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArtmann20123-48)[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAsper2010%C2%A7_para._4-49) The heart of the text is the [theorems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theorem "Theorem") scattered throughout.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAsper2010%C2%A7_para._2-43) Using Aristotle's terminology, these may be generally separated into two categories: "first principles" and "second principles".[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTESialaros2021%C2%A7_%22The_''Elements''%22-50) The first group includes statements labeled as a "definition" ([Ancient Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language "Ancient Greek language"): ὅρος or ὁρισμός), "postulate" (αἴτημα), or a "common notion" (κοινὴ ἔννοια);[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTESialaros2021%C2%A7_%22The_''Elements''%22-50)[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJahnke201018-51) only the first book includes postulates—later known as [axioms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiom "Axiom")—and common notions.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTETaisbakVan_der_Waerden2021%C2%A7_%22Sources_and_contents_of_the_''Elements''%22-45)[[i]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-52) The second group consists of propositions, presented alongside [mathematical proofs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_proof "Mathematical proof") and diagrams.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTESialaros2021%C2%A7_%22The_''Elements''%22-50) It is unknown if Euclid intended the _Elements_ as a textbook, but its method of presentation makes it a natural fit.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTESialaros2021%C2%A7_%22Works%22-10) As a whole, the [authorial voice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Authorial_voice "Authorial voice") remains general and impersonal.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAsper2010%C2%A7_para._6-44)

#### Contents

Book 1 of the _Elements_ is foundational for the entire text.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTETaisbakVan_der_Waerden2021%C2%A7_%22Sources_and_contents_of_the_''Elements''%22-45) It begins with a series of 20 definitions for basic geometric concepts such as [lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_(geometry) "Line (geometry)"), [angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle "Angle") and various [regular polygons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polygon "Regular polygon").[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArtmann20123%E2%80%934-55) Euclid then presents 10 assumptions (see table, right), grouped into five postulates (axioms) and five common notions.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWolfe19454-56)[[k]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-57) These assumptions are intended to provide the logical basis for every subsequent theorem, i.e. serve as an [axiomatic system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiomatic_system "Axiomatic system").[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPickover200956-58)[[l]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-60) The common notions exclusively concern the comparison of [magnitudes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnitude_(mathematics) "Magnitude (mathematics)").[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArtmann20124-61) While postulates 1 through 4 are relatively straightforward,[[m]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-62) the 5th is known as the [parallel postulate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_postulate "Parallel postulate") and particularly famous.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArtmann20124-61)[[n]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-64) Book 1 also includes 48 propositions, which can be loosely divided into those concerning basic theorems and constructions of plane geometry and [triangle congruence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle_congruence "Triangle congruence") (1–26); [parallel lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_line "Parallel line") (27–34); the [area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area "Area") of [triangles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle "Triangle") and [parallelograms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram "Parallelogram") (35–45); and the [Pythagorean theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagorean_theorem "Pythagorean theorem") (46–48).[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArtmann20124-61) The last of these includes the earliest surviving proof of the Pythagorean theorem, described by Sialaros as "remarkably delicate".[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTESialaros2021%C2%A7_%22The_''Elements''%22-50)

Book 2 is traditionally understood as concerning "[geometric algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_geometric_algebra "Greek geometric algebra")", though this interpretation has been heavily debated since the 1970s; critics describe the characterization as anachronistic, since the foundations of even nascent algebra occurred many centuries later.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTESialaros2021%C2%A7_%22The_''Elements''%22-50) The second book has a more focused scope and mostly provides algebraic theorems to accompany various geometric shapes.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTETaisbakVan_der_Waerden2021%C2%A7_%22Sources_and_contents_of_the_''Elements''%22-45)[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArtmann20124-61) It focuses on the area of [rectangles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle "Rectangle") and [squares](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square "Square") (see [Quadrature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrature_(geometry) "Quadrature (geometry)")), and leads up to a geometric precursor of the [law of cosines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law_of_cosines "Law of cosines").[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKatzMichalowicz202059-65) Book 3 focuses on circles, while the 4th discusses [regular polygons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polygons "Regular polygons"), especially the [pentagon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentagon "Pentagon").[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTETaisbakVan_der_Waerden2021%C2%A7_%22Sources_and_contents_of_the_''Elements''%22-45)[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArtmann20125-66) Book 5 is among the work's most important sections and presents what is usually termed as the "general theory of proportion".[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArtmann20125%E2%80%936-67)[[o]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-68) Book 6 utilizes the "theory of [ratios](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ratio "Ratio")" in the context of plane geometry.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTETaisbakVan_der_Waerden2021%C2%A7_%22Sources_and_contents_of_the_''Elements''%22-45) It is built almost entirely of its first proposition:[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArtmann20126-69) "Triangles and parallelograms which are under the same height are to one another as their bases".[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHeath1908b191-70)

[![Image 7](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Platonic_Solids_Transparent.svg/250px-Platonic_Solids_Transparent.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Platonic_Solids_Transparent.svg)

The five [Platonic solids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platonic_solids "Platonic solids"), foundational components of [solid geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_geometry "Solid geometry") which feature in Books 11–13

From Book 7 onwards, the mathematician [Benno Artmann](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Benno_Artmann&action=edit&redlink=1 "Benno Artmann (page does not exist)")[[de](https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benno_Artmann "de:Benno Artmann")] notes that "Euclid starts afresh. Nothing from the preceding books is used".[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArtmann20127-71)[Number theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_theory "Number theory") is covered by books 7 to 10, the former beginning with a set of 22 definitions for [parity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parity_(mathematics) "Parity (mathematics)"), [prime numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_number "Prime number") and other arithmetic-related concepts.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTETaisbakVan_der_Waerden2021%C2%A7_%22Sources_and_contents_of_the_''Elements''%22-45) Book 7 includes the [Euclidean algorithm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_algorithm "Euclidean algorithm"), a method for finding the [greatest common divisor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greatest_common_divisor "Greatest common divisor") of two numbers.[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArtmann20127-71) The 8th book discusses [geometric progressions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_progression "Geometric progression"), while book 9 includes the proposition, now called [Euclid's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid%27s_theorem "Euclid's theorem"), that there are infinitely many [prime numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_number "Prime number").[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTETaisbakVan_der_Waerden2021%C2%A7_%22Sources_and_contents_of_the_''Elements''%22-45) Of the _Elements_, book 10 is by far the largest and most complex, dealing with irrational numbers in the context of magnitudes.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTESialaros2021%C2%A7_%22The_''Elements''%22-50)

The final three books (11–13) primarily discuss [solid geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_geometry "Solid geometry").[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArtmann20123-48) By introducing a list of 37 definitions, Book 11 contextualizes the next two.[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArtmann20129-72) Although its foundational character resembles Book 1, unlike the latter it features no axiomatic system or postulates.[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArtmann20129-72) The three sections of Book 11 include content on solid geometry (1–19), solid angles (20–23) and [parallelepipedal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelepiped "Parallelepiped") solids (24–37).[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArtmann20129-72)

### Other works

[![Image 8](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Euclid_Dodecahedron_1.svg/250px-Euclid_Dodecahedron_1.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Euclid_Dodecahedron_1.svg)

Euclid's construction of a regular [dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dodecahedron "Dodecahedron")

In addition to the _Elements_, at least five works of Euclid have survived to the present day. They follow the same logical structure as _Elements_, with definitions and proved propositions.

*   _Catoptrics_ concerns the mathematical theory of mirrors, particularly the images formed in plane and spherical concave mirrors, though the attribution is sometimes questioned.[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTESialaros2021%C2%A7_%22Other_Works%22-73)
*   The _[Data](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid%27s\_Data "Euclid's Data")_ ([Ancient Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language "Ancient Greek language"): Δεδομένα), is a somewhat short text which deals with the nature and implications of "given" information in geometrical problems.[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTESialaros2021%C2%A7_%22Other_Works%22-73)
*   _On Divisions_ ([Ancient Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language "Ancient Greek language"): Περὶ Διαιρέσεων) survives only partially in [Arabic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabic_language "Arabic language") translation, and concerns the division of geometrical figures into two or more equal parts or into parts in given [ratios](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ratio "Ratio"). It includes thirty-six propositions and is similar to Apollonius' _Conics_.[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTESialaros2021%C2%A7_%22Other_Works%22-73)
*   The _[Optics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid%27s\_Optics "Euclid's Optics")_ ([Ancient Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language "Ancient Greek language"): Ὀπτικά) is the earliest surviving Greek treatise on perspective. It includes an introductory discussion of [geometrical optics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometrical_optics "Geometrical optics") and basic rules of [perspective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perspective_(graphical) "Perspective (graphical)").[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTESialaros2021%C2%A7_%22Other_Works%22-73)
*   The _[Phaenomena](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid%27s\_Phaenomena "Euclid's Phaenomena")_ ([Ancient Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language "Ancient Greek language"): Φαινόμενα) is a treatise on [spherical astronomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_astronomy "Spherical astronomy"), survives in Greek; it is similar to _On the Moving Sphere_ by [Autolycus of Pitane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autolycus_of_Pitane "Autolycus of Pitane"), who flourished around 310 BC.[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTESialaros2021%C2%A7_%22Other_Works%22-73)

### Lost works

Four other works are credibly attributed to Euclid, but have been lost.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTESialaros2021%C2%A7_%22Works%22-10)

*   The _Conics_ ([Ancient Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language "Ancient Greek language"): Κωνικά) was a four-book survey on [conic sections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conic_section "Conic section"), which was later superseded by Apollonius' more comprehensive treatment of the same name.[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTETaisbakVan_der_Waerden2021%C2%A7_%22Other_writings%22-74)[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTESialaros2021%C2%A7_%22Other_Works%22-73) The work's existence is known primarily from Pappus, who asserts that the first four books of Apollonius' _Conics_ are largely based on Euclid's earlier work.[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJones1986399%E2%80%93400-75) Doubt has been cast on this assertion by the historian [Alexander Jones](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Alexander_Jones_(classicist)&action=edit&redlink=1 "Alexander Jones (classicist) (page does not exist)")[[de](https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_Jones_(Wissenschaftshistoriker) "de:Alexander Jones (Wissenschaftshistoriker)")], owing to sparse evidence and no other corroboration of Pappus' account.[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJones1986399%E2%80%93400-75)
*   The _Pseudaria_ ([Ancient Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language "Ancient Greek language"): Ψευδάρια; lit.'Fallacies'), was—according to Proclus in (70.1–18)—a text in geometrical [reasoning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reasoning "Reasoning"), written to advise beginners in avoiding common fallacies.[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTETaisbakVan_der_Waerden2021%C2%A7_%22Other_writings%22-74)[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTESialaros2021%C2%A7_%22Other_Works%22-73) Very little is known of its specific contents aside from its scope and a few extant lines.[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAcerbi2008511-76)
*   The _Porisms_ ([Ancient Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language "Ancient Greek language"): Πορίσματα; lit.'Corollaries') was, based on accounts from Pappus and Proclus, probably a three-book treatise with approximately 200 propositions.[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTETaisbakVan_der_Waerden2021%C2%A7_%22Other_writings%22-74)[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTESialaros2021%C2%A7_%22Other_Works%22-73) The term '[porism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Porism "Porism")' in this context does not refer to a [corollary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corollary "Corollary"), but to "a third type of proposition—an intermediate between a theorem and a problem—the aim of which is to discover a feature of an existing geometrical entity, for example, to find the centre of a circle".[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTESialaros2021%C2%A7_%22Other_Works%22-73) The mathematician [Michel Chasles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michel_Chasles "Michel Chasles") speculated that these now-lost propositions included content related to the modern theories of [transversals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transversal_(geometry) "Transversal (geometry)") and [projective geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_geometry "Projective geometry").[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTETaisbakVan_der_Waerden2021%C2%A7_%22Other_writings%22-74)[[p]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-77)
*   The _Surface Loci_ ([Ancient Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language "Ancient Greek language"): Τόποι πρὸς ἐπιφανείᾳ) is of virtually unknown contents, aside from speculation based on the work's title.[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTETaisbakVan_der_Waerden2021%C2%A7_%22Other_writings%22-74) Conjecture based on later accounts has suggested it discussed cones and cylinders, among other subjects.[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTESialaros2021%C2%A7_%22Other_Works%22-73)

Legacy
------

The cover page of [Oliver Byrne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oliver_Byrne_(mathematician) "Oliver Byrne (mathematician)")'s 1847 colored edition of the _Elements_

Euclid is generally considered with Archimedes and Apollonius of Perga as among the greatest mathematicians of antiquity.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBall1960[httpsarchiveorgdetailsshortaccountofhi0000ballpage52mode2up_52]-16) Many commentators cite him as one of the most influential figures in the [history of mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_mathematics "History of mathematics").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBruno2003[httpsarchiveorgdetailsmathmathematicia00brunpage125_125]-2) The geometrical system established by the _Elements_ long dominated the field; however, today that system is often referred to as '[Euclidean geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_geometry "Euclidean geometry")' to distinguish it from other [non-Euclidean geometries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-Euclidean_geometry "Non-Euclidean geometry") discovered in the early 19th century.[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTETaisbakVan_der_Waerden2021%C2%A7_%22Legacy%22-78) Among Euclid's [many namesakes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_things_named_after_Euclid "List of things named after Euclid") are the [European Space Agency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Space_Agency "European Space Agency")'s (ESA) [Euclid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid_(spacecraft) "Euclid (spacecraft)") spacecraft,[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-79) the lunar crater [Euclides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclides_(crater) "Euclides (crater)"),[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-80) and the minor planet [4354 Euclides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/4354_Euclides "4354 Euclides").[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-81)

The _Elements_ is often considered after the [Bible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bible "Bible") as the most frequently translated, published, and studied book in the [Western World](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_World "Western World")'s history.[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTETaisbakVan_der_Waerden2021%C2%A7_%22Legacy%22-78) With Aristotle's _[Metaphysics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metaphysics\_(Aristotle) "Metaphysics (Aristotle)")_, the _Elements_ is perhaps the most successful ancient Greek text, and was the dominant mathematical textbook in the Medieval Arab and Latin worlds.[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTETaisbakVan_der_Waerden2021%C2%A7_%22Legacy%22-78)

The first English edition of the _Elements_ was published in 1570 by [Henry Billingsley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry_Billingsley "Henry Billingsley") and [John Dee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Dee "John Dee").[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoulding2010120-34) The mathematician [Oliver Byrne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oliver_Byrne_(mathematician) "Oliver Byrne (mathematician)") published a well-known version of the _Elements_ in 1847 entitled _The First Six Books of the Elements of Euclid in Which Coloured Diagrams and Symbols Are Used Instead of Letters for the Greater Ease of Learners_, which included colored diagrams intended to increase its pedagogical effect.[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHawesKolpas2015-82)[David Hilbert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Hilbert "David Hilbert") authored a [modern axiomatization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert%27s_axioms "Hilbert's axioms") of the _Elements_.[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEH%C3%A4hlPeters2022%C2%A7_para._1-83)[Edna St. Vincent Millay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edna_St._Vincent_Millay "Edna St. Vincent Millay") wrote that "Euclid alone has looked on Beauty bare."[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid#cite_note-84)
