[![Image 1: Structure of the Universe](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Structure_of_the_Universe.jpg/500px-Structure_of_the_Universe.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Structure_of_the_Universe.jpg)

Simulation of the matter distribution in a cubic section of the universe. The blue fiber structures represent the matter (primarily [dark matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter "Dark matter")) and the empty regions in between represent the cosmic voids.

**Cosmic voids** (also known as **dark space**) are vast spaces between [filaments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_filament "Galaxy filament") (the largest-scale structures in the [universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe "Universe")), which contain [very few](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Void_galaxy "Void galaxy") or no [galaxies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy "Galaxy"). In spite of their size, most galaxies are not located in voids. This is because most galaxies are [gravitationally](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitationally "Gravitationally") bound together, creating huge cosmic structures known as [galaxy filaments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_filaments "Galaxy filaments"). The cosmological evolution of the void regions differs drastically from the evolution of the universe as a whole: there is a long stage when [the curvature term](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shape_of_the_universe#Curvature_of_the_universe "Shape of the universe") dominates, which prevents the formation of galaxy clusters and massive galaxies. Hence, although even the emptiest regions of voids contain more than ~15% of the average matter density of the universe, the voids look almost empty to an observer.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_void#cite_note-1)

Voids typically have a diameter of 10 to 100 [megaparsecs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parsec#Megaparsecs_and_gigaparsecs "Parsec") (30 to 300 million [light-years](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light-year "Light-year")); particularly large voids, defined by the absence of rich [superclusters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supercluster "Supercluster"), are sometimes called **supervoids**. They were first discovered in 1978 in a pioneering study by Stephen Gregory and [Laird A. Thompson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laird_A._Thompson "Laird A. Thompson") at the [Kitt Peak National Observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kitt_Peak_National_Observatory "Kitt Peak National Observatory").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_void#cite_note-2)

Voids are believed to have been formed by [baryon acoustic oscillations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baryon_acoustic_oscillations "Baryon acoustic oscillations") in the [Big Bang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang "Big Bang"), collapses of mass followed by implosions of the compressed [baryonic matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baryonic_matter "Baryonic matter"). Starting from initially small [anisotropies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anisotropy "Anisotropy") from [quantum fluctuations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_fluctuation "Quantum fluctuation") in the early universe, the anisotropies grew larger in scale over time. Regions of higher density collapsed more rapidly under gravity, eventually resulting in the large-scale, foam-like structure or "cosmic web" of voids and galaxy filaments seen today. Voids located in high-density environments are smaller than voids situated in low-density spaces of the universe.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_void#cite_note-3)

Voids appear to correlate with the observed temperature of the [cosmic microwave background](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background "Cosmic microwave background") (CMB) because of the [Sachs–Wolfe effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sachs%E2%80%93Wolfe_effect "Sachs–Wolfe effect"). Colder regions correlate with voids, and hotter regions correlate with filaments because of [gravitational redshifting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_redshift "Gravitational redshift"). As the Sachs–Wolfe effect is only significant if the universe is dominated by [radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiation "Radiation") or [dark energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_energy "Dark energy"), the existence of voids is significant in providing physical evidence for dark energy.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_void#cite_note-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_void#cite_note-:1-5)

Large-scale structure
---------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Void_(astronomy)&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Large-scale structure")]

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Galaxy_superclusters_and_galaxy_voids.png/960px-Galaxy_superclusters_and_galaxy_voids.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Galaxy_superclusters_and_galaxy_voids.png)

A map of galaxy voids

The structure of the Universe can be broken down into components that can help describe the characteristics of individual regions of the cosmos. These are the main structural components of the cosmic web:

*   Voids – vast, largely spherical[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_void#cite_note-6) regions with very low cosmic mean densities, up to 100 [megaparsecs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parsec#Megaparsecs_and_gigaparsecs "Parsec") (Mpc) in diameter.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_void#cite_note-:0-7)
*   [Walls](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe "Observable universe") – the regions that contain the typical cosmic mean density of matter abundance. Walls can be further broken down into two smaller structural features: 
    *   [Clusters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_cluster "Galaxy cluster") – highly concentrated zones where walls meet and intersect, adding to the effective size of the local wall.
    *   [Filaments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_filament "Galaxy filament") – the branching arms of walls that can stretch for tens of megaparsecs.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_void#cite_note-ReferenceA-8)

Voids have a mean density less than a tenth of the average density of the universe. This serves as a working definition even though there is no single agreed-upon definition of what constitutes a void. The matter density value used for describing the cosmic mean density is usually based on a ratio of the number of [galaxies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy "Galaxy") per unit volume rather than the total mass of the matter contained in a unit volume.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_void#cite_note-Neyrinck2008-9)

Study of cosmic voids within the discipline of [astrophysics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrophysics "Astrophysics") began in the mid-1970s when [redshift surveys](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift_survey "Redshift survey") led two separate teams of astrophysicists in 1978 to identify superclusters and voids in the distribution of galaxies and [Abell clusters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abell_catalogue "Abell catalogue").[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_void#cite_note-Gregory1978-10)[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_void#cite_note-J%C3%B5eveer-11) The new redshift surveys revolutionized the field of astronomy by adding depth to the two-dimensional maps of cosmological structure, which were often densely packed and overlapping,[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_void#cite_note-:0-7) allowing for the first three-dimensional mapping of the universe. Through redshift surveys, their depth was calculated from the individual [redshifts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift "Redshift") of the galaxies due to the [expansion of the universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expansion_of_the_universe "Expansion of the universe") according to [Hubble's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble%27s_law "Hubble's law").[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_void#cite_note-RexBennett1998-12)

A summarized timeline of important events in the field of cosmic voids from its beginning to recent times is as follows:

*   1961 – [Large-scale structural](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe "Observable universe") features such as "second-order clusters", a specific type of [supercluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supercluster "Supercluster"), were brought to the astronomical community's attention.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_void#cite_note-Abell1961-13)
*   1978 – The first two papers on the topic of voids in the large-scale structure were published referencing voids found in the foreground of the Coma/A1367 clusters.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_void#cite_note-Gregory1978-10)[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_void#cite_note-14)
*   1981 – Discovery of a large void in the [Boötes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bo%C3%B6tes "Boötes") region of the sky that was nearly 50 _h_−1 Mpc in diameter (which was later recalculated to be about 34 _h_−1 Mpc).[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_void#cite_note-Kirshner1981-15)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_void#cite_note-Kirshner1987-16) Here _h_ is the [dimensionless Hubble parameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimensionless_Hubble_parameter "Dimensionless Hubble parameter"), approximately 0.7.
*   1983 – Computer simulations sophisticated enough to provide relatively reliable results of growth and evolution of the large-scale structure emerged and yielded insight on key features of the large-scale galaxy distribution.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_void#cite_note-Merlott1983-17)[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_void#cite_note-Frenk1983-18)
*   1985 – Details of the supercluster and void structure of the [Perseus–Pisces region](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perseus%E2%80%93Pisces_Supercluster "Perseus–Pisces Supercluster") were surveyed.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_void#cite_note-Giovanelli1985-19)
*   1989 – The [Center for Astrophysics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harvard%E2%80%93Smithsonian_Center_for_Astrophysics "Harvard–Smithsonian Center for Astrophysics") Redshift Survey revealed that large voids, sharp filaments, and the walls that surround them dominate the large-scale structure of the universe.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_void#cite_note-Geller1989-20)
*   1991 – The Las Campanas Redshift Survey confirmed the abundance of voids in the large-scale structure of the universe (Kirshner et al. 1991).[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_void#cite_note-21)
*   1995 – Comparisons of optically selected galaxy surveys indicate that the same voids are found regardless of the sample selection.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_void#cite_note-22)
*   2001 – The completed two-degree Field Galaxy Redshift Survey adds a significantly large amount of voids to the database of all known cosmic voids.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_void#cite_note-23)
*   2009 – The Sloan Digital Sky Survey (SDSS) data combined with previous large-scale surveys now provide the most complete view of the detailed structure of cosmic voids.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_void#cite_note-24)[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_void#cite_note-25)[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_void#cite_note-26)

Methods for finding
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Void_(astronomy)&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Methods for finding")]

There exist a number of ways for finding voids with the results of large-scale surveys of the universe. Of the many different algorithms, virtually all fall into one of three general categories.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_void#cite_note-Definition_2966-27) The first class consists of void finders that try to find empty regions of space based on local galaxy density.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_void#cite_note-28) The second class are those which try to find voids via the geometrical structures in the dark matter distribution as suggested by the galaxies.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_void#cite_note-29) The third class is made of those finders which identify structures dynamically by using gravitationally unstable points in the distribution of dark matter.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_void#cite_note-30) The three most popular methods through the study of cosmic voids are listed below.

### VoidFinder algorithm

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Void_(astronomy)&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: VoidFinder algorithm")]

This first-class method uses each galaxy in a catalog as its target and then uses the Nearest Neighbor Approximation to calculate the cosmic density in the region contained in a spherical radius determined by the distance to the third-closest galaxy.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_void#cite_note-ReferenceA-8) El Ad & Piran introduced this method in 1997 to allow a quick and effective method for standardizing the cataloging of voids. Once the spherical cells are mined from all of the structure data, each cell is expanded until the underdensity returns to average expected wall density values.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_void#cite_note-31) One of the helpful features of void regions is that their boundaries are very distinct and defined, with a cosmic mean density that starts at 10% in the body and quickly rises to 20% at the edge and then to 100% in the walls directly outside the edges. The remaining walls and overlapping void regions are then gridded into, respectively, distinct and intertwining zones of filaments, clusters, and near-empty voids. Any overlap of more than 10% with already known voids are considered to be subregions within those known voids. All voids admitted to the catalog had a minimum radius of 10 Mpc in order to ensure all identified voids were not accidentally cataloged due to sampling errors.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_void#cite_note-ReferenceA-8)

### Zone bordering on voidness (ZOBOV) algorithm

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Void_(astronomy)&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Zone bordering on voidness (ZOBOV) algorithm")]

This particular second-class algorithm uses a Voronoi tessellation technique and mock border particles in order to categorize regions based on a high-density contrasting border with a very low amount of bias.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_void#cite_note-32) Neyrinck introduced this algorithm in 2008 with the purpose of introducing a method that did not contain free parameters or presumed shape tessellations. Therefore, this technique can create more accurately shaped and sized void regions. Although this algorithm has some advantages in shape and size, it has been criticized often for sometimes providing loosely defined results. Since it has no free parameters, it mostly finds small and trivial voids, although the algorithm places a statistical significance on each void it finds. A physical significance parameter can be applied in order to reduce the number of trivial voids by including a minimum density to average density ratio of at least 1:5. Subvoids are also identified using this process which raises more philosophical questions on what qualifies as a void.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_void#cite_note-33) Void finders such as VIDE[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_void#cite_note-34) are based on ZOBOV.

### Dynamical void analysis (DIVA) algorithm

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Void_(astronomy)&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Dynamical void analysis (DIVA) algorithm")]

This third-class method is drastically different from the previous two algorithms listed. The most striking aspect is that it requires a different definition of what it means to be a void. Instead of the general notion that a void is a region of space with a low cosmic mean density; a hole in the distribution of galaxies, it defines voids to be regions in which matter is escaping; which corresponds to the [dark energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_energy "Dark energy") equation of state, _w_. Void centers are then considered to be the maximal source of the displacement field denoted as _S_ ψ. The purpose for this change in definitions was presented by Lavaux and [Wandelt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benjamin_D._Wandelt "Benjamin D. Wandelt") in 2009 as a way to yield cosmic voids such that exact analytical calculations can be made on their dynamical and geometrical properties. This allows DIVA to heavily explore the [ellipticity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flattening "Flattening") of voids and how they evolve in the large-scale structure, subsequently leading to the classification of three distinct types of voids. These three morphological classes are True voids, Pancake voids, and Filament voids. Another notable quality is that even though DIVA also contains selection function bias just as first-class methods do, DIVA is devised such that this bias can be precisely calibrated, leading to much more reliable results. Multiple shortfalls of this Lagrangian-Eulerian hybrid approach exist. One example is that the resulting voids from this method are intrinsically different than those found by other methods, which makes an all-data points inclusive comparison between results of differing algorithms very difficult.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_void#cite_note-Definition_2966-27)

Voids have contributed significantly to the modern understanding of the cosmos, with applications ranging from shedding light on the current understanding of [dark energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_energy "Dark energy"), to refining and constraining [cosmological evolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_cosmology#Formation_and_evolution_of_large-scale_structure "Physical cosmology") models. The [Milky Way](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milky_Way "Milky Way") Galaxy is in a cosmic void named the [KBC Void](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/KBC_Void "KBC Void").[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_void#cite_note-35) Some popular applications are mentioned in detail below.

The simultaneous existence of the largest-known voids and galaxy clusters requires about 70% dark energy in the universe today, consistent with the latest data from the cosmic microwave background.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_void#cite_note-:1-5) Voids act as bubbles in the universe that are sensitive to background cosmological changes. This means that the evolution of a void's shape is in part the result of the expansion of the universe. Since this acceleration is believed to be caused by dark energy, studying the changes of a void's shape over a period of time can be used to constrain the standard [Λ _CDM_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lambda-CDM_model "Lambda-CDM model") model,[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_void#cite_note-36)[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_void#cite_note-37) or further refine the [Quintessence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quintessence_(physics) "Quintessence (physics)") + Cold Dark Matter (_QCDM_) model and provide a more accurate dark energy [equation of state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equation_of_state_(cosmology) "Equation of state (cosmology)").[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_void#cite_note-38) Additionally the abundance of voids is a promising way to constrain the dark energy equation of state.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_void#cite_note-39)[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_void#cite_note-:2-40)

Neutrinos, due to their very small mass and extremely weak interaction with other matter, will free-stream in and out of voids which are smaller than the mean-free path of neutrinos. This has an effect on the size and depth distribution of voids, and is expected to make it possible with future astronomical surveys (e.g. the [Euclid satellite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid_(spacecraft) "Euclid (spacecraft)")) to measure the sum of the masses of all neutrino species by comparing the statistical properties of void samples to theoretical predictions.[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_void#cite_note-:2-40)

### Galactic formation and evolution models

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Void_(astronomy)&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Galactic formation and evolution models")]

[![Image 3: Large-scale structure formation](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/22/Large-scale_structure_formation.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Large-scale_structure_formation.gif)

A 43×43×43-megaparsec cube shows the evolution of the large-scale structure over a logarithmic period starting from a [redshift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift "Redshift") of 30 and ending at redshift 0. The model makes it clear to see how the matter-dense regions contract under the collective gravitational force while simultaneously aiding in the expansion of cosmic voids as the matter flees to the walls and filaments. 

Cosmic voids contain a mix of galaxies and matter that is slightly different than other regions in the universe. This unique mix supports the biased galaxy formation picture predicted in Gaussian adiabatic cold dark matter models. This phenomenon provides an opportunity to modify the morphology-density correlation that holds discrepancies with these voids. Such observations like the morphology-density correlation can help uncover new facets about how galaxies form and evolve on the large scale.[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_void#cite_note-41) On a more local scale, galaxies that reside in voids have differing morphological and spectral properties than those that are located in the walls. One feature that has been found is that voids have been shown to contain a significantly higher fraction of [starburst galaxies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Starburst_galaxy "Starburst galaxy") of young, hot stars when compared to samples of galaxies in walls.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_void#cite_note-42)

Voids offer opportunities to study the strength of intergalactic magnetic fields. For example, a 2015 study concluded, based on the deflection of [blazar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blazar "Blazar") gamma-ray emissions that travel through voids, that intergalactic space contains a magnetic field of strength at least 10-17[G](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauss_(unit) "Gauss (unit)"). The specific large-scale magnetic structure of the universe suggests primordial "magnetogenesis", which in turn could have played a role in the formation of magnetic fields within galaxies, and could also change estimates of the timeline of [recombination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recombination_(cosmology) "Recombination (cosmology)") in the early universe.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_void#cite_note-43)[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_void#cite_note-44)

### Anomalies in anisotropies

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Void_(astronomy)&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Anomalies in anisotropies")]

Cold spots in the [cosmic microwave background](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background "Cosmic microwave background"), such as the [WMAP cold spot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CMB_cold_spot "CMB cold spot") found by [Wilkinson Microwave Anisotropy Probe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe "Wilkinson Microwave Anisotropy Probe"), could possibly be explained by an extremely large cosmic void that has a radius of ~120 Mpc, as long as the late integrated [Sachs–Wolfe effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sachs%E2%80%93Wolfe_effect "Sachs–Wolfe effect") was accounted for in the possible solution. Anomalies in CMB screenings are now being potentially explained through the existence of large voids located down the line-of-sight in which the cold spots lie.[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_void#cite_note-45)

[![Image 4: Cosmic Microwave Background screening of Universe.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/WMAP_white.png/500px-WMAP_white.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:WMAP_white.png)

CMB screening of the universe

Although [dark energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_energy "Dark energy") is currently the most popular explanation for the [acceleration in the expansion of the universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe "Accelerating expansion of the universe"), another theory elaborates on the possibility of our galaxy being part of a very large, not-so-underdense, cosmic void. According to this theory, such an environment could naively lead to the demand for dark energy to solve the problem with the observed acceleration. As more data has been released on this topic the chances of it being a realistic solution in place of the current [Λ _CDM_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lambda-CDM_model "Lambda-CDM model") interpretation has been largely diminished but not all together abandoned.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_void#cite_note-46)

### Gravitational theories

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Void_(astronomy)&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Gravitational theories")]

The abundance of voids, particularly when combined with the abundance of clusters of galaxies, is a promising method for precision tests of deviations from [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity") on large scales and in low-density regions.[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_void#cite_note-47)[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_void#cite_note-48)

The insides of voids often seem to adhere to cosmological parameters which differ from those of the known universe[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]. It is because of this unique feature that cosmic voids are useful laboratories to study the effects that gravitational clustering and growth rates have on local galaxies and structure when the cosmological parameters have different values from the outside universe. Due to the observation that larger voids predominantly remain in a linear regime, with most structures within exhibiting spherical symmetry in the underdense environment; that is, the underdensity leads to near-negligible particle-particle gravitational interactions that would otherwise occur in a region of normal galactic density. Testing models for voids can be performed with very high accuracy. The cosmological parameters that differ in these voids are Ω m, Ω Λ, and _H_ 0.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_void#cite_note-49)
