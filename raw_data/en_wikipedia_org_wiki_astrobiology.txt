[Nucleic acids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleic_acids "Nucleic acids") may not be the only [biomolecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biomolecule "Biomolecule") in the universe capable of coding for life processes.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-AstroDNA-1)

**Astrobiology** (also **xenology** or **exobiology**) is a scientific field within the [life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_life_sciences "List of life sciences") and [environmental sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Environmental_science "Environmental science") that studies the [origins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abiogenesis "Abiogenesis"), [early evolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protocell "Protocell"), distribution, and future of [life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life "Life") in the [universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe "Universe") by investigating its deterministic conditions and contingent events.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-about-2) As a discipline, astrobiology is founded on the premise that life may exist beyond Earth.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-3)

Research in astrobiology comprises three main areas: the study of [habitable environments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_habitability "Planetary habitability") in the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System") and beyond, the search for planetary [biosignatures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosignatures "Biosignatures") of past or present extraterrestrial life, and the study of the [origin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abiogenesis "Abiogenesis") and [early evolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protocell "Protocell") of life on Earth.

The field of astrobiology has its origins in the 20th century with the advent of [space exploration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_exploration "Space exploration") and the discovery of [exoplanets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exoplanets "Exoplanets"). Early astrobiology research focused on the search for extraterrestrial life and the study of the potential for life to exist on other planets.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-about-2) In the 1960s and 1970s, NASA began its astrobiology pursuits within the [Viking program](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viking_program "Viking program"), which was the first US mission to land on Mars and search for [signs of life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viking_biological_experiments "Viking biological experiments").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-4) This mission, along with other early space exploration missions, laid the foundation for the development of astrobiology as a discipline.

Regarding [habitable environments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_habitability "Planetary habitability"), astrobiology investigates potential locations beyond Earth that could support life, such as [Mars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mars "Mars"), [Europa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Europa_(moon) "Europa (moon)"), and [exoplanets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exoplanets "Exoplanets"), through research into the [extremophiles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extremophile "Extremophile") populating austere environments on Earth, like volcanic and deep sea environments. Research within this topic is conducted utilising the methodology of the geosciences, especially [geobiology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geobiology "Geobiology"), for astrobiological applications.

The search for [biosignatures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosignatures "Biosignatures") involves the identification of signs of past or present life in the form of [organic compounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_interstellar_and_circumstellar_molecules "List of interstellar and circumstellar molecules"), isotopic ratios, or microbial fossils. Research within this topic is conducted utilising the methodology of [planetary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_science "Planetary science") and [environmental science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Environmental_science "Environmental science"), especially [atmospheric science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmospheric_science "Atmospheric science"), for astrobiological applications, and is often conducted through [remote sensing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Remote_sensing "Remote sensing") and in situ missions.

Astrobiology also concerns the study of the [origin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abiogenesis "Abiogenesis") and [early evolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protocell "Protocell") of life on Earth to try to understand the conditions that are necessary for life to form on other planets.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-5) This research seeks to understand how life emerged from non-living matter and how it evolved to become the diverse array of organisms we see today. Research within this topic is conducted utilising the methodology of paleosciences, especially [paleobiology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paleobiology "Paleobiology"), for astrobiological applications.

Astrobiology is a rapidly developing field with a strong interdisciplinary aspect that holds many challenges and opportunities for scientists. Astrobiology programs and research centres are present in many universities and research institutions around the world, and space agencies like [NASA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NASA "NASA") and [ESA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ESA "ESA") have dedicated departments and programs for astrobiology research.

The term astrobiology was first proposed by the [Russian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Union "Soviet Union") astronomer [Gavriil Tikhov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gavriil_Adrianovich_Tikhov "Gavriil Adrianovich Tikhov") in 1953.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-6) It is etymologically derived from the [Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek "Ancient Greek")ἄστρον, "star"; βίος, "life"; and -λογία, _[-logia](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-logia "wikt:-logia")_, "study". A close synonym is exobiology from the [Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek "Ancient Greek") Έξω, "external"; βίος, "life"; and -λογία, _[-logia](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-logia "wikt:-logia")_, "study", coined by American molecular biologist [Joshua Lederberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joshua_Lederberg "Joshua Lederberg"); exobiology is considered to have a narrow scope limited to search of life external to Earth.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-7) Another associated term is [xenobiology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xenobiology "Xenobiology"), from the Greek ξένος, "foreign"; βίος, "life"; and -λογία, "study", coined by American science fiction writer [Robert Heinlein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Heinlein "Robert Heinlein") in his 1954 work _[The Star Beast](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The\_Star\_Beast\_(novel) "The Star Beast (novel)")_;[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-8) xenobiology is now used in a more specialised sense, referring to 'biology based on foreign chemistry', whether of extraterrestrial or terrestrial (typically synthetic) origin.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-9)

While the potential for extraterrestrial life, especially intelligent life, has been explored throughout human history within philosophy and narrative, the question is a verifiable [hypothesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypothesis "Hypothesis") and thus a valid line of [scientific](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Science "Science") inquiry;[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-NAT-20170216-10)[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-NYT-20170215-11) planetary scientist David Grinspoon calls it a field of natural philosophy, grounding speculation on the unknown in known scientific theory.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-12)

The modern field of astrobiology can be traced back to the 1950s and 1960s with the advent of [space exploration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_exploration "Space exploration"), when scientists began to seriously consider the possibility of life on other planets. In 1957, the [Soviet Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Union "Soviet Union") launched [Sputnik 1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sputnik_1 "Sputnik 1"), the first artificial satellite, which marked the beginning of the [Space Age](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_Age "Space Age"). This event led to an increase in the study of the potential for life on other planets, as scientists began to consider the possibilities opened up by the new technology of space exploration. In 1959, NASA funded its first exobiology project, and in 1960, NASA founded the Exobiology Program, now one of four main elements of NASA's current Astrobiology Program.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-13) In 1971, NASA funded [Project Cyclops](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Project_Cyclops "Project Cyclops"),[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-14) part of the [search for extraterrestrial intelligence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SETI "SETI"), to search radio frequencies of the electromagnetic spectrum for interstellar communications transmitted by extraterrestrial life outside the Solar System. In the 1960s-1970s, NASA established the [Viking program](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viking_program "Viking program"), which was the first US mission to land on Mars and search for metabolic signs of present life; the results were inconclusive.

In the 1980s and 1990s, the field began to expand and diversify as new discoveries and technologies emerged. The discovery of microbial life in extreme environments on Earth, such as deep-sea hydrothermal vents, helped to clarify the feasibility of potential life existing in harsh conditions. The development of new techniques for the detection of biosignatures, such as the use of stable isotopes, also played a significant role in the evolution of the field.

The contemporary landscape of astrobiology emerged in the early 21st century, focused on utilising Earth and environmental science for applications within comparate space environments. Missions included the ESA's [Beagle 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beagle_2 "Beagle 2"), which failed minutes after landing on Mars, NASA's [_Phoenix_ lander](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phoenix_lander "Phoenix lander"), which probed the environment for past and present planetary habitability of microbial life on Mars and researched the history of water, and NASA's [_Curiosity_ rover](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curiosity_rover "Curiosity rover"), currently probing the environment for past and present planetary habitability of microbial life on Mars.

Theoretical foundations
-----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Astrobiology&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Theoretical foundations")]

### Planetary habitability

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Astrobiology&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Planetary habitability")]

Astrobiological research makes a number of simplifying assumptions when studying the necessary components for planetary habitability.

**Carbon and Organic Compounds**: Carbon is the [fourth most abundant element](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abundance_of_the_chemical_elements "Abundance of the chemical elements") in the universe and the energy required to make or break a bond is at just the appropriate level for building molecules which are not only stable, but also reactive. The fact that carbon atoms bond readily to other carbon atoms allows for the building of extremely long and complex molecules. As such, astrobiological research presumes that the vast majority of life forms in the Milky Way galaxy are based on [carbon chemistries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon-based_life "Carbon-based life"), as are all life forms on Earth.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-15)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-Pace-16) However, theoretical astrobiology entertains the potential for other [organic molecular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_compounds "Organic compounds") bases for life, thus astrobiological research often focuses on identifying environments that have the potential to support life based on the presence of organic compounds.

**Liquid water**: Liquid [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water "Water") is a common molecule that provides an excellent environment for the [formation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abiogenesis "Abiogenesis") of complicated carbon-based molecules, and is generally considered necessary for life as we know it to exist. Thus, astrobiological research presumes that extraterrestrial life similarly depends upon access to liquid water, and often focuses on identifying environments that have the potential to support liquid water.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-17)[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-SSR-20191212-18) Some researchers posit environments of water-[ammonia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia "Ammonia") mixtures as possible solvents for [hypothetical types of biochemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypothetical_types_of_biochemistry "Hypothetical types of biochemistry").[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-19)

**Environmental stability**: Where organisms adaptively evolve to the conditions of the environments in which they reside, environmental stability is considered necessary for life to exist. This presupposes the necessity of a stable [temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperature "Temperature"), pressure, and [radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiation "Radiation") levels; resultantly, astrobiological research focuses on planets orbiting [Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun "Sun")-like [red dwarf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_dwarf "Red dwarf")[stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star "Star").[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-20)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-Pace-16) This is because very large stars have relatively short lifetimes, meaning that life might not have time to emerge on planets orbiting them; very small stars provide so little heat and warmth that only planets in very close orbits around them would not be frozen solid, and in such close orbits these planets would be [tidally locked](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tidal_lock "Tidal lock") to the star;[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-21) whereas the long lifetimes of [red dwarfs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_dwarf "Red dwarf") could allow the development of habitable environments on planets with thick atmospheres.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-AstroecologyJBIS2005-22) This is significant as red dwarfs are extremely common. (_See also_: [Habitability of red dwarf systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habitability_of_red_dwarf_systems "Habitability of red dwarf systems")).

**Energy source**: It is assumed that any life elsewhere in the universe would also require an energy source. Previously, it was assumed that this would necessarily be from a [Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun "Sun")-like star, however with developments within [extremophile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extremophile "Extremophile") research contemporary astrobiological research often focuses on identifying environments that have the potential to support life based on the availability of an energy source, such as the presence of volcanic activity on a planet or moon that could provide a source of heat and energy.

It is important to note that these assumptions are based on our current understanding of life on Earth and the conditions under which it can exist. As our understanding of life and the potential for it to exist in different environments evolves, these assumptions may change.

### Studying terrestrial extremophiles

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Astrobiology&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Studying terrestrial extremophiles")]

Astrobiological research concerning the study of habitable environments in the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System") and beyond utilises methods within the geosciences. Research within this branch primarily concerns the geobiology of organisms that can survive in extreme environments on Earth, such as in volcanic or deep sea environments, to understand the limits of life, and the conditions under which life might be able to survive on other planets. This includes, but is not limited to:

**Deep-sea extremophiles**: Researchers are studying organisms that live in the extreme environments of deep-sea hydrothermal vents and cold seeps.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-23) These organisms survive in the absence of sunlight, and some are able to survive in high temperatures and pressures, and use chemical energy instead of sunlight to produce food.

**Desert extremophiles**: Researchers are studying organisms that can survive in extreme dry, high temperature conditions, such as in deserts.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-24)

**Microbes in extreme environments**: Researchers are investigating the diversity and activity of microorganisms in environments such as deep mines, subsurface soil, cold glaciers[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-25) and polar ice,[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-26) and high-altitude environments.

### Researching Earth's present environment

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Astrobiology&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Researching Earth's present environment")]

Research also regards the long-term survival of life on Earth, and the possibilities and hazards of life on other planets, including:

**Biodiversity and ecosystem resilience**: Scientists are studying how the diversity of life and the interactions between different species contribute to the resilience of ecosystems and their ability to recover from disturbances.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-27)

**[Climate change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change "Climate change") and extinction**: Researchers are investigating the impacts of climate change on different species and ecosystems, and how they may lead to extinction or adaptation.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-28) This includes the evolution of Earth's climate and geology, and their potential impact on the habitability of the planet in the future, especially for humans.

**Human impact on the biosphere**: Scientists are studying the ways in which human activities, such as deforestation, pollution, and the introduction of invasive species, are affecting the biosphere and the long-term survival of life on Earth.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-29)

**Long-term preservation of life**: Researchers are exploring ways to preserve samples of life on Earth for long periods of time, such as cryopreservation and genomic preservation, in the event of a catastrophic event that could wipe out most of life on Earth.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-30)

### Finding biosignatures on other worlds

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Astrobiology&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Finding biosignatures on other worlds")]

Emerging astrobiological research concerning the search for planetary biosignatures of past or present extraterrestrial life utilise methodologies within planetary sciences. These include:

**The study of microbial life in the subsurface of Mars**:

Scientists are using data from Mars rover missions to study the composition of the subsurface of [Mars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mars "Mars"), searching for biosignatures of past or present microbial life.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-31)**The study of liquid bodies on icy moons**:

Discoveries of surface and subsurface bodies of liquid on moons such as [Europa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Europa_(moon) "Europa (moon)"),[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-Europa-32)[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-EuroUpdate-33)[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-34)[Titan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Titan_(moon) "Titan (moon)")[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-Implications_of_Huygens-35) and [Enceladus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enceladus "Enceladus")[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-Kazan-36)[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-Lovett-37) showed possible habitability zones, making them viable targets for the search for extraterrestrial life. As of September 2024, missions like _[Europa Clipper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Europa\_Clipper "Europa Clipper")_ and _[Dragonfly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dragonfly\_(Titan\_space\_probe) "Dragonfly (Titan space probe)")_ are planned to search for biosignatures within these environments.

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/PIA01130_Interior_of_Europa.jpg/250px-PIA01130_Interior_of_Europa.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:PIA01130_Interior_of_Europa.jpg)

The interior of [Europa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Europa_(moon) "Europa (moon)")

**The study of the atmospheres of planets**:

Scientists are studying the potential for life to exist in the atmospheres of planets, with a focus on the study of the physical and chemical conditions necessary for such life to exist, namely the detection of organic molecules and biosignature gases; for example, the study of the possibility of life in the atmospheres of exoplanets that orbit red dwarfs and the study of the potential for microbial life in the upper atmosphere of Venus.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-38)

**Telescopes and remote sensing of exoplanets**: The discovery of thousands of exoplanets has opened up new opportunities for the search for biosignatures. Scientists are using telescopes such as the [James Webb Space Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Webb_Space_Telescope "James Webb Space Telescope") and the [Transiting Exoplanet Survey Satellite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transiting_Exoplanet_Survey_Satellite "Transiting Exoplanet Survey Satellite") to search for biosignatures on exoplanets. They are also developing new techniques for the detection of biosignatures, such as the use of remote sensing to search for biosignatures in the atmosphere of exoplanets.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-39)

**[SETI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SETI "SETI") and [CETI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communication_with_extraterrestrial_intelligence "Communication with extraterrestrial intelligence")**
Scientists search for signals from intelligent extraterrestrial civilizations using radio and optical telescopes within the discipline of [extraterrestrial intelligence communications](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communication_with_extraterrestrial_intelligence "Communication with extraterrestrial intelligence") (CETI). CETI focuses on composing and deciphering messages that could theoretically be understood by another technological civilization. Communication attempts by humans have included broadcasting mathematical languages, pictorial systems such as the [Arecibo message](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arecibo_message "Arecibo message"), and computational approaches to detecting and deciphering 'natural' language communication. While some high-profile scientists, such as [Carl Sagan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Sagan "Carl Sagan"), have advocated the transmission of messages,[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-40)[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-41) theoretical physicist [Stephen Hawking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stephen_Hawking "Stephen Hawking") warned against it, suggesting that aliens may raid Earth for its resources.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-42)

### Investigating the early Earth

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Astrobiology&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Investigating the early Earth")]

Emerging astrobiological research concerning the study of the origin and early evolution of life on Earth utilises methodologies within the palaeosciences. These include:

**The study of the early atmosphere**: Researchers are investigating the role of the early atmosphere in providing the right conditions for the emergence of life, such as the presence of gases that could have helped to stabilise the climate and the formation of organic molecules.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-43)

**The study of the early magnetic field**: Researchers are investigating the role of the early magnetic field in protecting the Earth from harmful radiation and helping to stabilise the climate.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-44) This research has immense astrobiological implications where the subjects of current astrobiological research like Mars lack such a field.

**The study of prebiotic chemistry**: Scientists are studying the chemical reactions that could have occurred on the early Earth that led to the formation of the building blocks of life- amino acids, nucleotides, and lipids- and how these molecules could have formed spontaneously under early Earth conditions.[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-45)

[![Image 3](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/We_are_star-stuff.png/250px-We_are_star-stuff.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:We_are_star-stuff.png)

Chart showing the theorized origin of the [chemical elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_elements "Chemical elements") that make up the human body

**The study of impact events**: Scientists are investigating the potential role of impact events- especially meteorites- in the delivery of water and organic molecules to early Earth.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-46)

**The study of the primordial soup**:

Researchers are investigating the conditions and ingredients that were present on the early Earth that could have led to the formation of the first living organisms, such as the presence of water and organic molecules, and how these ingredients could have led to the formation of the first living organisms.[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-47) This includes the role of water in the formation of the first cells and in catalysing chemical reactions.

**The study of the role of minerals**: Scientists are investigating the role of minerals like clay in catalysing the formation of organic molecules, thus playing a role in the emergence of life on Earth.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-48)

**The study of the role of energy and electricity**: Scientists are investigating the potential sources of energy and electricity that could have been available on the early Earth, and their role in the formation of organic molecules, thus the emergence of life.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-49)

**The study of the early oceans**: Scientists are investigating the composition and chemistry of the early oceans and how it may have played a role in the emergence of life, such as the presence of dissolved minerals that could have helped to catalyse the formation of organic molecules.[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-50)

**The study of hydrothermal vents**: Scientists are investigating the potential role of hydrothermal vents in the origin of life, as these environments may have provided the energy and chemical building blocks needed for its emergence.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-51)

**The study of plate tectonics**: Scientists are investigating the role of plate tectonics in creating a diverse range of environments on the early Earth.[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-52)

**The study of the early biosphere**: Researchers are investigating the diversity and activity of microorganisms in the early Earth, and how these organisms may have played a role in the emergence of life.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-53)

**The study of microbial fossils**: Scientists are investigating the presence of microbial fossils in ancient rocks, which can provide clues about the early evolution of life on Earth and the emergence of the first organisms.[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-54)

The systematic search for possible life outside Earth is a valid multidisciplinary scientific endeavor.[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-55) However, hypotheses and predictions as to its existence and origin vary widely, and at the present, the development of hypotheses firmly grounded on science may be considered astrobiology's most concrete practical application. It has been proposed that [viruses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viruses "Viruses") are likely to be encountered on other life-bearing planets,[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-Astro-20130814-56)[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-57) and may be present even if there are no biological cells.[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-58)

As of 2024, no evidence of extraterrestrial life has been identified.[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-59) Examination of the [Allan Hills 84001](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allan_Hills_84001 "Allan Hills 84001") meteorite, which was recovered in [Antarctica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antarctica "Antarctica") in 1984 and originated from [Mars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mars "Mars"), is thought by [David McKay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_S._McKay "David S. McKay"), as well as few other scientists, to contain [microfossils](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fossil#Microfossils "Fossil") of extraterrestrial origin; this interpretation is controversial.[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-disbelief-60)[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-life-61)[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-McKay-62)

[![Image 4](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/951_Gaspra.jpg/250px-951_Gaspra.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:951_Gaspra.jpg)

Asteroid(s) may have transported life to [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth "Earth").

[Yamato 000593](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yamato_000593 "Yamato 000593"), the [second largest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_Martian_meteorites#List "List of Martian meteorites")[meteorite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteorite "Meteorite") from [Mars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mars "Mars"), was found on Earth in 2000. At a microscopic level, [spheres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Martian_spherules "Martian spherules") are found in the meteorite that are rich in [carbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon "Carbon") compared to surrounding areas that lack such spheres. The carbon-rich spheres may have been formed by [biotic activity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biotic_material "Biotic material") according to some NASA scientists.[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-NASA-20140227-63)[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-AJ-20140219-64)[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-SP-20140228-65)

On 5 March 2011, [Richard B. Hoover](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_B._Hoover "Richard B. Hoover"), a scientist with the [Marshall Space Flight Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marshall_Space_Flight_Center "Marshall Space Flight Center"), speculated on the finding of alleged microfossils similar to [cyanobacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyanobacteria "Cyanobacteria") in [CI1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CI1_fossils "CI1 fossils") carbonaceous [meteorites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteorites "Meteorites") in the [fringe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fringe_science "Fringe science")_[Journal of Cosmology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Journal\_of\_Cosmology "Journal of Cosmology")_, a story widely reported on by [mainstream media](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mainstream_media "Mainstream media").[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-Tenney-66)[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-Hoover-67) However, NASA formally distanced itself from Hoover's claim.[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-68) According to American astrophysicist [Neil deGrasse Tyson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neil_deGrasse_Tyson "Neil deGrasse Tyson"): "At the moment, life on Earth is the only known life in the universe, but there are compelling arguments to suggest we are not alone."[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-69)

### Elements of astrobiology

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Astrobiology&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Elements of astrobiology")]

[![Image 5](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Artist%27s_Concept_of_Exoplanet_OGLE-2005-BLG-390L_b.jpg/250px-Artist%27s_Concept_of_Exoplanet_OGLE-2005-BLG-390L_b.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Artist%27s_Concept_of_Exoplanet_OGLE-2005-BLG-390L_b.jpg)

Artist's impression of the [extrasolar planet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extrasolar_planet "Extrasolar planet")[OGLE-2005-BLG-390Lb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OGLE-2005-BLG-390Lb "OGLE-2005-BLG-390Lb") orbiting its star 20,000 [light-years](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light-year "Light-year") from [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth "Earth"); this planet was discovered with [gravitational microlensing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_microlensing "Gravitational microlensing").

[![Image 6](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Telescope_Kepler-NASA.jpeg/250px-Telescope_Kepler-NASA.jpeg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Telescope_Kepler-NASA.jpeg)

The [NASA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NASA "NASA")[Kepler mission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler_mission "Kepler mission"), launched in March 2009, searches for [extrasolar planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extrasolar_planets "Extrasolar planets").

Most astronomy-related astrobiology research falls into the category of [extrasolar planet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extrasolar_planet "Extrasolar planet") (exoplanet) detection, the hypothesis being that if life arose on Earth, then it could also arise on other planets with similar characteristics. To that end, a number of instruments designed to detect Earth-sized exoplanets have been considered, most notably [NASA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NASA "NASA")'s [Terrestrial Planet Finder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terrestrial_Planet_Finder "Terrestrial Planet Finder") (TPF) and [ESA's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Space_Agency "European Space Agency")[Darwin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Darwin_(ESA) "Darwin (ESA)") programs, both of which have been cancelled. NASA launched the [Kepler mission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler_(spacecraft) "Kepler (spacecraft)") in March 2009, and the [French Space Agency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Space_Agency "French Space Agency") launched the [COROT](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/COROT "COROT") space mission in 2006.[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-70)[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-71) There are also several less ambitious ground-based efforts underway.

The goal of these missions is not only to detect Earth-sized planets but also to directly detect light from the planet so that it may be studied [spectroscopically](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopy "Spectroscopy"). By examining planetary spectra, it would be possible to determine the basic composition of an extrasolar planet's atmosphere and/or surface.[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-NYT-20220915-72) Given this knowledge, it may be possible to assess the likelihood of life being found on that planet. A NASA research group, the Virtual Planet Laboratory,[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-73) is using computer modeling to generate a wide variety of virtual planets to see what they would look like if viewed by TPF or Darwin. It is hoped that once these missions come online, their spectra can be cross-checked with these virtual planetary spectra for features that might indicate the presence of life.

An estimate for the number of planets with intelligent _communicative_[extraterrestrial life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extraterrestrial_life "Extraterrestrial life") can be gleaned from the [Drake equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drake_equation "Drake equation"), essentially an equation expressing the probability of intelligent life as the product of factors such as the fraction of planets that might be habitable and the fraction of planets on which life might arise:[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-74)

![Image 7: {\displaystyle N=R^{*}~\times ~f_{p}~\times ~n_{e}~\times ~f_{l}~\times ~f_{i}~\times ~f_{c}~\times ~L}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ef499c2f687f05bdeca3468d04df354fbb1f825f)
where:

*   _**N**_ = The number of communicative civilizations
*   _**R***_ = The rate of formation of suitable stars (stars such as the Sun)
*   _**f p**_ = The fraction of those stars with planets (current evidence indicates that planetary systems may be common for stars like the Sun)
*   _**n e**_ = The number of Earth-sized worlds per planetary system
*   _**f l**_ = The fraction of those Earth-sized planets where life actually develops
*   _**f i**_ = The fraction of life sites where intelligence develops
*   _**f c**_ = The fraction of communicative planets (those on which electromagnetic communications technology develops)
*   _**L**_ = The "lifetime" of communicating civilizations

However, whilst the rationale behind the equation is sound, it is unlikely that the equation will be constrained to reasonable limits of error any time soon. The problem with the formula is that it is not used to generate or support [hypotheses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypothesis "Hypothesis") because it contains factors that can never be verified. The first term, _**R***_, number of stars, is generally constrained within a few orders of magnitude. The second and third terms, _f p_, stars with planets and _f e_, planets with habitable conditions, are being evaluated for the star's neighborhood. Drake originally formulated the equation merely as an agenda for discussion at the Green Bank conference,[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-75) but some applications of the formula had been taken literally and related to simplistic or [pseudoscientific](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudoscientific "Pseudoscientific") arguments.[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-BC-76) Another associated topic is the [Fermi paradox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermi_paradox "Fermi paradox"), which suggests that if intelligent life is common in the [universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe "Universe"), then there should be obvious signs of it.

Another active research area in astrobiology is [planetary system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_system "Planetary system") formation. It has been suggested that the peculiarities of the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System") (for example, the presence of [Jupiter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jupiter "Jupiter") as a protective shield)[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-77) may have greatly increased the probability of intelligent life arising on Earth.[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-78)[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-79)

[![Image 8](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Blacksmoker_in_Atlantic_Ocean.jpg/250px-Blacksmoker_in_Atlantic_Ocean.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Blacksmoker_in_Atlantic_Ocean.jpg)

[Hydrothermal vents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrothermal_vent "Hydrothermal vent") support [extremophile bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extremophile "Extremophile") on [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth "Earth"), provided an energy-rich environment for the [origin of life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Origin_of_life "Origin of life"), and may also support life in other parts of the cosmos.

Biology cannot state that a process or phenomenon, by being mathematically possible, has to exist forcibly in an extraterrestrial body. Biologists specify what is speculative and what is not.[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-BC-76) The discovery of [extremophiles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extremophiles "Extremophiles"), organisms able to survive in extreme environments, became a core research element for astrobiologists, as they are important to understand four areas in the limits of life in planetary context: the potential for [panspermia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Panspermia "Panspermia"), forward contamination due to human exploration ventures, planetary colonization by humans, and the exploration of extinct and extant extraterrestrial life.[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-80)

Until the 1970s, [life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life "Life") was thought to be entirely dependent on energy from the [Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun "Sun"). Plants on Earth's surface capture energy from [sunlight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sunlight "Sunlight") to [photosynthesize](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photosynthesis "Photosynthesis") sugars from carbon dioxide and water, releasing oxygen in the process that is then consumed by oxygen-respiring organisms, passing their energy up the [food chain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Food_chain "Food chain"). Even life in the ocean depths, where sunlight cannot reach, was thought to obtain its nourishment either from consuming [organic detritus rained down from the surface waters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marine_snow "Marine snow") or from eating animals that did.[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-smoker-81) The world's ability to support life was thought to depend on its access to [sunlight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sunlight "Sunlight"). However, in 1977, during an exploratory dive to the [Galapagos Rift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galapagos_Rift "Galapagos Rift") in the deep-sea exploration submersible _[Alvin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DSV\_Alvin "DSV Alvin")_, scientists discovered colonies of [giant tube worms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giant_tube_worm "Giant tube worm"), [clams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clam "Clam"), [crustaceans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crustacean "Crustacean"), [mussels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mussel "Mussel"), and other assorted creatures clustered around undersea volcanic features known as [black smokers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_smoker "Black smoker").[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-smoker-81) These creatures thrive despite having no access to sunlight, and it was soon discovered that they form an entirely independent [ecosystem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem "Ecosystem"). Although most of these multicellular lifeforms need dissolved oxygen (produced by oxygenic photosynthesis) for their aerobic cellular respiration and thus are not completely independent from sunlight by themselves, the basis for their food chain is a form of [bacterium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacterium "Bacterium") that derives its energy from [oxidization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redox "Redox") of reactive chemicals, such as [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen") or [hydrogen sulfide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide "Hydrogen sulfide"), that bubble up from the Earth's interior. Other lifeforms entirely decoupled from the energy from sunlight are green sulfur bacteria which are capturing geothermal light for anoxygenic photosynthesis or bacteria running chemolithoautotrophy based on the radioactive decay of uranium.[[82]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-Trixler2013-82) This [chemosynthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemosynthesis "Chemosynthesis") revolutionized the study of biology and astrobiology by revealing that life need not be sunlight-dependent; it only requires water and an energy gradient in order to exist.

Biologists have found extremophiles that thrive in ice, boiling water, acid, alkali, the water core of nuclear reactors, salt crystals, toxic waste and in a range of other extreme habitats that were previously thought to be inhospitable for life.[[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-83)[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-Cavicchioli-84) This opened up a new avenue in astrobiology by massively expanding the number of possible extraterrestrial habitats. Characterization of these organisms, their environments and their evolutionary pathways, is considered a crucial component to understanding how life might evolve elsewhere in the universe. For example, some organisms able to withstand exposure to the vacuum and radiation of outer space include the lichen fungi _[Rhizocarpon geographicum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhizocarpon\_geographicum "Rhizocarpon geographicum")_ and _[Rusavskia elegans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rusavskia\_elegans "Rusavskia elegans")_,[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-85) the bacterium _[Bacillus safensis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacillus\_safensis "Bacillus safensis")_,[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-ThePlanetaryReport-86)_[Deinococcus radiodurans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deinococcus\_radiodurans "Deinococcus radiodurans")_,[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-ThePlanetaryReport-86)_[Bacillus subtilis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacillus\_subtilis "Bacillus subtilis")_,[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-ThePlanetaryReport-86) yeast _[Saccharomyces cerevisiae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saccharomyces\_cerevisiae "Saccharomyces cerevisiae")_,[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-ThePlanetaryReport-86) seeds from _[Arabidopsis thaliana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis\_thaliana "Arabidopsis thaliana")_ ('mouse-ear cress'),[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-ThePlanetaryReport-86) as well as the invertebrate animal [Tardigrade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tardigrade "Tardigrade").[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-ThePlanetaryReport-86) While [tardigrades](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tardigrade "Tardigrade") are not considered true extremophiles, they are considered extremotolerant microorganisms that have contributed to the field of astrobiology. Their extreme radiation tolerance and presence of DNA protection proteins may provide answers as to whether life can survive away from the protection of the Earth's atmosphere.[[87]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-87)

In addition to extremophile research, quantum-chemical studies have shown that organic molecules produced by living systems generally display narrower HOMO–LUMO gaps than those formed abiotically, with this distinction sharpened among more water-soluble compounds. Such gap measurements are thus emerging as a promising electronic biosignature for distinguishing biotic from abiotic chemistries in future life-detection missions. [[88]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-88)

Jupiter's moon, [Europa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Europa_(moon) "Europa (moon)"),[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-Cavicchioli-84)[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-89)[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-90)[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-91)[[92]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-92) and Saturn's moon, [Enceladus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enceladus "Enceladus"),[[93]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-93)[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-Kazan-36) are now considered the most likely locations for extant extraterrestrial life in the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System") due to their [subsurface water oceans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subsurface_ocean#Extraterrestrial_oceans "Subsurface ocean") where radiogenic and tidal heating enables liquid water to exist.[[82]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-Trixler2013-82)

The origin of life, known as [abiogenesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abiogenesis "Abiogenesis"), distinct from the [evolution of life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evolution#Evolution_of_life "Evolution"), is another ongoing field of research. [Oparin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oparin "Oparin") and [Haldane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J._B._S._Haldane "J. B. S. Haldane") postulated that the conditions on the early Earth were conducive to the formation of [organic compounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_compound "Organic compound") from [inorganic elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inorganic_chemistry "Inorganic chemistry") and thus to the formation of many of the chemicals common to all forms of life we see today. The study of this process, known as prebiotic chemistry, has made some progress, but it is still unclear whether or not life could have formed in such a manner on Earth. The alternative hypothesis of [panspermia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Panspermia "Panspermia") is that the first elements of life may have formed on another planet with even more favorable conditions (or even in interstellar space, asteroids, etc.) and then have been carried over to Earth.

The [cosmic dust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_dust "Cosmic dust") permeating the universe contains complex [organic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_compound "Organic compound") compounds ("amorphous organic solids with a mixed [aromatic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aromatic "Aromatic")-[aliphatic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aliphatic "Aliphatic") structure") that could be created naturally, and rapidly, by [stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stars "Stars").[[94]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-Space-20111026-94)[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-ScienceDaily-20111026-95)[[96]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-Nature-20111026-96) Further, a scientist suggested that these compounds may have been related to the development of life on Earth and said that, "If this is the case, life on Earth may have had an easier time getting started as these organics can serve as basic ingredients for life."[[94]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-Space-20111026-94)

More than 20% of the [carbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon "Carbon") in the universe may be associated with [polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polycyclic_aromatic_hydrocarbon "Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbon"), possible [starting materials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PAH_world_hypothesis "PAH world hypothesis") for the [formation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abiogenesis#PAH_world_hypothesis "Abiogenesis") of [life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life#Extraterrestrial "Life"). PAHs seem to have been formed shortly after the [Big Bang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang "Big Bang"), are widespread throughout the universe, and are associated with [new stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_formation "Star formation") and [exoplanets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exoplanets "Exoplanets").[[97]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-NASA-20140221-97) PAHs are subjected to [interstellar medium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_medium "Interstellar medium") conditions and are transformed through [hydrogenation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogenation "Hydrogenation"), [oxygenation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygenation_(environmental) "Oxygenation (environmental)") and [hydroxylation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroxylation "Hydroxylation"), to more complex [organics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_compound "Organic compound")—"a step along the path toward [amino acids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amino_acid "Amino acid") and [nucleotides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleotide "Nucleotide"), the raw materials of [proteins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protein "Protein") and [DNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNA "DNA"), respectively".[[98]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-Space-20120920-98)[[99]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-AJL-20120901-99)

In October 2020, astronomers proposed the idea of detecting life on distant planets by studying the shadows of trees at certain times of the day to find patterns that could be detected through observation of exoplanets.[[100]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-UT-20201006-100)[[101]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-IJA_20201001-101)

David Grinspoon called astrobiology a field of natural philosophy.[[102]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-Grinspoon-102) Astrobiology intersects with [philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophy "Philosophy") by raising questions about the nature and existence of life beyond Earth. Philosophical implications include the definition of [life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life "Life") itself, issues in the [philosophy of mind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophy_of_mind "Philosophy of mind") and [cognitive science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cognitive_science "Cognitive science") in case intelligent life is found, [epistemological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epistemology "Epistemology") questions about the nature of proof, [ethical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethics "Ethics") considerations of space exploration, along with the broader impact of discovering extraterrestrial life on human thought and society.

Dunér[[103]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-103) has emphasized philosophy of astrobiology as an ongoing existential exercise in individual and collective self-understanding, whose major task is constructing and debating concepts such as the concept of life. Key issues, for Dunér, are questions of resource money and monetary planning, [epistemological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epistemology "Epistemology") questions regarding astrobiological knowledge, [linguistics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linguistics "Linguistics") issues about interstellar communication, [cognitive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cognitive_science "Cognitive science") issues such as the definition of [intelligence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intelligence "Intelligence"), along with the possibility of [interplanetary contamination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_contamination "Interplanetary contamination"). Persson[[104]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-104) also emphasized key philosophical questions in astrobiology. They include ethical justification of resources, the question of life in general, the epistemological issues and knowledge about being alone in the universe, ethics towards extraterrestrial life, the question of [politics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics "Politics") and governing uninhabited worlds, along with questions of [ecology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecology "Ecology").

For von Hegner,[[105]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-105) the question of astrobiology and the possibility of [astrophilosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Astrophilosophy&action=edit&redlink=1 "Astrophilosophy (page does not exist)") differs. For him, the discipline needs to bifurcate into astrobiology and astrophilosophy since discussions made possible by astrobiology, but which have been astrophilosophical in nature, have existed as long as there have been discussions about extraterrestrial life. Astrobiology is a self-corrective interaction among observation, hypothesis, experiment, and theory, pertaining to the exploration of all natural phenomena. Astrophilosophy consists of methods of dialectic analysis and logical argumentation, pertaining to the clarification of the nature of reality. Šekrst[[106]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-106) argues that astrobiology requires the affirmation of astrophilosophy, but not as a separate cognate to astrobiology. The stance of conceptual speciesm, according to Šekrst, permeates astrobiology since the very name astrobiology tries to talk about not just [biology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology "Biology"), but about life in a general way, which includes terrestrial life as a subset. This leads us to either redefine philosophy, or consider the need for astrophilosophy as a more general discipline, to which philosophy is just a subset that deals with questions such as the nature of the human mind and other [anthropocentric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anthropocentrism "Anthropocentrism") questions.

Most of the philosophy of astrobiology deals with two main questions: the question of life and the ethics of space exploration. Kolb[[107]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-107) specifically emphasizes the question of [viruses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virus "Virus"), for which the question whether they are alive or not is based on the definitions of life that include [self-replication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-replication "Self-replication"). Schneider[[108]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-108) tried to defined exo-life, but concluded that we often start with our own prejudices and that defining extraterrestrial life seems futile using human concepts. For Dick, astrobiology relies on [metaphysical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metaphysics "Metaphysics") assumption that there is extraterrestrial life, which reaffirms questions in the [philosophy of cosmology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophy_of_cosmology "Philosophy of cosmology"), such as [fine-tuning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fine-tuning_(physics) "Fine-tuning (physics)") or the [anthropic principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anthropic_principle "Anthropic principle").

Rare Earth hypothesis
---------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Astrobiology&action=edit&section=16 "Edit section: Rare Earth hypothesis")]

The Rare Earth hypothesis postulates that multicellular life forms found on Earth may actually be more of a rarity than scientists assume. According to this hypothesis, life on Earth (and more, multi-cellular life) is possible because of a conjunction of the right circumstances (galaxy and location within it, [planetary system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_system "Planetary system"), star, orbit, planetary size, atmosphere, etc.); and the chance for all those circumstances to repeat elsewhere may be rare. It provides a possible answer to the [Fermi paradox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermi_paradox "Fermi paradox") which wonders: if extraterrestrial aliens are common, why are they not obvious? It is apparently in opposition to the [principle of mediocrity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principle_of_mediocrity "Principle of mediocrity"), assumed by famed astronomers [Frank Drake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frank_Drake "Frank Drake"), [Carl Sagan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Sagan "Carl Sagan"), and others. The principle of mediocrity suggests that life on Earth is not exceptional, and it is more than likely to be found on innumerable other worlds.

Research into the environmental limits of life and the workings of extreme [ecosystems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem "Ecosystem") is ongoing, enabling researchers to better predict what planetary environments might be most likely to harbor life. Missions such as the [_Phoenix_ lander](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phoenix_(spacecraft) "Phoenix (spacecraft)"), [Mars Science Laboratory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mars_Science_Laboratory "Mars Science Laboratory"), [ExoMars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ExoMars "ExoMars"), [Mars 2020](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mars_2020 "Mars 2020") rover to Mars, and the [_Cassini_ probe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens "Cassini–Huygens") to [Saturn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saturn "Saturn")'s moons aim to further explore the possibilities of life on other planets in the Solar System.

Viking program
The two [Viking landers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viking_program "Viking program") each carried four types of biological experiments to the surface of Mars in the late 1970s. These were the only Mars landers to carry out experiments looking specifically for [metabolism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metabolism "Metabolism") by current microbial [life on Mars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life_on_Mars_(planet) "Life on Mars (planet)"). The landers used a robotic arm to collect soil samples into sealed test containers on the craft. The two landers were identical, so the same tests were carried out at two places on Mars' surface; _[Viking 1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viking\_1 "Viking 1")_ near the equator and _[Viking 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viking\_2 "Viking 2")_ further north.[[109]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-Chambers-109) The result was inconclusive,[[110]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-110) and is still disputed by some scientists.[[111]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-Bianciardi-2012-111)[[112]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-Discovery-20120412-112)[[113]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-Navarro-113)[[114]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-Paepe-114)

[Norman Horowitz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norman_Horowitz "Norman Horowitz") was the chief of the [Jet Propulsion Laboratory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jet_Propulsion_Laboratory "Jet Propulsion Laboratory") bioscience section for the [Mariner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mariner_program "Mariner program") and [Viking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viking_program "Viking program") missions from 1965 to 1976. Horowitz considered that the great versatility of the carbon atom makes it the element most likely to provide solutions, even exotic solutions, to the problems of survival of life on other planets.[[115]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-Horowitz1986-115) However, he also considered that the conditions found on Mars were incompatible with carbon based life.

_[Beagle 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beagle\_2 "Beagle 2")_ was an unsuccessful [British](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Kingdom "United Kingdom") Mars lander that formed part of the [European Space Agency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Space_Agency "European Space Agency")'s 2003 [Mars Express](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mars_Express "Mars Express") mission. Its primary purpose was to search for signs of [life on Mars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life_on_Mars "Life on Mars"), past or present. Although it landed safely, it was unable to correctly deploy its solar panels and telecom antenna.[[116]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-116)

EXPOSE
[EXPOSE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/EXPOSE "EXPOSE") is a multi-user facility mounted in 2008 outside the [International Space Station](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Space_Station "International Space Station") dedicated to astrobiology.[[117]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-Rabbow-117)[[118]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-Experimental_methods-118) EXPOSE was developed by the [European Space Agency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Space_Agency "European Space Agency") (ESA) for [long-term spaceflights](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spaceflight "Spaceflight") that allow exposure of [organic chemicals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_chemical "Organic chemical") and biological samples to [outer space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_space "Outer space") in [low Earth orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit "Low Earth orbit").[[119]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-home-119)

Mars Science Laboratory
The [Mars Science Laboratory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mars_Science_Laboratory "Mars Science Laboratory") (MSL) mission landed the [_Curiosity_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curiosity_(rover) "Curiosity (rover)")[rover](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rover_(space_exploration) "Rover (space exploration)") that is currently in operation on [Mars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mars "Mars").[[120]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-MSLNameWebsite-120) It was launched 26 November 2011, and landed at [Gale Crater](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gale_(crater) "Gale (crater)") on 6 August 2012. Mission objectives are to help assess Mars' [habitability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_habitability "Planetary habitability") and in doing so, determine whether Mars is or has ever been able to support [life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life_on_Mars_(planet) "Life on Mars (planet)"),[[121]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-MSL-main_page-121) collect data for a future [human mission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_mission_to_Mars "Human mission to Mars"), study Martian geology, its climate, and further assess the role that [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water_on_Mars "Water on Mars"), an essential ingredient for life as we know it, played in forming minerals on Mars.

_Tanpopo_
The [_Tanpopo_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tanpopo_(mission) "Tanpopo (mission)") mission is an orbital astrobiology experiment investigating the potential interplanetary transfer of life, [organic compounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_compound "Organic compound"), and possible terrestrial particles in the low Earth orbit. The purpose is to assess the [panspermia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Panspermia "Panspermia") hypothesis and the possibility of natural interplanetary transport of microbial life as well as prebiotic organic compounds. Early mission results show evidence that some clumps of microorganism can survive for at least one year in space.[[122]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-AGU2017-122) This may support the idea that clumps greater than 0.5 millimeters of microorganisms could be one way for life to spread from planet to planet.[[122]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-AGU2017-122)

_ExoMars_ rover[![Image 11](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/ExoMars_model_at_ILA_2006.jpg/250px-ExoMars_model_at_ILA_2006.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:ExoMars_model_at_ILA_2006.jpg)

ExoMars rover model

_[ExoMars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ExoMars "ExoMars")_ is a robotic mission to Mars to search for possible [biosignatures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosignature "Biosignature") of [Martian life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life_on_Mars "Life on Mars"), past or present. This astrobiological mission was under development by the [European Space Agency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Space_Agency "European Space Agency") (ESA) in partnership with the [Russian Federal Space Agency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_Federal_Space_Agency "Russian Federal Space Agency") (Roscosmos); it was planned for a 2022 launch;[[123]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-still_keen-123)[[124]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-Svitak-124)[[125]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-Selding-125) however, technical and funding issues and the [Russian invasion of Ukraine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_invasion_of_Ukraine "Russian invasion of Ukraine") have forced ESA to repeatedly delay the rover's delivery to 2028.[[126]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-:0-126)

Mars 2020[![Image 12](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/PIA23962-Mars2020-Rover%26Helicopter-20200714.jpg/250px-PIA23962-Mars2020-Rover%26Helicopter-20200714.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:PIA23962-Mars2020-Rover%26Helicopter-20200714.jpg)

Artist's rendition of the [_Perseverance_ rover](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perseverance_(rover) "Perseverance (rover)") on Mars, with the mini-helicopter [_Ingenuity_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mars_Helicopter_Ingenuity "Mars Helicopter Ingenuity") in front

[Mars 2020](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mars_2020 "Mars 2020") successfully landed its rover _[Perseverance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perseverance\_(rover) "Perseverance (rover)")_ in [Jezero Crater](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jezero_Crater "Jezero Crater") on 18 February 2021. It will investigate environments on Mars relevant to astrobiology, investigate its surface [geological processes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geology_of_Mars "Geology of Mars") and history, including the assessment of its past [habitability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_habitability "Planetary habitability") and potential for preservation of [biosignatures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosignature "Biosignature") and [biomolecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biomolecule "Biomolecule") within accessible geological materials.[[127]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-Cowing-127) The Science Definition Team is proposing the rover collect and package at least 31 samples of rock cores and soil for a later mission to bring back for more definitive analysis in laboratories on Earth. The rover could make measurements and technology demonstrations to help designers of a [human expedition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_mission_to_Mars "Human mission to Mars") understand any hazards posed by Martian dust and demonstrate how to collect [carbon dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_dioxide "Carbon dioxide") (CO 2), which could be a resource for making molecular oxygen (O 2) and [rocket fuel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rocket_fuel "Rocket fuel").[[128]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-goals-128)[[129]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-FAQ-129)

_Europa Clipper_
_[Europa Clipper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Europa\_Clipper "Europa Clipper")_ is a mission launched by NASA on 14 October 2024 that will conduct detailed reconnaissance of [Jupiter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jupiter "Jupiter")'s moon [Europa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Europa_(moon) "Europa (moon)") beginning in 2030, and will investigate whether its internal ocean could harbor conditions suitable for life.[[130]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-130)[[131]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-missions-131)[[132]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-FPE-132) It will also aid in the selection of future [landing sites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lander_(spacecraft) "Lander (spacecraft)").[[133]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-Europa_Lander_2013-133)[[134]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-134)

_Dragonfly_
_[Dragonfly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dragonfly\_(Titan\_space\_probe) "Dragonfly (Titan space probe)")_ is a NASA mission scheduled to land on [Titan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Titan_(moon) "Titan (moon)") in 2036 to assess its microbial habitability and study its prebiotic chemistry. _Dragonfly_ is a [rotorcraft](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotorcraft "Rotorcraft") lander that will perform controlled flights between multiple locations on the surface, which allows sampling of diverse regions and geological contexts.[[135]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-APL_draft-135)

_Icebreaker Life_
_[Icebreaker Life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Icebreaker\_Life "Icebreaker Life")_ is a lander mission that was proposed for NASA's [Discovery Program](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discovery_Program "Discovery Program") for the 2021 launch opportunity,[[136]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-Icebreaker2021-136) but it was not selected for development. It would have had a stationary lander that would be a near copy of the successful 2008 [_Phoenix_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phoenix_(spacecraft) "Phoenix (spacecraft)") and it would have carried an upgraded astrobiology scientific payload, including a 1-meter-long core drill to sample ice-cemented ground in the northern plains to conduct a search for [organic molecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_molecule "Organic molecule") and evidence of current or past [life on Mars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life_on_Mars "Life on Mars").[[137]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-AstrobioMag-137)[[138]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-LPI_2012-138) One of the key goals of the _Icebreaker Life_ mission is to test the [hypothesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypothesis "Hypothesis") that the ice-rich ground in the polar regions has significant concentrations of organics due to protection by the ice from [oxidants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxidizing_agent "Oxidizing agent") and [radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sunlight "Sunlight").

_Journey to Enceladus and Titan_
_[Journey to Enceladus and Titan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Journey\_to\_Enceladus\_and\_Titan "Journey to Enceladus and Titan")_ (_JET_) is an astrobiology mission concept to assess the [habitability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_habitability "Planetary habitability") potential of [Saturn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saturn "Saturn")'s moons [Enceladus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enceladus "Enceladus") and [Titan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Titan_(moon) "Titan (moon)") by means of an orbiter.[[139]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-Sotin2011-139)[[140]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-JET_204-140)[[141]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-LCPM-2013-141)

_Enceladus Life Finder_
_[Enceladus Life Finder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enceladus\_Life\_Finder "Enceladus Life Finder")_ (_ELF_) is a proposed astrobiology mission concept for a space probe intended to assess the [habitability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_habitability "Planetary habitability") of the [internal aquatic ocean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extraterrestrial_liquid_water#Enceladus "Extraterrestrial liquid water") of [Enceladus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enceladus "Enceladus"), [Saturn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saturn "Saturn")'s [sixth-largest moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons_of_Saturn "Moons of Saturn").[[142]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-2015_LPSC-142)[[143]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-contestants-143)

_Life Investigation For Enceladus_
_[Life Investigation For Enceladus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life\_Investigation\_For\_Enceladus "Life Investigation For Enceladus")_ (_LIFE_) is a proposed astrobiology sample-return mission concept. The spacecraft would enter into [Saturn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saturn "Saturn") orbit and enable multiple flybys through Enceladus' icy plumes to collect icy plume particles and volatiles and return them to Earth on a capsule. The spacecraft may sample Enceladus' plumes, the [E ring of Saturn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rings_of_Saturn#E_Ring "Rings of Saturn"), and the upper atmosphere of [Titan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Titan_(moon) "Titan (moon)").[[144]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-Astro2012-144)[[145]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-Porco_2014-145)[[146]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-Doc_2013-146)

_Oceanus_
[_Oceanus_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oceanus_(Titan_orbiter) "Oceanus (Titan orbiter)") is an orbiter proposed in 2017 for the [New Frontiers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Frontiers_program "New Frontiers program") mission No. 4. It would travel to the moon of [Saturn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saturn "Saturn"), [Titan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Titan_(moon) "Titan (moon)"), to assess its [habitability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_habitability "Planetary habitability").[[147]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-sotin-147)_Oceanus_' objectives are to reveal Titan's [organic chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_chemistry "Organic chemistry"), geology, gravity, topography, collect 3D reconnaissance data, catalog the [organics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_compound "Organic compound") and determine where they may interact with liquid water.[[148]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-148)

_Explorer of Enceladus and Titan_
_[Explorer of Enceladus and Titan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Explorer\_of\_Enceladus\_and\_Titan "Explorer of Enceladus and Titan")_ (**E 2 T**) is an orbiter mission concept that would investigate the evolution and [habitability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_habitability "Planetary habitability") of the Saturnian satellites [Enceladus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enceladus_(moon) "Enceladus (moon)") and [Titan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Titan_(moon) "Titan (moon)"). The mission concept was proposed in 2017 by the [European Space Agency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Space_Agency "European Space Agency").[[149]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology#cite_note-E2T_2017-149)
