**Potassium chloride** (**KCl**, or **potassium salt**) is a [metal halide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metal_halide "Metal halide")[salt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salt_(chemistry) "Salt (chemistry)") composed of [potassium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium "Potassium") and [chlorine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chlorine "Chlorine"). It is [odorless](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Odor "Odor") and has a white or [colorless](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Color "Color")[vitreous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vitreous_lustre "Vitreous lustre")[crystal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystal "Crystal") appearance. The solid dissolves readily in water, and its [solutions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solution_(chemistry) "Solution (chemistry)") have a [salt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salt "Salt")-like taste. Potassium chloride can be obtained from ancient dried lake deposits.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_chloride#cite_note-7) KCl is used as a [salt substitute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salt_substitute "Salt substitute") for [table salt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Table_salt "Table salt") (NaCl), a fertilizer,[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_chloride#cite_note-8) as a [medication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medication "Medication"), in scientific applications, in domestic [water softeners](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water_softener "Water softener") (as a substitute for [sodium chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride "Sodium chloride") salt), as a [feedstock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feedstock "Feedstock"), and in [food processing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Food_preservation "Food preservation"), where it may be known as [E number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/E_number "E number") additive **E508**.

It occurs naturally as the [mineral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mineral "Mineral")[sylvite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sylvite "Sylvite"), which is named after salt's historical designations _sal degistivum [Sylvii](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Franciscus\_Sylvius "Franciscus Sylvius")_ and _sal febrifugum Sylvii_,[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_chloride#cite_note-9) and in combination with [sodium chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride "Sodium chloride") as [sylvinite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sylvinite "Sylvinite").[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_chloride#cite_note-Ullmann-10)

Potassium chloride, compacted, fertilizer grade

The majority of the potassium chloride produced is used for making [fertilizer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fertilizer "Fertilizer"), called [potash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potash "Potash"), since the growth of many [plants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant "Plant") is limited by potassium availability.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_chloride#cite_note-11)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_chloride#cite_note-12) The term "potash" refers to various mined and manufactured salts that contain potassium in water-soluble form. Potassium chloride sold as fertilizer is known as "muriate of potash"—it is the common name for potassium chloride (KCl) used in agriculture.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_chloride#cite_note-13)[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_chloride#cite_note-14)[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_chloride#cite_note-15)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_chloride#cite_note-Manning-2010-16) The vast majority of potash fertilizer worldwide is sold as muriate of potash.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_chloride#cite_note-17)[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_chloride#cite_note-18) The dominance of muriate of potash in the fertilizer market is due to its high potassium content (approximately 60% K

2 O equivalent) and relative affordability compared to other potassium sources like sulfate of potash ([potassium sulfate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_sulfate "Potassium sulfate")).[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_chloride#cite_note-Manning-2010-16)[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_chloride#cite_note-19) Potassium is one of the three primary macronutrients essential for plant growth, alongside nitrogen and phosphorus. Potassium plays a vital role in various plant physiological processes, including enzyme activation, photosynthesis, protein synthesis, and water regulation.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_chloride#cite_note-20)[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_chloride#cite_note-21) For watering plants, a moderate concentration of potassium chloride (KCl) is used to avoid potential toxicity: 6 mM (millimolar) is generally effective and safe for most plants, which is approximately 0.4 grams (0.014 oz) per liter of water.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_chloride#cite_note-22)[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_chloride#cite_note-23)

Potassium is vital in the [human body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_body "Human body"), and potassium chloride by mouth is the standard means to treat [low blood potassium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Low_blood_potassium "Low blood potassium"), although it can also be given intravenously. It is on the [World Health Organization's List of Essential Medicines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WHO_Model_List_of_Essential_Medicines "WHO Model List of Essential Medicines").[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_chloride#cite_note-WHO21st-24) It is also an ingredient in [Oral Rehydration Therapy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oral_Rehydration_Therapy "Oral Rehydration Therapy") (ORT)/solution (ORS) to reduce hypokalemia caused by diarrhoea,[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_chloride#cite_note-who-new-ors-2006-25) which is also on the [WHO's List of Essential Medicines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WHO_Model_List_of_Essential_Medicines "WHO Model List of Essential Medicines").[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_chloride#cite_note-WHO21st-24)

Potassium chloride contains 52% of elemental potassium by mass.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_chloride#cite_note-26)

Overdose causes [hyperkalemia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperkalemia "Hyperkalemia") which can disrupt cell signaling to the extent that the heart will stop, reversibly in the case of some [open heart surgeries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardiac_surgery "Cardiac surgery").[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_chloride#cite_note-pmid29679302-27)[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_chloride#cite_note-pmid37046560-28)[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_chloride#cite_note-pmid38195532-29)

Potassium chloride can be used as a [salt substitute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salt_substitute "Salt substitute") for [food](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Food "Food"), but because not everyone likes its [flavor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flavor_(taste) "Flavor (taste)"), it is often mixed with ordinary [table salt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Table_salt "Table salt") (sodium chloride) to improve the [taste](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taste "Taste"), to form [low sodium salt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Low_sodium_salt "Low sodium salt"). The addition of 1 ppm of [thaumatin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thaumatin "Thaumatin") considerably reduces this bitterness.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_chloride#cite_note-30) Complaints of bitterness or a chemical or metallic taste are also reported with potassium chloride used in food.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_chloride#cite_note-31)

The [World Health Organization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Health_Organization "World Health Organization") guideline _Use of lower-sodium salt substitutes_ strongly recommends reducing sodium intake to less than 2 g/day and conditionally recommends replacing regular table salt with lower-sodium salt substitutes that contain potassium. This recommendation is intended for adults (not pregnant women or children) in general populations, excluding individuals with kidney impairments or with other circumstances or conditions that might compromise potassium excretion.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_chloride#cite_note-32)[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_chloride#cite_note-33)[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_chloride#cite_note-34)

In the United States, potassium chloride is used as the final drug in the three-injection sequence of [lethal injection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lethal_injection "Lethal injection") as a form of [capital punishment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capital_punishment_in_the_United_States "Capital punishment in the United States"). It induces [cardiac arrest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardiac_arrest "Cardiac arrest"), ultimately killing the person.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_chloride#cite_note-35)

As a chemical [feedstock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feedstock "Feedstock"), the salt is used for the [manufacture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manufacture "Manufacture") of [potassium hydroxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_hydroxide "Potassium hydroxide") and [potassium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium "Potassium") metal. It is also used in medicine, [lethal injections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lethal_injection "Lethal injection"), [scientific](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Science "Science") applications, [food processing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Food_processing "Food processing"), [soaps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soap "Soap"), and as a sodium-free substitute for [table salt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride "Sodium chloride") for people concerned about the health effects of sodium.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

It is used as a supplement in animal feed to boost the potassium level in the feed. As an added benefit, it is known to increase milk production.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

It is sometimes used in solution as a [completion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Completion_(oil_and_gas_wells) "Completion (oil and gas wells)") fluid in [petroleum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petroleum "Petroleum") and [natural gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_gas "Natural gas") operations, as well as being an alternative to [sodium chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride "Sodium chloride") in household [water softener](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water_softener "Water softener") units.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

Glass manufacturers use granular potash as a [flux](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flux "Flux"), lowering the temperature at which a mixture melts. Because potash imparts excellent clarity to glass, it is commonly used in eyeglasses, glassware, televisions, and computer monitors.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

Because natural potassium contains a tiny amount of the isotope [potassium-40](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium-40 "Potassium-40"), potassium chloride is used as a [beta radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beta_radiation "Beta radiation") source to calibrate [radiation monitoring equipment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiation_monitoring_equipment "Radiation monitoring equipment"). It also emits a relatively low level of 511 keV gamma rays from positron annihilation, which can be used to calibrate medical scanners.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

Potassium chloride is used in some [de-icing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De-icing "De-icing") products designed to be safer for pets and plants, though these are inferior in melting quality to [calcium chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calcium_chloride "Calcium chloride"). It is also used in various brands of [bottled water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bottled_water "Bottled water").[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

Potassium chloride was once used as a [fire extinguishing agent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fire_retardant "Fire retardant"), and in portable and wheeled [fire extinguishers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fire_extinguisher "Fire extinguisher"). Known as Super-K dry chemical, it was more effective than [sodium bicarbonate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_bicarbonate "Sodium bicarbonate")-based dry chemicals and was compatible with [protein foam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fire_fighting_foam "Fire fighting foam"). This agent fell out of favor with the introduction of [potassium bicarbonate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_bicarbonate "Potassium bicarbonate") ([Purple-K](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Purple-K "Purple-K")) dry chemical in the late 1960s, which was much less [corrosive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corrosive "Corrosive"), as well as more effective. It is rated for B and C fires.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

Along with [sodium chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride "Sodium chloride") and [lithium chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lithium_chloride "Lithium chloride"), potassium chloride is used as a [flux](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flux_(metallurgy) "Flux (metallurgy)") for the [gas welding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas_welding "Gas welding") of [aluminium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aluminium "Aluminium").[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

Potassium chloride is also an optical crystal with a wide transmission range from 210 nm to 20 μm. While cheap, KCl crystals are [hygroscopic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hygroscopic "Hygroscopic"). This limits its application to protected environments or short-term uses such as prototyping. Exposed to free air, KCl optics will "rot". Whereas KCl components were formerly used for [infrared](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infrared "Infrared")[optics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optics "Optics"), they have been entirely replaced by much tougher crystals such as [zinc selenide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zinc_selenide "Zinc selenide").[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

Potassium chloride is used as a [scotophor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scotophor "Scotophor") with designation P10 in [dark-trace CRTs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark-trace_CRT "Dark-trace CRT"), e.g. in the [Skiatron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skiatron "Skiatron").[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

The typical amounts of potassium chloride found in the diet appear to be generally safe.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_chloride#cite_note-36) In larger quantities, however, potassium chloride is toxic. The [LD 50](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Median_lethal_dose "Median lethal dose") of orally ingested potassium chloride is approximately 2.5 g/kg, or 190 grams (6.7 oz) for a body mass of 75 kilograms (165 lb). In comparison, the [LD 50](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Median_lethal_dose "Median lethal dose") of [sodium chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride "Sodium chloride") (table salt) is 3.75 g/kg.

Intravenously, the [LD 50](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Median_lethal_dose "Median lethal dose") of potassium chloride is far smaller, at about 57.2 mg/kg to 66.7 mg/kg; this is found by dividing the lethal concentration of positive potassium ions (about 30 to 35 mg/kg)[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_chloride#cite_note-37) by the proportion by mass of potassium ions in potassium chloride (about 0.52445 mg K+/mg KCl).[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_chloride#cite_note-38)

Solutions of KCl are common standards, for example for [calibration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calibration "Calibration") of the [electrical conductivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrical_conductivity "Electrical conductivity") of (ionic) solutions, since KCl solutions are stable, allowing for reproducible measurements. In [aqueous solution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aqueous_solution "Aqueous solution"), it is essentially fully ionized into solvated K+ and Cl− ions.

### Redox and the conversion to potassium metal

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Potassium_chloride&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Redox and the conversion to potassium metal")]

Although potassium is more [electropositive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electropositive "Electropositive") than [sodium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium "Sodium"), KCl can be reduced to the metal by reaction with metallic sodium at 850°C because the more volatile potassium can be removed by distillation (see [Le Chatelier's principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Le_Chatelier%27s_principle "Le Chatelier's principle")):

KCl(l) + Na(l) ⇌ NaCl(l) + K(g)
This method is the main method for producing metallic potassium. [Electrolysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrolysis "Electrolysis") (used for sodium) fails because of the high solubility of potassium in molten KCl.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_chloride#cite_note-Ullmann-10)

### Other potassium chloride stoichiometries

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Potassium_chloride&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Other potassium chloride stoichiometries")]

Potassium chlorides with formulas other than KCl have been predicted to become stable under pressures of 20 GPa or more.[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_chloride#cite_note-40) Among these, two phases of KCl 3 were synthesized and characterized. At 20-40 GPa, a trigonal structure containing K+ and Cl 3− is obtained; above 40 GPa this gives way to a phase isostructural with the intermetallic compound Cr 3 Si.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

Physical properties
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Potassium_chloride&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Physical properties")]

Under ambient conditions, the crystal structure of potassium chloride is like that of NaCl. It adopts a face-centered cubic structure known as the B1 phase with a [lattice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystal_structure "Crystal structure") constant of roughly 6.3 Å. Crystals cleave easily in three directions. Other polymorphic and hydrated phases are adopted at high pressures.[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_chloride#cite_note-41)

Some other properties are

*   Transmission range: 210 nm to 20 μm
*   [Transmittivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transmittance "Transmittance") = 92% at 450 nm and rises linearly to 94% at 16 μm
*   Refractive index = 1.456 at 10 μm
*   Reflection loss = 6.8% at 10 μm (two surfaces)
*   d _N_/d _T_ (expansion coefficient)= −33.2×10−6/°C
*   d _L_/d _T_ (refractive index gradient)= 40×10−6/°C
*   Thermal conductivity = 0.036 W/(cm·K)
*   [Damage threshold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laser_damage_threshold "Laser damage threshold") (Newman and Novak): 4 GW/cm 2 or 2 J/cm 2 (0.5 or 1 ns pulse rate); 4.2 J/cm 2 (1.7 ns pulse rate Kovalev and Faizullov)

As with other compounds containing potassium, KCl in powdered form gives a lilac [flame](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flame_test "Flame test").

[![Image 19](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Sylvin_%28aka%29.jpg/250px-Sylvin_%28aka%29.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Sylvin_(aka).jpg)

Sylvite

[![Image 20](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Sylvinite.jpg/250px-Sylvinite.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Sylvinite.jpg)

Sylvinite

Potassium chloride is extracted from minerals [sylvite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sylvite "Sylvite"), [carnallite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carnallite "Carnallite"), and [potash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potash "Potash"). It is also extracted from [salt water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seawater "Seawater") and can be manufactured by crystallization from solution, [flotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flotation_process "Flotation process") or [electrostatic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrostatic "Electrostatic") separation from suitable minerals. It is a by-product of the production of [nitric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitric_acid "Nitric acid") from [potassium nitrate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_nitrate "Potassium nitrate") and [hydrochloric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid "Hydrochloric acid").

Most potassium chloride is produced as agricultural and industrial-grade potash in [Saskatchewan, Canada](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saskatchewan "Saskatchewan"), Russia, and Belarus. Saskatchewan alone accounted for over 25% of the world's potash production in 2017.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_chloride#cite_note-42)

Potassium chloride is inexpensively available and is rarely prepared intentionally in the laboratory. It can be generated by treating [potassium hydroxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_hydroxide "Potassium hydroxide") (or other potassium bases) with [hydrochloric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid "Hydrochloric acid"):

KOH + HCl → KCl + H 2 O
This conversion is an [acid-base neutralization reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutralization_(chemistry)#Acid-alkali "Neutralization (chemistry)"). The resulting salt can then be purified by recrystallization. Another method would be to allow potassium to burn in the presence of chlorine gas, also a very exothermic reaction:

2 K + Cl 2 → 2 KCl
