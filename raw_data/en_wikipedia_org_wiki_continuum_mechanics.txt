Markdown Content:
**Continuum mechanics** is a branch of [mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanics "Mechanics") that deals with the [deformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deformation_(physics) "Deformation (physics)") of and transmission of [forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Force "Force") through [materials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Material "Material") modeled as a _**continuous medium**_ (also called a _**continuum**_) rather than as [discrete particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_particle "Point particle").

Continuum mechanics deals with _deformable bodies_, as opposed to [rigid bodies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rigid_bodies "Rigid bodies"). A continuum model assumes that the substance of the object completely fills the space it occupies. While ignoring the fact that matter is made of [atoms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atom "Atom"), this provides a sufficiently accurate description of matter on length scales much greater than that of inter-atomic distances. The concept of a continuous medium allows for intuitive analysis of bulk matter by using differential equations that describe the behavior of such matter according to [physical laws](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservation_laws "Conservation laws"), such as [mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass "Mass") conservation, momentum conservation, and energy conservation. Information about the specific material is expressed in [constitutive relationships](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitutive_equation "Constitutive equation").

Continuum mechanics treats the physical properties of solids and fluids independently of any particular [coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_system "Coordinate system") in which they are observed. These properties are represented by [tensors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor "Tensor"), which are mathematical objects with the salient property of being independent of coordinate systems. This permits definition of physical properties at any point in the continuum, according to mathematically convenient [continuous functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_function "Continuous function"). The theories of [elasticity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elasticity_(physics) "Elasticity (physics)"), [plasticity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasticity_(physics) "Plasticity (physics)") and [fluid mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluid_mechanics "Fluid mechanics") are based on the concepts of continuum mechanics.

Concept of a continuum
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Continuum_mechanics&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Concept of a continuum")]

The concept of a continuum underlies the mathematical framework for studying large-scale forces and deformations in materials. Although materials are composed of discrete atoms and molecules, separated by empty space or microscopic cracks and [crystallographic defects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystallographic_defects "Crystallographic defects"), physical phenomena can often be modeled by considering a substance distributed throughout some region of space. A continuum is a body that can be continually sub-divided into [infinitesimal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinitesimal "Infinitesimal") elements with local material properties defined at any particular point. Properties of the bulk material can therefore be described by continuous functions, and their evolution can be studied using the mathematics of [calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus "Calculus").

Apart from the assumption of continuity, two other independent assumptions are often employed in the study of continuum mechanics. These are [homogeneity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homogeneity_(physics) "Homogeneity (physics)") (assumption of identical properties at all locations) and [isotropy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotropy "Isotropy") (assumption of directionally invariant vector properties).[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuum_mechanics#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMalvern19692-1) If these auxiliary assumptions are not globally applicable, the material may be segregated into sections where they are applicable in order to simplify the analysis. For more complex cases, one or both of these assumptions can be dropped. In these cases, computational methods are often used to solve the [differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_equation "Differential equation") describing the evolution of material properties.

Formulation of models
---------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Continuum_mechanics&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Formulation of models")]

[![Image 1](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Continuum_body.svg/250px-Continuum_body.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Continuum_body.svg)

Figure 1. Configuration of a continuum body.

Continuum mechanics models begin by assigning a region in three-dimensional [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space") to the material body ![Image 2: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {B}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e5622de88a69f68340f8dcb43d0b8bd443ba9e13) being modeled. The points within this region are called particles or material points. Different _configurations_ or states of the body correspond to different regions in Euclidean space. The region corresponding to the body's configuration at time ![Image 3: {\displaystyle t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/65658b7b223af9e1acc877d848888ecdb4466560) is labeled ![Image 4: {\displaystyle \kappa _{t}({\mathcal {B}})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ab55799debd650ba2af18cea74f18f1b962eec53).

A particular particle within the body in a particular configuration is characterized by a position [vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space")

![Image 5: {\displaystyle \mathbf {x} =\sum _{i=1}^{3}x_{i}\mathbf {e} _{i},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/05779d237cd7fb4437be505abdfbd27902c90d0e)
where ![Image 6: {\displaystyle \mathbf {e} _{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ba77c5a75ef9e230d1a36183785477a2eb3c5c1e) are the [coordinate vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_vector "Coordinate vector") in some [frame of reference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frame_of_reference "Frame of reference") chosen for the problem (See figure 1). This vector can be expressed as a [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)") of the particle position ![Image 7: {\displaystyle \mathbf {X} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9f75966a2f9d5672136fa9401ee1e75008f95ffd) in some _reference configuration_, for example the configuration at the initial time, so that

![Image 8: {\displaystyle \mathbf {x} =\kappa _{t}(\mathbf {X} ).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/adf6ebc64beebcb055cf9a56857112e0c4eff364)
This function needs to have various properties so that the model makes physical sense. ![Image 9: {\displaystyle \kappa _{t}(\cdot )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a80070c78b03bd1cb536034da6383302c9e05d6b) needs to be:

*   [continuous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuity_(mathematics) "Continuity (mathematics)") in time, so that the body changes in a way which is realistic,
*   globally [invertible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverse_function "Inverse function") at all times, so that the body cannot intersect itself,
*   [orientation-preserving](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orientation-preserving "Orientation-preserving"), as transformations which produce mirror reflections are not possible in nature.

For the mathematical formulation of the model, ![Image 10: {\displaystyle \kappa _{t}(\cdot )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a80070c78b03bd1cb536034da6383302c9e05d6b) is also assumed to be [twice continuously differentiable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuously_differentiable "Continuously differentiable"), so that differential equations describing the motion may be formulated.

Forces in a continuum
---------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Continuum_mechanics&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Forces in a continuum")]

A solid is a deformable body that possesses shear strength, _sc._ a solid can support shear forces (forces parallel to the material surface on which they act). Fluids, on the other hand, do not sustain shear forces.

Following the classical dynamics of [Newton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaac_Newton "Isaac Newton") and [Euler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leonhard_Euler "Leonhard Euler"), the motion of a material body is produced by the action of externally applied forces which are assumed to be of two kinds: surface forces ![Image 11: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} _{C}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ec02d4c7038179f042e482dc50edbab84666d0ba) and body forces ![Image 12: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} _{B}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/057973853da16f517f9b497242f106e1bdbdc6bc).[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuum_mechanics#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith199397-3) Thus, the total force ![Image 13: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {F}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/205d4b91000d9dcf1a5bbabdfa6a8395fa60b676) applied to a body or to a portion of the body can be expressed as:

![Image 14: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {F}}=\mathbf {F} _{C}+\mathbf {F} _{B}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1c57c22d05519626f12c9d0db2c77d2ca2713f66)
_[Surface forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface\_forces "Surface forces")_ or _contact forces_, expressed as force per unit area, can act either on the bounding surface of the body, as a result of mechanical contact with other bodies, or on imaginary internal surfaces that bound portions of the body, as a result of the mechanical interaction between the parts of the body to either side of the surface ([Euler-Cauchy's stress principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cauchy_stress_tensor "Cauchy stress tensor")). When a body is acted upon by external contact forces, internal contact forces are then transmitted from point to point inside the body to balance their action, according to [Newton's third law of motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_laws_of_motion "Newton's laws of motion") of conservation of [linear momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_momentum "Linear momentum") and [angular momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum "Angular momentum") (for continuous bodies these laws are called the [Euler's equations of motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler%27s_laws "Euler's laws")). The internal contact forces are related to the body's [deformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deformation_(mechanics) "Deformation (mechanics)") through [constitutive equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitutive_equations "Constitutive equations"). The internal contact forces may be mathematically described by how they relate to the motion of the body, independent of the body's material makeup.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

The distribution of internal contact forces throughout the volume of the body is assumed to be continuous. Therefore, there exists a _contact force density_ or _Cauchy traction field_[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuum_mechanics#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith1993-4)![Image 15: {\displaystyle \mathbf {T} (\mathbf {n} ,\mathbf {x} ,t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ad4ccbabe43d0ddc50adf7e5b1b5dbcb541741c2) that represents this distribution in a particular configuration of the body at a given time ![Image 16: {\displaystyle t\,\!}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/60ca0e6d5640c7a6280feeaa20db96e2c00adf94). It is not a vector field because it depends not only on the position ![Image 17: {\displaystyle \mathbf {x} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/32adf004df5eb0a8c7fd8c0b6b7405183c5a5ef2) of a particular material point, but also on the local orientation of the surface element as defined by its normal vector ![Image 18: {\displaystyle \mathbf {n} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4a720c341f39f52fd96028dab83edd34d400be46).[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuum_mechanics#cite_note-FOOTNOTELubliner2008-5)[_[page needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing\_sources "Wikipedia:Citing sources")_]

Any differential area ![Image 19: {\displaystyle dS\,\!}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/24028ff3f6b922fa1a55bbf848ca0002107565da) with normal vector ![Image 20: {\displaystyle \mathbf {n} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4a720c341f39f52fd96028dab83edd34d400be46) of a given internal surface area ![Image 21: {\displaystyle S\,\!}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/669ffe6a8a216bba6f72c39796067a4bbe4db003), bounding a portion of the body, experiences a contact force ![Image 22: {\displaystyle d\mathbf {F} _{C}\,\!}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4292d9e9269506caa51e25facef35167dc254208) arising from the contact between both portions of the body on each side of ![Image 23: {\displaystyle S\,\!}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/669ffe6a8a216bba6f72c39796067a4bbe4db003), and it is given by

![Image 24: {\displaystyle d\mathbf {F} _{C}=\mathbf {T} ^{(\mathbf {n} )}\,dS}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35f0df954d5f4288fe79491fb48ad6e9c7768a98)
where ![Image 25: {\displaystyle \mathbf {T} ^{(\mathbf {n} )}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0523584e28619d7f874568f449828fdc4686a2d5) is the _surface traction_,[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuum_mechanics#cite_note-FOOTNOTELiu2002-6) also called _stress vector_,[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuum_mechanics#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWu2004-7)_traction_,[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuum_mechanics#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFung1977-8)[_[page needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing\_sources "Wikipedia:Citing sources")_] or _traction vector_.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuum_mechanics#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMase1970-9) The stress vector is a frame-indifferent vector (see [Euler-Cauchy's stress principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cauchy_stress_tensor "Cauchy stress tensor")).

The total contact force on the particular internal surface ![Image 26: {\displaystyle S\,\!}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/669ffe6a8a216bba6f72c39796067a4bbe4db003) is then expressed as the sum ([surface integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_integral "Surface integral")) of the contact forces on all differential surfaces ![Image 27: {\displaystyle dS\,\!}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/24028ff3f6b922fa1a55bbf848ca0002107565da):

![Image 28: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} _{C}=\int _{S}\mathbf {T} ^{(\mathbf {n} )}\,dS}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bfcff74d7e57636f4b3d527ea702158e2044bd64)
In continuum mechanics a body is considered stress-free if the only forces present are those inter-atomic forces ([ionic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ionic_bond "Ionic bond"), [metallic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metallic_bond "Metallic bond"), and [van der Waals forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_der_Waals_force "Van der Waals force")) required to hold the body together and to keep its shape in the absence of all external influences, including gravitational attraction.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuum_mechanics#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMase1970-9)[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuum_mechanics#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAtanackovicGuran2000-10) Stresses generated during manufacture of the body to a specific configuration are also excluded when considering stresses in a body. Therefore, the stresses considered in continuum mechanics are only those produced by deformation of the body, _sc._ only relative changes in stress are considered, not the absolute values of stress.

_[Body forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Body\_forces "Body forces")_ are forces originating from sources outside of the body[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuum_mechanics#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIrgens2008-11) that act on the volume (or mass) of the body. Saying that body forces are due to outside sources implies that the interaction between different parts of the body (internal forces) are manifested through the contact forces alone.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuum_mechanics#cite_note-FOOTNOTELiu2002-6) These forces arise from the presence of the body in force fields, _e.g._[gravitational field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_field "Gravitational field") ([gravitational forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_force "Gravitational force")) or electromagnetic field ([electromagnetic forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_force "Electromagnetic force")), or from [inertial forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fictitious_force "Fictitious force") when bodies are in motion. As the mass of a continuous body is assumed to be continuously distributed, any force originating from the mass is also continuously distributed. Thus, body forces are specified by vector fields which are assumed to be continuous over the entire volume of the body,[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuum_mechanics#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChadwick1999-12)_i.e._ acting on every point in it. Body forces are represented by a body force density ![Image 29: {\displaystyle \mathbf {b} (\mathbf {x} ,t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a085d88549a7878b87420a7ec47ddd3949576276) (per unit of mass), which is a frame-indifferent vector field.

In the case of gravitational forces, the intensity of the force depends on, or is proportional to, the mass density ![Image 30: {\displaystyle \mathbf {\rho } (\mathbf {x} ,t)\,\!}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f7fb6f6f9b3962e1d4d2df30f51548937498c150) of the material, and it is specified in terms of force per unit mass (![Image 31: {\displaystyle b_{i}\,\!}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d2ac363cf7ec73b3a7d5d963e26aeea6b40bfd7a)) or per unit volume (![Image 32: {\displaystyle p_{i}\,\!}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9244e1fdd5dbac42cd1b427614f3986c87fbe3b1)). These two specifications are related through the material density by the equation ![Image 33: {\displaystyle \rho b_{i}=p_{i}\,\!}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3a124f098c0faff85ad4c6562f2c6ef43054ebab). Similarly, the intensity of electromagnetic forces depends upon the strength ([electric charge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_charge "Electric charge")) of the electromagnetic field.

The total body force applied to a continuous body is expressed as

Body forces and contact forces acting on the body lead to corresponding moments of force ([torques](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torque "Torque")) relative to a given point. Thus, the total applied torque ![Image 35: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {M}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2cc2abebd45ec020509a0ec548b67c9a2cb7cecd) about the origin is given by

![Image 36: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {M}}=\mathbf {M} _{C}+\mathbf {M} _{B}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/83d4ff4d88b8465ddb57950e40cf7c76a6944ff5)
In certain situations, not commonly considered in the analysis of the mechanical behavior of materials, it becomes necessary to include two other types of forces: these are _couple stresses_[[note 1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuum_mechanics#cite_note-14)[[note 2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuum_mechanics#cite_note-15) (surface couples,[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuum_mechanics#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIrgens2008-11) contact torques)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuum_mechanics#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChadwick1999-12) and _body moments_. Couple stresses are moments per unit area applied on a surface. Body moments, or body couples, are moments per unit volume or per unit mass applied to the volume of the body. Both are important in the analysis of stress for a polarized [dielectric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dielectric "Dielectric") solid under the action of an electric field, materials where the molecular structure is taken into consideration (_e.g._ bones), solids under the action of an external magnetic field, and the dislocation theory of metals.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuum_mechanics#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWu2004-7)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuum_mechanics#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFung1977-8)[_[page needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing\_sources "Wikipedia:Citing sources")_][[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuum_mechanics#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIrgens2008-11)

Materials that exhibit body couples and couple stresses in addition to moments produced exclusively by forces are called _polar materials_.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuum_mechanics#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFung1977-8)[_[page needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing\_sources "Wikipedia:Citing sources")_][[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuum_mechanics#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChadwick1999-12)_Non-polar materials_ are then those materials with only moments of forces. In the classical branches of continuum mechanics the development of the theory of stresses is based on non-polar materials.

Kinematics: motion and deformation
----------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Continuum_mechanics&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Kinematics: motion and deformation")]

[![Image 39](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Displacement_of_a_continuum.svg/500px-Displacement_of_a_continuum.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Displacement_of_a_continuum.svg)

Figure 2. Motion of a continuum body.

A change in the configuration of a continuum body results in a [displacement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Displacement_field_(mechanics) "Displacement field (mechanics)"). The displacement of a body has two components: a rigid-body displacement and a [deformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deformation_(mechanics) "Deformation (mechanics)"). A rigid-body displacement consists of a simultaneous translation and rotation of the body without changing its shape or size. Deformation implies the change in shape and/or size of the body from an initial or undeformed configuration ![Image 40: {\displaystyle \kappa _{0}({\mathcal {B}})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/001187fb472c2535da5d3fde2b64b5e82e305c50) to a current or deformed configuration ![Image 41: {\displaystyle \kappa _{t}({\mathcal {B}})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ab55799debd650ba2af18cea74f18f1b962eec53) (Figure 2).

The motion of a continuum body is a continuous time sequence of displacements. Thus, the material body will occupy different configurations at different times so that a particle occupies a series of points in space which describe a path line.

There is continuity during motion or deformation of a continuum body in the sense that:

*   The material points forming a closed curve at any instant will always form a closed curve at any subsequent time.
*   The material points forming a closed surface at any instant will always form a closed surface at any subsequent time and the matter within the closed surface will always remain within.

It is convenient to identify a reference configuration or initial condition which all subsequent configurations are referenced from. The reference configuration need not be one that the body will ever occupy. Often, the configuration at ![Image 42: {\displaystyle t=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/43469ec032d858feae5aa87029e22eaaf0109e9c) is considered the reference configuration, ![Image 43: {\displaystyle \kappa _{0}({\mathcal {B}})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/001187fb472c2535da5d3fde2b64b5e82e305c50). The components ![Image 44: {\displaystyle X_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af4a0955af42beb5f85aa05fb8c07abedc13990d) of the position vector ![Image 45: {\displaystyle \mathbf {X} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9f75966a2f9d5672136fa9401ee1e75008f95ffd) of a particle, taken with respect to the reference configuration, are called the material or reference coordinates.

When analyzing the motion or [deformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deformation_(mechanics) "Deformation (mechanics)") of solids, or the [flow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluid_mechanics "Fluid mechanics") of fluids, it is necessary to describe the sequence or evolution of configurations throughout time. One description for motion is made in terms of the material or referential coordinates, called material description or Lagrangian description.

### Lagrangian description

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Continuum_mechanics&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Lagrangian description")]

In the Lagrangian description the position and physical properties of the particles are described in terms of the material or referential coordinates and time. In this case **the reference configuration is the configuration at ![Image 46: {\displaystyle t=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/43469ec032d858feae5aa87029e22eaaf0109e9c)**. An observer standing in the frame of reference observes the changes in the position and physical properties as the material body moves in space as time progresses. The results obtained are independent of the choice of initial time and reference configuration, ![Image 47: {\displaystyle \kappa _{0}({\mathcal {B}})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/001187fb472c2535da5d3fde2b64b5e82e305c50). This description is normally used in [solid mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_mechanics "Solid mechanics").

In the Lagrangian description, the motion of a continuum body is expressed by the mapping function ![Image 48: {\displaystyle \chi (\cdot )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bd43bf5c5fc4d0e541636765f3118c79e3be0b57) (Figure 2),

![Image 49: {\displaystyle \mathbf {x} =\chi (\mathbf {X} ,t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9808bd9c87f93c558da291714fdd50fe82984e10)
which is a mapping of the initial configuration ![Image 50: {\displaystyle \kappa _{0}({\mathcal {B}})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/001187fb472c2535da5d3fde2b64b5e82e305c50) onto the current configuration ![Image 51: {\displaystyle \kappa _{t}({\mathcal {B}})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ab55799debd650ba2af18cea74f18f1b962eec53), giving a geometrical correspondence between them, i.e. giving the position vector ![Image 52: {\displaystyle \mathbf {x} =x_{i}\mathbf {e} _{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0141632f990f463206b9ca793785195eea6b8fc4) that a particle ![Image 53: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab), with a position vector ![Image 54: {\displaystyle \mathbf {X} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9f75966a2f9d5672136fa9401ee1e75008f95ffd) in the undeformed or reference configuration ![Image 55: {\displaystyle \kappa _{0}({\mathcal {B}})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/001187fb472c2535da5d3fde2b64b5e82e305c50), will occupy in the current or deformed configuration ![Image 56: {\displaystyle \kappa _{t}({\mathcal {B}})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ab55799debd650ba2af18cea74f18f1b962eec53) at time ![Image 57: {\displaystyle t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/65658b7b223af9e1acc877d848888ecdb4466560). The components ![Image 58: {\displaystyle x_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e87000dd6142b81d041896a30fe58f0c3acb2158) are called the spatial coordinates.

Physical and kinematic properties ![Image 59: {\displaystyle P_{ij\ldots }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/89dc778749aab3fef99766b136f3e1c801723225), i.e. thermodynamic properties and flow velocity, which describe or characterize features of the material body, are expressed as continuous functions of position and time, i.e. ![Image 60: {\displaystyle P_{ij\ldots }=P_{ij\ldots }(\mathbf {X} ,t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47107919e8793bd2691a3390fc32f3392e9ba486).

The material derivative of any property ![Image 61: {\displaystyle P_{ij\ldots }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/89dc778749aab3fef99766b136f3e1c801723225) of a continuum, which may be a scalar, vector, or tensor, is the time rate of change of that property for a specific group of particles of the moving continuum body. The material derivative is also known as the _substantial derivative_, or _comoving derivative_, or _convective derivative_. It can be thought as the rate at which the property changes when measured by an observer traveling with that group of particles.

In the Lagrangian description, the material derivative of ![Image 62: {\displaystyle P_{ij\ldots }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/89dc778749aab3fef99766b136f3e1c801723225) is simply the partial derivative with respect to time, and the position vector ![Image 63: {\displaystyle \mathbf {X} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9f75966a2f9d5672136fa9401ee1e75008f95ffd) is held constant as it does not change with time. Thus, we have

![Image 64: {\displaystyle {\frac {d}{dt}}[P_{ij\ldots }(\mathbf {X} ,t)]={\frac {\partial }{\partial t}}[P_{ij\ldots }(\mathbf {X} ,t)]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d57c030f01fac469178b29d8f47a3774b6759949)
The instantaneous position ![Image 65: {\displaystyle \mathbf {x} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/32adf004df5eb0a8c7fd8c0b6b7405183c5a5ef2) is a property of a particle, and its material derivative is the _instantaneous flow velocity_![Image 66: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35c1866e359fbfd2e0f606c725ba5cc37a5195d6) of the particle. Therefore, the flow velocity field of the continuum is given by

![Image 67: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} ={\dot {\mathbf {x} }}={\frac {d\mathbf {x} }{dt}}={\frac {\partial \chi (\mathbf {X} ,t)}{\partial t}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2391611226b74b9b0e341dbfc21e084718f5d60f)
Similarly, the acceleration field is given by

![Image 68: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} ={\dot {\mathbf {v} }}={\ddot {\mathbf {x} }}={\frac {d^{2}\mathbf {x} }{dt^{2}}}={\frac {\partial ^{2}\chi (\mathbf {X} ,t)}{\partial t^{2}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b833646a9dab612561bd53db845522f65e9b2043)
Continuity in the Lagrangian description is expressed by the spatial and temporal continuity of the mapping from the reference configuration to the current configuration of the material points. All physical quantities characterizing the continuum are described this way. In this sense, the function ![Image 69: {\displaystyle \chi (\cdot )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bd43bf5c5fc4d0e541636765f3118c79e3be0b57) and ![Image 70: {\displaystyle P_{ij\ldots }(\cdot )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0ded0f0d9078c3fcc25aa6a8f7cb001c3c56131b) are single-valued and continuous, with continuous derivatives with respect to space and time to whatever order is required, usually to the second or third.

### Eulerian description

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Continuum_mechanics&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Eulerian description")]

Continuity allows for the inverse of ![Image 71: {\displaystyle \chi (\cdot )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bd43bf5c5fc4d0e541636765f3118c79e3be0b57) to trace backwards where the particle currently located at ![Image 72: {\displaystyle \mathbf {x} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/32adf004df5eb0a8c7fd8c0b6b7405183c5a5ef2) was located in the initial or referenced configuration ![Image 73: {\displaystyle \kappa _{0}({\mathcal {B}})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/001187fb472c2535da5d3fde2b64b5e82e305c50). In this case the description of motion is made in terms of the spatial coordinates, in which case is called the spatial description or Eulerian description, i.e. **the current configuration is taken as the reference configuration**.

The Eulerian description, introduced by [d'Alembert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D%27Alembert "D'Alembert"), focuses on the current configuration ![Image 74: {\displaystyle \kappa _{t}({\mathcal {B}})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ab55799debd650ba2af18cea74f18f1b962eec53), giving attention to what is occurring at a fixed point in space as time progresses, instead of giving attention to individual particles as they move through space and time. This approach is conveniently applied in the study of [fluid flow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluid_mechanics "Fluid mechanics") where the kinematic property of greatest interest is the rate at which change is taking place rather than the shape of the body of fluid at a reference time.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuum_mechanics#cite_note-FOOTNOTESpencer198083-16)

Mathematically, the motion of a continuum using the Eulerian description is expressed by the mapping function

![Image 75: {\displaystyle \mathbf {X} =\chi ^{-1}(\mathbf {x} ,t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e286b2ddb237bb5db5f07caadc00918a8d3f6b59)
which provides a tracing of the particle which now occupies the position ![Image 76: {\displaystyle \mathbf {x} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/32adf004df5eb0a8c7fd8c0b6b7405183c5a5ef2) in the current configuration ![Image 77: {\displaystyle \kappa _{t}({\mathcal {B}})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ab55799debd650ba2af18cea74f18f1b962eec53) to its original position ![Image 78: {\displaystyle \mathbf {X} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9f75966a2f9d5672136fa9401ee1e75008f95ffd) in the initial configuration ![Image 79: {\displaystyle \kappa _{0}({\mathcal {B}})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/001187fb472c2535da5d3fde2b64b5e82e305c50).

A necessary and sufficient condition for this inverse function to exist is that the determinant of the [Jacobian matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacobian_matrix_and_determinant "Jacobian matrix and determinant"), often referred to simply as the Jacobian, should be different from zero. Thus,

In the Eulerian description, the physical properties ![Image 81: {\displaystyle P_{ij\ldots }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/89dc778749aab3fef99766b136f3e1c801723225) are expressed as

![Image 82: {\displaystyle P_{ij\ldots }=P_{ij\ldots }(\mathbf {X} ,t)=P_{ij\ldots }[\chi ^{-1}(\mathbf {x} ,t),t]=p_{ij\ldots }(\mathbf {x} ,t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8285f8b8ab3d209b5b91d73a3b79e2bc66dcb2cf)

The material derivative of ![Image 85: {\displaystyle p_{ij\ldots }(\mathbf {x} ,t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9cadafd3385e252ff02f6be5a8c67fca2ad369bc), using the chain rule, is then

The first term on the right-hand side of this equation gives the _local rate of change_ of the property ![Image 87: {\displaystyle p_{ij\ldots }(\mathbf {x} ,t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9cadafd3385e252ff02f6be5a8c67fca2ad369bc) occurring at position ![Image 88: {\displaystyle \mathbf {x} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/32adf004df5eb0a8c7fd8c0b6b7405183c5a5ef2). The second term of the right-hand side is the _convective rate of change_ and expresses the contribution of the particle changing position in space (motion).

Continuity in the Eulerian description is expressed by the spatial and temporal continuity and continuous differentiability of the flow velocity field. All physical quantities are defined this way at each instant of time, in the current configuration, as a function of the vector position ![Image 89: {\displaystyle \mathbf {x} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/32adf004df5eb0a8c7fd8c0b6b7405183c5a5ef2).

Governing equations
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Continuum_mechanics&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Governing equations")]

Continuum mechanics deals with the behavior of materials that can be approximated as continuous for certain length and time scales. The equations that govern the mechanics of such materials include the balance laws for [mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservation_of_mass "Conservation of mass"), [momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservation_of_momentum "Conservation of momentum"), and [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservation_of_energy "Conservation of energy"). [Kinematic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematics "Kinematics") relations and [constitutive equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitutive_equations "Constitutive equations") are needed to complete the system of governing equations. Physical restrictions on the form of the constitutive relations can be applied by requiring that the [second law of thermodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_law_of_thermodynamics "Second law of thermodynamics") be satisfied under all conditions. In the continuum mechanics of solids, the second law of thermodynamics is satisfied if the [Clausius–Duhem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clausius%E2%80%93Duhem_inequality "Clausius–Duhem inequality") form of the entropy inequality is satisfied.

The balance laws express the idea that the rate of change of a quantity (mass, momentum, energy) in a volume must arise from three causes:

1.   the physical quantity itself flows through the surface that bounds the volume,
2.   there is a source of the physical quantity on the surface of the volume, or/and,
3.   there is a source of the physical quantity inside the volume.

Let ![Image 108: {\displaystyle \Omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/24b0d5ca6f381068d756f6337c08e0af9d1eeb6f) be the body (an open subset of Euclidean space) and let ![Image 109: {\displaystyle \partial \Omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/16feddaad462c2a1c9efdaeee062a0484a023fde) be its surface (the boundary of ![Image 110: {\displaystyle \Omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/24b0d5ca6f381068d756f6337c08e0af9d1eeb6f)).

where ![Image 112: {\displaystyle \mathbf {X} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9f75966a2f9d5672136fa9401ee1e75008f95ffd) is the position of a point in the initial configuration and ![Image 113: {\displaystyle \mathbf {x} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/32adf004df5eb0a8c7fd8c0b6b7405183c5a5ef2) is the location of the same point in the deformed configuration.

### Clausius–Duhem inequality

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Continuum_mechanics&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Clausius–Duhem inequality")]

The [Clausius–Duhem inequality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clausius%E2%80%93Duhem_inequality "Clausius–Duhem inequality") can be used to express the second law of thermodynamics for elastic-plastic materials. This inequality is a statement concerning the irreversibility of natural processes, especially when energy dissipation is involved.

Just like in the balance laws in the previous section, we assume that there is a flux of a quantity, a source of the quantity, and an internal density of the quantity per unit mass. The quantity of interest in this case is the entropy. Thus, we assume that there is an entropy flux, an entropy source, an internal mass density ![Image 158: {\displaystyle \rho }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1f7d439671d1289b6a816e6af7a304be40608d64) and an internal specific entropy (i.e. entropy per unit mass) ![Image 159: {\displaystyle \eta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e4d701857cf5fbec133eebaf94deadf722537f64) in the region of interest.

Let ![Image 160: {\displaystyle \Omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/24b0d5ca6f381068d756f6337c08e0af9d1eeb6f) be such a region and let ![Image 161: {\displaystyle \partial \Omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/16feddaad462c2a1c9efdaeee062a0484a023fde) be its boundary. Then the second law of thermodynamics states that the rate of increase of ![Image 162: {\displaystyle \eta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e4d701857cf5fbec133eebaf94deadf722537f64) in this region is greater than or equal to the sum of that supplied to ![Image 163: {\displaystyle \Omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/24b0d5ca6f381068d756f6337c08e0af9d1eeb6f) (as a flux or from internal sources) and the change of the internal entropy density ![Image 164: {\displaystyle \rho \eta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bfd1cdcf94869d2e9ea6bfd39c59ae9dfde6b96b) due to material flowing in and out of the region.

Let ![Image 165: {\displaystyle \partial \Omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/16feddaad462c2a1c9efdaeee062a0484a023fde) move with a flow velocity ![Image 166: {\displaystyle u_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6c7339b0b08d3045d72ddbacf4a3e50d24640c2d) and let particles inside ![Image 167: {\displaystyle \Omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/24b0d5ca6f381068d756f6337c08e0af9d1eeb6f) have velocities ![Image 168: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35c1866e359fbfd2e0f606c725ba5cc37a5195d6). Let ![Image 169: {\displaystyle \mathbf {n} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4a720c341f39f52fd96028dab83edd34d400be46) be the unit outward normal to the surface ![Image 170: {\displaystyle \partial \Omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/16feddaad462c2a1c9efdaeee062a0484a023fde). Let ![Image 171: {\displaystyle \rho }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1f7d439671d1289b6a816e6af7a304be40608d64) be the density of matter in the region, ![Image 172: {\displaystyle {\bar {q}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/df40aaaa5f6213d6e896be45f87eabeb7bfd4806) be the entropy flux at the surface, and ![Image 173: {\displaystyle r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d1ecb613aa2984f0576f70f86650b7c2a132538) be the entropy source per unit mass. Then the entropy inequality may be written as

![Image 174: {\displaystyle {\cfrac {d}{dt}}\left(\int _{\Omega }\rho ~\eta ~{\text{dV}}\right)\geq \int _{\partial \Omega }\rho ~\eta ~(u_{n}-\mathbf {v} \cdot \mathbf {n} )~{\text{dA}}+\int _{\partial \Omega }{\bar {q}}~{\text{dA}}+\int _{\Omega }\rho ~r~{\text{dV}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e716427753c5e9aa236683709ef1d49875395426)
The scalar entropy flux can be related to the vector flux at the surface by the relation ![Image 175: {\displaystyle {\bar {q}}=-{\boldsymbol {\psi }}(\mathbf {x} )\cdot \mathbf {n} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2c1566120ea6c5a7add7be80dd8d5ac44e631826). Under the assumption of incrementally isothermal conditions, we have

![Image 176: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {\psi }}(\mathbf {x} )={\cfrac {\mathbf {q} (\mathbf {x} )}{T}}~;~~r={\cfrac {s}{T}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e3d3d008fce4cee089bb53381ecc3c2ea47acdbc)
where ![Image 177: {\displaystyle \mathbf {q} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7be005a326b7ac3fe4c24bca391369f44c4c2876) is the heat flux vector, ![Image 178: {\displaystyle s}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/01d131dfd7673938b947072a13a9744fe997e632) is an energy source per unit mass, and ![Image 179: {\displaystyle T}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ec7200acd984a1d3a3d7dc455e262fbe54f7f6e0) is the absolute temperature of a material point at ![Image 180: {\displaystyle \mathbf {x} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/32adf004df5eb0a8c7fd8c0b6b7405183c5a5ef2) at time ![Image 181: {\displaystyle t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/65658b7b223af9e1acc877d848888ecdb4466560).

We then have the Clausius–Duhem inequality in integral form:

![Image 182: {\displaystyle {{\cfrac {d}{dt}}\left(\int _{\Omega }\rho ~\eta ~{\text{dV}}\right)\geq \int _{\partial \Omega }\rho ~\eta ~(u_{n}-\mathbf {v} \cdot \mathbf {n} )~{\text{dA}}-\int _{\partial \Omega }{\cfrac {\mathbf {q} \cdot \mathbf {n} }{T}}~{\text{dA}}+\int _{\Omega }{\cfrac {\rho ~s}{T}}~{\text{dV}}.}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/915e081a74c4e66f27c0648c2b5554be7b90a769)
We can show that the entropy inequality may be written in differential form as

![Image 183: {\displaystyle {\rho ~{\dot {\eta }}\geq -{\boldsymbol {\nabla }}\cdot \left({\cfrac {\mathbf {q} }{T}}\right)+{\cfrac {\rho ~s}{T}}.}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6a25347e77891f46a0ccfef248b7ee3793cf8557)
In terms of the Cauchy stress and the internal energy, the Clausius–Duhem inequality may be written as

![Image 184: {\displaystyle {\rho ~({\dot {e}}-T~{\dot {\eta }})-{\boldsymbol {\sigma }}:{\boldsymbol {\nabla }}\mathbf {v} \leq -{\cfrac {\mathbf {q} \cdot {\boldsymbol {\nabla }}T}{T}}.}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/282645ebed72b90150d5b1af72754fcf7d65ed32)
The validity of the continuum assumption may be verified by a theoretical analysis, in which either some clear periodicity is identified or statistical [homogeneity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homogeneity_(physics) "Homogeneity (physics)") and [ergodicity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ergodicity "Ergodicity") of the [microstructure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microstructure "Microstructure") exist. More specifically, the continuum hypothesis hinges on the concepts of a representative elementary volume and separation of scales based on the Hill–Mandel condition. This condition provides a link between an experimentalist's and a theoretician's viewpoint on [constitutive equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitutive_equation "Constitutive equation") (linear and nonlinear elastic/inelastic or coupled fields) as well as a way of spatial and statistical averaging of the microstructure. When the separation of scales does not hold, or when one wants to establish a continuum of a finer resolution than the size of the representative volume element (RVE), a statistical volume element (SVE) is employed, which results in random continuum fields. The latter then provide a micromechanics basis for stochastic finite elements (SFE). The levels of SVE and RVE link continuum mechanics to [statistical mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistical_physics#Statistical_mechanics "Statistical physics"). Experimentally, the RVE can only be evaluated when the constitutive response is spatially homogenous.
