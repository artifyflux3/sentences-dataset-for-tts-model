This article is about gravitation and electromagnetism. For the mathematical generalization of [K theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K_theory "K theory"), see [KK-theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/KK-theory "KK-theory").

In [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics"), **Kaluza–Klein theory** (**KK theory**) is a classical [unified field theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unified_field_theory "Unified field theory") of [gravitation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitation "Gravitation") and [electromagnetism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetism "Electromagnetism") built around the idea of a [fifth dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Five-dimensional_space#Physics "Five-dimensional space") beyond the common 4D of [space and time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spacetime "Spacetime") and considered an important precursor to [string theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/String_theory "String theory"). In their setup, the vacuum has the usual 3 dimensions of space and one dimension of time but with another microscopic extra spatial dimension in the shape of a tiny circle. [Gunnar Nordström](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gunnar_Nordstr%C3%B6m "Gunnar Nordström") had an earlier, similar idea. But in that case, a fifth component was added to the electromagnetic vector potential, representing the Newtonian gravitational potential, and writing the Maxwell equations in five dimensions.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-nrd-1)

The five-dimensional (5D) theory developed in three steps. The original hypothesis came from [Theodor Kaluza](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theodor_Kaluza "Theodor Kaluza"), who sent his results to [Albert Einstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Einstein "Albert Einstein") in 1919[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-2) and published them in 1921.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-kal-3) Kaluza presented a purely classical extension of [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity") to 5D, with a metric [tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor "Tensor") of 15 components. Ten components are identified with the 4D spacetime metric, four components with the electromagnetic vector potential, and one component with an unidentified [scalar field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_field "Scalar field") sometimes called the "[radion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radion_(physics) "Radion (physics)")" or the "dilaton". Correspondingly, the 5D Einstein equations yield the 4D [Einstein field equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_field_equations "Einstein field equations"), the [Maxwell equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxwell_equations "Maxwell equations") for the [electromagnetic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_field "Electromagnetic field"), and an equation for the scalar field. Kaluza also introduced the "cylinder condition" hypothesis, that no component of the five-dimensional metric depends on the fifth dimension. Without this restriction, terms are introduced that involve derivatives of the fields with respect to the fifth coordinate, and this extra degree of freedom makes the mathematics of the fully variable 5D relativity enormously complex. Standard 4D physics seems to manifest this "cylinder condition" and, along with it, simpler mathematics.

In 1926, [Oskar Klein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oskar_Klein "Oskar Klein") gave Kaluza's classical five-dimensional theory a quantum interpretation,[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-KZ-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-KN-5) to accord with the then-recent discoveries of [Werner Heisenberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Werner_Heisenberg "Werner Heisenberg") and [Erwin Schrödinger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erwin_Schr%C3%B6dinger "Erwin Schrödinger"). Klein introduced the hypothesis that the fifth dimension was curled up and microscopic, to explain the cylinder condition. Klein suggested that the geometry of the extra fifth dimension could take the form of a circle, with the radius of 10−30 cm. More precisely, the radius of the circular dimension is 23 times the [Planck length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_units "Planck units"), which in turn is of the order of 10−33 cm.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-KN-5) Klein also made a contribution to the classical theory by providing a properly normalized 5D metric.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-KZ-4) Work continued on the Kaluza field theory during the 1930s by Einstein and colleagues at [Princeton University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Princeton_University "Princeton University").

In the 1940s, the classical theory was completed, and the full field equations including the scalar field were obtained by three independent research groups:[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-gon-6) Yves Thiry,[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-7)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-thry-8)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-9) working in France on his dissertation under [André Lichnerowicz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Lichnerowicz "André Lichnerowicz"); [Pascual Jordan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pascual_Jordan "Pascual Jordan"), Günther Ludwig, and Claus Müller in Germany,[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-jor1-10)[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-jor2-11)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-12)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-jor3-13)[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-14) with critical input from [Wolfgang Pauli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Pauli "Wolfgang Pauli") and [Markus Fierz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Markus_Fierz "Markus Fierz"); and [Paul Scherrer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Scherrer "Paul Scherrer")[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-15)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-16)[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-17) working alone in Switzerland. Jordan's work led to the scalar–tensor theory of [Brans–Dicke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brans%E2%80%93Dicke_theory "Brans–Dicke theory");[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-18)[Carl H. Brans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_H._Brans "Carl H. Brans") and [Robert H. Dicke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_H._Dicke "Robert H. Dicke") were apparently unaware of Thiry or Scherrer. The full Kaluza equations under the cylinder condition are quite complex, and most English-language reviews, as well as the English translations of Thiry, contain some errors. The curvature tensors for the complete Kaluza equations were evaluated using [tensor-algebra software](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_software "Tensor software") in 2015,[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-LLW-19) verifying results of J. A. Ferrari[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-fri-20) and R. Coquereaux & G. Esposito-Farese.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-coq-21) The 5D covariant form of the energy–momentum source terms is treated by L. L. Williams.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-LLW2-22)

In his 1921 article,[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-kal-3) Kaluza established all the elements of the classical five-dimensional theory: the [Kaluza–Klein metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_metric "Kaluza–Klein metric"), the [Kaluza–Klein–Einstein field equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein%E2%80%93Einstein_field_equations "Kaluza–Klein–Einstein field equations"), the equations of motion, the stress–energy tensor, and the cylinder condition. With no [free parameters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_parameter "Free parameter"), it merely extends general relativity to five dimensions. One starts by hypothesizing a form of the five-dimensional Kaluza–Klein metric ![Image 1: {\displaystyle {\widetilde {g}}_{ab}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6385a8c6c8614189b81d4dc61a317a23356b78cd), where Latin indices span five dimensions. Let one also introduce the four-dimensional spacetime metric ![Image 2: {\displaystyle {g}_{\mu \nu }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/736bbb3255629f109526646853257532f083c744), where Greek indices span the usual four dimensions of space and time; a 4-vector ![Image 3: {\displaystyle A^{\mu }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e1da55ba40017c25d210f7e269efb2d6d539a1b6) identified with the electromagnetic vector potential; and a scalar field ![Image 4: {\displaystyle \phi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/72b1f30316670aee6270a28334bdf4f5072cdde4). Then decompose the 5D metric so that the 4D metric is framed by the electromagnetic vector potential, with the scalar field at the fifth diagonal. This can be visualized as

![Image 5: {\displaystyle {\widetilde {g}}_{ab}\equiv {\begin{bmatrix}g_{\mu \nu }+\phi ^{2}A_{\mu }A_{\nu }&\phi ^{2}A_{\mu }\\\phi ^{2}A_{\nu }&\phi ^{2}\end{bmatrix}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/feb94ce3447d4bb9bf2d8d5615e2fa37ec5cbe9e)
One can write more precisely

![Image 6: {\displaystyle {\widetilde {g}}_{\mu \nu }\equiv g_{\mu \nu }+\phi ^{2}A_{\mu }A_{\nu },\qquad {\widetilde {g}}_{5\nu }\equiv {\widetilde {g}}_{\nu 5}\equiv \phi ^{2}A_{\nu },\qquad {\widetilde {g}}_{55}\equiv \phi ^{2},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a605f6d3f184b1cba8aa363a62f7effde49b54ab)
where the index ![Image 7: {\displaystyle 5}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/29483407999b8763f0ea335cf715a6a5e809f44b) indicates the fifth coordinate by convention, even though the first four coordinates are indexed with 0, 1, 2, and 3. The associated inverse metric is

![Image 8: {\displaystyle {\widetilde {g}}^{ab}\equiv {\begin{bmatrix}g^{\mu \nu }&-A^{\mu }\\-A^{\nu }&g_{\alpha \beta }A^{\alpha }A^{\beta }+{\frac {1}{\phi ^{2}}}\end{bmatrix}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3c753a3b3fa7f09805c3c177938a1a85756ff1ee)
This decomposition is quite general, and all terms are dimensionless. Kaluza then applies the machinery of standard [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity") to this metric. The field equations are obtained from five-dimensional [Einstein equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_equations "Einstein equations"), and the equations of motion from the five-dimensional geodesic hypothesis. The resulting field equations provide both the equations of general relativity and of electrodynamics; the equations of motion provide the four-dimensional [geodesic equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic_equation "Geodesic equation") and the [Lorentz force law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentz_force_law "Lorentz force law"), and one finds that electric charge is identified with motion in the fifth dimension.

The hypothesis for the metric implies an invariant five-dimensional length element ![Image 9: {\displaystyle ds}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5f0fb36e4308227d3e4a1f809c2571ec02527100):

![Image 10: {\displaystyle ds^{2}\equiv {\widetilde {g}}_{ab}\,dx^{a}\,dx^{b}=g_{\mu \nu }\,dx^{\mu }\,dx^{\nu }+\phi ^{2}(A_{\nu }\,dx^{\nu }+dx^{5})^{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/66fe7d4879a3224e183522c6dfe689e1b6f4d75b)

Field equations from the Kaluza hypothesis
------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Field equations from the Kaluza hypothesis")]

The [Kaluza–Klein–Einstein field equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein%E2%80%93Einstein_field_equations "Kaluza–Klein–Einstein field equations") of the five-dimensional theory were never adequately provided by Kaluza or Klein because they ignored the [scalar field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_field "Scalar field"). The full Kaluza field equations are generally attributed to Thiry,[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-thry-8) who obtained vacuum field equations, although Kaluza[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-kal-3) originally provided a stress–energy tensor for his theory, and Thiry included a stress–energy tensor in his thesis. But as described by Gonner,[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-gon-6) several independent groups worked on the field equations in the 1940s and earlier. Thiry is perhaps best known only because an English translation was provided by Applequist, Chodos, & Freund in their review book.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-23) Applequist et al. also provided an English translation of Kaluza's article. Translations of the three (1946, 1947, 1948) Jordan articles can be found on the [ResearchGate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ResearchGate "ResearchGate") and [Academia.edu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Academia.edu "Academia.edu") archives.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-jor1-10)[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-jor2-11)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-jor3-13) The first correct English-language Kaluza field equations, including the scalar field, were provided by Williams.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-LLW-19)

To obtain the 5D Kaluza–Klein–Einstein field equations, the 5D [Kaluza–Klein–Christoffel symbols](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein%E2%80%93Christoffel_symbol "Kaluza–Klein–Christoffel symbol")![Image 11: {\displaystyle {\widetilde {\Gamma }}_{bc}^{a}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/397e7bce431189d6cbf35febfadb6ad9653faadb) are calculated from the 5D Kaluza–Klein metric ![Image 12: {\displaystyle {\widetilde {g}}_{ab}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6385a8c6c8614189b81d4dc61a317a23356b78cd), and the 5D [Kaluza–Klein–Ricci tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein%E2%80%93Ricci_tensor "Kaluza–Klein–Ricci tensor")![Image 13: {\displaystyle {\widetilde {R}}_{ab}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c7135160330a3cc9274407baec584720429d88d4) is calculated from the 5D [connections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connection_(mathematics) "Connection (mathematics)").

The classic results of Thiry and other authors presume the cylinder condition:

![Image 14: {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial {\widetilde {g}}_{ab}}{\partial x^{5}}}=0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b0b8915a8464a57ca1446e9a32210fb196ed4767)
Without this assumption, the field equations become much more complex, providing many more degrees of freedom that can be identified with various new fields. Paul Wesson and colleagues have pursued relaxation of the cylinder condition to gain extra terms that can be identified with the matter fields,[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-24) for which Kaluza[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-kal-3) otherwise inserted a stress–energy tensor by hand.

It has been an objection to the original Kaluza hypothesis to invoke the fifth dimension only to negate its dynamics. But Thiry argued[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-gon-6) that the interpretation of the Lorentz force law in terms of a five-dimensional [geodesic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic "Geodesic") militates strongly for a fifth dimension irrespective of the cylinder condition. Most authors have therefore employed the cylinder condition in deriving the field equations. Furthermore, vacuum equations are typically assumed for which

![Image 15: {\displaystyle {\widetilde {R}}_{ab}=0,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ef7d5ad7118081fb1385ff70f960ae76f1d88762)
where

![Image 16: {\displaystyle {\widetilde {R}}_{ab}\equiv \partial _{c}{\widetilde {\Gamma }}_{ab}^{c}-\partial _{b}{\widetilde {\Gamma }}_{ca}^{c}+{\widetilde {\Gamma }}_{cd}^{c}{\widetilde {\Gamma }}_{ab}^{d}-{\widetilde {\Gamma }}_{bd}^{c}{\widetilde {\Gamma }}_{ac}^{d}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bbfb1f094fa874c26b2ad07f308f7f3bd97ebb25)
and

![Image 17: {\displaystyle {\widetilde {\Gamma }}_{bc}^{a}\equiv {\frac {1}{2}}{\widetilde {g}}^{ad}(\partial _{b}{\widetilde {g}}_{dc}+\partial _{c}{\widetilde {g}}_{db}-\partial _{d}{\widetilde {g}}_{bc}).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3cb03fc24e8fa8a264fca4fe64a45bf587b59fbd)
The vacuum field equations obtained in this way by Thiry[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-thry-8) and Jordan's group[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-jor1-10)[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-jor2-11)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-jor3-13) are as follows.

The field equation for ![Image 18: {\displaystyle \phi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/72b1f30316670aee6270a28334bdf4f5072cdde4) is obtained from

![Image 19: {\displaystyle {\widetilde {R}}_{55}=0\Rightarrow \Box \phi ={\frac {1}{4}}\phi ^{3}F^{\alpha \beta }F_{\alpha \beta },}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ed2b6692639801796a99e3c272ea48d715388355)
where ![Image 20: {\displaystyle F_{\alpha \beta }\equiv \partial _{\alpha }A_{\beta }-\partial _{\beta }A_{\alpha },}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8c8fb188124c462605c486377058c9901e9453df)![Image 21: {\displaystyle \Box \equiv g^{\mu \nu }\nabla _{\mu }\nabla _{\nu },}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/342cf8deef51bcf72dc6da26ef101d2e5a2f27bb) and ![Image 22: {\displaystyle \nabla _{\mu }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ce7b8d45eb6e24772c91595f15db13a5e3c7991d) is a standard, 4D [covariant derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covariant_derivative "Covariant derivative"). It shows that the electromagnetic field is a source for the [scalar field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_field "Scalar field"). Note that the scalar field cannot be set to a constant without constraining the electromagnetic field. The earlier treatments by Kaluza and Klein did not have an adequate description of the scalar field and did not realize the implied constraint on the electromagnetic field by assuming the scalar field to be constant.

The field equation for ![Image 23: {\displaystyle A^{\nu }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/723213348d681d164c76c7245099fae4f22a5fdc) is obtained from

![Image 24: {\displaystyle {\widetilde {R}}_{5\alpha }=0={\frac {1}{2\phi }}g^{\beta \mu }\nabla _{\mu }(\phi ^{3}F_{\alpha \beta })-A_{\alpha }\phi \Box \phi .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5cf45248a7382b8e77a18bf12e1cd7ea2e87d6f2)
It has the form of the vacuum Maxwell equations if the scalar field is constant.

The field equation for the 4D Ricci tensor ![Image 25: {\displaystyle R_{\mu \nu }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a5ee22d1a052bee0115efb8b5ffdaf10b04e42aa) is obtained from

![Image 26: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}{\widetilde {R}}_{\mu \nu }-{\frac {1}{2}}{\widetilde {g}}_{\mu \nu }{\widetilde {R}}&=0\Rightarrow \\R_{\mu \nu }-{\frac {1}{2}}g_{\mu \nu }R&={\frac {1}{2}}\phi ^{2}\left(g^{\alpha \beta }F_{\mu \alpha }F_{\nu \beta }-{\frac {1}{4}}g_{\mu \nu }F_{\alpha \beta }F^{\alpha \beta }\right)+{\frac {1}{\phi }}(\nabla _{\mu }\nabla _{\nu }\phi -g_{\mu \nu }\Box \phi ),\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0b196783ed870814839e8fe074cc614225d4b211)
where ![Image 27: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) is the standard 4D Ricci scalar.

This equation shows the remarkable result, called the "Kaluza miracle", that the precise form for the [electromagnetic stress–energy tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_stress%E2%80%93energy_tensor "Electromagnetic stress–energy tensor") emerges from the 5D vacuum equations as a source in the 4D equations: field from the vacuum. This relation allows the definitive identification of ![Image 28: {\displaystyle A^{\mu }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e1da55ba40017c25d210f7e269efb2d6d539a1b6) with the electromagnetic vector potential. Therefore, the field needs to be rescaled with a conversion constant ![Image 29: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40) such that ![Image 30: {\displaystyle A^{\mu }\to kA^{\mu }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5ee7d1c697751d9411388d9086627aefa37779d9).

The relation above shows that we must have

![Image 31: {\displaystyle {\frac {k^{2}}{2}}={\frac {8\pi G}{c^{4}}}{\frac {1}{\mu _{0}}}={\frac {2G}{c^{2}}}4\pi \epsilon _{0},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5211e66c56cf949e87384ad8320d3b7c6c1738cf)
where ![Image 32: {\displaystyle G}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5f3c8921a3b352de45446a6789b104458c9f90b) is the [gravitational constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_constant "Gravitational constant"), and ![Image 33: {\displaystyle \mu _{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fe2fd9b8decb38a3cd158e7b6c0c6e2d987fefcc) is the [permeability of free space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permeability_of_free_space "Permeability of free space"). In the Kaluza theory, the gravitational constant can be understood as an electromagnetic coupling constant in the metric. There is also a stress–energy tensor for the scalar field. The scalar field behaves like a variable gravitational constant, in terms of modulating the coupling of electromagnetic stress–energy to spacetime curvature. The sign of ![Image 34: {\displaystyle \phi ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1b9ddce0bebf1f6ab02aecb833a96e05fd73cbe9) in the metric is fixed by correspondence with 4D theory so that electromagnetic energy densities are positive. It is often assumed that the fifth coordinate is spacelike in its signature in the metric.

In the presence of matter, the 5D vacuum condition cannot be assumed. Indeed, Kaluza did not assume it. The full field equations require evaluation of the 5D [Kaluza–Klein–Einstein tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein%E2%80%93Einstein_tensor "Kaluza–Klein–Einstein tensor")

![Image 35: {\displaystyle {\widetilde {G}}_{ab}\equiv {\widetilde {R}}_{ab}-{\frac {1}{2}}{\widetilde {g}}_{ab}{\widetilde {R}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d1adc1f8bb4ed865af493daa181a737450ef8e73)
as seen in the recovery of the electromagnetic stress–energy tensor above. The 5D curvature tensors are complex, and most English-language reviews contain errors in either ![Image 36: {\displaystyle {\widetilde {G}}_{ab}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/97614eb5209040527937fca3dd57f23169d0b100) or ![Image 37: {\displaystyle {\widetilde {R}}_{ab}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c7135160330a3cc9274407baec584720429d88d4), as does the English translation of Thiry.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-thry-8) In 2015, a complete set of 5D curvature tensors under the cylinder condition, evaluated using tensor-algebra software, was produced.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-LLW-19)

Equations of motion from the Kaluza hypothesis
----------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Equations of motion from the Kaluza hypothesis")]

The equations of motion are obtained from the five-dimensional geodesic hypothesis[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-kal-3) in terms of a 5-velocity ![Image 38: {\displaystyle {\widetilde {U}}^{a}\equiv dx^{a}/ds}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ba16a00f67238b265881ddf18389f5f7253886fa):

![Image 39: {\displaystyle {\widetilde {U}}^{b}{\widetilde {\nabla }}_{b}{\widetilde {U}}^{a}={\frac {d{\widetilde {U}}^{a}}{ds}}+{\widetilde {\Gamma }}_{bc}^{a}{\widetilde {U}}^{b}{\widetilde {U}}^{c}=0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ac220d551ce68f3010db868a86f62cc311f94902)
This equation can be recast in several ways, and it has been studied in various forms by authors including Kaluza,[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-kal-3) Pauli,[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-25) Gross & Perry,[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-26) Gegenberg & Kunstatter,[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-27) and Wesson & Ponce de Leon,[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-28) but it is instructive to convert it back to the usual 4-dimensional length element ![Image 40: {\displaystyle c^{2}\,d\tau ^{2}\equiv g_{\mu \nu }\,dx^{\mu }\,dx^{\nu }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/51d49c78775d005eb6fd010f10672124b4905121), which is related to the 5-dimensional length element ![Image 41: {\displaystyle ds}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5f0fb36e4308227d3e4a1f809c2571ec02527100) as given above:

![Image 42: {\displaystyle ds^{2}=c^{2}\,d\tau ^{2}+\phi ^{2}(kA_{\nu }\,dx^{\nu }+dx^{5})^{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/42e3a6834a8c11c9b9bb585cb1dad8f442c9f430)
Then the 5D geodesic equation can be written[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-29) for the spacetime components of the 4-velocity:

![Image 43: {\displaystyle U^{\nu }\equiv {\frac {dx^{\nu }}{d\tau }},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/66dc214464ab1bfce58e5f41b27c50982a2e6d4e)![Image 44: {\displaystyle {\frac {dU^{\nu }}{d\tau }}+{\widetilde {\Gamma }}_{\alpha \beta }^{\mu }U^{\alpha }U^{\beta }+2{\widetilde {\Gamma }}_{5\alpha }^{\mu }U^{\alpha }U^{5}+{\widetilde {\Gamma }}_{55}^{\mu }(U^{5})^{2}+U^{\mu }{\frac {d}{d\tau }}\ln {\frac {c\,d\tau }{ds}}=0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4c76a7fd0e2e195fbe1db60d3bd7c5d02f7dbf0)
The term quadratic in ![Image 45: {\displaystyle U^{\nu }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0e0ff9dd6e361b13dec70c16c19fec46fad49a55) provides the 4D [geodesic equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesics_in_general_relativity "Geodesics in general relativity") plus some electromagnetic terms:

![Image 46: {\displaystyle {\widetilde {\Gamma }}_{\alpha \beta }^{\mu }=\Gamma _{\alpha \beta }^{\mu }+{\frac {1}{2}}g^{\mu \nu }k^{2}\phi ^{2}(A_{\alpha }F_{\beta \nu }+A_{\beta }F_{\alpha \nu }-A_{\alpha }A_{\beta }\partial _{\nu }\ln \phi ^{2}).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/52b2a52ba811310560e0bd03917e58421941395a)
The term linear in ![Image 47: {\displaystyle U^{\nu }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0e0ff9dd6e361b13dec70c16c19fec46fad49a55) provides the [Lorentz force law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentz_force_law "Lorentz force law"):

![Image 48: {\displaystyle {\widetilde {\Gamma }}_{5\alpha }^{\mu }={\frac {1}{2}}g^{\mu \nu }k\phi ^{2}(F_{\alpha \nu }-A_{\alpha }\partial _{\nu }\ln \phi ^{2}).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/82a7354e8e361324c18756b22e01d6f107b1bf35)
This is another expression of the "Kaluza miracle". The same hypothesis for the 5D metric that provides electromagnetic stress–energy in the Einstein equations, also provides the Lorentz force law in the equation of motions along with the 4D geodesic equation. Yet correspondence with the Lorentz force law requires that we identify the component of 5-velocity along the fifth dimension with electric charge:

![Image 49: {\displaystyle kU^{5}=k{\frac {dx^{5}}{d\tau }}\to {\frac {q}{mc}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e41127a7465a97ed7a4ae192c748f38e85c5530f)
where ![Image 50: {\displaystyle m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a07d98bb302f3856cbabc47b2b9016692e3f7bc) is particle mass, and ![Image 51: {\displaystyle q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/06809d64fa7c817ffc7e323f85997f783dbdf71d) is particle electric charge. Thus electric charge is understood as motion along the fifth dimension. The fact that the Lorentz force law could be understood as a geodesic in five dimensions was to Kaluza a primary motivation for considering the five-dimensional hypothesis, even in the presence of the aesthetically unpleasing cylinder condition.

Yet there is a problem: the term quadratic in ![Image 52: {\displaystyle U^{5}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ea7ab7383e4d1d4d76befb8cb2771c736b10e5e4),

![Image 53: {\displaystyle {\widetilde {\Gamma }}_{55}^{\mu }=-{\frac {1}{2}}g^{\mu \alpha }\partial _{\alpha }\phi ^{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/825bc6d522fa0cc39bca12bbccd9283aa3d395a6)
If there is no gradient in the scalar field, the term quadratic in ![Image 54: {\displaystyle U^{5}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ea7ab7383e4d1d4d76befb8cb2771c736b10e5e4) vanishes. But otherwise the expression above implies

![Image 55: {\displaystyle U^{5}\sim c{\frac {q/m}{G^{1/2}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a37b09f999ef744256cd6350eaf0931b698f4762)
For elementary particles, ![Image 56: {\displaystyle U^{5}>10^{20}c}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/55028d334268cb06b01acb608b7eeb4cccf02363). The term quadratic in ![Image 57: {\displaystyle U^{5}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ea7ab7383e4d1d4d76befb8cb2771c736b10e5e4) should dominate the equation, perhaps in contradiction to experience. This was the main shortfall of the five-dimensional theory as Kaluza saw it,[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-kal-3) and he gives it some discussion in his original article.[_[clarification needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Please\_clarify "Wikipedia:Please clarify")_]

The equation of motion for ![Image 58: {\displaystyle U^{5}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ea7ab7383e4d1d4d76befb8cb2771c736b10e5e4) is particularly simple under the cylinder condition. Start with the alternate form of the geodesic equation, written for the covariant 5-velocity:

![Image 59: {\displaystyle {\frac {d{\widetilde {U}}_{a}}{ds}}={\frac {1}{2}}{\widetilde {U}}^{b}{\widetilde {U}}^{c}{\frac {\partial {\widetilde {g}}_{bc}}{\partial x^{a}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35b40ac62111446767f43cf285686cfa11c6f55e)
This means that under the cylinder condition, ![Image 60: {\displaystyle {\widetilde {U}}_{5}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/11c8d69b55eadfe5a45bbacc3e9cb5dcc6d873ab) is a constant of the five-dimensional motion:

![Image 61: {\displaystyle {\widetilde {U}}_{5}={\widetilde {g}}_{5a}{\widetilde {U}}^{a}=\phi ^{2}{\frac {c\,d\tau }{ds}}(kA_{\nu }U^{\nu }+U^{5})={\text{constant}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/91df747f0423a208716f759761ece5f62b2cae54)

Kaluza's hypothesis for the matter stress–energy tensor
-------------------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Kaluza's hypothesis for the matter stress–energy tensor")]

Kaluza proposed[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-kal-3) a five-dimensional matter [stress tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stress%E2%80%93energy_tensor "Stress–energy tensor")![Image 62: {\displaystyle {\widetilde {T}}_{M}^{ab}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5c0dedf2a3bd847c862f780ae191aaa3f9777a7f) of the form

![Image 63: {\displaystyle {\widetilde {T}}_{M}^{ab}=\rho {\frac {dx^{a}}{ds}}{\frac {dx^{b}}{ds}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/76df335f9dc29d4042bfc9cdbaf83e45d3d5b142)
where ![Image 64: {\displaystyle \rho }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1f7d439671d1289b6a816e6af7a304be40608d64) is a density, and the length element ![Image 65: {\displaystyle ds}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5f0fb36e4308227d3e4a1f809c2571ec02527100) is as defined above.

Then the spacetime component gives a typical ["dust"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dust_solution "Dust solution") stress–energy tensor:

![Image 66: {\displaystyle {\widetilde {T}}_{M}^{\mu \nu }=\rho {\frac {dx^{\mu }}{ds}}{\frac {dx^{\nu }}{ds}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/953f0c6d8f346faf35da3c97ab9658ab5d5074c0)
The mixed component provides a 4-current source for the Maxwell equations:

![Image 67: {\displaystyle {\widetilde {T}}_{M}^{5\mu }=\rho {\frac {dx^{\mu }}{ds}}{\frac {dx^{5}}{ds}}=\rho U^{\mu }{\frac {q}{kmc}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/27cb065c8cb1b9bd1b8d9e79436ad808e72e922d)
Just as the five-dimensional metric comprises the four-dimensional metric framed by the electromagnetic vector potential, the five-dimensional stress–energy tensor comprises the four-dimensional stress–energy tensor framed by the vector 4-current.

Quantum interpretation of Klein
-------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Quantum interpretation of Klein")]

Kaluza's original hypothesis was purely classical and extended discoveries of general relativity. By the time of Klein's contribution, the discoveries of Heisenberg, Schrödinger, and [Louis de Broglie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis_de_Broglie "Louis de Broglie") were receiving a lot of attention. Klein's _[Nature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nature\_(journal) "Nature (journal)")_ article[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-KN-5) suggested that the fifth dimension is closed and periodic, and that the identification of electric charge with motion in the fifth dimension can be interpreted as standing waves of wavelength ![Image 68: {\displaystyle \lambda ^{5}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4f5f793c459aab62ee2a4434b7e99a5f892aa89e), much like the electrons around a nucleus in the [Bohr model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bohr_model "Bohr model") of the atom. The quantization of electric charge could then be nicely understood in terms of integer multiples of fifth-dimensional momentum. Combining the previous Kaluza result for ![Image 69: {\displaystyle U^{5}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ea7ab7383e4d1d4d76befb8cb2771c736b10e5e4) in terms of electric charge, and a [de Broglie relation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_Broglie_relation "De Broglie relation") for momentum ![Image 70: {\displaystyle p^{5}=h/\lambda ^{5}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/93da4d1e2c5fd8263fffdd8767e63a59bb6afafe), Klein obtained[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-KN-5) an expression for the 0th mode of such waves:

![Image 71: {\displaystyle mU^{5}={\frac {cq}{G^{1/2}}}={\frac {h}{\lambda ^{5}}}\quad \Rightarrow \quad \lambda ^{5}\sim {\frac {hG^{1/2}}{cq}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/953b906da19007c4d71940ccbc218b6ce1307797)
where ![Image 72: {\displaystyle h}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b26be3e694314bc90c3215047e4a2010c6ee184a) is the [Planck constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant "Planck constant"). Klein found that ![Image 73: {\displaystyle \lambda ^{5}\sim 10^{-30}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9e211e32b3b1865418a45d2c78960bb106f8e5f0)cm, and thereby an explanation for the cylinder condition in this small value.

Klein's _[Zeitschrift für Physik](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zeitschrift\_f%C3%BCr\_Physik "Zeitschrift für Physik")_ article of the same year,[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-KZ-4) gave a more detailed treatment that explicitly invoked the techniques of Schrödinger and de Broglie. It recapitulated much of the classical theory of Kaluza described above, and then departed into Klein's quantum interpretation. Klein solved a Schrödinger-like wave equation using an expansion in terms of fifth-dimensional waves resonating in the closed, compact fifth dimension.

Group theory interpretation
---------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Group theory interpretation")]

[![Image 74](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Kaluza_Klein_compactification.svg/253px-Kaluza_Klein_compactification.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Kaluza_Klein_compactification.svg)

The space _M_ × _C_ is compactified over the compact set _C_, and after Kaluza–Klein decomposition one has an [effective field theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Effective_field_theory "Effective field theory") over _M_.

In 1926, Oskar Klein proposed that the fourth spatial dimension is curled up in a circle of a very small [radius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radius "Radius"), so that a [particle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementary_particle "Elementary particle") moving a short distance along that axis would return to where it began. The distance a particle can travel before reaching its initial position is said to be the size of the dimension. This extra dimension is a [compact set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compact_set "Compact set"), and construction of this compact dimension is referred to as [compactification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compactification_(physics) "Compactification (physics)").

In modern geometry, the extra fifth dimension can be understood to be the [circle group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle_group "Circle group")[U(1)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U(1) "U(1)"), as [electromagnetism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetism "Electromagnetism") can essentially be formulated as a [gauge theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauge_theory "Gauge theory") on a [fiber bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiber_bundle "Fiber bundle"), the [circle bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle_bundle "Circle bundle"), with [gauge group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauge_group "Gauge group") U(1). In Kaluza–Klein theory this group suggests that gauge symmetry is the symmetry of circular compact dimensions. Once this geometrical interpretation is understood, it is relatively straightforward to replace U(1) by a general [Lie group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_group "Lie group"). Such generalizations are often called [Yang–Mills theories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yang%E2%80%93Mills_theory "Yang–Mills theory"). If a distinction is drawn, then it is that Yang–Mills theories occur on a flat spacetime, whereas Kaluza–Klein treats the more general case of curved spacetime. The base space of Kaluza–Klein theory need not be four-dimensional spacetime; it can be any ([pseudo-](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudo-Riemannian_manifold "Pseudo-Riemannian manifold"))[Riemannian manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_manifold "Riemannian manifold"), or even a [supersymmetric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supersymmetry "Supersymmetry") manifold or [orbifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbifold "Orbifold") or even a [noncommutative space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noncommutative_space "Noncommutative space").

The construction can be outlined, roughly, as follows.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-30) One starts by considering a [principal fiber bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principal_fiber_bundle "Principal fiber bundle")_P_ with [gauge group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauge_group "Gauge group")_G_ over a [manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manifold "Manifold") M. Given a [connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connection_(fibred_manifold) "Connection (fibred manifold)") on the bundle, and a [metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_tensor "Metric tensor") on the base manifold, and a gauge invariant metric on the tangent of each fiber, one can construct a [bundle metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_(vector_bundle) "Metric (vector bundle)") defined on the entire bundle. Computing the [scalar curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_curvature "Scalar curvature") of this bundle metric, one finds that it is constant on each fiber: this is the "Kaluza miracle". One did not have to explicitly impose a cylinder condition, or to compactify: by assumption, the gauge group is already compact. Next, one takes this scalar curvature as the [Lagrangian density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrangian_density "Lagrangian density"), and, from this, constructs the [Einstein–Hilbert action](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein%E2%80%93Hilbert_action "Einstein–Hilbert action") for the bundle, as a whole. The equations of motion, the [Euler–Lagrange equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler%E2%80%93Lagrange_equations "Euler–Lagrange equations"), can be then obtained by considering where the action is [stationary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stationary_state "Stationary state") with respect to variations of either the metric on the base manifold, or of the gauge connection. Variations with respect to the base metric gives the [Einstein field equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_field_equations "Einstein field equations") on the base manifold, with the [energy–momentum tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy%E2%80%93momentum_tensor "Energy–momentum tensor") given by the [curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvature_form "Curvature form") ([field strength](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_strength "Field strength")) of the gauge connection. On the flip side, the action is stationary against variations of the gauge connection precisely when the gauge connection solves the [Yang–Mills equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yang%E2%80%93Mills_theory "Yang–Mills theory"). Thus, by applying a single idea: the [principle of least action](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principle_of_least_action "Principle of least action"), to a single quantity: the scalar curvature on the bundle (as a whole), one obtains simultaneously all of the needed field equations, for both the spacetime and the gauge field.

As an approach to the unification of the forces, it is straightforward to apply the Kaluza–Klein theory in an attempt to unify gravity with the [strong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strong_force "Strong force") and [electroweak](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electroweak "Electroweak") forces by using the symmetry group of the [Standard Model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model "Standard Model"), [SU(3)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SU(3) "SU(3)") × [SU(2)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SU(2) "SU(2)") × [U(1)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U(1) "U(1)"). However, an attempt to convert this interesting geometrical construction into a bona-fide model of reality flounders on a number of issues, including the fact that the [fermions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermion "Fermion") must be introduced in an artificial way (in nonsupersymmetric models). Nonetheless, KK remains an important [touchstone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Touchstone_(metaphor) "Touchstone (metaphor)") in theoretical physics and is often embedded in more sophisticated theories. It is studied in its own right as an object of geometric interest in [K-theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K-theory "K-theory").

Even in the absence of a completely satisfying theoretical physics framework, the idea of exploring extra, compactified, dimensions is of considerable interest in the [experimental physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Experimental_physics "Experimental physics") and [astrophysics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrophysics "Astrophysics") communities. A variety of predictions, with real experimental consequences, can be made (in the case of [large extra dimensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Large_extra_dimension "Large extra dimension") and [warped models](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Warped_model "Warped model")). For example, on the simplest of principles, one might expect to have [standing waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standing_wave "Standing wave") in the extra compactified dimension(s). If a spatial extra dimension is of radius _R_, the invariant [mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass "Mass") of such standing waves would be _M_ _n_ = _nh_/_Rc_ with _n_ an [integer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer"), _h_ being the [Planck constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant "Planck constant") and _c_ the [speed of light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light "Speed of light"). This set of possible mass values is often called the **Kaluza–Klein tower**. Similarly, in [Thermal quantum field theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermal_quantum_field_theory "Thermal quantum field theory") a compactification of the euclidean time dimension leads to the [Matsubara frequencies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matsubara_frequency "Matsubara frequency") and thus to a discretized thermal energy spectrum.

However, Klein's approach to a quantum theory is flawed[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] and, for example, leads to a calculated electron mass in the order of magnitude of the [Planck mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_mass "Planck mass").[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-31)

Examples of experimental pursuits include work by the [CDF](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collider_Detector_at_Fermilab "Collider Detector at Fermilab") collaboration, which has re-analyzed [particle collider](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_collider "Particle collider") data for the signature of effects associated with large extra dimensions/[warped models](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Warped_model "Warped model").[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

[Robert Brandenberger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Brandenberger "Robert Brandenberger") and [Cumrun Vafa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cumrun_Vafa "Cumrun Vafa") have speculated that in the early universe, [cosmic inflation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_inflation "Cosmic inflation") causes three of the space dimensions to expand to cosmological size while the remaining dimensions of space remained microscopic.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

Space–time–matter theory
------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Space–time–matter theory")]

One particular variant of Kaluza–Klein theory is **space–time–matter theory** or **induced matter theory**, chiefly promulgated by [Paul Wesson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_S._Wesson "Paul S. Wesson") and other members of the Space–Time–Matter Consortium.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-32) In this version of the theory, it is noted that solutions to the equation

![Image 75: {\displaystyle {\widetilde {R}}_{ab}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8b90a084bf71238eb96e6e068fa8479b362f3459)
may be re-expressed so that in four dimensions, these solutions satisfy [Einstein's equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein%27s_equation "Einstein's equation")

![Image 76: {\displaystyle G_{\mu \nu }=8\pi T_{\mu \nu }\,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/72115d9e9788d737de9ad926484ec54c5b516442)
with the precise form of the _T_ _μν_ following from the [Ricci-flat condition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci-flat_condition "Ricci-flat condition") on the five-dimensional space. In other words, the cylinder condition of the previous development is dropped, and the stress–energy now comes from the derivatives of the 5D metric with respect to the fifth coordinate. Because the [energy–momentum tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy%E2%80%93momentum_tensor "Energy–momentum tensor") is normally understood to be due to concentrations of matter in four-dimensional space, the above result is interpreted as saying that four-dimensional matter is induced from geometry in five-dimensional space.

In particular, the [soliton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soliton "Soliton") solutions of ![Image 77: {\displaystyle {\widetilde {R}}_{ab}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8b90a084bf71238eb96e6e068fa8479b362f3459) can be shown to contain the [Friedmann–Lemaître–Robertson–Walker metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedmann%E2%80%93Lema%C3%AEtre%E2%80%93Robertson%E2%80%93Walker_metric "Friedmann–Lemaître–Robertson–Walker metric") in both radiation-dominated (early universe) and matter-dominated (later universe) forms. The general equations can be shown to be sufficiently consistent with classical [tests of general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity "Tests of general relativity") to be acceptable on physical principles, while still leaving considerable freedom to also provide interesting [cosmological models](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmological_model "Cosmological model").

Geometric interpretation
------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Geometric interpretation")]

The Kaluza–Klein theory has a particularly elegant presentation in terms of geometry. In a certain sense, it looks just like ordinary gravity in [free space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_space "Free space"), except that it is phrased in five dimensions instead of four.

The equations governing ordinary gravity in free space can be obtained from an [action](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_(physics) "Action (physics)"), by applying the [variational principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variational_principle "Variational principle") to a certain [action](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_(physics) "Action (physics)"). Let _M_ be a ([pseudo-](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudo-Riemannian_manifold "Pseudo-Riemannian manifold"))[Riemannian manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_manifold "Riemannian manifold"), which may be taken as the [spacetime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spacetime "Spacetime") of [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity"). If _g_ is the [metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_(mathematics) "Metric (mathematics)") on this manifold, one defines the [action](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_(physics) "Action (physics)")_S_(_g_) as

![Image 78: {\displaystyle S(g)=\int _{M}R(g)\operatorname {vol} (g),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3f75968159c42d9c6f8faed5598069b20f8bb0f7)
where _R_(_g_) is the [scalar curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_curvature "Scalar curvature"), and vol(_g_) is the [volume element](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume_element "Volume element"). By applying the [variational principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variational_principle "Variational principle") to the action

![Image 79: {\displaystyle {\frac {\delta S(g)}{\delta g}}=0,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/071ef8b50c8d510c8629327128e833ad0c0aeb3c)
one obtains precisely the [Einstein equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_equation "Einstein equation") for free space:

![Image 80: {\displaystyle R_{ij}-{\frac {1}{2}}g_{ij}R=0,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d1f851657b183b6e91c84b433376e8a014110c8a)
where _R_ _ij_ is the [Ricci tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_tensor "Ricci tensor").

By contrast, the [Maxwell equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxwell_equation "Maxwell equation") describing [electromagnetism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetism "Electromagnetism") can be understood to be the [Hodge equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_Rham_cohomology "De Rham cohomology") of a [principal ![Image 81: {\displaystyle \operatorname {U} (1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/39823af285de3e72d7d50f96fe5b8ba024017af2)-bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principal_U(1)-bundle "Principal U(1)-bundle") or [circle bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle_bundle "Circle bundle")![Image 82: {\displaystyle \pi :P\to M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9bc921dc155d2c06ba6ae891c0ae2c117b9ddbe9) with fiber [![Image 83: {\displaystyle \operatorname {U} (1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/39823af285de3e72d7d50f96fe5b8ba024017af2)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principal_U(1)-bundle "Principal U(1)-bundle"). That is, the [electromagnetic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_field "Electromagnetic field")![Image 84: {\displaystyle F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/545fd099af8541605f7ee55f08225526be88ce57) is a [harmonic 2-form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harmonic_form "Harmonic form") in the space ![Image 85: {\displaystyle \Omega ^{2}(M)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d13fb94a8c7d243906c16c59d738f4e0636ff7fa) of differentiable [2-forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2-form "2-form") on the manifold ![Image 86: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd). In the absence of charges and currents, the free-field Maxwell equations are

![Image 87: {\displaystyle \mathrm {d} F=0\quad {\text{and}}\quad \mathrm {d} {\star }F=0,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3cc9d5510d6aa2ccd5484991a3457cfed91cc96c)
where ![Image 88: {\displaystyle \star }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bd316a21eeb5079a850f223b1d096a06bfa788c0) is the [Hodge star operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hodge_star_operator "Hodge star operator").

### Kaluza–Klein geometry

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Kaluza–Klein geometry")]

To build the Kaluza–Klein theory, one picks an invariant metric on the circle ![Image 89: {\displaystyle S^{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/60796c8d0c03cf575637d3202463b214d9635880) that is the fiber of the U(1)-bundle of electromagnetism. In this discussion, an _invariant metric_ is simply one that is invariant under rotations of the circle. Suppose that this metric gives the circle a total length ![Image 90: {\displaystyle \Lambda }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0ac0a4a98a414e3480335f9ba652d12571ec6733). One then considers metrics ![Image 91: {\displaystyle {\widehat {g}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f03ff4b434f75ad5db89ea97ae9f4336927e6d9f) on the bundle ![Image 92: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a) that are consistent with both the fiber metric, and the metric on the underlying manifold ![Image 93: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd). The consistency conditions are:

The Kaluza–Klein action for such a metric is given by

![Image 94: {\displaystyle S({\widehat {g}})=\int _{P}R({\widehat {g}})\operatorname {vol} ({\widehat {g}}).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/42f1d0a76d6699623cefa8bb43dafd57646d33be)
The scalar curvature, written in components, then expands to

![Image 95: {\displaystyle R({\widehat {g}})=\pi ^{*}\left(R(g)-{\frac {\Lambda ^{2}}{2}}|F|^{2}\right),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/98eef8e59a548bc2d6f8e2b978ee39d1edee2a38)
where ![Image 96: {\displaystyle \pi ^{*}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f44ad69ec033a9a86437b2edaf620ea0b2c3f494) is the [pullback](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pullback_(differential_geometry) "Pullback (differential geometry)") of the fiber bundle projection ![Image 97: {\displaystyle \pi :P\to M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9bc921dc155d2c06ba6ae891c0ae2c117b9ddbe9). The connection ![Image 98: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3) on the fiber bundle is related to the electromagnetic field strength as

![Image 99: {\displaystyle \pi ^{*}F=dA.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4caaa7d177374a5e3ebdfa5e90bd0e38b34e16ff)
That there always exists such a connection, even for fiber bundles of arbitrarily complex topology, is a result from [homology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homology_(mathematics) "Homology (mathematics)") and specifically, [K-theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K-theory "K-theory"). Applying [Fubini's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fubini%27s_theorem "Fubini's theorem") and integrating on the fiber, one gets

![Image 100: {\displaystyle S({\widehat {g}})=\Lambda \int _{M}\left(R(g)-{\frac {1}{\Lambda ^{2}}}|F|^{2}\right)\operatorname {vol} (g).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a51de203d3b4fa409b891d9762c3309b7a4b57f1)
Varying the action with respect to the component ![Image 101: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3), one regains the Maxwell equations. Applying the variational principle to the base metric ![Image 102: {\displaystyle g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3556280e66fe2c0d0140df20935a6f057381d77), one gets the Einstein equations

![Image 103: {\displaystyle R_{ij}-{\frac {1}{2}}g_{ij}R={\frac {1}{\Lambda ^{2}}}T_{ij}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/33ef911d401e8c81837de53b56160a94a112265a)
with the [electromagnetic stress–energy tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_stress%E2%80%93energy_tensor "Electromagnetic stress–energy tensor") being given by

![Image 104: {\displaystyle T^{ij}=F^{ik}F^{jl}g_{kl}-{\frac {1}{4}}g^{ij}|F|^{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/32acdc19b3192734541fb5efe66c16567a6068fd)
The original theory identifies ![Image 105: {\displaystyle \Lambda }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0ac0a4a98a414e3480335f9ba652d12571ec6733) with the fiber metric ![Image 106: {\displaystyle g_{55}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9301606a8c2b3641347b4acd022ee81cdf5aab8b) and allows ![Image 107: {\displaystyle \Lambda }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0ac0a4a98a414e3480335f9ba652d12571ec6733) to vary from fiber to fiber. In this case, the coupling between gravity and the electromagnetic field is not constant, but has its own dynamical field, the [radion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radion_(physics) "Radion (physics)").

In the above, the size of the loop ![Image 108: {\displaystyle \Lambda }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0ac0a4a98a414e3480335f9ba652d12571ec6733) acts as a coupling constant between the gravitational field and the electromagnetic field. If the base manifold is four-dimensional, the Kaluza–Klein manifold _P_ is five-dimensional. The fifth dimension is a [compact space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compact_space "Compact space") and is called the **compact dimension**. The technique of introducing compact dimensions to obtain a higher-dimensional manifold is referred to as [compactification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compactification_(physics) "Compactification (physics)"). Compactification does not produce group actions on [chiral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chirality_(physics) "Chirality (physics)")[fermions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermions "Fermions") except in very specific cases: the dimension of the total space must be 2 mod 8, and the G-index of the Dirac operator of the compact space must be nonzero.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-33)

The above development generalizes in a more-or-less straightforward fashion to general [principal _G_-bundles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principal_G-bundle "Principal G-bundle") for some arbitrary [Lie group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_group "Lie group")_G_ taking the place of [U(1)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U(1) "U(1)"). In such a case, the theory is often referred to as a [Yang–Mills theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yang%E2%80%93Mills_theory "Yang–Mills theory") and is sometimes taken to be synonymous. If the underlying manifold is [supersymmetric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supersymmetric "Supersymmetric"), the resulting theory is a super-symmetric Yang–Mills theory.

No experimental or observational signs of extra dimensions have been officially reported. Many theoretical search techniques for detecting Kaluza–Klein resonances have been proposed using the mass couplings of such resonances with the [top quark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Top_quark "Top quark"). An analysis of results from the LHC in December 2010 severely constrains theories with [large extra dimensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Large_extra_dimensions "Large extra dimensions").[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-arxiv.org_1-34)

The observation of a [Higgs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Higgs_boson "Higgs boson")-like boson at the LHC establishes a new empirical test which can be applied to the search for Kaluza–Klein resonances and supersymmetric particles. The loop [Feynman diagrams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feynman_diagram "Feynman diagram") that exist in the Higgs interactions allow any particle with electric charge and mass to run in such a loop. Standard Model particles besides the [top quark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Top_quark "Top quark") and [W boson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/W_and_Z_bosons "W and Z bosons") do not make big contributions to the cross-section observed in the H → γγ decay, but if there are new particles beyond the Standard Model, they could potentially change the ratio of the predicted Standard Model H → γγ cross-section to the experimentally observed cross-section. Hence a measurement of any dramatic change to the H → γγ cross-section predicted by the Standard Model is crucial in probing the physics beyond it.

An article from July 2018[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory#cite_note-arxiv.org_2-35) gives some hope for this theory; in the article they dispute that gravity is leaking into higher dimensions as in [brane theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brane_theory "Brane theory"). However, the article does demonstrate that electromagnetism and gravity share the same number of dimensions, and this fact lends support to Kaluza–Klein theory; whether the number of dimensions is really 3+1 or in fact 4+1 is the subject of further debate.
