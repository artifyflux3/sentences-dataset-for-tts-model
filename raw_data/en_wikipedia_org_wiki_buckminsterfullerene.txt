**Buckminsterfullerene** is a type of [fullerene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fullerene "Fullerene") with the formula C

60. It has a cage-like fused-ring structure ([truncated icosahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Truncated_icosahedron "Truncated icosahedron")) made of twenty [hexagons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hexagon "Hexagon") and twelve [pentagons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentagon "Pentagon"), and resembles a [football](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ball_(association_football) "Ball (association football)"). Each of its 60 [carbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon "Carbon") atoms is [bonded](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_bond "Chemical bond") to its three neighbors.

Buckminsterfullerene is a black solid that dissolves in [hydrocarbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrocarbon "Hydrocarbon")[solvents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solvent "Solvent") to produce a purple solution. The substance was discovered in 1985 and has received intense study, although few real world applications have been found.

Molecules of buckminsterfullerene (or of fullerenes in general) are commonly nicknamed **buckyballs**.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-AZoM-2006-4)

Buckminsterfullerene is the most common naturally occurring fullerene. Small quantities of it can be found in [soot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soot "Soot").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-6)

It also [exists in space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_interstellar_and_circumstellar_molecules "List of interstellar and circumstellar molecules"). Neutral C

60 has been observed in [planetary nebulae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebulae "Planetary nebulae")[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-7) and several types of [star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star "Star").[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-8) The ionised form, C+

60, has been identified in the [interstellar medium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_medium "Interstellar medium"),[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-9) where it is the cause of several absorption features known as [diffuse interstellar bands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffuse_interstellar_band "Diffuse interstellar band") in the near-infrared.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-10)

60.

Theoretical predictions of buckminsterfullerene molecules appeared in the late 1960s and early 1970s.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-k363-11)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-12)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-13)[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-Smalley-1997-14) It was first generated in 1984 by Eric Rohlfing, Donald Cox, and Andrew Kaldor,[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-Smalley-1997-14)[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-15) using a laser to vaporize carbon in a supersonic [helium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium "Helium") beam, although the group did not realize that buckminsterfullerene had been produced. In 1985 their work was repeated by [Harold Kroto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harold_Kroto "Harold Kroto"), [James R. Heath](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_R._Heath "James R. Heath"), [Sean C. O'Brien](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sean_C._O%27Brien&action=edit&redlink=1 "Sean C. O'Brien (page does not exist)"), [Robert Curl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Curl "Robert Curl"), and [Richard Smalley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Smalley "Richard Smalley") at [Rice University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rice_University "Rice University"), who recognized the structure of C

60 as buckminsterfullerene.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-Kroto-16)

Concurrent but unconnected to the Kroto-Smalley work, astrophysicists were working with spectroscopists to study infrared emissions from giant red carbon stars.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-Dresselhaus-17)[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-18)[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-19) Smalley and team were able to use a laser vaporization technique to create carbon clusters which could potentially emit infrared at the same wavelength as had been emitted by the red carbon star.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-Dresselhaus-17)[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-20) Hence, the inspiration came to Smalley and team to use the laser technique on graphite to generate fullerenes.

Using [laser](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laser "Laser")[evaporation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evaporation "Evaporation") of [graphite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graphite "Graphite") the Smalley team found C _n_ clusters (where _n_> 20 and even) of which the most common were C

60 and C

70. A solid rotating graphite disk was used as the surface from which carbon was vaporized using a laser beam creating hot plasma that was then passed through a stream of high-density helium gas.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-Kroto-16) The carbon [species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_species "Chemical species") were subsequently cooled and ionized resulting in the formation of clusters. Clusters ranged in molecular masses, but Kroto and Smalley found predominance in a C

60 cluster that could be enhanced further by allowing the plasma to react longer. They also discovered that C

60 is a cage-like molecule, a regular [truncated icosahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Truncated_icosahedron "Truncated icosahedron").[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-Dresselhaus-17)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-Kroto-16)

The experimental evidence, a strong peak at 720 [daltons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dalton_(unit) "Dalton (unit)"), indicated that a carbon molecule with 60 carbon atoms was forming, but provided no structural information. The research group concluded after reactivity experiments, that the most likely structure was a spheroidal molecule. The idea was quickly rationalized as the basis of an [icosahedral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Icosahedral "Icosahedral")[symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry "Symmetry") closed cage structure.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-k363-11)

Kroto, Curl, and Smalley were awarded the 1996 [Nobel Prize in Chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Chemistry "Nobel Prize in Chemistry") for their roles in the discovery of buckminsterfullerene and the related class of molecules, the [fullerenes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fullerene "Fullerene").[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-k363-11)

In 1989 physicists [Wolfgang Krätschmer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Kr%C3%A4tschmer "Wolfgang Krätschmer"), [Konstantinos Fostiropoulos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Konstantinos_Fostiropoulos "Konstantinos Fostiropoulos"), and [Donald R. Huffman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Donald_Huffman "Donald Huffman") observed unusual optical absorptions in thin films of carbon dust (soot). The soot had been generated by an arc-process between two graphite [electrodes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrodes "Electrodes") in a helium atmosphere where the electrode material evaporates and condenses forming soot in the quenching atmosphere. Among other features, the [IR spectra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infrared_spectroscopy "Infrared spectroscopy") of the soot showed four discrete bands in close agreement to those proposed for C

60.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-21)[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-22)

Another paper on the characterization and verification of the molecular structure followed on in the same year (1990) from their thin film experiments, and detailed also the extraction of an evaporable as well as [benzene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene "Benzene")-soluble material from the arc-generated soot. This extract had [TEM](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transmission_electron_microscopy "Transmission electron microscopy") and [X-ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray "X-ray") crystal analysis consistent with arrays of spherical C

60 molecules, approximately 1.0 nm in [van der Waals diameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_der_Waals_radius "Van der Waals radius")[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-buckminsterfullerene3-23) as well as the expected molecular mass of 720 Da for C

60 (and 840 Da for C

70) in their [mass spectra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass_spectroscopy "Mass spectroscopy").[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-24) The method was simple and efficient to prepare the material in gram amounts per day (1990) which has boosted the fullerene research and is even today applied for the commercial production of fullerenes.

The discovery of practical routes to C

60 led to the exploration of a new field of chemistry involving the study of fullerenes.

The discoverers of the [allotrope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allotropy "Allotropy") named the newfound molecule after American architect [R. Buckminster Fuller](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminster_Fuller "Buckminster Fuller"), who designed many [geodesic dome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic_dome "Geodesic dome") structures that look similar to C

60 and who had died in 1983, the year before discovery.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-k363-11) Another common name for buckminsterfullerene is "buckyballs".[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-25)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-AZoM-2006-4)

Soot is produced by laser ablation of graphite or [pyrolysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrolysis "Pyrolysis") of [aromatic hydrocarbons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aromatic_hydrocarbon "Aromatic hydrocarbon"). Fullerenes are extracted from the soot with organic solvents using a [Soxhlet extractor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soxhlet_extractor "Soxhlet extractor").[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-26) This step yields a solution containing up to 75% of C

60, as well as other fullerenes. These fractions are separated using [chromatography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chromatography "Chromatography").[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-27) Generally, the fullerenes are dissolved in a hydrocarbon or halogenated hydrocarbon and separated using alumina columns.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-28)

Synthesis using the techniques of "classical [organic chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_synthesis "Organic synthesis")" is possible, but not economic.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-29)

Buckminsterfullerene is a [truncated icosahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Truncated_icosahedron "Truncated icosahedron") with 60 [vertices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_(geometry) "Vertex (geometry)"), 32 faces (20 hexagons and 12 pentagons where no pentagons share a vertex), and 90 edges (60 edges between 5-membered & 6-membered rings and 30 edges are shared between 6-membered & 6-membered rings), with a carbon atom at the vertices of each polygon and a bond along each polygon edge. The [van der Waals diameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_der_Waals_diameter "Van der Waals diameter") of a C

60 molecule is about 1.01[nanometers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nanometers "Nanometers") (nm). The nucleus to nucleus diameter of a C

60 molecule is about 0.71 nm. The C

60 molecule has two bond lengths. The 6:6 ring bonds (between two hexagons) can be considered "[double bonds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double_bond "Double bond")" and are shorter than the 6:5 bonds (between a hexagon and a pentagon). Its average bond length is 0.14 nm. Each carbon atom in the structure is bonded covalently with 3 others.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-30) A carbon atom in the C

60 can be substituted by a [nitrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen "Nitrogen") or [boron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boron "Boron") atom yielding a C

59 N or C

59 B respectively.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-31)

For a time buckminsterfullerene was the largest[_[quantify](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual\_of\_Style/Dates\_and\_numbers "Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers")_] known molecule observed to exhibit [wave–particle duality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wave%E2%80%93particle_duality "Wave–particle duality").[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-32) In 2020 the dye molecule [phthalocyanine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phthalocyanine "Phthalocyanine") exhibited the duality that is more famously attributed to light, electrons and other small particles and molecules.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-33)

Optical absorption spectrum of C

60 solution, showing diminished absorption for the blue (~450 nm) and red (~700 nm) light that results in the purple color.

Fullerenes are sparingly soluble in aromatic [solvents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solvent "Solvent") and [carbon disulfide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_disulfide "Carbon disulfide"), but insoluble in water. Solutions of pure C

60 have a deep purple color which leaves a brown residue upon evaporation. The reason for this color change is the relatively narrow energy width of the band of molecular levels responsible for green light absorption by individual C

60 molecules. Thus individual molecules transmit some blue and red light resulting in a purple color. Upon drying, intermolecular interaction results in the overlap and broadening of the energy bands, thereby eliminating the blue light transmittance and causing the purple to brown color change.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-Dresselhaus-17)

C

60 crystallises with some solvents in the lattice ("solvates"). For example, crystallization of C

60 from [benzene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene "Benzene") solution yields triclinic crystals with the formula C 60·4 C 6 H 6. Like other solvates, this one readily releases benzene to give the usual face-centred cubic C

60. Millimeter-sized crystals of C

60 and C

70 can be grown from solution both for solvates and for pure fullerenes.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-37)[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-38)

[![Image 23](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/C60-Fulleren-kristallin.JPG/250px-C60-Fulleren-kristallin.JPG)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:C60-Fulleren-kristallin.JPG)

Micrograph of C

60.

[![Image 24](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Fullerite_structure.jpg/250px-Fullerite_structure.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Fullerite_structure.jpg)

Packing of C

60 in crystal.

In solid buckminsterfullerene, the C

60 molecules adopt the fcc ([face-centered cubic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Face-centered_cubic "Face-centered cubic")) motif. They start rotating at about −20°C. This change is associated with a first-order phase transition to an fcc structure and a small, yet abrupt increase in the lattice constant from 1.411 to 1.4154 nm.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-k372-39)

C

60 solid is as soft as [graphite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graphite "Graphite"), but when compressed to less than 70% of its volume it transforms into a [superhard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superhard_materials "Superhard materials") form of [diamond](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diamond "Diamond") (see [aggregated diamond nanorod](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aggregated_diamond_nanorod "Aggregated diamond nanorod")). C

60 films and solution have strong non-linear optical properties; in particular, their optical absorption increases with light intensity (saturable absorption).

C

60 forms a brownish solid with an optical absorption threshold at ≈1.6 eV.[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-40) It is an n-type [semiconductor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semiconductor "Semiconductor") with a low activation energy of 0.1–0.3 eV; this conductivity is attributed to intrinsic or oxygen-related defects.[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-41) Fcc C

60 contains voids at its octahedral and tetrahedral sites which are sufficiently large (0.6 and 0.2 nm respectively) to accommodate impurity atoms. When alkali metals are [doped](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doping_(semiconductor) "Doping (semiconductor)") into these voids, C

60 converts from a semiconductor into a conductor or even superconductor.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-k372-39)[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-42)

Chemical reactions and properties
---------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Buckminsterfullerene&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Chemical reactions and properties")]

C

60 undergoes six reversible, one-electron reductions, ultimately generating C 6−

60. Its [oxidation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxidation "Oxidation") is irreversible. The first reduction occurs at ≈−1.0[V](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volt "Volt") ([Fc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ferrocene "Ferrocene")/Fc+

), showing that C

60 is a reluctant electron acceptor. C

60 tends to avoid having double bonds in the pentagonal rings, which makes electron [delocalization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Delocalization "Delocalization") poor, and results in C

60 not being "[superaromatic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superaromatic "Superaromatic")". C

60 behaves like an electron deficient [alkene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkene "Alkene"). For example, it reacts with some nucleophiles.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-buckminsterfullerene3-23)[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-Reed-43)

C

60 exhibits a small degree of aromatic character, but it still reflects localized double and single C–C bond characters. Therefore, C

60 can undergo addition with hydrogen to give polyhydrofullerenes. C

60 also undergoes [Birch reduction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Birch_reduction "Birch reduction"). For example, C

60 reacts with lithium in liquid ammonia, followed by _tert_-butanol to give a mixture of polyhydrofullerenes such as C 60 H 18, C 60 H 32, C 60 H 36, with C 60 H 32 being the dominating product. This mixture of polyhydrofullerenes can be re-oxidized by [2,3-dichloro-5,6-dicyano-1,4-benzoquinone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2,3-Dichloro-5,6-dicyano-1,4-benzoquinone "2,3-Dichloro-5,6-dicyano-1,4-benzoquinone") to give C

60 again.

A selective hydrogenation method exists. Reaction of C

60 with 9,9′,10,10′-dihydroanthracene under the same conditions, depending on the time of reaction, gives C 60 H 32 and C 60 H 18 respectively and selectively.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-InorgChem-44)

Addition of [fluorine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluorine "Fluorine"), [chlorine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chlorine "Chlorine"), and [bromine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bromine "Bromine") occurs for C

60. Fluorine atoms are small enough for a 1,2-addition, while Cl

2 and Br

2 add to remote C atoms due to [steric factors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steric_factor "Steric factor"). For example, in C 60 Br 8 and C 60 Br 24, the Br atoms are in 1,3- or 1,4-positions with respect to each other. Under various conditions a vast number of halogenated derivatives of C

60 can be produced, some with an extraordinary selectivity on one or two isomers over the other possible ones. Addition of fluorine and chlorine usually results in a flattening of the C

60 framework into a drum-shaped molecule.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-InorgChem-44)

### Addition of oxygen atoms

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Buckminsterfullerene&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Addition of oxygen atoms")]

Solutions of C

60 can be oxygenated to the [epoxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epoxide "Epoxide")C 60 O. Ozonation of C

60 in 1,2-xylene at 257K gives an intermediate ozonide C 60 O 3, which can be decomposed into 2 forms of C 60 O. Decomposition of C 60 O 3 at 296 K gives the epoxide, but photolysis gives a product in which the O atom bridges a 5,6-edge.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-InorgChem-44)

[![Image 25](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Addition_of_O_atom_into_C60_Scheme.png/330px-Addition_of_O_atom_into_C60_Scheme.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Addition_of_O_atom_into_C60_Scheme.png)

The [Diels–Alder reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diels%E2%80%93Alder_reaction "Diels–Alder reaction") is commonly employed to functionalize C

60. Reaction of C

60 with appropriate substituted diene gives the corresponding adduct.

The Diels–Alder reaction between C

60 and 3,6-diaryl-1,2,4,5-tetrazines affords C

62. The C

62 has the structure in which a four-membered ring is surrounded by four six-membered rings.

[![Image 26](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/3D_structure_of_C62_derivative_from_C60_update.jpg/250px-3D_structure_of_C62_derivative_from_C60_update.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:3D_structure_of_C62_derivative_from_C60_update.jpg)

A C

62 derivative [C 62(C 6 H 5-4-Me)2] synthesized from C

60 and 3,6-bis(4-methylphenyl)-3,6-dihydro-1,2,4,5-tetrazine

The C

60 molecules can also be coupled through a [2+2] [cycloaddition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cycloaddition "Cycloaddition"), giving the dumbbell-shaped compound C

120. The coupling is achieved by high-speed vibrating milling of C

60 with a catalytic amount of [KCN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_cyanide "Potassium cyanide"). The reaction is reversible as C

120 dissociates back to two C

60 molecules when heated at 450 K (177°C; 350°F). Under high pressure and temperature, repeated [2+2] cycloaddition between C

60 results in polymerized fullerene chains and networks. These polymers remain stable at ambient pressure and temperature once formed, and have remarkably interesting electronic and magnetic properties, such as being [ferromagnetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ferromagnetism "Ferromagnetism") above room temperature.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-InorgChem-44)

### Free radical reactions

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Buckminsterfullerene&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Free radical reactions")]

Reactions of C

60 with [free radicals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_radicals "Free radicals") readily occur. When C

60 is mixed with a disulfide RSSR, the radical C 60 SR• forms spontaneously upon irradiation of the mixture.

Stability of the radical species C 60 Y• depends largely on [steric factors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steric_factor "Steric factor") of Y. When _tert_-butyl halide is photolyzed and allowed to react with C

60, a reversible inter-cage C–C bond is formed:[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-InorgChem-44)

[![Image 27](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Free_radical_reaction_of_fullerene_with_tert-butyl_radical.png/960px-Free_radical_reaction_of_fullerene_with_tert-butyl_radical.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Free_radical_reaction_of_fullerene_with_tert-butyl_radical.png)

### Cyclopropanation (Bingel reaction)

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Buckminsterfullerene&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: Cyclopropanation (Bingel reaction)")]

Cyclopropanation (the [Bingel reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bingel_reaction "Bingel reaction")) is another common method for functionalizing C

60. Cyclopropanation of C

60 mostly occurs at the junction of 2 hexagons due to steric factors.

The first cyclopropanation was carried out by treating the β-bromomalonate with C

60 in the presence of a base. Cyclopropanation also occur readily with [diazomethanes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diazomethane "Diazomethane"). For example, diphenyldiazomethane reacts readily with C

60 to give the compound C 61 Ph 2.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-InorgChem-44)[Phenyl-C 61-butyric acid methyl ester](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phenyl-C61-butyric_acid_methyl_ester "Phenyl-C61-butyric acid methyl ester") derivative prepared through cyclopropanation has been studied for use in [organic solar cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_solar_cells "Organic solar cells").

The [LUMO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LUMO "LUMO") in C

60 is triply degenerate, with the [HOMO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HOMO "HOMO")–[LUMO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LUMO "LUMO") separation relatively small. This small gap suggests that reduction of C

60 should occur at mild potentials leading to fulleride anions, [C 60]_n_− (_n_=1–6). The midpoint potentials of 1-electron reduction of buckminsterfullerene and its anions is given in the table below:

| Reduction potential of C 60 at 213 K |
| --- |
| Half-reaction | _E_° (V) |
| C 60 + e− ⇌ C−60 | −0.169 |
| C−60 + e− ⇌ C 2−60 | −0.599 |
| C 2−60 + e− ⇌ C 3−60 | −1.129 |
| C 3−60 + e− ⇌ C 4−60 | −1.579 |
| C 4−60 + e− ⇌ C 5−60 | −2.069 |
| C 5−60 + e− ⇌ C 6−60 | −2.479 |

C

60 forms a variety of [charge-transfer complexes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charge-transfer_complexes "Charge-transfer complexes"), for example with [tetrakis(dimethylamino)ethylene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrakis(dimethylamino)ethylene "Tetrakis(dimethylamino)ethylene"):

C 60 + C 2(NMe 2)4 → [C 2(NMe 2)4]+[C 60]−
This salt exhibits [ferromagnetism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ferromagnetism "Ferromagnetism") at 16 K.

C

60 oxidizes with difficulty. Three reversible oxidation processes have been observed by using [cyclic voltammetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclic_voltammetry "Cyclic voltammetry") with ultra-dry [methylene chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methylene_chloride "Methylene chloride") and a supporting electrolyte with extremely high oxidation resistance and low nucleophilicity, such as [n Bu 4 N] [AsF 6].[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-Reed-43)

| Reduction potentials of C 60 oxidation at low temperatures |
| --- |
| Half-reaction | _E_° (V) |
| C 60 ⇌ C+60 | +1.27 |
| C+60 ⇌ C 2+60 | +1.71 |
| C 2+60 ⇌ C 3+60 | +2.14 |

C

60 forms complexes akin to the more common alkenes. Complexes have been reported [molybdenum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molybdenum "Molybdenum"), [tungsten](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tungsten "Tungsten"), [platinum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platinum "Platinum"), [palladium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palladium "Palladium"), [iridium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iridium "Iridium"), and [titanium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Titanium "Titanium"). The pentacarbonyl species are produced by [photochemical reactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photochemical_reaction "Photochemical reaction").

M(CO)6 + C 60 → M(η 2-C 60)(CO)5 + CO (M = Mo, W)
In the case of platinum complex, the labile ethylene ligand is the leaving group in a thermal reaction:

Pt(η 2-C 2 H 4)(PPh 3)2 + C 60 → Pt(η 2-C 60)(PPh 3)2 + C 2 H 4
[Titanocene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Titanocene "Titanocene") complexes have also been reported:

(η 5-[Cp](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclopentadienyl "Cyclopentadienyl"))2 Ti(η 2-(CH 3)3 SiC≡CSi(CH 3)3) + C 60 → (η 5-Cp)2 Ti(η 2-C 60) + (CH 3)3 SiC≡CSi(CH 3)3
Coordinatively unsaturated precursors, such as [Vaska's complex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vaska%27s_complex "Vaska's complex"), for [adducts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adduct "Adduct") with C

60:

_trans_-Ir(CO)Cl(PPh 3)2 + C 60 → Ir(CO)Cl(η 2-C 60)(PPh 3)2
One such iridium complex, [Ir(η 2-C 60)(CO)Cl(Ph 2 CH 2 C 6 H 4 OCH 2 Ph)2] has been prepared where the metal center projects two electron-rich 'arms' that embrace the C

60 guest.[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-SupraChem-45)

### Endohedral fullerenes

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Buckminsterfullerene&action=edit&section=20 "Edit section: Endohedral fullerenes")]

Metal atoms or certain small molecules such as H

2 and noble gas can be encapsulated inside the C

60 cage. These endohedral fullerenes are usually synthesized by doping in the metal atoms in an arc reactor or by laser evaporation. These methods gives low yields of endohedral fullerenes, and a better method involves the opening of the cage, packing in the atoms or molecules, and closing the opening using certain [organic reactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_reactions "Organic reactions"). This method, however, is still immature and only a few species have been synthesized this way.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-46)

Endohedral fullerenes show distinct and intriguing chemical properties that can be completely different from the encapsulated atom or molecule, as well as the fullerene itself. The encapsulated atoms have been shown to perform circular motions inside the C

60 cage, and their motion has been followed using [NMR spectroscopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NMR_spectroscopy "NMR spectroscopy").[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-SupraChem-45)

Potential applications in technology
------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Buckminsterfullerene&action=edit&section=21 "Edit section: Potential applications in technology")]

The optical absorption properties of C

60 match the solar spectrum in a way that suggests that C

60-based films could be useful for photovoltaic applications. Because of its high [electronic affinity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_affinity "Electron affinity")[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-47) it is one of the most common [electron acceptors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_acceptor "Electron acceptor") used in donor/acceptor based solar cells. Conversion efficiencies up to 5.7% have been reported in C

60–polymer cells.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-48)

Further information on the basic polymer of the C

60 monomer group: [Polyfullerene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyfullerene "Polyfullerene")

Potential applications in health
--------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Buckminsterfullerene&action=edit&section=22 "Edit section: Potential applications in health")]

### Ingestion and risks

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Buckminsterfullerene&action=edit&section=23 "Edit section: Ingestion and risks")]

C

60 is sensitive to light,[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-Taylor-1991-49) so leaving C

60 under light exposure causes it to degrade, becoming dangerous. The ingestion of C

60 solutions that have been exposed to light could lead to developing cancer (tumors).[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-Grohn,_Kristopher_J.-2021-50)[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-Grohn-2021-51) So the management of C

60 products for human ingestion requires cautionary measures[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-Grohn-2021-51) such as: elaboration in very dark environments, encasing into bottles of great opacity, and storing in dark places, and others like consumption under low light conditions and using labels to warn about the problems with light.

Solutions of C

60 dissolved in olive oil or water, as long as they are preserved from light, have been found nontoxic to rodents.[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-Baati-2012-52)

Otherwise, a study found that C

60 remains in the body for a longer time than usual, especially in the liver, where it tends to be accumulated, and therefore has the potential to induce detrimental health effects.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-53)

### Oils with C60 and risks

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Buckminsterfullerene&action=edit&section=24 "Edit section: Oils with C60 and risks")]

An experiment in 2011–2012 administered a solution of C

60 in olive oil to rats, achieving a 90% prolongation of their lifespan.[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-Baati-2012-52)C

60 in olive oil administered to mice resulted in no extension in lifespan.[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-54)C

60 in olive oil administered to [beagle dogs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beagle "Beagle") resulted in a large reduction of [C-reactive protein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C-reactive_protein "C-reactive protein"), which is commonly elevated in [cardiovascular disease](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardiovascular_disease "Cardiovascular disease") and [cerebrovascular disease](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cerebrovascular_disease "Cerebrovascular disease").[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-55)

Many oils with C

60 have been sold as antioxidant products, but it does not avoid the problem of their sensitivity to light, that can turn them toxic. A later research confirmed that exposure to light degrades solutions of C

60 in oil, making it toxic and leading to a "massive" increase of the risk of developing cancer (tumors) after its consumption.[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-Grohn,_Kristopher_J.-2021-50)[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-Grohn-2021-51)

To avoid the degradation by effect of light, C

60 oils must be made in very dark environments, encased into bottles of great opacity, and kept in darkness, consumed under low light conditions and accompanied by labels to warn about the dangers of light for C

60.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-Grohn-2021-51)[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-Taylor-1991-49)

Some producers have been able to dissolve C

60 in water to avoid possible problems with oils, but that would not protect C

60 from light, so the same cautions are needed.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene#cite_note-Taylor-1991-49)
