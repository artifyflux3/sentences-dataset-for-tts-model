The **gauss** (symbol: **G**, sometimes **Gs**) is a unit of measurement of magnetic induction, also known as [magnetic flux density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_flux_density "Magnetic flux density"). The unit is part of the [Gaussian system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaussian_units "Gaussian units") of units, which inherited it from the older [centimetre–gram–second electromagnetic units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centimetre%E2%80%93gram%E2%80%93second_system_of_units#Electromagnetic_units_.28EMU.29 "Centimetre–gram–second system of units") (CGS-EMU) system. It was named after the German mathematician and physicist [Carl Friedrich Gauss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Friedrich_Gauss "Carl Friedrich Gauss") in 1936. One gauss is defined as one [maxwell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxwell_(unit) "Maxwell (unit)") per square [centimetre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centimetre "Centimetre").

As the [centimetre–gram–second system of units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centimetre%E2%80%93gram%E2%80%93second_system_of_units "Centimetre–gram–second system of units") (cgs system) has been superseded by the [International System of Units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Units "International System of Units") (SI), the use of the gauss has been deprecated by the standards bodies, but is still regularly used in various subfields of science. The SI unit for magnetic flux density is the [tesla](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tesla_(unit) "Tesla (unit)") (symbol T),[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauss_(unit)#cite_note-3) which corresponds to 10,000 gauss.

Name, symbol, and metric prefixes
---------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Gauss_(unit)&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Name, symbol, and metric prefixes")]

Although not a component of the International System of Units, the usage of the gauss generally follows the rules for SI units. Since the name is derived from a person's name, its symbol is the uppercase letter "G". When the unit is spelled out, it is written in lowercase ("gauss"), unless it begins a sentence.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauss_(unit)#cite_note-SIBrochure9thEd-4): 147–148  The gauss may be combined with [metric prefixes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_prefix "Metric prefix"),[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauss_(unit)#cite_note-SIBrochure8th-5): 128  such as in milligauss, mG (or mGs), or kilogauss, kG (or kGs).

![Image 2: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}1\,{\rm {G}}&={\rm {Mx}}{\cdot }{\rm {cm}}^{-2}={\frac {\rm {g}}{{\rm {Bi}}{\cdot }{\rm {s}}^{2}}}\\&{\text{ ≘ }}10^{-4}\,{\rm {T}}=10^{-4}{\frac {\rm {kg}}{{\rm {A}}{\cdot }{\rm {s^{2}}}}}\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b53d2898096ea5eda6e25538c4417bd5e9f572fc)

The gauss is the unit of magnetic flux density **B** in the system of [Gaussian units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaussian_units "Gaussian units") and is equal to [Mx](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxwell_(unit) "Maxwell (unit)")/cm 2 or [g](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gram_(unit) "Gram (unit)")/[Bi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abampere "Abampere")/s 2, while the [oersted](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oersted "Oersted") is the unit of [**H**-field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_field#The_H-field "Magnetic field"). One [tesla](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tesla_(unit) "Tesla (unit)") (T) corresponds to 10 4 gauss, and one [ampere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ampere "Ampere") (A) per metre corresponds to 4π×10−3[oersted](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oersted "Oersted").

The units for [magnetic flux](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_flux "Magnetic flux")Φ, which is the [integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral "Integral") of [magnetic **B**-field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_field#The_B-field "Magnetic field") over an [area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area "Area"), are the [weber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weber_(unit) "Weber (unit)") (Wb) in the [SI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SI "SI") and the [maxwell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxwell_(unit) "Maxwell (unit)") (Mx) in the CGS-Gaussian system. The conversion factor is 10 8 maxwell per weber, since [flux](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flux "Flux") is the integral of field over an area, area having the units of the square of distance, thus 10 4 G/T (magnetic field conversion factor) times the square of 10 2 cm/m (linear distance conversion factor). 10 8 Mx/Wb = 10 4 G/T × (10 2 cm/m)2.

*   10−9–10−8 G – the magnetic field of the human brain
*   10−6–10−3 G – the magnetic field of Galactic [molecular clouds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_clouds "Molecular clouds"). Typical magnetic field strengths within the interstellar medium of the [Milky Way](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milky_Way "Milky Way") are ~5 μG.
*   0.25–0.60 G – the [Earth's magnetic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_magnetic_field "Earth's magnetic field") at its surface
*   4 G – near [Jupiter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jupiter "Jupiter")'s equator
*   25 G – the Earth's magnetic field in its [core](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_outer_core "Earth's outer core")[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauss_(unit)#cite_note-buff2010-6)
*   50 G – a typical [refrigerator magnet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Refrigerator_magnet "Refrigerator magnet")
*   100 G – an [iron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron "Iron")[magnet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnet "Magnet")
*   1500 G – within a [sun spot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun_spot "Sun spot")[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauss_(unit)#cite_note-7)
*   10000 to 13000 G – [remanence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Remanence "Remanence") of a [neodymium-iron-boron (NIB) magnet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neodymium_magnet "Neodymium magnet")[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauss_(unit)#cite_note-8)
*   16000 to 22000 G – [saturation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saturation_(magnetic) "Saturation (magnetic)") of high permeability iron alloys used in transformers[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauss_(unit)#cite_note-9)
*   3000–70000 G – a medical [magnetic resonance imaging machine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_resonance_imaging "Magnetic resonance imaging")
*   10 12–10 13 G – the surface of a [neutron star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron_star "Neutron star")[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauss_(unit)#cite_note-10)
*   4 × 10 13 G – the [Schwinger limit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schwinger_limit "Schwinger limit")
*   10 14 G – the magnetic field of [SGR J1745-2900](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SGR_J1745-2900 "SGR J1745-2900"), orbiting the supermassive black hole [Sgr A*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sagittarius_A* "Sagittarius A*") in the center of the Milky Way.
*   10 15 G – the magnetic field of some newly created [magnetars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetar "Magnetar")[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauss_(unit)#cite_note-sciam_article-11)
*   10 17 G – the upper limit to neutron star magnetism[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauss_(unit)#cite_note-sciam_article-11)
