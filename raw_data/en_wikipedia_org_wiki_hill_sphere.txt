In sectional/side view, a two-dimensional representation of the three-dimensional concept of the Hill sphere, here showing the Earth's "gravity well" (gravitational potential of Earth, blue line), the same for the Moon (red line) and their combined potential (black thick line). Point P is the force free spot, where gravitational forces of Earth and Moon cancel. The sizes of Earth and Moon are in the proportion, but distances and energies are not to scale.

The **Hill sphere** is a common model for the calculation of a [gravitational sphere of influence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere_of_influence_(astrodynamics) "Sphere of influence (astrodynamics)"). It is the most commonly used model to calculate the spatial extent of gravitational influence of an [astronomical body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_body "Astronomical body") (_m_) in which it dominates over the gravitational influence of other bodies, particularly a [primary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primary_(astronomy) "Primary (astronomy)") (_M_).[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hill_sphere#cite_note-Souami_Cresson_Biernacki_Pierret_p.-1) It is sometimes confused with other models of gravitational influence, such as the [Laplace sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laplace_sphere "Laplace sphere")[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hill_sphere#cite_note-Souami_Cresson_Biernacki_Pierret_p.-1) or the **Roche sphere**, the latter of which causes confusion with the [Roche limit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roche_limit "Roche limit").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hill_sphere#cite_note-Williams_2015_w404-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hill_sphere#cite_note-Hill_2022_p.-3) It was defined by the [American](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States "United States")[astronomer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomer "Astronomer")[George William Hill](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_William_Hill "George William Hill"), based on the work of the [French](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/France "France") astronomer [Édouard Roche](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%89douard_Roche "Édouard Roche").[_[not verified in body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

To be retained by a more gravitationally attracting astrophysical object—a planet by a more massive star, a [moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_satellite "Natural satellite") by a more massive planet—the less massive body must have an [orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbit "Orbit") that lies within the gravitational potential represented by the more massive body's Hill sphere.[_[not verified in body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] That moon would, in turn, have a Hill sphere of its own, and any object within that distance would tend to become a satellite of the moon, rather than of the planet itself.[_[not verified in body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Lagrange_points2.svg/330px-Lagrange_points2.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Lagrange_points2.svg)

A contour plot of the effective gravitational potential of a two-body system, here, the Sun and Earth, indicating the five [Lagrange points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrange_points "Lagrange points").[_[clarification needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Please\_clarify "Wikipedia:Please clarify")_][_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

One simple view of the extent of the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System") is that it is bounded by the Hill sphere of the [Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun "Sun") (engendered by the Sun's interaction with the [galactic nucleus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Active_galactic_nucleus "Active galactic nucleus") or other more massive stars).[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hill_sphere#cite_note-Chebotarev1964-4)[_[verification needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability "Wikipedia:Verifiability")_] A more complex example is the one at right, the Earth's Hill sphere, which extends between the [Lagrange points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrange_point "Lagrange point")[L 1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrange_point#L1 "Lagrange point") and [L 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrange_point#L2 "Lagrange point"),[_[clarification needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Please\_clarify "Wikipedia:Please clarify")_] which lie along the line of centers of the Earth and the more massive Sun.[_[not verified in body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] The gravitational influence of the less massive body is least in that direction, and so it acts as the limiting factor for the size of the Hill sphere;[_[clarification needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Please\_clarify "Wikipedia:Please clarify")_] beyond that distance, a third object in orbit around the Earth would spend at least part of its orbit outside the Hill sphere, and would be progressively perturbed by the tidal forces of the more massive body, the Sun, eventually ending up orbiting the latter.[_[not verified in body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

For two massive bodies with gravitational potentials and any given energy of a third object of negligible mass interacting with them, one can define a [zero-velocity surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero-velocity_surface "Zero-velocity surface") in space which cannot be passed, the contour of the [Jacobi integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacobi_integral "Jacobi integral").[_[not verified in body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] When the object's energy is low, the zero-velocity surface completely surrounds the less massive body (of this [restricted three-body system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Restricted_three-body_problem "Restricted three-body problem")), which means the third object cannot escape; at higher energy, there will be one or more gaps or bottlenecks by which the third object may escape the less massive body and go into orbit around the more massive one.[_[not verified in body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] If the energy is at the border between these two cases, then the third object cannot escape, but the zero-velocity surface confining it touches a larger zero-velocity surface around the less massive body[_[verification needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability "Wikipedia:Verifiability")_] at one of the nearby Lagrange points, forming a cone-like point there.[_[clarification needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Please\_clarify "Wikipedia:Please clarify")_][_[not verified in body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] At the opposite side of the less massive body, the zero-velocity surface gets close to the other Lagrange point.[_[not verified in body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

The Hill radius or sphere (the latter defined by the former radius[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]) has been described as "the region around a planetary body where its own gravity (compared to that of the Sun or other nearby bodies) is the dominant force in attracting satellites," both natural and artificial.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hill_sphere#cite_note-5)[_[better source needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTRS "Wikipedia:NOTRS")_]

As described by de Pater and Lissauer, all bodies within a system such as the Sun's [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System") "feel the gravitational force of one another", and while the motions of just two gravitationally interacting bodies—constituting a "two-body problem"—are "completely integrable ([meaning]...there exists one independent integral or constraint per degree of freedom)" and thus an exact, analytic solution, the interactions of _three_ (or more) such bodies "cannot be deduced analytically", requiring instead solutions by numerical integration, when possible.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hill_sphere#cite_note-dePaterLissauerPlanetarySciences-6): p.26  This is the case, unless the negligible mass of one of the three bodies allows approximation of the system as a two-body problem, known formally as a "restricted three-body problem".[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hill_sphere#cite_note-dePaterLissauerPlanetarySciences-6): p.26

For such two- or restricted three-body problems as its simplest examples—e.g., one more massive primary astrophysical body, mass of ![Image 4: {\displaystyle m_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/31aafa60e48d39ccce922404c0b80340b2cc777a), and a less massive secondary body, mass of ![Image 5: {\displaystyle m_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0ecebe334d5cadc3ffcf245eb02919034d7a2ec8)—the concept of a Hill radius or sphere is of the approximate limit to the secondary mass's "gravitational dominance",[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hill_sphere#cite_note-dePaterLissauerPlanetarySciences-6) a limit defined by "the extent" of its Hill sphere, which is represented mathematically as follows:[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hill_sphere#cite_note-dePaterLissauerPlanetarySciences-6): p.29 [[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hill_sphere#cite_note-HiguchiIdaTAJapril2017-7)

![Image 6: {\displaystyle R_{\mathrm {H} }\approx a{\sqrt[{3}]{\frac {m_{2}}{3(m_{1}+m_{2})}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eafb3c5ebd5b17a48527cd9d81b5c195a3d584b3),
where, in this representation, semi-major axis "![Image 7: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc)" can be understood as the "instantaneous heliocentric distance" between the two masses (elsewhere abbreviated _r p_).[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hill_sphere#cite_note-dePaterLissauerPlanetarySciences-6): p.29 [[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hill_sphere#cite_note-HiguchiIdaTAJapril2017-7)

Example and derivation
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hill_sphere&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Example and derivation")]

[![Image 14](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Comparison_of_Hill_sphere_and_Roche_limit.svg/250px-Comparison_of_Hill_sphere_and_Roche_limit.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Comparison_of_Hill_sphere_and_Roche_limit.svg)

A schematic, not-to-scale representation of Hill spheres (as 2D radii) and [Roche limits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roche_limit "Roche limit") of each body of the Sun-Earth-Moon system. The actual Hill radius for the Moon is on the order of 60,000 km (i.e., extending less than one-sixth the distance of the 378,000 km between the Moon and the Earth).[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hill_sphere#cite_note-9)

In the Earth-Sun example, the Earth (5.97×10 24 kg) orbits the Sun (1.99×10 30 kg) at a distance of 149.6 million km, or one [astronomical unit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_unit "Astronomical unit") (AU). The Hill sphere for Earth thus extends out to about 1.5 million km (0.01 AU). The Moon's orbit, at a distance of 0.384 million km from Earth, is comfortably within the gravitational [sphere of influence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere_of_influence_(astrodynamics) "Sphere of influence (astrodynamics)") of Earth and it is therefore not at risk of being pulled into an independent orbit around the Sun.

The earlier eccentricity-ignoring formula can be re-stated as follows:

Regions of stability
--------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hill_sphere&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Regions of stability")]

The Hill sphere is only an approximation, and other forces (such as [radiation pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiation_pressure "Radiation pressure") or the [Yarkovsky effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yarkovsky_effect "Yarkovsky effect")) can eventually perturb an object out of the sphere.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] As stated, the satellite (third mass) should be small enough that its gravity contributes negligibly.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hill_sphere#cite_note-dePaterLissauerPlanetarySciences-6): p.26ff

The region of stability for [retrograde orbits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Retrograde_orbit "Retrograde orbit") at a large distance from the primary is larger than the region for [prograde orbits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prograde_orbit "Prograde orbit") at a large distance from the primary. This was thought to explain the preponderance of retrograde moons around Jupiter; however, Saturn has a more even mix of retrograde/prograde moons so the reasons are more complicated.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hill_sphere#cite_note-10) In a two-planet system, the mutual Hill radius of the two planets must exceed ![Image 32: {\displaystyle 2{\sqrt {3}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2cdf91246d2cd0e905a1aaf5b4b85cfc5a5879cd) to be stable. Multi-planet systems of three or more with semi-major-axis differences of less than ten mutual Hill radii are always unstable. This is due to the loss of angular momentum due to perturbations by a third planet.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hill_sphere#cite_note-11)

It is possible for a Hill sphere to be so small that it is impossible to maintain an orbit around a body. For example, an astronaut could not have orbited the 104 [ton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tonnes "Tonnes")[Space Shuttle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_Shuttle "Space Shuttle") at an orbit 300 km above the Earth, because a 104-ton object at that altitude has a Hill sphere of only 120 cm in radius, much smaller than a Space Shuttle. A sphere of this size and mass would be denser than [lead](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lead "Lead"), and indeed, in [low Earth orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit "Low Earth orbit"), a spherical body must be more dense than lead in order to fit inside its own Hill sphere, or else it will be incapable of supporting an orbit. Satellites further out in [geostationary orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geostationary_satellite "Geostationary satellite"), however, would only need to be more than 6% of the density of water to fit inside their own Hill sphere.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

Within the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System"), the planet with the largest Hill radius is [Neptune](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune "Neptune"), with 116 million km, or 0.775 au; its great distance from the Sun amply compensates for its small mass relative to Jupiter (whose own Hill radius measures 53 million km). An [asteroid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asteroid "Asteroid") from the [asteroid belt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asteroid_belt "Asteroid belt") will have a Hill sphere that can reach 220,000 km (for [1 Ceres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1_Ceres "1 Ceres")), diminishing rapidly with decreasing mass. The Hill sphere of [66391 Moshup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/66391_Moshup "66391 Moshup"), a [Mercury-crossing asteroid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_Mercury-crossing_minor_planets "List of Mercury-crossing minor planets") that has a moon (named Squannit), measures 22 km in radius.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hill_sphere#cite_note-Moshup-12)

A typical [extrasolar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extrasolar_planet "Extrasolar planet") "[hot Jupiter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hot_Jupiter "Hot Jupiter")", [HD 209458 b](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HD_209458_b "HD 209458 b"),[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hill_sphere#cite_note-13) has a Hill sphere radius of 593,000 km, about eight times its physical radius of approx 71,000 km. Even the smallest close-in extrasolar planet, [CoRoT-7b](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CoRoT-7b "CoRoT-7b"),[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hill_sphere#cite_note-14) still has a Hill sphere radius (61,000 km), six times its physical radius (approx 10,000 km). Therefore, these planets could have small moons close in, although not within their respective [Roche limits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roche_limit "Roche limit").[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

Logarithmic plot of the Hill radii (in km) for the bodies of the solar system

*   [Laplace sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laplace_sphere "Laplace sphere")– A region in space where a satellite orbit is stable
*   [Interplanetary Transport Network](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_Transport_Network "Interplanetary Transport Network")– Low-energy trajectories in the Solar System
*   [_n_-body problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/N-body_problem "N-body problem")– Problem in physics and celestial mechanics
*   [Roche lobe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roche_lobe "Roche lobe")– Gravitationally-binding region around a star in a binary system
*   [Sphere of influence (astrodynamics)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere_of_influence_(astrodynamics) "Sphere of influence (astrodynamics)")– Region of space gravitationally dominated by a given body
*   [Sphere of influence (black hole)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere_of_influence_(black_hole) "Sphere of influence (black hole)")– Region where a supermassive black hole gravitationally dominates its galaxy
