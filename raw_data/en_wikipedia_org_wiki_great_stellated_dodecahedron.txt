3D model of a great stellated dodecahedron

In [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry"), the **great stellated dodecahedron** is a [Kepler–Poinsot polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler%E2%80%93Poinsot_polyhedron "Kepler–Poinsot polyhedron"), with [Schläfli symbol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schl%C3%A4fli_symbol "Schläfli symbol") {⁠5/2⁠,3}. It is one of four [nonconvex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nonconvex "Nonconvex")[regular polyhedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_regular_polytopes#Non-convex_2 "List of regular polytopes").

It is composed of 12 intersecting [pentagrammic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentagram "Pentagram") faces, with three pentagrams meeting at each vertex.

It shares its [vertex arrangement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_arrangement "Vertex arrangement"), although not its [vertex figure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_figure "Vertex figure") or [vertex configuration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_configuration "Vertex configuration"), with the regular [dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dodecahedron "Dodecahedron"), as well as being a [stellation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellation "Stellation") of a (smaller) dodecahedron. It is the only dodecahedral stellation with this property, apart from the dodecahedron itself. Its dual, the [great icosahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_icosahedron "Great icosahedron"), is related in a similar fashion to the [icosahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Icosahedron "Icosahedron").

Shaving the triangular pyramids off results in an [icosahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Icosahedron "Icosahedron").

If the pentagrammic faces are broken into triangles, it is topologically related to the [triakis icosahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triakis_icosahedron "Triakis icosahedron"), with the same face connectivity, but much taller [isosceles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isosceles "Isosceles") triangle faces. If the triangles are instead made to invert themselves and excavate the central icosahedron, the result is a [great dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_dodecahedron "Great dodecahedron").

The great stellated dodecahedron can be constructed analogously to the pentagram, its two-dimensional analogue, by attempting to stellate the _n_-dimensional [pentagonal polytope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentagonal_polytope "Pentagonal polytope") (which has pentagonal polytope faces and simplex vertex figures) until it can no longer be stellated; that is, it is its final stellation.

A truncation process applied to the great stellated dodecahedron produces a series of uniform polyhedra. Truncating edges down to points produces the [great icosidodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_icosidodecahedron "Great icosidodecahedron") as a rectified great stellated dodecahedron. The process completes as a birectification, reducing the original faces down to points, and producing the [great icosahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_icosahedron "Great icosahedron").

The [truncated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Truncation_(geometry) "Truncation (geometry)")_great stellated dodecahedron_ is a degenerate polyhedron, with 20 triangular faces from the truncated vertices, and 12 (hidden) pentagonal faces as truncations of the original pentagram faces, the latter forming a [great dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_dodecahedron "Great dodecahedron") inscribed within and sharing the edges of the icosahedron.
