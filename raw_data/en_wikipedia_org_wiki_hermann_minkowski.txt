**Hermann Minkowski**[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Minkowski#cite_note-2) (22 June 1864 – 12 January 1909) was a [mathematician](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematician "Mathematician") and professor at the [University of Königsberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_K%C3%B6nigsberg "University of Königsberg"), the [University of Zürich](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Z%C3%BCrich "University of Zürich"), and the [University of Göttingen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_G%C3%B6ttingen "University of Göttingen"), described variously as [German](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germany "Germany"),[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Minkowski#cite_note-Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica-3)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Minkowski#cite_note-:1-4)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Minkowski#cite_note-:2-5)[Polish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poland "Poland"),[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Minkowski#cite_note-:3-6)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Minkowski#cite_note-:4-7)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Minkowski#cite_note-:5-8)[Lithuanian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lithuania "Lithuania")-German,[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Minkowski#cite_note-:6-9) or [Russian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russia "Russia").[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Minkowski#cite_note-Marshall_Cavendish-10) He created and developed the [geometry of numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry_of_numbers "Geometry of numbers") and elements of [convex geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_geometry "Convex geometry"), and used [geometrical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry") methods to solve problems in [number theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_theory "Number theory"), [mathematical physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_physics "Mathematical physics"), and the [theory of relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theory_of_relativity "Theory of relativity").

Minkowski is perhaps best known for his foundational work describing space and time as a [four-dimensional space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Four-dimensional_space "Four-dimensional space"), now known as "[Minkowski spacetime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minkowski_spacetime "Minkowski spacetime")", which facilitated geometric interpretations of [Albert Einstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Einstein "Albert Einstein")'s [special theory of relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_theory_of_relativity "Special theory of relativity") (1905).

Personal life and family
------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hermann_Minkowski&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Personal life and family")]

Hermann Minkowski was born in the town of [Aleksota](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aleksota "Aleksota"), the [Suwałki Governorate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suwa%C5%82ki_Governorate "Suwałki Governorate"), the [Kingdom of Poland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congress_Poland "Congress Poland"), since 1864 part of the [Russian Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_Empire "Russian Empire"), to Lewin Boruch Minkowski, a merchant who subsidized the building of the [choral synagogue in Kovno](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaunas_Synagogue "Kaunas Synagogue"),[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Minkowski#cite_note-11)[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Minkowski#cite_note-12)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Minkowski#cite_note-13) and Rachel Taubmann, both of [Jewish descent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_Jews_in_Poland "History of Jews in Poland").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Minkowski#cite_note-14) Hermann was a younger brother of the [medical researcher](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medical_research "Medical research")[Oskar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oskar_Minkowski "Oskar Minkowski") (born 1858).[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Minkowski#cite_note-15) In different sources Minkowski's nationality is variously given as German,[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Minkowski#cite_note-Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica-3)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Minkowski#cite_note-:1-4)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Minkowski#cite_note-:2-5)[Polish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poland "Poland"),[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Minkowski#cite_note-:3-6)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Minkowski#cite_note-:4-7)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Minkowski#cite_note-:5-8) or Lithuanian-German,[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Minkowski#cite_note-:6-9) or Russian.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Minkowski#cite_note-Marshall_Cavendish-10)

To escape Jewish [persecution in the Russian Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anti-Jewish_pogroms_in_the_Russian_Empire "Anti-Jewish pogroms in the Russian Empire"), the family moved to Königsberg in 1872,[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Minkowski#cite_note-:0-16) where the father became involved in rag export and later in manufacture of mechanical clockwork tin toys (he operated his firm Lewin Minkowski & Son with his eldest son Max).[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Minkowski#cite_note-17)

Minkowski studied in [Königsberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K%C3%B6nigsberg "Königsberg") and taught in [Bonn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bonn "Bonn") (1887–1894), Königsberg (1894–1896) and [Zürich](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Z%C3%BCrich "Zürich") (1896–1902), and finally in [Göttingen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G%C3%B6ttingen "Göttingen") from 1902 until his death in 1909. He married Auguste Adler in 1897 with whom he had two daughters; the [electrical engineer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrical_engineering "Electrical engineering") and inventor [Reinhold Rudenberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reinhold_Rudenberg "Reinhold Rudenberg") was his son-in-law.

Minkowski died of [appendicitis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Appendicitis "Appendicitis") in Göttingen on 12 January 1909. [Max Born](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Max_Born "Max Born") delivered the obituary on behalf of the mathematics students at Göttingen.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Minkowski#cite_note-18)[David Hilbert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Hilbert "David Hilbert")'s obituary of Minkowski illustrates the deep friendship between the two mathematicians:

> Since my student years, Minkowski was my best, most dependable friend who supported me with all the depth and loyalty that was so characteristic of him. Science, which we loved above all else, brought us together; it seemed to us a garden full of flowers. In it, we enjoyed looking for hidden pathways and discovered many a new perspective that appealed to our sense of beauty, and when one of us showed it to the other and we marveled over it together, our joy was complete. He was for me a rare gift from heaven and I must be grateful to have possessed that gift for so long. Now death has suddenly torn him from our midst. However, what death cannot take away is his noble image in our hearts and the knowledge that his spirit continues to be active in us.

— Hilbert, 1909

The main-belt [asteroid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asteroid "Asteroid")[12493 Minkowski](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/12493_Minkowski "12493 Minkowski") and [M-matrices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M-matrix "M-matrix") are named in Minkowski's honor.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Minkowski#cite_note-springer-19)

Education and career
--------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hermann_Minkowski&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Education and career")]

[![Image 3](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Hermann_Minkowski.png/250px-Hermann_Minkowski.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Hermann_Minkowski.png)

Minkowski in 1883, at the time of being awarded the Mathematics Prize of the [French Academy of Sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Academy_of_Sciences "French Academy of Sciences")

Minkowski was educated in [East Prussia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_Prussia "East Prussia") at the _Albertina_ University of Königsberg, where he earned his doctorate in 1885 under the direction of [Ferdinand von Lindemann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ferdinand_von_Lindemann "Ferdinand von Lindemann"). In 1883, while still a student at Königsberg, he was awarded the Mathematics Prize of the [French Academy of Sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Academy_of_Sciences "French Academy of Sciences") for his manuscript on the theory of [quadratic forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadratic_form "Quadratic form"). Because of his very young age of 18, which was unheard of in the mathematics community and his obscurity as a mathematician at the time, his sharing the award with eminent English mathematician [Henry Smith](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry_John_Stephen_Smith "Henry John Stephen Smith") (who was certainly a great deal more famous than Hermann and to whom the prize was awarded posthumously) caused severe unrest among English mathematicians. The prize committee, despite the numerous complaints, never changed their decision. He also became a friend of another renowned mathematician, David Hilbert. His brother, [Oskar Minkowski](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oskar_Minkowski "Oskar Minkowski") (1858–1931), was a well-known physician and researcher.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Minkowski#cite_note-:0-16)

Minkowski taught at the universities of Bonn, Königsberg, Zürich, and Göttingen. At the [_Eidgenössische Polytechnikum_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federal_Polytechnic_Institute "Federal Polytechnic Institute"), today the [ETH Zurich](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ETH_Zurich "ETH Zurich"), he was one of Einstein's teachers.

Minkowski explored the arithmetic of [quadratic forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadratic_form "Quadratic form"), especially concerning _n_ variables, and his research into that topic led him to consider certain geometric properties in a space of _n_[dimensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension "Dimension"). In 1896, he presented his _[geometry of numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry\_of\_numbers "Geometry of numbers")_, a geometrical method that solved problems in [number theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_theory "Number theory"). He is also the creator of the [Minkowski Sausage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minkowski_Sausage "Minkowski Sausage")[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Minkowski#cite_note-20) and the [Minkowski cover](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minkowski_cover "Minkowski cover")[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Minkowski#cite_note-21) of a curve.

In 1902, he joined the Mathematics Department of Göttingen and became a close colleague of David Hilbert, whom he first met at university in Königsberg. [Constantin Carathéodory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constantin_Carath%C3%A9odory "Constantin Carathéodory") was one of his students there.

By 1908 Minkowski realized that the special theory of relativity, introduced by his former student [Albert Einstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Einstein "Albert Einstein") in 1905 and based on the previous work of [Lorentz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hendrik_Lorentz "Hendrik Lorentz") and [Poincaré](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Poincar%C3%A9 "Henri Poincaré"), could best be understood in a four-dimensional space, since known as the "Minkowski spacetime", in which [time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time "Time") and [space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space "Space") are not separated entities but intermingled in a four-dimensional [space–time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space%E2%80%93time "Space–time"), and in which the [Lorentz geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentz_transformation "Lorentz transformation") of special relativity can be effectively represented using the invariant interval ![Image 4: {\displaystyle x^{2}+y^{2}+z^{2}-c^{2}t^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/101d249c29d5d550f759172c0b4dd9dbc1279af1) (see [History of special relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_special_relativity "History of special relativity")).

The mathematical basis of Minkowski space can also be found in the [hyperboloid model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperboloid_model "Hyperboloid model") of [hyperbolic space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_space "Hyperbolic space") already known in the 19th century, because isometries (or motions) in hyperbolic space can be related to [Lorentz transformations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentz_transformations "Lorentz transformations"), which included contributions of [Wilhelm Killing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilhelm_Killing "Wilhelm Killing") (1880, 1885), [Henri Poincaré](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Poincar%C3%A9 "Henri Poincaré") (1881), [Homersham Cox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homersham_Cox_(mathematician) "Homersham Cox (mathematician)") (1881), [Alexander Macfarlane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_Macfarlane "Alexander Macfarlane") (1894) and others (see [History of Lorentz transformations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_Lorentz_transformations "History of Lorentz transformations")).

The beginning part of his address called "Space and Time" delivered at the 80th Assembly of German Natural Scientists and Physicians (21 September 1908) is now famous:

> The views of space and time which I wish to lay before you have sprung from the soil of experimental physics, and therein lies their strength. They are radical. Henceforth space by itself, and time by itself, are doomed to fade away into mere shadows, and only a kind of union of the two will preserve an independent reality.
