**Area** is the [measure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Measure_(mathematics) "Measure (mathematics)") of a [region](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_(mathematical_analysis) "Domain (mathematical analysis)")'s size on a [surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_(mathematics) "Surface (mathematics)"). The area of a plane region or _plane area_ refers to the area of a [shape](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shape "Shape") or [planar lamina](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planar_lamina "Planar lamina"), while _[surface area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface\_area "Surface area")_ refers to the area of an [open surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_surface "Open surface") or the [boundary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boundary_(mathematics) "Boundary (mathematics)") of a [three-dimensional object](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_geometry "Solid geometry"). Area can be understood as the amount of material with a given thickness that would be necessary to fashion a model of the shape, or the amount of [paint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paint "Paint") necessary to cover the surface with a single coat.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area#cite_note-MathWorld-1) It is the two-dimensional analogue of the [length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Length "Length") of a [curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_curve "Plane curve") (a one-dimensional concept) or the [volume](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume "Volume") of a solid (a three-dimensional concept). Two different regions may have the same area (as in [squaring the circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Squaring_the_circle "Squaring the circle")); by [synecdoche](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synecdoche "Synecdoche"), "area" sometimes is used to refer to the region, as in a "[polygonal area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygonal_area "Polygonal area")".

The area of a shape can be measured by comparing the shape to [squares](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square "Square") of a fixed size.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area#cite_note-AF-2) In the [International System of Units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Units "International System of Units") (SI), the standard unit of area is the [square metre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_metre "Square metre") (written as m 2), which is the area of a square whose sides are one [metre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metre "Metre") long.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area#cite_note-B-3) A shape with an area of three square metres would have the same area as three such squares. In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), the [unit square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_square "Unit square") is defined to have area one, and the area of any other shape or surface is a [dimensionless](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimensionless_quantity "Dimensionless quantity")[real number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number").

There are several well-known [formulas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formula "Formula") for the areas of simple shapes such as [triangles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle "Triangle"), [rectangles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle "Rectangle"), and [circles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle "Circle"). Using these formulas, the area of any [polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon "Polygon") can be found by [dividing the polygon into triangles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon_triangulation "Polygon triangulation").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area#cite_note-bkos-4) For shapes with curved boundary, [calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus "Calculus") is usually required to compute the area. Indeed, the problem of determining the area of plane figures was a major motivation for the [historical development of calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_calculus "History of calculus").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area#cite_note-5)

For a solid shape such as a [sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere "Sphere"), cone, or cylinder, the area of its boundary surface is called the [surface area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_area "Surface area").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area#cite_note-MathWorld-1)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area#cite_note-MathWorldSurfaceArea-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area#cite_note-7) Formulas for the surface areas of simple shapes were computed by the [ancient Greeks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_mathematics "Greek mathematics"), but computing the surface area of a more complicated shape usually requires [multivariable calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multivariable_calculus "Multivariable calculus").

Area plays an important role in modern mathematics. In addition to its obvious importance in [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry") and calculus, area is related to the definition of [determinants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Determinant "Determinant") in [linear algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_algebra "Linear algebra"), and is a basic property of surfaces in [differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry").[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area#cite_note-doCarmo-8) In [analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analysis "Analysis"), the area of a subset of the plane is defined using [Lebesgue measure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebesgue_measure "Lebesgue measure"),[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area#cite_note-Rudin-9) though not every subset is measurable if one supposes the axiom of choice.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area#cite_note-10) In general, area in higher mathematics is seen as a special case of volume for two-dimensional regions.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area#cite_note-MathWorld-1)

Area can be defined through the use of axioms, defining it as a function of a collection of certain plane figures to the set of real numbers. It can be proved that such a function exists.

An approach to defining what is meant by "area" is through [axioms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiom "Axiom"). "Area" can be defined as a function from a collection M of a special kinds of plane figures (termed measurable sets) to the set of real numbers, which satisfies the following properties:[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area#cite_note-Apostol-11)

*   For all _S_ in _M_, _a_(_S_) ≥ 0.
*   If _S_ and _T_ are in _M_ then so are _S_ ∪ _T_ and _S_ ∩ _T_, and also _a_(_S_∪_T_) = _a_(_S_) + _a_(_T_) − _a_(_S_ ∩ _T_).
*   If _S_ and _T_ are in _M_ with _S_ ⊆ _T_ then _T_ − _S_ is in _M_ and _a_(_T_−_S_) = _a_(_T_) − _a_(_S_).
*   If a set _S_ is in _M_ and _S_ is congruent to _T_ then _T_ is also in _M_ and _a_(_S_) = _a_(_T_).
*   Every rectangle _R_ is in _M_. If the rectangle has length _h_ and breadth _k_ then _a_(_R_) = _hk_.
*   Let _Q_ be a set enclosed between two step regions _S_ and _T_. A step region is formed from a finite union of adjacent rectangles resting on a common base, i.e. _S_ ⊆ _Q_ ⊆ _T_. If there is a unique number _c_ such that _a_(_S_) ≤ c ≤ _a_(_T_) for all such step regions _S_ and _T_, then _a_(_Q_) = _c_.

It can be proved that such an area function actually exists.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area#cite_note-Moise-12)

A square metre [quadrat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrat "Quadrat") made of PVC pipe

Every [unit of length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_of_length "Unit of length") has a corresponding unit of area, namely the area of a square with the given side length. Thus areas can be measured in [square metres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_metre "Square metre") (m 2), square centimetres (cm 2), square millimetres (mm 2), [square kilometres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_kilometre "Square kilometre") (km 2), [square feet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_foot "Square foot") (ft 2), [square yards](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_yard "Square yard") (yd 2), [square miles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_mile "Square mile") (mi 2), and so forth.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area#cite_note-BIPM2006Ch5-13) Algebraically, these units can be thought of as the [squares](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_(algebra) "Square (algebra)") of the corresponding length units.

The SI unit of area is the square metre, which is considered an [SI derived unit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SI_derived_unit "SI derived unit").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area#cite_note-B-3)

[![Image 4: A diagram showing the conversion factor between different areas](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Area_conversion_-_square_mm_in_a_square_cm.png/250px-Area_conversion_-_square_mm_in_a_square_cm.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Area_conversion_-_square_mm_in_a_square_cm.png)

Although there are 10 mm in 1 cm, there are 100 mm 2 in 1 cm 2.

Calculation of the area of a square whose length and width are 1 metre would be:

1 metre × 1 metre = 1 m 2

and so, a rectangle with different sides (say length of 3 metres and width of 2 metres) would have an area in square units that can be calculated as:

3 metres × 2 metres = 6 m 2. This is equivalent to 6 million square millimetres. Other useful conversions are:

*   1 square kilometre = [1,000,000](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Million "Million") square metres
*   1 square metre = [10,000](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/10000_(number) "10000 (number)") square centimetres = 1,000,000 square millimetres
*   1 square centimetre = [100](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/100_(number) "100 (number)") square millimetres.

In non-metric units, the conversion between two square units is the [square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_(algebra) "Square (algebra)") of the conversion between the corresponding length units.

1 [foot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foot_(unit) "Foot (unit)") = 12 [inches](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inch "Inch"),
the relationship between square feet and square inches is

1 square foot = 144 square inches,
where 144 = 12 2 = 12 × 12. Similarly:

*   1 square yard = [9](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/9_(number) "9 (number)") square feet
*   1 square mile = 3,097,600 square yards = 27,878,400 square feet

In addition, conversion factors include:

*   1 square inch = 6.4516 square centimetres
*   1 square foot = 0.092 903 04 square metres
*   1 square yard = 0.836 127 36 square metres
*   1 square mile = 2.589 988 110 336 square kilometres

### Other units including historical

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Area&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Other units including historical")]

There are several other common units for area. The [are](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Are_(unit) "Are (unit)") was the original unit of area in the [metric system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_system "Metric system"), with:

*   1 are = 100 square metres

Though the are has fallen out of use, the [hectare](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hectare "Hectare") is still commonly used to measure land:[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area#cite_note-BIPM2006Ch5-13)

*   1 hectare = 100 ares = 10,000 square metres = 0.01 square kilometres

Other uncommon metric units of area include the [tetrad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrad_(unit_of_area) "Tetrad (unit of area)"), the [hectad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hectad "Hectad"), and the [myriad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Myriad_(area) "Myriad (area)").

The [acre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acre "Acre") is also commonly used to measure land areas, where

*   1 acre = 4,840 square yards = 43,560 square feet.

An acre is approximately 40% of a hectare.

On the atomic scale, area is measured in units of [barns](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barn_(unit) "Barn (unit)"), such that:[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area#cite_note-BIPM2006Ch5-13)

*   1 barn = 10−28 square meters.

The barn is commonly used in describing the cross-sectional area of interaction in [nuclear physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_physics "Nuclear physics").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area#cite_note-BIPM2006Ch5-13)

In [South Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Asia "South Asia") (mainly Indians), although the countries use SI units as official, many South Asians still use traditional units. Each administrative division has its own area unit, some of them have same names, but with different values. There's no official consensus about the traditional units values. Thus, the conversions between the SI units and the traditional units may have different results, depending on what reference that has been used.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area#cite_note-14)[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area#cite_note-15)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area#cite_note-16)[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area#cite_note-17)

Some traditional South Asian units that have fixed value:

*   1 Killa = 1 acre
*   1 Ghumaon = 1 acre
*   1 Kanal = 0.125 acre (1 acre = 8 kanal)
*   1 Decimal = 48.4 square yards
*   1 Chatak = 180 square feet

In the 5th century BCE, [Hippocrates of Chios](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hippocrates_of_Chios "Hippocrates of Chios") was the first to show that the area of a disk (the region enclosed by a circle) is proportional to the square of its diameter, as part of his [quadrature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrature_(geometry) "Quadrature (geometry)") of the [lune of Hippocrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lune_of_Hippocrates "Lune of Hippocrates"),[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area#cite_note-heath-18) but did not identify the [constant of proportionality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constant_of_proportionality "Constant of proportionality"). [Eudoxus of Cnidus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eudoxus_of_Cnidus "Eudoxus of Cnidus"), also in the 5th century BCE, also found that the area of a disk is proportional to its radius squared.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area#cite_note-19)

Subsequently, Book I of [Euclid's _Elements_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid%27s_Elements "Euclid's Elements") dealt with equality of areas between two-dimensional figures. The mathematician [Archimedes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archimedes "Archimedes") used the tools of [Euclidean geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_geometry "Euclidean geometry") to show that the area inside a circle is equal to that of a [right triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_triangle "Right triangle") whose base has the length of the circle's circumference and whose height equals the circle's radius, in his book _[Measurement of a Circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Measurement\_of\_a\_Circle "Measurement of a Circle")_. (The circumference is 2 π _r_, and the area of a triangle is half the base times the height, yielding the area π _r_ 2 for the disk.) Archimedes approximated the value of π (and hence the area of a unit-radius circle) with [his doubling method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area_of_a_disk#Archimedes'_doubling_method "Area of a disk"), in which he inscribed a regular triangle in a circle and noted its area, then doubled the number of sides to give a regular [hexagon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hexagon "Hexagon"), then repeatedly doubled the number of sides as the polygon's area got closer and closer to that of the circle (and did the same with [circumscribed polygons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumscribed_polygon "Circumscribed polygon")).

[Heron of Alexandria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heron_of_Alexandria "Heron of Alexandria") found what is known as [Heron's formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heron%27s_formula "Heron's formula") for the area of a triangle in terms of its sides, and a proof can be found in his book, _Metrica_, written around 60 CE. It has been suggested that [Archimedes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archimedes "Archimedes") knew the formula over two centuries earlier,[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area#cite_note-20) and since _Metrica_ is a collection of the mathematical knowledge available in the ancient world, it is possible that the formula predates the reference given in that work.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area#cite_note-21) In 300 BCE Greek mathematician [Euclid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid "Euclid") proved that the area of a triangle is half that of a parallelogram with the same base and height in his book _Elements of Geometry_.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area#cite_note-22)

In 499 [Aryabhata](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aryabhata "Aryabhata"), a great [mathematician](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematician "Mathematician")-[astronomer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomer "Astronomer") from the classical age of [Indian mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_mathematics "Indian mathematics") and [Indian astronomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_astronomy "Indian astronomy"), expressed the area of a triangle as one-half the base times the height in the _[Aryabhatiya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aryabhatiya "Aryabhatiya")_.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area#cite_note-23)

A formula equivalent to Heron's was discovered by the Chinese independently of the Greeks. It was published in 1247 in _Shushu Jiuzhang_ ("[Mathematical Treatise in Nine Sections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_Treatise_in_Nine_Sections "Mathematical Treatise in Nine Sections")"), written by [Qin Jiushao](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qin_Jiushao "Qin Jiushao").[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area#cite_note-24)

In the 7th century CE, [Brahmagupta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brahmagupta "Brahmagupta") developed a formula, now known as [Brahmagupta's formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brahmagupta%27s_formula "Brahmagupta's formula"), for the area of a [cyclic quadrilateral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclic_quadrilateral "Cyclic quadrilateral") (a [quadrilateral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral "Quadrilateral")[inscribed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inscribed_figure "Inscribed figure") in a circle) in terms of its sides. In 1842, the German mathematicians [Carl Anton Bretschneider](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Anton_Bretschneider "Carl Anton Bretschneider") and [Karl Georg Christian von Staudt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karl_Georg_Christian_von_Staudt "Karl Georg Christian von Staudt") independently found a formula, known as [Bretschneider's formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bretschneider%27s_formula "Bretschneider's formula"), for the area of any quadrilateral.

### General polygon area

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Area&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: General polygon area")]

The development of [Cartesian coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system "Cartesian coordinate system") by [René Descartes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Descartes "René Descartes") in the 17th century allowed the development of the [surveyor's formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shoelace_formula "Shoelace formula") for the area of any polygon with known [vertex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_(geometry) "Vertex (geometry)") locations by [Gauss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauss "Gauss") in the 19th century.

### Areas determined using calculus

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Area&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Areas determined using calculus")]

The development of [integral calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral_calculus "Integral calculus") in the late 17th century provided tools that could subsequently be used for computing more complicated areas, such as the area of an [ellipse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse#Area "Ellipse") and the [surface areas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_area "Surface area") of various curved three-dimensional objects.

For a non-self-intersecting ([simple](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simple_polygon "Simple polygon")) polygon, the [Cartesian coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system "Cartesian coordinate system")![Image 5: {\displaystyle (x_{i},y_{i})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d6dbb919b91ccacf17ed47898048428a1baf9703) (_i_=0, 1, ..., _n_-1) of whose _n_[vertices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_(geometry) "Vertex (geometry)") are known, the area is given by the [surveyor's formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shoelace_formula "Shoelace formula"):[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area#cite_note-25)

![Image 6: {\displaystyle A={\frac {1}{2}}{\Biggl \vert }\sum _{i=0}^{n-1}(x_{i}y_{i+1}-x_{i+1}y_{i}){\Biggr \vert }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/75a7a8f3daee3a89bf30d86734f007b563007a4f)
where when _i_=_n_-1, then _i_+1 is expressed as [modulus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modular_arithmetic "Modular arithmetic")_n_ and so refers to 0.

[![Image 7: A rectangle with length and width labelled](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/RectangleLengthWidth.svg/190px-RectangleLengthWidth.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:RectangleLengthWidth.svg)

The area of this rectangle is lw.

The most basic area formula is the formula for the area of a [rectangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle "Rectangle"). Given a rectangle with length l and width w, the formula for the area is:[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area#cite_note-AF-2)

_A_ = _lw_(rectangle).
That is, the area of the rectangle is the length multiplied by the width. As a special case, as _l_ = _w_ in the case of a square, the area of a square with side length s is given by the formula:[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area#cite_note-MathWorld-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area#cite_note-AF-2)

_A_ = _s_ 2(square).
The formula for the area of a rectangle follows directly from the basic properties of area, and is sometimes taken as a [definition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Definition "Definition") or [axiom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiom "Axiom"). On the other hand, if [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry") is developed before [arithmetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic "Arithmetic"), this formula can be used to define [multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication "Multiplication") of [real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number").

#### Dissection, parallelograms, and triangles

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Area&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: Dissection, parallelograms, and triangles")]

[![Image 8](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/ParallelogramArea.svg/190px-ParallelogramArea.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:ParallelogramArea.svg)

A parallelogram can be cut up and re-arranged to form a rectangle.

Most other simple formulas for area follow from the method of [dissection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dissection_(geometry) "Dissection (geometry)"). This involves cutting a shape into pieces, whose areas must [sum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Addition "Addition") to the area of the original shape. For an example, any [parallelogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram "Parallelogram") can be subdivided into a [trapezoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid "Trapezoid") and a [right triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_triangle "Right triangle"), as shown in figure to the left. If the triangle is moved to the other side of the trapezoid, then the resulting figure is a rectangle. It follows that the area of the parallelogram is the same as the area of the rectangle:[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area#cite_note-AF-2)

_A_ = _bh_(parallelogram).[![Image 9](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/TriangleArea.svg/190px-TriangleArea.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:TriangleArea.svg)

A parallelogram split into two equal triangles

However, the same parallelogram can also be cut along a [diagonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diagonal "Diagonal") into two [congruent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congruence_(geometry) "Congruence (geometry)") triangles, as shown in the figure to the right. It follows that the area of each [triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle "Triangle") is half the area of the parallelogram:[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area#cite_note-AF-2)

![Image 10: {\displaystyle A={\frac {1}{2}}bh}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/78bb1af1b9493a6b26955f711ae9b95a68f671be)(triangle).
Similar arguments can be used to find area formulas for the [trapezoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid "Trapezoid")[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area#cite_note-26) as well as more complicated [polygons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon "Polygon").[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area#cite_note-27)

### Area of curved shapes

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Area&action=edit&section=16 "Edit section: Area of curved shapes")]

[![Image 11: A circle divided into many sectors can be re-arranged roughly to form a parallelogram](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/CircleArea.svg/250px-CircleArea.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:CircleArea.svg)

A circle can be divided into [sectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circular_sector "Circular sector") which rearrange to form an approximate [parallelogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram "Parallelogram").

The formula for the area of a [circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle "Circle") (more properly called the area enclosed by a circle or the area of a [disk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disk_(mathematics) "Disk (mathematics)")) is based on a similar method. Given a circle of radius _r_, it is possible to partition the circle into [sectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circular_sector "Circular sector"), as shown in the figure to the right. Each sector is approximately triangular in shape, and the sectors can be rearranged to form an approximate parallelogram. The height of this parallelogram is _r_, and the width is half the [circumference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumference "Circumference") of the circle, or π _r_. Thus, the total area of the circle is π _r_ 2:[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area#cite_note-AF-2)

_A_ = π _r_ 2(circle).
Though the dissection used in this formula is only approximate, the error becomes smaller and smaller as the circle is partitioned into more and more sectors. The [limit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limit_(mathematics) "Limit (mathematics)") of the areas of the approximate parallelograms is exactly π _r_ 2, which is the area of the circle.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area#cite_note-Surveyor-28)

This argument is actually a simple application of the ideas of [calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus "Calculus"). In ancient times, the [method of exhaustion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Method_of_exhaustion "Method of exhaustion") was used in a similar way to find the area of the circle, and this method is now recognized as a precursor to [integral calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral_calculus "Integral calculus"). Using modern methods, the area of a circle can be computed using a [definite integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Definite_integral "Definite integral"):

![Image 12: {\displaystyle A\;=\;2\int _{-r}^{r}{\sqrt {r^{2}-x^{2}}}\,dx\;=\;\pi r^{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e6b8d5ed507b330b0f07bf4310d64c8b687ea2ac)
The formula for the area enclosed by an [ellipse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse "Ellipse") is related to the formula of a circle; for an ellipse with [semi-major](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semi-major_axis "Semi-major axis") and [semi-minor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semi-minor_axis "Semi-minor axis") axes _x_ and _y_ the formula is:[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area#cite_note-AF-2)

![Image 13: {\displaystyle A=\pi xy.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a81da558d3d525f9ff7688980d2788087713390f)

### Non-planar surface area

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Area&action=edit&section=19 "Edit section: Non-planar surface area")]

[![Image 14: A blue sphere inside a cylinder of the same height and radius](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Archimedes_sphere_and_cylinder.svg/250px-Archimedes_sphere_and_cylinder.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Archimedes_sphere_and_cylinder.svg)

[Archimedes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archimedes "Archimedes") showed that the surface area of a [sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere "Sphere") is exactly four times the area of a flat [disk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disk_(mathematics) "Disk (mathematics)") of the same radius, and the volume enclosed by the sphere is exactly 2/3 of the volume of a [cylinder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylinder_(geometry) "Cylinder (geometry)") of the same height and radius.

Most basic formulas for [surface area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_area "Surface area") can be obtained by cutting surfaces and flattening them out (see: [developable surfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Developable_surface "Developable surface")). For example, if the side surface of a [cylinder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylinder_(geometry) "Cylinder (geometry)") (or any [prism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prism_(geometry) "Prism (geometry)")) is cut lengthwise, the surface can be flattened out into a rectangle. Similarly, if a cut is made along the side of a [cone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cone_(geometry) "Cone (geometry)"), the side surface can be flattened out into a sector of a circle, and the resulting area computed.

The formula for the surface area of a [sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere "Sphere") is more difficult to derive: because a sphere has nonzero [Gaussian curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaussian_curvature "Gaussian curvature"), it cannot be flattened out. The formula for the surface area of a sphere was first obtained by [Archimedes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archimedes "Archimedes") in his work _[On the Sphere and Cylinder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/On\_the\_Sphere\_and\_Cylinder "On the Sphere and Cylinder")_. The formula is:[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area#cite_note-MathWorldSurfaceArea-6)

_A_ = 4 _πr_ 2(sphere),
where _r_ is the radius of the sphere. As with the formula for the area of a circle, any derivation of this formula inherently uses methods similar to [calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus "Calculus").

#### Areas of 2-dimensional figures

Triangle area ![Image 16: {\displaystyle A={\tfrac {b\cdot h}{2}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3d4d00ca1155c1418f0f6dcef9bdc0c9e4bb6236)

*   A [triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle "Triangle"): ![Image 17: {\displaystyle {\tfrac {1}{2}}Bh}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f09dd787bfe06fe8b0eb87d06b87f8d2c05b91ea) (where _B_ is any side, and _h_ is the distance from the line on which _B_ lies to the other vertex of the triangle). This formula can be used if the height _h_ is known. If the lengths of the three sides are known then _[Heron's formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heron%27s\_formula "Heron's formula")_ can be used: ![Image 18: {\displaystyle {\sqrt {s(s-a)(s-b)(s-c)}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fd7e3feb227eeda3c19b052c797ee19a01a6c467) where _a_, _b_, _c_ are the sides of the triangle, and ![Image 19: {\displaystyle s={\tfrac {1}{2}}(a+b+c)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ed2c4193212526a50585182f301e85e2f1cdfde8) is half of its perimeter.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area#cite_note-AF-2) If an angle and its two included sides are given, the area is ![Image 20: {\displaystyle {\tfrac {1}{2}}ab\sin(C)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f3719986bc49ddbdd159b5920e995f1c79f0b6c6) where _C_ is the given angle and _a_ and _b_ are its included sides.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area#cite_note-AF-2) If the triangle is graphed on a coordinate plane, a matrix can be used and is simplified to the absolute value of ![Image 21: {\displaystyle {\tfrac {1}{2}}(x_{1}y_{2}+x_{2}y_{3}+x_{3}y_{1}-x_{2}y_{1}-x_{3}y_{2}-x_{1}y_{3})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5bc87b3de461b79f0fc4f5fde09a0cb00ced460e). This formula is also known as the [shoelace formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shoelace_formula "Shoelace formula") and is an easy way to solve for the area of a coordinate triangle by substituting the 3 points _(x 1,y 1)_, _(x 2,y 2)_, and _(x 3,y 3)_. The shoelace formula can also be used to find the areas of other polygons when their vertices are known. Another approach for a coordinate triangle is to use [calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus "Calculus") to find the area.
*   A [simple polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simple_polygon "Simple polygon") constructed on a grid of equal-distanced points (i.e., points with [integer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer") coordinates) such that all the polygon's vertices are grid points: ![Image 22: {\displaystyle i+{\frac {b}{2}}-1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f6f6bf0fc9eaa8a4693b7dacc2a9e1a420dcedc6), where _i_ is the number of grid points inside the polygon and _b_ is the number of boundary points. This result is known as [Pick's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pick%27s_theorem "Pick's theorem").[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area#cite_note-Pick-29)

[![Image 23: A diagram showing the area between a given curve and the x-axis](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Integral_as_region_under_curve.svg/250px-Integral_as_region_under_curve.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Integral_as_region_under_curve.svg)

![Image 29: {\displaystyle A={1 \over 2}\int r^{2}\,d\theta .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e9ceec9961f3bfb4a2e5edfe77b4465adff35c37)![Image 30: {\displaystyle \oint _{t_{0}}^{t_{1}}x{\dot {y}}\,dt=-\oint _{t_{0}}^{t_{1}}y{\dot {x}}\,dt={1 \over 2}\oint _{t_{0}}^{t_{1}}(x{\dot {y}}-y{\dot {x}})\,dt}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/940fad5094a1ce067667a15559feac98c0f02d5a)or the _z_-component of ![Image 31: {\displaystyle {1 \over 2}\oint _{t_{0}}^{t_{1}}{\vec {u}}\times {\dot {\vec {u}}}\,dt.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ce6bf91b1745a4a27fe07a8b76f66324fb212233)(For details, see [Green's theorem §Area calculation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green%27s_theorem#Area_calculation "Green's theorem").) This is the principle of the [planimeter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planimeter "Planimeter") mechanical device.

#### Bounded area between two quadratic functions

By the area integral formulas above and [Vieta's formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vieta%27s_formulas "Vieta's formulas"), we can obtain that[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area#cite_note-30)[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area#cite_note-31)![Image 33: {\displaystyle A={\frac {(b^{2}-4ac)^{3/2}}{6a^{2}}}={\frac {a}{6}}(\beta -\alpha )^{3},\qquad a\neq 0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6a3244866c46c29a0bb151501fc90f375a1ec3a2) The above remains valid if one of the bounding functions is linear instead of quadratic.

### Relation of area to perimeter

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Area&action=edit&section=27 "Edit section: Relation of area to perimeter")]

The [isoperimetric inequality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isoperimetric_inequality "Isoperimetric inequality") states that, for a closed curve of length _L_ (so the region it encloses has [perimeter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perimeter "Perimeter")_L_) and for area _A_ of the region that it encloses,

![Image 113: {\displaystyle 4\pi A\leq L^{2},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dd8bd2c0d6a1230d3672a79f0974b1488aa89df1)
and equality holds if and only if the curve is a [circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle "Circle"). Thus a circle has the largest area of any closed figure with a given perimeter.

At the other extreme, a figure with given perimeter _L_ could have an arbitrarily small area, as illustrated by a [rhombus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhombus "Rhombus") that is "tipped over" arbitrarily far so that two of its [angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle "Angle") are arbitrarily close to 0° and the other two are arbitrarily close to 180°.

For a circle, the ratio of the area to the [circumference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumference "Circumference") (the term for the perimeter of a circle) equals half the [radius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radius "Radius")_r_. This can be seen from the area formula _πr_ 2 and the circumference formula 2 _πr_.

The area of a [regular polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polygon "Regular polygon") is half its perimeter times the [apothem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apothem "Apothem") (where the apothem is the distance from the center to the nearest point on any side).

Doubling the edge lengths of a polygon multiplies its area by four, which is two (the ratio of the new to the old side length) raised to the power of two (the dimension of the space the polygon resides in). But if the one-dimensional lengths of a [fractal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal "Fractal") drawn in two dimensions are all doubled, the spatial content of the fractal scales by a power of two that is not necessarily an integer. This power is called the [fractal dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension "Fractal dimension") of the fractal. [[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area#cite_note-Mandelbrot1983-33)

There are an infinitude of lines that bisect the area of a triangle. Three of them are the [medians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Median_(triangle) "Median (triangle)") of the triangle (which connect the sides' midpoints with the opposite vertices), and these are [concurrent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concurrent_lines "Concurrent lines") at the triangle's [centroid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centroid "Centroid"); indeed, they are the only area bisectors that go through the centroid. Any line through a triangle that splits both the triangle's area and its perimeter in half goes through the triangle's incenter (the center of its [incircle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Incircle "Incircle")). There are either one, two, or three of these for any given triangle.

Any line through the midpoint of a parallelogram bisects the area.

All area bisectors of a circle or other ellipse go through the center, and any [chords](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chord_(geometry) "Chord (geometry)") through the center bisect the area. In the case of a circle they are the diameters of the circle.

Given a wire contour, the surface of least area spanning ("filling") it is a [minimal surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minimal_surface "Minimal surface"). Familiar examples include [soap bubbles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soap_bubble "Soap bubble").

The question of the [filling area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Filling_area_conjecture "Filling area conjecture") of the [Riemannian circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_circle "Riemannian circle") remains open.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area#cite_note-34)

The circle has the largest area of any two-dimensional object having the same perimeter.

A [cyclic polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclic_polygon "Cyclic polygon") (one inscribed in a circle) has the largest area of any polygon with a given number of sides of the same lengths.

A version of the [isoperimetric inequality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isoperimetric_inequality "Isoperimetric inequality") for triangles states that the triangle of greatest area among all those with a given perimeter is [equilateral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equilateral "Equilateral").[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area#cite_note-Chakerian-35)

The triangle of largest area of all those inscribed in a given circle is equilateral; and the triangle of smallest area of all those circumscribed around a given circle is equilateral.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area#cite_note-36)

The ratio of the area of the incircle to the area of an equilateral triangle, ![Image 114: {\displaystyle {\frac {\pi }{3{\sqrt {3}}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9e87580f1fcf9c9b18a8e4a5d9261491e79b8bf8), is larger than that of any non-equilateral triangle.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area#cite_note-37)

The ratio of the area to the square of the perimeter of an equilateral triangle, ![Image 115: {\displaystyle {\frac {1}{12{\sqrt {3}}}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/844409c74d8d57e537c55a4c22aeea1a25ecd65a) is larger than that for any other triangle.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area#cite_note-Chakerian-35)
