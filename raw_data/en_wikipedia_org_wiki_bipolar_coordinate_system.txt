Bipolar coordinate system

**Bipolar coordinates** are a two-dimensional [orthogonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_coordinates "Orthogonal coordinates")[coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_system "Coordinate system") based on the [Apollonian circles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apollonian_circles "Apollonian circles").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bipolar_coordinate_system#cite_note-bip-1) There is also a third system, based on two poles ([biangular coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biangular_coordinates "Biangular coordinates")).

The term "bipolar" is further used on occasion to describe other curves having two singular points (foci), such as [ellipses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse "Ellipse"), [hyperbolas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbola "Hyperbola"), and [Cassini ovals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cassini_oval "Cassini oval"). However, the term _bipolar coordinates_ is reserved for the coordinates described here, and never used for systems associated with those other curves, such as [elliptic coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptic_coordinates "Elliptic coordinates").

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Bipolar_coordinates.svg/500px-Bipolar_coordinates.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Bipolar_coordinates.svg)

Geometric interpretation of the bipolar coordinates. The angle σ is formed by the two foci and the point **P**, whereas _τ_ is the logarithm of the ratio of distances to the foci. The corresponding circles of constant _σ_ and _τ_ are shown in red and blue, respectively, and meet at right angles (magenta box); they are orthogonal.

The system is based on two [foci](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Focus_(geometry) "Focus (geometry)")_F_ 1 and _F_ 2. Referring to the figure at right, the _σ_-coordinate of a point _P_ equals the angle _F_ 1 _P_ _F_ 2, and the _τ_-coordinate equals the [natural logarithm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_logarithm "Natural logarithm") of the ratio of the distances _d_ 1 and _d_ 2:

![Image 3: {\displaystyle \tau =\ln {\frac {d_{1}}{d_{2}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/56b69b750c0b0da35f9a3d7545e7fdd0fed14be4)
If, in the Cartesian system, the foci are taken to lie at (−_a_,0) and (_a_,0), the coordinates of the point _P_ are

![Image 4: {\displaystyle x=a\ {\frac {\sinh \tau }{\cosh \tau -\cos \sigma }},\qquad y=a\ {\frac {\sin \sigma }{\cosh \tau -\cos \sigma }}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fec478689219b949da25f33312ac761d5c0ba0b6)
The coordinate _τ_ ranges from ![Image 5: {\displaystyle -\infty }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ca2608c4b5fd3bffc73585f8c67e379b4e99b6f1) (for points close to _F_ 1) to ![Image 6: {\displaystyle \infty }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c26c105004f30c27aa7c2a9c601550a4183b1f21) (for points close to _F_ 2). The coordinate _σ_ is only defined modulo _2π_, and is best taken to range from −_π_ to _π_, by taking it as the negative of the acute angle _F_ 1 _P_ _F_ 2 if _P_ is in the lower half plane.

Proof that coordinate system is orthogonal
------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bipolar_coordinates&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Proof that coordinate system is orthogonal")]

The equations for _x_ and _y_ can be combined to give

![Image 7: {\displaystyle x+iy=ai\cot \left({\frac {\sigma +i\tau }{2}}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/58659881662029ed0bccc56429e3cdb7598278b0)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bipolar_coordinate_system#cite_note-Polyanin-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bipolar_coordinate_system#cite_note-Happel-3)
or

![Image 8: {\displaystyle x+iy=a\coth \left({\frac {\tau -i\sigma }{2}}\right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9d339e4d0f81bcb04ae73c33da1e3c793a0ebc1f)
This equation shows that _σ_ and _τ_ are the real and imaginary parts of an [analytic function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytic_function "Analytic function") of _x+iy_ (with logarithmic branch points at the foci), which in turn proves (by appeal to the general theory of [conformal mapping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conformal_mapping "Conformal mapping")) (the [Cauchy-Riemann equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cauchy-Riemann_equations "Cauchy-Riemann equations")) that these particular curves of _σ_ and _τ_ intersect at right angles, i.e., it is an [orthogonal coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_coordinate_system "Orthogonal coordinate system").

Curves of constant _σ_ and _τ_
------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bipolar_coordinates&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Curves of constant σ and τ")]

[![Image 9](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Bipolar_sigma_isosurfaces.png/330px-Bipolar_sigma_isosurfaces.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Bipolar_sigma_isosurfaces.png)

[![Image 10](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Bipolar_tau_isosurfaces.png/330px-Bipolar_tau_isosurfaces.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Bipolar_tau_isosurfaces.png)

The curves of constant _σ_ correspond to non-concentric circles

![Image 11: {\displaystyle x^{2}+\left(y-a\cot \sigma \right)^{2}={\frac {a^{2}}{\sin ^{2}\sigma }}=a^{2}(1+\cot ^{2}\sigma )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5fc9ad23d53ced1f5c24a73449ee43ceaf9d440f)(1)

that intersect at the two foci. The centers of the constant-_σ_ circles lie on the _y_-axis at ![Image 12: {\displaystyle a\cot \sigma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c6459f83d3d95d637c99388bbddb9be154477842) with radius ![Image 13: {\displaystyle {\tfrac {a}{\sin \sigma }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/603617881b18f631d2716eb832da531db0f23ef3). Circles of positive _σ_ are centered above the _x_-axis, whereas those of negative _σ_ lie below the axis. As the magnitude |_σ_| − _π_/2 decreases, the radius of the circles decreases and the center approaches the origin (0,0), which is reached when |_σ_| = _π_/2. (From elementary geometry, all triangles on a circle with 2 vertices on opposite ends of a diameter are right triangles.)

The curves of constant ![Image 14: {\displaystyle \tau }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38a7dcde9730ef0853809fefc18d88771f95206c) are non-intersecting circles of different radii

![Image 15: {\displaystyle \left(x-a\coth \tau \right)^{2}+y^{2}={\frac {a^{2}}{\sinh ^{2}\tau }}=a^{2}(\coth ^{2}\tau -1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ac791343c93dee9319190558b875722154d185b0)(2)

that surround the foci but again are not concentric. The centers of the constant-_τ_ circles lie on the _x_-axis at ![Image 16: {\displaystyle a\coth \tau }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2402a0c6a92ecba6b402843a947fd88666e9f7be) with radius ![Image 17: {\displaystyle {\tfrac {a}{\sinh \tau }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/50df21fe660e42bc7b045bb90e98bd13f848da52). The circles of positive _τ_ lie in the right-hand side of the plane (_x_>0), whereas the circles of negative _τ_ lie in the left-hand side of the plane (_x_<0). The _τ_=0 curve corresponds to the _y_-axis (_x_=0). As the magnitude of _τ_ increases, the radius of the circles decreases and their centers approach the foci.

The passage from the Cartesian coordinates towards the bipolar coordinates can be done via the following formulas:

![Image 18: {\displaystyle \tau ={\frac {1}{2}}\ln {\frac {(x+a)^{2}+y^{2}}{(x-a)^{2}+y^{2}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2d44997a1be42ff87763b14d84dafc8bc71bca6b)
and

![Image 19: {\displaystyle \pi -\sigma =2\arctan {\frac {2ay}{a^{2}-x^{2}-y^{2}+{\sqrt {(a^{2}-x^{2}-y^{2})^{2}+4a^{2}y^{2}}}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fdecb46e59536586535d815060ae0fe53719743a)
The coordinates also have the identities:

![Image 20: {\displaystyle \tanh \tau ={\frac {2ax}{x^{2}+y^{2}+a^{2}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b40ade98f3bb02e20f726a245aa2b70ebb8f8e2)
and

![Image 21: {\displaystyle \tan \sigma ={\frac {2ay}{x^{2}+y^{2}-a^{2}}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a30ee4b90ae846b23c685dfba2b029e433167822)
which can derived by solving Eq. (1) and (2) for ![Image 22: {\displaystyle \cot \sigma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e44ea489f10a6dbfc679899830f643ad30da1d4d) and ![Image 23: {\displaystyle \coth \tau }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/662f99430d7e4ae8322acfe3f8d1516c78a05b2e), respectively.

To obtain the scale factors for bipolar coordinates, we take the differential of the equation for ![Image 24: {\displaystyle x+iy}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ceb1c6ce62a20dbfe9cb3d82dca889577b469703), which gives

![Image 25: {\displaystyle dx+i\,dy={\frac {-ia}{\sin ^{2}{\bigl (}{\tfrac {1}{2}}(\sigma +i\tau ){\bigr )}}}(d\sigma +i\,d\tau ).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/64e8817da9f4a53259484c3baeb2505fb59795ae)
Multiplying this equation with its [complex conjugate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_conjugate "Complex conjugate") yields

![Image 26: {\displaystyle (dx)^{2}+(dy)^{2}={\frac {a^{2}}{{\bigl [}2\sin {\tfrac {1}{2}}{\bigl (}\sigma +i\tau {\bigr )}\sin {\tfrac {1}{2}}{\bigl (}\sigma -i\tau {\bigr )}{\bigr ]}^{2}}}{\bigl (}(d\sigma )^{2}+(d\tau )^{2}{\bigr )}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bdabb55a6189bc11ed8a5febcfd33169e9b179cf)
Employing the trigonometric identities for products of sines and cosines, we obtain

![Image 27: {\displaystyle 2\sin {\tfrac {1}{2}}{\bigl (}\sigma +i\tau {\bigr )}\sin {\tfrac {1}{2}}{\bigl (}\sigma -i\tau {\bigr )}=\cos \sigma -\cosh \tau ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8a3240f997e508e51c985c6b077fa920c501865d)
from which it follows that

![Image 28: {\displaystyle (dx)^{2}+(dy)^{2}={\frac {a^{2}}{(\cosh \tau -\cos \sigma )^{2}}}{\bigl (}(d\sigma )^{2}+(d\tau )^{2}{\bigr )}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/574ad5fc8f24fd64d055ad011399906a4a2d7b3d)
Hence the scale factors for _σ_ and _τ_ are equal, and given by

![Image 29: {\displaystyle h_{\sigma }=h_{\tau }={\frac {a}{\cosh \tau -\cos \sigma }}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9b3faa77e70a20fb0b77fee77f49870d716208a7)
Many results now follow in quick succession from the general formulae for [orthogonal coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_coordinates "Orthogonal coordinates"). Thus, the [infinitesimal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinitesimal "Infinitesimal") area element equals

![Image 30: {\displaystyle dA={\frac {a^{2}}{\left(\cosh \tau -\cos \sigma \right)^{2}}}\,d\sigma \,d\tau ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/76b445a449b85fd4de6a2a2a7f1eb791879a28d8)
and the [Laplacian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laplacian "Laplacian") is given by

![Image 31: {\displaystyle \nabla ^{2}\Phi ={\frac {1}{a^{2}}}\left(\cosh \tau -\cos \sigma \right)^{2}\left({\frac {\partial ^{2}\Phi }{\partial \sigma ^{2}}}+{\frac {\partial ^{2}\Phi }{\partial \tau ^{2}}}\right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b0c53d3f1c8a081c58294f25d7975267a4d35f4)
Expressions for ![Image 32: {\displaystyle \nabla f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b7b4d6de89b52c5a5e6e1583cb63eaee263e307b), ![Image 33: {\displaystyle \nabla \cdot \mathbf {F} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1f4f5b9e6560d7fdaf1a69d9fdeff6beb1f327cc), and ![Image 34: {\displaystyle \nabla \times \mathbf {F} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c6e2429b7e3cb6965d1a6651703ff5ef0c88b378) can be expressed obtained by substituting the scale factors into the general formulae found in [orthogonal coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_coordinates "Orthogonal coordinates").

The classic applications of bipolar coordinates are in solving [partial differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_differential_equations "Partial differential equations"), e.g., [Laplace's equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laplace%27s_equation "Laplace's equation") or the [Helmholtz equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helmholtz_equation "Helmholtz equation"), for which bipolar coordinates allow a [separation of variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separation_of_variables#pde "Separation of variables"). An example is the [electric field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_field "Electric field") surrounding two parallel cylindrical conductors with unequal diameters.

Extension to 3-dimensions
-------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bipolar_coordinates&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Extension to 3-dimensions")]

Bipolar coordinates form the basis for several sets of three-dimensional [orthogonal coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_coordinates "Orthogonal coordinates").

*   The [bipolar cylindrical coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bipolar_cylindrical_coordinates "Bipolar cylindrical coordinates") are produced by translating the bipolar coordinates along the _z_-axis, i.e., the out-of-plane axis.

*   The [bispherical coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bispherical_coordinates "Bispherical coordinates") are produced by rotating the bipolar coordinates about the _x_-axis, i.e., the axis connecting the foci.

*   The [toroidal coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toroidal_coordinates "Toroidal coordinates") are produced by rotating the bipolar coordinates about the _y_-axis, i.e., the axis separating the foci.
