**C 70 fullerene** is the [fullerene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fullerene "Fullerene") molecule consisting of 70 [carbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon "Carbon") atoms. It is a cage-like fused-ring structure which resembles a rugby ball, made of 25 [hexagons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hexagon "Hexagon") and 12 [pentagons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentagon "Pentagon"), with a carbon atom at the vertices of each polygon and a bond along each polygon edge. A related fullerene molecule, named [buckminsterfullerene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene "Buckminsterfullerene") (or C 60 fullerene) consists of 60 carbon atoms.

It was first intentionally prepared in 1985 by [Harold Kroto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harold_Kroto "Harold Kroto"), [James R. Heath](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_R._Heath "James R. Heath"), Sean O'Brien, [Robert Curl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Curl "Robert Curl") and [Richard Smalley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Smalley "Richard Smalley") at [Rice University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rice_University "Rice University"). Kroto, Curl and Smalley were awarded the 1996 [Nobel Prize in Chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Chemistry "Nobel Prize in Chemistry") for their roles in the discovery of cage-like fullerenes. The name is a homage to [Buckminster Fuller](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminster_Fuller "Buckminster Fuller"), whose [geodesic domes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic_domes "Geodesic domes") these molecules resemble.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C70_fullerene#cite_note-4)

[![Image 8](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Hkroto.jpg/120px-Hkroto.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Hkroto.jpg)

Theoretical predictions of buckyball molecules appeared in the late 1960s to early 1970s,[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C70_fullerene#cite_note-k363-5) but they went largely unnoticed. In the early 1970s, the chemistry of unsaturated carbon configurations was studied by a group at the [University of Sussex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Sussex "University of Sussex"), led by Harry Kroto and David Walton. In the 1980s a technique was developed by Richard Smalley and Bob Curl at [Rice University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rice_University "Rice University"), Texas to isolate these substances. They used laser [vaporization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vaporization "Vaporization") of a suitable target to produce clusters of atoms. Kroto realized that by using a [graphite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graphite "Graphite") target.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C70_fullerene#cite_note-k368-6)

C 70 was discovered in 1985 by Robert Curl, Harold Kroto and Richard Smalley. Using [laser](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laser "Laser")[evaporation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evaporation "Evaporation") of [graphite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graphite "Graphite") they found C _n_ clusters (for even _n_ with _n_>20) of which the most common were C 60 and C 70. For this discovery they were awarded the [1996 Nobel Prize in Chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Chemistry "Nobel Prize in Chemistry"). The discovery of buckyballs was serendipitous, as the scientists were aiming to produce carbon [plasmas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasma_(physics) "Plasma (physics)") to replicate and characterize unidentified [interstellar matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_matter "Interstellar matter"). [Mass spectrometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass_spectrometry "Mass spectrometry") analysis of the product indicated the formation of spheroidal carbon molecules.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C70_fullerene#cite_note-k363-5)

In 1990, K.Fostiropoulos, W.Krätchmer and D.R.Huffman developed a simple and efficient method of producing fullerenes in gram and even kilogram amounts which boosted fullerene research. In this technique, carbon soot is produced from two high-purity graphite electrodes by igniting an arc discharge between them in an inert atmosphere (helium gas). Alternatively, soot is produced by [laser ablation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laser_ablation "Laser ablation") of graphite or [pyrolysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrolysis "Pyrolysis") of [aromatic hydrocarbons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aromatic_hydrocarbon "Aromatic hydrocarbon"). Fullerenes are extracted from the soot using a multistep procedure. First, the soot is dissolved in appropriate organic solvents. This step yields a solution containing up to 70% of C 60 and 15% of C 70, as well as other fullerenes. These fractions are separated using [chromatography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chromatography "Chromatography").[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C70_fullerene#cite_note-7)

The C 70 molecule has a [D 5h symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_symmetry "Molecular symmetry") and contains 37 faces (25 hexagons and 12 pentagons) with a carbon atom at the vertices of each polygon and a bond along each polygon edge. Its structure is similar to that of C 60 molecule (20 hexagons and 12 pentagons), but has a belt of 5 hexagons inserted at the equator. The molecule has eight bond lengths ranging between 0.137 and 0.146 nm. Each carbon atom in the structure is bonded covalently with 3 others.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C70_fullerene#cite_note-r1-8)

The structure of C 70 molecule. Red atoms indicate five hexagons additional to the C 60 molecule. 

C 70 can undergo six reversible, one-electron reductions to C 6−

70, whereas [oxidation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxidation "Oxidation") is irreversible. The first reduction requires around 1.0 V ([Fc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ferrocene "Ferrocene")/Fc+

), indicating that C 70 is an electron acceptor.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C70_fullerene#cite_note-buckminsterfullerene3-9)

Fullerenes are sparingly soluble in many aromatic [solvents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solvent "Solvent") such as [toluene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toluene "Toluene") and others like [carbon disulfide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_disulfide "Carbon disulfide"), but not in water. Solutions of C 70 are a reddish brown. Millimeter-sized crystals of C 70 can be grown from solution.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C70_fullerene#cite_note-11)

Solid C 70 crystallizes in [monoclinic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monoclinic "Monoclinic"), hexagonal, [rhombohedral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trigonal_crystal_system "Trigonal crystal system"), and face-centered cubic (fcc) polymorphs at room temperature. The fcc phase is more stable at temperatures above 70°C. The presence of these phases is rationalized as follows. In a solid, C 70 molecules form an fcc arrangement where the overall symmetry depends on their relative orientations. The low-symmetry monoclinic form is observed when molecular rotation is locked by temperature or strain. Partial rotation along one of the symmetry axes of the molecule results in the higher hexagonal or rhombohedral symmetries, which turn into a cubic structure when the molecules start freely rotating.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C70_fullerene#cite_note-r3-2)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C70_fullerene#cite_note-r2-12)

All phases of C 70 form brownish crystals with a [bandgap](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bandgap "Bandgap") of 1.77 eV;[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C70_fullerene#cite_note-r3-2) they are n-type [semiconductors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semiconductor "Semiconductor") where conductivity is attributed to oxygen diffusion into the solid from atmosphere.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C70_fullerene#cite_note-r4-13) The unit cell of fcc C 70 solid contains voids at 4 octahedral and 12 tetrahedral sites.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C70_fullerene#cite_note-14) They are large enough to accommodate impurity atoms. When electron-donating elements, such as alkali metals, are doped into these voids, C 70 converts into a conductor with conductivity up to around 2[S](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siemens_(unit) "Siemens (unit)")/cm.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C70_fullerene#cite_note-r5-15)
