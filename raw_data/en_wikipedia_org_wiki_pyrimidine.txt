**Pyrimidine** (C 4 H 4 N 2; ) is an [aromatic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aromatic "Aromatic"), [heterocyclic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heterocyclic_compound "Heterocyclic compound"), [organic compound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_compound "Organic compound") similar to [pyridine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyridine "Pyridine") (C 5 H 5 N).[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrimidine#cite_note-isbn0-582-27843-0-3) One of the three [diazines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diazine "Diazine") (six-membered heterocyclics with two [nitrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen "Nitrogen") atoms in the ring), it has nitrogen atoms at positions 1 and 3 in the ring.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrimidine#cite_note-JouleMills5th-4): 250  The other diazines are [pyrazine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrazine "Pyrazine") (nitrogen atoms at the 1 and 4 positions) and [pyridazine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyridazine "Pyridazine") (nitrogen atoms at the 1 and 2 positions).

In [nucleic acids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleic_acids "Nucleic acids"), three types of [nucleobases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleobases "Nucleobases") are pyrimidine [derivatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative_(chemistry) "Derivative (chemistry)"): [cytosine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cytosine "Cytosine") (C), [thymine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thymine "Thymine") (T), and [uracil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uracil "Uracil") (U).

Occurrence and history
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Pyrimidine&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Occurrence and history")]

[![Image 20](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a9/PinnerPyrimidin.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:PinnerPyrimidin.png)

Pinner's 1885 structure for pyrimidine

The pyrimidine ring system has wide occurrence in nature[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrimidine#cite_note-Lagoja1-5) as substituted and ring fused compounds and derivatives, including the [nucleotides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrimidine#Nucleotides)[cytosine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cytosine "Cytosine"), [thymine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thymine "Thymine") and [uracil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uracil "Uracil"), [thiamine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thiamine "Thiamine") (vitamin B1) and [alloxan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alloxan "Alloxan"). It is also found in many synthetic compounds such as [barbiturates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barbiturate "Barbiturate") and the HIV drug [zidovudine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zidovudine "Zidovudine"). Although pyrimidine derivatives such as alloxan were known in the early 19th century, a laboratory synthesis of a pyrimidine was not carried out until 1879,[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrimidine#cite_note-Lagoja1-5) when Grimaux reported the preparation of [barbituric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barbituric_acid "Barbituric acid") from [urea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urea "Urea") and [malonic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malonic_acid "Malonic acid") in the presence of [phosphorus oxychloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphorus_oxychloride "Phosphorus oxychloride").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrimidine#cite_note-Grimaux1879-6) The systematic study of pyrimidines began[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrimidine#cite_note-ElderfieldVol6-7) in 1884 with [Pinner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adolf_Pinner "Adolf Pinner"),[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrimidine#cite_note-Pinner1884-8) who synthesized derivatives by condensing [ethyl acetoacetate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethyl_acetoacetate "Ethyl acetoacetate") with [amidines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amidine "Amidine"). Pinner first proposed the name “pyrimidin” in 1885.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrimidine#cite_note-Pinner1885-9) The parent compound was first prepared by [Gabriel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siegmund_Gabriel "Siegmund Gabriel") and Colman in 1900,[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrimidine#cite_note-Gabriel1900-10)[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrimidine#cite_note-Lythgoe1951-11) by conversion of [barbituric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barbituric_acid "Barbituric acid") to 2,4,6-trichloropyrimidine followed by reduction using [zinc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zinc "Zinc") dust in hot water.

The nomenclature of pyrimidines is straightforward. However, like other heterocyclics, [tautomeric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tautomer "Tautomer")[hydroxyl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroxyl "Hydroxyl") groups yield complications since they exist primarily in the cyclic [amide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amide "Amide") form. For example, 2-hydroxypyrimidine is more properly named 2-pyrimidone. A partial list of trivial names of various pyrimidines exists.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrimidine#cite_note-BrownPyrimidines1994-12): 5–6

Chemical properties
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Pyrimidine&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Chemical properties")]

Per the classification by [Albert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adrien_Albert "Adrien Albert"),[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrimidine#cite_note-Albert1968-13): 56–62  six-membered heterocycles can be described as π-deficient. Substitution by electronegative groups or additional nitrogen atoms in the ring significantly increase the π-deficiency. These effects also decrease the basicity.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrimidine#cite_note-Albert1968-13): 437–439

Like pyridines, in pyrimidines the π-electron density is decreased to an even greater extent. Therefore, [electrophilic aromatic substitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrophilic_aromatic_substitution "Electrophilic aromatic substitution") is more difficult while [nucleophilic aromatic substitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleophilic_aromatic_substitution "Nucleophilic aromatic substitution") is facilitated. An example of the last reaction type is the displacement of the [amino](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amino "Amino") group in 2-aminopyrimidine by [chlorine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chlorine "Chlorine")[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrimidine#cite_note-14) and its reverse.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrimidine#cite_note-15)

Electron [lone pair](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lone_pair "Lone pair") availability ([basicity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basicity "Basicity")) is decreased compared to pyridine. Compared to pyridine, [_N_-alkylation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/N-alkylation "N-alkylation") and [_N_-oxidation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/N-oxidation "N-oxidation") are more difficult. The [p _K_ a](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PKa "PKa") value for protonated pyrimidine is 1.23 compared to 5.30 for pyridine. Protonation and other electrophilic additions will occur at only one nitrogen due to further deactivation by the second nitrogen.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrimidine#cite_note-JouleMills5th-4): 250  The 2-, 4-, and 6- positions on the pyrimidine ring are electron deficient analogous to those in pyridine and nitro- and dinitrobenzene. The 5-position is less electron deficient and substituents there are quite stable. However, electrophilic substitution is relatively facile at the 5-position, including [nitration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitration "Nitration") and halogenation.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrimidine#cite_note-BrownPyrimidines1994-12): 4–8

Reduction in [resonance stabilization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resonance_stabilization "Resonance stabilization") of pyrimidines may lead to addition and ring cleavage reactions rather than substitutions. One such manifestation is observed in the [Dimroth rearrangement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimroth_rearrangement "Dimroth rearrangement").

Pyrimidine is also found in [meteorites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteorite "Meteorite"), but scientists still do not know its origin. Pyrimidine also [photolytically](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photolysis "Photolysis") decomposes into [uracil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uracil "Uracil") under [ultraviolet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultraviolet "Ultraviolet") light.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrimidine#cite_note-pmid19778279-16)

[Pyrimidine biosynthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrimidine_metabolism "Pyrimidine metabolism") creates derivatives —like orotate, thymine, cytosine, and uracil— _de novo_ from carbamoyl phosphate and aspartate.

As is often the case with parent heterocyclic ring systems, the synthesis of pyrimidine is not that common and is usually performed by removing functional groups from derivatives. Primary syntheses in quantity involving [formamide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formamide "Formamide") have been reported.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrimidine#cite_note-BrownPyrimidines1994-12): 241–242

As a class, pyrimidines are typically synthesized by the principal synthesis involving cyclization of β-di[carbonyl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonyl "Carbonyl") compounds with N–C–N compounds. Reaction of the former with [amidines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amidine "Amidine") to give 2-substituted pyrimidines, with [urea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urea "Urea") to give 2-[pyrimidinones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrimidinone "Pyrimidinone"), and [guanidines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guanidine "Guanidine") to give 2-[aminopyrimidines](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Aminopyrimidine&action=edit&redlink=1 "Aminopyrimidine (page does not exist)") are typical.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrimidine#cite_note-BrownPyrimidines1994-12): 149–239

Pyrimidines can be prepared via the [Biginelli reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biginelli_reaction "Biginelli reaction") and other [multicomponent reactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multicomponent_reaction "Multicomponent reaction").[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrimidine#cite_note-17) Many other methods rely on [condensation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensation "Condensation") of [carbonyls](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonyl "Carbonyl") with diamines for instance the synthesis of 2-thio-6-methyluracil from [thiourea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thiourea "Thiourea") and [ethyl acetoacetate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethyl_acetoacetate "Ethyl acetoacetate")[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrimidine#cite_note-18) or the synthesis of 4-methylpyrimidine with 4,4-dimethoxy-2-butanone and [formamide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formamide "Formamide").[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrimidine#cite_note-19)

A novel method is by reaction of _N_-vinyl and _N_-aryl [amides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amide "Amide") with [carbonitriles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonitrile "Carbonitrile") under electrophilic activation of the amide with 2-chloro-pyridine and [trifluoromethanesulfonic anhydride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trifluoromethanesulfonic_anhydride "Trifluoromethanesulfonic anhydride"):[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrimidine#cite_note-20)

[![Image 21: Pyrimidine synthesis (Movassaghi 2006)](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/PyrimidineSynthAmideCarbonitrile.png/500px-PyrimidineSynthAmideCarbonitrile.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:PyrimidineSynthAmideCarbonitrile.png "Pyrimidine synthesis (Movassaghi 2006)")
Because of the decreased basicity compared to pyridine, electrophilic substitution of pyrimidine is less facile. [Protonation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protonation "Protonation") or [alkylation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkylation "Alkylation") typically takes place at only one of the ring nitrogen atoms. Mono-_N_-oxidation occurs by reaction with peracids.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrimidine#cite_note-JouleMills5th-4): 253–254

[Electrophilic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrophilic "Electrophilic")_C_-substitution of pyrimidine occurs at the 5-position, the least electron-deficient. [Nitration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitration "Nitration"), [nitrosation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrosation "Nitrosation"), [azo coupling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Azo_coupling "Azo coupling"), [halogenation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halogen "Halogen"), [sulfonation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfonation "Sulfonation"), [formylation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formyl "Formyl"), hydroxymethylation, and aminomethylation have been observed with substituted pyrimidines.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrimidine#cite_note-BrownPyrimidines1994-12): 9–13

[Nucleophilic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleophilic "Nucleophilic")_C_-substitution should be facilitated at the 2-, 4-, and 6-positions but there are only a few examples. Amination and hydroxylation have been observed for substituted pyrimidines. Reactions with Grignard or alkyllithium reagents yield 4-alkyl- or 4-aryl pyrimidine after aromatization.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrimidine#cite_note-BrownPyrimidines1994-12): 14–15

Free radical attack has been observed for pyrimidine and photochemical reactions have been observed for substituted pyrimidines.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrimidine#cite_note-BrownPyrimidines1994-12): 15–16  Pyrimidine can be hydrogenated to give tetrahydropyrimidine.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrimidine#cite_note-BrownPyrimidines1994-12): 17

The pyrimidine nitrogen bases found in [DNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNA "DNA") and [RNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RNA "RNA").

Three [nucleobases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleobase "Nucleobase") found in [nucleic acids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleic_acid "Nucleic acid"), [cytosine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cytosine "Cytosine") (C), [thymine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thymine "Thymine") (T), and [uracil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uracil "Uracil") (U), are pyrimidine derivatives:

In [DNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNA "DNA") and [RNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RNA "RNA"), these bases form [hydrogen bonds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_bond "Hydrogen bond") with their [complementary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complementarity_(molecular_biology) "Complementarity (molecular biology)")[purines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Purine "Purine"). Thus, in DNA, the [purines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Purines "Purines")[adenine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adenine "Adenine") (A) and [guanine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guanine "Guanine") (G) pair up with the pyrimidines thymine (T) and cytosine (C), respectively.

In [RNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RNA "RNA"), the complement of [adenine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adenine "Adenine") (A) is [uracil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uracil "Uracil") (U) instead of [thymine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thymine "Thymine") (T), so the pairs that form are [adenine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adenine "Adenine"):[uracil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uracil "Uracil") and [guanine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guanine "Guanine"):[cytosine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cytosine "Cytosine").

Very rarely, thymine can appear in RNA, or uracil in DNA, but when the other three major pyrimidine bases are represented, some minor pyrimidine bases can also occur in [nucleic acids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleic_acids "Nucleic acids"). These minor pyrimidines are usually [methylated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methylation "Methylation") versions of major ones and are postulated to have regulatory functions.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrimidine#cite_note-21)

These hydrogen bonding modes are for classical Watson–Crick [base pairing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Base_pair "Base pair"). Other hydrogen bonding modes ("wobble pairings") are available in both DNA and RNA, although the additional 2′-hydroxyl group of [RNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RNA "RNA") expands the configurations, through which RNA can form hydrogen bonds.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrimidine#cite_note-22)

Theoretical aspects
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Pyrimidine&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Theoretical aspects")]

In March 2015, [NASA Ames](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NASA_Ames "NASA Ames") scientists reported that, for the first time, complex [DNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNA "DNA") and [RNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RNA "RNA")[organic compounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_compound "Organic compound") of [life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life "Life"), including [uracil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uracil "Uracil"), [cytosine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cytosine "Cytosine") and [thymine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thymine "Thymine"), have been formed in the laboratory under [outer space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_space "Outer space") conditions, using starting chemicals, such as pyrimidine, found in [meteorites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteorite "Meteorite"). Pyrimidine, like [polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polycyclic_aromatic_hydrocarbons "Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons") (PAHs), the most carbon-rich chemical found in the [universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe "Universe"), may have been formed in [red giants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_giant "Red giant") or in [interstellar dust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_dust "Cosmic dust") and gas clouds.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrimidine#cite_note-NASA-20150303-23)[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrimidine#cite_note-Nuevo-24)[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrimidine#cite_note-Sanford-25)

### Prebiotic synthesis of pyrimidine nucleotides

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Pyrimidine&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Prebiotic synthesis of pyrimidine nucleotides")]

In order to understand how [life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life "Life") arose, knowledge is required of the chemical pathways that permit formation of the key building blocks of life under plausible [prebiotic conditions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abiogenesis "Abiogenesis"). The [RNA world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RNA_world "RNA world") hypothesis holds that in the [primordial soup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primordial_soup "Primordial soup") there existed free-floating [ribonucleotides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ribonucleotide "Ribonucleotide"), the fundamental molecules that combine in series to form [RNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RNA "RNA"). Complex molecules such as RNA must have emerged from relatively small molecules whose reactivity was governed by physico-chemical processes. RNA is composed of pyrimidine and [purine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Purine "Purine") nucleotides, both of which are necessary for reliable information transfer, and thus natural selection and Darwinian [evolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evolution "Evolution"). Becker et al. showed how pyrimidine [nucleosides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleoside "Nucleoside") can be synthesized from small molecules and [ribose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ribose "Ribose"), driven solely by wet-dry cycles.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrimidine#cite_note-26) Purine nucleosides can be synthesized by a similar pathway. 5’-mono-and diphosphates also form selectively from phosphate-containing minerals, allowing concurrent formation of [polyribonucleotides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynucleotide "Polynucleotide") with both the pyrimidine and purine bases. Thus a reaction network towards the pyrimidine and purine RNA building blocks can be established starting from simple atmospheric or volcanic molecules.
