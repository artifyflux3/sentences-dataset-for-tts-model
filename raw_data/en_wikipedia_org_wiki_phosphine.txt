**Phosphine** ([IUPAC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IUPAC "IUPAC") name: **phosphane**) is a colorless, flammable, highly toxic compound with the [chemical formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_formula "Chemical formula")[P](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphorus "Phosphorus")[H](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen")3, classed as a [pnictogen hydride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pnictogen_hydride "Pnictogen hydride"). Pure phosphine is odorless, but [technical grade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_purity "Chemical purity") samples have a highly [unpleasant odor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Odor#Types "Odor") like rotting fish, due to the presence of [substituted](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Substitution_reaction "Substitution reaction") phosphine and [diphosphane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diphosphane "Diphosphane") (P 2 H 4). With traces of P 2 H 4 present, PH 3 is spontaneously flammable in air ([pyrophoric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrophoric "Pyrophoric")), burning with a luminous flame. Phosphine is a highly toxic respiratory poison, and is [immediately dangerous to life or health](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immediately_dangerous_to_life_or_health "Immediately dangerous to life or health") at 50 ppm. Phosphine has a [trigonal pyramidal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trigonal_pyramidal_molecular_geometry "Trigonal pyramidal molecular geometry") structure.

**Phosphines** are compounds that include PH 3 and the [organophosphines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organophosphine "Organophosphine"), which are derived from PH 3 by substituting one or more hydrogen atoms with organic groups.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine#cite_note-goldbook_phosphines-4) They have the general formula PH 3−_n_ R _n_. **Phosphanes** are saturated phosphorus hydrides of the form P _n_ H _n_+2, such as [triphosphane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triphosphane "Triphosphane").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine#cite_note-iupac_phosphanes-5) Phosphine (PH 3) is the smallest of the phosphines and the smallest of the phosphanes.

Philippe Gengembre (1764–1838), a student of [Lavoisier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lavoisier "Lavoisier"), first obtained phosphine in 1783 by heating [white phosphorus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_phosphorus "White phosphorus") in an aqueous solution of [potash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potash "Potash") (potassium carbonate).[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine#cite_note-6)[[NB 1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine#cite_note-7)

Perhaps because of its strong association with elemental [phosphorus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphorus "Phosphorus"), phosphine was once regarded as a gaseous form of the element, but Lavoisier (1789) recognised it as a combination of phosphorus with hydrogen and described it as _phosphure d'hydrogène_ (phosphide of hydrogen).[[NB 2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine#cite_note-8)

In 1844, Paul Thénard, son of the French chemist [Louis Jacques Thénard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis_Jacques_Th%C3%A9nard "Louis Jacques Thénard"), used a [cold trap](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cold_trap "Cold trap") to separate diphosphine from phosphine that had been generated from [calcium phosphide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calcium_phosphide "Calcium phosphide"), thereby demonstrating that P 2 H 4 is responsible for spontaneous flammability associated with PH 3, and also for the characteristic orange/brown color that can form on surfaces, which is a polymerisation product.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine#cite_note-9) He considered diphosphine's formula to be PH 2, and thus an intermediate between elemental phosphorus, the higher polymers, and phosphine. [Calcium phosphide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calcium_phosphide "Calcium phosphide") (nominally Ca 3 P 2) produces more P 2 H 4 than other phosphides because of the preponderance of P-P bonds in the starting material.

The name "phosphine" was first used for organophosphorus compounds in 1857, being analogous to organic [amines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amine "Amine") (NR 3).[[NB 3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine#cite_note-10)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine#cite_note-11) The gas PH 3 was named "phosphine" by 1865 (or earlier).[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine#cite_note-12)

Structure and reactions
-----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Phosphine&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Structure and reactions")]

PH 3 is a [trigonal pyramidal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trigonal_pyramid "Trigonal pyramid") molecule with _C_ 3 _v_[molecular symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_symmetry "Molecular symmetry"). The [length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bond_length "Bond length") of the P−H bond is 1.42[Å](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angstrom "Angstrom"), the H−P−H [bond angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bond_angle "Bond angle") are 93.5[°](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_(angle) "Degree (angle)"). The [dipole moment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bond_dipole_moment "Bond dipole moment") is 0.58 D, which increases with [substitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Substitution_(chemistry) "Substitution (chemistry)") of [methyl groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methyl_group "Methyl group") in the series: CH 3 PH 2, 1.10 D; (CH 3)2 PH, 1.23 D; (CH 3)3 P, 1.19 D. In contrast, the dipole moments of amines decrease with substitution, starting with [ammonia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia "Ammonia"), which has a dipole moment of 1.47 D. The low dipole moment and almost orthogonal bond angles lead to the conclusion that in PH 3 the P−H bonds are almost entirely pσ(P) – sσ(H) and phosphorus 3s orbital contributes little to the P-H bonding. For this reason, the lone pair on phosphorus is predominantly formed by the 3s orbital of phosphorus. The upfield chemical shift of its [31 P NMR](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphorus-31_nuclear_magnetic_resonance "Phosphorus-31 nuclear magnetic resonance") signal accords with the conclusion that the lone pair electrons occupy the 3s orbital (Fluck, 1973). This electronic structure leads to a lack of [nucleophilicity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleophilicity "Nucleophilicity") in general and lack of basicity in particular (p _K_ aH = −14),[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine#cite_note-13) as well as an ability to form only weak [hydrogen bonds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_bonds "Hydrogen bonds").[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine#cite_note-14)

The aqueous [solubility](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solubility "Solubility") of PH 3 is slight: 0.22 cm 3 of gas dissolves in 1 cm 3 of water. Phosphine dissolves more readily in [non-polar solvents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solvent#Solvent_classifications "Solvent") than in water because of the non-polar P−H bonds. It is technically [amphoteric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amphoterism "Amphoterism") in water, but acid and base activity is poor. Proton exchange proceeds via a [phosphonium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphonium "Phosphonium") (PH+4) ion in acidic solutions and via [phosphanide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphanide "Phosphanide") (PH−2) at high pH, with equilibrium constants _K_ b = 4×10−28 and _K_ a = 41.6×10−29. Phosphine reacts with water only at high pressure and temperature, producing [phosphoric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphoric_acid "Phosphoric acid") and hydrogen:[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine#cite_note-:0-15)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine#cite_note-16)

PH 3 + 4H 2 O pressure & temperature→

H 3 PO 4 + 4H 2.

Burning phosphine in the air produces [phosphoric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphoric_acid "Phosphoric acid"):[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine#cite_note-17)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine#cite_note-:0-15)

PH 3 + 2 O 2 150°C→

H 3 PO 4.

Preparation and occurrence
--------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Phosphine&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Preparation and occurrence")]

Phosphine may be prepared in a variety of ways.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine#cite_note-18) Industrially it can be made by the reaction of white [phosphorus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphorus "Phosphorus") with [sodium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide "Sodium hydroxide") or [potassium hydroxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_hydroxide "Potassium hydroxide"), producing [potassium](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Potassium_hypophosphite&action=edit&redlink=1 "Potassium hypophosphite (page does not exist)") or [sodium hypophosphite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_hypophosphite "Sodium hypophosphite") as a by-product.

3 KOH + P 4 + 3 H 2 O → 3 KH 2 PO 2 + PH 3

3 NaOH + P 4 + 3 H 2 O → 3 NaH 2 PO 2 + PH 3

Alternatively, the acid-catalyzed [disproportionation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disproportionation "Disproportionation") of white [phosphorus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphorus "Phosphorus") yields [phosphoric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphoric_acid "Phosphoric acid") and phosphine. Both routes have industrial significance; the acid route is the preferred method if further reaction of the phosphine to substituted phosphines is needed. The acid route requires purification and pressurizing.

It is prepared in the laboratory by [disproportionation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disproportionation "Disproportionation") of [phosphorous acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphorous_acid "Phosphorous acid"):[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine#cite_note-19)

4 H 3 PO 3 → PH 3 + 3 H 3 PO 4 Phosphine evolution occurs at around 200°C.

Alternative methods are the hydrolysis [zinc phosphide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zinc_phosphide "Zinc phosphide"):

Zn 3 P 2 + 6 H 2 O → 3 Zn(OH)2 + 2 PH 3

[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine#cite_note-20) Some other metal phosphides could be used including [aluminium phosphide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aluminium_phosphide "Aluminium phosphide"), or [calcium phosphide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calcium_phosphide "Calcium phosphide"). Pure samples of phosphine, free from P 2 H 4, may be prepared using the action of [potassium hydroxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_hydroxide "Potassium hydroxide") on [phosphonium iodide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphonium_iodide "Phosphonium iodide"):

[PH 4]I + KOH → PH 3 + KI + H 2 O

Phosphine is a worldwide constituent of the Earth's atmosphere at very low and highly variable concentrations.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine#cite_note-21) It may contribute significantly to the global [phosphorus biochemical cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphorus_cycle "Phosphorus cycle"). The most likely source is [reduction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reduction_(chemistry) "Reduction (chemistry)") of [phosphate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphate "Phosphate") in decaying organic matter, possibly via partial reductions and [disproportionations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disproportionation "Disproportionation"), since environmental systems do not have known reducing agents of sufficient strength to directly convert phosphate to phosphine.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine#cite_note-22)

It is also found in [Jupiter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jupiter "Jupiter")'s atmosphere.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine#cite_note-23)

In 2020 a spectroscopic analysis was reported to show signs of phosphine in the [atmosphere of Venus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Venus "Atmosphere of Venus") in quantities that could not be explained by known [abiotic processes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abiotic_component "Abiotic component").[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine#cite_note-2020AsBio..20..235S-24)[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine#cite_note-NewsMIT-20191218-25)[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine#cite_note-SciNews-20191226-26) Later re-analysis of this work showed interpolation errors had been made, and re-analysis of data with the fixed algorithm do not result in the detection of phosphine.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine#cite_note-27)[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine#cite_note-Thompson2020-28) The authors of the original study then claimed to detect it with a much lower concentration of 1 ppb.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine#cite_note-Greaves202011-29)[_[disputed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Disputed\_statement "Wikipedia:Disputed statement") – [discuss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Talk:Phosphine "Talk:Phosphine")_]

### Organophosphorus chemistry

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Phosphine&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Organophosphorus chemistry")]

Phosphine is a precursor to many [organophosphorus compounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organophosphorus_compounds "Organophosphorus compounds"). It reacts with formaldehyde in the presence of [hydrogen chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_chloride "Hydrogen chloride") to give [tetrakis(hydroxymethyl)phosphonium chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrakis(hydroxymethyl)phosphonium_chloride "Tetrakis(hydroxymethyl)phosphonium chloride"), which is used in textiles. The [hydrophosphination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrophosphination "Hydrophosphination") of alkenes is versatile route to a variety of phosphines. For example, in the presence of basic catalysts PH 3 adds of [Michael acceptors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michael_acceptor "Michael acceptor"). Thus with [acrylonitrile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acrylonitrile "Acrylonitrile"), it reacts to give [tris(cyanoethyl)phosphine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tris(cyanoethyl)phosphine "Tris(cyanoethyl)phosphine"):[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine#cite_note-RussRev-30)

PH 3 + 3 CH 2=CHZ → P(CH 2 CH 2 Z)3 (Z is

NO 2, CN, or

C(O)NH 2)

Acid catalysis is applicable to hydrophosphination with [isobutylene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isobutylene "Isobutylene") and related analogues:

PH 3 + R 2 C=CH 2 → R 2(CH 3)CPH 2

where R is CH 3, alkyl, etc.

Phosphine is used as a [dopant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dopant "Dopant") in the [semiconductor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semiconductor "Semiconductor") industry, and a precursor for the deposition of [compound semiconductors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compound_semiconductor "Compound semiconductor"). Commercially significant products include [gallium phosphide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gallium_phosphide "Gallium phosphide") and [indium phosphide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indium_phosphide "Indium phosphide").[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine#cite_note-31)

### Fumigant (pest control)

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Phosphine&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Fumigant (pest control)")]

Phosphine is an attractive fumigant because it is lethal to insects and rodents, but degrades to phosphoric acid, which is non-toxic. As sources of phosphine, for [farm use](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agriculture "Agriculture"), pellets of [aluminium phosphide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aluminium_phosphide "Aluminium phosphide") (AlP), [calcium phosphide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calcium_phosphide "Calcium phosphide") (Ca 3 P 2), or [zinc phosphide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zinc_phosphide "Zinc phosphide") (Zn 3 P 2) are used. These phosphides release phosphine upon contact with atmospheric water or rodents' stomach acid. These pellets also contain reagents to reduce the potential for [ignition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Combustion "Combustion") or [explosion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Explosion "Explosion") of the released phosphine.

An alternative is the use of phosphine gas itself which requires dilution with either CO 2 or N 2 or even air to bring it below the flammability point. Use of the gas avoids the issues related with the solid residues left by metal phosphide and results in faster, more efficient control of the target pests.

One problem with phosphine fumigants is the increased resistance by insects.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine#cite_note-DLD-32)

Toxicity and safety
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Phosphine&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Toxicity and safety")]

Deaths have resulted from accidental exposure to fumigation materials containing [aluminium phosphide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aluminium_phosphide "Aluminium phosphide") or phosphine.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine#cite_note-haaretz.com-33)[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine#cite_note-34)[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine#cite_note-cbcth-35)[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine#cite_note-wp17-36) It can be absorbed either by [inhalation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inhalation "Inhalation") or [transdermally](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transdermal "Transdermal").[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine#cite_note-haaretz.com-33) As a respiratory poison, it affects the transport of oxygen or interferes with the utilization of oxygen by various cells in the body.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine#cite_note-cbcth-35) Exposure results in [pulmonary edema](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulmonary_edema "Pulmonary edema") (the lungs fill with fluid).[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine#cite_note-wp17-36) Phosphine gas is heavier than air so it stays near the floor.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine#cite_note-cbcfm-37)

Phosphine appears to be mainly a redox toxin, causing cell damage by inducing [oxidative stress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxidative_stress "Oxidative stress") and mitochondrial dysfunction.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine#cite_note-38) Resistance in insects is caused by a mutation in a mitochondrial metabolic gene.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine#cite_note-DLD-32)

Phosphine can be absorbed into the body by inhalation. The main target organ of phosphine gas is the respiratory tract.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine#cite_note-39) According to the 2009 U.S. [National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Institute_for_Occupational_Safety_and_Health "National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health") (NIOSH) pocket guide, and U.S. [Occupational Safety and Health Administration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Occupational_Safety_and_Health_Administration "Occupational Safety and Health Administration") (OSHA) regulation, the 8 hour average respiratory exposure should not exceed 0.3 ppm. NIOSH recommends that the short term respiratory exposure to phosphine gas should not exceed 1 ppm. The [Immediately Dangerous to Life or Health](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IDLH "IDLH") level is 50 ppm. Overexposure to phosphine gas causes nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, diarrhea, thirst, chest tightness, [dyspnea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dyspnea "Dyspnea") (breathing difficulty), muscle pain, chills, stupor or syncope, and pulmonary edema.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine#cite_note-40)[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine#cite_note-41) Phosphine has been reported to have the odor of decaying fish or garlic at concentrations below 0.3 ppm. The smell is normally restricted to laboratory areas or phosphine processing since the smell comes from the way the phosphine is extracted from the environment. However, it may occur elsewhere, such as in industrial waste landfills. Exposure to higher concentrations may cause [olfactory fatigue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Olfactory_fatigue "Olfactory fatigue").[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine#cite_note-42)

Phosphine is used for [pest control](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pest_control "Pest control"), but its usage is strictly regulated due to high toxicity.[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine#cite_note-43)[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine#cite_note-44) Gas from phosphine has high mortality rate[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine#cite_note-45) and has caused deaths in Sweden and other countries.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine#cite_note-46)[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine#cite_note-LJ_p.-47)[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine#cite_note-Moirangthem_Vidua_Jahan_Patnaik_2023_pp._350%E2%80%93353-48)

Because the previously popular [fumigant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fumigant "Fumigant")[methyl bromide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methyl_bromide "Methyl bromide") has been phased out in some countries under the [Montreal Protocol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Montreal_Protocol "Montreal Protocol"), phosphine is the only widely used, cost-effective, rapidly acting fumigant that does not leave residues on the stored product. Pests with high levels of [resistance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pesticide_resistance "Pesticide resistance") toward phosphine have become common in Asia, Australia and Brazil. High level resistance is also likely to occur in other regions, but has not been as closely monitored. Genetic variants that contribute to high level resistance to phosphine have been identified in the [dihydrolipoamide dehydrogenase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihydrolipoamide_dehydrogenase "Dihydrolipoamide dehydrogenase") gene.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine#cite_note-DLD-32) Identification of this gene now allows rapid molecular identification of resistant insects.

Phosphine gas is denser than air and hence may collect in low-lying areas. It can form explosive mixtures with air, and may also self-ignite.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine#cite_note-:0-15)

[Anne McCaffrey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anne_McCaffrey "Anne McCaffrey")'s Dragonriders of Pern series features genetically engineered dragons that breathe fire by producing phosphine by extracting it from minerals of their native planet.

In the 2008 [pilot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pilot_(Breaking_Bad) "Pilot (Breaking Bad)") of the crime drama television series _[Breaking Bad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Breaking\_Bad "Breaking Bad")_, [Walter White](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Walter_White_(Breaking_Bad) "Walter White (Breaking Bad)") poisons two rival gangsters by adding red phosphorus to boiling water to produce phosphine gas. However, this reaction in reality would require white phosphorus instead, and for the water to contain [sodium hydroxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide "Sodium hydroxide").[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine#cite_note-49)
