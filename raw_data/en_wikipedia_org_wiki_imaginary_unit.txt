[![Image 1](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/ImaginaryUnit5.svg/250px-ImaginaryUnit5.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:ImaginaryUnit5.svg)

The imaginary unit i in the [complex plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_plane "Complex plane"): Real numbers are conventionally drawn on the horizontal axis, and imaginary numbers on the vertical axis.

The **imaginary unit** or **unit imaginary number** (**i**) is a [mathematical constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_constant "Mathematical constant") that is a solution to the [quadratic equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadratic_equation "Quadratic equation")_x_ 2 + 1 = 0. Although there is no [real number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number") with this property, i can be used to extend the real numbers to what are called [complex numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number"), using [addition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Addition "Addition") and [multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication "Multiplication"). A simple example of the use of i in a complex number is 2 + 3 _i_.

[Imaginary numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imaginary_number "Imaginary number") are an important mathematical concept; they extend the real number system ![Image 2: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc) to the complex number system ![Image 3: {\displaystyle \mathbb {C} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c6ff6a3dc2982018ff20f1d2c927afc74a217be6) in which at least one [root](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Root_of_a_function "Root of a function") for every nonconstant [polynomial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial "Polynomial") exists (see [Algebraic closure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_closure "Algebraic closure") and [Fundamental theorem of algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_theorem_of_algebra "Fundamental theorem of algebra")). Here, the term _imaginary_ is used because there is no [real number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number") having a negative [square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_(algebra) "Square (algebra)").

There are two complex square roots of −1:i and −_i_, just as there are two complex [square roots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_root "Square root") of every real number other than [zero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero "Zero") (which has one [double square root](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiple_root "Multiple root")).

In contexts in which use of the letter i is ambiguous or problematic, the letter j is sometimes used instead. For example, in [electrical engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrical_engineering "Electrical engineering") and [control systems engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Control_systems_engineering "Control systems engineering"), the imaginary unit is normally denoted by j instead of i, because i is commonly used to denote [electric current](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_current "Electric current").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imaginary_unit#cite_note-1)

Square roots of negative numbers are called _imaginary_ because in [early-modern mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_mathematics#Renaissance "History of mathematics"), only what are now called [real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_numbers "Real numbers"), obtainable by physical measurements or basic arithmetic, were considered to be numbers at all – even [negative numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Negative_numbers "Negative numbers") were treated with skepticism – so the square root of a negative number was previously considered undefined or nonsensical. The name _imaginary_ is generally credited to [René Descartes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Descartes "René Descartes"), and [Isaac Newton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaac_Newton "Isaac Newton") used the term as early as 1670.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imaginary_unit#cite_note-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imaginary_unit#cite_note-3) The i notation was introduced by [Leonhard Euler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leonhard_Euler "Leonhard Euler").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imaginary_unit#cite_note-Boyer-4)

A _unit_ is an undivided whole, and _unity_ or the _unit number_ is the number [one](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1 "1") (1).

The imaginary unit i is defined solely by the property that its square is −1: ![Image 18: {\displaystyle i^{2}=-1.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/52ca91534578075175b2088069fb6a9a414eaa7b)

With i defined this way, it follows directly from [algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra "Algebra") that i and −_i_ are both square roots of −1.

Although the construction is called _imaginary_, and although the concept of an imaginary number may be intuitively more difficult to grasp than that of a real number, the construction is valid from a mathematical standpoint. Real number operations can be extended to imaginary and complex numbers, by treating i as an unknown quantity while manipulating an expression (and using the definition to replace any occurrence of _i_ 2 with −1). Higher integral powers of i are thus ![Image 19: {\displaystyle {\begin{alignedat}{3}i^{3}&=i^{2}i&&=(-1)i&&=-i,\\[3mu]i^{4}&=i^{3}i&&=\;\!(-i)i&&=\ \,1,\\[3mu]i^{5}&=i^{4}i&&=\ \,(1)i&&=\ \ i,\end{alignedat}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/381aa96747a694603793737c7038a20b314534e9) and so on, cycling through the four values 1, i, −1, and −_i_. As with any non-zero real number, _i_ 0 = 1.

As a complex number, i can be represented in [rectangular form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangular_coordinate_system "Rectangular coordinate system") as 0 + 1 _i_, with a zero real component and a unit imaginary component. In [polar form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_form "Polar form"), i can be represented as 1 × _e_ _πi_ /2 (or just _e_ _πi_ /2), with an [absolute value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_value "Absolute value") (or magnitude) of 1 and an [argument](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Argument_(complex_analysis) "Argument (complex analysis)") (or angle) of ![Image 20: {\displaystyle {\tfrac {\pi }{2}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4e31a202557dfbf326b44ebcc914ba3ab08fff1)[radians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radian "Radian"). (Adding any integer multiple of 2 _π_ to this angle works as well.) In the [complex plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_plane "Complex plane"), which is a special interpretation of a [Cartesian plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_plane "Cartesian plane"), i is the point located one unit from the origin along the [imaginary axis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imaginary_axis "Imaginary axis") (which is [perpendicular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular "Perpendicular") to the [real axis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_axis "Real axis")).

Being a [quadratic polynomial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadratic_polynomial "Quadratic polynomial") with no [multiple root](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiple_root "Multiple root"), the defining equation _x_ 2 = −1 has _two_ distinct solutions, which are equally valid and which happen to be [additive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Additive_inverse "Additive inverse") and [multiplicative inverses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplicative_inverse "Multiplicative inverse") of each other. Although the two solutions are distinct numbers, their properties are indistinguishable; there is no property that one has that the other does not. One of these two solutions is labelled +_i_ (or simply i) and the other is labelled −_i_, though it is inherently ambiguous which is which.

The only differences between +_i_ and −_i_ arise from this labelling. For example, by convention +_i_ is said to have an [argument](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Argument_(complex_analysis) "Argument (complex analysis)") of ![Image 21: {\displaystyle +{\tfrac {\pi }{2}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6d907696021fff2f7a76b1f6d592702ca2b971bb) and −_i_ is said to have an argument of ![Image 22: {\displaystyle -{\tfrac {\pi }{2}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9646bd065f3aecf19e0e4f6bb9a7a33a187da62b) related to the convention of labelling orientations in the [Cartesian plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_plane "Cartesian plane") relative to the positive x-axis with positive angles turning [anticlockwise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anticlockwise "Anticlockwise") in the direction of the positive y-axis. Also, despite the signs written with them, neither +_i_ nor −_i_ is inherently positive or negative in the sense that real numbers are.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imaginary_unit#cite_note-5)

A more formal expression of this indistinguishability of +_i_ and −_i_ is that, although the complex [field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(algebra) "Field (algebra)") is [unique](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unique_(mathematics) "Unique (mathematics)") (as an extension of the real numbers) [up to](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Up_to "Up to")[isomorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isomorphism "Isomorphism"), it is _not_ unique up to a _unique_ isomorphism. That is, there are two [field automorphisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Automorphism "Automorphism") of the complex numbers ![Image 23: {\displaystyle \mathbb {C} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f9add4085095b9b6d28d045fd9c92c2c09f549a7) that keep each real number fixed, namely the identity and [complex conjugation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_conjugation "Complex conjugation"). For more on this general phenomenon, see [Galois group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galois_group "Galois group").

Using the concepts of [matrices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_(mathematics) "Matrix (mathematics)") and [matrix multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_multiplication "Matrix multiplication"), complex numbers can be represented in linear algebra. The real unit 1 and imaginary unit i can be represented by any pair of matrices I and J satisfying _I_ 2 = _I_,_IJ_ = _JI_ = _J_, and _J_ 2 = −_I_. Then a complex number _a_ + _bi_ can be represented by the matrix _aI_ + _bJ_, and all of the ordinary rules of complex arithmetic can be derived from the rules of matrix arithmetic.

The most common choice is to represent 1 and i by the 2 × 2[identity matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Identity_matrix "Identity matrix")I and the matrix J,

![Image 24: {\displaystyle I={\begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&1\end{pmatrix}},\quad J={\begin{pmatrix}0&-1\\1&0\end{pmatrix}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf9eb1443afc06fa9a2932e06ec8abe87ca125ec)

Then an arbitrary complex number _a_ + _bi_ can be represented by:

![Image 25: {\displaystyle aI+bJ={\begin{pmatrix}a&-b\\b&a\end{pmatrix}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0758b687a01ef572e195feca9984608da9e795cb)

More generally, any real-valued 2 × 2 matrix with a [trace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trace_(linear_algebra) "Trace (linear algebra)") of zero and a [determinant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Determinant "Determinant") of one squares to −_I_, so could be chosen for J. Larger matrices could also be used; for example, 1 could be represented by the 4 × 4 identity matrix and i could be represented by any of the [Dirac matrices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dirac_matrices "Dirac matrices") for spatial dimensions.

[Polynomials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial "Polynomial") (weighted sums of the powers of a variable) are a basic tool in algebra. Polynomials whose [coefficients](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coefficient "Coefficient") are real numbers form a [ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ring_(mathematics) "Ring (mathematics)"), denoted ![Image 26: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} [x],}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8a990f60289eb6d302674d3d54b8e52f1c9955ad) an algebraic structure with addition and multiplication and sharing many properties with the ring of [integers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer").

The polynomial ![Image 27: {\displaystyle x^{2}+1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/92a3a8d23f9f8123651e496dcf8490990c65cf9a) has no real-number [roots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Root_of_a_polynomial "Root of a polynomial"), but the set of all real-coefficient polynomials divisible by ![Image 28: {\displaystyle x^{2}+1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/92a3a8d23f9f8123651e496dcf8490990c65cf9a) forms an [ideal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ideal_(ring_theory) "Ideal (ring theory)"), and so there is a [quotient ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial_ring#Quotient_ring "Polynomial ring")![Image 29: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} [x]/\langle x^{2}+1\rangle .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c90eaee192547028462852200009bcb20cf10261) This quotient ring is [isomorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isomorphism "Isomorphism") to the complex numbers, and the variable ![Image 30: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) expresses the imaginary unit.

### Graphic representation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Imaginary_unit&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Graphic representation")]

The complex numbers can be represented graphically by drawing the real [number line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_line "Number line") as the horizontal axis and the imaginary numbers as the vertical axis of a [Cartesian plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system "Cartesian coordinate system") called the _[complex plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex\_plane "Complex plane")_. In this representation, the numbers 1 and i are at the same distance from 0, with a right angle between them. Addition by a complex number corresponds to [translation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Translation_(geometry) "Translation (geometry)") in the plane, while multiplication by a unit-magnitude complex number corresponds to rotation about the origin. Every [similarity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Similarity_(geometry) "Similarity (geometry)") transformation of the plane can be represented by a complex-linear function ![Image 31: {\displaystyle z\mapsto az+b.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/37b1c613f6185677fe5204bf6e3925bb93f589ea)

In the [geometric algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_algebra "Geometric algebra") of the [Euclidean plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_plane "Euclidean plane"), the geometric product or quotient of two arbitrary [vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_vector "Euclidean vector") is a sum of a scalar (real number) part and a [bivector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bivector "Bivector") part. (A scalar is a quantity with no orientation, a vector is a quantity oriented like a line, and a bivector is a quantity oriented like a plane.) The square of any vector is a positive scalar, representing its length squared, while the square of any bivector is a negative scalar.

The quotient of a vector with itself is the scalar 1 = _u_/_u_, and when multiplied by any vector leaves it unchanged (the [identity transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Identity_function "Identity function")). The quotient of any two perpendicular vectors of the same magnitude, _J_ = _u_/_v_, which when multiplied rotates the divisor a quarter turn into the dividend, _Jv_ = _u_, is a unit bivector which squares to −1, and can thus be taken as a representative of the imaginary unit. Any sum of a scalar and bivector can be multiplied by a vector to scale and rotate it, and the algebra of such sums is [isomorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isomorphic "Isomorphic") to the algebra of complex numbers. In this interpretation points, vectors, and sums of scalars and bivectors are all distinct types of geometric objects.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imaginary_unit#cite_note-6)

More generally, in the geometric algebra of any higher-dimensional [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space"), a unit bivector of any arbitrary planar orientation squares to −1, so can be taken to represent the imaginary unit i.

The imaginary unit was historically written ![Image 32: {\textstyle {\sqrt {-1}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bec99ead8ec9d1e87896212e7d9a95b1f8371988) and still is in some modern works. However, great care needs to be taken when manipulating formulas involving [radicals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nth_root "Nth root"). The radical sign notation ![Image 33: {\textstyle {\sqrt {x}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/02f01419d50f8331ed8f948d3b0dce7d5bd75950) is reserved either for the principal (positive) square root of a positive real number or for the [principal square root of a complex number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_root#Principal_square_root_of_a_complex_number "Square root"). Attempting to apply the calculation rules of square roots of positive real numbers to manipulate square roots of complex numbers can produce false results:[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imaginary_unit#cite_note-7)![Image 34: {\displaystyle -1=i\cdot i={\sqrt {-1}}\cdot {\sqrt {-1}}\mathrel {\stackrel {\mathrm {fallacy} }{=}} {\textstyle {\sqrt {(-1)\cdot (-1)}}}={\sqrt {1}}=1\qquad {\text{(incorrect).}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/74e568993446fe147ca254950e88d32358286914)

Generally, the calculation rules ![Image 35: {\textstyle {\sqrt {x{\vphantom {ty}}}}\cdot \!{\sqrt {y{\vphantom {ty}}}}={\sqrt {x\cdot y{\vphantom {ty}}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0e92426bb7eaa8da544ca56ed9a2246df22973e7) and ![Image 36: {\textstyle {\sqrt {x{\vphantom {ty}}}}{\big /}\!{\sqrt {y{\vphantom {ty}}}}={\sqrt {x/y}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/803d9bf9e7ba2bb45d700a729a33ec4882c51b19) are guaranteed to be valid only when x and y are both positive real numbers.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imaginary_unit#cite_note-8)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imaginary_unit#cite_note-9)[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imaginary_unit#cite_note-10)

When x or y is real but negative, these problems can be avoided by writing and manipulating expressions like ![Image 37: {\textstyle i{\sqrt {7}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fa5508cd971f570f2b4357d77a7ed67c82175a57), rather than ![Image 38: {\textstyle {\sqrt {-7}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f4b6c234373104826f5748b928578b8cb35ce3a3). For a more thorough discussion, see the articles [Square root](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_root "Square root") and [Branch point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Branch_point "Branch point").

As a complex number, the imaginary unit follows all of the rules of [complex arithmetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number#Relations_and_operations "Complex number").

### Imaginary integers and imaginary numbers

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Imaginary_unit&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Imaginary integers and imaginary numbers")]

When the imaginary unit is repeatedly added or subtracted, the result is some [integer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer") times the imaginary unit, an _imaginary integer_; any such numbers can be added and the result is also an imaginary integer:

![Image 39: {\displaystyle ai+bi=(a+b)i.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b988697ffc5743f59938491429a5e3c2acb1f391)

Thus, the imaginary unit is the generator of a [group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_(mathematics) "Group (mathematics)") under addition, specifically an infinite [cyclic group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclic_group "Cyclic group").

The imaginary unit can also be multiplied by any arbitrary [real number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number") to form an [imaginary number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imaginary_number "Imaginary number"). These numbers can be pictured on a [number line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_line "Number line"), the _imaginary axis_, which as part of the complex plane is typically drawn with a vertical orientation, perpendicular to the real axis which is drawn horizontally.

Integer sums of the real unit 1 and the imaginary unit i form a [square lattice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_lattice "Square lattice") in the complex plane called the [Gaussian integers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaussian_integers "Gaussian integers"). The sum, difference, or product of Gaussian integers is also a Gaussian integer:

![Image 40: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}(a+bi)+(c+di)&=(a+c)+(b+d)i,\\[5mu](a+bi)(c+di)&=(ac-bd)+(ad+bc)i.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a410e1941798b67ee103d73e09a01fe5f61747ec)

### Quarter-turn rotation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Imaginary_unit&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Quarter-turn rotation")]

When multiplied by the imaginary unit i, any arbitrary complex number in the complex plane is rotated by a quarter turn (![Image 41: {\displaystyle {\tfrac {1}{2}}\pi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cfe27b6e44239efb1973b09f9eae178854860d5e) radians or 90°) [anticlockwise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anticlockwise "Anticlockwise"). When multiplied by −_i_, any arbitrary complex number is rotated by a quarter turn clockwise. In polar form:

![Image 42: {\displaystyle i\,re^{\varphi i}=re^{(\varphi +\pi /2)i},\quad -i\,re^{\varphi i}=re^{(\varphi -\pi /2)i}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6f98f1a2fb2d42749b2bcd8227da8f034ff73174)

In rectangular form,

![Image 43: {\displaystyle i(a+bi)=-b+ai,\quad -i(a+bi)=b-ai.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c6403d7d588920796783a4bca921a1757908c84f)

The powers of i repeat in a cycle expressible with the following pattern, where n is any integer:

![Image 44: {\displaystyle i^{4n}=1,\quad i^{4n+1}=i,\quad i^{4n+2}=-1,\quad i^{4n+3}=-i.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/78340d5e5ee55d274a1985b4d32fccf425c83655)

Thus, under multiplication, i is a generator of a [cyclic group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclic_group "Cyclic group") of order 4, a discrete subgroup of the continuous [circle group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle_group "Circle group") of the unit complex numbers under multiplication.

Written as a special case of [Euler's formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler%27s_formula "Euler's formula") for an integer n,

![Image 45: {\displaystyle i^{n}={\exp }{\bigl (}{\tfrac {1}{2}}\pi i{\bigr )}^{n}={\exp }{\bigl (}{\tfrac {1}{2}}n\pi i{\bigr )}={\cos }{\bigl (}{\tfrac {1}{2}}n\pi {\bigr )}+{i\sin }{\bigl (}{\tfrac {1}{2}}n\pi {\bigr )}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/89992fdfee7761f03e1b66ae327bcb9a401d23b9)

With a careful choice of [branch cuts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Branch_cut "Branch cut") and [principal values](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principal_value "Principal value"), this last equation can also apply to arbitrary complex values of n, including cases like _n_ = _i_.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

[![Image 46](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Imaginary2Root.svg/250px-Imaginary2Root.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Imaginary2Root.svg)

### Exponential and logarithm

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Imaginary_unit&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: Exponential and logarithm")]

The [complex exponential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_exponential "Complex exponential") function relates complex addition in the domain to [complex multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_multiplication "Complex multiplication") in the codomain. Real values in the domain represent scaling in the codomain (multiplication by a real scalar) with 1 representing multiplication by e, while imaginary values in the domain represent rotation in the codomain (multiplication by a unit complex number) with i representing a rotation by 1 radian. The complex exponential is thus a [periodic function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Periodic_function "Periodic function") in the imaginary direction, with period 2 _πi_ and image 1 at points 2 _kπi_ for all integers k, a real multiple of the lattice of imaginary integers.

The complex exponential can be broken into [even and odd](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Even_and_odd_functions "Even and odd functions") components, the [hyperbolic functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_functions "Hyperbolic functions")cosh and sinh or the [trigonometric functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trigonometric_functions "Trigonometric functions")cos and sin:

![Image 54: {\displaystyle \exp z=\cosh z+\sinh z=\cos(-iz)+i\sin(-iz)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dfa2bd90065079aec5e51900e91458fe07688d2e)

[Euler's formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler%27s_formula "Euler's formula") decomposes the exponential of an imaginary number representing a rotation:

![Image 55: {\displaystyle \exp i\varphi =\cos \varphi +i\sin \varphi .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/95418ad5c2ea235288cda8dfff428b275e997d40)

This fact can be used to demonstrate, among other things, the apparently counterintuitive result that ![Image 56: {\displaystyle i^{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7d4035ae18d005ce695d4fc30cb23c0771ab4cd8) is a real number.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imaginary_unit#cite_note-14)

The quotient coth _z_ = cosh _z_ / sinh _z_, with appropriate scaling, can be represented as an infinite [partial fraction decomposition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_fraction_decomposition "Partial fraction decomposition") as the sum of [reciprocal functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reciprocal_function "Reciprocal function") translated by imaginary integers:[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imaginary_unit#cite_note-15)![Image 57: {\displaystyle \pi \coth \pi z=\lim _{n\to \infty }\sum _{k=-n}^{n}{\frac {1}{z+ki}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/40f1b8c8e6346bd8891d63b85d51a9d3677803b7)

Other functions based on the complex exponential are well-defined with imaginary inputs. For example, a number raised to the ni power is: ![Image 58: {\displaystyle x^{ni}=\cos(n\ln x)+i\sin(n\ln x).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0aab83b5684f9db8957d5469b0ef9bcc4ae8cc25)

Because the exponential is periodic, its inverse the [complex logarithm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_logarithm "Complex logarithm") is a [multi-valued function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multi-valued_function "Multi-valued function"), with each complex number in the domain corresponding to multiple values in the codomain, separated from each-other by any integer multiple of 2 _πi_. One way of obtaining a single-valued function is to treat the codomain as a [cylinder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylinder "Cylinder"), with complex values separated by any integer multiple of 2 _πi_ treated as the same value; another is to take the domain to be a [Riemann surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_surface "Riemann surface") consisting of multiple copies of the complex plane stitched together along the negative real axis as a [branch cut](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Branch_cut "Branch cut"), with each branch in the domain corresponding to one infinite strip in the codomain.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imaginary_unit#cite_note-16) Functions depending on the complex logarithm therefore depend on careful choice of branch to define and evaluate clearly.

For example, if one chooses any branch where ![Image 59: {\displaystyle \ln i={\tfrac {1}{2}}\pi i}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/48d4a4afe147b7f42f1d029b2610bbf70ddc0743) then when x is a positive real number, ![Image 60: {\displaystyle \log _{i}x=-{\frac {2i\ln x}{\pi }}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/03bb9e65545890b04c55922cbabb05e6b065dd60)

The [factorial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Factorial "Factorial") of the imaginary unit i is most often given in terms of the [gamma function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamma_function "Gamma function") evaluated at 1 + _i_:[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imaginary_unit#cite_note-17)

![Image 61: {\displaystyle i!=\Gamma (1+i)=i\Gamma (i)\approx 0.4980-0.1549\,i.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7eca8e1ebb9d85c66ec31d102dafedd45c8c2a61)

The magnitude and argument of this number are:[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imaginary_unit#cite_note-18)

![Image 62: {\displaystyle |\Gamma (1+i)|={\sqrt {\frac {\pi }{\sinh \pi }}}\approx 0.5216,\quad \arg {\Gamma (1+i)}\approx -0.3016.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/94c854c93c0025e22f3b65057aabac4e496d077c)
