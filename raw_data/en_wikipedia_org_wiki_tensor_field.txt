In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics") and [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics"), a **tensor field** is a [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)") assigning a [tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor "Tensor") to each point of a [region](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Region_(mathematics) "Region (mathematics)") of a [mathematical space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_space "Mathematical space") (typically a [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space") or [manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manifold "Manifold")) or of the [physical space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_space "Physical space"). Tensor fields are used in [differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry"), [algebraic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_geometry "Algebraic geometry"), [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity"), in the analysis of [stress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stress_(physics) "Stress (physics)") and [strain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strain_tensor "Strain tensor") in material object, and in numerous applications in the [physical sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_sciences "Physical sciences"). As a tensor is a generalization of a [scalar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_(physics) "Scalar (physics)") (a pure number representing a value, for example speed) and a [vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_(physics) "Vector (physics)") (a magnitude and a direction, like velocity), a tensor field is a generalization of a _[scalar field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar\_field "Scalar field")_ and a _[vector field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector\_field "Vector field")_ that assigns, respectively, a scalar or vector to each point of space. If a tensor A is defined on a vector fields set X(M) over a module M, we call A a tensor field on M.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_field#cite_note-1) A tensor field, in common usage, is often referred to in the shorter form "tensor". For example, the _[Riemann curvature tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann\_curvature\_tensor "Riemann curvature tensor")_ refers a tensor _field_, as it associates a tensor to each point of a [Riemannian manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_manifold "Riemannian manifold"), a [topological space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_space "Topological space").

[![Image 1](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Tensor_field.png/960px-Tensor_field.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Tensor_field.png)

Compared to a scalar field which has 1 value at a given point, and a vector field which has 2 (direction and magnitude), a tensor field has more than 2 values at each point, here represented by an ellipse at each point with semi-major axis length, semi-minor axis length, and direction

Geometric introduction
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tensor_field&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Geometric introduction")]

Intuitively, a vector field is best visualized as an "arrow" attached to each point of a region, with variable length and direction. One example of a vector field on a [curved space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curved_space "Curved space") is a weather map showing horizontal wind velocity at each point of the Earth's surface.

Now consider more complicated fields. For example, if the manifold is Riemannian, then it has a metric field ![Image 34: {\displaystyle g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3556280e66fe2c0d0140df20935a6f057381d77), such that given any two vectors ![Image 35: {\displaystyle v,w}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6425c6e94fa47976601cb44d7564b5d04dcfbfef) at point ![Image 36: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4), their inner product is ![Image 37: {\displaystyle g_{x}(v,w)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/813ee644f6cd5cfc8eadef74e6752300a548d8bf). The field ![Image 38: {\displaystyle g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3556280e66fe2c0d0140df20935a6f057381d77) could be given in matrix form, but it depends on a choice of coordinates. It could instead be given as an ellipsoid of radius 1 at each point, which is coordinate-free. Applied to the Earth's surface, this is [Tissot's indicatrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tissot%27s_indicatrix "Tissot's indicatrix").

In general, we want to specify tensor fields in a coordinate-independent way: It should exist independently of latitude and longitude, or whatever particular "cartographic projection" we are using to introduce numerical coordinates.

Via coordinate transitions
--------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tensor_field&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Via coordinate transitions")]

Following [Schouten (1951)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_field#CITEREFSchouten1951) and [McConnell (1957)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_field#CITEREFMcConnell1957), the concept of a tensor relies on a concept of a reference frame (or [coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_system "Coordinate system")), which may be fixed (relative to some background reference frame), but in general may be allowed to vary within some class of transformations of these coordinate systems.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_field#cite_note-2)

For example, coordinates belonging to the _n_-dimensional [real coordinate space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_coordinate_space "Real coordinate space")![Image 39: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d) may be subjected to arbitrary [affine transformations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_transformation "Affine transformation"):

![Image 40: {\displaystyle x^{k}\mapsto A_{j}^{k}x^{j}+a^{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/010e062eea17bbfa49a6985aacdcee34ffdc8f80)
(with _n_-dimensional indices, [summation implied](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_summation_convention "Einstein summation convention")). A covariant vector, or covector, is a system of functions ![Image 41: {\displaystyle v_{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d142b4083872eb72f81c1e20fd2c91d02b4a9838) that transforms under this affine transformation by the rule

![Image 42: {\displaystyle v_{k}\mapsto v_{i}A_{k}^{i}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5d49c9d0f5605f5de96ce0d4bfbb490f366eed71)
The list of Cartesian coordinate basis vectors ![Image 43: {\displaystyle \mathbf {e} _{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/afc2926ab5c390c48c2abe97d1cc7bf00e972677) transforms as a covector, since under the affine transformation ![Image 44: {\displaystyle \mathbf {e} _{k}\mapsto A_{k}^{i}\mathbf {e} _{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3d4d4b1a532eb33b7eb9e6fe32bd971ae0645ef1). A contravariant vector is a system of functions ![Image 45: {\displaystyle v^{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a5323ed8f5cc12a3bd966f11d3f4d7546b5c8268) of the coordinates that, under such an affine transformation undergoes a transformation

![Image 46: {\displaystyle v^{k}\mapsto (A^{-1})_{j}^{k}v^{j}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7c0d59e9684eadd24ed80a1e8f642d57494fab4d)
This is precisely the requirement needed to ensure that the quantity ![Image 47: {\displaystyle v^{k}\mathbf {e} _{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1659eaba600f244b720c209a51cc0966b1116372) is an invariant object that does not depend on the coordinate system chosen. More generally, the coordinates of a tensor of valence (_p_,_q_) have _p_ upper indices and _q_ lower indices, with the transformation law being

![Image 48: {\displaystyle {T^{i_{1}\cdots i_{p}}}_{j_{1}\cdots j_{q}}\mapsto A_{i'_{1}}^{i_{1}}\cdots A_{i'_{p}}^{i_{p}}{T^{i'_{1}\cdots i'_{p}}}_{j'_{1}\cdots j'_{q}}(A^{-1})_{j_{1}}^{j'_{1}}\cdots (A^{-1})_{j_{q}}^{j'_{q}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8d937ee8e0f88cbd9574a8c52ccc74c37fae33f3)
The concept of a tensor field may be obtained by specializing the allowed coordinate transformations to be [smooth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smooth_function "Smooth function") (or [differentiable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differentiable_function "Differentiable function"), [analytic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytic_function "Analytic function"), etc.). A covector field is a function ![Image 49: {\displaystyle v_{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d142b4083872eb72f81c1e20fd2c91d02b4a9838) of the coordinates that transforms by the [Jacobian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacobian_matrix "Jacobian matrix") of the transition functions (in the given class). Likewise, a contravariant vector field ![Image 50: {\displaystyle v^{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a5323ed8f5cc12a3bd966f11d3f4d7546b5c8268) transforms by the inverse Jacobian.

A tensor bundle is a [fiber bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiber_bundle "Fiber bundle") where the fiber is a tensor product of any number of copies of the [tangent space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_space "Tangent space") and/or [cotangent space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cotangent_space "Cotangent space") of the base space, which is a manifold. As such, the fiber is a [vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space") and the tensor bundle is a special kind of [vector bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_bundle "Vector bundle").

The vector bundle is a natural idea of "vector space depending continuously (or smoothly) on parameters" – the parameters being the points of a manifold _M_. For example, a _vector space of one dimension depending on an angle_ could look like a [Möbius strip](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M%C3%B6bius_strip "Möbius strip") or alternatively like a [cylinder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylinder_(geometry) "Cylinder (geometry)"). Given a vector bundle _V_ over _M_, the corresponding field concept is called a _section_ of the bundle: for _m_ varying over _M_, a choice of vector

_v m_ in _V m_,
where _V m_ is the vector space "at" _m_.

Since the [tensor product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_product "Tensor product") concept is independent of any choice of basis, taking the tensor product of two vector bundles on _M_ is routine. Starting with the [tangent bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_bundle "Tangent bundle") (the bundle of [tangent spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_space "Tangent space")) the whole apparatus explained at [component-free treatment of tensors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Component-free_treatment_of_tensors "Component-free treatment of tensors") carries over in a routine way – again independently of coordinates, as mentioned in the introduction.

We therefore can give a definition of **tensor field**, namely as a [section](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Section_(fiber_bundle) "Section (fiber bundle)") of some [tensor bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_bundle "Tensor bundle"). (There are vector bundles that are not tensor bundles: the Möbius band for instance.) This is then guaranteed geometric content, since everything has been done in an intrinsic way. More precisely, a tensor field assigns to any given point of the manifold a tensor in the space

![Image 51: {\displaystyle V\otimes \cdots \otimes V\otimes V^{*}\otimes \cdots \otimes V^{*},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9b8acf562eadd38731e92d26cd1543fdf8c9c029)
where _V_ is the [tangent space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_space "Tangent space") at that point and _V_∗ is the [cotangent space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cotangent_space "Cotangent space"). See also [tangent bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_bundle "Tangent bundle") and [cotangent bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cotangent_bundle "Cotangent bundle").

Given two tensor bundles _E_ → _M_ and _F_ → _M_, a linear map _A_: Γ(_E_) → Γ(_F_) from the space of sections of _E_ to sections of _F_ can be considered itself as a tensor section of ![Image 52: {\displaystyle \scriptstyle E^{*}\otimes F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a962578c4bb2134f1df2f3a9f17ffda172ef897c) if and only if it satisfies _A_(_fs_) = _fA_(_s_), for each section _s_ in Γ(_E_) and each smooth function _f_ on _M_. Thus a tensor section is not only a linear map on the vector space of sections, but a _C_∞(_M_)-linear map on the [module](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Module_(mathematics) "Module (mathematics)") of sections. This property is used to check, for example, that even though the [Lie derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_derivative "Lie derivative") and [covariant derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covariant_derivative "Covariant derivative") are not tensors, the [torsion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torsion_tensor "Torsion tensor") and [curvature tensors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_connection "Affine connection") built from them are.

The notation for tensor fields can sometimes be confusingly similar to the notation for tensor spaces. Thus, the tangent bundle _TM_ = _T_(_M_) might sometimes be written as

![Image 53: {\displaystyle T_{0}^{1}(M)=T(M)=TM}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1716f0bf2e9b608de2dc1add0008e84481403144)
to emphasize that the tangent bundle is the range space of the (1,0) tensor fields (i.e., vector fields) on the manifold _M_. This should not be confused with the very similar looking notation

![Image 54: {\displaystyle T_{0}^{1}(V)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e12edb0bdfa786843471270e2932b3cefdcaa195);
in the latter case, we just have one tensor space, whereas in the former, we have a tensor space defined for each point in the manifold _M_.

Curly (script) letters are sometimes used to denote the set of [infinitely-differentiable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smooth_function "Smooth function") tensor fields on _M_. Thus,

![Image 55: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {T}}_{n}^{m}(M)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/63e5d0a5eb83c73d4e204fcee51b411968fbc1eb)
are the sections of the (_m_,_n_) tensor bundle on _M_ that are infinitely-differentiable. A tensor field is an element of this set.

Tensor fields as multilinear forms
----------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tensor_field&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Tensor fields as multilinear forms")]

There is another more abstract (but often useful) way of characterizing tensor fields on a manifold _M_, which makes tensor fields into honest tensors (i.e. _single_ multilinear mappings), though of a different type (although this is _not_ usually why one often says "tensor" when one really means "tensor field"). First, we may consider the set of all smooth (_C_∞) vector fields on _M_, ![Image 56: {\displaystyle {\mathfrak {X}}(M):={\mathcal {T}}_{0}^{1}(M)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/16169969df1c40d30cd4d5e9b71285cff2dd3f0b) (see the section on notation above) as a single space – a [module](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Module_(mathematics) "Module (mathematics)") over the [ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ring_(mathematics) "Ring (mathematics)") of smooth functions, _C_∞(_M_), by pointwise scalar multiplication. The notions of multilinearity and tensor products extend easily to the case of modules over any [commutative ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_ring "Commutative ring").

As a motivating example, consider the space ![Image 57: {\displaystyle \Omega ^{1}(M)={\mathcal {T}}_{1}^{0}(M)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f80270e8c59076d114acf6d291fab9b3540e101a) of smooth covector fields ([1-forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_form "Differential form")), also a module over the smooth functions. These act on smooth vector fields to yield smooth functions by pointwise evaluation, namely, given a covector field _ω_ and a vector field _X_, we define

![Image 58: {\displaystyle {\tilde {\omega }}(X)(p):=\omega (p)(X(p)).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5259fe4d292ddd8b072771bb438c82f60b49014d)
Because of the pointwise nature of everything involved, the action of ![Image 59: {\displaystyle {\tilde {\omega }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e60cbeebb31255ddce04c7526d2631bfd49fb8bf) on _X_ is a _C_∞(_M_)-linear map, that is,

![Image 60: {\displaystyle {\tilde {\omega }}(fX)(p)=\omega (p)((fX)(p))=\omega (p)(f(p)X(p))=f(p)\omega (p)(X(p))=(f\omega )(p)(X(p))=(f{\tilde {\omega }})(X)(p)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c1c4e7ebc2d3ecba9d0ee4fed7482ce91427473d)
for any _p_ in _M_ and smooth function _f_. Thus we can regard covector fields not just as sections of the cotangent bundle, but also linear mappings of vector fields into functions. By the double-dual construction, vector fields can similarly be expressed as mappings of covector fields into functions (namely, we could start "natively" with covector fields and work up from there).

In a complete parallel to the construction of ordinary single tensors (not tensor fields!) on _M_ as multilinear maps on vectors and covectors, we can regard general (_k_,_l_) tensor fields on _M_ as _C_∞(_M_)-multilinear maps defined on _k_ copies of ![Image 61: {\displaystyle {\mathfrak {X}}(M)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/98adcdb076cb8069c2d19d06030cf7e2c2f237f3) and _l_ copies of ![Image 62: {\displaystyle \Omega ^{1}(M)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cb6e51da90c4ca5b118c211c7b6c89105e3b7c95) into _C_∞(_M_).

Now, given any arbitrary mapping _T_ from a product of _k_ copies of ![Image 63: {\displaystyle {\mathfrak {X}}(M)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/98adcdb076cb8069c2d19d06030cf7e2c2f237f3) and _l_ copies of ![Image 64: {\displaystyle \Omega ^{1}(M)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cb6e51da90c4ca5b118c211c7b6c89105e3b7c95) into _C_∞(_M_), it turns out that it arises from a tensor field on _M_ if and only if it is multilinear over _C_∞(_M_). Namely _C_∞(_M_)-module of tensor fields of type ![Image 65: {\displaystyle (k,l)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c6596d38bbba96f6eda9c984af791cdd7790a802) over _M_ is canonically isomorphic to _C_∞(_M_)-module of _C_∞(_M_)-[multilinear forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multilinear_form "Multilinear form")

![Image 66: {\displaystyle \underbrace {\Omega ^{1}(M)\times \ldots \times \Omega ^{1}(M)} _{l\ \mathrm {times} }\times \underbrace {{\mathfrak {X}}(M)\times \ldots \times {\mathfrak {X}}(M)} _{k\ \mathrm {times} }\to C^{\infty }(M).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/24d69c0835100689d2aeb07011f2b215e3622f6e)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_field#cite_note-3)
This kind of multilinearity implicitly expresses the fact that we're really dealing with a pointwise-defined object, i.e. a tensor field, as opposed to a function which, even when evaluated at a single point, depends on all the values of vector fields and 1-forms simultaneously.

A frequent example application of this general rule is showing that the [Levi-Civita connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_connection "Levi-Civita connection"), which is a mapping of smooth vector fields ![Image 67: {\displaystyle (X,Y)\mapsto \nabla _{X}Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8c2cfcc75d0612d0510f1335ad8bb4df160f1f55) taking a pair of vector fields to a vector field, does not define a tensor field on _M_. This is because it is only ![Image 68: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc)-linear in _Y_ (in place of full _C_∞(_M_)-linearity, it satisfies the _Leibniz rule,_![Image 69: {\displaystyle \nabla _{X}(fY)=(Xf)Y+f\nabla _{X}Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fca329a81e231f8ac0cdc94a434ac0673a0cfd52))). Nevertheless, it must be stressed that even though it is not a tensor field, it still qualifies as a geometric object with a component-free interpretation.

The curvature tensor is discussed in differential geometry and the [stress–energy tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stress%E2%80%93energy_tensor "Stress–energy tensor") is important in physics, and these two tensors are related by Einstein's theory of [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity").

In [electromagnetism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetism "Electromagnetism"), the electric and magnetic fields are combined into an [electromagnetic tensor field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_tensor "Electromagnetic tensor").

[Differential forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_form "Differential form"), used in defining integration on manifolds, are a type of tensor field.

In [theoretical physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theoretical_physics "Theoretical physics") and other fields, [differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_equation "Differential equation") posed in terms of tensor fields provide a very general way to express relationships that are both geometric in nature (guaranteed by the tensor nature) and conventionally linked to [differential calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_calculus "Differential calculus"). Even to formulate such equations requires a fresh notion, the [covariant derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covariant_derivative "Covariant derivative"). This handles the formulation of variation of a tensor field _along_ a [vector field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_field "Vector field"). The original _absolute differential calculus_ notion, which was later called _[tensor calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor\_calculus "Tensor calculus")_, led to the isolation of the geometric concept of [connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connection_(differential_geometry) "Connection (differential geometry)").

Twisting by a line bundle
-------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tensor_field&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Twisting by a line bundle")]

An extension of the tensor field idea incorporates an extra [line bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_bundle "Line bundle")_L_ on _M_. If _W_ is the tensor product bundle of _V_ with _L_, then _W_ is a bundle of vector spaces of just the same dimension as _V_. This allows one to define the concept of **tensor density**, a 'twisted' type of tensor field. A _tensor density_ is the special case where _L_ is the bundle of _densities on a manifold_, namely the [determinant bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Determinant_bundle "Determinant bundle") of the [cotangent bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cotangent_bundle "Cotangent bundle"). (To be strictly accurate, one should also apply the [absolute value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_value "Absolute value") to the [transition functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology "Topology") – this makes little difference for an [orientable manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orientable_manifold "Orientable manifold").) For a more traditional explanation see the [tensor density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_density "Tensor density") article.

One feature of the bundle of densities (again assuming orientability) _L_ is that _L_ _s_ is well-defined for real number values of _s_; this can be read from the transition functions, which take strictly positive real values. This means for example that we can take a _half-density_, the case where _s_ = ⁠1/2⁠. In general we can take sections of _W_, the tensor product of _V_ with _L_ _s_, and consider **tensor density fields** with weight _s_.

Half-densities are applied in areas such as defining [integral operators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral_operator "Integral operator") on manifolds, and [geometric quantization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_quantization "Geometric quantization").

When _M_ is a [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space") and all the fields are taken to be invariant by [translations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Translation_(geometry) "Translation (geometry)") by the vectors of _M_, we get back to a situation where a tensor field is synonymous with a tensor 'sitting at the origin'. This does no great harm, and is often used in applications. As applied to tensor densities, it _does_ make a difference. The bundle of densities cannot seriously be defined 'at a point'; and therefore a limitation of the contemporary mathematical treatment of tensors is that tensor densities are defined in a roundabout fashion.

Cocycles and chain rules
------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tensor_field&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Cocycles and chain rules")]

As an advanced explanation of the _tensor_ concept, one can interpret the [chain rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chain_rule "Chain rule") in the multivariable case, as applied to coordinate changes, also as the requirement for self-consistent concepts of tensor giving rise to tensor fields.

Abstractly, we can identify the chain rule as a 1-[cocycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cochain_(algebraic_topology) "Cochain (algebraic topology)"). It gives the consistency required to define the tangent bundle in an intrinsic way. The other vector bundles of tensors have comparable cocycles, which come from applying [functorial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functorial "Functorial") properties of tensor constructions to the chain rule itself; this is why they also are intrinsic (read, 'natural') concepts.

What is usually spoken of as the 'classical' approach to tensors tries to read this backwards – and is therefore a heuristic, _post hoc_ approach rather than truly a foundational one. Implicit in defining tensors by how they transform under a coordinate change is the kind of self-consistency the cocycle expresses. The construction of tensor densities is a 'twisting' at the cocycle level. Geometers have not been in any doubt about the _geometric_ nature of tensor _quantities_; this kind of [descent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Descent_(category_theory) "Descent (category theory)") argument justifies abstractly the whole theory.

The concept of a tensor field can be generalized by considering objects that transform differently. An object that transforms as an ordinary tensor field under coordinate transformations, except that it is also multiplied by the determinant of the [Jacobian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacobian_matrix_and_determinant "Jacobian matrix and determinant") of the inverse coordinate transformation to the _w_ th power, is called a tensor density with weight _w_.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_field#cite_note-4) Invariantly, in the language of multilinear algebra, one can think of tensor densities as [multilinear maps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multilinear_map "Multilinear map") taking their values in a [density bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Density_bundle "Density bundle") such as the (1-dimensional) space of _n_-forms (where _n_ is the dimension of the space), as opposed to taking their values in just **R**. Higher "weights" then just correspond to taking additional tensor products with this space in the range.

A special case are the scalar densities. Scalar 1-densities are especially important because it makes sense to define their integral over a manifold. They appear, for instance, in the [Einstein–Hilbert action](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein%E2%80%93Hilbert_action "Einstein–Hilbert action") in general relativity. The most common example of a scalar 1-density is the [volume element](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume_element "Volume element"), which in the presence of a metric tensor _g_ is the square root of its [determinant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Determinant "Determinant") in coordinates, denoted ![Image 70: {\displaystyle {\sqrt {\det g}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1d1c11a4b11cee04bca273c82817fffb72ecaa96). The metric tensor is a covariant tensor of order 2, and so its determinant scales by the square of the coordinate transition:

![Image 71: {\displaystyle \det(g')=\left(\det {\frac {\partial x}{\partial x'}}\right)^{2}\det(g),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9137cf596737b5963c21f27e2e5f2721bbdac061)
which is the transformation law for a scalar density of weight+2.

More generally, any tensor density is the product of an ordinary tensor with a scalar density of the appropriate weight. In the language of [vector bundles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_bundle "Vector bundle"), the determinant bundle of the [tangent bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_bundle "Tangent bundle") is a [line bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_bundle "Line bundle") that can be used to 'twist' other bundles _w_ times. While locally the more general transformation law can indeed be used to recognise these tensors, there is a global question that arises, reflecting that in the transformation law one may write either the Jacobian determinant, or its absolute value. Non-integral powers of the (positive) transition functions of the bundle of densities make sense, so that the weight of a density, in that sense, is not restricted to integer values. Restricting to changes of coordinates with positive Jacobian determinant is possible on [orientable manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orientable_manifold "Orientable manifold"), because there is a consistent global way to eliminate the minus signs; but otherwise the line bundle of densities and the line bundle of _n_-forms are distinct. For more on the intrinsic meaning, see _[Density on a manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Density\_on\_a\_manifold "Density on a manifold")_.
