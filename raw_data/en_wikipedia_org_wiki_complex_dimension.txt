In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), **complex dimension** usually refers to the dimension of a [complex manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_manifold "Complex manifold") or a complex [algebraic variety](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension_of_an_algebraic_variety "Dimension of an algebraic variety").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_dimension#cite_note-1) These are spaces in which the local neighborhoods of points (or of non-singular points in the case of a variety) are modeled on a Cartesian product of the form ![Image 1: {\displaystyle \mathbb {C} ^{d}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dcc165f5f6c2360e365b6693209c45fe805a0781) for some ![Image 2: {\displaystyle d}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e85ff03cbe0c7341af6b982e47e9f90d235c66ab), and the complex dimension is the exponent ![Image 3: {\displaystyle d}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e85ff03cbe0c7341af6b982e47e9f90d235c66ab) in this product. Because ![Image 4: {\displaystyle \mathbb {C} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f9add4085095b9b6d28d045fd9c92c2c09f549a7) can in turn be modeled by ![Image 5: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e150115ab9f63023215109595b76686a1ff890fd), a space with complex dimension ![Image 6: {\displaystyle d}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e85ff03cbe0c7341af6b982e47e9f90d235c66ab) will have **real dimension**![Image 7: {\displaystyle 2d}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d8106478cb4da6af49992eeb3a3b8690d27797ad).[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_dimension#cite_note-2) That is, a [smooth manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smooth_manifold "Smooth manifold") of complex dimension ![Image 8: {\displaystyle d}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e85ff03cbe0c7341af6b982e47e9f90d235c66ab) has real dimension ![Image 9: {\displaystyle 2d}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d8106478cb4da6af49992eeb3a3b8690d27797ad); and a complex algebraic variety of complex dimension ![Image 10: {\displaystyle d}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e85ff03cbe0c7341af6b982e47e9f90d235c66ab), away from any [singular point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Singular_point_of_an_algebraic_variety "Singular point of an algebraic variety"), will also be a smooth manifold of real dimension ![Image 11: {\displaystyle 2d}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d8106478cb4da6af49992eeb3a3b8690d27797ad).

However, for a [real algebraic variety](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_algebraic_variety "Real algebraic variety") (that is a variety defined by equations with real coefficients), its [dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension_of_an_algebraic_variety "Dimension of an algebraic variety") refers commonly to its complex dimension, and its [real dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension_of_an_algebraic_variety#Real_dimension "Dimension of an algebraic variety") refers to the maximum of the dimensions of the manifolds contained in the set of its real points. The real dimension is not greater than the dimension, and equals it if the variety is irreducible and has real points that are [nonsingular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Singular_point_of_an_algebraic_variety "Singular point of an algebraic variety"). For example, the equation ![Image 12: {\displaystyle x^{2}+y^{2}+z^{2}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ef3b5f6877cc182edbe473740d7b52f99703d1f2) defines a variety of (complex) dimension 2 (a surface), but of real dimension 0 — it has only one real point, (0, 0, 0), which is singular.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_dimension#cite_note-3)

The same considerations apply to [codimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Codimension "Codimension"). For example a smooth [complex hypersurface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_hypersurface "Complex hypersurface") in [complex projective space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_projective_space "Complex projective space") of dimension _n_ will be a manifold of dimension 2(_n_ − 1). A complex [hyperplane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperplane "Hyperplane") does not separate a complex projective space into two components, because it has real codimension 2.
