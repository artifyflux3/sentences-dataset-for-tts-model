From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

(Redirected from [Homeomorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Homeomorphic&redirect=no "Homeomorphic"))

Mapping which preserves all topological properties of a given space

For homeomorphisms in graph theory, see [Homeomorphism (graph theory)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homeomorphism_(graph_theory) "Homeomorphism (graph theory)").

Not to be confused with [Homomorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homomorphism "Homomorphism").

"Topological equivalence" redirects here. For the concept in dynamical systems, see [Topological conjugacy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_conjugacy "Topological conjugacy").

[![Image 40](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/26/Mug_and_Torus_morph.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Mug_and_Torus_morph.gif)

An often-repeated [mathematical joke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_joke "Mathematical joke") is that topologists cannot tell the difference between a [coffee mug](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coffee_mug "Coffee mug") and a [donut](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Donut "Donut"),[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homeomorphic#cite_note-1) since a sufficiently pliable donut could be reshaped to the form of a coffee mug by creating a dimple and progressively enlarging it, while preserving the donut hole in the mug's handle. This illustrates that a coffee mug and a donut are homeomorphic.

In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics") and more specifically in [topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology "Topology"), a **homeomorphism** ([from Greek roots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neoclassical_compound "Neoclassical compound") meaning "similar shape", named by [Henri Poincar√©](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Poincar%C3%A9 "Henri Poincar√©")),[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homeomorphic#cite_note-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homeomorphic#cite_note-3) also called **topological isomorphism**, or **bicontinuous function**, is a [bijective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bijective "Bijective") and [continuous function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_function#Continuous_functions_between_topological_spaces "Continuous function") between [topological spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_space "Topological space") that has a continuous [inverse function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverse_function "Inverse function"). Homeomorphisms are the [isomorphisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isomorphism "Isomorphism") in the [category of topological spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category_of_topological_spaces "Category of topological spaces")‚Äîthat is, they are the [mappings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Map_(mathematics) "Map (mathematics)") that preserve all the [topological properties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_property "Topological property") of a given space. Two spaces with a homeomorphism between them are called **homeomorphic**, and from a topological viewpoint they are the same.

Very roughly speaking, a topological space is a [geometric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry") object, and a homeomorphism results from a continuous deformation of the object into a new shape. Thus, a [square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_(geometry) "Square (geometry)") and a [circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle "Circle") are homeomorphic to each other, but a [sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere "Sphere") and a [torus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torus "Torus") are not. However, this description can be misleading. Some continuous deformations do not produce homeomorphisms, such as the deformation of a line into a point. Some homeomorphisms do not result from continuous deformations, such as the homeomorphism between a [trefoil knot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trefoil_knot "Trefoil knot") and a circle. [Homotopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homotopy "Homotopy") and [isotopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homotopy#Isotopy "Homotopy") are precise definitions for the informal concept of _continuous deformation_.

Definition
----------

*   f{\displaystyle f}![Image 43: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) is [continuous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuity_(topology) "Continuity (topology)"),
*   the [inverse function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverse_function "Inverse function")f‚àí1{\displaystyle f^{-1}}![Image 44: {\displaystyle f^{-1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3e5cfa2f5c08d6fe7d046b73faa6e3f213acc802) is continuous (f{\displaystyle f}![Image 45: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) is an [open mapping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_mapping "Open mapping")).

A homeomorphism is sometimes called a _bicontinuous_ function. If such a function exists, X{\displaystyle X}![Image 46: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) and Y{\displaystyle Y}![Image 47: {\displaystyle Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/961d67d6b454b4df2301ac571808a3538b3a6d3f) are **homeomorphic**. A **self-homeomorphism** is a homeomorphism from a topological space onto itself. Being "homeomorphic" is an [equivalence relation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equivalence_relation "Equivalence relation") on topological spaces. Its [equivalence classes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equivalence_class "Equivalence class") are called **homeomorphism classes**.

The third requirement, that f‚àí1{\textstyle f^{-1}}![Image 48: {\textstyle f^{-1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dc0065a95e2fe093d5ca75b7be17c789055516e6) be [continuous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_function "Continuous function"), is essential. The function f‚àí1{\textstyle f^{-1}}![Image 54: {\textstyle f^{-1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dc0065a95e2fe093d5ca75b7be17c789055516e6) is not continuous at the point (1,0),{\textstyle (1,0),}![Image 55: {\textstyle (1,0),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/91b9f7ebf042bd7c60da5e568553173d5e9e1e60) because although f‚àí1{\textstyle f^{-1}}![Image 56: {\textstyle f^{-1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dc0065a95e2fe093d5ca75b7be17c789055516e6) maps (1,0){\textstyle (1,0)}![Image 57: {\textstyle (1,0)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3001244c5e48ca643088e691728c4c9afb731c88) to 0,{\textstyle 0,}![Image 58: {\textstyle 0,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e475772cfb8913b45e99dadfd42cb663b9be4903) any [neighbourhood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neighbourhood_(mathematics) "Neighbourhood (mathematics)") of this point also includes points that the function maps close to 2 œÄ,{\textstyle 2\pi ,}![Image 59: {\textstyle 2\pi ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/61b0f63aa22961a3f4e1cad29d9b0d9e9861c86e) but the points it maps to numbers in between lie outside the neighbourhood.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homeomorphic#cite_note-4)

Homeomorphisms are the [isomorphisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isomorphism "Isomorphism") in the [category of topological spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category_of_topological_spaces "Category of topological spaces"). In some contexts, there are homeomorphic objects that cannot be continuously deformed from one to the other. [Homotopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homotopy "Homotopy") and [isotopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homotopy#Isotopy "Homotopy") are equivalence relations that have been introduced for dealing with such situations.

Properties
----------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Homeomorphism&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Properties")]

*   Two homeomorphic spaces share the same [topological properties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_property "Topological property"). For example, if one of them is [compact](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compact_space "Compact space"), then the other is as well; if one of them is [connected](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connectedness "Connectedness"), then the other is as well; if one of them is [Hausdorff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hausdorff_space "Hausdorff space"), then the other is as well; their [homotopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homotopy "Homotopy") and [homology groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homology_group "Homology group") will coincide. Note however that this does not extend to properties defined via a [metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space "Metric space"); there are metric spaces that are homeomorphic even though one of them is [complete](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Completeness_(topology) "Completeness (topology)") and the other is not.
*   A homeomorphism is simultaneously an [open mapping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_mapping "Open mapping") and a [closed mapping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_mapping "Closed mapping"); that is, it maps [open sets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_set "Open set") to open sets and [closed sets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_set "Closed set") to closed sets.
*   Every self-homeomorphism in S 1{\displaystyle S^{1}}![Image 90: {\displaystyle S^{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/60796c8d0c03cf575637d3202463b214d9635880) can be extended to a self-homeomorphism of the whole disk D 2{\displaystyle D^{2}}![Image 91: {\displaystyle D^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7a1e7d554748b105413b259fdfb8c9587acb9f1d) ([Alexander's trick](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander%27s_trick "Alexander's trick")).

Informal discussion
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Homeomorphism&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Informal discussion")]

The intuitive criterion of stretching, bending, cutting and gluing back together takes a certain amount of practice to apply correctly‚Äîit may not be obvious from the description above that deforming a [line segment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_segment "Line segment") to a point is impermissible, for instance. It is thus important to realize that it is the formal definition given above that counts. In this case, for example, the line segment possesses infinitely many points, and therefore cannot be put into a bijection with a set containing only a finite number of points, including a single point.

This characterization of a homeomorphism often leads to a confusion with the concept of [homotopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homotopy "Homotopy"), which is actually _defined_ as a continuous deformation, but from one _function_ to another, rather than one space to another. In the case of a homeomorphism, envisioning a continuous deformation is a mental tool for keeping track of which points on space _X_ correspond to which points on _Y_‚Äîone just follows them as _X_ deforms. In the case of homotopy, the continuous deformation from one map to the other is of the essence, and it is also less restrictive, since none of the maps involved need to be one-to-one or onto. Homotopy does lead to a relation on spaces: [homotopy equivalence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homotopy_equivalence "Homotopy equivalence").

There is a name for the kind of deformation involved in visualizing a homeomorphism. It is (except when cutting and regluing are required) an [isotopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homotopy "Homotopy") between the [identity map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Identity_function "Identity function") on _X_ and the homeomorphism from _X_ to _Y_.
