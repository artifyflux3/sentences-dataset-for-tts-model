A **cube** is a [three-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_space "Three-dimensional space") solid object in [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry"). A [polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyhedron "Polyhedron"), its eight vertices and twelve straight edges of the same length form six [square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_(geometry) "Square (geometry)") faces of the same size. It is a type of [parallelepiped](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelepiped "Parallelepiped"), with pairs of parallel opposite faces with the [same shape and size](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congruence_(geometry) "Congruence (geometry)"), and is also a [rectangular cuboid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangular_cuboid "Rectangular cuboid") with [right angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_angle "Right angle") between pairs of intersecting faces and pairs of intersecting edges. It is an example of many classes of polyhedra, such as [Platonic solids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platonic_solid "Platonic solid"), [regular polyhedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polyhedron "Regular polyhedron"), [parallelohedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelohedra "Parallelohedra"), [zonohedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zonohedron "Zonohedron"), and [plesiohedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plesiohedron "Plesiohedron"). The [dual polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_polyhedron "Dual polyhedron") of a cube is the [regular octahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_octahedron "Regular octahedron").

The cube can be represented in many ways, such as the cubical graph, which can be constructed by using the [Cartesian product of graphs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_product_of_graphs "Cartesian product of graphs"). The cube is the three-dimensional [hypercube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypercube "Hypercube"), a family of [polytopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polytope "Polytope") also including the two-dimensional square and four-dimensional [tesseract](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tesseract "Tesseract"). A cube with [unit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1 "1") side length is the canonical unit of [volume](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume "Volume") in three-dimensional space, relative to which other solid objects are measured. Other related figures involve the construction of polyhedra, [space-filling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space-filling_polyhedron "Space-filling polyhedron") and [honeycombs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Honeycomb_(geometry) "Honeycomb (geometry)"), and [polycubes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polycube "Polycube"), as well as cubes in compounds, spherical, and topological space.

The cube was discovered in antiquity, and associated with the nature of [earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth_(classical_element) "Earth (classical element)") by [Plato](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plato "Plato"), for whom the Platonic solids are named. It can be derived differently to create more polyhedra, and it has applications to construct a new [polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyhedron "Polyhedron") by attaching others. Other applications are found in toys and games, arts, optical illusions, architectural buildings, natural science, and technology.

A [cuboid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cuboid "Cuboid") is a [polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyhedron "Polyhedron") that consists of six [quadrilateral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral "Quadrilateral") faces. When all of its [interior angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interior_angle "Interior angle") (the angle formed inside) are [right angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_angle "Right angle"), 90°, the faces are transformed into [rectangles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle "Rectangle"), which is known as [rectangular cuboid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangular_cuboid "Rectangular cuboid"). A cube becomes a special case of a rectangular cuboid when all of the edges are equal in length.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-mk-1) Like a rectangular cuboid, every face of a cube has four vertices, each of which connects with three lines of the same length. These edges form [square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square "Square") faces, so the [dihedral angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihedral_angle "Dihedral angle") of a cube—the angle between every two adjacent squares—is the interior angle of a square as well. Hence, the cube has six faces, twelve edges, and eight vertices.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-johnson-2) As for all convex polyhedra, the cube has [Euler characteristic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler_characteristic "Euler characteristic") of 2, according to the formula ![Image 6: {\displaystyle V-E+F=2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/759601e482258ff7a359a7db381abf60372c5b06); the three letters denote respectively the number of vertices, edges, and faces.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-richeson-3)

Every three square faces surrounding a vertex are [orthogonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonality "Orthogonality") to each other, meaning the planes are perpendicular, forming a right angle between two adjacent squares. Hence, the cube is classified as an [orthogonal polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_polyhedron "Orthogonal polyhedron").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-jessen-4) Other special cases for a cube are a [parallelepiped](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelepiped "Parallelepiped")—a polyhedron with six [parallelograms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram "Parallelogram") faces—because its pairs of opposite faces are congruent,[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-calter-5) a [rhombohedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhombohedron "Rhombohedron")—as a special case of a parallelepiped with six [rhombi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhombi "Rhombi") faces—because the interior angle of all of the faces is right,[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHoffmann2020[httpbooksgooglecombooksid16H0DwAAQBAJpgPA83_83]-6) and a [trigonal trapezohedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trigonal_trapezohedron "Trigonal trapezohedron")—a polyhedron with congruent quadrilateral faces—since its square faces are the special cases of rhombi.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-cc-7)

### Measurement and other metric properties

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cube&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Measurement and other metric properties")]

[![Image 7](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Cube_diagonals.svg/250px-Cube_diagonals.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Cube_diagonals.svg)

A face diagonal in red and space diagonal in blue

Given a cube with edge length ![Image 8: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc), the [face diagonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Face_diagonal "Face diagonal") of the cube is the [diagonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diagonal "Diagonal") of a square ![Image 9: {\displaystyle a{\sqrt {2}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/04f5c1e0c6480a9d660cc0e67063dfc3b872654a), and the [space diagonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_diagonal "Space diagonal") of the cube is a line connecting two vertices that is not in the same face, formulated as ![Image 10: {\displaystyle a{\sqrt {3}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c265627f35e18499654722022d0dee669febfe19). Both formulas can be determined by using the [Pythagorean theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagorean_theorem "Pythagorean theorem"). The surface area of a cube ![Image 11: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3) is six times the area of a square:[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-khattar-8)![Image 12: {\displaystyle A=6a^{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f29b63c94a8387e8174bc6bb41042dc2cfa9ec78) The volume of a cuboid is the product of its length, width, and height. Because all the edges of a cube are equal in length, the formula for the volume of a cube is the third power of its side length, leading to the use of the term _[cubic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube\_(algebra) "Cube (algebra)")_ to mean raising any number to the third power:[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-thomson-9)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-khattar-8)![Image 13: {\displaystyle V=a^{3}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/95a4c0cc0c239e1b1b30998a0b475a51d38dd97f)

[![Image 14](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Prince_Ruperts_cube.png/250px-Prince_Ruperts_cube.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Prince_Ruperts_cube.png)

[Prince Rupert's cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prince_Rupert%27s_cube "Prince Rupert's cube")

One special case is the [unit cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_cube "Unit cube"), so named for measuring a single [unit of length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_of_length "Unit of length") along each edge. It follows that each face is a [unit square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_square "Unit square") and that the entire figure has a volume of 1 cubic unit.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-ball-10)[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-hr-w-11)[Prince Rupert of the Rhine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prince_Rupert_of_the_Rhine "Prince Rupert of the Rhine"), known for [Prince Rupert's drop](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prince_Rupert%27s_drop "Prince Rupert's drop"), questioned whether [a cube could pass through a hole cut into the unit cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prince_Rupert%27s_cube "Prince Rupert's cube"). Despite having sides approximately 6% longer, such a cube can pass through a copy of itself of the same size or smaller.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-sriraman-12)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-jwy-13) An ancient problem of [doubling the cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doubling_the_cube "Doubling the cube")—alternatively known as the _Delian problem_—requires the construction of a cube with a volume twice the original by using only a [compass and straightedge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compass_and_straightedge "Compass and straightedge"). This was concluded by French mathematician [Pierre Wantzel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre_Wantzel "Pierre Wantzel") in 1837, proving that it is impossible to implement since a cube with twice the volume of the original—the [cube root](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube_root "Cube root") of 2, ![Image 15: {\displaystyle {\sqrt[{3}]{2}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9ca071ab504481c2bb76081aacb03f5519930710)—is not [constructible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constructible_number "Constructible number").[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-lutzen-14)

The cube has three types of [closed geodesics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_geodesic "Closed geodesic"), or paths on a cube's surface that are locally straight. In other words, they avoid the vertices, follow line segments across the faces that they cross, and form [complementary angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complementary_angle "Complementary angle") on the two incident faces of each edge that they cross. One type lies in a plane parallel to any face of the cube, forming a square, with the length being equal to the perimeter of a face, four times the length of each edge. Another type lies in a plane perpendicular to the long diagonal, forming a regular hexagon; its length is ![Image 16: {\displaystyle 3{\sqrt {2}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2c80b47c8599062032c22694f7c4994d50075b9a) times that of an edge. The third type is a non-planar hexagon.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-fuchs-15)

### Relation to the spheres

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cube&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Relation to the spheres")]

With edge length ![Image 17: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc), the [inscribed sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inscribed_sphere "Inscribed sphere") of a cube is the sphere tangent to the faces of a cube at their [centroids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centroid "Centroid"), with radius ![Image 18: {\textstyle {\frac {1}{2}}a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/29c80af0f6bd462d58cdeef00e005c9ed9d8182e). The [midsphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Midsphere "Midsphere") of a cube is the sphere tangent to the edges of a cube, with radius ![Image 19: {\textstyle {\frac {\sqrt {2}}{2}}a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f3fe7f0acc54574a31863824db814aaa73fb2553). The [circumscribed sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumscribed_sphere "Circumscribed sphere") of a cube is the sphere tangent to the vertices of a cube, with radius ![Image 20: {\textstyle {\frac {\sqrt {3}}{2}}a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b6c587445ca61256c03872fd0a38b5047987e878).[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-radii-16)

For a cube whose circumscribed sphere has radius ![Image 21: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33), and for a given point in its three-dimensional space with distances ![Image 22: {\displaystyle d_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/abe3154db7d4f92fb42dd1f80f52f528c6312e4a) from the cube's eight vertices, it is:[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-poo-sung-17)![Image 23: {\displaystyle {\frac {1}{8}}\sum _{i=1}^{8}d_{i}^{4}+{\frac {16R^{4}}{9}}={\biggl (}{\frac {1}{8}}\sum _{i=1}^{8}d_{i}^{2}+{\frac {2R^{2}}{3}}{\biggr )}^{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/361217a3c17be7ea1e0c1ac25d9516c2ce1c040b)

[![Image 24](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Dual_Cube-Octahedron.svg/250px-Dual_Cube-Octahedron.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Dual_Cube-Octahedron.svg)

The dual polyhedron of a cube is the regular octahedron. Both have [octahedral symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octahedral_symmetry "Octahedral symmetry").

The cube has [octahedral symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octahedral_symmetry "Octahedral symmetry")![Image 25: {\displaystyle \mathrm {O} _{\mathrm {h} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3c0a7669167dbe00f64525845a9c7bff1b39ef9) of order 48. In other words, the cube has 48 [isometries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isometry "Isometry"), transformations in order to maintain the symmetrical appearance. These transformations include nine [reflection symmetries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflection_symmetries "Reflection symmetries") (where two halves cut by a plane are identical): five cut the cube from the midpoints of its edges, and four cut diagonally. It is also include octahedral [rotational symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_symmetry "Rotational symmetry") (whereby rotation around the axis results in an identical appearance): three axes pass through the centroids of the cube's opposite faces, six through the midpoints of the cube's opposite edges, and four through the cube's opposite vertices; these axes are respectively four-fold rotational symmetry (0°, 90°, 180°, and 270°), two-fold rotational symmetry (0° and 180°), and three-fold rotational symmetry (0°, 120°, and 240°).[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-french-18)[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-cromwell-19)[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-cp-20)[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-kane-21)

The [dual polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_polyhedron "Dual polyhedron") can be obtained from each of the polyhedra's vertices tangent to a plane by a process known as [polar reciprocation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_reciprocation "Polar reciprocation").[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-cr-22) One property of dual polyhedra is that the polyhedron and its dual share their [three-dimensional symmetry point group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_groups_in_three_dimensions "Point groups in three dimensions"). In this case, the dual polyhedron of a cube is the [regular octahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_octahedron "Regular octahedron"), and both of these polyhedra have the same octahedral symmetry.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-erickson-23)

The cube is [face-transitive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Face-transitive "Face-transitive"), meaning its two squares are alike and can be mapped by rotation and reflection.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-mclean-24) It is [vertex-transitive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex-transitive "Vertex-transitive"), meaning all of its vertices are equivalent and can be mapped [isometrically](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isometry "Isometry") under its symmetry.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-grunbaum-1997-25) It is also [edge-transitive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edge-transitive "Edge-transitive"), meaning the same kind of faces surround each of its vertices in the same or reverse order, and each pair of adjacent faces has the same dihedral angle. Therefore, the cube is a [regular polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polyhedron "Regular polyhedron").[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-senechal-26) Each vertex is surrounded by three squares, so the cube is ![Image 26: {\displaystyle 4.4.4}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ca545ed1e391d4ad76a923393555f70660fb06c3) by [vertex configuration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_configuration "Vertex configuration") or ![Image 27: {\displaystyle \{4,3\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f119d7ae6f9f443d28af07cc9a03657bea37dd05) in a [Schläfli symbol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schl%C3%A4fli_symbol "Schläfli symbol").[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-wd-27)

Cubes are also found in natural science and technology. It is applied to the [unit cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_cell "Unit cell") of a crystal known as a [cubic crystal system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cubic_crystal_system "Cubic crystal system").[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-tisza-37)[Table salt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Table_salt "Table salt") is an example of a [mineral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mineral "Mineral") with a commonly cubic shape.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-chieh-38)[Pyrite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrite "Pyrite") has the same shape as well, although there are many variations.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-hoffmann-39) The [radiolarian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiolarian "Radiolarian")_Lithocubus geometricus_, discovered by [Ernst Haeckel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ernst_Haeckel "Ernst Haeckel"), has a cubic shape.[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-haeckel-40) A historical attempt to unify three physics ideas of [relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galilean_relativity "Galilean relativity"), [gravitation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitation "Gravitation"), and [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics") used the framework of a cube known as a [_cGh_ cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CGh_physics "CGh physics").[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-padmanabhan-41)[Cubane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cubane "Cubane") is a synthetic [hydrocarbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrocarbon "Hydrocarbon") consisting of eight carbon [atoms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atom "Atom") arranged at the corners of a cube, with one [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen") atom attached to each carbon atom.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-biegasiewicz-42)

Other technological cubes include the spacecraft device [CubeSat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CubeSat "CubeSat"),[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-helvajian-43) and [thermal radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermal_radiation "Thermal radiation") demonstration device [Leslie cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leslie_cube "Leslie cube").[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-vm-44) Cubical grids are usual in three-dimensional [Cartesian coordinate systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system "Cartesian coordinate system").[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-knstv-45) In [computer graphics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_graphics "Computer graphics"), [an algorithm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marching_cubes "Marching cubes") divides the input volume into a discrete set of cubes known as the unit on [isosurface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isosurface "Isosurface"),[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-cmsi-46) and the faces of a cube can be used for [mapping a shape](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube_mapping "Cube mapping").[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-greene-47)

Sketch of a cube by Johannes Kepler

[![Image 32](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Mysterium_Cosmographicum_solar_system_model.jpg/250px-Mysterium_Cosmographicum_solar_system_model.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Mysterium_Cosmographicum_solar_system_model.jpg)

The [Platonic solids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platonic_solid "Platonic solid") are five polyhedra known since antiquity. The set is named for [Plato](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plato "Plato") who, in his dialogue [_Timaeus_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timaeus_(dialogue) "Timaeus (dialogue)"), attributed these solids to nature. One of them, the cube, represented the [classical element](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_element "Classical element") of [earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth_(classical_element) "Earth (classical element)") because of its stability.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-FOOTNOTECromwell1997[httpsarchiveorgdetailspolyhedra0000crompage55_55]-48)[Euclid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid "Euclid")'s [_Elements_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid%27s_Elements "Euclid's Elements") defined the Platonic solids, including the cube, and showed how to find the ratio of the circumscribed sphere's diameter to the edge length.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-heath-49) Following Plato's use of the regular polyhedra as symbols of nature, [Johannes Kepler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johannes_Kepler "Johannes Kepler") in his _[Harmonices Mundi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harmonices\_Mundi "Harmonices Mundi")_ sketched each of the Platonic solids; he decorated the cube's side with a tree.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-FOOTNOTECromwell1997[httpsarchiveorgdetailspolyhedra0000crompage55_55]-48) In his _[Mysterium Cosmographicum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mysterium\_Cosmographicum "Mysterium Cosmographicum")_, Kepler also proposed that the ratios between sizes of the orbits of the planets are the ratios between the sizes of the [inscribed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inscribed_sphere "Inscribed sphere") and [circumscribed spheres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumscribed_sphere "Circumscribed sphere") of the Platonic solids. That is, if the orbits are great circles on spheres, the sphere of Mercury is tangent to a [regular octahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_octahedron "Regular octahedron"), whose vertices lie on the sphere of Venus, which is in turn tangent to a [regular icosahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron "Regular icosahedron"), within the sphere of Earth, within a [regular dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron "Regular dodecahedron"), within the sphere of Mars, within a [regular tetrahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_tetrahedron "Regular tetrahedron"), within the sphere of Jupiter, within a cube, within the sphere of Saturn. In fact, the orbits are not circles but ellipses (as Kepler himself later showed), and these relations are only approximate.[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-livio-50)

[![Image 33](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/The_11_cubic_nets.svg/250px-The_11_cubic_nets.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:The_11_cubic_nets.svg)

The eleven nets of a cube

An elementary way to construct a cube is using its [net](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Net_(polyhedron) "Net (polyhedron)"), an arrangement of edge-joined polygons, by connecting the free edges of those polygons. Eleven nets for the cube are possible.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-jeon-51)[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-turney-52)

In [analytic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytic_geometry "Analytic geometry"), a cube may be constructed using the [Cartesian coordinate systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_systems "Cartesian coordinate systems"). For a cube centered at the origin, with edges parallel to the axes and with an edge length of 2, the [Cartesian coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinates "Cartesian coordinates") of the vertices are ![Image 34: {\displaystyle (\pm 1,\pm 1,\pm 1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6152764ebd90dc441dfb5e681af0e9c45c41067c).[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-smith-53) Its interior consists of all points ![Image 35: {\displaystyle (x_{0},x_{1},x_{2})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eba969259566b79a2af66826e29ccf36732ff1f8) with ![Image 36: {\displaystyle -1<x_{i}<1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/73df5ce0b50971fd4aad2a51c1a0463fb8c05f00) for all ![Image 37: {\displaystyle i}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/add78d8608ad86e54951b8c8bd6c8d8416533d20). A cube's surface with center ![Image 38: {\displaystyle (x_{0},y_{0},z_{0})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/39177ddeeb9f9a393b664e522bc8e3bf0face153) and edge length of ![Image 39: {\displaystyle 2a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d325c24be7d760207674a169b078892bdd5cbc76) is the [locus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Locus_(mathematics) "Locus (mathematics)") of all points ![Image 40: {\displaystyle (x,y,z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/22a8c93372e8f8b6e24d523bd5545aed3430baf4) such that ![Image 41: {\displaystyle \max\{|x-x_{0}|,|y-y_{0}|,|z-z_{0}|\}=a.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/acf86b1adcc2f8ca8cadcea5041c795d416c26d8)

The cube is a [Hanner polytope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hanner_polytope "Hanner polytope"), because it can be constructed by using the [Cartesian product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_product "Cartesian product") of three line segments. Its dual polyhedron, the regular octahedron, is constructed by the [direct sum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direct_sum "Direct sum") of three line segments.[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-kozachok-54)

The graph of a cube

According to [Steinitz's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steinitz%27s_theorem "Steinitz's theorem"), a [graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_(discrete_mathematics) "Graph (discrete mathematics)") can be represented as the [vertex-edge graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_of_a_polytope "Graph of a polytope") of a polyhedron. Such a graph has two properties: [planar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planar_graph "Planar graph") (the edges of a graph are connected to every vertex without crossing other edges), and [3-connected](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K-vertex-connected_graph "K-vertex-connected graph") (whenever a graph with more than three vertices, and two of the vertices are removed, the edges remain connected).[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-grunbaum-2003-55)[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-ziegler-56) The skeleton of a cube, represented as the graph, is called the **cubical graph**, a [Platonic graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platonic_graph "Platonic graph"). It has the same number of vertices and edges as the cube, twelve vertices and eight edges.[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-rudolph-57) The cubical graph is also classified as a [prism graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prism_graph "Prism graph"), resembling the skeleton of a cuboid.[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-ps-58)

The cubical graph is a special case of [hypercube graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypercube_graph "Hypercube graph") or ![Image 43: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)-cube—denoted as ![Image 44: {\displaystyle Q_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/503d0af3998f76cd4eaf8b3cc5e8834e254cb71b)—because it can be constructed by using the [Cartesian product of graphs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_product_of_graphs "Cartesian product of graphs"): two graphs connecting the pair of vertices with an edge to form a new graph.[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-hh-59) In the case of the cubical graph, it is the product of ![Image 45: {\displaystyle Q_{2}\mathbin {\Box } Q_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2d935c829704d643fa660094dcc9bd56d8ecebc5). In other words, the cubical graph is constructed by connecting each vertex of two squares with an edge. Notationally, the cubical graph is ![Image 46: {\displaystyle Q_{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f7b6c2a91a49263a333768fc2ebebdc379ddf5d1).[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-cz-60) Like any hypercube graph, it has a [cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cycle_(graph_theory) "Cycle (graph theory)") which visits [every vertex exactly once](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamiltonian_path "Hamiltonian path"),[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-ly-61) and it is also an example of a [unit distance graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_distance_graph "Unit distance graph").[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-hp-62)

The cubical graph is [bipartite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bipartite_graph "Bipartite graph"), meaning every [independent set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Independent_set_(graph_theory) "Independent set (graph theory)") of four vertices can be [disjoint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disjoint_set "Disjoint set") and the edges connected in those sets.[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-berman-graph-63) However, every vertex in one set cannot connect all vertices in the second, so this bipartite graph is not [complete](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complete_bipartite_graph "Complete bipartite graph").[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-aw-64) It is an example of both a [crown graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crown_graph "Crown graph") and a [bipartite Kneser graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bipartite_Kneser_graph "Bipartite Kneser graph").[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-kl-65)[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-berman-graph-63)

### In orthogonal projection

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cube&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: In orthogonal projection")]

An object illuminated by parallel rays of light casts a shadow on a plane perpendicular to those rays, called an [orthogonal projection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthographic_projection "Orthographic projection"). A polyhedron is considered [equiprojective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equiprojective_polyhedra "Equiprojective polyhedra") if, for some position of the light, its orthogonal projection is a regular polygon. The cube is equiprojective because, if the light is parallel to one of the four lines joining a vertex to the opposite vertex, its projection is a [regular hexagon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_hexagon "Regular hexagon").[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-hhlnqr-66)

### As a configuration matrix

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cube&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: As a configuration matrix")]

The cube can be represented as a [configuration matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platonic_solid#As_a_configuration "Platonic solid"), a [matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_(mathematics) "Matrix (mathematics)") in which the rows and columns correspond to the elements of a polyhedron as the vertices, edges, and faces. The [diagonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Main_diagonal "Main diagonal") of a matrix denotes the number of each element that appears in a polyhedron, whereas the non-diagonal of a matrix denotes the number of the column's elements that occur in or at the row's element. The cube's eight vertices, twelve edges, and six faces are denoted by each element in a matrix's diagonal (8, 12, and 6). The first column of the middle row indicates that there are two vertices on each edge, denoted as 2; the middle column of the first row indicates that three edges meet at each vertex, denoted as 3. ### Construction of polyhedra

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cube&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Construction of polyhedra")]

[![Image 48](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/CubeAndStel.svg/157px-CubeAndStel.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:CubeAndStel.svg)

[![Image 49](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Tetrakishexahedron.jpg/250px-Tetrakishexahedron.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Tetrakishexahedron.jpg)

The cube can appear in the construction of a polyhedron, and some of its types can be derived differently in the following:

*   When [faceting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Faceting "Faceting") a cube, meaning removing part of the polygonal faces without creating new vertices of a cube, the resulting polyhedron is the [stellated octahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellated_octahedron "Stellated octahedron").[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-inchbald-68)
*   The cube is a [non-composite polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-composite_polyhedron "Non-composite polyhedron"), meaning it is a convex polyhedron that cannot be separated into two or more regular polyhedra. The cube can be applied to construct a new convex polyhedron by attaching another.[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-timofeenko-2010-69) Attaching a [square pyramid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_pyramid "Square pyramid") to each square face of a cube produces its [Kleetope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kleetope "Kleetope"), a polyhedron known as the [tetrakis hexahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrakis_hexahedron "Tetrakis hexahedron").[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-sod-70) If one and two equilateral square pyramids are attached to the square faces, the [elongated square pyramid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elongated_square_pyramid "Elongated square pyramid") and [elongated square bipyramid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elongated_square_bipyramid "Elongated square bipyramid") respectively, the [Johnson solid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johnson_solid "Johnson solid")'s examples, are constructed.[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-rajwade-71)
*   Each of the cube's vertices can be [truncated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Truncation_(geometry) "Truncation (geometry)"), and the resulting polyhedron is the [Archimedean solid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archimedean_solid "Archimedean solid"), the [truncated cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Truncated_cube "Truncated cube").[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-FOOTNOTECromwell1997[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidOJowej1QWpoCpgPA81_81%E2%80%9382]-72) When its edges are truncated, it is a [rhombicuboctahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhombicuboctahedron "Rhombicuboctahedron").[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-linti-73) Relatedly, the rhombicuboctahedron can also be constructed by separating the cube's faces and then spreading away, after which adding other triangular and square faces between them; this is known as the "expanded cube". Similarly, it is constructed by the cube's dual, the regular octahedron.[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-vxac-74)
*   The [barycentric subdivision](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barycentric_subdivision "Barycentric subdivision") of a cube (or its dual, the regular octahedron) is the [disdyakis dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disdyakis_dodecahedron "Disdyakis dodecahedron"), a [Catalan solid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catalan_solid "Catalan solid").[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-ls-75)
*   The corner region of a cube can also be truncated by a plane (e.g., spanned by the three neighboring vertices), resulting in a [trirectangular tetrahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trirectangular_tetrahedron "Trirectangular tetrahedron").[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-FOOTNOTECoxeter1973[httpbooksgooglecombooksid2ee7AQAAQBAJpgPA71_71]-76)
*   The [snub cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snub_cube "Snub cube") is an Archimedean solid that can be constructed by separating the cube's faces, and filling the gaps with twisted angle equilateral triangles, a process known as a [snub](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snub_(geometry) "Snub (geometry)").[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-holme-77)

The cube can be constructed with six [square pyramids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_pyramid "Square pyramid"), tiling space by attaching their apices. In some cases, this produces the [rhombic dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhombic_dodecahedron "Rhombic dodecahedron") circumscribing a cube.[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-barnes-78)[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-cundy-79)

[![Image 50](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Net_of_tesseract.gif/250px-Net_of_tesseract.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Net_of_tesseract.gif)

[Dali cross](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dali_cross "Dali cross"), the net of a [tesseract](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tesseract "Tesseract")

The [polycube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polycube "Polycube") is a polyhedron in which the faces of many cubes are attached. Analogously, it can be interpreted as the [polyominoes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyominoes "Polyominoes") in three-dimensional space.[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-lunnon-80) When four cubes are stacked vertically, and the other four are attached to the second-from-top cube of the stack, the resulting polycube is the [Dali cross](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dali_cross "Dali cross"), named after [Salvador Dali](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salvador_Dali "Salvador Dali"). The Dali cross is a tile space polyhedron,[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-hut-81)[[82]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-pucc-82) which can be represented as the net of a [tesseract](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tesseract "Tesseract"). A tesseract is a cube's analogous [four-dimensional space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Four-dimensional_space "Four-dimensional space") bounded by twenty-four squares and eight cubes. Both cube and tesseract are known for three-dimensional and four-dimensional [hypercube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypercube "Hypercube"), respectively.[[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-hall-83)

### Space-filling and honeycombs

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cube&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Space-filling and honeycombs")]

[Hilbert's third problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert%27s_third_problem "Hilbert's third problem") asks whether every two equal-volume polyhedra can always be dissected into polyhedral pieces and reassembled into each other. If yes, then the volume of any polyhedron could be defined axiomatically as the volume of an equivalent cube into which it could be reassembled. [Max Dehn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Max_Dehn "Max Dehn") solved this problem by inventing the [Dehn invariant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dehn_invariant "Dehn invariant"), answering that not all polyhedra can be reassembled into a cube.[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-gruber-84) It showed that two equal volume polyhedra should have the same Dehn invariant, except for the two tetrahedra whose Dehn invariants were different.[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-zeeman-85)

[![Image 51](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Partial_cubic_honeycomb.png/250px-Partial_cubic_honeycomb.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Partial_cubic_honeycomb.png)

[Cubic honeycomb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cubic_honeycomb "Cubic honeycomb")

The cube has a Dehn invariant of zero, meaning that cubes can achieve a [honeycomb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Honeycomb_(geometry) "Honeycomb (geometry)"). It is also a [space-filling tile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space-filling_polyhedron "Space-filling polyhedron") in three-dimensional space in which the construction begins by attaching a polyhedron onto its faces without leaving a gap.[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-lm-86) The cube is a [plesiohedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plesiohedron "Plesiohedron"), a special kind of space-filling polyhedron that can be defined as the [Voronoi cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voronoi_cell "Voronoi cell") of a symmetric [Delone set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Delone_set "Delone set").[[87]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-erdahl-87) The plesiohedra include the [parallelohedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelohedra "Parallelohedra"), which can be [translated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Translation_(geometry) "Translation (geometry)") without rotating to fill a space in which each face of any of its copies is attached to a like face of another copy. There are five kinds of parallelohedra, one of which is the cuboid.[[88]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-alexandrov-88) Every three-dimensional parallelohedron is a [zonohedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zonohedron "Zonohedron"), a [centrally symmetric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centrally_symmetric "Centrally symmetric") polyhedron whose faces are [centrally symmetric polygons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zonogon "Zonogon").[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-shephard-89) In the case of the cube, it can be represented as a [cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_(geometry) "Cell (geometry)"). Some honeycombs have cubes as the only cells; one example is the [cubic honeycomb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cubic_honeycomb "Cubic honeycomb"), the only regular honeycomb in Euclidean three-dimensional space, which has four cubes around each edge.[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-twelveessay-90)[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-ns-91)

The [polyhedral compounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyhedral_compound "Polyhedral compound"), in which the cubes share the same centre, are [uniform polyhedron compounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uniform_polyhedron_compound "Uniform polyhedron compound"), meaning they are polyhedral compounds whose constituents are identical (although possibly [enantiomorphous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enantiomorphous "Enantiomorphous")) [uniform polyhedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uniform_polyhedra "Uniform polyhedra"), in an arrangement that is also uniform. Respectively, the list of compounds enumerated by [Skilling (1976)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#CITEREFSkilling1976) in the seventh to ninth uniform compounds for the compound of six cubes with rotational freedom, [three cubes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compound_of_three_cubes "Compound of three cubes"), and [five cubes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compound_of_five_cubes "Compound of five cubes").[[92]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-skilling-92) Two compounds, consisting of two and three cubes were found in [Escher](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M._C._Escher "M. C. Escher")'s [wood engraving](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wood_engraving "Wood engraving") print [_Stars_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stars_(M._C._Escher) "Stars (M. C. Escher)") and [Max Brückner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Max_Br%C3%BCckner "Max Brückner")'s book _Vielecke und Vielflache_.[[93]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-hart-93)

Spherical cube

[![Image 56](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/3-Manifold_3-Torus.png/250px-3-Manifold_3-Torus.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:3-Manifold_3-Torus.png)

The spherical cube represents the [spherical polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_polyhedron "Spherical polyhedron"), which can be modeled by the [arc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arc_(geometry) "Arc (geometry)") of [great circles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_circle "Great circle"), creating bounds as the edges of a [spherical square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_polygon "Spherical polygon").[[94]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-yackel-94) Hence, the spherical cube consists of six spherical squares with 120° interior angles on each vertex. It has [vector equilibrium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_equilibrium "Vector equilibrium"), meaning that the distance from the centroid and each vertex is the same as the distance from that and each edge.[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-popko-95)[[96]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-fuller-96) Its dual is the [spherical octahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_octahedron "Spherical octahedron").[[94]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-yackel-94)

The topological object [three-dimensional torus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3-torus "3-torus") is a topological space defined to be [homeomorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homeomorphic "Homeomorphic") to the Cartesian product of three circles. It can be represented as a three-dimensional model of the cube shape.[[97]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube#cite_note-marar-97)

*   [Bhargava cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bhargava_cube "Bhargava cube"), a configuration to study the law of [binary quadratic form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_quadratic_form "Binary quadratic form") and other such forms, of which the cube's vertices represent the [integer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer").
*   [Chazelle polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chazelle_polyhedron "Chazelle polyhedron"), a notched opposite faces of a cube.
*   [Cubism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cubism "Cubism"), an [art movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Art_movement "Art movement") of revolutionized painting and the visual arts.
*   [Hemicube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hemicube_(geometry) "Hemicube (geometry)"), an abstract polyhedron produced by identifying opposite faces of a cube
*   [Schläfli double six](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schl%C3%A4fli_double_six "Schläfli double six"), a configuration of 30 points and 12 lines in three-dimensional Euclidean space
*   [Squaring the square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Squaring_the_square "Squaring the square")'s three-dimensional analogue, [cubing the cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cubing_the_cube "Cubing the cube")
*   [Superellipsoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superellipsoid "Superellipsoid"), a solid whose horizontal sections are of the same squareness
