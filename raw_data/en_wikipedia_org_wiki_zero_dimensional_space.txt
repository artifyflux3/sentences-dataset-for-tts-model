In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), a **zero-dimensional topological space** (or **nildimensional space**) is a [topological space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_space "Topological space") that has dimension zero with respect to one of several inequivalent notions of assigning a [dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension "Dimension") to a given topological space.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero-dimensional_space#cite_note-1) A graphical illustration of a zero-dimensional space is a [point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_(geometry) "Point (geometry)").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero-dimensional_space#cite_note-2)

Definition
----------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Zero-dimensional_space&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Definition")]

Specifically:

*   A topological space is zero-dimensional with respect to the [Lebesgue covering dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebesgue_covering_dimension "Lebesgue covering dimension") if every [open cover](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_cover "Open cover") of the space has a [refinement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Refinement_(topology) "Refinement (topology)") that is a cover by disjoint open sets.
*   A topological space is zero-dimensional with respect to the finite-to-finite covering dimension if every finite open cover of the space has a refinement that is a finite open cover such that any point in the space is contained in exactly one open set of this refinement.
*   A topological space is zero-dimensional with respect to the [small inductive dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Small_inductive_dimension "Small inductive dimension") if it has a [base](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Base_(topology) "Base (topology)") consisting of [clopen sets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clopen_set "Clopen set").

The three notions above agree for [separable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separable_space "Separable space"), [metrisable spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metrisable_space "Metrisable space").[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_][_[clarification needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Please\_clarify "Wikipedia:Please clarify")_]

Properties of spaces with small inductive dimension zero
--------------------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Zero-dimensional_space&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Properties of spaces with small inductive dimension zero")]

*   A zero-dimensional [Hausdorff space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hausdorff_space "Hausdorff space") is necessarily [totally disconnected](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Totally_disconnected "Totally disconnected"), but the converse fails. However, a [locally compact](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Locally_compact "Locally compact") Hausdorff space is zero-dimensional if and only if it is totally disconnected. (See ([Arhangel'skii & Tkachenko 2008](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero-dimensional_space#CITEREFArhangel'skiiTkachenko2008), Proposition 3.1.7, p.136) for the non-trivial direction.)
*   Zero-dimensional [Polish spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polish_space "Polish space") are a particularly convenient setting for [descriptive set theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Descriptive_set_theory "Descriptive set theory"). Examples of such spaces include the [Cantor space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cantor_space "Cantor space") and [Baire space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baire_space_(set_theory) "Baire space (set theory)").
*   Hausdorff zero-dimensional spaces are precisely the [subspaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subspace_topology "Subspace topology") of topological [powers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_set "Power set")2 I{\displaystyle 2^{I}}![Image 5: {\displaystyle 2^{I}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4714005ae9e8fe65f1fa43660de05edd2dd172b7) where 2={0,1}{\displaystyle 2=\{0,1\}}![Image 6: {\displaystyle 2=\{0,1\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dfe9491853a6dbf61328b21d83141dc74c48e72b) is given the [discrete topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discrete_topology "Discrete topology"). Such a space is sometimes called a [Cantor cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cantor_cube "Cantor cube"). If I is [countably infinite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Countable_set "Countable set"), 2 I{\displaystyle 2^{I}}![Image 7: {\displaystyle 2^{I}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4714005ae9e8fe65f1fa43660de05edd2dd172b7) is the Cantor space.

Manifolds
---------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Zero-dimensional_space&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Manifolds")]

All points of a zero-dimensional [manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manifold "Manifold") are [isolated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isolated_point "Isolated point").
