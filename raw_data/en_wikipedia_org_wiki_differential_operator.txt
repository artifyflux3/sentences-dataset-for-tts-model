A harmonic function defined on an [annulus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Annulus_(mathematics) "Annulus (mathematics)"). Harmonic functions are exactly those functions which lie in the [kernel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kernel_(linear_algebra) "Kernel (linear algebra)") of the [Laplace operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laplace_operator "Laplace operator"), an important differential operator.

In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), a **differential operator** is an [operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operator_(mathematics) "Operator (mathematics)") defined as a function of the [differentiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative "Derivative") operator. It is helpful, as a matter of notation first, to consider differentiation as an abstract operation that accepts a [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)") and returns another function (in the style of a [higher-order function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Higher-order_function "Higher-order function") in [computer science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_science "Computer science")).

This article considers mainly [linear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_map "Linear map") differential operators, which are the most common type. However, non-linear differential operators also exist, such as the [Schwarzian derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schwarzian_derivative "Schwarzian derivative").

Given a nonnegative integer _m_, an order-![Image 2: {\displaystyle m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a07d98bb302f3856cbabc47b2b9016692e3f7bc) linear differential operator is a map ![Image 3: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a) from a [function space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_space "Function space")![Image 4: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {F}}_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d392bc6fb93f473201227731129df52c55d996e1) on ![Image 5: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d) to another function space ![Image 6: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {F}}_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e50876b20378d232fb8274ed5c8e46c51bd56125) that can be written as:

![Image 7: {\displaystyle P=\sum _{|\alpha |\leq m}a_{\alpha }(x)D^{\alpha }\ ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/213b6920b7621dad2732a8e18d030cc1457ac7b3) where ![Image 8: {\displaystyle \alpha =(\alpha _{1},\alpha _{2},\cdots ,\alpha _{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5b7eaa5690b7240ce4f491ec0efdf869f55c58f2) is a [multi-index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multi-index "Multi-index") of non-negative [integers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer"), ![Image 9: {\displaystyle |\alpha |=\alpha _{1}+\alpha _{2}+\cdots +\alpha _{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7832f93f42cce41671070ecf5a4135255fb4c93a), and for each ![Image 10: {\displaystyle \alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b79333175c8b3f0840bfb4ec41b8072c83ea88d3), ![Image 11: {\displaystyle a_{\alpha }(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e0a5742014780b2a27bad01cb4285cd6e40cd9d3) is a function on some open domain in _n_-dimensional space. The operator ![Image 12: {\displaystyle D^{\alpha }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/346ffaf97bb2e947e9dfcb633d99001b4b191254) is interpreted as

![Image 13: {\displaystyle D^{\alpha }={\frac {\partial ^{|\alpha |}}{\partial x_{1}^{\alpha _{1}}\partial x_{2}^{\alpha _{2}}\cdots \partial x_{n}^{\alpha _{n}}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/241e6ad37132e101dc2fad5a45ddb3d5c0245b1f)

Thus for a function ![Image 14: {\displaystyle f\in {\mathcal {F}}_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5c71add55fc33178daabfa5890272444fe7af4ae):

![Image 15: {\displaystyle Pf=\sum _{|\alpha |\leq m}a_{\alpha }(x){\frac {\partial ^{|\alpha |}f}{\partial x_{1}^{\alpha _{1}}\partial x_{2}^{\alpha _{2}}\cdots \partial x_{n}^{\alpha _{n}}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d90c9f95624b3cefdb31291ffde6b2d0ad13d201)

The notation ![Image 16: {\displaystyle D^{\alpha }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/346ffaf97bb2e947e9dfcb633d99001b4b191254) is justified (i.e., independent of order of differentiation) because of the [symmetry of second derivatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry_of_second_derivatives "Symmetry of second derivatives").

The polynomial _p_ obtained by replacing partials ![Image 17: {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial }{\partial x_{i}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1b5cefd7e4d03f0131d6a3c63eead46ffd3e621f) by variables ![Image 18: {\displaystyle \xi _{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4cf3bf8407299b66e55ad17ab166e08a93d3466c) in _P_ is called the **total symbol** of _P_; i.e., the total symbol of _P_ above is: ![Image 19: {\displaystyle p(x,\xi )=\sum _{|\alpha |\leq m}a_{\alpha }(x)\xi ^{\alpha }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bd21487ac7f05614c6f8eaab17dfa87310536678) where ![Image 20: {\displaystyle \xi ^{\alpha }=\xi _{1}^{\alpha _{1}}\cdots \xi _{n}^{\alpha _{n}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6c273f964bec8dfbc0d79898b7cfb7fb908cc898) The highest homogeneous component of the symbol, namely,

![Image 21: {\displaystyle \sigma (x,\xi )=\sum _{|\alpha |=m}a_{\alpha }(x)\xi ^{\alpha }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b07777b844aed57e583ccdb98e050e9edf97e31e)
is called the **principal symbol** of _P_.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_operator#cite_note-FOOTNOTEH%C3%B6rmander1983151-1) While the total symbol is not intrinsically defined, the principal symbol is intrinsically defined (i.e., it is a function on the cotangent bundle).[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_operator#cite_note-2)

More generally, let _E_ and _F_ be [vector bundles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_bundle "Vector bundle") over a manifold _X_. Then the linear operator

![Image 22: {\displaystyle P:C^{\infty }(E)\to C^{\infty }(F)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/024654d97ca2f767e4a84e528856d54da2a7b94c)
is a differential operator of order ![Image 23: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40) if, in [local coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_coordinates "Local coordinates") on _X_, we have

![Image 24: {\displaystyle Pu(x)=\sum _{|\alpha |=k}P^{\alpha }(x){\frac {\partial ^{\alpha }u}{\partial x^{\alpha }}}+{\text{lower-order terms}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/322a443af9ae479186482c1b06f22a206fadc0f3)
where, for each [multi-index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multi-index "Multi-index") α, ![Image 25: {\displaystyle P^{\alpha }(x):E\to F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dbe3c327e25760023f7804cf4b1f95608e085086) is a [bundle map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bundle_map "Bundle map"), symmetric on the indices α.

The _k_ th order coefficients of _P_ transform as a [symmetric tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetric_tensor "Symmetric tensor")

![Image 26: {\displaystyle \sigma _{P}:S^{k}(T^{*}X)\otimes E\to F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e41599693748cd6ced7795a1d0d9849b4786bf6d)
whose domain is the [tensor product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_product "Tensor product") of the _k_ th[symmetric power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetric_power "Symmetric power") of the [cotangent bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cotangent_bundle "Cotangent bundle") of _X_ with _E_, and whose codomain is _F_. This symmetric tensor is known as the **principal symbol** (or just the **symbol**) of _P_.

The coordinate system _x_ _i_ permits a local trivialization of the cotangent bundle by the coordinate differentials d _x_ _i_, which determine fiber coordinates ξ _i_. In terms of a basis of frames _e_ μ, _f_ ν of _E_ and _F_, respectively, the differential operator _P_ decomposes into components

![Image 27: {\displaystyle (Pu)_{\nu }=\sum _{\mu }P_{\nu \mu }u_{\mu }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d6d59e0c3dc13180cb2767efb4968221793cda4f)
on each section _u_ of _E_. Here _P_ νμ is the scalar differential operator defined by

![Image 28: {\displaystyle P_{\nu \mu }=\sum _{\alpha }P_{\nu \mu }^{\alpha }{\frac {\partial }{\partial x^{\alpha }}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b71da62698904dd8eaf2bcf821816d9149a0113b)
With this trivialization, the principal symbol can now be written

![Image 29: {\displaystyle (\sigma _{P}(\xi )u)_{\nu }=\sum _{|\alpha |=k}\sum _{\mu }P_{\nu \mu }^{\alpha }(x)\xi _{\alpha }u_{\mu }.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8f9dd09d63f6492804da4b8dda3cc79e10f4b501)
In the cotangent space over a fixed point _x_ of _X_, the symbol ![Image 30: {\displaystyle \sigma _{P}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8e9872eb650117545176b9fde54569d40d52946b) defines a [homogeneous polynomial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homogeneous_polynomial "Homogeneous polynomial") of degree _k_ in ![Image 31: {\displaystyle T_{x}^{*}X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/232d2d103e32947890143498fb44b07cf8bcb5c9) with values in ![Image 32: {\displaystyle \operatorname {Hom} (E_{x},F_{x})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/910b3aa934ad3e173ddcbd057aa9a424e20dd5e5).

Fourier interpretation
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Differential_operator&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Fourier interpretation")]

A differential operator _P_ and its symbol appear naturally in connection with the [Fourier transform](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourier_transform "Fourier transform") as follows. Let ƒ be a [Schwartz function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schwartz_function "Schwartz function"). Then by the inverse Fourier transform,

![Image 33: {\displaystyle Pf(x)={\frac {1}{(2\pi )^{\frac {d}{2}}}}\int \limits _{\mathbf {R} ^{d}}e^{ix\cdot \xi }p(x,i\xi ){\hat {f}}(\xi )\,d\xi .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/010980e98bc0fab22f2fb447eaea5ab61e6919e3)
This exhibits _P_ as a [Fourier multiplier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourier_multiplier "Fourier multiplier"). A more general class of functions _p_(_x_,ξ) which satisfy at most polynomial growth conditions in ξ under which this integral is well-behaved comprises the [pseudo-differential operators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudo-differential_operator "Pseudo-differential operator").

*   The differential operator ![Image 34: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a) is [elliptic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptic_differential_operator "Elliptic differential operator") if its symbol is invertible; that is for each nonzero ![Image 35: {\displaystyle \theta \in T^{*}X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/31f4e25d16baf241de4a6436496e1725985a0052) the bundle map ![Image 36: {\displaystyle \sigma _{P}(\theta ,\dots ,\theta )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7e1201555b3502d721c512fceb942f65709d2172) is invertible. On a [compact manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compact_manifold "Compact manifold"), it follows from the elliptic theory that _P_ is a [Fredholm operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fredholm_operator "Fredholm operator"): it has finite-dimensional [kernel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kernel_(algebra) "Kernel (algebra)") and cokernel.
*   In the study of [hyperbolic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_partial_differential_equation "Hyperbolic partial differential equation") and [parabolic partial differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parabolic_partial_differential_equation "Parabolic partial differential equation"), zeros of the principal symbol correspond to the [characteristics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Method_of_characteristics "Method of characteristics") of the partial differential equation.
*   In applications to the physical sciences, operators such as the [Laplace operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laplace_operator "Laplace operator") play a major role in setting up and solving [partial differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_differential_equation "Partial differential equation").
*   In [differential topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_topology "Differential topology"), the [exterior derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_derivative "Exterior derivative") and [Lie derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_derivative "Lie derivative") operators have intrinsic meaning.
*   In [abstract algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abstract_algebra "Abstract algebra"), the concept of a [derivation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivation_(abstract_algebra) "Derivation (abstract algebra)") allows for generalizations of differential operators, which do not require the use of calculus. Frequently such generalizations are employed in [algebraic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_geometry "Algebraic geometry") and [commutative algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_algebra "Commutative algebra"). See also [Jet (mathematics)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jet_(mathematics) "Jet (mathematics)").
*   In the development of [holomorphic functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holomorphic_function "Holomorphic function") of a [complex variable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_variable "Complex variable")_z_ = _x_ + _i_ _y_, sometimes a complex function is considered to be a function of two real variables _x_ and _y_. Use is made of the [Wirtinger derivatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wirtinger_derivative "Wirtinger derivative"), which are partial differential operators: ![Image 37: {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial }{\partial z}}={\frac {1}{2}}\left({\frac {\partial }{\partial x}}-i{\frac {\partial }{\partial y}}\right)\ ,\quad {\frac {\partial }{\partial {\bar {z}}}}={\frac {1}{2}}\left({\frac {\partial }{\partial x}}+i{\frac {\partial }{\partial y}}\right)\ .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/acb2d046c7a344dec159203f70005a92c62c3eae) This approach is also used to study functions of [several complex variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Several_complex_variables "Several complex variables") and functions of a [motor variable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Motor_variable "Motor variable").
*   The differential operator [del](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Del "Del"), also called _nabla_, is an important [vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_vector "Euclidean vector") differential operator. It appears frequently in [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics") in places like the differential form of [Maxwell's equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxwell%27s_equations "Maxwell's equations"). In three-dimensional [Cartesian coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinates "Cartesian coordinates"), del is defined as

![Image 38: {\displaystyle \nabla =\mathbf {\hat {x}} {\partial  \over \partial x}+\mathbf {\hat {y}} {\partial  \over \partial y}+\mathbf {\hat {z}} {\partial  \over \partial z}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f470bbe9418b64df954a44a8b325f3650510f7b8)Del defines the [gradient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gradient "Gradient"), and is used to calculate the [curl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curl_(mathematics) "Curl (mathematics)"), [divergence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divergence "Divergence"), and [Laplacian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laplacian "Laplacian") of various objects.
*   A [chiral differential operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Chiral_differential_operator&action=edit&redlink=1 "Chiral differential operator (page does not exist)"). For now, see [[1]](https://ncatlab.org/nlab/show/chiral+differential+operator)

The conceptual step of writing a differential operator as something free-standing is attributed to [Louis François Antoine Arbogast](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis_Fran%C3%A7ois_Antoine_Arbogast "Louis François Antoine Arbogast") in 1800.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_operator#cite_note-3)

Adjoint of an operator
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Differential_operator&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Adjoint of an operator")]

Given a linear differential operator ![Image 57: {\displaystyle T}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ec7200acd984a1d3a3d7dc455e262fbe54f7f6e0)![Image 58: {\displaystyle Tu=\sum _{k=0}^{n}a_{k}(x)D^{k}u}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/84cbdbe333464a03227865de3d1a9ad61de5eb3e) the [adjoint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermitian_adjoint "Hermitian adjoint") of this operator is defined as the operator ![Image 59: {\displaystyle T^{*}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5426ed7abdea7d2ff995ad0e01bc4ca62a273855) such that ![Image 60: {\displaystyle \langle Tu,v\rangle =\langle u,T^{*}v\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e77f03211f61d51c55b1a949914f58f055cf2afd) where the notation ![Image 61: {\displaystyle \langle \cdot ,\cdot \rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9a50080b735975d8001c9552ac2134b49ad534c0) is used for the [scalar product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_product "Scalar product") or [inner product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inner_product "Inner product"). This definition therefore depends on the definition of the scalar product (or inner product).

### Formal adjoint in one variable

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Differential_operator&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Formal adjoint in one variable")]

In the functional space of [square-integrable functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square-integrable_function "Square-integrable function") on a [real](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number")[interval](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interval_(mathematics) "Interval (mathematics)")(_a_, _b_), the scalar product is defined by ![Image 62: {\displaystyle \langle f,g\rangle =\int _{a}^{b}{\overline {f(x)}}\,g(x)\,dx,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6c32baa17e66178b9eeebeddecff55a6cd1dfdd7)

where the line over _f_(_x_) denotes the [complex conjugate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_conjugate "Complex conjugate") of _f_(_x_). If one moreover adds the condition that _f_ or _g_ vanishes as ![Image 63: {\displaystyle x\to a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d566e0ecdd92745b25ad40bae39614468c01f6e2) and ![Image 64: {\displaystyle x\to b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/37c27a556d0293a768ebbdfebbb984578d8ad5b8), one can also define the adjoint of _T_ by ![Image 65: {\displaystyle T^{*}u=\sum _{k=0}^{n}(-1)^{k}D^{k}\left[{\overline {a_{k}(x)}}u\right].}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786020c57d778a84ba7c17220f2f4fa8470eaa5a)

This formula does not explicitly depend on the definition of the scalar product. It is therefore sometimes chosen as a definition of the adjoint operator. When ![Image 66: {\displaystyle T^{*}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5426ed7abdea7d2ff995ad0e01bc4ca62a273855) is defined according to this formula, it is called the **formal adjoint** of _T_.

A (formally) **[self-adjoint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-adjoint_operator "Self-adjoint operator")** operator is an operator equal to its own (formal) adjoint.

If Ω is a domain in **R**_n_, and _P_ a differential operator on Ω, then the adjoint of _P_ is defined in [_L_ 2(Ω)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lp_space "Lp space") by duality in the analogous manner:

![Image 67: {\displaystyle \langle f,P^{*}g\rangle _{L^{2}(\Omega )}=\langle Pf,g\rangle _{L^{2}(\Omega )}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/082767411a6bcd52b1cd4c26e8bc09b39f8213fa)
for all smooth _L_ 2 functions _f_, _g_. Since smooth functions are dense in _L_ 2, this defines the adjoint on a dense subset of _L_ 2: P* is a [densely defined operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Densely_defined_operator "Densely defined operator").

The [Sturm–Liouville](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sturm%E2%80%93Liouville_theory "Sturm–Liouville theory") operator is a well-known example of a formal self-adjoint operator. This second-order linear differential operator _L_ can be written in the form

![Image 68: {\displaystyle Lu=-(pu')'+qu=-(pu''+p'u')+qu=-pu''-p'u'+qu=(-p)D^{2}u+(-p')Du+(q)u.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/46baf3cbcc5447d81baeb589160d11b5eacbdf04)
This property can be proven using the formal adjoint definition above.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_operator#cite_note-5)

This operator is central to [Sturm–Liouville theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sturm%E2%80%93Liouville_theory "Sturm–Liouville theory") where the [eigenfunctions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eigenfunctions "Eigenfunctions") (analogues to [eigenvectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eigenvectors "Eigenvectors")) of this operator are considered.

Differentiation is [linear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_map "Linear map"), i.e.

![Image 69: {\displaystyle D(f+g)=(Df)+(Dg),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3939951e47d3f239395bbd63d45d99d9263ed432)![Image 70: {\displaystyle D(af)=a(Df),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8b2ac26ed425629f709e27f96479e1c26537f09d)
where _f_ and _g_ are functions, and _a_ is a constant.

Any [polynomial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial "Polynomial") in _D_ with function coefficients is also a differential operator. We may also [compose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_composition "Function composition") differential operators by the rule

![Image 71: {\displaystyle (D_{1}\circ D_{2})(f)=D_{1}(D_{2}(f)).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3dfd6acea3f12fa0a31aead839a11b2616f7d922)
Some care is then required: firstly any function coefficients in the operator _D_ 2 must be [differentiable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differentiable_function "Differentiable function") as many times as the application of _D_ 1 requires. To get a [ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ring_(mathematics) "Ring (mathematics)") of such operators we must assume derivatives of all orders of the coefficients used. Secondly, this ring will not be [commutative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_ring "Commutative ring"): an operator _gD_ isn't the same in general as _Dg_. For example we have the relation basic in [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics"):

![Image 72: {\displaystyle Dx-xD=1.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1af0a12f001a5a75ace6d7d720cb90574f24086e)
The subring of operators that are polynomials in _D_ with [constant coefficients](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constant_coefficients "Constant coefficients") is, by contrast, commutative. It can be characterised another way: it consists of the translation-invariant operators.

The differential operators also obey the [shift theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shift_theorem "Shift theorem").

Ring of polynomial differential operators
-----------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Differential_operator&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Ring of polynomial differential operators")]

### Ring of univariate polynomial differential operators

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Differential_operator&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Ring of univariate polynomial differential operators")]

If _R_ is a ring, let ![Image 73: {\displaystyle R\langle D,X\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cbc79e22ffea52cac48d8cba36de3714b39ee9ed) be the [non-commutative polynomial ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-commutative_polynomial_ring "Non-commutative polynomial ring") over _R_ in the variables _D_ and _X_, and _I_ the two-sided [ideal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ideal_(ring_theory) "Ideal (ring theory)") generated by _DX_ − _XD_ − 1. Then the ring of univariate polynomial differential operators over _R_ is the [quotient ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quotient_ring "Quotient ring")![Image 74: {\displaystyle R\langle D,X\rangle /I}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a55f7334ce87c3946ad4fdb901204a05dad61d2d). This is a non-commutative[simple ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simple_ring "Simple ring"). Every element can be written in a unique way as a _R_-linear combination of monomials of the form ![Image 75: {\displaystyle X^{a}D^{b}{\text{ mod }}I}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/83b0cec4f710131d3f25979184c9b38b5afdd94c). It supports an analogue of [Euclidean division of polynomials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_division_of_polynomials "Euclidean division of polynomials").

Differential modules[_[clarification needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Please\_clarify "Wikipedia:Please clarify")_] over ![Image 76: {\displaystyle R[X]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/afeccd52deabb878398a8485755c3ceea80caf9a) (for the standard derivation) can be identified with [modules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Module_(mathematics) "Module (mathematics)") over ![Image 77: {\displaystyle R\langle D,X\rangle /I}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a55f7334ce87c3946ad4fdb901204a05dad61d2d).

### Ring of multivariate polynomial differential operators

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Differential_operator&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Ring of multivariate polynomial differential operators")]

If _R_ is a ring, let ![Image 78: {\displaystyle R\langle D_{1},\ldots ,D_{n},X_{1},\ldots ,X_{n}\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eae2441e3b789ba33f7ef6832bf95084e7e9fe65) be the non-commutative polynomial ring over _R_ in the variables ![Image 79: {\displaystyle D_{1},\ldots ,D_{n},X_{1},\ldots ,X_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f0b18628fcd8d2435b551c591441969fd58df1ca), and _I_ the two-sided ideal generated by the elements

![Image 80: {\displaystyle (D_{i}X_{j}-X_{j}D_{i})-\delta _{i,j},\ \ \ D_{i}D_{j}-D_{j}D_{i},\ \ \ X_{i}X_{j}-X_{j}X_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8f6053d0e20e5ded2679da5db4af4b9fc4e2b18a)
for all ![Image 81: {\displaystyle 1\leq i,j\leq n,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/297ee6d9e5772c656b35b0a159f30f922cc70ea1) where ![Image 82: {\displaystyle \delta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c5321cfa797202b3e1f8620663ff43c4660ea03a) is [Kronecker delta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kronecker_delta "Kronecker delta"). Then the ring of multivariate polynomial differential operators over _R_ is the quotient ring ![Image 83: {\displaystyle R\langle D_{1},\ldots ,D_{n},X_{1},\ldots ,X_{n}\rangle /I}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/04af22bb86510f191b255a3df6ed96e9683d6863).

This is a non-commutative[simple ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simple_ring "Simple ring"). Every element can be written in a unique way as a _R_-linear combination of monomials of the form ![Image 84: {\displaystyle X_{1}^{a_{1}}\ldots X_{n}^{a_{n}}D_{1}^{b_{1}}\ldots D_{n}^{b_{n}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/be2e14737e271164f65c4671ca36fdc908e8b227).

Coordinate-independent description
----------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Differential_operator&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Coordinate-independent description")]

In [differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry") and [algebraic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_geometry "Algebraic geometry") it is often convenient to have a [coordinate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate "Coordinate")-independent description of differential operators between two [vector bundles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_bundle "Vector bundle"). Let _E_ and _F_ be two vector bundles over a [differentiable manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differentiable_manifold "Differentiable manifold")_M_. An **R**-linear mapping of [sections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_bundle "Vector bundle")_P_: Γ(_E_) → Γ(_F_) is said to be a **_k_ th-order linear differential operator** if it factors through the [jet bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jet_bundle "Jet bundle")_J_ _k_(_E_). In other words, there exists a linear mapping of vector bundles

![Image 85: {\displaystyle i_{P}:J^{k}(E)\to F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/63e686598dccd8bad0b90a385ebb7bfe6e28e8c3)
such that

![Image 86: {\displaystyle P=i_{P}\circ j^{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fa4a55e85acfd226244ed25da7b3f9c3a68086fb)
where _j_ _k_: Γ(_E_) → Γ(_J_ _k_(_E_)) is the prolongation that associates to any section of _E_ its [_k_-jet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jet_(mathematics) "Jet (mathematics)").

This just means that for a given [section](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_bundle "Vector bundle")_s_ of _E_, the value of _P_(_s_) at a point _x_∈_M_ is fully determined by the _k_ th-order infinitesimal behavior of _s_ in _x_. In particular this implies that _P_(_s_)(_x_) is determined by the [germ](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sheaf_(mathematics) "Sheaf (mathematics)") of _s_ in _x_, which is expressed by saying that differential operators are local. A foundational result is the [Peetre theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peetre_theorem "Peetre theorem") showing that the converse is also true: any (linear) local operator is differential.

### Relation to commutative algebra

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Differential_operator&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: Relation to commutative algebra")]

An equivalent, but purely algebraic description of linear differential operators is as follows: an **R**-linear map _P_ is a _k_ th-order linear differential operator, if for any _k_+ 1 smooth functions ![Image 87: {\displaystyle f_{0},\ldots ,f_{k}\in C^{\infty }(M)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0ffe1af7f446520065f0ab7c6c9176074de8b79c) we have

![Image 88: {\displaystyle [f_{k},[f_{k-1},[\cdots [f_{0},P]\cdots ]]=0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/24bb2111918496a5d3640a1b1c5f4e674b59b70d)
Here the bracket ![Image 89: {\displaystyle [f,P]:\Gamma (E)\to \Gamma (F)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f37a30580cbc91e9277251ecf8d3fdcd2d78726d) is defined as the commutator

![Image 90: {\displaystyle [f,P](s)=P(f\cdot s)-f\cdot P(s).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ef1cfcf50fe7cfb77dde6bc9b4e2ae629ec3c2a0)
This characterization of linear differential operators shows that they are particular mappings between [modules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Module_(mathematics) "Module (mathematics)") over a [commutative algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_algebra_(structure) "Commutative algebra (structure)"), allowing the concept to be seen as a part of [commutative algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_algebra "Commutative algebra").

### A differential operator of infinite order

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Differential_operator&action=edit&section=17 "Edit section: A differential operator of infinite order")]

A differential operator of infinite order is (roughly) a differential operator whose total symbol is a [power series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_series "Power series") instead of a polynomial.

### Bidifferential operator

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Differential_operator&action=edit&section=18 "Edit section: Bidifferential operator")]

A differential operator acting on two functions ![Image 91: {\displaystyle D(g,f)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a203bfde2536f623644e9d59233a06168a2a1f4) is called a **bidifferential operator**. The notion appears, for instance, in an associative algebra structure on a deformation quantization of a Poisson algebra.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_operator#cite_note-6)

### Microdifferential operator

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Differential_operator&action=edit&section=19 "Edit section: Microdifferential operator")]

A [microdifferential operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microdifferential_operator "Microdifferential operator") is a type of operator on an open subset of a cotangent bundle, as opposed to an open subset of a manifold. It is obtained by extending the notion of a differential operator to the cotangent bundle.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_operator#cite_note-7)
