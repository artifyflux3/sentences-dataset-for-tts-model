Meteor seen from the site of the [Atacama Large Millimeter Array](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atacama_Large_Millimeter_Array "Atacama Large Millimeter Array") (ALMA)[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor#cite_note-1)

A **meteor**, known colloquially as a **shooting star**, is a glowing streak of a small body (usually [meteoroid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteoroid "Meteoroid")) going through Earth's atmosphere, after being heated to incandescence by collisions with air molecules in the upper atmosphere,[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor#cite_note-IMO-meteoroid-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor#cite_note-M-W-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor#cite_note-4) creating a streak of light via its rapid motion and sometimes also by shedding glowing material in its wake. Although a meteor may seem to be a few thousand feet from the Earth,[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor#cite_note-5) meteors typically occur in the [mesosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mesosphere "Mesosphere") at altitudes from 76–100 kilometres (47–62 miles).[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor#cite_note-MIT-pje-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor#cite_note-AMS-2-7) The root word _meteor_ comes from the [Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek "Ancient Greek")_meteōros_, meaning "high in the air".[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor#cite_note-M-W-3)

Millions of meteors occur in Earth's atmosphere daily. Most meteoroids that cause meteors are about the size of a grain of sand, i.e. they are usually one millimeter (3⁄64 inch) or smaller. Meteoroid sizes can be calculated from their mass and density which, in turn, can be estimated from the observed meteor trajectory in the upper atmosphere.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor#cite_note-AJ-18-8) Meteors may occur in [showers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor_shower "Meteor shower"), which arise when Earth passes through a stream of debris left by a comet, or as "random" or "sporadic" meteors, not associated with a specific stream of [space debris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_debris "Space debris"). A number of specific meteors have been observed, largely by members of the public and largely by accident, but with enough detail that orbits of the meteoroids producing the meteors have been calculated. The atmospheric velocities of meteors result from the movement of Earth around the Sun at about 30 kilometres per second (18.6 miles per second),[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor#cite_note-NASA-earthfact-9) the orbital speeds of meteoroids, and the [gravity well](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity_well "Gravity well") of Earth.

Meteors become visible between about 75 to 120 km (47 to 75 mi) above Earth. They usually disintegrate at altitudes of 50 to 95 kilometres (31 to 59 mi).[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor#cite_note-meteorsandtheirparentcomets-10) Meteors have roughly a fifty percent chance of a daylight (or near daylight) collision with Earth. Most meteors are, however, observed at night, when darkness allows fainter objects to be recognized. For bodies with a size scale larger than 10 centimeters (3+7⁄8 inches) to several meters meteor visibility is due to the atmospheric [ram pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ram_pressure "Ram pressure") (not friction) that heats the meteoroid so that it glows and creates a shining trail of gases and melted meteoroid particles. The gases include vaporised meteoroid material and atmospheric gases that heat up when the meteoroid passes through the atmosphere. Most meteors glow for about a second.

Meteors were not known to be an astronomical phenomenon until early in the nineteenth century. Before that, they were seen in the West as an atmospheric phenomenon, like lightning, and were not connected with strange stories of rocks falling from the sky. In 1807, [Yale University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yale_University "Yale University") chemistry professor [Benjamin Silliman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benjamin_Silliman "Benjamin Silliman") investigated a [meteorite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteorite "Meteorite") that fell in [Weston, Connecticut](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weston_meteorite "Weston meteorite").[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor#cite_note-jefferson-11) Silliman believed the meteor had a cosmic origin, but meteors did not attract much attention from astronomers until the spectacular meteor storm of November 1833.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor#cite_note-1833leonids-12) People all across the eastern United States saw thousands of meteors, radiating from a single point in the sky. Careful observers noticed that [the radiant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiant_(meteor_shower) "Radiant (meteor shower)"), as the point is called, moved with the stars, staying in the constellation Leo.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor#cite_note-AJSA-1834-13)

The astronomer [Denison Olmsted](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Denison_Olmsted "Denison Olmsted") extensively studied this storm, concluding that it had a cosmic origin. After reviewing historical records, [Heinrich Wilhelm Matthias Olbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heinrich_Wilhelm_Matthias_Olbers "Heinrich Wilhelm Matthias Olbers") predicted the storm's return in 1867, drawing other astronomers' attention to the phenomenon. [Hubert A. Newton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubert_Anson_Newton "Hubert Anson Newton")'s more thorough historical work led to a refined prediction of 1866, which proved correct.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor#cite_note-1833leonids-12) With [Giovanni Schiaparelli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giovanni_Schiaparelli "Giovanni Schiaparelli")'s success in connecting the [Leonids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leonids "Leonids") (as they are called) with comet [Tempel-Tuttle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tempel-Tuttle "Tempel-Tuttle"), the cosmic origin of meteors was firmly established. Still, they remain an atmospheric phenomenon and retain their name "meteor" from the Greek word for "atmospheric".[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor#cite_note-AP-318-14)

Camera recording of a [superbolide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chelyabinsk_meteor "Chelyabinsk meteor") passage above [Chelyabinsk Oblast](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chelyabinsk_Oblast "Chelyabinsk Oblast"), Russia on 15th February 2013. The object in this video is estimated to be 17 to 20 metres (56 to 66 ft) in diameter before atmospheric entry.

A **fireball** is a brighter-than-usual meteor which can be also seen during daylight. The [International Astronomical Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union "International Astronomical Union") (IAU) defines a fireball as "a meteor brighter than any of the planets" ([apparent magnitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apparent_magnitude "Apparent magnitude") −4 or greater).[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor#cite_note-meteor-13871-15) The [International Meteor Organization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Meteor_Organization "International Meteor Organization") (an amateur organization that studies meteors) has a more rigid definition. It defines a fireball as a meteor that would have at least magnitude of −3 if seen at [zenith](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zenith "Zenith"). This definition corrects for the greater distance between an observer and a meteor near the horizon. For example, a meteor of magnitude −1 at 5 degrees above the horizon would be classified as a fireball because, if the observer had been directly below the meteor, it would have appeared as magnitude −6.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor#cite_note-IMO-fireball-16)

Fireballs reaching apparent magnitude −14 or brighter are called [bolides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bolide "Bolide").[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor#cite_note-Belton-17) The IAU has no official definition of "bolide", and generally considers the term synonymous with "fireball". Astronomers often use "bolide" to identify an exceptionally bright fireball, particularly one that explodes in a [meteor air burst](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor_air_burst "Meteor air burst").[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor#cite_note-18) They are sometimes called detonating fireballs. It may also be used to mean a fireball which creates audible sounds. In the late twentieth century, bolide has also come to mean any object that hits Earth and explodes, with no regard to its composition (asteroid or comet).[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor#cite_note-Google-Book-jR84AAAAIAAJ-19) The word _bolide_ comes from the [Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_language "Greek language") βολίς (_bolis_) [[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor#cite_note-myet-bolide-20) which can mean _a missile_ or _to flash_. If the magnitude of a bolide reaches −17 or brighter it is known as a [superbolide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superbolide "Superbolide").[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor#cite_note-Belton-17)[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor#cite_note-Google-Book-3Hd8Lw1hExUC-21) A relatively small percentage of fireballs hit Earth's atmosphere and then pass out again: these are termed [Earth-grazing fireballs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth-grazing_fireball "Earth-grazing fireball"). Such an event happened in [broad daylight over North America in 1972](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Great_Daylight_1972_Fireball "The Great Daylight 1972 Fireball"). Another rare phenomenon is a [meteor procession](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor_procession "Meteor procession"), where the meteor breaks up into several fireballs traveling nearly parallel to the surface of Earth.

A steadily growing number of fireballs are recorded at the [American Meteor Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Meteor_Society "American Meteor Society") every year.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor#cite_note-AMS-Fireballs-22) There are several thousand fireballs a day,[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor#cite_note-AMS-FAQ-23) but most go unnoticed because most occur over the ocean and half occur during daytime. A [European Fireball Network](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Fireball_Network "European Fireball Network") and a NASA All-sky Fireball Network detect and track many fireballs.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor#cite_note-24)

Effect on atmosphere
--------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Meteor&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Effect on atmosphere")]

A meteoroid of the [Perseids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perseids "Perseids") with a size of about ten millimeters (3⁄8 inch) entering the earth's atmosphere in real time. The meteoroid is at the bright head of the trail, and the ionisation of the [mesosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mesosphere "Mesosphere") is still visible in the tail.

The entry of meteoroids into Earth's atmosphere produces three main effects: ionization of atmospheric molecules, dust that the meteoroid sheds, and the sound of passage. During the entry of a meteoroid or asteroid into the [upper atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Upper_atmosphere "Upper atmosphere"), an **ionization trail** is created, where the air molecules are [ionized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ionization "Ionization") by the passage of the meteor. Such ionization trails can last up to 45 minutes at a time.

Small, [sand-grain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sand-grain "Sand-grain") sized meteoroids are entering the atmosphere constantly, essentially every few seconds in any given region of the atmosphere, and thus ionization trails can be found in the upper atmosphere more or less continuously. When radio waves are bounced off these trails, it is called [meteor burst communications](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor_burst_communications "Meteor burst communications"). Meteor radars can measure atmospheric density and winds by measuring the [decay rate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decay_rate "Decay rate") and [Doppler shift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doppler_shift "Doppler shift") of a meteor trail. Most meteoroids burn up when they enter the atmosphere. The left-over debris is called _meteoric dust_ or just meteor dust. Meteor dust particles can persist in the atmosphere for up to several months. These particles might affect climate, both by scattering electromagnetic radiation and by catalyzing chemical reactions in the upper atmosphere.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor#cite_note-Nature-443141a-25)

Meteoroids or their fragments enter a phase called **dark flight** after deceleration to [terminal velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terminal_velocity "Terminal velocity") when they are no longer emitting visible light.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor#cite_note-IMO-meteorites-26) Dark flight starts when they decelerate to about 2–4 kilometres per second (1.2–2.5 miles per second). [[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor#cite_note-AMS-Fireball-27) Larger fragments fall further down the [strewn field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strewn_field "Strewn field").

[![Image 3](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Leonid_Meteor.jpg/250px-Leonid_Meteor.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Leonid_Meteor.jpg)

A meteor of the [Leonid meteor shower](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leonid_meteor_shower "Leonid meteor shower"); the photograph shows the meteor, afterglow, and wake as distinct components

The visible light produced by a meteor may take on various hues, depending on the chemical composition of the meteoroid, and the speed of its movement through the atmosphere. As layers of the meteoroid abrade and ionize, the colour of the light emitted may change according to the layering of minerals. Colours of meteors depend on the relative influence of the metallic content of the meteoroid versus the superheated air plasma, which its passage engenders:[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor#cite_note-28)

*   Orange-yellow ([sodium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium "Sodium"))
*   Yellow ([iron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron "Iron"))
*   Blue-green ([magnesium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnesium "Magnesium"))
*   Violet ([calcium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calcium "Calcium"))
*   Red (atmospheric [nitrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen "Nitrogen") and [oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen "Oxygen"))

### Acoustic manifestations

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Meteor&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Acoustic manifestations")]

The sound generated by a meteor in the upper atmosphere, such as a [sonic boom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sonic_boom "Sonic boom"), typically arrives many seconds after the meteor event itself. Occasionally, as with the [Leonid meteor shower](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leonid_meteor_shower "Leonid meteor shower") of 2001, "crackling", "swishing", or "hissing" sounds have been reported,[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor#cite_note-find-87854873-29) occurring at the same instant as a meteor flare. These are sometimes called [electrophonic sounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrophonic_sounds "Electrophonic sounds").[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor#cite_note-30) Similar sounds have also been reported during intense displays of Earth's [auroras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aurora_(astronomy) "Aurora (astronomy)").[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor#cite_note-auroral-sounds-31)[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor#cite_note-auroral-acoustics-32)[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor#cite_note-AG16-155-33)[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor#cite_note-JRASC-373K-34)

Theories on the generation of these sounds may partially explain them. For example, scientists at NASA suggested that the turbulent ionized wake of a meteor interacts with [Earth's magnetic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geomagnetic_field "Geomagnetic field"), generating pulses of [radio waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radio_wave "Radio wave"). As the trail dissipates, [megawatts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Megawatt "Megawatt") of electromagnetic power could be released, with a peak in the [power spectrum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_spectrum "Power spectrum") at [audio frequencies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Audio_frequencies "Audio frequencies"). Physical [vibrations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vibration "Vibration") induced by the electromagnetic impulses would then be heard if they are powerful enough to make grasses, plants, eyeglass frames, the hearer's own body (see [microwave auditory effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microwave_auditory_effect "Microwave auditory effect")), and other conductive materials vibrate.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor#cite_note-NASA-ast26-35)[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor#cite_note-36)[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor#cite_note-37)[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor#cite_note-38) This proposed mechanism, although proven plausible by laboratory work, remains unsupported by corresponding measurements in the field. Sound recordings made under controlled conditions in Mongolia in 1998 support the contention that the sounds are real.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor#cite_note-BBC-321596-39)_(Also see [Bolide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bolide "Bolide").)_

A [meteor shower](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor_shower "Meteor shower") is the result of an interaction between a planet, such as Earth, and streams of debris from a [comet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comet "Comet") or other source. The passage of Earth through cosmic debris from comets and other sources is a [recurring event](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_meteor_showers "List of meteor showers") in many cases. Comets can produce debris by water vapor drag, as demonstrated by [Fred Whipple](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fred_Whipple "Fred Whipple") in 1951,[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor#cite_note-Harvard-464W-40) and by breakup. Each time a comet swings by the Sun in its [orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbit "Orbit"), some of its ice vaporizes and a certain amount of meteoroids are shed. The meteoroids spread out along the entire orbit of the comet to form a meteoroid stream, also known as a "dust trail" (as opposed to a comet's "dust tail" caused by the very small particles that are quickly blown away by solar radiation pressure).

The frequency of [fireball](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fireball_(meteor) "Fireball (meteor)") sightings increases by about 10–30% during the weeks of [vernal equinox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equinox "Equinox").[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor#cite_note-fireball_season_NASA-41) Even [meteorite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteorite "Meteorite") falls are more common during the [northern hemisphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere "Northern Hemisphere")'s spring season. Although this phenomenon has been known for quite some time, the reason behind the anomaly is not fully understood by scientists. Some researchers attribute this to an intrinsic variation in the meteoroid population along Earth's orbit, with a peak in big fireball-producing debris around spring and early summer. Others have pointed out that during this period the ecliptic is (in the northern hemisphere) high in the sky in the late afternoon and early evening. This means that fireball radiants with an asteroidal source are high in the sky (facilitating relatively high rates) at the moment the meteoroids "catch up" with Earth, coming from behind going in the same direction as Earth. This causes relatively low relative speeds and from this low entry speeds, which facilitates survival of meteorites.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor#cite_note-42) It also generates high fireball rates in the early evening, increasing chances of eyewitness reports. This explains a part, but perhaps not all of the seasonal variation. Research is in progress for mapping the orbits of the meteors to gain a better understanding of the phenomenon.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor#cite_note-camera_network_NASA-43)

1992– Peekskill, New York The [Peekskill Meteorite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peekskill_Meteorite "Peekskill Meteorite") was recorded on October 9, 1992 by at least 16 independent videographers.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor#cite_note-uwo-peeks-44) Eyewitness accounts indicate the fireball entry of the Peekskill meteorite started over West Virginia at 23:48 UT (±1 min). The fireball, which traveled in a northeasterly direction, had a pronounced greenish colour, and attained an estimated peak visual magnitude of −13. During a luminous flight time that exceeded 40 seconds the fireball covered a [ground path](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ground_path "Ground path") of some 430 to 500 mi (700 to 800 km).[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor#cite_note-Nature-6464-45) One meteorite recovered at [Peekskill, New York](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peekskill,_New_York "Peekskill, New York"), for which the event and object gained their name, had a mass of 27 lb (12.4 kg) and was subsequently identified as an H6 monomict breccia meteorite.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor#cite_note-MB-75-46) The video record suggests that the Peekskill meteorite had several companions over a wide area. The [superbolide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superbolide "Superbolide") meteor was part of the [South Taurids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taurids "Taurids") meteor shower.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor#cite_note-53) It traveled east to west ending its flight somewhere near [Wellsville](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wellsville,_Missouri "Wellsville, Missouri"), [Missouri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Missouri "Missouri").[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor#cite_note-54)[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor#cite_note-55)[![Image 7](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/SmallAsteroidImpacts-Frequency-Bolide-20141114.jpg/250px-SmallAsteroidImpacts-Frequency-Bolide-20141114.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:SmallAsteroidImpacts-Frequency-Bolide-20141114.jpg)
