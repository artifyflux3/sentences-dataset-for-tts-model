[![Image 1: Four types of separating decimals: a) 1,234.56. b) 1.234,56. c) 1'234,56. d) ١٬٢٣٤٫٥٦.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Decimal_separators.svg/250px-Decimal_separators.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Decimal_separators.svg)

Both a [comma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comma "Comma") and a [full stop](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Full_stop "Full stop") (or period) are generally accepted decimal separators for international use. The [apostrophe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apostrophe "Apostrophe") and Arabic decimal separator are also used in certain contexts.

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/3a/Thousands_separators.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Thousands_separators.gif)

Three ways to group the number ten thousand with digit group separators: 

*   [Space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_(punctuation) "Space (punctuation)"), the internationally recommended thousands separator.
*   [Full stop](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Full_stop "Full stop") (or period), the thousands separator used in many non-English speaking countries.
*   [Comma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comma "Comma"), the thousands separator used in most English-speaking countries.

A **decimal separator** is a symbol that separates the [integer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer") part from the [fractional part](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractional_part "Fractional part") of a [number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number "Number") written in [decimal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal "Decimal") form. Different countries officially designate different symbols for use as the separator. The choice of symbol can also affect the choice of symbol for the [thousands separator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#Digit_grouping) used in digit grouping.

Any such symbol can be called a **decimal mark**, **decimal marker**, or **decimal sign**. Symbol-specific names are also used; **decimal point** and **decimal comma** refer to a dot (either [baseline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baseline_dot "Baseline dot") or [middle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_dot "Middle dot")) and [comma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comma "Comma") respectively, when it is used as a decimal separator; these are the usual terms used in English,[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#cite_note-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#cite_note-:0-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#cite_note-3) with the aforementioned generic terms reserved for abstract usage.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#cite_note-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#cite_note-5)

In many contexts, when a number is spoken, the function of the separator is assumed by the spoken name of the symbol: _comma_ or _point_ in most cases.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#cite_note-6)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#cite_note-:0-2)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#cite_note-7) In some specialized contexts, the word _decimal_ is instead used for this purpose (such as in [International Civil Aviation Organization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Civil_Aviation_Organization "International Civil Aviation Organization")-regulated [air traffic control](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Air_traffic_control "Air traffic control") communications). In mathematics, the decimal separator is a type of [radix point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#Radix_point), a term that also applies to number systems with bases other than ten.

### Hellenistic–Renaissance eras

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Decimal_separator&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Hellenistic–Renaissance eras")]

In the [Middle Ages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_Ages "Middle Ages"), before printing, a [bar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Overline "Overline") ( ¯ ) over the [units digit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Units_digit "Units digit") was used to separate the integral part of a number from its [fractional part](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractional_part "Fractional part"), as in 9 9 95 (meaning 99.95 in [decimal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal "Decimal") point format). A similar notation remains in common use as an underbar to superscript digits, especially for monetary values without a decimal separator, as in 99 95. Later, a "separatrix" (i.e., a short, roughly vertical ink stroke) between the units and tenths position became the norm among [Arab mathematicians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics_in_medieval_Islam "Mathematics in medieval Islam") (e.g. 99**ˌ**95), while an L-shaped or [vertical bar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertical_bar "Vertical bar") (|) served as the separatrix in England.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#cite_note-8) When this character was [typeset](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Typeset "Typeset"), it was convenient to use the existing [comma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comma_(punctuation) "Comma (punctuation)") (99**,**95) or [full stop](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Full_stop "Full stop") (99**.**95) instead.

Positional [decimal fractions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_fraction "Decimal fraction") appear for the first time in a book by the Arab mathematician [Abu'l-Hasan al-Uqlidisi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abu%27l-Hasan_al-Uqlidisi "Abu'l-Hasan al-Uqlidisi") written in the 10th century.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#cite_note-Berggren-9) The practice is ultimately derived from the decimal [Hindu–Arabic numeral system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hindu%E2%80%93Arabic_numeral_system "Hindu–Arabic numeral system") used in [Indian mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_mathematics "Indian mathematics"),[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#cite_note-10) and popularized by the [Persian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persian_people "Persian people") mathematician [Al-Khwarizmi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Khwarizmi "Al-Khwarizmi"),[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#cite_note-11) when [Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin "Latin") translation of [his work](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#Arithmetic) on the [Indian numerals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_numerals "Indian numerals") introduced the decimal [positional number system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positional_notation "Positional notation") to the Western world. His _[Compendious Book on Calculation by Completion and Balancing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compendious\_Book\_on\_Calculation\_by\_Completion\_and\_Balancing "Compendious Book on Calculation by Completion and Balancing")_ presented the first systematic solution of [linear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_equation "Linear equation") and [quadratic equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadratic_equation "Quadratic equation") in Arabic.

[Gerbert of Aurillac](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pope_Sylvester_II "Pope Sylvester II") marked triples of columns with an arc (called a "Pythagorean arc"), when using his Hindu–Arabic numeral-based abacus in the 10th century. [Fibonacci](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fibonacci "Fibonacci") followed this convention when writing numbers, such as in his influential work _[Liber Abaci](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liber\_Abaci "Liber Abaci")_ in the 13th century.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#cite_note-12)

The earliest known record of using the decimal point is in the astronomical tables compiled by the Italian merchant and mathematician [Giovanni Bianchini](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giovanni_Bianchini "Giovanni Bianchini") in the 1440s.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#cite_note-13)[_[contradictory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History\_of\_mathematical\_notation#Indian\_and\_Arabic\_numerals\_and\_notation "History of mathematical notation")_]

Tables of [logarithms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logarithm "Logarithm") prepared by [John Napier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Napier "John Napier") in 1614 and 1619 used the period (full stop) as the decimal separator, which was then adopted by [Henry Briggs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry_Briggs_(mathematician) "Henry Briggs (mathematician)") in his influential 17th century work.

In [France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/France "France"), the full stop was already in use in printing to make [Roman numerals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_numerals "Roman numerals") more readable, so the comma was chosen.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#cite_note-Santillana-1996-14)

Many other countries, such as Italy, also chose to use the comma to mark the decimal units position.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#cite_note-Santillana-1996-14) It has been [made standard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO_80000-2 "ISO 80000-2") by the [ISO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization "International Organization for Standardization") for international blueprints.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#cite_note-15) However, English-speaking countries took the comma to separate sequences of three digits. In some countries, a raised dot or dash (_upper comma_) may be used for grouping or decimal separator; this is particularly common in handwriting.

### English-speaking countries

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Decimal_separator&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: English-speaking countries")]

In the [United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States "United States"), the full stop or period (.) is used as the standard decimal separator.

[![Image 3](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Interpunct_as_decimal_point_in_Henderson_1839.png/500px-Interpunct_as_decimal_point_in_Henderson_1839.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Interpunct_as_decimal_point_in_Henderson_1839.png)

The [interpunct](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interpunct "Interpunct") (·) used as a decimal separator in a British print from 1839[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#cite_note-16)

In the nations of the [British Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Empire "British Empire") (and, later, the [Commonwealth of Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations "Commonwealth of Nations")), the full stop could be used in typewritten material and its use was not banned, although the [interpunct](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interpunct "Interpunct") (a.k.a. decimal point, point or mid dot) was preferred as a decimal separator, in printing technologies that could accommodate it, e.g.  99·95 .[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#cite_note-17) However, as the mid dot was already in common use in the mathematics world to indicate multiplication, the [SI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SI "SI") rejected its use as the decimal separator.

During the beginning of British [metrication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metrication "Metrication") in the late 1960s and with impending currency [decimalisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimalisation "Decimalisation"), there was some debate in the United Kingdom as to whether the decimal comma or decimal point should be preferred: the [British Standards Institution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Standards_Institution "British Standards Institution") and some sectors of industry advocated the comma and the [Decimal Currency Board](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_Day "Decimal Day") advocated for the point. In the event, the point was chosen by the [Ministry of Technology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ministry_of_Technology "Ministry of Technology") in 1968.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#cite_note-18)

[![Image 4](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/MUTCD-CA_G11-6.svg/120px-MUTCD-CA_G11-6.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:MUTCD-CA_G11-6.svg)

California milepost marker at mile 144.44

When South Africa [adopted the metric system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metrication "Metrication"), it adopted the comma as its decimal separator,[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#cite_note-19) although a number of house styles, including some English-language newspapers such as _[The Sunday Times](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sunday\_Times\_(South\_Africa) "Sunday Times (South Africa)")_, continue to use the full stop.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

Previously, signs along [California](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/California "California") roads expressed distances in decimal numbers with the decimal part in superscript, as in 3 7, meaning 3.7.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#cite_note-20) Though California has since transitioned to [mixed numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mixed_number "Mixed number") with [common fractions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Common_fraction "Common fraction"), the older style remains on [postmile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/California_postmile "California postmile") markers and bridge inventory markers.

### Constructed languages

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Decimal_separator&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Constructed languages")]

The three most spoken [international auxiliary languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_auxiliary_language "International auxiliary language"), [Ido](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ido "Ido"), [Esperanto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Esperanto "Esperanto"), and [Interlingua](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interlingua "Interlingua"), all use the comma as the decimal separator.

Interlingua has used the comma as its decimal separator since the publication of the [Interlingua Grammar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interlingua:_A_Grammar_of_the_International_Language "Interlingua: A Grammar of the International Language") in 1951.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#cite_note-21)

Esperanto also uses the comma as its official decimal separator, whilst thousands are usually separated by [non-breaking spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-breaking_space "Non-breaking space") (e.g. 12 345 678,9). It is possible to separate thousands by a [full stop](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Full_stop "Full stop") (e.g. 12.345.678,9), though this is not as common.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#cite_note-22)

Ido's _Kompleta Gramatiko Detaloza di la Linguo Internaciona Ido_ (Complete Detailed Grammar of the International Language Ido) officially states that commas are used for the decimal separator whilst full stops are used to separate thousands, millions, etc. So the number 12,345,678.90123 (in American notation), for instance, would be written _12.345.678,90123_ in Ido.

The 1931 grammar of [Volapük](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volap%C3%BCk "Volapük") uses the comma as its decimal separator but, somewhat unusually, the middle dot as its thousands separator (12·345·678,90123).[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#cite_note-23)

In 1958, disputes between European and American delegates over the correct representation of the decimal separator nearly stalled the development of the [ALGOL](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ALGOL "ALGOL") computer programming language.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#cite_note-24) ALGOL ended up allowing different decimal separators, but most computer languages and standard data formats (e.g., [C](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C_(programming_language) "C (programming language)"), [Java](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Java_(programming_language) "Java (programming language)"), [Fortran](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fortran "Fortran"), [Cascading Style Sheets (CSS)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CSS "CSS")) specify a dot. [C++](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C%2B%2B14#Digit_separators "C++14") and a couple of others permit a quote (') as thousands separator, and many others like Python and Julia, (only) allow '_' as such a separator (it's usually ignored, i.e. also allows 1_00_00_000 aligning with the Indian number style of 1,00,00,000 that would be 10,000,000 in the US).

In mathematics and [computing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computing "Computing"), a **radix point** or **radix character** is a symbol used in the display of numbers to separate the [integer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer") part of the value from its [fractional part](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractional_part "Fractional part"). In English and many other languages (including many that are written right-to-left), the integer part is at the left of the radix point, and the fraction part at the right of it.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#cite_note-25)

A radix point is most often used in [decimal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal "Decimal") (base 10) notation, when it is more commonly called the _decimal point_ (with [deci-](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/deci- "wiktionary:deci-") indicating [base 10](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Base_10 "Base 10")). In [English-speaking countries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_countries_where_English_is_an_official_language "List of countries where English is an official language"), the decimal point is usually a small dot (.) placed either on the baseline, or halfway between the baseline and the top of the [digits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Numerical_digit "Numerical digit") (**·**)[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#cite_note-oracle-26)[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#cite_note-oracle_quote-27) In many other countries, the radix point is a comma (,) placed on the baseline.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#cite_note-oracle-26)[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#cite_note-oracle_quote-27)

These conventions are generally used both in machine displays ([printing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Printing "Printing"), [computer monitors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_monitor "Computer monitor")) and in [handwriting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Handwriting "Handwriting"). It is important to know which notation is being used when working in different software programs. The respective [ISO 31-0 standard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO_31-0#Numbers "ISO 31-0") defines both the comma and the small dot as decimal markers, but does not explicitly define universal radix marks for bases other than 10.

Fractional numbers are rarely displayed in other [number bases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radix "Radix"), but, when they are, a radix character may be used for the same purpose. When used with the [binary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_number "Binary number") (base 2) representation, it may be called "binary point".

The 22nd [General Conference on Weights and Measures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Conference_on_Weights_and_Measures "General Conference on Weights and Measures")[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#cite_note-CGPM-22nd-R10-28) declared in 2003, "The symbol for the decimal marker shall be either the point on the line or the comma on the line." It further reaffirmed,[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#cite_note-CGPM-22nd-R10-28)

> Numbers may be divided in groups of three in order to facilitate reading; neither dots nor commas are ever inserted in the spaces between groups

That is, "1 000 000 000" is preferred over "1,000,000,000" or "1.000.000.000". This use has therefore been recommended by technical organizations, such as the United States's [National Institute of Standards and Technology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Institute_of_Standards_and_Technology "National Institute of Standards and Technology").[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#cite_note-29)

Past versions of [ISO 8601](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO_8601 "ISO 8601"), but not the 2019 revision, also stipulated normative notation based on SI conventions, adding that the comma is preferred over the full stop.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#cite_note-30)

[ISO 80000-1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO_80000-1 "ISO 80000-1") stipulates, "The decimal sign is either a comma or a point on the line." The standard does not stipulate any preference, observing that usage will depend on customary usage in the language concerned, but adds a note that as per ISO/IEC directives, all ISO standards should use the comma as the decimal marker.

For ease of reading, numbers with many digits (e.g. numbers over 999) may be divided into groups using a [delimiter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Delimiter "Delimiter"),[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#cite_note-AMA10p793-31) such as comma (`,`), dot (`.`), half-space or [thin space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thin_space "Thin space") (" "), [space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_(punctuation) "Space (punctuation)") (""), underscore (`_`; as in maritime "21_450"),[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] or apostrophe (`'`). In some countries, these "digit group separators" are only employed to the left of the decimal separator; in others, they are also used to separate numbers with a long [fractional part](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractional_part "Fractional part"). An important reason for grouping is that it allows rapid judgement of the number of digits, via telling at a glance ("[subitizing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subitizing "Subitizing")") rather than counting (contrast, for example, 100 000 000 with 100000000 for one hundred million).

The use of thin spaces as separators[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#cite_note-SIBrochure-32): 133  instead of dots or commas (for example: 20 000 and 1 000 000 for "twenty thousand" and "one million"), has been official policy of the [International Bureau of Weights and Measures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Bureau_of_Weights_and_Measures "International Bureau of Weights and Measures") (BIPM) since 1948 (and reaffirmed in 2003), [[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#cite_note-CGPM-22nd-R10-28) as well as of the [International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Union_of_Pure_and_Applied_Chemistry "International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry") (IUPAC),[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#cite_note-BIPM-33)[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#cite_note-old.iupac.org-34) the [American Medical Association](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Medical_Association "American Medical Association")'s widely followed _[AMA Manual of Style](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AMA\_Manual\_of\_Style "AMA Manual of Style")_, and the UK [Metrication Board](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metrication_Board "Metrication Board"), among others.

The groups created by the delimiters tend to follow the usages of local languages, which vary. In European languages, large numbers are read in groups of thousands, and the delimiter (occurring every three digits when used) may be called a "thousands separator". In [East Asian cultures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_Asian_culture "East Asian culture"), particularly [China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_numerals "Chinese numerals"), [Japan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japanese_numerals "Japanese numerals"), and [Korea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_numerals "Korean numerals"), large numbers are read in groups of [myriads](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Myriad "Myriad") (10 000s), but the delimiter often separates the digits into groups of three.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

The [Indian numbering system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_numbering_system "Indian numbering system") is more complex: It groups the rightmost three digits together (until the hundreds place) and then groups digits in sets of two. For example, one trillion would be written "10,00,00,00,00,000" or "10 [_kharab_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_numbering_system "Indian numbering system")".[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#cite_note-35)

The convention for digit group separators historically varied among countries, but usually sought to distinguish the delimiter from the decimal separator. Traditionally, [English-speaking countries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English-speaking_countries "English-speaking countries") (except South Africa)[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#cite_note-36) employed commas as the delimiter – 10,000 – and other European countries employed periods (full stops) or spaces: 10.000 or 10 000. Because of the confusion that could result in international documents, in recent years, the use of spaces as separators has been advocated by the superseded [SI/ISO 31-0 standard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO_31-0#Numbers "ISO 31-0"),[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#cite_note-nist.gov-37) as well as by the BIPM and IUPAC. These groups have also begun advocating the use of a "thin space" in "groups of three".[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#cite_note-BIPM-33)[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#cite_note-old.iupac.org-34)

Within the United States, the American Medical Association's widely followed _AMA Manual of Style_ also calls for a thin space.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#cite_note-AMA10p793-31) In programming languages and online [encoding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Character_encoding "Character encoding") environments (for example, [ASCII](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ASCII "ASCII")-only languages and environments) a thin space is not practical or available. Often, either underscores[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#cite_note-38) and regular word spaces, or no delimiters at all are used instead.

### Data vis-à-vis mask

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Decimal_separator&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Data vis-à-vis mask")]

Digit group separators can occur either as part of the data or as a mask through which the data is displayed. This is an example of the [separation of presentation and content](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separation_of_presentation_and_content "Separation of presentation and content"), making it possible to display numbers in spaced groups while not inserting any [whitespace characters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whitespace_character "Whitespace character") into the string of digits that make up those numbers. In many computing contexts, it is preferred to omit the digit group separators from the data and instead overlay them as a mask (an [input mask](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Input_mask "Input mask") or an output mask).

Common examples include [spreadsheets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spreadsheet "Spreadsheet") and [databases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Database "Database"), in which currency values are entered without such marks but are displayed with them inserted. Similarly, phone numbers can have hyphens, spaces or parentheses as a mask rather than as data. In [web content](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Web_content "Web content"), digit grouping can be done with [CSS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CSS "CSS"). This is useful because the number can be copied and pasted elsewhere (such as into a calculator) and parsed by the computer as-is (i.e., without the user manually purging the extraneous characters). For example:

*   149 597 870 700 metres is 1 [astronomical unit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_unit "Astronomical unit").
*   3.14159 26535 89793 23846 is [π](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi "Pi") rounded to 20 decimal places.
*   2.71828 18284 59045 23536 is [e](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/E_(mathematical_constant) "E (mathematical constant)") rounded to 20 decimal places.

In some [programming languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Programming_language "Programming language"), it is possible to group the digits in the program's [source code](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Source_code "Source code") to make it easier to read (see: [Integer literal §Digit separators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer_literal#Digit_separators "Integer literal")). Examples include: [Ada](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ada_programming_language "Ada programming language"), [C#](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C_Sharp_(programming_language) "C Sharp (programming language)") (since version 7.0),[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#cite_note-39)[D](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D_(programming_language) "D (programming language)"), [Go](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Go_(programming_language) "Go (programming language)") (since version 1.13), [Haskell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haskell_(programming_language) "Haskell (programming language)") (from GHC version 8.6.1), [Java](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Java_(programming_language) "Java (programming language)"), [JavaScript](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/JavaScript "JavaScript") (since [ES2021](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ECMAScript_version_history#12th_Edition_%E2%80%93_ECMAScript_2021 "ECMAScript version history")), [Kotlin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kotlin_(programming_language) "Kotlin (programming language)"),[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#cite_note-40)[OCaml](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OCaml "OCaml"), [Perl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perl "Perl"), [Python](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Python_(programming_language) "Python (programming language)") (since version 3.6), [PHP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PHP "PHP") (since version 7.4),[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#cite_note-41)[Ruby](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ruby_(programming_language) "Ruby (programming language)"), [Rust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rust_(programming_language) "Rust (programming language)") and [Zig](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zig_(programming_language) "Zig (programming language)").

Java, JavaScript, Python, [Swift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swift_(programming_language) "Swift (programming language)"), [Julia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Julia_(programming_language) "Julia (programming language)") and free-form [Fortran 90](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fortran_90 "Fortran 90") use the [underscore](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Underscore "Underscore") (`_`) character for this purpose. As such, these languages would allow the number seven hundred million to be entered as "700_000_000". On the other hand, fixed-form Fortran ignores whitespace in all contexts, so "700 000 000" would be allowed. In [C++14](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C%2B%2B14 "C++14"), [Rebol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rebol "Rebol") and [Red](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_(programming_language) "Red (programming language)"), the use of an [apostrophe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apostrophe "Apostrophe") for digit grouping is allowed. Thus, "700'000'000" would be allowed in those languages.

The code shown below, written in Kotlin, illustrates the use of separators to increase readability:

val exampleNumber = 12_000_000 // twelve million

### Exceptions to digit grouping

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Decimal_separator&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Exceptions to digit grouping")]

The International Bureau of Weights and Measures states that "when there are only four digits before or after the decimal marker, it is customary not to use a space to isolate a single digit."[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#cite_note-BIPM-33) Likewise, some [manuals of style](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Style_guide "Style guide") state that thousands separators should not be used in normal text for numbers from 1000 to 9999 where no decimal fractional part is shown (or, in other words, for four-digit whole numbers), whereas others use thousands separators and others use both. For example, [APA style](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/APA_style "APA style") stipulates a thousands separator for "most figures of 1000 or more" except for page numbers, binary digits, temperatures, etc.

There are always "common-sense" country-specific exceptions to digit grouping, such as year numbers, [postal codes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postal_code "Postal code"), and ID numbers of predefined nongrouped format, which style guides usually point out.

### In non-base-10 numbering systems

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Decimal_separator&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: In non-base-10 numbering systems")]

In binary (base-2), a full space can be used between groups of four digits, corresponding to a [nibble](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nibble "Nibble"), or equivalently to a [hexadecimal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hexadecimal "Hexadecimal") digit. For integer numbers, dots are used as well to separate groups of four bits.[[b]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#cite_note-DR-DEBUG_H-42) Alternatively, binary digits may be grouped by threes, corresponding to an [octal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octal "Octal") digit. Similarly, in hexadecimal (base-16), full spaces are usually used to group digits into twos, making each group correspond to a [byte](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byte "Byte").[[c]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#cite_note-DR-DEBUG_D-43) Additionally, groups of eight bytes are often separated by a hyphen.[[c]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#cite_note-DR-DEBUG_D-43)

Influence of calculators and computers
--------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Decimal_separator&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Influence of calculators and computers")]

In countries with a decimal comma, the decimal point is also common as the "international" notation[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] because of the influence of devices, such as [electronic calculators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronic_calculator "Electronic calculator"), which use the decimal point. Most computer [operating systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operating_systems "Operating systems") allow selection of the decimal separator; programs that have been carefully [internationalized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/I18n "I18n") will follow this, but some programs ignore it and a few may even fail to operate if the setting has been changed.

Computer interfaces may be set to the Unicode international "Common locale" using `LC_NUMERIC=C` as defined at ["Unicode CLDR project"](http://cldr.unicode.org/index). _[Unicode Consortium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unicode\_Consortium "Unicode Consortium")_. Details of the current (2020) definitions may be found at ["01102-POSIX15897"](http://www.unicode.org/L2/L2001/01102-POSIX15897.htm). _[Unicode Consortium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unicode\_Consortium "Unicode Consortium")_.

### Other numeral systems

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Decimal_separator&action=edit&section=16 "Edit section: Other numeral systems")]

Unicode defines a _decimal separator key symbol_ (⎖ in hex U+2396, decimal 9110) which looks similar to the [apostrophe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apostrophe "Apostrophe"). This symbol is from [ISO/IEC 9995](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO/IEC_9995 "ISO/IEC 9995") and is intended for use on a keyboard to indicate a key that performs decimal separation.

In the [Arab world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_world "Arab world"), where [Eastern Arabic numerals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastern_Arabic_numerals "Eastern Arabic numerals") are used for writing numbers, a different character is used to separate the integer and fractional parts of numbers. It is referred to as an [Arabic decimal separator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabic_(Unicode_block) "Arabic (Unicode block)") (U+066B, rendered: ٫‎) in [Unicode](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unicode "Unicode"). An Arabic thousands separator (U+066C, rendered: ٬‎) also exists. Example: ۹٬۹۹۹٫۹۹‎ (9,999.99)

In [Persian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persian_language "Persian language"), the decimal separator is called _momayyez_. The Unicode Consortium's investigation concluded that "computer programs should render U+066B as a shortened, lowered, and possibly more slanted [slash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slash_(punctuation) "Slash (punctuation)") (٫); this should be distinguishable from the slash at the first sight." To separate [sequences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sequence "Sequence") of three digits, an Arabic thousands separator (rendered as: ٬), a Latin comma, or a [blank space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_(punctuation) "Space (punctuation)") may be used; however this is not a standard.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#cite_note-55)[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#cite_note-56)[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_marker#cite_note-57) Example: ۹٬۹۹۹٫۹۹ (9,999.99)

In [English Braille](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English_Braille "English Braille"), the decimal point, ⠨, is distinct from both the comma, ⠂, and the full stop, ⠲.
