**Dihydroxyacetone** (; **DHA**), also known as **glycerone**, is a simple [saccharide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saccharide "Saccharide") (a [triose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triose "Triose")) with formula C

3 H

6 O

3.

DHA is primarily used as an ingredient in [sunless tanning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sunless_tanning "Sunless tanning") products. It is often derived from plant sources such as [sugar beets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sugar_beet "Sugar beet") and [sugar cane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sugar_cane "Sugar cane"), and by the fermentation of [glycerin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycerin "Glycerin").

DHA is a [hygroscopic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hygroscopic "Hygroscopic") white crystalline powder. It has a sweet cooling taste and a characteristic odor. It is the simplest of all [ketoses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ketose "Ketose") and has no [chiral center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chiral_center "Chiral center"). The normal form is a [dimer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimer_(chemistry) "Dimer (chemistry)") (2,5-bis(hydroxymethyl)-1,4-dioxane-2,5-diol). The dimer slowly dissolves in water,[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihydroxyacetone#cite_note-3) whereupon it converts to the monomer. These solutions are stable at pH's between 4 and 6. In more basic solution, it degrades to brown product.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihydroxyacetone#cite_note-Levy-4)

[![Image 18: Conversion of dihydroxyacetone dimer to monomer](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/DHA_dimer_monomer.svg/250px-DHA_dimer_monomer.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:DHA_dimer_monomer.svg "Conversion of dihydroxyacetone dimer to monomer")
This skin browning effect is attributed to a [Maillard reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maillard_reaction "Maillard reaction"). DHA condenses with the [amino acid residues](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amino_acid_residues "Amino acid residues") in the protein [keratin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Keratin "Keratin"), the major component of the skin surface. When injected, no pigmentation occurs, consistent with a role for oxygen in color development.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihydroxyacetone#cite_note-Levy-4) The resulting pigments, which can be removed by abrasion, are called [melanoidins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Melanoidin "Melanoidin"). These are similar in coloration to [melanin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Melanin "Melanin"), the natural substance in the deeper skin layers which brown or "tan" from exposure to [UV rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultraviolet "Ultraviolet").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihydroxyacetone#cite_note-Garone15-5)

Its [phosphorylated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphorylation "Phosphorylation") form, [dihydroxyacetone phosphate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihydroxyacetone_phosphate "Dihydroxyacetone phosphate") (DHAP), takes part in [glycolysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycolysis "Glycolysis"), and it is an intermediate product of [fructose metabolism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fructose#Fructolysis "Fructose").

DHA may be prepared, along with [glyceraldehyde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glyceraldehyde "Glyceraldehyde"), by the mild oxidation of [glycerol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycerol "Glycerol"), for example with [hydrogen peroxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_peroxide "Hydrogen peroxide") and a [ferrous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ferrous "Ferrous") salt as [catalyst](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catalyst "Catalyst"). It can also be prepared in high yield and selectivity at room temperature from [glycerol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycerol "Glycerol") using cationic [palladium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palladium "Palladium")-based [catalysts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catalysts "Catalysts") with oxygen, air or [benzoquinone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzoquinone "Benzoquinone") acting as [co-oxidants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catalytic_cycle "Catalytic cycle").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihydroxyacetone#cite_note-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihydroxyacetone#cite_note-7)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihydroxyacetone#cite_note-8) Glyceraldehyde is a [structural isomer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Structural_isomer "Structural isomer") of dihydroxyacetone.

DHA can be derived from plants.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihydroxyacetone#cite_note-Garone15-5)

DHA is the main active ingredient in many sunless tanning skincare preparations, which may be classified as _topical sunless tanner_ or _temporary bronzers_. As opposed to bronzers containing pigment to coat the skin, DHA bronzers cause a coloration of the surface layer of the skin and do not wash off easily. Current sunless tanners are formulated into sprays, lotions, gels, mousses, and cosmetic wipes.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihydroxyacetone#cite_note-Garone15-5) Professional applied products include spray tanning booths, [airbrush](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Airbrush "Airbrush") tan applications, and hand applied lotions, gels, mousses and wipes. Lotions often last longer than sprays but may cause blotching and streaking. Mousses and gels tend to dry faster. Mousses generally contain less moisture.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihydroxyacetone#cite_note-Garone15-5)

Lotion manufacturers also produce a wide variety of sunless tanning preparations that replace DHA with natural bronzing agents such as [black walnut](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_walnut "Black walnut") shell. DHA may be used alone or combined with other tanning components such as [erythrulose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erythrulose "Erythrulose"). DHA is considered the most effective sun-free tanning additive.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

[Sunless tanning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sunless_tanning "Sunless tanning") products contain DHA in concentrations ranging from 1% to 20%. Most drugstore products range from 3% to 5%, with professional products ranging from 5% to 20%. The percentages correspond with the product coloration levels from light to dark. Lighter products are more beginner-friendly, but may require multiple coats to produce the desired color depth. Darker products produce a dark tan in one coat, but are also more prone to streaking, unevenness, or off-color tones. The artificial tan takes 2 to 4 hours to begin appearing on the skin surface, and will continue to darken for 24 to 72 hours, depending on formulation type.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihydroxyacetone#cite_note-Garone15-5)

Once the darkening effect has occurred, the tan will not sweat off or wash away with soap or water. It will fade gradually over 3 to 7 days as a result of the regular skin exfoliation. Scrubbing the skin, prolonged water submersion, or heavy sweating can lighten the tan, as these all contribute to rapid dead skin cell exfoliation (the dead skin cells are the tinted portion of the sunless tan).[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihydroxyacetone#cite_note-Garone15-5)

It is recommended to shave, wash, or [exfoliate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exfoliation_(cosmetology) "Exfoliation (cosmetology)") the skin before application in order to produce a more even tan. The skin should not be moisturized before application except very dry areas. Application should be done evenly with a circular motion.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihydroxyacetone#cite_note-Garone15-5)

Bronzers contain DHA in concentrations from 1 to 15% allowing to adjust the intensity of coloration. DHA formulations have improved in several ways since the introduction in the 1960s.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihydroxyacetone#cite_note-Garone15-5)

### Safe use and side effects

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Dihydroxyacetone&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Safe use and side effects")]

The [United States Food and Drug Administration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Food_and_Drug_Administration "United States Food and Drug Administration") (FDA) has approved DHA for topical application on the skin, but not for contact with eyes, lips, and [mucous membranes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mucous_membrane "Mucous membrane"). DHA may not be used in spray tanning booths due to risk of exposure of these sensitive areas.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihydroxyacetone#cite_note-Garone15-5)

DHA-containing spray tans have been associated to side effects such as rashes, cough, dizziness, and fainting. Frequent exposure to spray tans may increase the risk of pulmonary disease, asthma, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), and cancer. [[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihydroxyacetone#cite_note-Garone15-5)

The chemical reaction of DHA in the skin may lead to an unpleasant smell. Fragrances are often added to the formulation to mask the smell, which may lead to allergic reactions or worsen asthma symptoms. Some products contain [parabens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parabens "Parabens") as chemical preservatives that can cause [rosacea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rosacea "Rosacea") and [allergic contact dermatitis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allergic_contact_dermatitis "Allergic contact dermatitis") and act as weak [estrogens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Estrogen "Estrogen").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihydroxyacetone#cite_note-Garone15-5)

Frequent use of DHA containing products may to be problematic due to a number of side effects. A FDA report concluded that 11 percent of the applied DHA may penetrate into the living cells of the epidermis and dermis. A previous study linked DHA to DNA damage, cell-cycle block, and [apoptosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apoptosis "Apoptosis") in living cells. Skin coloring induced by frequent use of DHA may interfere with vitamin D production in the skin cells which depends on the exposure to UVB in sunlight. Topical DHA use in levels of more than 5% seem to increase free-radical damage from sunlight for 24 hours after application. Antioxidants may be added to the formulation to produce a more natural tan, and may potentially counteract free radical damage. The use of sunless tanning products may increase the likelihood of sunburn as the artificial coloration of the skin interferes with the natural adaption of the skin to sun exposure. The tanning induced by DHA has been rated with a low sun protection factor (SPF) of 3. It can therefore not replace the application of a regular [sunscreen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sunscreen "Sunscreen") before exposing the skin to intensive sunlight.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihydroxyacetone#cite_note-Garone15-5)

[Erythrulose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erythrulose "Erythrulose") is a similar agent contained in red [raspberries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raspberries "Raspberries") that produces a short-lived reddish tan. The general tanning effect is usually improved when used in combination with DHA. Erythrulose also increases the occurrence of free radicals.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihydroxyacetone#cite_note-Garone15-5)

DHA may be contained in [moisturizers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moisturizer "Moisturizer") with added fruit extracts sold as "maximizers". Tanning agents labelled as "tingles" contain [benzyl nicotinate](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Benzyl_nicotinate&action=edit&redlink=1 "Benzyl nicotinate (page does not exist)") which presumably aids the production of [melanin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Melanin "Melanin") by increasing blood circulation in the skin, thus supplying more oxygen to melanocytes, and often induces a burning or tingling feeling. The effect of products called "optimizers" or "accelerators" containing the protein [tyrosine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tyrosine "Tyrosine") as a precursor in the production of melanin is disputed.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihydroxyacetone#cite_note-Garone15-5)

DHA was first recognized as a skin coloring agent by German scientists in the 1920s. Through its use in the [X-ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray "X-ray") process, it was noted as causing the skin surface to turn brown when spilled.

In the 1950s, Eva Wittgenstein at the University of Cincinnati did further research with dihydroxyacetone.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihydroxyacetone#cite_note-9)[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihydroxyacetone#cite_note-10)[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihydroxyacetone#cite_note-11)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihydroxyacetone#cite_note-12) Her studies involved using DHA as an oral drug for assisting children with [glycogen storage disease](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycogen_storage_disease "Glycogen storage disease"). The children received large doses of DHA by mouth, and sometimes spat or spilled the substance onto their skin. Healthcare workers noticed that the skin turned brown after a few hours of DHA exposure. Wittgenstein continued to experiment with DHA, painting liquid solutions of it onto her own skin. She was able to consistently reproduce the [pigmentation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pigmentation "Pigmentation") effect, and noted that DHA did not appear to penetrate beyond the [stratum corneum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stratum_corneum "Stratum corneum"), or dead skin surface layer (the FDA eventually concluded this is not entirely true[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihydroxyacetone#cite_note-abcnews.go.com-13)). Research then continued on DHA's skin coloring effect in relation to treatment for patients with [vitiligo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vitiligo "Vitiligo").

[Coppertone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coppertone_(sunscreen) "Coppertone (sunscreen)") introduced the first consumer [sunless tanning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sunless_tanning "Sunless tanning") lotion into the marketplace in the 1960s. This product was called "Quick Tan" or "QT". It was sold as an overnight tanning agent, and other companies followed suit with similar products. Consumers soon tired of this product due to unattractive results such as orange palms, streaking and poor coloration. Because of the QT experience, many people still associate sunless tanning with fake-looking orange tans.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

In the 1970s the [United States Food and Drug Administration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Food_and_Drug_Administration_(United_States) "Food and Drug Administration (United States)") (FDA) added DHA permanently to their list of approved cosmetic ingredients.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihydroxyacetone#cite_note-14)

By the 1980s, new sunless tanning formulations appeared on the market and refinements in the DHA manufacturing process created products that produced a more natural looking color and better fading. Consumer concerns surrounding damage associated with UV tanning options ([sunbathing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sunbathing "Sunbathing"), [indoor tanning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indoor_tanning "Indoor tanning")) spurred popularity of sunless tanning products as an alternative to UV tanning. Dozens of brands appeared on drugstore shelves, in numerous formulations.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

Both [acetic acid bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid_bacteria "Acetic acid bacteria")_[Acetobacter aceti](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetobacter\_aceti "Acetobacter aceti")_ and _[Gluconobacter oxydans](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Gluconobacter\_oxydans&action=edit&redlink=1 "Gluconobacter oxydans (page does not exist)")_ use [glycerol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycerol "Glycerol") as a carbon source to form dihydroxyacetone. DHA is formed by [ketogenesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ketogenesis "Ketogenesis") of glycerol.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihydroxyacetone#cite_note-Drysdale1988-15) It can affect the sensory quality of the wine with sweet/etherish properties. DHA can also react with [proline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proline "Proline") to produce a "crust-like" aroma.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihydroxyacetone#cite_note-Drysdale1988-15)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihydroxyacetone#cite_note-16)[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihydroxyacetone#cite_note-17) Dihydroxyacetone can affect the anti-microbial activity in wine, as it has the ability to bind [SO 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide "Sulfur dioxide").[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihydroxyacetone#cite_note-18)
