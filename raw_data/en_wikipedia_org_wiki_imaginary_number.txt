An **imaginary number** is the product of a [real number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number") and the [imaginary unit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imaginary_unit "Imaginary unit")i,[[note 1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imaginary_number#cite_note-1) which is defined by its property _i_ 2 = −1.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imaginary_number#cite_note-2)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imaginary_number#cite_note-3) The [square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_(algebra) "Square (algebra)") of an imaginary number bi is −_b_ 2. For example, 5 _i_ is an imaginary number, and its square is −25. The number [zero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/0 "0") is considered to be both real and imaginary.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imaginary_number#cite_note-4)

Originally coined in the 17th century by [René Descartes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Descartes "René Descartes")[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imaginary_number#cite_note-5) as a derogatory term and regarded as fictitious or useless, the concept gained wide acceptance following the work of [Leonhard Euler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leonhard_Euler "Leonhard Euler") (in the 18th century) and [Augustin-Louis Cauchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Augustin-Louis_Cauchy "Augustin-Louis Cauchy") and [Carl Friedrich Gauss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Friedrich_Gauss "Carl Friedrich Gauss") (in the early 19th century).

An imaginary number _bi_ can be added to a real number a to form a [complex number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number") of the form _a_ + _bi_, where the real numbers a and b are called, respectively, the _real part_ and the _imaginary part_ of the complex number.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imaginary_number#cite_note-6)

[![Image 13](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Complex_conjugate_picture.svg/250px-Complex_conjugate_picture.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Complex_conjugate_picture.svg)

An illustration of the complex plane. The imaginary numbers are on the vertical coordinate axis.

Although the Greek [mathematician](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematician "Mathematician") and [engineer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineer "Engineer")[Heron of Alexandria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heron_of_Alexandria "Heron of Alexandria") is noted as the first to present a calculation involving the square root of a negative number,[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imaginary_number#cite_note-7)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imaginary_number#cite_note-8) it was [Rafael Bombelli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rafael_Bombelli "Rafael Bombelli") who first set down the rules for multiplication of [complex numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number") in 1572. The concept had appeared in print earlier, such as in work by [Gerolamo Cardano](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gerolamo_Cardano "Gerolamo Cardano"). At the time, imaginary numbers and negative numbers were poorly understood and were regarded by some as fictitious or useless, much as zero once was. Many other mathematicians were slow to adopt the use of imaginary numbers, including [René Descartes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Descartes "René Descartes"), who wrote about them in his _[La Géométrie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/La\_G%C3%A9om%C3%A9trie "La Géométrie")_ in which he coined the term _imaginary_ and meant it to be derogatory.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imaginary_number#cite_note-9)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imaginary_number#cite_note-Martinez-10) The use of imaginary numbers was not widely accepted until the work of [Leonhard Euler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leonhard_Euler "Leonhard Euler") (1707–1783) and [Carl Friedrich Gauss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Friedrich_Gauss "Carl Friedrich Gauss") (1777–1855). The geometric significance of complex numbers as points in a plane was first described by [Caspar Wessel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caspar_Wessel "Caspar Wessel") (1745–1818).[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imaginary_number#cite_note-11)

In 1843, [William Rowan Hamilton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Rowan_Hamilton "William Rowan Hamilton") extended the idea of an axis of imaginary numbers in the plane to a four-dimensional space of [quaternion imaginaries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quaternion#Definition "Quaternion") in which three of the dimensions are analogous to the imaginary numbers in the complex field.

Geometric interpretation
------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Imaginary_number&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Geometric interpretation")]

[![Image 14](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Rotations_on_the_complex_plane.svg/250px-Rotations_on_the_complex_plane.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Rotations_on_the_complex_plane.svg)

90-degree rotations in the [complex plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_plane "Complex plane")

Geometrically, imaginary numbers are found on the vertical axis of the [complex number plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_plane "Complex plane"), which allows them to be presented [perpendicular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular "Perpendicular") to the real axis. One way of viewing imaginary numbers is to consider a standard [number line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_line "Number line") positively increasing in magnitude to the right and negatively increasing in magnitude to the left. At 0 on the x-axis, a y-axis can be drawn with "positive" direction going up; "positive" imaginary numbers then increase in magnitude upwards, and "negative" imaginary numbers increase in magnitude downwards. This vertical axis is often called the "imaginary axis"[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imaginary_number#cite_note-Meier-12) and is denoted ![Image 15: {\displaystyle i\mathbb {R} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4101e29dac4163483908e013cb36da367ff57077)![Image 16: {\displaystyle \mathbb {I} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b9a597bf0d4ea70e457a2cab15df7975d456d977) or ℑ.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imaginary_number#cite_note-13)

In this representation, multiplication by i corresponds to a counterclockwise [rotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation "Rotation") of 90 degrees about the origin, which is a quarter of a circle. Multiplication by−_i_ corresponds to a clockwise rotation of 90 degrees about the origin. Similarly, multiplying by a purely imaginary number bi, with b a real number, both causes a counterclockwise rotation about the origin by 90 degrees and scales the answer by a factor of b. When _b_< 0, this can instead be described as a clockwise rotation by 90 degrees and a scaling by |_b_|.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imaginary_number#cite_note-14)

Square roots of negative numbers
--------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Imaginary_number&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Square roots of negative numbers")]

Care must be used when working with imaginary numbers that are expressed as the [principal values](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principal_value "Principal value") of the [square roots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_root "Square root") of [negative numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Negative_number "Negative number").[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imaginary_number#cite_note-15) For example, if x and y are both positive real numbers, the following chain of equalities appears reasonable at first glance:

![Image 17: {\displaystyle \textstyle {\sqrt {x\cdot y{\vphantom {t}}}}={\sqrt {(-x)\cdot (-y)}}\mathrel {\stackrel {\text{ (fallacy) }}{=}} {\sqrt {-x{\vphantom {ty}}}}\cdot {\sqrt {-y{\vphantom {ty}}}}=i{\sqrt {x{\vphantom {ty}}}}\cdot i{\sqrt {y{\vphantom {ty}}}}=-{\sqrt {x\cdot y{\vphantom {ty}}}}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/025a42201c002a69c9ba688f02db44a32cca020a)
But the result is clearly nonsense. The step where the square root was broken apart was illegitimate. (See [Mathematical fallacy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_fallacy "Mathematical fallacy").)
