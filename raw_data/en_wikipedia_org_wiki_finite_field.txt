In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), a **finite field** or **Galois field** (so-named in honor of [Évariste Galois](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%89variste_Galois "Évariste Galois")) is a [field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)") that has a [finite number of elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_set "Finite set"). As with any field, a finite field is a [set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_(mathematics) "Set (mathematics)") on which the operations of multiplication, addition, subtraction and division are defined and satisfy certain basic rules. The most common examples of finite fields are the [integers mod ![Image 1: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integers_mod_n "Integers mod n") when ![Image 2: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) is a [prime number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_number "Prime number").

The _order_ of a finite field is its number of elements, which is either a prime number or a [prime power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_power "Prime power"). For every prime number ![Image 3: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) and every positive integer ![Image 4: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40) there are fields of order ![Image 5: {\displaystyle p^{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e017c102135ab13bdf501dc1c1b5fd1840a97822). All finite fields of a given order are [isomorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isomorphism "Isomorphism").

Finite fields are fundamental in a number of areas of mathematics and [computer science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_science "Computer science"), including [number theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_theory "Number theory"), [algebraic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_geometry "Algebraic geometry"), [Galois theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galois_theory "Galois theory"), [finite geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_geometry "Finite geometry"), [cryptography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cryptography "Cryptography") and [coding theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coding_theory "Coding theory").

A finite field is a [field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)") that is a [finite set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_set "Finite set"); this means that it has a finite number of elements on which multiplication, addition, subtraction and division (excluding division by zero) are defined and satisfy the [field axioms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_axioms "Field axioms").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_field#cite_note-:0-1)

The number of elements of a finite field is called its _order_ or, sometimes, its _size_. A finite field of order ![Image 6: {\displaystyle q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/06809d64fa7c817ffc7e323f85997f783dbdf71d) exists if and only if ![Image 7: {\displaystyle q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/06809d64fa7c817ffc7e323f85997f783dbdf71d) is a [prime power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_power "Prime power")![Image 8: {\displaystyle p^{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e017c102135ab13bdf501dc1c1b5fd1840a97822) (where ![Image 9: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) is a prime number and ![Image 10: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40) is a positive integer). In a field of order ![Image 11: {\displaystyle p^{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e017c102135ab13bdf501dc1c1b5fd1840a97822), adding ![Image 12: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) copies of any element always results in zero; that is, the [characteristic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Characteristic_(algebra) "Characteristic (algebra)") of the field is ![Image 13: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36).[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_field#cite_note-:0-1)

For ![Image 14: {\displaystyle q=p^{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9535c3e1dfaf62cc46386c4a0d8aabf56b40699a), all fields of order ![Image 15: {\displaystyle q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/06809d64fa7c817ffc7e323f85997f783dbdf71d) are [isomorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isomorphic "Isomorphic") (see _[§Existence and uniqueness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite\_field#Existence\_and\_uniqueness)_ below).[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_field#cite_note-moore-2) Moreover, a field cannot contain two different finite [subfields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_extension "Field extension") with the same order. One may therefore identify all finite fields with the same order, and they are unambiguously denoted ![Image 16: {\displaystyle \mathbb {F} _{q}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dbb96e056c071d13fc7702013f9273e7f5cd88a7), ![Image 17: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} _{q}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d56b782043e983a17e7221c01e349b0c8232e32c) or ![Image 18: {\displaystyle \mathrm {GF} (q)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b5668d264af0b08b75fef8f823e6588c68c25a3e), where the letters GF stand for "Galois field".[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_field#cite_note-3)

In a finite field of order ![Image 19: {\displaystyle q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/06809d64fa7c817ffc7e323f85997f783dbdf71d), the [polynomial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial "Polynomial")![Image 20: {\displaystyle X^{q}-X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/66ab5e3894b0bea4ae4ba01072f5ac2816ff34f1) has all ![Image 21: {\displaystyle q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/06809d64fa7c817ffc7e323f85997f783dbdf71d) elements of the finite field as [roots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Root_of_a_polynomial "Root of a polynomial"). The non-zero elements of a finite field form a [multiplicative group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplicative_group "Multiplicative group"). This group is [cyclic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclic_group "Cyclic group"), so all non-zero elements can be expressed as powers of a single element called a [primitive element](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primitive_element_(finite_field) "Primitive element (finite field)") of the field. (In general there will be several primitive elements for a given field.)[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_field#cite_note-:0-1)

The simplest examples of finite fields are the fields of prime order: for each [prime number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_number "Prime number")![Image 22: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36), the [prime field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_field "Prime field") of order ![Image 23: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) may be constructed as the [integers modulo ![Image 24: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modular_arithmetic "Modular arithmetic"), ![Image 25: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} /p\mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/57869a3a3c4c431cc49c4c7ab1d9c7ea692b517b).[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_field#cite_note-:0-1)

The elements of the prime field of order ![Image 26: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) may be represented by integers in the range ![Image 27: {\displaystyle 0,\ldots ,p-1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/698e393515ea92da237000a1bf6fce26a341e3c9). The sum, the difference and the product are the [remainder of the division](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_division "Euclidean division") by ![Image 28: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) of the result of the corresponding integer operation. The multiplicative inverse of an element may be computed by using the [extended Euclidean algorithm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extended_Euclidean_algorithm "Extended Euclidean algorithm") (see _[Extended Euclidean algorithm §Modular integers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extended\_Euclidean\_algorithm#Modular\_integers "Extended Euclidean algorithm")_).[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_field#cite_note-:0-1)

Let ![Image 29: {\displaystyle F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/545fd099af8541605f7ee55f08225526be88ce57) be a finite field. For any element ![Image 30: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) in ![Image 31: {\displaystyle F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/545fd099af8541605f7ee55f08225526be88ce57) and any [integer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer")![Image 32: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b), denote by ![Image 33: {\displaystyle n\cdot x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9c862c3f6a040c95d3b60af8d71c305e7ff0fb4e) the sum of ![Image 34: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) copies of ![Image 35: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4). The least positive ![Image 36: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) such that ![Image 37: {\displaystyle n\cdot 1=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/228126e9061d7a131eb389730c97b966290850a0) is the characteristic ![Image 38: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) of the field. This allows defining a multiplication ![Image 39: {\displaystyle (k,x)\mapsto k\cdot x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3dd1f5643a043d2e07f4269e1fc177d1479fe219) of an element ![Image 40: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40) of ![Image 41: {\displaystyle \mathrm {GF} (p)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1dcb6720c421397627afb8ecce23a98b152d432f) by an element ![Image 42: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) of ![Image 43: {\displaystyle F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/545fd099af8541605f7ee55f08225526be88ce57) by choosing an integer representative for ![Image 44: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40). This multiplication makes ![Image 45: {\displaystyle F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/545fd099af8541605f7ee55f08225526be88ce57) into a ![Image 46: {\displaystyle \mathrm {GF} (p)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1dcb6720c421397627afb8ecce23a98b152d432f)-[vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space") It follows that the number of elements of ![Image 47: {\displaystyle F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/545fd099af8541605f7ee55f08225526be88ce57) is ![Image 48: {\displaystyle p^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c6a7a7e74ae90ab94f01e1629177758fb68b423b) for some integer ![Image 49: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b).[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_field#cite_note-:0-1)

The [identity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Identity_(mathematics) "Identity (mathematics)")![Image 50: {\displaystyle (x+y)^{p}=x^{p}+y^{p}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ac9c35247ed004b6fe4b3b4ca807e0e558a8a94f) (sometimes called the [freshman's dream](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freshman%27s_dream "Freshman's dream")[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_field#cite_note-4)) is true in a field of characteristic ![Image 51: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36). This follows from the [binomial theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binomial_theorem "Binomial theorem"), as each [binomial coefficient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binomial_coefficient "Binomial coefficient") of the expansion of ![Image 52: {\displaystyle (x+y)^{p}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fdcc6069ec6957bcf6865bcf289bf723dcf6bc94), except the first and the last, is a multiple of ![Image 53: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36).[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_field#cite_note-:0-1): 548

By [Fermat's little theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermat%27s_little_theorem "Fermat's little theorem"), if ![Image 54: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) is a prime number and ![Image 55: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) is in the field ![Image 56: {\displaystyle \mathrm {GF} (p)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1dcb6720c421397627afb8ecce23a98b152d432f) then ![Image 57: {\displaystyle x^{p}=x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/da460092643abfe81093662660377ef0a133b85a). This implies the equality ![Image 58: {\displaystyle X^{p}-X=\prod _{a\in \mathrm {GF} (p)}(X-a)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/32be60f2de3f95c7bd866a74d12b29da14e3f096) for polynomials over ![Image 59: {\displaystyle \mathrm {GF} (p)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1dcb6720c421397627afb8ecce23a98b152d432f). More generally, every element in ![Image 60: {\displaystyle \mathrm {GF} (p^{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf2eb1a39dba2417e062d29df7f1aea13dff6c9d) satisfies the polynomial equation ![Image 61: {\displaystyle x^{p^{n}}-x=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/838a8ef8d292f2a687fc3faf6228023904a7cf0f).[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

Any finite [field extension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_extension "Field extension") of a finite field is [separable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separable_extension "Separable extension") and simple. That is, if ![Image 62: {\displaystyle E}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4232c9de2ee3eec0a9c0a19b15ab92daa6223f9b) is a finite field and ![Image 63: {\displaystyle F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/545fd099af8541605f7ee55f08225526be88ce57) is a subfield of ![Image 64: {\displaystyle E}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4232c9de2ee3eec0a9c0a19b15ab92daa6223f9b), then ![Image 65: {\displaystyle E}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4232c9de2ee3eec0a9c0a19b15ab92daa6223f9b) is obtained from ![Image 66: {\displaystyle F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/545fd099af8541605f7ee55f08225526be88ce57) by adjoining a single element whose [minimal polynomial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minimal_polynomial_(field_theory) "Minimal polynomial (field theory)") is [separable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separable_polynomial "Separable polynomial"). To use a piece of jargon, finite fields are [perfect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perfect_field "Perfect field").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_field#cite_note-:0-1)

A more general [algebraic structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_structure "Algebraic structure") that satisfies all the other axioms of a field, but whose multiplication is not required to be [commutative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_property "Commutative property"), is called a [division ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_ring "Division ring") (or sometimes _skew field_). By [Wedderburn's little theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wedderburn%27s_little_theorem "Wedderburn's little theorem"), any finite division ring is commutative, and hence is a finite field.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_field#cite_note-:0-1)

Existence and uniqueness
------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Finite_field&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Existence and uniqueness")]

Let ![Image 67: {\displaystyle q=p^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7b95f6525025c9ae1432ea9bd50c3c1cab4b7c6c) be a [prime power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_power "Prime power"), and ![Image 68: {\displaystyle F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/545fd099af8541605f7ee55f08225526be88ce57) be the [splitting field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Splitting_field "Splitting field") of the polynomial ![Image 69: {\displaystyle P=X^{q}-X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0c1cd4ae6f42df843c85a8f7a92970c6b57b7356) over the prime field ![Image 70: {\displaystyle \mathrm {GF} (p)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1dcb6720c421397627afb8ecce23a98b152d432f). This means that ![Image 71: {\displaystyle F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/545fd099af8541605f7ee55f08225526be88ce57) is a finite field of lowest order, in which ![Image 72: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a) has ![Image 73: {\displaystyle q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/06809d64fa7c817ffc7e323f85997f783dbdf71d) distinct roots (the [formal derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formal_derivative "Formal derivative") of ![Image 74: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a) is ![Image 75: {\displaystyle P'=-1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3ab886a566e6e148fed52992e73f5b27d5cc45f6), implying that ![Image 76: {\displaystyle \mathrm {gcd} (P,P')=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e4d6321bb2c6707b2b9729219a97658ce1823a8d), which in general implies that the splitting field is a [separable extension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separable_extension "Separable extension") of the original). The [above identity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_field#powersum) shows that the sum and the product of two roots of ![Image 77: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a) are roots of ![Image 78: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a), as well as the multiplicative inverse of a root of ![Image 79: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a). In other words, the roots of ![Image 80: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a) form a field of order ![Image 81: {\displaystyle q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/06809d64fa7c817ffc7e323f85997f783dbdf71d), which is equal to ![Image 82: {\displaystyle F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/545fd099af8541605f7ee55f08225526be88ce57) by the minimality of the splitting field.

The uniqueness up to isomorphism of splitting fields implies thus that all fields of order ![Image 83: {\displaystyle q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/06809d64fa7c817ffc7e323f85997f783dbdf71d) are isomorphic. Also, if a field ![Image 84: {\displaystyle F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/545fd099af8541605f7ee55f08225526be88ce57) has a field of order ![Image 85: {\displaystyle q=p^{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9535c3e1dfaf62cc46386c4a0d8aabf56b40699a) as a subfield, its elements are the ![Image 86: {\displaystyle q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/06809d64fa7c817ffc7e323f85997f783dbdf71d) roots of ![Image 87: {\displaystyle X^{q}-X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/66ab5e3894b0bea4ae4ba01072f5ac2816ff34f1), and ![Image 88: {\displaystyle F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/545fd099af8541605f7ee55f08225526be88ce57) cannot contain another subfield of order ![Image 89: {\displaystyle q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/06809d64fa7c817ffc7e323f85997f783dbdf71d).

In summary, we have the following classification theorem first proved in 1893 by [E.H.Moore](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/E._H._Moore "E. H. Moore"):[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_field#cite_note-moore-2)

> The order of a finite field is a prime power. For every prime power ![Image 90: {\displaystyle q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/06809d64fa7c817ffc7e323f85997f783dbdf71d) there are fields of order ![Image 91: {\displaystyle q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/06809d64fa7c817ffc7e323f85997f783dbdf71d), and they are all isomorphic. In these fields, every element satisfies ![Image 92: {\displaystyle x^{q}=x,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b2b801980e4e4e1e4f6e339b001ac263933e1400) and the polynomial ![Image 93: {\displaystyle X^{q}-X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/66ab5e3894b0bea4ae4ba01072f5ac2816ff34f1) factors as ![Image 94: {\displaystyle X^{q}-X=\prod _{a\in F}(X-a).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5363b844e3f482ec46bf6669f7bafa9ec54237ba)

It follows that ![Image 95: {\displaystyle \mathrm {GF} (p^{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf2eb1a39dba2417e062d29df7f1aea13dff6c9d) contains a subfield isomorphic to ![Image 96: {\displaystyle \mathrm {GF} (p^{m})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c10b4cca67765531b15e118a318002ba763580ea) if and only if ![Image 97: {\displaystyle m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a07d98bb302f3856cbabc47b2b9016692e3f7bc) is a divisor of ![Image 98: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b); in that case, this subfield is unique. In fact, the polynomial ![Image 99: {\displaystyle X^{p^{m}}-X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bd700d16cab00293fc0cc780ca1da93f2c485317) divides ![Image 100: {\displaystyle X^{p^{n}}-X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/26c5444ae298745e11c69beb75e5308379f41ec6) if and only if ![Image 101: {\displaystyle m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a07d98bb302f3856cbabc47b2b9016692e3f7bc) is a divisor of ![Image 102: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b).

Explicit construction
---------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Finite_field&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Explicit construction")]

Given a prime power ![Image 103: {\displaystyle q=p^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7b95f6525025c9ae1432ea9bd50c3c1cab4b7c6c) with ![Image 104: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) prime and ![Image 105: {\displaystyle n>1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ee74e1cc07e7041edf0fcbd4481f5cd32ad17b64), the field ![Image 106: {\displaystyle \mathrm {GF} (q)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b5668d264af0b08b75fef8f823e6588c68c25a3e) may be explicitly constructed in the following way. One first chooses an [irreducible polynomial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irreducible_polynomial "Irreducible polynomial")![Image 107: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a) in ![Image 108: {\displaystyle \mathrm {GF} (p)[X]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6d261049065497b38d7d18f92481109a4b5c1d1b) of degree ![Image 109: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) (such an irreducible polynomial always exists). Then the [quotient ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quotient_ring "Quotient ring")![Image 110: {\displaystyle \mathrm {GF} (q)=\mathrm {GF} (p)[X]/(P)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1c99172a80603d0f3b3ce895ea3e499e0cd4ff79) of the polynomial ring ![Image 111: {\displaystyle \mathrm {GF} (p)[X]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6d261049065497b38d7d18f92481109a4b5c1d1b) by the ideal generated by ![Image 112: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a) is a field of order ![Image 113: {\displaystyle q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/06809d64fa7c817ffc7e323f85997f783dbdf71d).

More explicitly, the elements of ![Image 114: {\displaystyle \mathrm {GF} (q)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b5668d264af0b08b75fef8f823e6588c68c25a3e) are the polynomials over ![Image 115: {\displaystyle \mathrm {GF} (p)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1dcb6720c421397627afb8ecce23a98b152d432f) whose degree is strictly less than ![Image 116: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b). The addition and the subtraction are those of polynomials over ![Image 117: {\displaystyle \mathrm {GF} (p)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1dcb6720c421397627afb8ecce23a98b152d432f). The product of two elements is the remainder of the [Euclidean division](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_division_of_polynomials "Euclidean division of polynomials") by ![Image 118: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a) of the product in ![Image 119: {\displaystyle \mathrm {GF} (q)[X]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0e4589d72f51153fe7f6bc15aaf70453b0e1d146). The multiplicative inverse of a non-zero element may be computed with the extended Euclidean algorithm; see _[Extended Euclidean algorithm §Simple algebraic field extensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extended\_Euclidean\_algorithm#Simple\_algebraic\_field\_extensions "Extended Euclidean algorithm")_.

However, with this representation, elements of ![Image 120: {\displaystyle \mathrm {GF} (q)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b5668d264af0b08b75fef8f823e6588c68c25a3e) may be difficult to distinguish from the corresponding polynomials. Therefore, it is common to give a name, commonly ![Image 121: {\displaystyle \alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b79333175c8b3f0840bfb4ec41b8072c83ea88d3) to the element of ![Image 122: {\displaystyle \mathrm {GF} (q)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b5668d264af0b08b75fef8f823e6588c68c25a3e) that corresponds to the polynomial ![Image 123: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab). So, the elements of ![Image 124: {\displaystyle \mathrm {GF} (q)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b5668d264af0b08b75fef8f823e6588c68c25a3e) become polynomials in ![Image 125: {\displaystyle \alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b79333175c8b3f0840bfb4ec41b8072c83ea88d3), where ![Image 126: {\displaystyle P(\alpha )=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c47730d546c54586d0538f7d6ed04a00dd6f025d), and, when one encounters a polynomial in ![Image 127: {\displaystyle \alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b79333175c8b3f0840bfb4ec41b8072c83ea88d3) of degree greater or equal to ![Image 128: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) (for example after a multiplication), one knows that one has to use the relation ![Image 129: {\displaystyle P(\alpha )=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c47730d546c54586d0538f7d6ed04a00dd6f025d) to reduce its degree (it is what Euclidean division is doing).

Except in the construction of ![Image 130: {\displaystyle \mathrm {GF} (4)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b7ab0eeaa1fed58ae8bc125cb3d109b9520e075), there are several possible choices for ![Image 131: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a), which produce isomorphic results. To simplify the Euclidean division, one commonly chooses for ![Image 132: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a) a polynomial of the form ![Image 133: {\displaystyle X^{n}+aX+b,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/226c533f09e7810951d53edb6e6c2f7ce32aefc6) which make the needed Euclidean divisions very efficient. However, for some fields, typically in characteristic ![Image 134: {\displaystyle 2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/901fc910c19990d0dbaaefe4726ceb1a4e217a0f), irreducible polynomials of the form ![Image 135: {\displaystyle X^{n}+aX+b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ed2af5dd839c6a65dea1c34ea3bb4527a27da429) may not exist. In characteristic ![Image 136: {\displaystyle 2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/901fc910c19990d0dbaaefe4726ceb1a4e217a0f), if the polynomial ![Image 137: {\displaystyle X^{n}+X+1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/15e7f4909a9db9a44e3f8cf739eeef4f6fad4e87) is reducible, it is recommended to choose ![Image 138: {\displaystyle X^{n}+X^{k}+1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/82e2cf1b47ae2a85319641f19a472b0148bc2431) with the lowest possible ![Image 139: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40) that makes the polynomial irreducible. If all these [trinomials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trinomial "Trinomial") are reducible, one chooses "pentanomials" ![Image 140: {\displaystyle X^{n}+X^{a}+X^{b}+X^{c}+1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/48f885daafaa4b3844be9d6f6c3fd7909e1e9e14), as polynomials of degree greater than ![Image 141: {\displaystyle 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/92d98b82a3778f043108d4e20960a9193df57cbf), with an even number of terms, are never irreducible in characteristic ![Image 142: {\displaystyle 2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/901fc910c19990d0dbaaefe4726ceb1a4e217a0f), having ![Image 143: {\displaystyle 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/92d98b82a3778f043108d4e20960a9193df57cbf) as a root.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_field#cite_note-5)

A possible choice for such a polynomial is given by [Conway polynomials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conway_polynomial_(finite_fields) "Conway polynomial (finite fields)"). They ensure a certain compatibility between the representation of a field and the representations of its subfields.

In the next sections, we will show how the general construction method outlined above works for small finite fields.

### Field with four elements

The smallest non-prime field is the field with four elements, which is commonly denoted ![Image 144: {\displaystyle \mathrm {GF} (4)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b7ab0eeaa1fed58ae8bc125cb3d109b9520e075) or ![Image 145: {\displaystyle \mathbb {F} _{4}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2e60469aa744d02503eddc8d6a0c48ec2accdee3) It consists of the four elements ![Image 146: {\displaystyle 0,1,\alpha ,1+\alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e4c6748a5ca2a630c9ba8884fed3edff19e9d7bf) such that ![Image 147: {\displaystyle \alpha ^{2}=1+\alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e0421d6b72277c4dab5ece1113c8db36cfb0be06), ![Image 148: {\displaystyle 1\cdot \alpha =\alpha \cdot 1=\alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6403e649e776a0527642b4921fda86f709e4242a), ![Image 149: {\displaystyle x+x=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ba7770d8452d80ee8c0b5d092ef3379781bd474b), and ![Image 150: {\displaystyle x\cdot 0=0\cdot x=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/41f9bfcabe34e28ebdf940425d865f6c3bcbaaee), for every ![Image 151: {\displaystyle x\in \mathrm {GF} (4)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c7b74b162916cfdbc5e00b6b9ec0ae3f0f0196a7), the other operation results being easily deduced from the [distributive law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distributive_law "Distributive law"). ### GF(_p_ 2) for an odd prime _p_

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Finite_field&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: GF(p2) for an odd prime p")]

For applying the [above general construction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_field#Non-prime_fields) of finite fields in the case of ![Image 180: {\displaystyle \mathrm {GF} (p^{2})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f905f08a3dcf98d020992a1b9de240087a03f79e), one has to find an irreducible polynomial of degree 2. For ![Image 181: {\displaystyle p=2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d62e4100b94c1939c67f2d4b8580d26c78106c44), this has been done in the preceding section. If ![Image 182: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) is an odd prime, there are always irreducible polynomials of the form ![Image 183: {\displaystyle X^{2}-r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/983420d8f7564cac9acf7657a4e34057e8213647), with ![Image 184: {\displaystyle r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d1ecb613aa2984f0576f70f86650b7c2a132538) in ![Image 185: {\displaystyle \mathrm {GF} (p)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1dcb6720c421397627afb8ecce23a98b152d432f).

More precisely, the polynomial ![Image 186: {\displaystyle X^{2}-r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/983420d8f7564cac9acf7657a4e34057e8213647) is irreducible over ![Image 187: {\displaystyle \mathrm {GF} (p)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1dcb6720c421397627afb8ecce23a98b152d432f) if and only if ![Image 188: {\displaystyle r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d1ecb613aa2984f0576f70f86650b7c2a132538) is a [quadratic non-residue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadratic_non-residue "Quadratic non-residue") modulo ![Image 189: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) (this is almost the definition of a quadratic non-residue). There are ![Image 190: {\displaystyle {\frac {p-1}{2}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a733b8495b0d55ed5bc9d795b7b6821efb1d858b) quadratic non-residues modulo ![Image 191: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36). Multiplicative structure
------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Finite_field&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Multiplicative structure")]

The set of non-zero elements in ![Image 263: {\displaystyle \mathrm {GF} (q)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b5668d264af0b08b75fef8f823e6588c68c25a3e) is an [abelian group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abelian_group "Abelian group") under the multiplication, of order ![Image 264: {\displaystyle q-1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9bfc49678846b112cde021e3cb52d9b3b15decaf). By [Lagrange's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrange%27s_theorem_(group_theory) "Lagrange's theorem (group theory)"), there exists a divisor ![Image 265: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40) of ![Image 266: {\displaystyle q-1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9bfc49678846b112cde021e3cb52d9b3b15decaf) such that ![Image 267: {\displaystyle x^{k}=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/788abdbf725bf5986c0c49c776ca858453191f8f) for every non-zero ![Image 268: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) in ![Image 269: {\displaystyle \mathrm {GF} (q)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b5668d264af0b08b75fef8f823e6588c68c25a3e). As the equation ![Image 270: {\displaystyle x^{k}=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/788abdbf725bf5986c0c49c776ca858453191f8f) has at most ![Image 271: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40) solutions in any field, ![Image 272: {\displaystyle q-1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9bfc49678846b112cde021e3cb52d9b3b15decaf) is the lowest possible value for ![Image 273: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40). The [structure theorem of finite abelian groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abelian_group#Classification "Abelian group") implies that this multiplicative group is [cyclic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclic_group "Cyclic group"), that is, all non-zero elements are powers of a single element. In summary:

_The multiplicative group of the non-zero elements in_![Image 274: {\displaystyle \mathrm {GF} (q)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b5668d264af0b08b75fef8f823e6588c68c25a3e)_is cyclic, i.e., there exists an element_![Image 275: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc), _such that the_![Image 276: {\displaystyle q-1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9bfc49678846b112cde021e3cb52d9b3b15decaf)_non-zero elements of_![Image 277: {\displaystyle \mathrm {GF} (q)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b5668d264af0b08b75fef8f823e6588c68c25a3e)_are_![Image 278: {\displaystyle a,a^{2},\ldots ,a^{q-2},a^{q-1}=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f9140508465e1e7e309082801a6ed8f92b43e86a).

Such an element ![Image 279: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc) is called a [primitive element](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primitive_element_(finite_field) "Primitive element (finite field)") of ![Image 280: {\displaystyle \mathrm {GF} (q)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b5668d264af0b08b75fef8f823e6588c68c25a3e). Unless ![Image 281: {\displaystyle q=2,3}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dd97439deedc725c3f302b1601c57b00d212b33a), the primitive element is not unique. The number of primitive elements is ![Image 282: {\displaystyle \phi (q-1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/072c7bf3aef9a071393a61d9600aaf1aa00d8bba) where ![Image 283: {\displaystyle \phi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/72b1f30316670aee6270a28334bdf4f5072cdde4) is [Euler's totient function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler%27s_totient_function "Euler's totient function").

The result above implies that ![Image 284: {\displaystyle x^{q}=x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e6bc709662a3fbdf3cdb7001c63e8dd9f4dc19d6) for every ![Image 285: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) in ![Image 286: {\displaystyle \mathrm {GF} (q)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b5668d264af0b08b75fef8f823e6588c68c25a3e). The particular case where ![Image 287: {\displaystyle q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/06809d64fa7c817ffc7e323f85997f783dbdf71d) is prime is [Fermat's little theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermat%27s_little_theorem "Fermat's little theorem").

If ![Image 288: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc) is a primitive element in ![Image 289: {\displaystyle \mathrm {GF} (q)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b5668d264af0b08b75fef8f823e6588c68c25a3e), then for any non-zero element ![Image 290: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) in ![Image 291: {\displaystyle F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/545fd099af8541605f7ee55f08225526be88ce57), there is a unique integer ![Image 292: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) with ![Image 293: {\displaystyle 0\leq n\leq q-2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/370dcad53a82be2fc33e1ec59fdb3ad25c10f2fc) such that ![Image 294: {\displaystyle x=a^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/407e45335787b25bf78954908ec685cfb6b23622). This integer ![Image 295: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) is called the [discrete logarithm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discrete_logarithm "Discrete logarithm") of ![Image 296: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) to the base ![Image 297: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc).

While ![Image 298: {\displaystyle a^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2687184a7698e75db65a25bea7afd207bff3d03b) can be computed very quickly, for example using [exponentiation by squaring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponentiation_by_squaring "Exponentiation by squaring"), there is no known efficient algorithm for computing the inverse operation, the discrete logarithm. This has been used in various [cryptographic protocols](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cryptographic_protocol "Cryptographic protocol"), see _[Discrete logarithm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discrete\_logarithm "Discrete logarithm")_ for details.

When the nonzero elements of ![Image 299: {\displaystyle \mathrm {GF} (q)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b5668d264af0b08b75fef8f823e6588c68c25a3e) are represented by their discrete logarithms, multiplication and division are easy, as they reduce to addition and subtraction modulo ![Image 300: {\displaystyle q-1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9bfc49678846b112cde021e3cb52d9b3b15decaf). However, addition amounts to computing the discrete logarithm of ![Image 301: {\displaystyle a^{m}+a^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3cf6009f17e5bdd1dfee73b6e52a0753910c8a1e). The identity ![Image 302: {\displaystyle a^{m}+a^{n}=a^{n}\left(a^{m-n}+1\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6d5d5cc2e62eca8b7313c1a32e5807a621bb0913) allows one to solve this problem by constructing the table of the discrete logarithms of ![Image 303: {\displaystyle a^{n}+1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87276e8cfc7d979578ecabf361d29150e67c6727), called [Zech's logarithms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zech%27s_logarithm "Zech's logarithm"), for ![Image 304: {\displaystyle n=0,\ldots ,q-2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/470d0ff700dec1704d3a0dcbd15aaf7e37ebf8a8) (it is convenient to define the discrete logarithm of zero as being ![Image 305: {\displaystyle -\infty }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ca2608c4b5fd3bffc73585f8c67e379b4e99b6f1)).

Zech's logarithms are useful for large computations, such as [linear algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_algebra "Linear algebra") over medium-sized fields, that is, fields that are sufficiently large for making natural algorithms inefficient, but not too large, as one has to pre-compute a table of the same size as the order of the field.

Every nonzero element of a finite field is a [root of unity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Root_of_unity "Root of unity"), as ![Image 306: {\displaystyle x^{q-1}=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cdf77aa68a5dec431f627fdd3d14cf62ac0914bc) for every nonzero element of ![Image 307: {\displaystyle \mathrm {GF} (q)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b5668d264af0b08b75fef8f823e6588c68c25a3e).

If ![Image 308: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) is a positive integer, an ![Image 309: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)th **primitive root of unity** is a solution of the equation ![Image 310: {\displaystyle x^{n}=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8741a3eae6f729b4f1c4273a74c135d23987d5fd) that is not a solution of the equation ![Image 311: {\displaystyle x^{m}=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4616231c58c82d6c8a3992e73daaeaf4d46428f3) for any positive integer ![Image 312: {\displaystyle m<n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/490c01b0cb770144f28afd17bb5fef277daf6f38). If ![Image 313: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc) is a ![Image 314: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)th primitive root of unity in a field ![Image 315: {\displaystyle F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/545fd099af8541605f7ee55f08225526be88ce57), then ![Image 316: {\displaystyle F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/545fd099af8541605f7ee55f08225526be88ce57) contains all the ![Image 317: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) roots of unity, which are ![Image 318: {\displaystyle 1,a,a^{2},\ldots ,a^{n-1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54dd2bb76602f677dedec13c5fc39ac4114f7c7a).

Frobenius automorphism and Galois theory
----------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Finite_field&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Frobenius automorphism and Galois theory")]

In this section, ![Image 364: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) is a prime number, and ![Image 365: {\displaystyle q=p^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7b95f6525025c9ae1432ea9bd50c3c1cab4b7c6c) is a power of ![Image 366: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36).

In ![Image 367: {\displaystyle \mathrm {GF} (q)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b5668d264af0b08b75fef8f823e6588c68c25a3e), the identity (_x_ + _y_)_p_ = _x p_ + _y p_ implies that the map ![Image 368: {\displaystyle \varphi :x\mapsto x^{p}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8a7b6ab3b06a3f2339f542217b2079ff35391dfc) is a ![Image 369: {\displaystyle \mathrm {GF} (p)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1dcb6720c421397627afb8ecce23a98b152d432f)-[linear endomorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_map "Linear map") and a [field automorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_automorphism "Field automorphism") of ![Image 370: {\displaystyle \mathrm {GF} (q)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b5668d264af0b08b75fef8f823e6588c68c25a3e), which fixes every element of the subfield ![Image 371: {\displaystyle \mathrm {GF} (p)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1dcb6720c421397627afb8ecce23a98b152d432f). It is called the [Frobenius automorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frobenius_automorphism "Frobenius automorphism"), after [Ferdinand Georg Frobenius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ferdinand_Georg_Frobenius "Ferdinand Georg Frobenius").

Denoting by _φ k_ the [composition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_composition "Function composition") of _φ_ with itself _k_ times, we have ![Image 372: {\displaystyle \varphi ^{k}:x\mapsto x^{p^{k}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a953e4ee0207e38d99bb714e12ec6e974f93929e) It has been shown in the preceding section that _φ_ _n_ is the identity. For 0 <_k_<_n_, the automorphism _φ_ _k_ is not the identity, as, otherwise, the polynomial ![Image 373: {\displaystyle X^{p^{k}}-X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/61acbb56af5042aacfed225f80688e14c452796a) would have more than _p k_ roots.

There are no other GF(_p_)-automorphisms of GF(_q_). In other words, GF(_p n_) has exactly _n_ GF(_p_)-automorphisms, which are ![Image 374: {\displaystyle \mathrm {Id} =\varphi ^{0},\varphi ,\varphi ^{2},\ldots ,\varphi ^{n-1}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3543b0e9a296fe2cdcbcd1714c52ebeca0538e9f)

In terms of [Galois theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galois_theory "Galois theory"), this means that GF(_p_ _n_) is a [Galois extension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galois_extension "Galois extension") of GF(_p_), which has a [cyclic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclic_group "Cyclic group") Galois group.

The fact that the Frobenius map is surjective implies that every finite field is [perfect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perfect_field "Perfect field").

Polynomial factorization
------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Finite_field&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Polynomial factorization")]

If _F_ is a finite field, a non-constant [monic polynomial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monic_polynomial "Monic polynomial") with coefficients in _F_ is [irreducible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irreducible_polynomial "Irreducible polynomial") over _F_, if it is not the product of two non-constant monic polynomials, with coefficients in _F_.

As every [polynomial ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial_ring "Polynomial ring") over a field is a [unique factorization domain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unique_factorization_domain "Unique factorization domain"), every monic polynomial over a finite field may be factored in a unique way (up to the order of the factors) into a product of irreducible monic polynomials.

There are efficient algorithms for testing polynomial irreducibility and factoring polynomials over finite fields. They are a key step for factoring polynomials over the integers or the [rational numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_numbers "Rational numbers"). At least for this reason, every [computer algebra system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_algebra_system "Computer algebra system") has functions for factoring polynomials over finite fields, or, at least, over finite prime fields.

### Irreducible polynomials of a given degree

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Finite_field&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: Irreducible polynomials of a given degree")]

The polynomial ![Image 375: {\displaystyle X^{q}-X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/66ab5e3894b0bea4ae4ba01072f5ac2816ff34f1) factors into linear factors over a field of order _q_. More precisely, this polynomial is the product of all monic polynomials of degree one over a field of order _q_.

This implies that, if _q_ = _p n_ then _X q_ − _X_ is the product of all monic irreducible polynomials over GF(_p_), whose degree divides _n_. In fact, if _P_ is an irreducible factor over GF(_p_) of _X q_ − _X_, its degree divides _n_, as its [splitting field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Splitting_field "Splitting field") is contained in GF(_p_ _n_). Conversely, if _P_ is an irreducible monic polynomial over GF(_p_) of degree _d_ dividing _n_, it defines a field extension of degree _d_, which is contained in GF(_p_ _n_), and all roots of _P_ belong to GF(_p_ _n_), and are roots of _X q_ − _X_; thus _P_ divides _X q_ − _X_. As _X q_ − _X_ does not have any multiple factor, it is thus the product of all the irreducible monic polynomials that divide it.

This property is used to compute the product of the irreducible factors of each degree of polynomials over GF(_p_); see _[Distinct degree factorization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distinct\_degree\_factorization "Distinct degree factorization")_.

### Number of monic irreducible polynomials of a given degree over a finite field

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Finite_field&action=edit&section=16 "Edit section: Number of monic irreducible polynomials of a given degree over a finite field")]

The number _N_(_q_, _n_) of monic irreducible polynomials of degree _n_ over GF(_q_) is given by[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_field#cite_note-6)![Image 376: {\displaystyle N(q,n)={\frac {1}{n}}\sum _{d\mid n}\mu (d)q^{n/d},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a7df4ec6f8ba99b2b24edf150e51ddd556dddd44) where _μ_ is the [Möbius function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M%C3%B6bius_function "Möbius function"). This formula is an immediate consequence of the property of _X_ _q_ − _X_ above and the [Möbius inversion formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M%C3%B6bius_inversion_formula "Möbius inversion formula").

By the above formula, the number of irreducible (not necessarily monic) polynomials of degree _n_ over GF(_q_) is (_q_ − 1)_N_(_q_, _n_).

The exact formula implies the inequality ![Image 377: {\displaystyle N(q,n)\geq {\frac {1}{n}}\left(q^{n}-\sum _{\ell \mid n,\ \ell {\text{ prime}}}q^{n/\ell }\right);}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bb18dc9a76b151091625145ffddeb98da26cedad) this is sharp if and only if _n_ is a power of some prime. For every _q_ and every _n_, the right hand side is positive, so there is at least one irreducible polynomial of degree _n_ over GF(_q_).

In [cryptography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cryptography "Cryptography"), the difficulty of the [discrete logarithm problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discrete_logarithm_problem "Discrete logarithm problem") in a finite fields or in an [elliptic curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptic_curve "Elliptic curve") over a finite field is the basis of several widely used protocols, such as the [Diffie–Hellman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffie%E2%80%93Hellman "Diffie–Hellman") protocol. For example, in 2014, a secure internet connection to Wikipedia involved the elliptic curve Diffie–Hellman protocol ([ECDHE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ECDHE "ECDHE")) over a large finite field.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_field#cite_note-7) In [coding theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coding_theory "Coding theory"), many codes are constructed as [subspaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_subspace "Linear subspace") of [vector spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space") over finite fields.

Finite fields are used by many [error correction codes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Error_correction_code "Error correction code"), such as [Reed–Solomon error correction code](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reed%E2%80%93Solomon_error_correction "Reed–Solomon error correction") or [BCH code](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BCH_code "BCH code"). The finite field almost always has characteristic of 2, since computer data is stored in binary. For example, a byte of data can be interpreted as an element of GF(2 8). One exception is [PDF417](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PDF417 "PDF417") bar code, which is GF(929). Some CPUs have special instructions that can be useful for finite fields of characteristic 2, generally variations of [carry-less product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carry-less_product "Carry-less product").

Finite fields are widely used in [number theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_theory "Number theory"), as many problems over the integers may be solved by reducing them [modulo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modular_arithmetic "Modular arithmetic") one or several [prime numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_number "Prime number"). For example, the fastest known algorithms for [polynomial factorization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial_factorization "Polynomial factorization") and [linear algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_algebra "Linear algebra") over the field of [rational numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_number "Rational number") proceed by reduction modulo one or several primes, and then reconstruction of the solution by using [Chinese remainder theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_remainder_theorem "Chinese remainder theorem"), [Hensel lifting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hensel_lifting "Hensel lifting") or the [LLL algorithm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LLL_algorithm "LLL algorithm").

Similarly many theoretical problems in number theory can be solved by considering their reductions modulo some or all prime numbers. See, for example, _[Hasse principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hasse\_principle "Hasse principle")_. Many recent developments of [algebraic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_geometry "Algebraic geometry") were motivated by the need to enlarge the power of these modular methods. [Wiles' proof of Fermat's Last Theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wiles%27_proof_of_Fermat%27s_Last_Theorem "Wiles' proof of Fermat's Last Theorem") is an example of a deep result involving many mathematical tools, including finite fields.

The [Weil conjectures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weil_conjectures "Weil conjectures") concern the number of points on [algebraic varieties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_variety "Algebraic variety") over finite fields and the theory has many applications including [exponential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponential_sum "Exponential sum") and [character sum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Character_sum "Character sum") estimates.

Finite fields have widespread application in [combinatorics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Combinatorics "Combinatorics"), two well known examples being the definition of [Paley Graphs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paley_graph "Paley graph") and the related construction for [Hadamard Matrices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paley_construction "Paley construction"). In [arithmetic combinatorics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic_combinatorics "Arithmetic combinatorics") finite fields[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_field#cite_note-8) and finite field models[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_field#cite_note-9)[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_field#cite_note-10) are used extensively, such as in [Szemerédi's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Szemer%C3%A9di%27s_theorem "Szemerédi's theorem") on arithmetic progressions.

### Wedderburn's little theorem

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Finite_field&action=edit&section=19 "Edit section: Wedderburn's little theorem")]

A [division ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_ring "Division ring") is a generalization of field. Division rings are not assumed to be commutative. There are no non-commutative finite division rings: [Wedderburn's little theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wedderburn%27s_little_theorem "Wedderburn's little theorem") states that all finite [division rings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_ring "Division ring") are commutative, and hence are finite fields. This result holds even if we relax the [associativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Associativity "Associativity") axiom to [alternativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alternativity "Alternativity"), that is, all finite [alternative division rings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alternative_division_ring "Alternative division ring") are finite fields, by the [Artin–Zorn theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artin%E2%80%93Zorn_theorem "Artin–Zorn theorem").[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_field#cite_note-11)

A finite field ![Image 378: {\displaystyle F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/545fd099af8541605f7ee55f08225526be88ce57) is not algebraically closed: the polynomial ![Image 379: {\displaystyle f(T)=1+\prod _{\alpha \in F}(T-\alpha ),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/51f31075c7f080ec5efb498d528538080ec313c9) has no roots in ![Image 380: {\displaystyle F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/545fd099af8541605f7ee55f08225526be88ce57), since _f_ (_α_) = 1 for all ![Image 381: {\displaystyle \alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b79333175c8b3f0840bfb4ec41b8072c83ea88d3) in ![Image 382: {\displaystyle F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/545fd099af8541605f7ee55f08225526be88ce57).

Given a prime number p, let ![Image 383: {\displaystyle {\overline {\mathbb {F} }}_{p}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/309cb236f56d2dc87d207f798406dfa864fb57b2) be an algebraic closure of ![Image 384: {\displaystyle \mathbb {F} _{p}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/20992e2f112734263ffbd8cdf7f09bb9201d89db) It is not only unique [up to](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Up_to "Up to") an isomorphism, as do all algebraic closures, but contrarily to the general case, all its subfield are fixed by all its automorphisms, and it is also the algebraic closure of all finite fields of the same characteristic p.

This property results mainly from the fact that the elements of ![Image 385: {\displaystyle \mathbb {F} _{p^{n}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b7ef6f7b38b948ec1d7d54dfa483c00db7dedd5a) are exactly the roots of ![Image 386: {\displaystyle x^{p^{n}}-x,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4e780e2dd95c033dd9dc9a6dc9b8bfd1c055b8fe) and this defines an inclusion ![Image 387: {\displaystyle \mathbb {\mathbb {F} } _{p^{n}}\subset \mathbb {F} _{p^{nm}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cf350bf35ce7d0aae114737eb3a24e9597af6238) for ![Image 388: {\displaystyle m>1.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4f27cd4f2f453c4eef1cbb129153a60749920a48) These inclusions allow writing informally ![Image 389: {\displaystyle {\overline {\mathbb {F} }}_{p}=\bigcup _{n\geq 1}\mathbb {F} _{p^{n}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f7e88e6bd5d32905400f438589a4537fbdd0ea33) The formal validation of this notation results from the fact that the above field inclusions form a [directed set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Directed_set "Directed set") of fields; Its [direct limit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direct_limit "Direct limit") is ![Image 390: {\displaystyle {\overline {\mathbb {F} }}_{p},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/066478259c77e5d8de8a4b303a48be9c0529877c) which may thus be considered as "directed union".

#### Primitive elements in the algebraic closure

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Finite_field&action=edit&section=21 "Edit section: Primitive elements in the algebraic closure")]

Given a [primitive element](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primitive_element_(field_theory) "Primitive element (field theory)")![Image 391: {\displaystyle g_{mn}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b3e1ef7337f5dcf294f71f742f2cd5abde2f6712) of ![Image 392: {\displaystyle \mathbb {F} _{q^{mn}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/94f0e00b5f36f3c15d1c1890c3c7f6f59d81d0bd) then ![Image 393: {\displaystyle g_{mn}^{m}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e23b1a2b1f2a9ba22245ed5fb36a56d6b6e4e2c3) is a primitive element of ![Image 394: {\displaystyle \mathbb {F} _{q^{n}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4f08ee88a4854e856c7f6b1cd27867026b6256db)

For explicit computations, it may be useful to have a coherent choice of the primitive elements for all finite fields; that is, to choose the primitive element ![Image 395: {\displaystyle g_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/177cf6000dd244d8051d352342a8377c0059db0d) of ![Image 396: {\displaystyle \mathbb {F} _{q^{n}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/885cafbbd811da8ecf8097b4dda7c6587f50100a) in order that, whenever ![Image 397: {\displaystyle n=mh,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ab85ebdba2b20ae115824e753e0f2b07e34711a9) one has ![Image 398: {\displaystyle g_{m}=g_{n}^{h},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/51de58d0de9ba1a7ba4acc3805c0e82ea7f47f69) where ![Image 399: {\displaystyle g_{m}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/28dbe49b383cddeba92dfccc07b285a189b443a4) is the primitive element already chosen for ![Image 400: {\displaystyle \mathbb {F} _{q^{m}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0e8a1891b748cfa0ff8db66cc694470dbb5971e4)

Such a construction may be obtained by [Conway polynomials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conway_polynomial_(finite_fields) "Conway polynomial (finite fields)").

#### Quasi-algebraic closure

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Finite_field&action=edit&section=22 "Edit section: Quasi-algebraic closure")]

Although finite fields are not algebraically closed, they are [quasi-algebraically closed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasi-algebraically_closed_field "Quasi-algebraically closed field"), which means that every [homogeneous polynomial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homogeneous_polynomial "Homogeneous polynomial") over a finite field has a non-trivial zero whose components are in the field if the number of its variables is more than its degree. This was a conjecture of [Artin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emil_Artin "Emil Artin") and [Dickson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leonard_Eugene_Dickson "Leonard Eugene Dickson") proved by [Chevalley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Claude_Chevalley "Claude Chevalley") (see _[Chevalley–Warning theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chevalley%E2%80%93Warning\_theorem "Chevalley–Warning theorem")_).
