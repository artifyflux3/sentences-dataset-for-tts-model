In [mathematical physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_physics "Mathematical physics"), a **pseudo-Riemannian manifold**,[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudo-Riemannian_manifold#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBennTucker1987172-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudo-Riemannian_manifold#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBishopGoldberg1968208-2) also called a **semi-Riemannian manifold**, is a [differentiable manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differentiable_manifold "Differentiable manifold") with a [metric tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_tensor "Metric tensor") that is everywhere [nondegenerate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-degenerate_bilinear_form "Non-degenerate bilinear form"). This is a generalization of a [Riemannian manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_manifold "Riemannian manifold") in which the requirement of [positive-definiteness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positive-definite_bilinear_form "Positive-definite bilinear form") is relaxed.

Every [tangent space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_space "Tangent space") of a pseudo-Riemannian manifold is a [pseudo-Euclidean vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudo-Euclidean_vector_space "Pseudo-Euclidean vector space").

A special case used in [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity") is a four-dimensional **Lorentzian manifold** for modeling [spacetime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spacetime "Spacetime"), where tangent vectors can be classified as [timelike, null, and spacelike](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Causal_structure "Causal structure").

### Manifolds

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Pseudo-Riemannian_manifold&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Manifolds")]

Main articles: [Manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manifold "Manifold") and [Differentiable manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differentiable_manifold "Differentiable manifold")

In [differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry"), a [differentiable manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differentiable_manifold "Differentiable manifold") is a space that is locally similar to a [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space"). In an _n_-dimensional Euclidean space any point can be specified by _n_ real numbers. These are called the [coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate "Coordinate") of the point.

An _n_-dimensional differentiable manifold is a generalisation of _n_-dimensional Euclidean space. In a manifold it may only be possible to define coordinates _locally_. This is achieved by defining [coordinate patches](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_patch "Coordinate patch"): subsets of the manifold that can be mapped into _n_-dimensional Euclidean space.

See _[Manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manifold "Manifold")_, _[Differentiable manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differentiable\_manifold "Differentiable manifold")_, _[Coordinate patch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate\_patch "Coordinate patch")_ for more details.

### Metric signatures

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Pseudo-Riemannian_manifold&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Metric signatures")]

Main article: [Metric signature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_signature "Metric signature")

Given a metric tensor _g_ on an _n_-dimensional real manifold, the [quadratic form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadratic_form "Quadratic form")_q_(_x_) = _g_(_x_, _x_) associated with the metric tensor applied to each vector of any [orthogonal basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_basis "Orthogonal basis") produces _n_ real values. By [Sylvester's law of inertia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sylvester%27s_law_of_inertia#Law_of_inertia_for_quadratic_forms "Sylvester's law of inertia"), the number of each positive, negative and zero values produced in this manner are invariants of the metric tensor, independent of the choice of orthogonal basis. The **[signature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_signature "Metric signature")**(_p_, _q_, _r_) of the metric tensor gives these numbers, shown in the same order. A non-degenerate metric tensor has _r_ = 0 and the signature may be denoted (_p_, _q_), where _p_ + _q_ = _n_.

Definition
----------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Pseudo-Riemannian_manifold&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Definition")]

A **pseudo-Riemannian manifold**(_M_, _g_) is a [differentiable manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differentiable_manifold "Differentiable manifold")_M_ that is equipped with an everywhere non-degenerate, smooth, symmetric [metric tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_tensor "Metric tensor")_g_.

Such a metric is called a **pseudo-Riemannian metric**. Applied to a vector field, the resulting scalar field value at any point of the manifold can be positive, negative or zero.

The signature of a pseudo-Riemannian metric is (_p_, _q_), where both _p_ and _q_ are non-negative. The non-degeneracy condition together with continuity implies that _p_ and _q_ remain unchanged throughout the manifold (assuming it is connected).

Lorentzian manifold
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Pseudo-Riemannian_manifold&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Lorentzian manifold")]

A **Lorentzian manifold** is an important special case of a pseudo-Riemannian manifold in which the [signature of the metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_signature "Metric signature") is (1, _n_−1) (equivalently, (_n_−1, 1); see _[Sign convention](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sign\_convention "Sign convention")_). Such metrics are called **Lorentzian metrics**. They are named after the Dutch physicist [Hendrik Lorentz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hendrik_Lorentz "Hendrik Lorentz").

### Applications in physics

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Pseudo-Riemannian_manifold&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Applications in physics")]

After Riemannian manifolds, Lorentzian manifolds form the most important subclass of pseudo-Riemannian manifolds. They are important in applications of [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity").

A principal premise of general relativity is that [spacetime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spacetime "Spacetime") can be modeled as a 4-dimensional Lorentzian manifold of signature (3, 1) or, equivalently, (1, 3). Unlike Riemannian manifolds with positive-definite metrics, an indefinite signature allows tangent vectors to be classified into _timelike_, _null_ or _spacelike_. With a signature of (_p_, 1) or (1, _q_), the manifold is also locally (and possibly globally) time-orientable (see _[Causal structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Causal\_structure "Causal structure")_).

Properties of pseudo-Riemannian manifolds
-----------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Pseudo-Riemannian_manifold&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Properties of pseudo-Riemannian manifolds")]

Just as [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space")R n{\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}![Image 49: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d) can be thought of as the local model of a [Riemannian manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_manifold "Riemannian manifold"), [Minkowski space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minkowski_space "Minkowski space")R n−1,1{\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n-1,1}}![Image 50: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n-1,1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/39b068d78f4a2667d979f08ab76d8341c2966e24) with the flat [Minkowski metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minkowski_metric "Minkowski metric") is the local model of a Lorentzian manifold. Likewise, the model space for a pseudo-Riemannian manifold of signature (_p_, _q_) is [pseudo-Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudo-Euclidean_space "Pseudo-Euclidean space")R p,q{\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{p,q}}![Image 51: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{p,q}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/982b9980e5bcede60b471a3f9b57541ba984d29e), for which there exist coordinates _x_ _i_ such that

g=d x 1 2+⋯+d x p 2−d x p+1 2−⋯−d x p+q 2{\displaystyle g=dx_{1}^{2}+\cdots +dx_{p}^{2}-dx_{p+1}^{2}-\cdots -dx_{p+q}^{2}}![Image 52: {\displaystyle g=dx_{1}^{2}+\cdots +dx_{p}^{2}-dx_{p+1}^{2}-\cdots -dx_{p+q}^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3ce90e70c3f659f4aa02da7399dd72b785c9578e)
Some theorems of Riemannian geometry can be generalized to the pseudo-Riemannian case. In particular, the [fundamental theorem of Riemannian geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_theorem_of_Riemannian_geometry "Fundamental theorem of Riemannian geometry") is true of all pseudo-Riemannian manifolds. This allows one to speak of the [Levi-Civita connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_connection "Levi-Civita connection") on a pseudo-Riemannian manifold along with the associated [curvature tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_curvature_tensor "Riemann curvature tensor"). On the other hand, there are many theorems in Riemannian geometry that do not hold in the generalized case. For example, it is _not_ true that every smooth manifold admits a pseudo-Riemannian metric of a given signature; there are certain [topological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology "Topology") obstructions. Furthermore, a [submanifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Submanifold "Submanifold") does not always inherit the structure of a pseudo-Riemannian manifold; for example, the metric tensor becomes zero on any [light-like](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minkowski_space#Causal_structure "Minkowski space")[curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curve "Curve"). The [Clifton–Pohl torus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifton%E2%80%93Pohl_torus "Clifton–Pohl torus") provides an example of a pseudo-Riemannian manifold that is compact but not complete, a combination of properties that the [Hopf–Rinow theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hopf%E2%80%93Rinow_theorem "Hopf–Rinow theorem") disallows for Riemannian manifolds.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudo-Riemannian_manifold#cite_note-FOOTNOTEO'Neill1983193-3)
