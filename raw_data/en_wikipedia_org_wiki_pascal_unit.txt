The **pascal** (symbol: **Pa**) is the unit of [pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pressure "Pressure") in the [International System of Units (SI)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Units "International System of Units"). It is also used to quantify [internal pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internal_pressure "Internal pressure"), [stress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stress_(physics) "Stress (physics)"), [Young's modulus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Young%27s_modulus "Young's modulus"), and [ultimate tensile strength](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultimate_tensile_strength "Ultimate tensile strength"). The unit, named after [Blaise Pascal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blaise_Pascal "Blaise Pascal"), is an [SI coherent derived unit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SI_coherent_derived_unit "SI coherent derived unit") defined as one [newton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton_(unit) "Newton (unit)") per [square metre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_metre "Square metre") (N/m 2).[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pascal_(unit)#cite_note-1) It is also equivalent to 10 [barye](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barye "Barye") (10 Ba) in the [CGS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centimetre%E2%80%93gram%E2%80%93second_system_of_units "Centimetre–gram–second system of units") system. Common multiple units of the pascal are the hectopascal (1 hPa = 100 Pa), which is equal to one [millibar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bar_(unit)#Usage "Bar (unit)"), and the kilopascal (1 kPa = 1000 Pa), which is equal to one centibar.

The unit of measurement called _[standard atmosphere (atm)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard\_atmosphere\_(unit) "Standard atmosphere (unit)")_ is defined as 101 325 Pa.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pascal_(unit)#cite_note-BIPM-2)[Meteorological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteorology "Meteorology") observations typically report [atmospheric pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure "Atmospheric pressure") in hectopascals per the recommendation of the [World Meteorological Organization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization "World Meteorological Organization"), thus a standard atmosphere (atm) or typical sea-level air pressure is about 1013 hPa. Reports in the United States typically use [inches of mercury](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inches_of_mercury "Inches of mercury")[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pascal_(unit)#cite_note-3) or millibars (hectopascals).[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pascal_(unit)#cite_note-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pascal_(unit)#cite_note-5) In Canada, these reports are given in kilopascals.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pascal_(unit)#cite_note-6)

The unit is named after [Blaise Pascal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blaise_Pascal "Blaise Pascal"), noted for his contributions to hydrodynamics and hydrostatics, and experiments with a [barometer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barometer "Barometer"). The name _pascal_ was adopted for the SI unit newton per square metre (N/m 2) by the 14th [General Conference on Weights and Measures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Conference_on_Weights_and_Measures "General Conference on Weights and Measures") in 1971.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pascal_(unit)#cite_note-7)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pascal_(unit)#cite_note-CGPM14-8)

The pascal can be expressed using [SI derived units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SI_derived_unit "SI derived unit"), or alternatively solely [SI base units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SI_base_unit "SI base unit"), as:

![Image 2: {\displaystyle {\rm {1~Pa=1~N/m^{2}=1~kg/(m{\cdot }s^{2})=1~J/m^{3}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/deb9ff07cf667a7055291fbf04ff6c98c411382e)
where N is the [newton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton_(unit) "Newton (unit)"), m is the [metre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metre "Metre"), kg is the [kilogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kilogram "Kilogram"), s is the [second](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second "Second"), and J is the [joule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joule "Joule").[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pascal_(unit)#cite_note-9)

One pascal is the pressure exerted by a force of one newton perpendicularly upon an area of one square metre.

The unit of measurement called an [atmosphere or a standard atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_atmosphere_(unit) "Standard atmosphere (unit)") (atm) is 101 325 Pa (101.325 kPa).[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pascal_(unit)#cite_note-10) This value is often used as a reference pressure and specified as such in some national and international standards, such as the [International Organization for Standardization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization "International Organization for Standardization")'s ISO 2787 (pneumatic tools and compressors), [ISO 2533](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO_2533 "ISO 2533") (aerospace) and ISO 5024 (petroleum). In contrast, [International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Union_of_Pure_and_Applied_Chemistry "International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry") (IUPAC) recommends the use of 100 kPa as a standard pressure when reporting the properties of substances.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pascal_(unit)#cite_note-11)

[Unicode](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unicode "Unicode") has dedicated code-points U+33A9㎩SQUARE PA and U+33AA㎪SQUARE KPA in the [CJK Compatibility](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CJK_Compatibility "CJK Compatibility") block, but these exist only for backward-compatibility with some older ideographic character-sets and are therefore [deprecated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deprecate "Deprecate").[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pascal_(unit)#cite_note-12)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pascal_(unit)#cite_note-13)

The pascal (Pa) or kilopascal (kPa) as a unit of pressure measurement is widely used throughout the world and has largely replaced the [pounds per square inch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pounds_per_square_inch "Pounds per square inch") (psi) unit, except in some countries that still use the [imperial measurement system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imperial_measurement_system "Imperial measurement system") or the [US customary system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/US_customary_system "US customary system"), including the United States.

[Geophysicists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geophysics "Geophysics") use the gigapascal (GPa) in measuring or calculating [tectonic stresses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tectonophysics "Tectonophysics") and pressures within the [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth "Earth").

Medical [elastography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elastography "Elastography") measures tissue stiffness non-invasively with [ultrasound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medical_ultrasonography "Medical ultrasonography") or [magnetic resonance imaging](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_resonance_imaging "Magnetic resonance imaging"), and often displays the [Young's modulus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Young%27s_modulus "Young's modulus") or [shear modulus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shear_modulus "Shear modulus") of tissue in kilopascals.

In [materials science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Materials_science "Materials science") and [engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineering "Engineering"), the pascal measures the [stiffness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Young%27s_modulus "Young's modulus"), [tensile strength](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensile_strength "Tensile strength") and [compressive strength](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compressive_strength "Compressive strength") of materials. In engineering the megapascal (MPa) is the preferred unit for these uses, because the pascal represents a very small quantity.

The pascal is also equivalent to the SI unit of [energy density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_density "Energy density"), the joule per cubic metre. This applies not only to the thermodynamics of pressurised gases, but also to the energy density of [electric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_potential_energy "Electric potential energy"), [magnetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_field#Energy_stored_in_magnetic_fields "Magnetic field"), and [gravitational](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_energy "Gravitational energy") fields.

The pascal is used to measure [sound pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sound_pressure "Sound pressure"). [Loudness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Loudness "Loudness") is the subjective experience of sound pressure and is measured as a [sound pressure level](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sound_pressure#Sound_pressure_level "Sound pressure") (SPL) on a logarithmic scale of the sound pressure relative to some reference pressure. For sound in air, a pressure of 20 μPa is considered to be at the [threshold of hearing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Threshold_of_hearing "Threshold of hearing") for humans and is a common reference pressure, so that its SPL is zero.

[The airtightness of buildings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blower_door "Blower door") is measured at 50 Pa.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pascal_(unit)#cite_note-15)

In medicine, [blood pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_pressure "Blood pressure") is measured in [millimeters of mercury](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Millimetre_of_mercury "Millimetre of mercury") (mmHg, very close to one [Torr](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torr "Torr")). The normal adult blood pressure is less than 120 mmHg systolic BP (SBP) and less than 80 mmHg diastolic BP (DBP).[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pascal_(unit)#cite_note-16) Convert mmHg to SI units as follows: 1 mmHg = 0.133 32 kPa. Hence the normal blood pressure in SI units is less than 16.0 kPa SBP and less than 10.7 kPa DBP. These values are similar to the pressure of water column of average human height; so pressure has to be measured on arm roughly at the level of the heart.

### Hectopascal and millibar units

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Pascal_(unit)&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Hectopascal and millibar units")]

The units of [atmospheric pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure "Atmospheric pressure") commonly used in [meteorology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteorology "Meteorology") were formerly the [bar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bar_(unit) "Bar (unit)") (100 000 Pa), which is close to the average air pressure on Earth, and the millibar. Since the introduction of [SI units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Units "International System of Units"), meteorologists generally measure atmospheric pressure in hectopascals (hPa), equal to 100 pascals or 1 millibar.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pascal_(unit)#cite_note-17)[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pascal_(unit)#cite_note-18)[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pascal_(unit)#cite_note-19)[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pascal_(unit)#cite_note-20)[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pascal_(unit)#cite_note-21)[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pascal_(unit)#cite_note-22)[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pascal_(unit)#cite_note-23) Exceptions include Canada, which uses kilopascals (kPa). In many other fields of science, prefixes that are a power of 1000 are preferred, which theoretically excludes hectopascal from use.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pascal_(unit)#cite_note-24)[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pascal_(unit)#cite_note-25)

Many countries still use millibars to measure atmospheric pressure. In practically all other fields, the kilopascal is used instead.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pascal_(unit)#cite_note-26)
