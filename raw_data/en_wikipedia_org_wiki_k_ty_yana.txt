**Kātyāyana** (कात्यायन) also spelled as Katyayana (c. 3rd century BCE)[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K%C4%81ty%C4%81yana#cite_note-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K%C4%81ty%C4%81yana#cite_note-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K%C4%81ty%C4%81yana#cite_note-3) was a [Sanskrit grammarian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sanskrit_grammarian "Sanskrit grammarian"), [mathematician](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_mathematics "Indian mathematics") and [Vedic priest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Historical_Vedic_religion "Historical Vedic religion") who lived in [ancient India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_India "History of India").

Origins
-------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=K%C4%81ty%C4%81yana&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Origins")]

According to some legends[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_], he was born in the Katya lineage originating from [Vishwamitra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vishwamitra "Vishwamitra"), thus[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] called Katyayana.

The [Kathāsaritsāgara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kath%C4%81sarits%C4%81gara "Kathāsaritsāgara") mentions Kātyāyana as another name of [Vararuci](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vararuci "Vararuci"), a re-incarnation of Lord [Shiva](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shiva "Shiva")'s gana or follower Pushpadanta. The story also mentions him learning grammar from Shiva's son [Kartikeya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kartikeya "Kartikeya") which is corroborated in the [Garuda Purana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Garuda_Purana "Garuda Purana") where [Kartikeya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kartikeya "Kartikeya") (also called Kumara) teaches Katyayana the rules of grammar in a way that it could be understood even by children.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K%C4%81ty%C4%81yana#cite_note-4) It may be that his full name was in fact Vararuci Kātyāyana.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K%C4%81ty%C4%81yana#cite_note-Winternitz-5)

Relation to Goddess Katyayini
-----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=K%C4%81ty%C4%81yana&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Relation to Goddess Katyayini")]

In texts like [Kalika Purana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kalika_Purana "Kalika Purana"), it is mentioned that he worshipped Mother Goddess to be born as his daughter hence she came to be known as [Katyayani](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Katyayani "Katyayani") or the "daughter of Katyayan" who is worshipped on the sixth day of [Navratri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Navratri "Navratri") festival.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K%C4%81ty%C4%81yana#cite_note-6) According to the [Vamana Purana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vamana_Purana "Vamana Purana") once the gods had gathered together to discuss the atrocities of the demon [Mahishasura](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mahishasura "Mahishasura") and their anger manifested itself in the form of energy rays. The rays crystallized in the hermitage of Kātyāyana [Rishi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rishi "Rishi"), who gave it proper form therefore she is also called Katyayani. [[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K%C4%81ty%C4%81yana#cite_note-7)

Works
-----

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=K%C4%81ty%C4%81yana&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Works")]

He is known for two works:

*   The _[Vārttikakāra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/V%C4%81rttikak%C4%81ra "Vārttikakāra")_, an elaboration on [Pāṇini](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/P%C4%81%E1%B9%87ini "Pāṇini") grammar. Along with the _[Mahābhāṣya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mah%C4%81bh%C4%81%E1%B9%A3ya "Mahābhāṣya")_ of [Patañjali](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pata%C3%B1jali "Patañjali"), this text became a core part of the _[Vyākaraṇa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vy%C4%81kara%E1%B9%87a "Vyākaraṇa")_ ([grammar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grammar "Grammar")) canon. This was one of the six [Vedangas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vedanga "Vedanga"), and constituted compulsory education for students in the following twelve centuries.
*   He also composed one of the later [Śulbasūtras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C5%9Aulbas%C5%ABtras "Śulbasūtras"), a series of nine texts on the geometry of altar constructions, dealing with rectangles, right-sided triangles, rhombuses, etc.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K%C4%81ty%C4%81yana#cite_note-8)

Views
-----

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=K%C4%81ty%C4%81yana&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Views")]

Kātyāyana's views on the sentence-meaning connection tended towards naturalism. Kātyāyana believed, that the word-meaning relationship was not a result of human convention. For Kātyāyana, word-meaning relations were _siddha_, given to us, eternal. Though the object a word is referring to is non-eternal, the substance of its meaning, like a lump of gold used to make different ornaments, remains undistorted, and is therefore permanent.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

Realizing that each word represented a categorization, he came up with the following conundrum (following [Bimal Krishna Matilal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bimal_Krishna_Matilal "Bimal Krishna Matilal")):

"If the 'basis' for the use of the word 'cow' is _cowhood_ (a universal) what would be the 'basis' for the use of the word 'cowhood'[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]?
Clearly, this leads to infinite regress. Kātyāyana's solution to this was to restrict the universal category to that of the word itself — the _basis_ for the use of any word is to be the very same word-universal itself."

This view may have been the nucleus of the [Sphoṭa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spho%E1%B9%ADa "Sphoṭa") doctrine enunciated by [Bhartṛhari](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bhart%E1%B9%9Bhari "Bhartṛhari") in the 5th century, in which he elaborates the word-universal as the superposition of two structures — the meaning-universal or the [semantic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semantic "Semantic") structure (_artha-jāti_) is superposed on the sound-universal or the [phonological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phonological "Phonological") structure (_śabda-jāti_).

In the tradition of scholars like [Pingala](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pingala "Pingala"), Kātyāyana was also interested in mathematics. Here his text on the sulvasutras dealt with [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry"), and extended the treatment of the [Pythagorean theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagorean_theorem "Pythagorean theorem") as first presented in 800 BCE by [Baudhayana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baudhayana "Baudhayana").[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K%C4%81ty%C4%81yana#cite_note-9)

Kātyāyana belonged to the [Aindra School of Grammar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aindra_School_of_Grammar "Aindra School of Grammar")[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_].
