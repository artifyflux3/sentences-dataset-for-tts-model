**Guillemets** (,[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guillemet#cite_note-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guillemet#cite_note-2)also ,[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guillemet#cite_note-3) ,[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guillemet#cite_note-4)French:[[ɡij(ə)mɛ]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/French "Help:IPA/French")) are a pair of [punctuation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Punctuation "Punctuation") marks in the form of sideways double [chevrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chevron_(insignia) "Chevron (insignia)"), « and », used as [quotation marks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quotation_mark "Quotation mark") in some languages. In some of these languages, "single" guillemets, ‹ and ›, are used for a quotation inside another quotation. Guillemets are not conventionally used in [English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English_language "English language").

Guillemets may also be called **angle**, **Latin**, **Castilian**, **Spanish**, or **French quotes/quotation marks**.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

_Guillemet_ is a [diminutive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diminutive "Diminutive") of the French name _\_Guillaume\__, apparently after the French [printer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Printing "Printing") and [punchcutter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Punchcutter "Punchcutter")[Guillaume Le Bé](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guillaume_Le_B%C3%A9 "Guillaume Le Bé") (1525–1598),[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guillemet#cite_note-Microsoft-etym-5) though he did not invent the symbols: they first appear in a 1527 book printed by [Josse Bade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jodocus_Badius "Jodocus Badius").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guillemet#cite_note-6)

In [Adobe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adobe_Inc. "Adobe Inc.") software, its file format specifications, and in all fonts derived from these that contain the characters, the glyph names are incorrectly spelled `guillemotleft` and `guillemotright` (a [malapropism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malapropism "Malapropism"): [guillemot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guillemot "Guillemot") is actually a species of seabird). Adobe has acknowledged the error.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guillemet#cite_note-7) Likewise, [X11](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X_Window_System "X Window System") mistakenly uses `XK_guillemotleft` and `XK_guillemotright` to name keys producing the characters.

Guillemets are smaller than [less-than](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Less-than_sign "Less-than sign") and [greater-than signs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greater-than_sign "Greater-than sign"), which in turn are smaller than [angle brackets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle_bracket "Angle bracket").

Guillemets are used pointing outwards (**«**like this**»**) to indicate speech in these languages and regions:

*   [Albanian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albanian_language "Albanian language")
*   [Arabic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabic "Arabic")
*   [Armenian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Armenian_language "Armenian language")
*   [Azerbaijani](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Azerbaijani_language "Azerbaijani language") (mostly in the [Cyrillic script](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Azerbaijani_alphabet "Azerbaijani alphabet"))
*   [Belarusian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belarusian_language "Belarusian language")
*   [Breton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Breton_language "Breton language")
*   [Bulgarian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bulgarian_language "Bulgarian language") (rarely used; „...“ is official)
*   [Catalan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catalan_language "Catalan language")
*   [Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_language "Chinese language") (《 and 》 are used to indicate a book or album title)
*   [Esperanto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Esperanto "Esperanto") (usage varies)
*   [Estonian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Estonian_language "Estonian language") (marked usage; „...“ prevails)
*   [Franco-Provençal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Franco-Proven%C3%A7al "Franco-Provençal")
*   [French](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_language "French language") (spaced out by [thin spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thin_space "Thin space")**«** like this **»**, except no spaces in Switzerland)
*   [Galician](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galician_language "Galician language")
*   [Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_language "Greek language")
*   [Italian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italian_language "Italian language")

*   [Northern](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North%E2%80%93South_differences_in_the_Korean_language "North–South differences in the Korean language")[Korean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_language "Korean language") (in Southern Korean, “...” is used)
*   [Kurdish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kurdish_languages "Kurdish languages")
*   [Latvian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latvian_language "Latvian language")[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guillemet#cite_note-8) (_stūrainās pēdiņas_)
*   [Norwegian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norwegian_language "Norwegian language")
*   [Persian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persian_language "Persian language")
*   [Portuguese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portuguese_language "Portuguese language") (used mostly in [European Portuguese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Portuguese "European Portuguese"), due to its presence in [typical computer keyboards](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/QWERTY#Portugal "QWERTY"); considered obsolete in [Brazilian Portuguese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brazilian_Portuguese "Brazilian Portuguese"))
*   [Romanian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Romanian_language "Romanian language"); only to indicate a quotation within a quotation
*   [Russian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_language "Russian language"), and [some languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Languages_of_the_Soviet_Union "Languages of the Soviet Union") of the [former Soviet Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Post-Soviet_states "Post-Soviet states") using [Cyrillic script](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyrillic_script "Cyrillic script") („...“ is also used for nested quotes and in hand-written text.)
*   [Spanish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spanish_language "Spanish language") (uncommon in daily usage, but commonly used in publishing)
*   [Swiss languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Languages_of_Switzerland "Languages of Switzerland")
*   [Turkish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turkish_language "Turkish language") (dated usage; almost entirely replaced with “...” by late 20th century)
*   [Uyghur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uyghur_language "Uyghur language")
*   [Ukrainian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ukrainian_language "Ukrainian language")
*   [Uzbek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uzbek_language "Uzbek language") (mostly in the [Cyrillic script](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uzbek_alphabet "Uzbek alphabet"))
*   [Vietnamese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vietnamese_language "Vietnamese language") (previously, now “...” is official)

Guillemets are used pointing inwards (**»**like this**«**) to indicate speech in these languages:

*   [Croatian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Croatian_language "Croatian language") (preferred by typographers,[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guillemet#cite_note-9) alternate pair „...“ is in common use)
*   [Czech](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Czech_language "Czech language") (traditional but declining usage; „...“ prevails)
*   [Danish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Danish_language "Danish language") (“...” is also used)
*   [Esperanto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Esperanto "Esperanto") (very uncommon)
*   [German](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_language "German language") (guillemets are preferred for books, while „...“ is preferred in newspapers and handwriting; see above for usage in Swiss German)
*   [Hungarian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hungarian_language "Hungarian language") (only used „inside a section »as a secondary quote«marked by the usual quotes” like this)
*   [Polish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polish_language "Polish language") (used to indicate a quote inside a quote as defined by dictionaries; more common usage in practice. See also: [Polish orthography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polish_orthography#Punctuation "Polish orthography"))
*   [Serbian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Serbian_language "Serbian language") (marked usage; „...“ prevails)
*   [Slovak](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slovak_language "Slovak language") (traditional but declining usage; „...“ prevails)
*   [Slovene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slovene_language "Slovene language") („...“ and “...” also used)
*   [Swedish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swedish_language "Swedish language") (this style, and»...» are considered typographically fancy; ”...” is the common form of quotation)

Guillemets are used pointing right (**»**like this**»**) to indicate speech in these languages:

*   [Finnish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finnish_language "Finnish language") (”...” is the common and correct form)
*   [Swedish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swedish_language "Swedish language") (this style, and»...«are considered typographically fancy; ”...” is the common form of quotation)

In Quebec, the right-hand guillemet, », called a _guillemet itératif_, is used as a [ditto mark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ditto_mark "Ditto mark").[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guillemet#cite_note-10)

Guillemets are used in [Unified Modeling Language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unified_Modeling_Language "Unified Modeling Language") to indicate a [stereotype](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stereotype_(UML) "Stereotype (UML)") of a standard element.

[Microsoft Word](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microsoft_Word "Microsoft Word") uses guillemets when creating [mail merges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mail_merge "Mail merge"). Microsoft use these punctuation marks to denote a mail merge "field", such as «Title», «AddressBlock» or «GreetingLine». On the final printout, the guillemet-marked tags are replaced by each instance of the corresponding data item intended for that field by the user.

Double guillemets are present in many 8-bit [extended ASCII](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extended_ASCII "Extended ASCII") character sets. They were at 0xAE and 0xAF (174 and 175) in [CP437](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CP437 "CP437") on the IBM PC, and 0xC7 and 0xC8 in [Mac OS Roman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mac_OS_Roman "Mac OS Roman"), and placed in several of [ISO 8859](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO_8859 "ISO 8859") code pages (namely: [-1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO_8859-1 "ISO 8859-1"), [-7](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO_8859-7 "ISO 8859-7"), [-8](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO_8859-8 "ISO 8859-8"), [-9](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO_8859-9 "ISO 8859-9"), [-13](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO_8859-13 "ISO 8859-13"), [-15](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO_8859-15 "ISO 8859-15"), [-16](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO_8859-16 "ISO 8859-16")) at 0xAB and 0xBB (171 and 187).

Microsoft added the single guillemets to [CP1252](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CP1252 "CP1252") and similar sets used in Windows at 0x8B and 0x9B (139 and 155) (where the ISO standard placed [C1 control codes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C1_control_code "C1 control code")).

The ISO 8859 locations were inherited by Unicode, which added the single guillemets at new locations:

*   U+00AB«LEFT-POINTING DOUBLE ANGLE QUOTATION MARK (&laquo;)
*   U+00BB»RIGHT-POINTING DOUBLE ANGLE QUOTATION MARK (&raquo;)
*   U+2039‹SINGLE LEFT-POINTING ANGLE QUOTATION MARK (&lsaquo;)
*   U+203A›SINGLE RIGHT-POINTING ANGLE QUOTATION MARK (&rsaquo;)

Despite their names, the characters are mirrored when used in [right-to-left](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Text_direction "Text direction") contexts.

*   A related pair of symbols, '[angle brackets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#Angle_brackets "Bracket")' (a single chevron), `⟨` and `⟩`, is used for another purpose, in mathematics and computing.
*   [Chevron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chevron_(insignia) "Chevron (insignia)")– V-shaped or inverted-V-shaped symbol (as an insignia)

*   [Computer keyboard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_keyboard "Computer keyboard")– Data input device
*   [Unicode input](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unicode_input "Unicode input")– Input characters using their Unicode code points, such as the guillemets
