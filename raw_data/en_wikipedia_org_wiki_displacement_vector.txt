In [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry") and [mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanics "Mechanics"), a **displacement** is a [vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_vector "Euclidean vector") whose length is the shortest [distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distance "Distance") from the initial to the final [position](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Position_(vector) "Position (vector)") of a point P undergoing [motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Motion "Motion").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Displacement_(vector)#cite_note-1) It quantifies both the distance and [direction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direction_(geometry) "Direction (geometry)") of the net or total motion along a straight line from the initial position to the final position of the point [trajectory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trajectory "Trajectory"). A displacement may be identified with the [translation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Translation_(geometry) "Translation (geometry)") that maps the initial position to the final position. Displacement is the shift in location when an object in motion changes from one position to another.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Displacement_(vector)#cite_note-2) For motion over a given interval of time, the displacement divided by the length of the time interval defines the [average velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Average_velocity "Average velocity") (a vector), whose magnitude is the [average speed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Average_speed "Average speed") (a scalar quantity).

Formulation
-----------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Displacement_(geometry)&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Formulation")]

A displacement may be formulated as a _[relative position](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relative\_position "Relative position")_ (resulting from the motion), that is, as the final position _x_ f of a point relative to its initial position _x_ i. The corresponding displacement vector can be defined as the [difference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_space#Subtraction_and_Weyl's_axioms "Affine space") between the final and initial positions: s=x f−x i=Δ x{\displaystyle s=x_{\textrm {f}}-x_{\textrm {i}}=\Delta {x}}![Image 17: {\displaystyle s=x_{\textrm {f}}-x_{\textrm {i}}=\Delta {x}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47171f63649e331e6ebfc41f6e5c4f2a12889af1)

Rigid body
----------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Displacement_(geometry)&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Rigid body")]

In dealing with the motion of a [rigid body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rigid_body "Rigid body"), the term _displacement_ may also include the [rotations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation "Rotation") of the body. In this case, the displacement of a particle of the body is called **linear displacement** (displacement along a line), while the rotation of the body is called _[angular displacement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular\_displacement "Angular displacement")_.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Displacement_(vector)#cite_note-3)

Derivatives
-----------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Displacement_(geometry)&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Derivatives")]

For a position vector s{\displaystyle \mathbf {s} }![Image 18: {\displaystyle \mathbf {s} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/644ae690160e658898a141e568a7fb0ee6040004) that is a function of time t{\displaystyle t}![Image 19: {\displaystyle t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/65658b7b223af9e1acc877d848888ecdb4466560), the derivatives can be computed with respect to t{\displaystyle t}![Image 20: {\displaystyle t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/65658b7b223af9e1acc877d848888ecdb4466560). The first two derivatives are frequently encountered in physics.

These common names correspond to terminology used in basic kinematics.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Displacement_(vector)#cite_note-stewart-4) By extension, the higher order derivatives can be computed in a similar fashion. Study of these higher order derivatives can improve approximations of the original displacement function. Such higher-order terms are required in order to accurately represent the displacement function as [a sum of an infinite series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taylor_series "Taylor series"), enabling several analytical techniques in engineering and physics. The fourth order derivative is called [jounce](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jounce "Jounce").

Discussion
----------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Displacement_(geometry)&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Discussion")]

In considering motions of objects over time, the instantaneous [velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity "Velocity") of the object is the rate of change of the displacement as a function of time. The instantaneous [speed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed "Speed"), then, is distinct from velocity, or the [time rate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_rate "Time rate") of change of the distance travelled along a specific path. The velocity may be equivalently defined as the time rate of change of the position vector. If one considers a moving initial position, or equivalently a moving origin (e.g. an initial position or origin which is fixed to a train wagon, which in turn moves on its rail track), the velocity of P (e.g. a point representing the position of a passenger walking on the train) may be referred to as a _[relative velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relative\_velocity "Relative velocity")_; this is opposed to an _[absolute velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute\_velocity "Absolute velocity")_, which is computed with respect to a point and coordinate axes which are considered to be at rest (a [inertial frame of reference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inertial_frame_of_reference "Inertial frame of reference") such as, for instance, a point fixed on the floor of the train station and the usual vertical and horizontal directions).
