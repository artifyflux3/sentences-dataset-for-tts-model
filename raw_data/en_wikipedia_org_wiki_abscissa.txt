Cartesian plane with marked points (signed ordered pairs of coordinates). For any point, the _abscissa_ is the first value (x coordinate), and the _ordinate_ is the second value (y coordinate).

In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), the **abscissa** (; plural _abscissae_ or _abscissas_) and the **ordinate** are respectively the first and second [coordinate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate "Coordinate") of a [point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_(geometry) "Point (geometry)") in a [Cartesian coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system "Cartesian coordinate system"):[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abscissa#cite_note-WolframAlpha-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abscissa#cite_note-2)

**abscissa**![Image 2: {\displaystyle \equiv x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ba01a0c837740f00c53b65ca1c8ea97a805dc749)-axis (horizontal) coordinate**ordinate**![Image 3: {\displaystyle \equiv y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e5cbf7f98f70d074d1c61f65f28b2043c906609c)-axis (vertical) coordinate
Together they form an [ordered pair](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordered_pair "Ordered pair") which defines the location of a point in two-dimensional [rectangular space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangular_coordinate_system "Rectangular coordinate system").

More technically, the abscissa of a point is the signed measure of its projection on the primary axis. Its [absolute value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_value "Absolute value") is the distance between the projection and the [origin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Origin_(mathematics) "Origin (mathematics)") of the axis, and its [sign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sign_(mathematics) "Sign (mathematics)") is given by the location on the projection relative to the origin (before: negative; after: positive). Similarly, the ordinate of a point is the signed measure of its projection on the secondary axis. [In three dimensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system "Cartesian coordinate system"), the third direction is sometimes referred to as the _[applicate](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/applicate#Noun "wiktionary:applicate")_.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abscissa#cite_note-3)

Though the word "abscissa" (from [Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin_language "Latin language")_linea abscissa_'a line cut off') has been used at least since _De Practica Geometrie_ (1220) by [Fibonacci](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fibonacci "Fibonacci") (Leonardo of Pisa), its use in its modern sense may be due to Venetian mathematician [Stefano degli Angeli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stefano_degli_Angeli "Stefano degli Angeli") in his work _Miscellaneum Hyperbolicum, et Parabolicum_ (1659).[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abscissa#cite_note-4) Historically, the term was used in the more general sense of a 'distance'.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abscissa#cite_note-5)

In his 1892 work _\_Vorlesungen über die Geschichte der Mathematik\__ ("_Lectures on history of mathematics_"), volume 2, German [historian of mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_mathematics "History of mathematics")[Moritz Cantor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moritz_Cantor "Moritz Cantor") writes:

> _Gleichwohl ist durch [Stefano degli Angeli] vermuthlich ein Wort in den mathematischen Sprachschatz eingeführt worden, welches gerade in der analytischen Geometrie sich als zukunftsreich bewährt hat. […] Wir kennen keine ältere Benutzung des Wortes Abscisse in lateinischen Originalschriften. Vielleicht kommt das Wort in Uebersetzungen der [Apollonischen Kegelschnitte](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apollonius\_of\_Perga "Apollonius of Perga") vor, wo Buch I Satz 20 von ἀποτεμνομέναις die Rede ist, wofür es kaum ein entsprechenderes lateinisches Wort als abscissa geben möchte._[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abscissa#cite_note-6)
> 
> 
> At the same time it was presumably by [Stefano degli Angeli] that a word was introduced into the mathematical vocabulary for which especially in analytic geometry the future proved to have much in store. […] We know of no earlier use of the word _abscissa_ in Latin original texts. Maybe the word appears in translations of the [Apollonian conics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apollonius_of_Perga "Apollonius of Perga"), where [in] Book I, Chapter 20 there is mention of _ἀποτεμνομέναις,_ for which there would hardly be a more appropriate Latin word than _abscissa_.

The use of the word _ordinate_ is related to the Latin phrase _linea ordinata appliicata_ 'line applied parallel'.

In parametric equations
-----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Abscissa_and_ordinate&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: In parametric equations")]

In a somewhat obsolete variant usage, the abscissa of a point may also refer to any number that describes the point's location along some path, e.g. the parameter of a [parametric equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parametric_equation "Parametric equation").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abscissa#cite_note-WolframAlpha-1) Used in this way, the abscissa can be thought of as a coordinate-geometry analog to the [independent variable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dependent_and_independent_variables "Dependent and independent variables") in a [mathematical model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_model "Mathematical model") or experiment (with any ordinates filling a role analogous to [dependent variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dependent_and_independent_variables "Dependent and independent variables")).
