A cross section of Van Allen radiation belts

The **Van Allen radiation belt** is a zone of [energetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy "Energy")[charged particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charged_particle "Charged particle"), most of which originate from the [solar wind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_wind "Solar wind"), that are captured by and held around a planet by that planet's [magnetosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetosphere "Magnetosphere"). [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth "Earth") has two such belts, and sometimes others may be temporarily created. The belts are named after [James Van Allen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Van_Allen "James Van Allen"), who published an article describing the belts in 1958.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-dordn-2)

Earth's two main belts extend from an [altitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Altitude "Altitude") of about 640 to 58,000 km (400 to 36,040 mi)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-3) above the surface, in which region [radiation levels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiation_level "Radiation level") vary. The belts are in the inner region of [Earth's magnetic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_magnetic_field "Earth's magnetic field"). They trap energetic [electrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron "Electron") and [protons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton "Proton"). Other nuclei, such as [alpha particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_particle "Alpha particle"), are less prevalent. Most of the particles that form the belts are thought to come from the solar wind while others arrive as [cosmic rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_ray "Cosmic ray").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-howstuffworks_van_allen_belts-4) By trapping the solar wind, the magnetic field deflects those energetic particles and protects [the atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth "Atmosphere of Earth") from destruction.

The belts endanger [satellites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satellite "Satellite"), which must have their sensitive components protected with adequate shielding if they spend significant time near that zone. [Apollo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apollo_program "Apollo program") astronauts going through the Van Allen belts received a very low and harmless dose of radiation.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-popsci.com-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-Woods-6)

In 2013, the [Van Allen Probes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_Probes "Van Allen Probes") detected a transient, third radiation belt, which persisted for four weeks.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-7)

[Kristian Birkeland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kristian_Birkeland "Kristian Birkeland"), [Carl Størmer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_St%C3%B8rmer "Carl Størmer"), [Nicholas Christofilos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nicholas_Christofilos "Nicholas Christofilos"), and Enrico Medi had investigated the possibility of trapped charged particles in 1895, forming a theoretical basis for the formation of radiation belts.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-8) The second Soviet satellite [Sputnik 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sputnik_2 "Sputnik 2") which had detectors designed by [Sergei Vernov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sergei_Vernov "Sergei Vernov"),[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-9) followed by the US satellites [Explorer 1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Explorer_1 "Explorer 1") and [Explorer 3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Explorer_3 "Explorer 3"),[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-10) confirmed the existence of the belt in early 1958, later named after [James Van Allen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Van_Allen "James Van Allen") from the [University of Iowa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Iowa "University of Iowa").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-dordn-2) The trapped radiation was first mapped by [Explorer 4](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Explorer_4 "Explorer 4"), [Pioneer 3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pioneer_3 "Pioneer 3"), and [Luna 1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luna_1 "Luna 1").

The term _Van Allen belts_ refers specifically to the radiation belts surrounding Earth; however, similar radiation belts have been discovered around other [planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planet "Planet"). The Sun does not support long-term radiation belts, as it lacks a stable, global dipole field. The Earth's atmosphere limits the belts' particles to regions above 200–1,000 km,[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-intro-11) (124–620 miles) while the belts do not extend past 8 [Earth radii](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth_radius "Earth radius")_R E_.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-intro-11) The belts are confined to a volume which extends about 65[°](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_(angle) "Degree (angle)")[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-intro-11) on either side of the [celestial equator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celestial_sphere "Celestial sphere").

In 1958 the US detonated low yield [nuclear bombs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_bombs "Nuclear bombs") at an altitude of 300 miles, producing a temporary increase in the electron content of the radiation belts.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-12)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-13) The tests, dubbed [Project Argus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Project_Argus "Project Argus"), were designed to test the [Christofilos effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christofilos_effect "Christofilos effect"), the idea that nuclear explosions in space would release sufficient electrons trapped in the Earth's magnetic field to disable the warheads on [intercontinental ballistic missiles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intercontinental_ballistic_missile "Intercontinental ballistic missile").[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-14) The project was discontinued due to the treaty banning atmospheric testing and the fear that additional radiation could prevent the Apollo moon mission.

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Jupiter_radio.jpg/250px-Jupiter_radio.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Jupiter_radio.jpg)

Jupiter's variable radiation belts

The NASA [Van Allen Probes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_Probes "Van Allen Probes") mission aims at understanding (to the point of predictability) how populations of [relativistic electrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_electron_beam "Relativistic electron beam") and ions in space form or change in response to changes in [solar activity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_activity "Solar activity") and the solar wind. [NASA Institute for Advanced Concepts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NASA_Institute_for_Advanced_Concepts "NASA Institute for Advanced Concepts")–funded studies have proposed magnetic scoops to collect [antimatter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antimatter "Antimatter") that naturally occurs in the Van Allen belts of Earth, although only about 10 micrograms of [antiprotons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiproton "Antiproton") are estimated to exist in the entire belt.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-15)

The Van Allen Probes mission successfully launched on August 30, 2012. The primary mission was scheduled to last two years with expendables expected to last four. The probes were deactivated in 2019 after running out of fuel and are expected to [deorbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deorbit "Deorbit") during the 2030s.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-16) NASA's [Goddard Space Flight Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goddard_Space_Flight_Center "Goddard Space Flight Center") manages the [Living With a Star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Living_With_a_Star "Living With a Star") program—of which the Van Allen Probes were a project, along with [Solar Dynamics Observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_Dynamics_Observatory "Solar Dynamics Observatory") (SDO). The [Applied Physics Laboratory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Applied_Physics_Laboratory "Applied Physics Laboratory") was responsible for the implementation and instrument management for the Van Allen Probes.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-17)

Radiation belts exist around other planets and moons in the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System") that have magnetic fields powerful and stable enough to sustain them. Radiation belts have been detected at [Jupiter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jupiter "Jupiter"), [Saturn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saturn "Saturn"), [Uranus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uranus "Uranus") and [Neptune](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune "Neptune") through in-situ observations, such as by the _[Galileo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galileo\_(spacecraft) "Galileo (spacecraft)")_ and _[Juno](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Juno\_(spacecraft) "Juno (spacecraft)")_ spacecraft at Jupiter, _[Cassini–Huygens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens "Cassini–Huygens")_ at Saturn, and fly-bys from the [Voyager program](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voyager_program "Voyager program") and [Pioneer program](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pioneer_program "Pioneer program"). Observations of radio emissions from highly energetic particles that are trapped in a planets magnetic field have also been used to remotely detect radiation belts, including at Jupiter [[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-18) and at the ultracool dwarf [LSR J1835+3259](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LSR_J1835%2B3259 "LSR J1835+3259").[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-19) It is possible that [Mercury](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mercury_(planet) "Mercury (planet)") may be able to trap charged particles in its magnetic field,[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-20) although its highly dynamic magnetosphere (which varies on the order of minutes [[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-21)) may not be able to sustain stable radiation belts. [Venus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Venus "Venus") and [Mars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mars "Mars") do not have radiation belts, as their magnetospheric configurations do not trap energetic charged particles in orbit around the planet.

[Geomagnetic storms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geomagnetic_storm "Geomagnetic storm") can cause electron density to increase or decrease relatively quickly (i.e., approximately one day or less). Longer-timescale processes determine the overall configuration of the belts. After electron injection increases electron density, electron density is often observed to decay exponentially. Those decay time constants are called "lifetimes." Measurements from the Van Allen Probe B's Magnetic Electron Ion Spectrometer (MagEIS) show long electron lifetimes (i.e., longer than 100 days) in the inner belt; short electron lifetimes of around one or two days are observed in the "slot" between the belts; and energy-dependent electron lifetimes of roughly five to 20 days are found in the outer belt.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-22)

[Cutaway drawing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cutaway_drawing "Cutaway drawing") of two radiation belts around Earth: the inner belt (red) dominated by protons and the outer one (blue) by electrons. Image Credit: NASA

The inner Van Allen Belt extends typically from an altitude of 0.2 to 2 Earth radii ([L values](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L-shell "L-shell") of 1.2 to 3) or 1,000 km (620 mi) to 12,000 km (7,500 mi) above the Earth.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-howstuffworks_van_allen_belts-4)[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-23) In certain cases, when solar activity is stronger or in geographical areas such as the [South Atlantic Anomaly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Atlantic_Anomaly "South Atlantic Anomaly"), the inner boundary may decline to roughly 200 km[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-24) above the Earth's surface. The inner belt contains high concentrations of electrons in the range of hundreds of [keV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronvolt "Electronvolt") and energetic protons with energies exceeding 100 MeV—trapped by the relatively strong magnetic fields in the region (as compared to the outer belt).[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-25)

It is thought that proton energies exceeding 50 MeV in the lower belts at lower altitudes are the result of the [beta decay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beta_decay "Beta decay") of [neutrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron "Neutron") created by cosmic ray collisions with nuclei of the upper atmosphere. The source of lower energy protons is believed to be proton diffusion, due to changes in the magnetic field during geomagnetic storms.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-26)

Due to the slight offset of the belts from Earth's geometric center, the inner Van Allen belt makes its closest approach to the surface at the [South Atlantic Anomaly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Atlantic_Anomaly "South Atlantic Anomaly").[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-Goddard-27)[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-28)

In March 2014, a pattern resembling "zebra stripes" was observed in the radiation belts by the Radiation Belt Storm Probes Ion Composition Experiment (RBSPICE) onboard [Van Allen Probes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_Probes "Van Allen Probes"). The initial theory proposed in 2014 was that—due to the tilt in Earth's magnetic field axis—the planet's rotation generated an oscillating, weak electric field that permeates through the entire inner radiation belt.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-29) A 2016 study instead concluded that the zebra stripes were an imprint of [ionospheric winds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ionospheric_dynamo_region "Ionospheric dynamo region") on radiation belts.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-30)

[![Image 4](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Birkeland-anode-globe-fig259.jpg/330px-Birkeland-anode-globe-fig259.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Birkeland-anode-globe-fig259.jpg)

Laboratory simulation of the Van Allen belt's influence on the Solar Wind; these aurora-like [Birkeland currents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Birkeland_current "Birkeland current") were created by the scientist [Kristian Birkeland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kristian_Birkeland "Kristian Birkeland") in his [terrella](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terrella "Terrella"), a magnetized anode globe in an evacuated chamber

The outer belt consists mainly of high-energy (0.1–10[MeV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronvolt "Electronvolt")) electrons trapped by the Earth's magnetosphere. It is more variable than the inner belt, as it is more easily influenced by solar activity. It is almost [toroidal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torus "Torus") in shape, beginning at an altitude of 3 Earth radii and extending to 10 Earth radii (_R E_)—13,000 to 60,000 kilometres (8,100 to 37,300 mi) above the Earth's surface.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] Its greatest intensity is usually around 4 to 5 _R E_. The outer electron radiation belt is mostly produced by inward radial diffusion[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-31)[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-32) and local acceleration[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-nature437-33) due to transfer of energy from whistler-mode [plasma waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Waves_in_plasmas "Waves in plasmas") to radiation belt electrons. Radiation belt electrons are also constantly removed by collisions with Earth's atmosphere,[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-nature437-33) losses to the [magnetopause](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetopause "Magnetopause"), and their outward radial diffusion. The [gyroradii](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gyroradius "Gyroradius") of energetic protons would be large enough to bring them into contact with the Earth's atmosphere. Within this belt, the electrons have a high [flux](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flux "Flux") and at the outer edge (close to the magnetopause), where [geomagnetic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_magnetic_field "Earth's magnetic field") lines open into the [geomagnetic "tail"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetosphere#Magnetotail "Magnetosphere"), the flux of energetic electrons can drop to the low interplanetary levels within about 100 km (62 mi)—a decrease by a factor of 1,000.

In 2014, it was discovered that the inner edge of the outer belt is characterized by a very sharp transition, below which highly relativistic electrons (> 5MeV) cannot penetrate.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-34) The reason for this shield-like behavior is not well understood.

The trapped particle population of the outer belt is varied, containing electrons and various ions. Most of the ions are in the form of energetic protons, but a certain percentage are alpha particles and O+ oxygen ions—similar to those in the [ionosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ionosphere "Ionosphere") but much more energetic. This mixture of ions suggests that [ring current](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ring_current "Ring current") particles probably originate from more than one source.

The outer belt is larger than the inner belt, and its particle population fluctuates widely. Energetic (radiation) particle fluxes can increase and decrease dramatically in response to [geomagnetic storms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geomagnetic_storm "Geomagnetic storm"), which are themselves triggered by magnetic field and plasma disturbances produced by the Sun. The increases are due to storm-related injections and acceleration of particles from the tail of the magnetosphere. Another cause of variability of the outer belt particle populations is the [wave-particle interactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two_stream_instability#Wave-particle_interactions "Two stream instability") with various [plasma waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasma_waves "Plasma waves") in a broad range of frequencies.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-35)

On February 28, 2013, a third radiation belt—consisting of high-energy [ultrarelativistic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultrarelativistic_limit "Ultrarelativistic limit") charged particles—was reported to be discovered. In a news conference by NASA's Van Allen Probe team, it was stated that this third belt is a product of [coronal mass ejection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coronal_mass_ejection "Coronal mass ejection") from the Sun. It has been represented as a separate creation which splits the Outer Belt, like a knife, on its outer side, and exists separately as a storage container of particles for a month's time, before merging once again with the Outer Belt.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-36)

The unusual stability of this third, transient belt has been explained as due to a 'trapping' by the Earth's magnetic field of ultrarelativistic particles as they are lost from the second, traditional outer belt. While the outer zone, which forms and disappears over a day, is highly variable due to interactions with the atmosphere, the ultrarelativistic particles of the third belt are thought not to scatter into the atmosphere, as they are too energetic to interact with atmospheric waves at low latitudes.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-nphys2760-37) This absence of scattering and the trapping allows them to persist for a long time, finally only being destroyed by an unusual event, such as the shock wave from the Sun.

In the belts the flux of particles varies substantially with position, energy, and solar activity.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-38) Measured fluxes of protons with enough energy (>20MeV) to penetrate 0.25mm of aluminum range up to 100,000 per cm 2 per sec. Electron over 1.5MeV can penetrate that thickness of aluminum and their flux ranges up to a million particles per square cenitmeter per second.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-39)

The proton belts contain protons with kinetic energies ranging from about 100 keV, which can penetrate 0.6 μm of [lead](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lead "Lead"), to over 400 MeV, which can penetrate 143 mm of lead.[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-40)

Radiation levels in the belts would be dangerous to humans if they were exposed for an extended period of time. The Apollo missions minimised hazards for astronauts by sending spacecraft at high speeds through the thinner areas of the upper belts, bypassing inner belts completely, except for the Apollo 14 mission where the spacecraft traveled through the heart of the trapped radiation belts.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-Goddard-27)[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-41)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-popsci.com-5)[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-42)

Antimatter confinement
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Van_Allen_radiation_belt&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Antimatter confinement")]

In 2011, a study confirmed earlier speculation that the Van Allen belt could confine antiparticles. The [Payload for Antimatter Matter Exploration and Light-nuclei Astrophysics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PAMELA_detector "PAMELA detector") (PAMELA) experiment detected levels of [antiprotons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiproton "Antiproton") orders of magnitude higher than are expected from normal [particle decays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_decay "Particle decay") while passing through the [South Atlantic Anomaly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Atlantic_Anomaly "South Atlantic Anomaly"). This suggests the Van Allen belts confine a significant flux of antiprotons produced by the interaction of the Earth's upper atmosphere with cosmic rays.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-43) The energy of the antiprotons has been measured in the range from 60 to 750 MeV.

The very high energy released in antimatter annihilation has led to proposals to harness these antiprotons for spacecraft propulsion. The concept relies on the development of antimatter collectors and containers.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-44)

Implications for space travel
-----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Van_Allen_radiation_belt&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Implications for space travel")]

[![Image 8](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Comparison_satellite_navigation_orbits.svg/250px-Comparison_satellite_navigation_orbits.svg.png)](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b4/Comparison_satellite_navigation_orbits.svg)

Clickable image, highlighting [medium altitude orbits around Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medium_Earth_Orbit "Medium Earth Orbit"),[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-45) from [Low Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit "Low Earth orbit") to the lowest [High Earth orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High_Earth_orbit "High Earth orbit") ([geostationary orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geostationary_Earth_orbit "Geostationary Earth orbit") and its [graveyard orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graveyard_orbit "Graveyard orbit"), at one ninth of the [Moon's orbital distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunar_distance "Lunar distance")),[[b]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-46) with the [Van Allen radiation belts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt) and the [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth "Earth") to scale

Spacecraft travelling beyond [low Earth orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit "Low Earth orbit") enter the zone of radiation of the Van Allen belts. Beyond the belts, they face additional hazards from cosmic rays and [solar particle events](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_particle_event "Solar particle event"). A region between the inner and outer Van Allen belts lies at 2 to 4 Earth radii and is sometimes referred to as the "safe zone".[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-47)[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-48)

[Solar cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_cell "Solar cell"), [integrated circuits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integrated_circuit "Integrated circuit"), and [sensors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sensor "Sensor") can be damaged by radiation. Geomagnetic storms occasionally damage [electronic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronics "Electronics") components on spacecraft. Miniaturization and [digitization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digital_electronics "Digital electronics") of electronics and [logic circuits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logic_gate "Logic gate") have made satellites more vulnerable to radiation, as the total [electric charge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_charge "Electric charge") in these circuits is now small enough so as to be comparable with the charge of incoming ions. Electronics on satellites must be [hardened](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiation_hardening "Radiation hardening") against radiation to operate reliably. The [Chandra Space Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chandra_Space_Telescope "Chandra Space Telescope"), has its sensors turned off when passing through the Van Allen belts.[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-49) The [INTEGRAL](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/INTEGRAL "INTEGRAL") space telescope was placed in an orbit designed to avoid time within the belts.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-50)

The [Apollo missions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_Apollo_missions "List of Apollo missions") marked the first event where humans traveled through the Van Allen belts, which was one of several radiation hazards known by mission planners.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-Vernon-51) The astronauts had low exposure in the Van Allen belts due to the short period of time spent flying through them.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-popsci.com-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-Woods-6)

It is generally understood that the inner and outer Van Allen belts result from different processes. The inner belt is mainly composed of energetic protons produced from the decay of [neutrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutrons "Neutrons"), which are themselves the result of [cosmic ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_ray "Cosmic ray") collisions in the upper atmosphere. The outer Van Allen belt consists mainly of electrons. They are injected from the geomagnetic tail following geomagnetic storms, and are subsequently energized through [wave-particle interactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two_stream_instability#Wave-particle_interactions "Two stream instability").

In the inner belt, particles that originate from the Sun are trapped in the Earth's magnetic field. Particles spiral along the magnetic lines of flux as they move "latitudinally" along those lines. As particles move toward the poles, the magnetic field line density increases, and their "latitudinal" velocity is slowed and can be reversed, deflecting the particles back towards the equatorial region, causing them to bounce back and forth between the Earth's poles.[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-52) In addition to both spiralling around and moving along the flux lines, the electrons drift slowly in an eastward direction, while the protons drift westward.

The gap between the inner and outer Van Allen belts is sometimes called the "safe zone" or "safe slot", and is the location of [medium Earth orbits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medium_Earth_orbit "Medium Earth orbit"). The gap is caused by the [VLF radio waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Very_low_frequency "Very low frequency"), which scatter particles in [pitch angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pitch_angle_(particle_motion) "Pitch angle (particle motion)"), which adds new ions to the atmosphere. Solar outbursts can also dump particles into the gap, but those drain out in a matter of days. The VLF radio waves were previously thought to be generated by turbulence in the radiation belts, but recent work by [J.L. Green](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_L._Green "James L. Green") of the [Goddard Space Flight Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goddard_Space_Flight_Center "Goddard Space Flight Center")[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] compared maps of lightning activity collected by the [Microlab 1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microlab_1 "Microlab 1") spacecraft with data on radio waves in the radiation-belt gap from the [IMAGE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IMAGE_(spacecraft) "IMAGE (spacecraft)") spacecraft; the results suggest that the radio waves are actually generated by lightning within Earth's atmosphere. The generated radio waves strike the ionosphere at the correct angle to pass through only at high latitudes, where the lower ends of the gap approach the upper atmosphere. These results are still being debated in the scientific community.

Draining the charged particles from the Van Allen belts would open up new orbits for satellites and make travel safer for astronauts.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-choi-53) Draining radiation belts around other planets has also been proposed, for example, before exploring [Europa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Europa_(moon) "Europa (moon)"), which orbits within [Jupiter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jupiter "Jupiter")'s radiation belt.[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-54) Since the radiation belts are part of a complex system, it is unknown if there could be [unintended consequences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unintended_consequences "Unintended consequences") to removing these radiation belts.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-choi-53)

One concept proposed to drain and remove the radiation fields of the Van Allen radiation belts is known as High Voltage Orbiting Long Tether, or HiVOLT, a concept proposed by Russian physicist [V. V. Danilov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Valentin_Danilov "Valentin Danilov") and further refined by [Robert P. Hoyt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_P._Hoyt "Robert P. Hoyt") and [Robert L. Forward](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_L._Forward "Robert L. Forward").[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-55) that surround the Earth.[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-mirnov1996-56) Another proposal for draining the Van Allen belts involves beaming very-low-frequency (VLF) radio waves from the ground into the Van Allen belts.[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt#cite_note-57)
