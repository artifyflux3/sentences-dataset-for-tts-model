In [science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Science "Science"), a **null result** is a result without the expected content: that is, the proposed result is absent.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Null_result#cite_note-1) It is an experimental outcome which does not show an otherwise expected effect. This does not imply a result of zero or nothing, simply a result that does not support the [hypothesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypothesis "Hypothesis").

In [statistical hypothesis testing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistical_hypothesis_testing "Statistical hypothesis testing"), a null result occurs when an experimental result is not significantly different from what is to be expected under the [null hypothesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Null_hypothesis "Null hypothesis"); its probability (under the null hypothesis) does not exceed the [significance level](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Significance_level "Significance level"), i.e., the threshold set prior to testing for rejection of the null hypothesis. The significance level varies, but common choices include 0.10, 0.05, and 0.01.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Null_result#cite_note-2) However, a non-significant result does not necessarily mean that an effect is absent.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Null_result#cite_note-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Null_result#cite_note-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Null_result#cite_note-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Null_result#cite_note-6)

As an example in [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics"), the results of the [Michelson–Morley experiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michelson%E2%80%93Morley_experiment "Michelson–Morley experiment") were of this type, as it did not detect the expected velocity relative to the postulated [luminiferous aether](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luminiferous_aether "Luminiferous aether"). This experiment's famous failed detection, commonly referred to as the _null result_, contributed to the development of [special relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_relativity "Special relativity"). The experiment did appear to measure a non-zero "drift", but the value was far too small to account for the theoretically expected results; it is generally thought to be inside the [noise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noise_(signal_processing) "Noise (signal processing)") level of the experiment.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Null_result#cite_note-7)

Despite similar quality of execution and [design](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Design_of_experiments "Design of experiments"),[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Null_result#cite_note-Easterbrook-8) papers with statistically significant results are three times more likely to be published than those with null results.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Null_result#cite_note-9) This unduly motivates researchers to manipulate their practices to ensure statistically significant results, such as by [data dredging](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_dredging "Data dredging").[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Null_result#cite_note-10)

Many factors contribute to publication bias.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Null_result#cite_note-Rothstein2005-11)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Null_result#cite_note-12) For instance, once a scientific finding is well established, it may become newsworthy to publish reliable papers that fail to reject the null hypothesis.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Null_result#cite_note-13) Most commonly, investigators simply decline to submit results, leading to [non-response bias](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-response_bias "Non-response bias"). Investigators may also assume they made a mistake, find that the null result fails to support a known finding, lose interest in the topic, or anticipate that others will be uninterested in the null results.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Null_result#cite_note-Easterbrook-8)

There are several scientific journals dedicated to the publication of negative or null results, including the following:

*   _[Journal of Negative Results in Biomedicine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Journal\_of\_Negative\_Results\_in\_Biomedicine "Journal of Negative Results in Biomedicine")_ (defunct)
*   _[Journal of Pharmaceutical Negative Results](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Journal\_of\_Pharmaceutical\_Negative\_Results "Journal of Pharmaceutical Negative Results")_
*   _[Journal of Unsolved Questions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Journal\_of\_Unsolved\_Questions "Journal of Unsolved Questions")_

While it is not exclusively dedicated to publishing negative results, [BMC Research Notes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BioMed_Central "BioMed Central") also publishes negative results in the form of research or data notes.
