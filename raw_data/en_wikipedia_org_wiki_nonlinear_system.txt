In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics") and [science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Science "Science"), a **nonlinear system** (or a **non-linear system**) is a [system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/System "System") in which the change of the output is not [proportional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proportionality_(mathematics) "Proportionality (mathematics)") to the change of the input.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nonlinear_system#cite_note-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nonlinear_system#cite_note-2) Nonlinear problems are of interest to [engineers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineer "Engineer"), [biologists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biologist "Biologist"),[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nonlinear_system#cite_note-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nonlinear_system#cite_note-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nonlinear_system#cite_note-5)[physicists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physicist "Physicist"),[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nonlinear_system#cite_note-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nonlinear_system#cite_note-7)[mathematicians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematician "Mathematician"), and many other [scientists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientist "Scientist") since most systems are inherently nonlinear in nature.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nonlinear_system#cite_note-8) Nonlinear [dynamical systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynamical_system "Dynamical system"), describing changes in variables over time, may appear chaotic, unpredictable, or counterintuitive, contrasting with much simpler [linear systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_system "Linear system").

Typically, the behavior of a nonlinear system is described in mathematics by a **nonlinear system of equations**, which is a set of simultaneous [equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equation "Equation") in which the unknowns (or the unknown functions in the case of [differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_equation "Differential equation")) appear as variables of a [polynomial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial "Polynomial") of degree higher than one or in the argument of a [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)") which is not a polynomial of degree one. In other words, in a nonlinear system of equations, the equation(s) to be solved cannot be written as a [linear combination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_combination "Linear combination") of the unknown [variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variable_(mathematics) "Variable (mathematics)") or [functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)") that appear in them. Systems can be defined as nonlinear, regardless of whether known linear functions appear in the equations. In particular, a differential equation is _linear_ if it is linear in terms of the unknown function and its derivatives, even if nonlinear in terms of the other variables appearing in it.

As nonlinear dynamical equations are difficult to solve, nonlinear systems are commonly approximated by linear equations ([linearization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linearization "Linearization")). This works well up to some accuracy and some range for the input values, but some interesting phenomena such as [solitons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soliton "Soliton"), [chaos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chaos_theory "Chaos theory"),[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nonlinear_system#cite_note-9) and [singularities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_singularity "Mathematical singularity") are hidden by linearization. It follows that some aspects of the dynamic behavior of a nonlinear system can appear to be counterintuitive, unpredictable or even chaotic. Although such chaotic behavior may resemble [random](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Randomness "Randomness") behavior, it is in fact not random. For example, some aspects of the weather are seen to be chaotic, where simple changes in one part of the system produce complex effects throughout. This nonlinearity is one of the reasons why accurate long-term forecasts are impossible with current technology.

Some authors use the term **nonlinear science** for the study of nonlinear systems. This term is disputed by others:

> Using a term like nonlinear science is like referring to the bulk of zoology as the study of non-elephant animals.

For example, an [antilinear map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antilinear_map "Antilinear map") is additive but not homogeneous. The conditions of additivity and homogeneity are often combined in the superposition principle

Nonlinear systems of equations
------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Nonlinear_system&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Nonlinear systems of equations")]

A nonlinear system of equations consists of a set of equations in several variables such that at least one of them is not a [linear equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_equation "Linear equation").

For a single equation of the form ![Image 12: {\displaystyle f(x)=0,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/234abb558dc1c499e7f776c5a3ddd9581152dfda) many methods have been designed; see [Root-finding algorithm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Root-finding_algorithm "Root-finding algorithm"). In the case where f is a [polynomial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial "Polynomial"), one has a _[polynomial equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial\_equation "Polynomial equation")_ such as ![Image 13: {\displaystyle x^{2}+x-1=0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d6465b24065d972602818f67629035203a0af849) The general root-finding algorithms apply to polynomial roots, but, generally they do not find all the roots, and when they fail to find a root, this does not imply that there is no roots. Specific methods for polynomials allow finding all roots or the [real](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number") roots; see [real-root isolation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real-root_isolation "Real-root isolation").

Solving [systems of polynomial equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Systems_of_polynomial_equations "Systems of polynomial equations"), that is finding the common zeros of a set of several polynomials in several variables is a difficult problem for which elaborate algorithms have been designed, such as [Gröbner base](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gr%C3%B6bner_base "Gröbner base") algorithms.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nonlinear_system#cite_note-11)

For the general case of system of equations formed by equating to zero several [differentiable functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differentiable_function "Differentiable function"), the main method is [Newton's method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_method#Systems_of_equations "Newton's method") and its variants. Generally they may provide a solution, but do not provide any information on the number of solutions.

Nonlinear recurrence relations
------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Nonlinear_system&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Nonlinear recurrence relations")]

A nonlinear [recurrence relation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recurrence_relation "Recurrence relation") defines successive terms of a [sequence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sequence "Sequence") as a nonlinear function of preceding terms. Examples of nonlinear recurrence relations are the [logistic map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logistic_map "Logistic map") and the relations that define the various [Hofstadter sequences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hofstadter_sequence "Hofstadter sequence"). Nonlinear discrete models that represent a wide class of nonlinear recurrence relationships include the NARMAX (Nonlinear Autoregressive Moving Average with eXogenous inputs) model and the related [nonlinear system identification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nonlinear_system_identification "Nonlinear system identification") and analysis procedures.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nonlinear_system#cite_note-SAB1-12) These approaches can be used to study a wide class of complex nonlinear behaviors in the time, frequency, and spatio-temporal domains.

Nonlinear differential equations
--------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Nonlinear_system&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Nonlinear differential equations")]

A [system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simultaneous_equations "Simultaneous equations") of [differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_equation "Differential equation") is said to be nonlinear if it is not a [system of linear equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/System_of_linear_equations "System of linear equations"). Problems involving nonlinear differential equations are extremely diverse, and methods of solution or analysis are problem dependent. Examples of nonlinear differential equations are the [Navier–Stokes equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Navier%E2%80%93Stokes_equations "Navier–Stokes equations") in fluid dynamics and the [Lotka–Volterra equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lotka%E2%80%93Volterra_equations "Lotka–Volterra equations") in biology.

One of the greatest difficulties of nonlinear problems is that it is not generally possible to combine known solutions into new solutions. In linear problems, for example, a family of [linearly independent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linearly_independent "Linearly independent") solutions can be used to construct general solutions through the [superposition principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superposition_principle "Superposition principle"). A good example of this is one-dimensional heat transport with [Dirichlet boundary conditions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dirichlet_boundary_condition "Dirichlet boundary condition"), the solution of which can be written as a time-dependent linear combination of sinusoids of differing frequencies; this makes solutions very flexible. It is often possible to find several very specific solutions to nonlinear equations, however the lack of a superposition principle prevents the construction of new solutions.

### Ordinary differential equations

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Nonlinear_system&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Ordinary differential equations")]

First order [ordinary differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordinary_differential_equation "Ordinary differential equation") are often exactly solvable by [separation of variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separation_of_variables "Separation of variables"), especially for autonomous equations. For example, the nonlinear equation

![Image 14: {\displaystyle {\frac {du}{dx}}=-u^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6899a5ebd039f236b442d307af38e91c45609d80)
has ![Image 15: {\displaystyle u={\frac {1}{x+C}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/18fa6bc3048f6c78730036605f24912827e0b9b2) as a general solution (and also the special solution ![Image 16: {\displaystyle u=0,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3066ba3977d1575e96f26d821caf3576bd0af4ee) corresponding to the limit of the general solution when _C_ tends to infinity). The equation is nonlinear because it may be written as

![Image 17: {\displaystyle {\frac {du}{dx}}+u^{2}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/56d01cb1daffa24c4525aca9919e822898618e79)
and the left-hand side of the equation is not a linear function of ![Image 18: {\displaystyle u}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3e6bb763d22c20916ed4f0bb6bd49d7470cffd8) and its derivatives. Note that if the ![Image 19: {\displaystyle u^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/291091343cb7c98ba83d15fdba9fc794a940829f) term were replaced with ![Image 20: {\displaystyle u}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3e6bb763d22c20916ed4f0bb6bd49d7470cffd8), the problem would be linear (the [exponential decay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponential_decay "Exponential decay") problem).

Second and higher order ordinary differential equations (more generally, systems of nonlinear equations) rarely yield [closed-form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed-form_expression "Closed-form expression") solutions, though implicit solutions and solutions involving [nonelementary integrals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nonelementary_integral "Nonelementary integral") are encountered.

Common methods for the qualitative analysis of nonlinear ordinary differential equations include:

*   Examination of any [conserved quantities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conserved_quantities "Conserved quantities"), especially in [Hamiltonian systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamiltonian_system "Hamiltonian system")
*   Examination of dissipative quantities (see [Lyapunov function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lyapunov_function "Lyapunov function")) analogous to conserved quantities
*   Linearization via [Taylor expansion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taylor_expansion "Taylor expansion")
*   Change of variables into something easier to study
*   [Bifurcation theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bifurcation_theory "Bifurcation theory")
*   [Perturbation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perturbation_theory "Perturbation theory") methods (can be applied to algebraic equations too)
*   Existence of solutions of Finite-Duration,[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nonlinear_system#cite_note-13) which can happen under specific conditions for some non-linear ordinary differential equations.

### Partial differential equations

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Nonlinear_system&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Partial differential equations")]

The most common basic approach to studying nonlinear [partial differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_differential_equation "Partial differential equation") is to change the variables (or otherwise transform the problem) so that the resulting problem is simpler (possibly linear). Sometimes, the equation may be transformed into one or more [ordinary differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordinary_differential_equation "Ordinary differential equation"), as seen in [separation of variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separation_of_variables "Separation of variables"), which is always useful whether or not the resulting ordinary differential equation(s) is solvable.

Another common (though less mathematical) tactic, often exploited in fluid and heat mechanics, is to use [scale analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scale_analysis_(mathematics) "Scale analysis (mathematics)") to simplify a general, natural equation in a certain specific [boundary value problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boundary_value_problem "Boundary value problem"). For example, the (very) nonlinear [Navier-Stokes equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Navier-Stokes_equations "Navier-Stokes equations") can be simplified into one linear partial differential equation in the case of transient, laminar, one dimensional flow in a circular pipe; the scale analysis provides conditions under which the flow is laminar and one dimensional and also yields the simplified equation.

Other methods include examining the [characteristics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Method_of_characteristics "Method of characteristics") and using the methods outlined above for ordinary differential equations.

Linearizations of a pendulum

A classic, extensively studied nonlinear problem is the dynamics of a frictionless [pendulum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pendulum_(mathematics) "Pendulum (mathematics)") under the influence of [gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity "Gravity"). Using [Lagrangian mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrangian_mechanics "Lagrangian mechanics"), it may be shown[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nonlinear_system#cite_note-14) that the motion of a pendulum can be described by the [dimensionless](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimensionless "Dimensionless") nonlinear equation

![Image 23: {\displaystyle {\frac {d^{2}\theta }{dt^{2}}}+\sin(\theta )=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/874c2e8a0d854a1d8eb2fc9279b335488564d3b1)
where gravity points "downwards" and ![Image 24: {\displaystyle \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e5ab2664b422d53eb0c7df3b87e1360d75ad9af) is the angle the pendulum forms with its rest position, as shown in the figure at right. One approach to "solving" this equation is to use ![Image 25: {\displaystyle d\theta /dt}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9fe083af9808068de003c10018962791d930c010) as an [integrating factor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integrating_factor "Integrating factor"), which would eventually yield

![Image 26: {\displaystyle \int {\frac {d\theta }{\sqrt {C_{0}+2\cos(\theta )}}}=t+C_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cbacaebec8939b83f97d79268a8e818bf55765cb)
which is an implicit solution involving an [elliptic integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptic_integral "Elliptic integral"). This "solution" generally does not have many uses because most of the nature of the solution is hidden in the [nonelementary integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nonelementary_integral "Nonelementary integral") (nonelementary unless ![Image 27: {\displaystyle C_{0}=2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a7e6968c898cf6bc6c1ac6aa3df45a4a0bb71b6)).

Another way to approach the problem is to linearize any nonlinearity (the sine function term in this case) at the various points of interest through [Taylor expansions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taylor_expansion "Taylor expansion"). For example, the linearization at ![Image 28: {\displaystyle \theta =0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2a7bc6e34b53e0e8a8815159c356b1acccf7ea24), called the small angle approximation, is

![Image 29: {\displaystyle {\frac {d^{2}\theta }{dt^{2}}}+\theta =0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ce90f07eb2959affb81e22076f8e9224a735108f)
since ![Image 30: {\displaystyle \sin(\theta )\approx \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/09cf7dbfccf4569a47c4eeafcfb3f7075d0c4dac) for ![Image 31: {\displaystyle \theta \approx 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/45fa623dea25165982c15695fcc75fe61d9bd404). This is a [simple harmonic oscillator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simple_harmonic_oscillator "Simple harmonic oscillator") corresponding to oscillations of the pendulum near the bottom of its path. Another linearization would be at ![Image 32: {\displaystyle \theta =\pi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ab4db588619489e27efb50a1d0d5aa016c49ce15), corresponding to the pendulum being straight up:

![Image 33: {\displaystyle {\frac {d^{2}\theta }{dt^{2}}}+\pi -\theta =0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b1b0ec2ee1b77e7484cdef080a94a26a0f66807d)
since ![Image 34: {\displaystyle \sin(\theta )\approx \pi -\theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/22d28b5c7c5afa97b37d313a2397240c08ecaf36) for ![Image 35: {\displaystyle \theta \approx \pi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3e40af60fdf1675b2e8a1d87584185280d67971c). The solution to this problem involves [hyperbolic sinusoids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_sinusoid "Hyperbolic sinusoid"), and note that unlike the small angle approximation, this approximation is unstable, meaning that ![Image 36: {\displaystyle |\theta |}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c2880a52da62c0d740bf1f92d9c6ffdbe4772b01) will usually grow without limit, though bounded solutions are possible. This corresponds to the difficulty of balancing a pendulum upright, it is literally an unstable state.

One more interesting linearization is possible around ![Image 37: {\displaystyle \theta =\pi /2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9cc4628b0f731f81bfabcd8edaca00aa186f03bd), around which ![Image 38: {\displaystyle \sin(\theta )\approx 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9105777edb335a85f687b7914f6741cfce405c17):

![Image 39: {\displaystyle {\frac {d^{2}\theta }{dt^{2}}}+1=0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7ccbb5b457c207034fc20a11c2fa3a76d44d959f)
This corresponds to a free fall problem. A very useful qualitative picture of the pendulum's dynamics may be obtained by piecing together such linearizations, as seen in the figure at right. Other techniques may be used to find (exact) [phase portraits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phase_portrait "Phase portrait") and approximate periods.

Types of nonlinear dynamic behaviors
------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Nonlinear_system&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Types of nonlinear dynamic behaviors")]

*   [Amplitude death](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amplitude_death "Amplitude death") – any oscillations present in the system cease due to some kind of interaction with other system or feedback by the same system
*   [Chaos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chaos_theory "Chaos theory") – values of a system cannot be predicted indefinitely far into the future, and fluctuations are [aperiodic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aperiodic "Aperiodic")
*   [Multistability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multistability "Multistability") – the presence of two or more stable states
*   [Solitons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soliton "Soliton") – self-reinforcing solitary waves
*   [Limit cycles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limit_cycle "Limit cycle") – asymptotic periodic orbits to which destabilized fixed points are attracted.
*   [Self-oscillations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-oscillation "Self-oscillation") – feedback oscillations taking place in open dissipative physical systems.
