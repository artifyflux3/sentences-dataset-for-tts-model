In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), a **free module** is a [module](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Module_(mathematics) "Module (mathematics)") that has a _basis_, that is, a [generating set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generating_set_of_a_module "Generating set of a module") that is [linearly independent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linearly_independent "Linearly independent"). Every [vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space") is a free module,[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_module#cite_note-1) but, if the [ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ring_(mathematics) "Ring (mathematics)") of the coefficients is not a [division ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_ring "Division ring") (not a [field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)") in the [commutative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_ring "Commutative ring") case), then there exist non-free modules.

Given any [set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_(mathematics) "Set (mathematics)")_S_ and ring _R_, there is a free _R_-module with basis _S_, which is called the _free module on_ _S_ or _module of formal_ _R_-_linear combinations_ of the elements of _S_.

A [free abelian group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_abelian_group "Free abelian group") is precisely a free module over the ring ![Image 1: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/449494a083e0a1fda2b61c62b2f09b6bee4633dc) of [integers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer").

For a [ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ring_(mathematics) "Ring (mathematics)")![Image 2: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) and an ![Image 3: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33)-[module](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Module_(mathematics) "Module (mathematics)")![Image 4: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd), the set ![Image 5: {\displaystyle E\subseteq M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5fbe408f36b55e9249974a3c3282f666c5d85764) is a basis for ![Image 6: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) if:

A free module is a module with a basis.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_module#cite_note-2)

An immediate consequence of the second half of the definition is that the coefficients in the first half are unique for each element of _M_.

If ![Image 7: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) has [invariant basis number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invariant_basis_number "Invariant basis number"), then by definition any two bases have the same cardinality. For example, nonzero commutative rings have invariant basis number. The cardinality of any (and therefore every) basis is called the **rank** of the free module ![Image 8: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd). If this cardinality is finite, the free module is said to be _free of finite rank_, or _free of rank_ n if the rank is known to be n.

Let _R_ be a ring.

*   _R_ is a free module of rank one over itself (either as a left or right module); any unit element is a basis.
*   More generally, If _R_ is commutative, a nonzero ideal _I_ of _R_ is free if and only if it is a [principal ideal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principal_ideal "Principal ideal") generated by a [nonzerodivisor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nonzerodivisor "Nonzerodivisor"), with a generator being a basis.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_module#cite_note-3)
*   Over a [principal ideal domain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principal_ideal_domain "Principal ideal domain") (e.g., ![Image 9: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/449494a083e0a1fda2b61c62b2f09b6bee4633dc)), a submodule of a free module is free.
*   If _R_ is commutative, the polynomial ring ![Image 10: {\displaystyle R[X]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/afeccd52deabb878398a8485755c3ceea80caf9a) in indeterminate _X_ is a free module with a possible basis 1, _X_, _X_ 2, ....
*   Let ![Image 11: {\displaystyle A[t]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/13daf73f25e64310e7e5d67c5aa8dd4f97cb884f) be a [polynomial ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial_ring "Polynomial ring") over a commutative ring _A_, _f_ a [monic polynomial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monic_polynomial "Monic polynomial") of degree _d_ there, ![Image 12: {\displaystyle B=A[t]/(f)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/768151c0a8d11b44136db1811bee3ddd9ac55df5) and ![Image 13: {\displaystyle \xi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e0b461aaf61091abd5d2c808931c48b8ff9647db) the image of _t_ in _B_. Then _B_ contains _A_ as a subring and is free as an _A_-module with a basis ![Image 14: {\displaystyle 1,\xi ,\dots ,\xi ^{d-1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf4cd6ef7fbd922de5a455ae8886e7de473da89d).
*   For any non-negative integer _n_, ![Image 15: {\displaystyle R^{n}=R\times \cdots \times R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c8b3d2cbfd56cfeaf4bd62b60aaff8d7fd920de4), the [cartesian product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direct_product#Direct_product_of_modules "Direct product") of _n_ copies of _R_ as a left _R_-module, is free. If _R_ has [invariant basis number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invariant_basis_number "Invariant basis number"), then its [rank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rank_of_a_module "Rank of a module") is _n_.
*   A [direct sum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direct_sum_of_modules "Direct sum of modules") of free modules is free, while an infinite cartesian product of free modules is generally _not_ free (cf. the [Baer–Specker group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baer%E2%80%93Specker_group "Baer–Specker group")).
*   A finitely generated module over a commutative [local ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_ring "Local ring") is free if and only if it is [faithfully flat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flat_module#Faithful_flatness "Flat module").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_module#cite_note-4) Also, [Kaplansky's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaplansky%27s_theorem_on_projective_modules "Kaplansky's theorem on projective modules") states a projective module over a (possibly non-commutative) local ring is free.
*   Sometimes, whether a module is free or not is [undecidable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Undecidable_problem#Examples_of_undecidable_statements "Undecidable problem") in the set-theoretic sense. A famous example is the [Whitehead problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whitehead_problem "Whitehead problem"), which asks whether a Whitehead group is free or not. As it turns out, the problem is independent of ZFC.

Formal linear combinations
--------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Free_module&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Formal linear combinations")]

Given a set _E_ and ring _R_, there is a free _R_-module that has _E_ as a basis: namely, the [direct sum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direct_sum_of_modules "Direct sum of modules") of copies of _R_ indexed by _E_

![Image 16: {\displaystyle R^{(E)}=\bigoplus _{e\in E}R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3ac3de90ee538e14832ff65d9762fb202dee900a).
Explicitly, it is the submodule of the [Cartesian product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direct_product#Direct_product_of_modules "Direct product")![Image 17: {\textstyle \prod _{E}R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4c08fc11c01eb79d2ae3c6e09c4898cb42756cbc) (_R_ is viewed as say a left module) that consists of the elements that have only finitely many nonzero components. One can [embed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Embedding "Embedding")_E_ into _R_(_E_) as a subset by identifying an element _e_ with that of _R_(_E_) whose _e_-th component is 1 (the unity of _R_) and all the other components are zero. Then each element of _R_(_E_) can be written uniquely as

![Image 18: {\displaystyle \sum _{e\in E}c_{e}e,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e77dc2c0fca27c08ba811e80369def9276093882)
where only finitely many ![Image 19: {\displaystyle c_{e}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6f4115c10cda4e7ee514243f54529571ff23ca66) are nonzero. It is called a _[formal linear combination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formal\_linear\_combination "Formal linear combination")_ of elements of _E_.

A similar argument shows that every free left (resp. right) _R_-module is isomorphic to a direct sum of copies of _R_ as left (resp. right) module.

### Another construction

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Free_module&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Another construction")]

The free module _R_(_E_) may also be constructed in the following equivalent way.

Given a ring _R_ and a set _E_, first as a set we let

![Image 20: {\displaystyle R^{(E)}=\{f:E\to R\mid f(x)=0{\text{ for all but finitely many }}x\in E\}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9efd697b50d643fa5c2227c530ca2ef8815214dc)
We equip it with a structure of a left module such that the addition is defined by: for _x_ in _E_,

![Image 21: {\displaystyle (f+g)(x)=f(x)+g(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cf80cb50218eac1e40d4a0908bd039db3bd0863c)
and the scalar multiplication by: for _r_ in _R_ and _x_ in _E_,

![Image 22: {\displaystyle (rf)(x)=rf(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d21727ffb55476f406fdf73b601e8b75d20e2353)
Now, as an _R_-valued [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)") on _E_, each _f_ in ![Image 23: {\displaystyle R^{(E)}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d5579f97648ac91d2d5c103542a6b0ef0730aa59) can be written uniquely as

![Image 24: {\displaystyle f=\sum _{e\in E}c_{e}\delta _{e}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/374ce6b12723bbb2ad12697b78fe835565a53101)
where ![Image 25: {\displaystyle c_{e}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6f4115c10cda4e7ee514243f54529571ff23ca66) are in _R_ and only finitely many of them are nonzero and ![Image 26: {\displaystyle \delta _{e}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e18d0dbba5d6a01d4292950d6f49d12dcee61670) is given as

![Image 27: {\displaystyle \delta _{e}(x)={\begin{cases}1_{R}\quad {\mbox{if }}x=e\\0_{R}\quad {\mbox{if }}x\neq e\end{cases}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d57aaa33eadf2c2b88571210888e36f2c1e0d7eb)
(this is a variant of the [Kronecker delta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kronecker_delta "Kronecker delta")). The above means that the subset ![Image 28: {\displaystyle \{\delta _{e}\mid e\in E\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3fb86bca3f39c13a3726cee2ba1c60b7aa8f2c01) of ![Image 29: {\displaystyle R^{(E)}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d5579f97648ac91d2d5c103542a6b0ef0730aa59) is a basis of ![Image 30: {\displaystyle R^{(E)}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d5579f97648ac91d2d5c103542a6b0ef0730aa59). The mapping ![Image 31: {\displaystyle e\mapsto \delta _{e}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b9d7d00f8171106f9ccf65ff61f626c2fa9a16ec) is a [bijection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bijection "Bijection") between _E_ and this basis. Through this bijection, ![Image 32: {\displaystyle R^{(E)}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d5579f97648ac91d2d5c103542a6b0ef0730aa59) is a free module with the basis _E_.

The inclusion mapping ![Image 33: {\displaystyle \iota :E\to R^{(E)}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8b8d4c679b700284aca5669f9ba9f85b592477fc) defined above is [universal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universal_property "Universal property") in the following sense. Given an arbitrary function ![Image 34: {\displaystyle f:E\to N}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dc52e613e943dff31f5ae106e8903b7cd349c949) from a set _E_ to a left _R_-module _N_, there exists a unique [module homomorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Module_homomorphism "Module homomorphism")![Image 35: {\displaystyle {\overline {f}}:R^{(E)}\to N}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b054ebe8ab9e251095354d792342d3c621a9b948) such that ![Image 36: {\displaystyle f={\overline {f}}\circ \iota }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ccb96645b0a4241c4330d54682041c799c2689b9); namely, ![Image 37: {\displaystyle {\overline {f}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4de4554a5933ca51745edacd34a5a6aaea9a9f4e) is defined by the formula:

![Image 38: {\displaystyle {\overline {f}}\left(\sum _{e\in E}r_{e}e\right)=\sum _{e\in E}r_{e}f(e)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1ebe42d8e7c1a832488dd48b8cc3114fdfa53436)
and ![Image 39: {\displaystyle {\overline {f}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4de4554a5933ca51745edacd34a5a6aaea9a9f4e) is said to be obtained by _extending ![Image 40: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest\_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) by linearity._ The uniqueness means that each _R_-linear map ![Image 41: {\displaystyle R^{(E)}\to N}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/447c9996de2bdf587d6754fcc27e172ce57ca8be) is uniquely determined by its [restriction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Restriction_(mathematics) "Restriction (mathematics)") to _E_.

As usual for universal properties, this defines _R_(_E_)[up to](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Up_to "Up to") a [canonical isomorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canonical_isomorphism "Canonical isomorphism"). Also the formation of ![Image 42: {\displaystyle \iota :E\to R^{(E)}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8b8d4c679b700284aca5669f9ba9f85b592477fc) for each set _E_ determines a [functor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functor "Functor")

![Image 43: {\displaystyle R^{(-)}:{\textbf {Set}}\to R{\text{-}}{\mathsf {Mod}},\,E\mapsto R^{(E)}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9776a7460caa3b16bfba962524e31901cb508bb3),
from the [category of sets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category_of_sets "Category of sets") to the category of left _R_-modules. It is called the [free functor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_functor "Free functor") and satisfies a natural relation: for each set _E_ and a left module _N_,

![Image 44: {\displaystyle \operatorname {Hom} _{\textbf {Set}}(E,U(N))\simeq \operatorname {Hom} _{R}(R^{(E)},N),\,f\mapsto {\overline {f}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9e6a02378ac006104c4fe5d080f35a96c7e05a4a)
where ![Image 45: {\displaystyle U:R{\text{-}}{\mathsf {Mod}}\to {\textbf {Set}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c47838b61fc2a6da58e356e0e136148f6e28bfe2) is the [forgetful functor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forgetful_functor "Forgetful functor"), meaning ![Image 46: {\displaystyle R^{(-)}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bffd9d2daae73dedee8d42abf63a9df4b9ddb8af) is a [left adjoint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Left_adjoint "Left adjoint") of the forgetful functor.

Many statements true for free modules extend to certain larger classes of modules. [Projective modules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_module "Projective module") are direct summands of free modules. [Flat modules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flat_module "Flat module") are defined by the property that tensoring with them preserves exact sequences. [Torsion-free modules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torsion-free_module "Torsion-free module") form an even broader class. For a finitely generated module over a PID (such as **Z**), the properties free, projective, flat, and torsion-free are equivalent.
