Value indicating the relation of a proposition to truth

In [logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logic "Logic") and [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), a **truth value**, sometimes called a **logical value**, is a value indicating the relation of a [proposition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proposition "Proposition") to [truth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Truth "Truth"), which in [classical logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_logic "Classical logic") has only two possible values (_[true](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logical\_truth "Logical truth")_ or _[false](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/False\_(logic) "False (logic)")_).[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Truth_value#cite_note-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Truth_value#cite_note-2) Truth values are used in [computing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computing "Computing") as well as various types of [logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logic "Logic").

Computing
---------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Truth_value&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Computing")]

See also: [Implicit type conversion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Implicit_type_conversion "Implicit type conversion")

In some programming languages, any [expression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expression_(computer_science) "Expression (computer science)") can be evaluated in a context that expects a [Boolean data type](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boolean_data_type "Boolean data type"). Typically (though this varies by programming language) expressions like the number [zero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero "Zero"), the [empty string](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empty_string "Empty string"), empty lists, and [null](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Null_pointer "Null pointer") are treated as false, and strings with content (like "abc"), other numbers, and objects evaluate to true. Sometimes these classes of expressions are called **falsy** and **truthy**. For example, in [Lisp](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lisp_(programming_language) "Lisp (programming language)"), [nil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Null_pointer "Null pointer"), the empty list, is treated as false, and all other values are treated as true. In [C](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C_(programming_language) "C (programming language)"), the number 0 or 0.0 is false, and all other values are treated as true.

In [JavaScript](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/JavaScript "JavaScript"), the empty string (`""`), `null`, `undefined`, [`NaN`](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NaN "NaN"), +0, [−0](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E2%88%920 "−0") and `false`[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Truth_value#cite_note-3) are sometimes called _falsy_ (of which the [complement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complement_(set_theory) "Complement (set theory)") is _truthy_) to distinguish between strictly [type-checked](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Type_safety "Type safety") and [coerced](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Type_conversion "Type conversion") Booleans (see also: [JavaScript syntax#Type conversion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/JavaScript_syntax#Type_conversion "JavaScript syntax")).[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Truth_value#cite_note-4) As opposed to Python, empty containers (Arrays, Maps, Sets) are considered truthy. Languages such as [PHP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PHP "PHP") also use this approach.

Classical logic
---------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Truth_value&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Classical logic")]

⊤·**∧**·
true[conjunction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logical_conjunction "Logical conjunction")
¬↕↕
⊥·**∨**·
false[disjunction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logical_disjunction "Logical disjunction")
Negation interchanges 

true with false and 

conjunction with disjunction.

In [classical logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_logic "Classical logic"), with its intended semantics, the truth values are _[true](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logical\_truth "Logical truth")_ (denoted by _1_ or the [verum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Verum "Verum") ⊤), and _[untrue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/False\_(logic) "False (logic)")_ or _[false](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/False\_(logic) "False (logic)")_ (denoted by _0_ or the [falsum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Falsum "Falsum") ⊥); that is, classical logic is a [two-valued logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two-valued_logic "Two-valued logic"). This set of two values is also called the [Boolean domain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boolean_domain "Boolean domain"). Corresponding semantics of [logical connectives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logical_connective "Logical connective") are [truth functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Truth_function "Truth function"), whose values are expressed in the form of [truth tables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Truth_table "Truth table"). [Logical biconditional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logical_biconditional "Logical biconditional") becomes the [equality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equality_(mathematics) "Equality (mathematics)") binary relation, and [negation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Negation "Negation") becomes a [bijection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bijection "Bijection") which [permutes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permutation "Permutation") true and false. Conjunction and disjunction are [dual](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_(mathematics)#Duality_in_logic_and_set_theory "Dual (mathematics)") with respect to negation, which is expressed by [De Morgan's laws](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_Morgan%27s_laws "De Morgan's laws"):

¬(p ∧ q) ⇔ ¬p ∨ ¬q¬(p ∨ q) ⇔ ¬p ∧ ¬q
[Propositional variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propositional_variable "Propositional variable") become [variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variable_(computer_science) "Variable (computer science)") in the Boolean domain. Assigning values for propositional variables is referred to as [valuation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Valuation_(logic) "Valuation (logic)").

Intuitionistic and constructive logic
-------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Truth_value&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Intuitionistic and constructive logic")]

Main article: [Constructivism (mathematics)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constructivism_(mathematics) "Constructivism (mathematics)")

Whereas in classical logic truth values form a [Boolean algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boolean_algebra "Boolean algebra"), in [intuitionistic logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intuitionistic_logic "Intuitionistic logic"), and more generally, [constructive mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constructivism_(mathematics) "Constructivism (mathematics)"), the truth values form a [Heyting algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heyting_algebra "Heyting algebra"). Such truth values may express various aspects of validity, including locality, temporality, or computational content.

For example, one may use the [open sets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_set "Open set") of a topological space as intuitionistic truth values, in which case the truth value of a formula expresses _where_ the formula holds, not whether it holds.

In [realizability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Realizability "Realizability") truth values are sets of programs, which can be understood as computational evidence of validity of a formula. For example, the truth value of the statement "for every number there is a prime larger than it" is the set of all programs that take as input a number n{\displaystyle n}![Image 9: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b), and output a prime larger than n{\displaystyle n}![Image 10: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b).

In [category theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category_theory "Category theory"), truth values appear as the elements of the [subobject classifier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subobject_classifier "Subobject classifier"). In particular, in a [topos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topos "Topos") every formula of [higher-order logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Higher-order_logic "Higher-order logic") may be assigned a truth value in the subobject classifier.

Even though a Heyting algebra may have many elements, this should not be understood as there being truth values that are neither true nor false, because intuitionistic logic proves ¬(p≠⊤∧p≠⊥){\displaystyle \neg (p\neq \top \land p\neq \bot )}![Image 11: {\displaystyle \neg (p\neq \top \land p\neq \bot )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e346c85eddf9b4cb29021ccdd905b734103f7b3b) ("it is not the case that p{\displaystyle p}![Image 12: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) is neither true nor false").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Truth_value#cite_note-5)

In [intuitionistic type theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intuitionistic_type_theory "Intuitionistic type theory"), the [Curry-Howard correspondence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Type_theory#Curry-Howard_correspondence "Type theory") exhibits an equivalence of propositions and types, according to which validity is equivalent to inhabitation of a type.

For other notions of intuitionistic truth values, see the [Brouwer–Heyting–Kolmogorov interpretation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brouwer%E2%80%93Heyting%E2%80%93Kolmogorov_interpretation "Brouwer–Heyting–Kolmogorov interpretation") and [Intuitionistic logic §Semantics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intuitionistic_logic#Semantics "Intuitionistic logic").

Multi-valued logic
------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Truth_value&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Multi-valued logic")]

[Multi-valued logics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multi-valued_logic "Multi-valued logic") (such as [fuzzy logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fuzzy_logic "Fuzzy logic") and [relevance logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relevance_logic "Relevance logic")) allow for more than two truth values, possibly containing some internal structure. For example, on the [unit interval](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_interval "Unit interval")[0,1] such structure is a [total order](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Total_order "Total order"); this may be expressed as the existence of various [degrees of truth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degrees_of_truth "Degrees of truth").

Algebraic semantics
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Truth_value&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Algebraic semantics")]

Main article: [Algebraic logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_logic "Algebraic logic")

Not all [logical systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logical_system "Logical system") are truth-valuational in the sense that logical connectives may be interpreted as truth functions. For example, [intuitionistic logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intuitionistic_logic "Intuitionistic logic") lacks a complete set of truth values because its semantics, the [Brouwer–Heyting–Kolmogorov interpretation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brouwer%E2%80%93Heyting%E2%80%93Kolmogorov_interpretation "Brouwer–Heyting–Kolmogorov interpretation"), is specified in terms of [provability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proof_theory "Proof theory") conditions, and not directly in terms of the [necessary truth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Necessarily_true "Necessarily true") of formulae.

But even non-truth-valuational logics can associate values with logical formulae, as is done in [algebraic semantics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_semantics_(mathematical_logic) "Algebraic semantics (mathematical logic)"). The algebraic semantics of intuitionistic logic is given in terms of [Heyting algebras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heyting_algebra "Heyting algebra"), compared to [Boolean algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boolean_algebra_(structure) "Boolean algebra (structure)") semantics of classical propositional calculus.
