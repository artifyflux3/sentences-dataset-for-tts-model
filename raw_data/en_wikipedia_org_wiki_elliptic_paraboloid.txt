Paraboloid of revolution

In [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry"), a **paraboloid** is a [quadric surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadric_surface "Quadric surface") that has exactly one [axis of symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axial_symmetry "Axial symmetry") and no [center of symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_symmetry "Central symmetry"). The term "paraboloid" is derived from [parabola](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parabola "Parabola"), which refers to a [conic section](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conic_section "Conic section") that has a similar property of symmetry.

Every [plane section](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_section "Plane section") of a paraboloid made by a plane [parallel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_(geometry)#A_line_and_a_plane "Parallel (geometry)") to the axis of symmetry is a parabola. The paraboloid is **hyperbolic** if every other plane section is either a [hyperbola](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbola "Hyperbola"), or two crossing lines (in the case of a section by a tangent plane). The paraboloid is **elliptic** if every other nonempty plane section is either an [ellipse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse "Ellipse"), or a single point (in the case of a section by a tangent plane). A paraboloid is either elliptic or hyperbolic.

Equivalently, a paraboloid may be defined as a quadric surface that is not a [cylinder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylinder "Cylinder"), and has an [implicit equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Implicit_surface "Implicit surface") whose part of degree two may be factored over the [complex numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number") into two different linear factors. The paraboloid is hyperbolic if the factors are real; elliptic if the factors are [complex conjugate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_conjugate "Complex conjugate").

An elliptic paraboloid is shaped like an oval cup and has a [maximum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maximum "Maximum") or minimum point when its axis is vertical. In a suitable [coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_system "Coordinate system") with three axes _x_, _y_, and _z_, it can be represented by the equation[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptic_paraboloid#cite_note-1)![Image 2: {\displaystyle z={\frac {x^{2}}{a^{2}}}+{\frac {y^{2}}{b^{2}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eefe02439e570b322e44c61b7ad146e7699292fc) where _a_ and _b_ are constants that dictate the level of curvature in the _xz_ and _yz_ planes respectively. In this position, the elliptic paraboloid opens upward.

[![Image 3](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/HyperbolicParaboloid.svg/250px-HyperbolicParaboloid.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:HyperbolicParaboloid.svg)

Hyperbolic paraboloid

A hyperbolic paraboloid (not to be confused with a [hyperboloid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperboloid "Hyperboloid")) is a [doubly ruled surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doubly_ruled_surface "Doubly ruled surface") shaped like a [saddle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saddle "Saddle"). In a suitable coordinate system, a hyperbolic paraboloid can be represented by the equation[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptic_paraboloid#cite_note-Weisstein-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptic_paraboloid#cite_note-3)![Image 4: {\displaystyle z={\frac {y^{2}}{b^{2}}}-{\frac {x^{2}}{a^{2}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a200f7ce765c68ec28e12538ed425f855edd4469) In this position, the hyperbolic paraboloid opens downward along the _x_-axis and upward along the _y_-axis (that is, the parabola in the plane _x_ = 0 opens upward and the parabola in the plane _y_ = 0 opens downward).

Any paraboloid (elliptic or hyperbolic) is a [translation surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Translation_surface_(differential_geometry) "Translation surface (differential geometry)"), as it can be generated by a moving parabola directed by a second parabola.

### Elliptic paraboloid

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Paraboloid&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Elliptic paraboloid")]

[![Image 5](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Paraboloid-3dmesh.png/250px-Paraboloid-3dmesh.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Paraboloid-3dmesh.png)

[Polygon mesh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon_mesh "Polygon mesh") of a circular paraboloid

[![Image 6](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Paraboloid3d.png/250px-Paraboloid3d.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Paraboloid3d.png)

Circular paraboloid

In a suitable [Cartesian coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system "Cartesian coordinate system"), an elliptic paraboloid has the equation ![Image 7: {\displaystyle z={\frac {x^{2}}{a^{2}}}+{\frac {y^{2}}{b^{2}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eefe02439e570b322e44c61b7ad146e7699292fc)

If _a_ = _b_, an elliptic paraboloid is a **circular paraboloid** or **paraboloid of revolution**. It is a [surface of revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_of_revolution "Surface of revolution") obtained by revolving a [parabola](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parabola "Parabola") around its axis.

A circular paraboloid contains circles. This is also true in the general case (see [Circular section](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circular_section "Circular section")).

From the point of view of [projective geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_geometry "Projective geometry"), an elliptic paraboloid is an [ellipsoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipsoid "Ellipsoid") that is [tangent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_space "Tangent space") to the [plane at infinity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_at_infinity "Plane at infinity").

Plane sections
The plane sections of an elliptic paraboloid can be:

*   a _parabola_, if the plane is parallel to the axis,
*   a _point_, if the plane is a [tangent plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_plane "Tangent plane").
*   an _ellipse_ or _empty_, otherwise.

#### Parabolic reflector

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Paraboloid&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Parabolic reflector")]

On the axis of a circular paraboloid, there is a point called the [_focus_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Focus_(optics) "Focus (optics)") (or _focal point_), such that, if the paraboloid is a mirror, light (or other waves) from a point source at the focus is reflected into a parallel beam, parallel to the axis of the paraboloid. This also works the other way around: a parallel beam of light that is parallel to the axis of the paraboloid is concentrated at the focal point. For a proof, see [Parabola §Proof of the reflective property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parabola#Proof_of_the_reflective_property "Parabola").

Therefore, the shape of a circular paraboloid is widely used in [astronomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy "Astronomy") for parabolic reflectors and parabolic antennas.

The surface of a rotating liquid is also a circular paraboloid. This is used in [liquid-mirror telescopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liquid-mirror_telescope "Liquid-mirror telescope") and in making solid telescope mirrors (see [rotating furnace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotating_furnace "Rotating furnace")).

### Hyperbolic paraboloid

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Paraboloid&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Hyperbolic paraboloid")]

[![Image 11](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Hyperbolic-paraboloid.svg/250px-Hyperbolic-paraboloid.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Hyperbolic-paraboloid.svg)

A hyperbolic paraboloid with lines contained in it

[![Image 12](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Pringles_chips.JPG/250px-Pringles_chips.JPG)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Pringles_chips.JPG)

[Pringles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pringles "Pringles") fried snacks are in the shape of a hyperbolic paraboloid.

The hyperbolic paraboloid is a [doubly ruled surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doubly_ruled_surface "Doubly ruled surface"): it contains two families of mutually [skew lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skew_lines "Skew lines"). The lines in each family are parallel to a common plane, but not to each other. Hence the hyperbolic paraboloid is a [conoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conoid "Conoid").

These properties characterize hyperbolic paraboloids and are used in one of the oldest definitions of hyperbolic paraboloids: _a hyperbolic paraboloid is a surface that may be generated by a moving line that is parallel to a fixed plane and crosses two fixed [skew lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skew\_lines "Skew lines")_.

This property makes it simple to manufacture a hyperbolic paraboloid from a variety of materials and for a variety of purposes, from concrete roofs to snack foods. In particular, [Pringles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pringles "Pringles") fried snacks resemble a truncated hyperbolic paraboloid.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptic_paraboloid#cite_note-4)

A hyperbolic paraboloid is a [saddle surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saddle_surface "Saddle surface"), as its [Gauss curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauss_curvature "Gauss curvature") is negative at every point. Therefore, although it is a ruled surface, it is not [developable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Developable_surface "Developable surface").

From the point of view of [projective geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_geometry "Projective geometry"), a hyperbolic paraboloid is [one-sheet hyperboloid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One-sheet_hyperboloid "One-sheet hyperboloid") that is [tangent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_space "Tangent space") to the [plane at infinity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_at_infinity "Plane at infinity").

A hyperbolic paraboloid of equation ![Image 13: {\displaystyle z=axy}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68341aeb8d8c77d90d0638235bd80d15f7286b0e) or ![Image 14: {\displaystyle z={\tfrac {a}{2}}(x^{2}-y^{2})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/224a088ed529dd937c1f441f50aa5daa475dde0d) (this is the same [up to](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Up_to "Up to") a [rotation of axes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation_of_axes "Rotation of axes")) may be called a _rectangular hyperbolic paraboloid_, by analogy with [rectangular hyperbolas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangular_hyperbola "Rectangular hyperbola").

Plane sections[![Image 15](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/ParabHyper.png/250px-ParabHyper.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:ParabHyper.png)

A hyperbolic paraboloid with hyperbolas and parabolas

A plane section of a hyperbolic paraboloid with equation ![Image 16: {\displaystyle z={\frac {x^{2}}{a^{2}}}-{\frac {y^{2}}{b^{2}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e57c3765a60e103cc747d0a8b218304293df9d69) can be

*   a _line_, if the plane is parallel to the z-axis, and has an equation of the form ![Image 17: {\displaystyle bx\pm ay+b=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cd75c3f4ba77bc670b6409c906af76d69aa3f249),
*   a _parabola_, if the plane is parallel to the z-axis, and the section is not a line,
*   a pair of _intersecting lines_, if the plane is a [tangent plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_plane "Tangent plane"),
*   a _hyperbola_, otherwise.

Geometric representation of multiplication table
------------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Paraboloid&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Geometric representation of multiplication table")]

If the hyperbolic paraboloid ![Image 35: {\displaystyle z={\frac {x^{2}}{a^{2}}}-{\frac {y^{2}}{b^{2}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e57c3765a60e103cc747d0a8b218304293df9d69) is rotated by an angle of ⁠π/4⁠ in the +_z_ direction (according to the [right hand rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_hand_rule "Right hand rule")), the result is the surface ![Image 36: {\displaystyle z=\left({\frac {x^{2}+y^{2}}{2}}\right)\left({\frac {1}{a^{2}}}-{\frac {1}{b^{2}}}\right)+xy\left({\frac {1}{a^{2}}}+{\frac {1}{b^{2}}}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ccdb9c5d5ea0d093ad31285e7fd7909146428692) and if _a_ = _b_ then this simplifies to ![Image 37: {\displaystyle z={\frac {2xy}{a^{2}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0174b426b10baed8f9684d5185fa25ba582b12b5) Finally, letting _a_ = √2, we see that the hyperbolic paraboloid ![Image 38: {\displaystyle z={\frac {x^{2}-y^{2}}{2}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c9da8f10a7fc54998a499662367c0804a6ac92b2) is congruent to the surface ![Image 39: {\displaystyle z=xy}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/46602cba442d7bb02978c0c8c328e180738e7d03) which can be thought of as the geometric representation (a three-dimensional [nomograph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nomogram "Nomogram"), as it were) of a [multiplication table](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication_table "Multiplication table").

The two paraboloidal **R**2 → **R** functions ![Image 40: {\displaystyle z_{1}(x,y)={\frac {x^{2}-y^{2}}{2}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/45650d0e5a0fa05761a0c4164ed318f6ef382f14) and ![Image 41: {\displaystyle z_{2}(x,y)=xy}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1a4e01986545c7156ee1e33a06e8a6ab0a604fe9) are [harmonic conjugates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harmonic_conjugate "Harmonic conjugate"), and together form the [analytic function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytic_function "Analytic function")![Image 42: {\displaystyle f(z)={\frac {z^{2}}{2}}=f(x+yi)=z_{1}(x,y)+iz_{2}(x,y)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/89d9b9b2681025eb4afa1cd43b2206961b5f0a9b) which is the [analytic continuation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytic_continuation "Analytic continuation") of the **R** → **R** parabolic function _f_(_x_) = ⁠_x_ 2/2⁠.

Dimensions of a paraboloidal dish
---------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Paraboloid&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Dimensions of a paraboloidal dish")]

The dimensions of a symmetrical paraboloidal dish are related by the equation ![Image 43: {\displaystyle 4FD=R^{2},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e4491078a0967549b194a4a54aae082286e9ff43) where _F_ is the focal length, _D_ is the depth of the dish (measured along the axis of symmetry from the vertex to the plane of the rim), and _R_ is the radius of the rim. They must all be in the same [unit of length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_of_length "Unit of length"). If two of these three lengths are known, this equation can be used to calculate the third.

A more complex calculation is needed to find the diameter of the dish _measured along its surface_. This is sometimes called the "linear diameter", and equals the diameter of a flat, circular sheet of material, usually metal, which is the right size to be cut and bent to make the dish. Two intermediate results are useful in the calculation: _P_ = 2 _F_ (or the equivalent: _P_ = ⁠_R_ 2/2 _D_⁠) and _Q_ = √_P_ 2 + _R_ 2, where _F_, _D_, and _R_ are defined as above. The diameter of the dish, measured along the surface, is then given by ![Image 44: {\displaystyle {\frac {RQ}{P}}+P\ln \left({\frac {R+Q}{P}}\right),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5874e0c25ef31e74855a9629ce5a10709ae2d4c2) where ln _x_ means the [natural logarithm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_logarithm "Natural logarithm") of _x_, i.e. its logarithm to base _[e](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/E\_(mathematical\_constant) "E (mathematical constant)")_.

The volume of the dish, the amount of liquid it could hold if the rim were horizontal and the vertex at the bottom (e.g. the capacity of a paraboloidal [wok](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wok "Wok")), is given by ![Image 45: {\displaystyle {\frac {\pi }{2}}R^{2}D,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b29851d280f2cddf9a99af72fc84299d4b72c506) where the symbols are defined as above. This can be compared with the formulae for the volumes of a [cylinder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylinder_(geometry) "Cylinder (geometry)") (π _R_ 2 _D_), a [hemisphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere "Sphere") (⁠2π/3⁠_R_ 2 _D_, where _D_ = _R_), and a [cone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cone_(geometry) "Cone (geometry)") (⁠π/3⁠_R_ 2 _D_). π _R_ 2 is the aperture area of the dish, the area enclosed by the rim, which is proportional to the amount of sunlight a reflector dish can intercept. The surface area of a parabolic dish can be found using the area formula for a [surface of revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_of_revolution#Area_formula "Surface of revolution") which gives ![Image 46: {\displaystyle A={\frac {\pi R\left({\sqrt {(R^{2}+4D^{2})^{3}}}-R^{3}\right)}{6D^{2}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7590d5d797f81f0ee8151ed8c43c72670eb40a3b)
