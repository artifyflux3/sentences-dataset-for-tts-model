**Irony punctuation** is any form of notation proposed or used to denote [irony](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irony "Irony") or [sarcasm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sarcasm "Sarcasm") in written text. Written text, in [English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English_language "English language") and other languages, lacks a standard way to mark irony, and several forms of [punctuation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Punctuation "Punctuation") have been proposed to fill the gap. The oldest is the **percontation point** in the form of a reversed [question mark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Question_mark "Question mark") (**‚∏Æ**), proposed by English printer [Henry Denham](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry_Denham "Henry Denham") in the 1580s for marking [rhetorical questions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhetorical_question "Rhetorical question"), which can be a form of irony. Specific **irony marks** have also been proposed, such as in the form of an open upward arrow (‚ñ≥

|), used by [Marcellin Jobard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marcellin_Jobard "Marcellin Jobard") in the 19th century, and in a form resembling a reversed question mark ([![Image 1](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Point_d%27ironie_de_Alcanter_de_Brahm.svg/20px-Point_d%27ironie_de_Alcanter_de_Brahm.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Point_d%27ironie_de_Alcanter_de_Brahm.svg)), proposed by French poet [Alcanter de Brahm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alcanter_de_Brahm "Alcanter de Brahm") during the 19th century.

Irony punctuation is primarily used to indicate that a sentence should be understood at a second level. A bracketed [exclamation point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exclamation_point#English "Exclamation point") or question mark as well as [scare quotes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scare_quotes "Scare quotes") are also occasionally used to express irony or sarcasm.

The percontation point ([![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Irony_mark_full.svg/20px-Irony_mark_full.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Irony_mark_full.svg)) , a reversed question mark later referred to as a rhetorical question mark, was proposed by [Henry Denham](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry_Denham "Henry Denham") in the 1580s and was used at the end of a question that does not require an answer‚Äîa [rhetorical question](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhetorical_question "Rhetorical question"). Its use died out in the 17th century.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irony_punctuation#cite_note-T9FD8-1) This character can be represented using the reversed question mark (‚∏Æ) found in Unicode as U+2E2E; another character approximating it is [the Arabic question mark (ÿü)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Question_mark#Mirrored_question_mark_in_right-to-left_scripts "Question mark"), U+061F.

The modern question mark (? U+003F) is descended from the "punctus interrogativus" (described as "a lightning flash, striking from right to left"),[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irony_punctuation#cite_note-LNOef-2) but unlike the modern question mark, the punctus interrogativus may be contrasted with the punctus percontativus‚Äîthe former marking questions that require an answer while the latter marks rhetorical questions.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irony_punctuation#cite_note-percontation-3)

In 1668, [John Wilkins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Wilkins "John Wilkins"), in _[An Essay Towards a Real Character, and a Philosophical Language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/An\_Essay\_towards\_a\_Real\_Character\_and\_a\_Philosophical\_Language "An Essay towards a Real Character and a Philosophical Language")_, proposed using an [inverted exclamation mark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverted_question_and_exclamation_marks "Inverted question and exclamation marks") to punctuate rhetorical questions.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irony_punctuation#cite_note-4qYun-4)

In an article dated 11 October 1841, [Marcellin Jobard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marcellin_Jobard "Marcellin Jobard"), a Belgian newspaper publisher, introduced an "irony mark" ([French](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_language "French language"): _point d'ironie_) in the shape of an oversized arrow head with small stem (rather like an [ideogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ideogram "Ideogram") of a [Christmas tree](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christmas_tree "Christmas tree")).[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irony_punctuation#cite_note-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irony_punctuation#cite_note-6) The next year he expanded his idea, suggesting the symbol could be used in various orientations (on its side, upside down, etc.) to mark "a point of irritation, an indignation point, a point of hesitation".[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irony_punctuation#cite_note-7)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irony_punctuation#cite_note-7OMC0-8)

Reverse italics (Sartalics)
---------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Irony_punctuation&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Reverse italics (Sartalics)")]

[Tom Driberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tom_Driberg "Tom Driberg") recommended that ironic statements be printed in [leftward-slanting italics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italic_type#Left-leaning_italics "Italic type"), which he also called sartalics, to distinguish irony from the emphasis indicated by conventional rightward-slanting italics.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irony_punctuation#cite_note-bAsl8-14)[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irony_punctuation#cite_note-15)

[Scare quotes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scare_quotes "Scare quotes") are a particular use of [quotation marks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quotation_mark "Quotation mark"). They are placed around a word or phrase to indicate that it is not used in the fashion that the writer would personally use it. In contrast to the nominal typographic purpose of quotation marks, the enclosed words are not necessarily quoted from another source. When read aloud, various techniques are used to convey the sense, such as prepending the addition of "so-called" or a similar word or phrase of disdain, using a sarcastic or mocking tone, or using [air quotes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Air_quotes "Air quotes"), or any combination of the above.

In certain [Ethiopic languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethiopic_languages "Ethiopic languages"), sarcasm and unreal phrases are indicated at the end of a sentence with a sarcasm mark called _temherte slaq_[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irony_punctuation#cite_note-dCOO8-16)[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irony_punctuation#cite_note-17) or _timirte slaq_[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irony_punctuation#cite_note-:0-18) ([Amharic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amharic_language "Amharic language"): ·âµ·ä•·àù·à≠·â∞·ç°·à•·àã·âÖ),[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irony_punctuation#cite_note-:0-18)[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irony_punctuation#cite_note-19) a character that looks like the [inverted exclamation point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverted_exclamation_point "Inverted exclamation point") (U+00A1) (**¬°**).[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irony_punctuation#cite_note-dCOO8-16)

"/s" redirects here. For further information, see [tone indicator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tone_indicator "Tone indicator").

It is common in online conversation among some Internet users to use a fictitious closing tag patterned after [HTML](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HTML "HTML"): `</sarcasm>`. Over time, it has evolved to lose the angle brackets (`/sarcasm`) and has subsequently been shortened to `/sarc` or `/s` (not to be confused with the valid HTML end tag `</s>` used to end a [struck-through](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strikethrough "Strikethrough") passage).[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irony_punctuation#cite_note-4fQFb-20) Users of the website [Reddit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reddit "Reddit") frequently denote sarcasm through the use of `/s`, as shorthand.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irony_punctuation#cite_note-21) This usage later evolved into [tone indicators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tone_indicator "Tone indicator").

Rhetorical questions in some informal situations can use a bracketed question mark, e.g., "`Oh, really[?]`". The equivalent for an ironic or sarcastic statement would be a bracketed exclamation mark, e.g., "`Oh, really[!]`". Subtitles, such as in [Teletext](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teletext "Teletext"), sometimes use an exclamation mark within brackets or parentheses to mark sarcasm.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irony_punctuation#cite_note-VuBJ2-22)

Another method of expressing sarcasm is by placing a [tilde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tilde "Tilde") (`~`) adjacent to the punctuation. This allows for easy use with any keyboard, as well as variation. Variations include dry sarcasm (`~.`), enthusiastic sarcasm (`~!`), and sarcastic questions (`~?`). The sports blog _Card Chronicle_ has adopted this methodology by inserting (`~`) after the period at the end of the sentence.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irony_punctuation#cite_note-pfO0M-23) It has also been adopted by the [Udacity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Udacity "Udacity") Machine Learning Nanodegree community.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irony_punctuation#cite_note-19UzI-24)

### Capitalization patterns

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Irony_punctuation&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Capitalization patterns")]

On the Internet, it is common to see [alternating uppercase and lowercase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alternating_caps "Alternating caps") lettering to convey a mocking or sarcastic tone, often in the form of [memes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internet_meme "Internet meme"). One example is the "Mocking SpongeBob" meme, which consists of a caption paired with a still taken from the _[SpongeBob SquarePants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SpongeBob\_SquarePants "SpongeBob SquarePants")_ episode _"[Little Yellow Book](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SpongeBob\_SquarePants\_season\_9 "SpongeBob SquarePants season 9")"_ of the character [SpongeBob SquarePants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SpongeBob_SquarePants_(character) "SpongeBob SquarePants (character)") acting like a chicken.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irony_punctuation#cite_note-8nJKp-25)[_[better source needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTRS "Wikipedia:NOTRS")_]

Some might use the "victory hand" [dingbat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dingbat "Dingbat") / [emoji](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emoji "Emoji") ([![Image 17: ‚úå](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Emojione_270C.svg/20px-Emojione_270C.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Emojione_270C.svg)) character to simulate "[scare quotes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scare_quotes "Scare quotes")".[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irony_punctuation#cite_note-qgPfM-26)
