The Sun's core temperature (about 1.57×10 7 K, with ⁠![Image 2: {\displaystyle log_{10}T=7.20}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2038da50d66aa2cb55747b2fdb275702f4c39bd4)⁠), at which PP is more efficient.

In [astrophysics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrophysics "Astrophysics"), **stellar nucleosynthesis** is the [creation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleosynthesis "Nucleosynthesis") of [chemical elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_element "Chemical element") by [nuclear fusion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_fusion "Nuclear fusion") reactions within [stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star "Star"). Stellar nucleosynthesis has occurred since the [original creation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_nucleosynthesis "Big Bang nucleosynthesis") of [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen"), [helium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium "Helium") and [lithium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lithium "Lithium") during the [Big Bang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang "Big Bang"). As a [predictive theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Predictive_power "Predictive power"), it yields accurate estimates of the [observed abundances](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abundance_of_the_chemical_elements "Abundance of the chemical elements") of the elements. It explains why the observed abundances of elements change over time and why some elements and their [isotopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotope "Isotope") are much more abundant than others. The theory was initially proposed by [Fred Hoyle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fred_Hoyle "Fred Hoyle") in 1946,[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis#cite_note-Hoyle1946-1) who later refined it in 1954.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis#cite_note-Hoyle1954-2) Further advances were made, especially to nucleosynthesis by [neutron capture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron_capture "Neutron capture") of the elements heavier than [iron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron "Iron"), by [Margaret](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Margaret_Burbidge "Margaret Burbidge") and [Geoffrey Burbidge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geoffrey_Burbidge "Geoffrey Burbidge"), [William Alfred Fowler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Alfred_Fowler "William Alfred Fowler") and [Fred Hoyle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fred_Hoyle "Fred Hoyle") in their famous 1957 [B 2 FH paper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/B2FH_paper "B2FH paper"),[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis#cite_note-B2FH-3) which became one of the most heavily cited papers in astrophysics history.

[Stars evolve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_evolution "Stellar evolution") because of changes in their composition (the abundance of their constituent elements) over their lifespans, first by [burning hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_burning "Hydrogen burning") ([main sequence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Main_sequence "Main sequence") star), then [helium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_burning "Helium burning") ([horizontal branch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horizontal_branch "Horizontal branch") star), and progressively burning [higher elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triple-alpha_process "Triple-alpha process"). However, this does not by itself significantly alter the abundances of elements in the universe as the elements are contained within the star. Later in its life, a low-mass star will slowly eject its atmosphere via [stellar wind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_wind "Stellar wind"), forming a [planetary nebula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula "Planetary nebula"), while a higher–mass star will eject mass via a sudden catastrophic event called a [supernova](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supernova "Supernova"). The term [supernova nucleosynthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supernova_nucleosynthesis "Supernova nucleosynthesis") is used to describe the creation of elements during the explosion of a massive star or [white dwarf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_dwarf "White dwarf").

The advanced sequence of burning fuels is driven by [gravitational collapse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_collapse "Gravitational collapse") and its associated heating, resulting in the subsequent burning of [carbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_burning "Carbon burning"), [oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen-burning_process "Oxygen-burning process") and [silicon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_burning "Silicon burning"). However, most of the nucleosynthesis in the mass range _[A](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass\_number "Mass number")_ = 28–56 (from silicon to nickel) is actually caused by the upper layers of the star [collapsing onto the core](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supernova#Core_collapse "Supernova"), creating a compressional [shock wave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shock_wave "Shock wave") rebounding outward. The shock front briefly raises temperatures by roughly 50%, thereby causing furious burning for about a second. This final burning in massive stars, called _explosive nucleosynthesis_ or [supernova nucleosynthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supernova_nucleosynthesis "Supernova nucleosynthesis"), is the final epoch of stellar nucleosynthesis.

A stimulus to the development of the theory of nucleosynthesis was the discovery of variations in the [abundances of elements found in the universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abundance_of_the_chemical_elements#Universe "Abundance of the chemical elements"). The need for a physical description was already inspired by the relative abundances of the chemical elements in the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System"). Those abundances, when plotted on a graph as a function of the atomic number of the element, have a jagged sawtooth shape that varies by factors of tens of millions (see [history of nucleosynthesis theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_nucleosynthesis_theory "History of nucleosynthesis theory")).[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis#cite_note-4) This suggested a natural process that is not random. A second stimulus to understanding the processes of stellar nucleosynthesis occurred during the 20th century, when it was realized that the [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy "Energy") released from nuclear fusion reactions accounted for the longevity of the [Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun "Sun") as a source of heat and light.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis#cite_note-Clayton1968-5)

[![Image 3](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Arthur_Stanley_Eddington.jpg/250px-Arthur_Stanley_Eddington.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Arthur_Stanley_Eddington.jpg)

In 1920, [Arthur Eddington](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arthur_Eddington "Arthur Eddington") proposed that stars obtained their energy from [nuclear fusion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_fusion#Stellar_reaction_chains "Nuclear fusion") of [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen") to form [helium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium "Helium") and also raised the possibility that the heavier elements are produced in stars.

In 1920, [Arthur Eddington](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arthur_Eddington "Arthur Eddington"), on the basis of the precise measurements of atomic masses by [F.W. Aston](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Francis_William_Aston "Francis William Aston") and a preliminary suggestion by [Jean Perrin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_Perrin "Jean Perrin"), proposed that stars obtained their energy from [nuclear fusion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_fusion "Nuclear fusion") of [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen") to form [helium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium "Helium") and raised the possibility that the heavier elements are produced in stars.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis#cite_note-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis#cite_note-7)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis#cite_note-8) This was a preliminary step toward the idea of stellar nucleosynthesis. In 1928 [George Gamow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_Gamow "George Gamow") derived what is now called the [Gamow factor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamow_factor "Gamow factor"), a [quantum-mechanical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics") formula yielding the probability for two contiguous nuclei to overcome the electrostatic [Coulomb barrier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coulomb_barrier "Coulomb barrier") between them and approach each other closely enough to undergo nuclear reaction due to the [strong nuclear force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strong_nuclear_force "Strong nuclear force") which is effective only at very short distances.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis#cite_note-9): 410  In the following decade the Gamow factor was used by [Robert d'Escourt Atkinson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_d%27Escourt_Atkinson "Robert d'Escourt Atkinson") and [Fritz Houtermans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fritz_Houtermans "Fritz Houtermans") and later by [Edward Teller](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edward_Teller "Edward Teller") and Gamow himself to derive the rate at which nuclear reactions would occur at the high temperatures believed to exist in stellar interiors.

In 1939, in a [Nobel lecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nobel_Prize#Nobel_lecture "Nobel Prize") entitled "Energy Production in Stars", [Hans Bethe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hans_Bethe "Hans Bethe") analyzed the different possibilities for reactions by which hydrogen is fused into helium.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis#cite_note-10) He defined two processes that he believed to be the sources of energy in stars. The first one, the [proton–proton chain reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton%E2%80%93proton_chain_reaction "Proton–proton chain reaction"), is the dominant energy source in stars with masses up to about the mass of the Sun. The second process, the [carbon–nitrogen–oxygen cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CNO_cycle "CNO cycle"), which was also considered by [Carl Friedrich von Weizsäcker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Friedrich_von_Weizs%C3%A4cker "Carl Friedrich von Weizsäcker") in 1938, is more important in more massive main-sequence stars.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis#cite_note-11): 167  These works concerned the energy generation capable of keeping stars hot. A clear physical description of the proton–proton chain and of the CNO cycle appears in a 1968 textbook.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis#cite_note-12): 365  Bethe's two papers did not address the creation of heavier nuclei, however. That theory was begun by Fred Hoyle in 1946 with his argument that a collection of very hot nuclei would assemble thermodynamically into [iron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron "Iron").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis#cite_note-Hoyle1946-1) Hoyle followed that in 1954 with a paper describing how advanced fusion stages within massive stars would synthesize the elements from carbon to iron in mass.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis#cite_note-Hoyle1954-2)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis#cite_note-13)

Hoyle's theory was extended to other processes, beginning with the publication of the 1957 review paper "Synthesis of the Elements in Stars" by [Margaret Burbidge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Margaret_Burbidge "Margaret Burbidge"), [Geoffrey Burbidge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geoffrey_Burbidge "Geoffrey Burbidge"), [William Alfred Fowler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Alfred_Fowler "William Alfred Fowler") and [Fred Hoyle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fred_Hoyle "Fred Hoyle"), more commonly referred to as the [B 2 FH paper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/B2FH_paper "B2FH paper").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis#cite_note-B2FH-3) This review paper collected and refined earlier research into a heavily cited picture that gave promise of accounting for the observed relative abundances of the elements; but it did not itself enlarge Hoyle's 1954 picture for the origin of primary nuclei as much as many assumed, except in the understanding of nucleosynthesis of those elements heavier than iron by neutron capture. Significant improvements were made by [Alastair G. W. Cameron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alastair_G._W._Cameron "Alastair G. W. Cameron") and by [Donald D. Clayton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Donald_D._Clayton "Donald D. Clayton"). In 1957 Cameron presented his own independent approach to nucleosynthesis,[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis#cite_note-14) informed by Hoyle's example, and introduced computers into time-dependent calculations of evolution of nuclear systems. Clayton calculated the first time-dependent models of the [_s_-process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S-process "S-process") in 1961[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis#cite_note-15) and of the [_r_-process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/R-process "R-process") in 1965,[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis#cite_note-Seeger1965-16) as well as of the burning of silicon into the abundant alpha-particle nuclei and iron-group elements in 1968,[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis#cite_note-Bodansky1968a-17)[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis#cite_note-Bodansky1968b-18) and discovered radiogenic chronologies[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis#cite_note-19) for determining the age of the elements.

The most important reactions in stellar nucleosynthesis:

*   [Hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen") fusion: 
    *   [Deuterium fusion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deuterium_fusion "Deuterium fusion")
    *   The [proton–proton chain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton%E2%80%93proton_chain "Proton–proton chain")
    *   The [carbon–nitrogen–oxygen cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CNO_cycle "CNO cycle")

*   [Helium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium "Helium") fusion: 
    *   The [triple-alpha process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triple-alpha_process "Triple-alpha process")
    *   The [alpha process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_process "Alpha process")

*   Fusion of heavier elements: 
    *   [Lithium burning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lithium_burning "Lithium burning"): a process found most commonly in [brown dwarfs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brown_dwarf "Brown dwarf")
    *   [Carbon-burning process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon-burning_process "Carbon-burning process")
    *   [Neon-burning process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neon-burning_process "Neon-burning process")
    *   [Oxygen-burning process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen-burning_process "Oxygen-burning process")
    *   [Silicon-burning process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon-burning_process "Silicon-burning process")

*   Production of elements heavier than [iron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron "Iron"): 
    *   [Neutron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron "Neutron") capture: 
        *   The [r-process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/R-process "R-process")
        *   The [s-process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S-process "S-process")

    *   [Proton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton "Proton") capture: 
        *   The [rp-process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rp-process "Rp-process")
        *   The [p-process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/P-process "P-process")

    *   [Photodisintegration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photodisintegration "Photodisintegration")

[![Image 7](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/CNO_Cycle.svg/330px-CNO_Cycle.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:CNO_Cycle.svg)

**CNO-I cycle**

Hydrogen fusion (nuclear fusion of four protons to form a [helium-4](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium-4 "Helium-4") nucleus[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis#cite_note-jones2009-20)) is the dominant process that generates energy in the cores of [main-sequence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Main_sequence "Main sequence") stars. It is also called "hydrogen burning", which should not be confused with the [chemical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_reaction "Chemical reaction")[combustion of hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#Combustion "Hydrogen") in an [oxidizing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxidizing "Oxidizing") atmosphere. There are two predominant processes by which stellar hydrogen fusion occurs: [proton–proton chain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton%E2%80%93proton_chain "Proton–proton chain") and the carbon–nitrogen–oxygen (CNO) cycle. Ninety percent of all stars, with the exception of [white dwarfs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_dwarfs "White dwarfs"), are fusing hydrogen by these two processes.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis#cite_note-21): 245

In the cores of lower-mass main-sequence stars such as the [Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun "Sun"), the dominant energy production process is the [proton–proton chain reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton%E2%80%93proton_chain_reaction "Proton–proton chain reaction"). This creates a helium-4 nucleus through a sequence of reactions that begin with the fusion of two protons to form a [deuterium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deuterium "Deuterium") nucleus (one proton plus one neutron) along with an ejected positron and neutrino.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis#cite_note-bohm_vitense1992-22) In each complete fusion cycle, the proton–proton chain reaction releases about 26.2 MeV.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis#cite_note-bohm_vitense1992-22) Proton-proton chain with a dependence of approximately T 4, meaning the reaction cycle is highly sensitive to temperature; a 10% rise of temperature would increase energy production by this method by 46%, hence, this hydrogen fusion process can occur in up to a third of the star's radius and occupy half the star's mass. For stars above 35% of the Sun's mass,[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis#cite_note-aaa496_3_787-23) the [energy flux](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_flux "Energy flux") toward the surface is sufficiently low and energy transfer from the core region remains by [radiative heat transfer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiative_heat_transfer "Radiative heat transfer"), rather than by [convective heat transfer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convection_(heat_transfer) "Convection (heat transfer)").[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis#cite_note-deloore_doom1992-24) As a result, there is little mixing of fresh hydrogen into the core or fusion products outward.

In higher-mass stars, the dominant energy production process is the [CNO cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CNO_cycle "CNO cycle"), which is a [catalytic cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catalytic_cycle "Catalytic cycle") that uses nuclei of carbon, nitrogen and oxygen as intermediaries and in the end produces a helium nucleus as with the proton–proton chain.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis#cite_note-bohm_vitense1992-22) During a complete CNO cycle, 25.0 MeV of energy is released. The difference in energy production of this cycle, compared to the proton–proton chain reaction, is accounted for by the energy lost through [neutrino](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutrino "Neutrino") emission.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis#cite_note-bohm_vitense1992-22) CNO cycle is highly sensitive to temperature, with rates proportional to the 16th to 20th power of the temperature; a 10% increase in temperature would result in a 350% increase in energy production. About 90% of the CNO cycle energy generation occurs within the inner 15% of the star's mass, hence it is strongly concentrated at the core.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis#cite_note-jeffrey2010-25) This results in such an intense outward energy flux that [convective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convective "Convective") energy transfer becomes more important than does [radiative transfer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiative_transfer "Radiative transfer"). As a result, the core region becomes a [convection zone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convection_zone "Convection zone"), which stirs the hydrogen fusion region and keeps it well mixed with the surrounding proton-rich region.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis#cite_note-karttunen_oja2007-26) This core convection occurs in stars where the CNO cycle contributes more than 20% of the total energy. As the star ages and the core temperature increases, the region occupied by the convection zone slowly shrinks from 20% of the mass down to the inner 8% of the mass.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis#cite_note-jeffrey2010-25) The Sun produces on the order of 1% of its energy from the CNO cycle.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis#cite_note-27)[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis#cite_note-28)[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis#cite_note-29): 357 [[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis#cite_note-30)[[b]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis#cite_note-31)

The type of hydrogen fusion process that dominates in a star is determined by the temperature dependency differences between the two reactions. The proton–proton chain reaction starts at temperatures about 4×10 6[K](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvin "Kelvin"),[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis#cite_note-reid_hawley2005-32) making it the dominant fusion mechanism in smaller stars. A self-maintaining CNO chain requires a higher temperature of approximately 1.6×10 7 K, but thereafter it increases more rapidly in efficiency as the temperature rises, than does the proton–proton reaction.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis#cite_note-salaris_cassini2005-33) Above approximately 1.7×10 7 K, the CNO cycle becomes the dominant source of energy. This temperature is achieved in the cores of main-sequence stars with at least 1.3 times the mass of the [Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun "Sun").[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis#cite_note-apj701_1_837-34) The Sun itself has a core temperature of about 1.57×10 7 K.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis#cite_note-35): 5  As a main-sequence star ages, the core temperature will rise, resulting in a steadily increasing contribution from its CNO cycle.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis#cite_note-jeffrey2010-25)

Main sequence stars accumulate helium in their cores as a result of hydrogen fusion, but the core does not become hot enough to initiate helium fusion. Helium fusion first begins when a star leaves the [red giant branch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_giant_branch "Red giant branch") after accumulating sufficient helium in its core to ignite it. In stars around the mass of the Sun, this begins at the tip of the red giant branch with a [helium flash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_flash "Helium flash") from a [degenerate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degenerate_matter "Degenerate matter") helium core, and the star moves to the [horizontal branch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horizontal_branch "Horizontal branch") where it burns helium in its core. More massive stars ignite helium in their core without a flash and execute a [blue loop](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blue_loop "Blue loop") before reaching the [asymptotic giant branch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asymptotic_giant_branch "Asymptotic giant branch"). Such a star initially moves away from the AGB toward bluer colours, then loops back again to what is called the [Hayashi track](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hayashi_track "Hayashi track"). An important consequence of blue loops is that they give rise to classical [Cepheid variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cepheid_variable "Cepheid variable"), of central importance in determining distances in the [Milky Way](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milky_Way "Milky Way") and to nearby galaxies.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis#cite_note-36): 250  Despite the name, stars on a blue loop from the red giant branch are typically not blue in colour but are rather yellow giants, possibly Cepheid variables. They fuse helium until the core is largely [carbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon "Carbon") and [oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen "Oxygen"). The most massive stars become supergiants when they leave the main sequence and quickly start helium fusion as they become [red supergiants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_supergiant "Red supergiant"). After the helium is exhausted in the core of a star, helium fusion will continue in a shell around the carbon–oxygen core.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis#cite_note-jones2009-20)[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis#cite_note-deloore_doom1992-24)

In all cases, helium is fused to carbon via the triple-alpha process, i.e., three helium nuclei are transformed into carbon via [8 Be](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beryllium-8 "Beryllium-8").[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis#cite_note-37): 30  This can then form oxygen, neon, and heavier elements via the alpha process. In this way, the alpha process preferentially produces elements with even numbers of protons by the capture of helium nuclei. Elements with odd numbers of protons are formed by other fusion pathways.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis#cite_note-38): 398

The reaction rate density between species _A_ and _B_, having number densities _n_ _A_,_B_, is given by:![Image 8: {\displaystyle r=n_{A}\,n_{B}\,k_{r}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b535e8690b58411d85bd554693ecedcf57744509)where _k r_ is the [reaction rate constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reaction_rate_constant "Reaction rate constant") of each single elementary binary reaction composing the [nuclear fusion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_fusion "Nuclear fusion") process;![Image 9: {\displaystyle k_{r}=\langle \sigma (v)\,v\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/179fad029b39cb52334afa4798b8c104ce503282)where _σ_(_v_) is the cross-section at relative velocity _v_, and averaging is performed over all velocities.

Semi-classically, the cross section is proportional to ![Image 10: {\textstyle \pi \,\lambda ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7a0e996a7a553ca6e95fc7e97be867e7a9810cd5), where ![Image 11: {\textstyle \lambda =h/p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e9f3cdc73b97d28cb77a9288b0bcf4ae8d84daae) is the [de Broglie wavelength](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matter_wave "Matter wave"). Thus semi-classically the cross section is proportional to ![Image 12: {\textstyle {\frac {E}{m}}=c^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/72c7ba5eccdcf3cdb6546e897055e7e4f3c0bfde).

However, since the reaction involves [quantum tunneling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_tunneling "Quantum tunneling"), there is an exponential damping at low energies that depends on [Gamow factor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamow_factor "Gamow factor")_E_ G, given by an [Arrhenius-type equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arrhenius_equation "Arrhenius equation"):![Image 13: {\displaystyle \sigma (E)={\frac {S(E)}{E}}e^{-{\sqrt {\frac {E_{\text{G}}}{E}}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9a61ab7fce2a22dc7a5a263fcc8f76f239eaecdc)Here astrophysical [_S_-factor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S-factor "S-factor")_S_(_E_) depends on the details of the nuclear interaction, and has the dimension of an energy multiplied by a [cross section](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barn_(unit) "Barn (unit)").

One then integrates over all energies to get the total reaction rate, using the [Maxwell–Boltzmann distribution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxwell%E2%80%93Boltzmann_distribution#Distribution_for_the_energy "Maxwell–Boltzmann distribution") and the relation:![Image 14: {\displaystyle {\frac {r}{V}}=n_{A}n_{B}\int _{0}^{\infty }{\Bigl (}{\frac {S(E)}{E}}\,e^{-{\sqrt {\frac {E_{\text{G}}}{E}}}}\cdot 2{\sqrt {\frac {E}{\pi (kT)^{3}}}}\,e^{-{\frac {E}{kT}}}\,\cdot {\sqrt {\frac {2E}{m_{\text{R}}}}}{\Bigr )}dE}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/507b76e415111a48ca96c0be0297816e630d8da1)where _k_ = 86,17 μeV/K, ![Image 15: {\displaystyle m_{\text{R}}={\frac {m_{A}m_{B}}{m_{A}+m_{B}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2e9bacba76ebde9a61c3380363a0bffb96d4a365) is the [reduced mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reduced_mass "Reduced mass"). The integrand equals

![Image 16: {\displaystyle S(E)\,e^{-{\sqrt {\frac {E_{\text{G}}}{E}}}}\cdot 2{\sqrt {2/\pi }}(kT)^{-3/2}\,e^{-{\frac {E}{kT}}}\,/{\sqrt {m_{\text{R}}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d51cf042149c187b05fed09b1f5034b7470fe714)

Since this integration of _f_(_E_, constant _T_) has an exponential damping at high energies of the form ![Image 17: {\textstyle \sim e^{-{\frac {E}{kT}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e504a1e1ba63a964464c0953b611cf76d6fa5873) and at low energies from the Gamow factor, the integral almost vanishes everywhere except around the peak at E 0, called **Gamow peak.**[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis#cite_note-39): 185  There:![Image 18: {\displaystyle -{\frac {\partial }{\partial E}}\left({\sqrt {\frac {E_{\text{G}}}{E}}}+{\frac {E}{kT}}\right)\,=\,0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3ec49d397dd2d7cfabc5f9fd234d290a81d83c38)

Thus:

![Image 19: {\displaystyle E_{0}=\left({\frac {1}{2}}kT{\sqrt {E_{\text{G}}}}\right)^{\frac {2}{3}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8dc47e257209ad44cbcc08464423a1b66ce2b673) and ![Image 20: {\displaystyle {\sqrt {E_{\text{G}}}}=E_{0}^{\frac {3}{2}}/{\frac {1}{2}}kT}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b3bf7d4dc7fe17b5308fcb495965572e7d7b3454)

The exponent can then be approximated around _E_ 0 as:![Image 21: {\displaystyle e^{-({\frac {E}{kT}}+{\sqrt {\frac {E_{\text{G}}}{E}}})}\approx e^{-{\frac {3E_{0}}{kT}}}e^{{\bigl (}-{\frac {3(E-E_{0})^{2}}{4E_{0}kT}}{\bigr )}}=e^{-{\frac {3E_{0}}{kT}}{\bigl (}1+({\frac {E-E_{0}}{2E_{0}}})^{2}{\bigr )}}=e^{-{\frac {3E_{0}}{kT}}{\bigl (}1+(E/E_{0}-1)^{2}/4{\bigr )}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cb5a914befe395644ba993c3901da8fd917424bf)

And the reaction rate is approximated as:[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis#cite_note-40)![Image 22: {\displaystyle {\frac {r}{V}}\approx n_{A}\,n_{B}\,{\frac {4{\sqrt {(}}2/3)}{\sqrt {m_{\text{R}}}}}\,{\sqrt {E_{0}}}{\frac {S(E_{0})}{kT}}\,e^{-{\frac {3E_{0}}{kT}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bcfdac9026e351ff41ac66d55cd3aa219c5d88d4)

Values of _S_(_E_ 0) are typically 10−3 – 10 3[keV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/KeV "KeV")·[b](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barn_(unit) "Barn (unit)"), but are damped by a huge factor when involving a [beta decay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beta_decay "Beta decay"), due to the relation between the intermediate bound state (e.g. [diproton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diproton "Diproton")) [half-life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Half-life "Half-life") and the beta decay half-life, as in the [proton–proton chain reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton%E2%80%93proton_chain_reaction "Proton–proton chain reaction"). Note that typical core temperatures in [main-sequence stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Main-sequence_star "Main-sequence star") (the Sun) give _kT_ of the order of 1 keV:[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis#cite_note-41)![Image 23: {\textstyle \log _{10}kT=-16+\log _{10}2.17}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/547e51b5ea55546c5272e7e29c21b06737ac7754).[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis#cite_note-42): ch. 3

Thus, the limiting reaction in the [CNO cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CNO_cycle "CNO cycle"), proton capture by [14 7 N](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen-14 "Nitrogen-14"), has _S_(_E_ 0) ~ _S_(0) = 3.5 keV·b, while the limiting reaction in the [proton–proton chain reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton%E2%80%93proton_chain_reaction "Proton–proton chain reaction"), the creation of [deuterium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deuterium "Deuterium") from two protons, has a much lower _S_(_E_ 0) ~ _S_(0) = 4×10−22 keV·b.[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis#cite_note-43)[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis#cite_note-44) Incidentally, since the former reaction has a much higher Gamow factor, and due to the relative [abundance of elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abundance_of_elements "Abundance of elements") in typical stars, the two reaction rates are equal at a temperature value that is within the core temperature ranges of main-sequence stars.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis#cite_note-45)
