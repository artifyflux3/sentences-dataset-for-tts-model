In [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry"), a **hyperboloid of revolution**, sometimes called a **circular hyperboloid**, is the [surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_(mathematics) "Surface (mathematics)") generated by rotating a [hyperbola](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbola "Hyperbola") around one of its [principal axes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbola#Equation "Hyperbola"). A **hyperboloid** is the surface obtained from a hyperboloid of revolution by deforming it by means of directional [scalings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scaling_(geometry) "Scaling (geometry)"), or more generally, of an [affine transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_transformation "Affine transformation").

A hyperboloid is a [quadric surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadric_surface "Quadric surface"), that is, a [surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_(mathematics) "Surface (mathematics)") defined as the [zero set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero_set "Zero set") of a [polynomial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial "Polynomial") of degree two in three variables. Among quadric surfaces, a hyperboloid is characterized by not being a [cone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conical_surface "Conical surface") or a [cylinder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylinder "Cylinder"), having a [center of symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_symmetry "Central symmetry"), and intersecting many [planes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_(geometry) "Plane (geometry)") into hyperbolas. A hyperboloid has three pairwise [perpendicular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular "Perpendicular")[axes of symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_symmetry "Rotational symmetry"), and three pairwise perpendicular [planes of symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflection_symmetry "Reflection symmetry").

Given a hyperboloid, one can choose a [Cartesian coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system "Cartesian coordinate system") such that the hyperboloid is defined by one of the following equations: ![Image 1: {\displaystyle {x^{2} \over a^{2}}+{y^{2} \over b^{2}}-{z^{2} \over c^{2}}=1,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dc4513c2ed7df1db75e6d90d686ab1bfab62abbc) or ![Image 2: {\displaystyle {x^{2} \over a^{2}}+{y^{2} \over b^{2}}-{z^{2} \over c^{2}}=-1.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/73ae714046ed8b18c4490494d3ad4716ad853f66) The coordinate axes are axes of symmetry of the hyperboloid and the origin is the center of symmetry of the hyperboloid. In any case, the hyperboloid is [asymptotic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asymptotic "Asymptotic") to the cone of the equations: ![Image 3: {\displaystyle {x^{2} \over a^{2}}+{y^{2} \over b^{2}}-{z^{2} \over c^{2}}=0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/01517e09c5238b1c49c47dad06d1038ec1d3221f)

One has a hyperboloid of revolution if and only if ![Image 4: {\displaystyle a^{2}=b^{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b6c88090579caa3824ae787f52eedfbc6bece032) Otherwise, the axes are uniquely defined ([up to](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Up_to "Up to") the exchange of the _x_-axis and the _y_-axis).

There are two kinds of hyperboloids. In the first case (+1 in the right-hand side of the equation): a **one-sheet hyperboloid**, also called a **hyperbolic hyperboloid**. It is a [connected surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connected_set "Connected set"), which has a negative [Gaussian curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaussian_curvature "Gaussian curvature") at every point. This implies near every point the intersection of the hyperboloid and its [tangent plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_plane "Tangent plane") at the point consists of two branches of curve that have distinct tangents at the point. In the case of the one-sheet hyperboloid, these branches of curves are [lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_(geometry) "Line (geometry)") and thus the one-sheet hyperboloid is a [doubly ruled](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doubly_ruled "Doubly ruled") surface.

In the second case (−1 in the right-hand side of the equation): a **two-sheet hyperboloid**, also called an **elliptic hyperboloid**. The surface has two [connected components](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connected_component_(topology) "Connected component (topology)") and a positive Gaussian curvature at every point. The surface is _convex_ in the sense that the tangent plane at every point intersects the surface only in this point.

### Generalised equations

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hyperboloid&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Generalised equations")]

More generally, an arbitrarily oriented hyperboloid, centered at **v**, is defined by the equation ![Image 16: {\displaystyle (\mathbf {x} -\mathbf {v} )^{\mathrm {T} }A(\mathbf {x} -\mathbf {v} )=1,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e756b1802fcbcc5f1adcb13eeb66277b4ce9bd2d) where _A_ is a [matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_(mathematics) "Matrix (mathematics)") and **x**, **v** are [vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_vector "Euclidean vector").

The [eigenvectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eigenvector "Eigenvector") of _A_ define the principal directions of the hyperboloid and the [eigenvalues](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eigenvalue "Eigenvalue") of A are the [reciprocals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplicative_inverse "Multiplicative inverse") of the squares of the semi-axes: ![Image 17: {\displaystyle {1/a^{2}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/530d79544c3605817a9a00ed9f0c286d9e93835b), ![Image 18: {\displaystyle {1/b^{2}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/04fa2baa823521b23ed15b4eaaa94448a6565abd) and ![Image 19: {\displaystyle {1/c^{2}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2fea3653c3977e29fb732f79aa6337f19c0a0567). The one-sheet hyperboloid has two positive eigenvalues and one negative eigenvalue. The two-sheet hyperboloid has one positive eigenvalue and two negative eigenvalues.

### Hyperboloid of one sheet

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hyperboloid&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Hyperboloid of one sheet")]

#### Lines on the surface

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hyperboloid&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Lines on the surface")]

*   A hyperboloid of one sheet contains two pencils of lines. It is a [doubly ruled surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doubly_ruled_surface "Doubly ruled surface").

If the hyperboloid has the equation ![Image 20: {\displaystyle {x^{2} \over a^{2}}+{y^{2} \over b^{2}}-{z^{2} \over c^{2}}=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/74dc61c02972f87421206576792c52b46c35c610) then the lines ![Image 21: {\displaystyle g_{\alpha }^{\pm }:\mathbf {x} (t)={\begin{pmatrix}a\cos \alpha \\b\sin \alpha \\0\end{pmatrix}}+t\cdot {\begin{pmatrix}-a\sin \alpha \\b\cos \alpha \\\pm c\end{pmatrix}}\ ,\quad t\in \mathbb {R} ,\ 0\leq \alpha \leq 2\pi \ }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/602d21062fc58e9f5d40f9f1b46ec7f7ea40292d) are contained in the surface.

In case ![Image 22: {\displaystyle a=b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1956b03d1314c7071ac1f45ed7b1e29422dcfcc4) the hyperboloid is a surface of revolution and can be generated by rotating one of the two lines ![Image 23: {\displaystyle g_{0}^{+}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a0d96b7cb6da2dadd6ed3a709b836ddb86730458) or ![Image 24: {\displaystyle g_{0}^{-}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dd432ed4844ce649f82f39d4bf438ebad4e3b60f), which are skew to the rotation axis (see picture). This property is called _[Wren](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christopher\_Wren "Christopher Wren")'s theorem_.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperboloid_of_one_sheet#cite_note-1) The more common generation of a one-sheet hyperboloid of revolution is rotating a [hyperbola](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbola "Hyperbola") around its [semi-minor axis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semi-major_and_semi-minor_axes#Hyperbola "Semi-major and semi-minor axes") (see picture; rotating the hyperbola around its other axis gives a two-sheet hyperbola of revolution).

A hyperboloid of one sheet is _[projectively](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective\_geometry "Projective geometry")_ equivalent to a [hyperbolic paraboloid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_paraboloid "Hyperbolic paraboloid").

For simplicity the plane sections of the _unit hyperboloid_ with equation ![Image 25: {\displaystyle \ H_{1}:x^{2}+y^{2}-z^{2}=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/872735598e70e1bdb5d3d11fec1dc61f8bc5d8ed) are considered. Because a hyperboloid in general position is an affine image of the unit hyperboloid, the result applies to the general case, too.

Obviously, any one-sheet hyperboloid of revolution contains circles. This is also true, but less obvious, in the general case (see [circular section](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circular_section "Circular section")).

### Hyperboloid of two sheets

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hyperboloid&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Hyperboloid of two sheets")]

[![Image 26](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Hyperboloid-2s.svg/250px-Hyperboloid-2s.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Hyperboloid-2s.svg)

hyperboloid of two sheets: generation by rotating a hyperbola

[![Image 27](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Hyperbo-2s-ca.svg/250px-Hyperbo-2s-ca.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Hyperbo-2s-ca.svg)

hyperboloid of two sheets: plane sections

The hyperboloid of two sheets does _not_ contain lines. The discussion of plane sections can be performed for the _unit hyperboloid of two sheets_ with equation ![Image 28: {\displaystyle H_{2}:\ x^{2}+y^{2}-z^{2}=-1.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/30bc67c5b9c1e7000d769ecb2b74409a0dc038c5) which can be generated by a rotating [hyperbola](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbola "Hyperbola") around one of its axes (the one that cuts the hyperbola)

Obviously, any two-sheet hyperboloid of revolution contains circles. This is also true, but less obvious, in the general case (see [circular section](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circular_section "Circular section")).

_Remark_: A hyperboloid of two sheets is _projectively_ equivalent to a sphere.

The hyperboloids with equations ![Image 29: {\displaystyle {\frac {x^{2}}{a^{2}}}+{\frac {y^{2}}{b^{2}}}-{\frac {z^{2}}{c^{2}}}=1,\quad {\frac {x^{2}}{a^{2}}}+{\frac {y^{2}}{b^{2}}}-{\frac {z^{2}}{c^{2}}}=-1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fdc651ff03a77e410aec9d5485266b5aff250563) are

*   _pointsymmetric_ to the origin,
*   _symmetric to the coordinate planes_ and
*   _rotational symmetric_ to the z-axis and symmetric to any plane containing the z-axis, in case of ![Image 30: {\displaystyle a=b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1956b03d1314c7071ac1f45ed7b1e29422dcfcc4) (hyperboloid of revolution).

Whereas the [Gaussian curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaussian_curvature "Gaussian curvature") of a hyperboloid of one sheet is negative, that of a two-sheet hyperboloid is positive. In spite of its positive curvature, the hyperboloid of two sheets with another suitably chosen metric can also be used as a [model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperboloid_model "Hyperboloid model") for hyperbolic geometry.

In more than three dimensions
-----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hyperboloid&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: In more than three dimensions")]

Hyperboloids are frequently found in mathematics of higher dimensions. For example, in a [pseudo-Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudo-Euclidean_space "Pseudo-Euclidean space") one has the use of a [quadratic form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadratic_form "Quadratic form"): ![Image 31: {\displaystyle q(x)=\left(x_{1}^{2}+\cdots +x_{k}^{2}\right)-\left(x_{k+1}^{2}+\cdots +x_{n}^{2}\right),\quad k<n.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/053badfca866cfc466a14c4ec4182e2436be9947) When _c_ is any [constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constant_(mathematics) "Constant (mathematics)"), then the part of the space given by ![Image 32: {\displaystyle \lbrace x\ :\ q(x)=c\rbrace }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b0c1f0f2d9ef26bc35e2ca594dea4cd7b7bae5eb) is called a _hyperboloid_. The degenerate case corresponds to _c_ = 0.

As an example, consider the following passage:[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperboloid_of_one_sheet#cite_note-4)

> ... the velocity vectors always lie on a surface which Minkowski calls a four-dimensional hyperboloid since, expressed in terms of purely real coordinates (_y_ 1, ..., _y_ 4), its equation is _y_ 2
> 
> 1 + _y_ 2
> 
> 2 + _y_ 2
> 
> 3 − _y_ 2
> 
> 4 = −1, analogous to the hyperboloid _y_ 2
> 
> 1 + _y_ 2
> 
> 2 − _y_ 2
> 
> 3 = −1 of three-dimensional space.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperboloid_of_one_sheet#cite_note-6)

However, the term **quasi-sphere** is also used in this context since the sphere and hyperboloid have some commonality (See _[§Relation to the sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperboloid\_of\_one\_sheet#Relation\_to\_the\_sphere)_ below).

Hyperboloid structures
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hyperboloid&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Hyperboloid structures")]

One-sheeted hyperboloids are used in construction, with the structures called [hyperboloid structures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperboloid_structure "Hyperboloid structure"). A hyperboloid is a [doubly ruled surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doubly_ruled_surface "Doubly ruled surface"); thus, it can be built with straight steel beams, producing a strong structure at a lower cost than other methods. Examples include [cooling towers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cooling_tower "Cooling tower"), especially of [power stations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_station "Power station"), and [many other structures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_hyperboloid_structures "List of hyperboloid structures").

Relation to the sphere
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hyperboloid&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Relation to the sphere")]

In 1853 [William Rowan Hamilton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Rowan_Hamilton "William Rowan Hamilton") published his _Lectures on Quaternions_ which included presentation of [biquaternions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biquaternion "Biquaternion"). The following passage from page 673 shows how Hamilton uses biquaternion algebra and vectors from [quaternions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quaternion "Quaternion") to produce hyperboloids from the equation of a [sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere "Sphere"):

> ... the _equation of the unit sphere_ _ρ_ 2 + 1 = 0, and change the vector _ρ_ to a _bivector form_, such as _σ_ + _τ_√−1. The equation of the sphere then breaks up into the system of the two following,
> 
> 
> _σ_ 2 − _τ_ 2 + 1 = 0, **S**._στ_ = 0;
> 
> 
> and suggests our considering _σ_ and _τ_ as two real and rectangular vectors, such that
> 
> 
> **T**_τ_ = (**T**_σ_ 2 − 1 )1/2.
> 
> 
> Hence it is easy to infer that if we assume _σ_ || _λ_, where _λ_ is a vector in a given position, the _new real vector_ _σ_ + _τ_ will terminate on the surface of a _double-sheeted and equilateral hyperboloid_; and that if, on the other hand, we assume _τ_ || _λ_, then the locus of the extremity of the real vector _σ_ + _τ_ will be an _equilateral but single-sheeted hyperboloid_. The study of these two hyperboloids is, therefore, in this way connected very simply, through biquaternions, with the study of the sphere; ...

In this passage **S** is the operator giving the scalar part of a quaternion, and **T** is the "tensor", now called [norm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norm_(mathematics) "Norm (mathematics)"), of a quaternion.

A modern view of the unification of the sphere and hyperboloid uses the idea of a [conic section](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conic_section "Conic section") as a [slice of a quadratic form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conic_section#As_slice_of_quadratic_form "Conic section"). Instead of a [conical surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conical_surface "Conical surface"), one requires conical [hypersurfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypersurface "Hypersurface") in [four-dimensional space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Four-dimensional_space "Four-dimensional space") with points _p_ = (_w_, _x_, _y_, _z_) ∈ **R**4 determined by [quadratic forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadratic_form "Quadratic form").
