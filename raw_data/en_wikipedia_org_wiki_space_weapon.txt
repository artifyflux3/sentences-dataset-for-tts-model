A [United States Space Command](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Space_Command "United States Space Command") impression of a conceptual [satellite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satellite "Satellite")-based [directed-energy weapon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Directed-energy_weapon "Directed-energy weapon") used to precisely strike targets on [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth "Earth")

**Space weapons** are [weapons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weapon "Weapon") used in [space warfare](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_warfare "Space warfare"). They include weapons that can attack space systems in orbit (for example, [anti-satellite weapons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anti-satellite_weapon "Anti-satellite weapon")), [attack targets on the earth from space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_bombardment "Kinetic bombardment") or [disable missiles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Missile_defense "Missile defense") travelling through space. In the course of the [militarisation of space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Militarisation_of_space "Militarisation of space"), such weapons were developed mainly by the contesting [superpowers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superpower "Superpower") during the [Cold War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cold_War "Cold War"), and some remain under development today. Space weapons are also a central theme in [military science fiction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Military_science_fiction "Military science fiction") and sci-fi video games.

Space-to-space weapons
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Space_weapon&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Space-to-space weapons")]

The Soviet [Almaz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Almaz "Almaz") secret military [space station](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_station "Space station") program was equipped with a fixed [23mm autocannon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salyut_3#On-board_gun "Salyut 3") to prevent hostile interception or boarding by hostile forces.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_weapon#cite_note-1) This was the first and so far the only space-to-space weapon to be fired in orbit.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_weapon#cite_note-2)[_[better source needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTRS "Wikipedia:NOTRS")_]

The Soviet uncrewed [Polyus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyus_(spacecraft) "Polyus (spacecraft)") weapons platform was designed to be equipped with a megawatt [carbon-dioxide laser](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon-dioxide_laser "Carbon-dioxide laser") and a self-defense cannon.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_weapon#cite_note-3)

Earth-to-space weapons
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Space_weapon&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Earth-to-space weapons")]

[![Image 10](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/SM-3-launch-USS_Shiloh-20060622.jpg/250px-SM-3-launch-USS_Shiloh-20060622.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:SM-3-launch-USS_Shiloh-20060622.jpg)

[RIM-161 Standard Missile 3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RIM-161_Standard_Missile_3 "RIM-161 Standard Missile 3")[anti-ballistic missile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anti-ballistic_missile "Anti-ballistic missile") can engage targets in low Earth orbit.

[Anti-satellite weapons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anti-satellite_weapon "Anti-satellite weapon"), which are primarily surface-to-space and air-to-space missiles, have been developed by the United States, the [USSR](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/USSR "USSR")/Russia, India and the People's Republic of China. Multiple test firings have been done as part of recent Chinese and U.S test programs that involved destroying an orbiting satellite. In general, the use of explosive and [kinetic kill](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projectile#Kinetic_projectiles "Projectile") systems is limited to relatively low altitudes due to [space debris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_debris "Space debris") issues and so as to avoid leaving debris from launch in orbit.

### Strategic Defense Initiative

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Space_weapon&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Strategic Defense Initiative")]

Main article: [Strategic Defense Initiative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strategic_Defense_Initiative "Strategic Defense Initiative")

On March 23, 1983, President [Ronald Reagan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ronald_Reagan "Ronald Reagan") proposed the [Strategic Defense Initiative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strategic_Defense_Initiative "Strategic Defense Initiative"), a research program with a goal of developing a defensive system which would destroy enemy [ICBMs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intercontinental_ballistic_missile "Intercontinental ballistic missile"). The defensive system was nicknamed [Star Wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_Wars "Star Wars"), after the movie, by its detractors. Some concepts of the system included [Brilliant Pebbles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brilliant_Pebbles "Brilliant Pebbles"), which were Kinetic Kill Vehicles, essentially small rockets launched from satellites toward their targets (a warhead, warhead bus, or even an upper stage of an ICBM). Other aspects included [satellites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satellite "Satellite") in [orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbit "Orbit") carrying powerful [laser weapons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laser_weapon "Laser weapon"), [plasma weapons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasma_weapon "Plasma weapon"), or [particle beams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_beam "Particle beam"). When a missile launch was detected, the satellite would fire at the missile (or warheads) and destroy it. Although no real hardware was ever manufactured for deployment, the military did test the use of lasers mounted on [Boeing 747s](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boeing_YAL-1 "Boeing YAL-1") to destroy missiles in the 2000s, however these were discontinued due to practical limitations of keeping a constant fleet airborne near potential launch sites due to the lasers range limitations keeping a small number from being sufficient. The tests took place at [Edwards Air Force Base](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edwards_Air_Force_Base "Edwards Air Force Base").[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

### Orbital weaponry

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Space_weapon&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Orbital weaponry")]

[![Image 11](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Silbervogel02.png/250px-Silbervogel02.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Silbervogel02.png)

_[Silbervogel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silbervogel "Silbervogel")_: [Nazi Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nazi_Germany "Nazi Germany") space weapon project.

Orbital weaponry is any weapon that is in [orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbit "Orbit") around a large body such as a planet or moon. As of December 2022, there are no known operative orbital weapons systems, but several nations have deployed orbital surveillance networks to observe other nations or armed forces. Several orbital weaponry systems were designed by the United States and the [Soviet Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Union "Soviet Union") during the [Cold War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cold_War "Cold War"). During World War II [Nazi Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nazi_Germany "Nazi Germany") was also developing plans for an orbital weapon called the [Sun gun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun_gun "Sun gun"), an orbital mirror that would have been used to focus and weaponize beams of sunlight.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_weapon#cite_note-Time-4)

Development of orbital weaponry was largely halted after the entry into force of the [Outer Space Treaty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty "Outer Space Treaty") and the [SALT II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SALT_II "SALT II") treaty. These agreements prohibit [weapons of mass destruction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weapons_of_mass_destruction "Weapons of mass destruction") from being placed in space. As other weapons exist, notably those using [kinetic bombardment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_bombardment "Kinetic bombardment"), that would not violate these treaties, some private groups and government officials have proposed a [Space Preservation Treaty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_Preservation_Treaty "Space Preservation Treaty") which would ban the placement of any weaponry in outer space.

The United States is seeking to develop Space-Based Interceptors for missile defense.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_weapon#cite_note-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_weapon#cite_note-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_weapon#cite_note-7)

### Orbital bombardment

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Space_weapon&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Orbital bombardment")]

See also: [Kinetic bombardment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_bombardment "Kinetic bombardment")

Orbital bombardment is the act of attacking targets on a planet, moon or other [astronomical object](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_object "Astronomical object") from [orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbit "Orbit") around the object, rather than from an aircraft, or a platform beyond orbit. It has been proposed as a means of attack for several weapons systems concepts, including [kinetic bombardment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_bombardment "Kinetic bombardment") and as a nuclear delivery system.

During the [Cold War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cold_War "Cold War"), the [Soviet Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Union "Soviet Union") deployed a [Fractional Orbital Bombardment System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractional_Orbital_Bombardment_System "Fractional Orbital Bombardment System") from 1968 to 1983. Using this system, a [nuclear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_weapon "Nuclear weapon") warhead could be placed in [low Earth orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit "Low Earth orbit"), and later de-orbited to hit any location on the Earth's surface. While the Soviets deployed a working version of the system, they were forbidden by the [Outer Space Treaty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty "Outer Space Treaty") to place live warheads in space. The fractional orbital bombardment system was phased out in January 1983 in compliance with the [SALT II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SALT_II "SALT II") treaty of 1979, which, among other things, prohibited the deployment of systems capable of placing [weapons of mass destruction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weapons_of_mass_destruction "Weapons of mass destruction") in such a partial orbit.

Orbital bombardment systems with conventional warheads are permitted under the terms of [SALT II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SALT_II "SALT II"). Some of the proposed systems rely on large [tungsten carbide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tungsten_carbide "Tungsten carbide")/[uranium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uranium "Uranium")[cermet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cermet "Cermet") rods dropped from orbit and depend on [kinetic energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_energy "Kinetic energy"), rather than explosives, but their mass makes them prohibitively difficult to transport to orbit.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_weapon#cite_note-8)

As of 2020 the only true orbital bombardment in history has been executed for scientific purposes. On 5 April 2019 the Japanese _[Hayabusa2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hayabusa2 "Hayabusa2")_ robotic space probe released an explosive device called an "impactor" from space onto the surface of asteroid [162173 Ryugu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/162173_Ryugu "162173 Ryugu"), in order to collect debris released by the explosion. The mission was successful and _Hayabusa2_ retrieved valuable samples of the celestial body which it brought back to Earth.
