In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), an **algebraic group** is an [algebraic variety](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_variety "Algebraic variety") endowed with a [group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_(mathematics) "Group (mathematics)") structure that is compatible with its structure as an algebraic variety. Thus the study of algebraic groups belongs both to [algebraic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_geometry "Algebraic geometry") and [group theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_theory "Group theory").

Many groups of [geometric transformations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_transformation "Geometric transformation") are algebraic groups, including [orthogonal groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_group "Orthogonal group"), [general linear groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_linear_group "General linear group"), [projective groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_linear_group "Projective linear group"), [Euclidean groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_group "Euclidean group"), etc. Many [matrix groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_group "Matrix group") are also algebraic. Other algebraic groups occur naturally in algebraic geometry, such as [elliptic curves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptic_curve "Elliptic curve") and [Jacobian varieties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacobian_varieties "Jacobian varieties").

An important class of algebraic groups is given by the [affine algebraic groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_algebraic_group "Affine algebraic group"), those whose underlying algebraic variety is an [affine variety](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_variety "Affine variety"); they are exactly the algebraic subgroups of the [general linear group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_linear_group "General linear group"), and are therefore also called _linear algebraic groups_.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_group#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBorel1991p.54-1) Another class is formed by the [abelian varieties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abelian_varieties "Abelian varieties"), which are the algebraic groups whose underlying variety is a [projective variety](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_variety "Projective variety"). [Chevalley's structure theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chevalley%27s_structure_theorem "Chevalley's structure theorem") states that every algebraic group can be constructed from groups in those two families.

Formally, an algebraic group over a field ![Image 1: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40) is an algebraic variety ![Image 2: {\displaystyle \mathrm {G} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d895cb984f1cafde4d7c7f4993e6b92d72b6ad15) over ![Image 3: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40), together with a distinguished element ![Image 4: {\displaystyle e\in \mathrm {G} (k)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/59dedcf4ee5c338f3d977be35e95a1235f616179) (the [neutral element](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutral_element "Neutral element")), and [regular maps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_map_(algebraic_geometry) "Regular map (algebraic geometry)")![Image 5: {\displaystyle \mathrm {G} \times \mathrm {G} \to \mathrm {G} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/be9e1b30def187a2a81144d2b041c2515fce145a) (the multiplication operation) and ![Image 6: {\displaystyle \mathrm {G} \to \mathrm {G} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/05897ecd8f5089268288819994a7e0007f239355) (the inversion operation) that satisfy the group axioms.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_group#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBorel1991p._46-2)

An **algebraic subgroup** of an algebraic group ![Image 7: {\displaystyle \mathrm {G} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d895cb984f1cafde4d7c7f4993e6b92d72b6ad15) is a [subvariety](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_variety#Subvariety "Algebraic variety")![Image 8: {\displaystyle \mathrm {H} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/32db8e791eaa12e32afc8fc1d60386643e43e315) of ![Image 9: {\displaystyle \mathrm {G} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d895cb984f1cafde4d7c7f4993e6b92d72b6ad15) that is also a subgroup of ![Image 10: {\displaystyle \mathrm {G} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d895cb984f1cafde4d7c7f4993e6b92d72b6ad15) (that is, the maps ![Image 11: {\displaystyle \mathrm {G} \times \mathrm {G} \to \mathrm {G} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/be9e1b30def187a2a81144d2b041c2515fce145a) and ![Image 12: {\displaystyle \mathrm {G} \to \mathrm {G} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/05897ecd8f5089268288819994a7e0007f239355) defining the group structure map ![Image 13: {\displaystyle \mathrm {H} \times \mathrm {H} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7e02cdecfaf0ed8cadb629ffae99f4460ad65b17) and ![Image 14: {\displaystyle \mathrm {H} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/32db8e791eaa12e32afc8fc1d60386643e43e315), respectively, into ![Image 15: {\displaystyle \mathrm {H} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/32db8e791eaa12e32afc8fc1d60386643e43e315)).

A _morphism_ between two algebraic groups ![Image 16: {\displaystyle \mathrm {G} ,\mathrm {G} '}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d8032d9f6c1f28634e8187eeef0c7d2db58bdaea) is a regular map ![Image 17: {\displaystyle \mathrm {G} \to \mathrm {G} '}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3cac1d788cd922a9a2d84aab952f904258ef3876) that is also a group homomorphism. Its kernel is an algebraic subgroup of ![Image 18: {\displaystyle \mathrm {G} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d895cb984f1cafde4d7c7f4993e6b92d72b6ad15), and its image is an algebraic subgroup of ![Image 19: {\displaystyle \mathrm {G} '}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1a3d1a31ef835d87640fcae8a0a9fe56f51324c3).[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_group#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBorel1991Corollary_1.4,_p._47-4)

[Quotients](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quotient_group "Quotient group") in the category of algebraic groups are more delicate to deal with. An algebraic subgroup is said to be _normal_ if it is stable under every [inner automorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inner_automorphism "Inner automorphism") (which are regular maps). If ![Image 20: {\displaystyle \mathrm {H} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/32db8e791eaa12e32afc8fc1d60386643e43e315) is a normal algebraic subgroup of ![Image 21: {\displaystyle \mathrm {G} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d895cb984f1cafde4d7c7f4993e6b92d72b6ad15), then there exists an algebraic group ![Image 22: {\displaystyle \mathrm {G} /\mathrm {H} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c1ed3d488c091d1d58c5391686a01862676e9b21) and a surjective morphism ![Image 23: {\displaystyle \pi :\mathrm {G} \to \mathrm {G} /\mathrm {H} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3a77803f4862d0373fb140d3d4fb27d061c9d5ac) such that ![Image 24: {\displaystyle \mathrm {H} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/32db8e791eaa12e32afc8fc1d60386643e43e315) is the kernel of ![Image 25: {\displaystyle \pi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9be4ba0bb8df3af72e90a0535fabcc17431e540a).[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_group#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBorel1991Theorem_6.8,_p._98-5) Note that if the field ![Image 26: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40) is not algebraically closed, then the morphism of groups ![Image 27: {\displaystyle \mathrm {G} (k)\to \mathrm {G} (k)/\mathrm {H} (k)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a8fc774a2ae84a933f3d4e436f4b5c4a9e1df65) may not be surjective (the defect of surjectivity is measured by [Galois cohomology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galois_cohomology "Galois cohomology")).

### Lie algebra of an algebraic group

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Algebraic_group&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Lie algebra of an algebraic group")]

Similarly to the [Lie group–Lie algebra correspondence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_group%E2%80%93Lie_algebra_correspondence "Lie group–Lie algebra correspondence"), to an algebraic group over a field ![Image 28: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40) is associated a [Lie algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_algebra "Lie algebra") over ![Image 29: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40). As a vector space, the Lie algebra is isomorphic to the [tangent space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_space "Tangent space") at the identity element. The Lie bracket can be constructed from its interpretation as a space of [derivations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivation_(differential_algebra) "Derivation (differential algebra)").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_group#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBorel19913.5,_p._65-6)

### Alternative definitions

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Algebraic_group&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Alternative definitions")]

A more sophisticated definition of an algebraic group over a field ![Image 30: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40) is that it is a [group scheme](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_scheme "Group scheme") over ![Image 31: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40) (group schemes can more generally be defined over [commutative rings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_ring "Commutative ring")).

Yet another definition of the concept is to say that an algebraic group over ![Image 32: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40) is a [group object](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_object "Group object") in the [category](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category_(mathematics) "Category (mathematics)") of algebraic varieties over ![Image 33: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40).

Affine algebraic groups
-----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Algebraic_group&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Affine algebraic groups")]

An algebraic group is said to be _affine_ if its underlying algebraic variety is an affine variety. Among the examples above, the additive, multiplicative, general linear, and special linear groups are affine. Using the action of an affine algebraic group on its [coordinate ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_ring "Coordinate ring"), it can be shown that every affine algebraic group is a linear (or matrix) group, meaning that it is isomorphic to an algebraic subgroup of the general linear group.

For example, the additive group can be embedded in ![Image 34: {\displaystyle \mathrm {GL} _{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/64d65e7b41879672c9bc2fa8f68a318052024352) by the morphism ![Image 35: {\displaystyle x\mapsto \left({\begin{matrix}1&x\\0&1\end{matrix}}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e1881d48317e6075da31ae84d0677fa5a7147bec).

There are many examples of such groups beyond those given previously, including [orthogonal groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_group "Orthogonal group"), [symplectic groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symplectic_group "Symplectic group"), [unipotent groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unipotent_group "Unipotent group"), [algebraic tori](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_torus "Algebraic torus"), and certain [semidirect products](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semidirect_product "Semidirect product"),[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_group#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBorel1991pp._55-56-7) such as [jet groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jet_group "Jet group"), or some [solvable groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solvable_group "Solvable group") such as that of invertible [triangular matrices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangular_matrix "Triangular matrix").

Linear algebraic groups can be classified to a certain extent. [Levi's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi%27s_theorem "Levi's theorem") states that every linear algebraic group is (essentially) a semidirect product of a unipotent group (its [unipotent radical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unipotent_radical "Unipotent radical")) with a [reductive group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reductive_group "Reductive group"). In turn, a reductive group is decomposed as (again essentially) a product of its center (an algebraic torus) with a [semisimple group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semisimple_group "Semisimple group"). The latter are classified over algebraically closed fields via their [Lie algebras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semisimple_Lie_algebra#Classification "Semisimple Lie algebra").[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_group#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBorel199124.1-8) The classification over arbitrary fields is more involved, but still well-understood.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_group#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBorel199124.2-9) If can be made very explicit in some cases, such as over the real or [_p_-adic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/P-adic "P-adic") fields, and thereby over [number fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_field "Number field") via [local-global principles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local-global_principle "Local-global principle").

Abelian varieties are connected projective algebraic groups, such as elliptic curves. They are always commutative. They arise naturally in various situations in algebraic geometry and number theory, such as the [Jacobian varieties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacobian_variety "Jacobian variety") of curves.

Structure theorem for general algebraic groups
----------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Algebraic_group&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Structure theorem for general algebraic groups")]

Not all algebraic groups are linear groups or abelian varieties; for instance, some [group schemes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_scheme "Group scheme") occurring naturally in [arithmetic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic_geometry "Arithmetic geometry") are neither.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_group#cite_note-10)[Chevalley's structure theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chevalley%27s_structure_theorem "Chevalley's structure theorem") asserts that every connected algebraic group is an extension of an [abelian variety](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abelian_variety "Abelian variety") by a [linear algebraic group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_algebraic_group "Linear algebraic group"). More precisely, if _K_ is a [perfect field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perfect_field "Perfect field"), and _G_ a connected algebraic group over _K_, then there exists a unique normal closed subgroup _H_ in _G_, such that _H_ is a connected linear algebraic group and _G_/_H_ an abelian variety.

As an algebraic variety, ![Image 36: {\displaystyle \mathrm {G} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d895cb984f1cafde4d7c7f4993e6b92d72b6ad15) carries a [Zariski topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zariski_topology "Zariski topology"). It is not in general a [group topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_group "Topological group"); that is, the group operations may not be continuous for this topology (because the Zariski topology on the product is not the product of Zariski topologies on the factors[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_group#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBorel1991p._16-11)).

An algebraic group is said to be _connected_ if the underlying algebraic variety is connected for the Zariski topology. For an algebraic group, this means that it is not the union of two proper algebraic subsets.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_group#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBorel1991p._47-12)

Examples of groups that are not connected are given by the algebraic subgroup of ![Image 37: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)th [roots of unity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Root_of_unity "Root of unity") in the multiplicative group ![Image 38: {\displaystyle \mathrm {G} _{m}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/caf525c822cb64a45e9b105ba6edd745955b610a) (each point is a Zariski-closed subset so it is not connected for ![Image 39: {\displaystyle n\geq 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d8ce9ce38d06f6bf5a3fe063118c09c2b6202bfe)). This group is generally denoted by ![Image 40: {\displaystyle \mu _{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/267d03f9351dcc8d3d3ac7cad59ea3ba4fecbfef). Other non-connected groups are the orthogonal group in even dimension (the determinant gives a surjective morphism to ![Image 41: {\displaystyle \mu _{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2f841461ae8f2eafec3fe879f7c061a73c2f7170)).

More generally, every finite group is an algebraic group (it can be realised as a finite, hence Zariski-closed, subgroup of some ![Image 42: {\displaystyle \mathrm {GL} _{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b3b1b008d4ce8c41db8241a16ee90c7166217fbe) by [Cayley's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cayley%27s_theorem "Cayley's theorem")). In addition it is both affine and projective. Thus, in particular for classification purposes, it is natural to restrict statements to connected algebraic groups.

Algebraic groups over local fields and Lie groups
-------------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Algebraic_group&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Algebraic groups over local fields and Lie groups")]

If the field ![Image 43: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40) is a [local field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_field "Local field") (for instance the real or complex numbers, or a _p_-adic field) and ![Image 44: {\displaystyle \mathrm {G} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d895cb984f1cafde4d7c7f4993e6b92d72b6ad15) is a ![Image 45: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40)-group, then the group ![Image 46: {\displaystyle \mathrm {G} (k)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b403824e297b1ff8703785d2a3478ca1a1d1af64) is endowed with the analytic topology coming from any embedding into a projective space ![Image 47: {\displaystyle \mathbb {P} ^{n}(k)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1a48c0ea3c7f59b5db430046d87ffb8237cf4917) as a quasi-projective variety. This is a group topology, and it makes ![Image 48: {\displaystyle \mathrm {G} (k)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b403824e297b1ff8703785d2a3478ca1a1d1af64) into a [topological group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_group "Topological group"). Such groups are important examples in the general theory of topological groups.

If ![Image 49: {\displaystyle k=\mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8bf4d723f5664de728ebf62b66da9f83cb55d4fe) or ![Image 50: {\displaystyle \mathbb {C} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f9add4085095b9b6d28d045fd9c92c2c09f549a7), then this makes ![Image 51: {\displaystyle \mathrm {G} (k)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b403824e297b1ff8703785d2a3478ca1a1d1af64) into a [Lie group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_group "Lie group"). Not all Lie groups can be obtained via this procedure; for example, the universal cover of [SL 2(**R**)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SL2(R) "SL2(R)"), or the quotient of the [Heisenberg group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heisenberg_group "Heisenberg group") by an infinite normal discrete subgroup.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_group#cite_note-13) An algebraic group over the real or complex numbers may have closed subgroups (in the analytic topology) that do not have the same connected component of the identity as any algebraic subgroup.

Coxeter groups and algebraic groups
-----------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Algebraic_group&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Coxeter groups and algebraic groups")]

There are a number of analogous results between algebraic groups and [Coxeter groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coxeter_group "Coxeter group") – for instance, the number of elements of the symmetric group is ![Image 52: {\displaystyle n!}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bae971720be3cc9b8d82f4cdac89cb89877514a6), and the number of elements of the general linear group over a finite field is (up to some factor) the [_q_-factorial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Q-factorial "Q-factorial")![Image 53: {\displaystyle [n]_{q}!}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/265dcf73f45dc7d6cff172fe6b6d0f457f1c107f); thus, the symmetric group behaves as though it were a linear group over "the field with one element". This is formalized by the [field with one element](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_with_one_element "Field with one element"), which considers Coxeter groups to be simple algebraic groups over the field with one element.
