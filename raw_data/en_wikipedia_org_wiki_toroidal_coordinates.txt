Toroidal coordinates are a three-dimensional orthogonal coordinate system that results from rotating the two-dimensional bipolar coordinate system about the axis that separates its two foci.  Thus, the two foci F 1 {\displaystyle F_{1}} and F 2 {\displaystyle F_{2}} in bipolar coordinates become a ring of radius a {\displaystyle a} in the x y {\displaystyle xy} plane of the toroidal coordinate system; the z {\displaystyle z} -axis is the axis of rotation. The focal ring is also known as the reference circle.

Toroidal harmonics [ edit ] Standard separation [ edit ] The 3-variable Laplace equation ∇ ∇ 2 Φ Φ = 0 {\displaystyle \nabla ^{2}\Phi =0} admits solution via separation of variables in toroidal coordinates. Making the substitution Φ Φ = U cosh ⁡ ⁡ τ τ − − cos ⁡ ⁡ σ σ {\displaystyle \Phi =U{\sqrt {\cosh \tau -\cos \sigma }}} A separable equation is then obtained. A particular solution obtained by separation of variables is: Φ Φ = cosh ⁡ ⁡ τ τ − − cos ⁡ ⁡ σ σ S ν ν ( σ σ ) T μ μ ν ν ( τ τ ) V μ μ ( ϕ ϕ ) {\displaystyle \Phi ={\sqrt {\cosh \tau -\cos \sigma }}\,\,S_{\nu }(\sigma )T_{\mu \nu }(\tau )V_{\mu }(\phi )} where each function is a linear combination of: S ν ν ( σ σ ) = e i ν ν σ σ a n d e − − i ν ν σ σ {\displaystyle S_{\nu }(\sigma )=e^{i\nu \sigma }\,\,\,\,\mathrm {and} \,\,\,\,e^{-i\nu \sigma }} T μ μ ν ν ( τ τ ) = P ν ν − − 1 / 2 μ μ ( cosh ⁡ ⁡ τ τ ) a n d Q ν ν − − 1 / 2 μ μ ( cosh ⁡ ⁡ τ τ ) {\displaystyle T_{\mu \nu }(\tau )=P_{\nu -1/2}^{\mu }(\cosh \tau )\,\,\,\,\mathrm {and} \,\,\,\,Q_{\nu -1/2}^{\mu }(\cosh \tau )} V μ μ ( ϕ ϕ ) = e i μ μ ϕ ϕ a n d e − − i μ μ ϕ ϕ {\displaystyle V_{\mu }(\phi )=e^{i\mu \phi }\,\,\,\,\mathrm {and} \,\,\,\,e^{-i\mu \phi }} Where P and Q are associated Legendre functions of the first and second kind. These Legendre functions are often referred to as toroidal harmonics.

Toroidal harmonics have many interesting properties.  If you make a variable substitution z = cosh ⁡ ⁡ τ τ > 1 {\displaystyle z=\cosh \tau >1} then, for instance, with vanishing order μ μ = 0 {\displaystyle \mu =0} (the convention is to not write the order when it vanishes) and ν ν = 0 {\displaystyle \nu =0} Q − − 1 2 ( z ) = 2 1 + z K ( 2 1 + z ) {\displaystyle Q_{-{\frac {1}{2}}}(z)={\sqrt {\frac {2}{1+z}}}K\left({\sqrt {\frac {2}{1+z}}}\right)} and P − − 1 2 ( z ) = 2 π π 2 1 + z K ( z − − 1 z + 1 ) {\displaystyle P_{-{\frac {1}{2}}}(z)={\frac {2}{\pi }}{\sqrt {\frac {2}{1+z}}}K\left({\sqrt {\frac {z-1}{z+1}}}\right)} where K {\displaystyle \,\!K} and E {\displaystyle \,\!E} are the complete elliptic integrals of the first and second kind respectively.  The rest of the toroidal harmonics can be obtained, for instance, in terms of the complete elliptic integrals, by using recurrence relations for associated Legendre functions.

The classic applications of toroidal coordinates are in solving partial differential equations , 
e.g., Laplace's equation for which toroidal coordinates allow a separation of variables or the Helmholtz equation , for which toroidal coordinates do not allow a separation of variables. Typical examples would be the electric potential and electric field of a conducting torus, or in the degenerate case, an electric current-ring (Hulme 1982).
