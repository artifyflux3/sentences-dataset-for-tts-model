Illustration of toroidal coordinates, which are obtained by rotating a two-dimensional [bipolar coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bipolar_coordinates "Bipolar coordinates") about the axis separating its two foci. The foci are located at a distance 1 from the vertical _z_-axis. The portion of the red sphere that lies above the ![Image 2: {\displaystyle xy}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c72eb345e496513fb8b2fa4aa8c4d89b855f9a01)-plane is the σ=30° isosurface, the blue torus is the τ=0.5 isosurface, and the yellow half-plane is the φ=60° isosurface. The green half-plane marks the _x_-_z_ plane, from which φ is measured. The black point is located at the intersection of the red, blue and yellow isosurfaces, at Cartesian coordinates roughly (0.996,−1.725,1.911).

**Toroidal coordinates** are a three-dimensional [orthogonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_coordinates "Orthogonal coordinates")[coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_system "Coordinate system") that results from rotating the two-dimensional [bipolar coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bipolar_coordinates "Bipolar coordinates") about the axis that separates its two foci. Thus, the two [foci](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Focus_(geometry) "Focus (geometry)")![Image 3: {\displaystyle F_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/100c7fbf174fe8b06eacc2a6b0bb2e1badd1c7ce) and ![Image 4: {\displaystyle F_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0fd17e0779153d765b40ebef91533489b87b2e37) in [bipolar coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bipolar_coordinates "Bipolar coordinates") become a ring of radius ![Image 5: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc) in the ![Image 6: {\displaystyle xy}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c72eb345e496513fb8b2fa4aa8c4d89b855f9a01) plane of the toroidal coordinate system; the ![Image 7: {\displaystyle z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf368e72c009decd9b6686ee84a375632e11de98)-axis is the axis of rotation. The focal ring is also known as the reference circle.

### Coordinate surfaces

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Toroidal_coordinates&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Coordinate surfaces")]

[![Image 23](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Apollonian_circles.svg/350px-Apollonian_circles.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Apollonian_circles.svg)

Rotating this two-dimensional [bipolar coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bipolar_coordinates "Bipolar coordinates") about the vertical axis produces the three-dimensional toroidal coordinate system above. A circle on the vertical axis becomes the red [sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere "Sphere"), whereas a circle on the horizontal axis becomes the blue [torus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torus "Torus").

Surfaces of constant ![Image 24: {\displaystyle \sigma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/59f59b7c3e6fdb1d0365a494b81fb9a696138c36) correspond to spheres of different radii

![Image 25: {\displaystyle \left(x^{2}+y^{2}\right)+\left(z-a\cot \sigma \right)^{2}={\frac {a^{2}}{\sin ^{2}\sigma }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bc2f6d6ff0860576ab6da46828cf6752047726f1)
that all pass through the focal ring but are not concentric. The surfaces of constant ![Image 26: {\displaystyle \tau }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38a7dcde9730ef0853809fefc18d88771f95206c) are non-intersecting tori of different radii

![Image 27: {\displaystyle z^{2}+\left({\sqrt {x^{2}+y^{2}}}-a\coth \tau \right)^{2}={\frac {a^{2}}{\sinh ^{2}\tau }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/532b1b939985f91d59f8846a108c50da99902b0e)
that surround the focal ring. The centers of the constant-![Image 28: {\displaystyle \sigma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/59f59b7c3e6fdb1d0365a494b81fb9a696138c36) spheres lie along the ![Image 29: {\displaystyle z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf368e72c009decd9b6686ee84a375632e11de98)-axis, whereas the constant-![Image 30: {\displaystyle \tau }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38a7dcde9730ef0853809fefc18d88771f95206c) tori are centered in the ![Image 31: {\displaystyle xy}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c72eb345e496513fb8b2fa4aa8c4d89b855f9a01) plane.

Geometric interpretation of the coordinates σ and τ of a point **P**. Observed in the plane of constant azimuthal angle ![Image 41: {\displaystyle \phi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/72b1f30316670aee6270a28334bdf4f5072cdde4), toroidal coordinates are equivalent to [bipolar coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bipolar_coordinates "Bipolar coordinates"). The angle ![Image 42: {\displaystyle \sigma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/59f59b7c3e6fdb1d0365a494b81fb9a696138c36) is formed by the two foci in this plane and **P**, whereas ![Image 43: {\displaystyle \tau }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38a7dcde9730ef0853809fefc18d88771f95206c) is the logarithm of the ratio of distances to the foci. The corresponding circles of constant ![Image 44: {\displaystyle \sigma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/59f59b7c3e6fdb1d0365a494b81fb9a696138c36) and ![Image 45: {\displaystyle \tau }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38a7dcde9730ef0853809fefc18d88771f95206c) are shown in red and blue, respectively, and meet at right angles (magenta box); they are orthogonal.

The coordinate ![Image 46: {\displaystyle \tau }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38a7dcde9730ef0853809fefc18d88771f95206c) equals the [natural logarithm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_logarithm "Natural logarithm") of the focal distances

![Image 47: {\displaystyle \tau =\ln {\frac {d_{1}}{d_{2}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/18362215eee1a1e4d0695ab357d50c66cbbd687b)
whereas ![Image 48: {\displaystyle |\sigma |}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/341d08870411734e3f7332eac96f709e6f7a3f2d) equals the angle between the rays to the foci, which may be determined from the [law of cosines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law_of_cosines "Law of cosines")

![Image 49: {\displaystyle \cos \sigma ={\frac {d_{1}^{2}+d_{2}^{2}-4a^{2}}{2d_{1}d_{2}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c09b5090ed15432b867e036867ef0c570cb852)
Or explicitly, including the sign,

![Image 50: {\displaystyle \sigma =\mathrm {sign} (z)\arccos {\frac {r^{2}-a^{2}}{\sqrt {(r^{2}-a^{2})^{2}+4a^{2}z^{2}}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38e566ea12b98a4f9b836ea1149473fd8e2842cb)

### Standard separation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Toroidal_coordinates&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Standard separation")]

The 3-variable [Laplace equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laplace_equation "Laplace equation")

and

![Image 75: {\displaystyle P_{-{\frac {1}{2}}}(z)={\frac {2}{\pi }}{\sqrt {\frac {2}{1+z}}}K\left({\sqrt {\frac {z-1}{z+1}}}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffea0069d1a86e86a37d356d43a6f0370aab747a)
where ![Image 76: {\displaystyle \,\!K}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4464e71dc4c00812265606d57bec801fa1eb8afd) and ![Image 77: {\displaystyle \,\!E}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ee426805a88a8bedd6bcb057ddfca82de92c1892) are the complete [elliptic integrals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptic_integrals "Elliptic integrals") of the [first](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptic_integral#Complete_elliptic_integral_of_the_first_kind "Elliptic integral") and [second](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptic_integral#Complete_elliptic_integral_of_the_second_kind "Elliptic integral") kind respectively. The rest of the toroidal harmonics can be obtained, for instance, in terms of the complete elliptic integrals, by using recurrence relations for associated Legendre functions.

The classic applications of toroidal coordinates are in solving [partial differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_differential_equations "Partial differential equations"), e.g., [Laplace's equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laplace%27s_equation "Laplace's equation") for which toroidal coordinates allow a [separation of variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separation_of_variables "Separation of variables") or the [Helmholtz equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helmholtz_equation "Helmholtz equation"), for which toroidal coordinates do not allow a separation of variables. Typical examples would be the [electric potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_potential "Electric potential") and [electric field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_field "Electric field") of a conducting torus, or in the degenerate case, an electric current-ring (Hulme 1982).

where each function is a linear combination of:

![Image 81: {\displaystyle S_{\nu }(\sigma )=e^{i\nu \sigma }\,\,\,\,\mathrm {and} \,\,\,\,e^{-i\nu \sigma }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c4c206ca78123bda6aac5f99b698d4110ec196ae)![Image 82: {\displaystyle T_{\mu \nu }(\tau )=P_{\mu -1/2}^{\nu }(\coth \tau )\,\,\,\,\mathrm {and} \,\,\,\,Q_{\mu -1/2}^{\nu }(\coth \tau )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fc29f998763b529c041b58dd5d0462f661c227cb)![Image 83: {\displaystyle V_{\mu }(\phi )=e^{i\mu \phi }\,\,\,\,\mathrm {and} \,\,\,\,e^{-i\mu \phi }.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8bd4f065019a3d13dc79b3482a49d5258df67857)
Note that although the toroidal harmonics are used again for the _T_ function, the argument is ![Image 84: {\displaystyle \coth \tau }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/662f99430d7e4ae8322acfe3f8d1516c78a05b2e) rather than ![Image 85: {\displaystyle \cosh \tau }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f1f2e943244cda72bea7acfe905bde3d283ec322) and the ![Image 86: {\displaystyle \mu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9fd47b2a39f7a7856952afec1f1db72c67af6161) and ![Image 87: {\displaystyle \nu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c15bbbb971240cf328aba572178f091684585468) indices are exchanged. This method is useful for situations in which the boundary conditions are independent of the spherical angle ![Image 88: {\displaystyle \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e5ab2664b422d53eb0c7df3b87e1360d75ad9af), such as the charged ring, an infinite half plane, or two parallel planes. For identities relating the toroidal harmonics with argument hyperbolic cosine with those of argument hyperbolic cotangent, see the [Whipple formulae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whipple_formulae "Whipple formulae").
