In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), a **unit circle** is a [circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle "Circle") of unit [radius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radius "Radius")—that is, a radius of 1.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_circle#cite_note-1) Frequently, especially in [trigonometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trigonometry "Trigonometry"), the unit circle is the circle of radius 1 centered at the origin (0, 0) in the [Cartesian coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system "Cartesian coordinate system") in the [Euclidean plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_plane "Euclidean plane"). In [topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology "Topology"), it is often denoted as _S_ 1 because it is a one-dimensional unit [_n_-sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/N-sphere "N-sphere").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_circle#cite_note-Unit_sphere-2)[[note 1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_circle#cite_note-3)

If (_x_, _y_) is a point on the unit circle's [circumference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumference "Circumference"), then |_x_| and |_y_| are the lengths of the legs of a [right triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_triangle "Right triangle") whose hypotenuse has length 1. Thus, by the [Pythagorean theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagorean_theorem "Pythagorean theorem"), _x_ and _y_ satisfy the equation ![Image 3: {\displaystyle x^{2}+y^{2}=1.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bd4ba9a57bd82841e5889575e2ff3e1ef5fad8e9)

Since _x_ 2 = (−_x_)2 for all _x_, and since the reflection of any point on the unit circle about the _x_- or _y_-axis is also on the unit circle, the above equation holds for all points (_x_, _y_) on the unit circle, not only those in the first quadrant.

The interior of the unit circle is called the open [unit disk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_disk "Unit disk"), while the interior of the unit circle combined with the unit circle itself is called the closed unit disk.

One may also use other notions of "distance" to define other "unit circles", such as the [Riemannian circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_circle "Riemannian circle"); see the article on [mathematical norms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norm_(mathematics) "Norm (mathematics)") for additional examples.

In the complex plane
--------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Unit_circle&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: In the complex plane")]

[![Image 4](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Unitycircle-complex.gif/250px-Unitycircle-complex.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Unitycircle-complex.gif)

Animation of the unit circle with angles

In the [complex plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_plane "Complex plane"), numbers of unit magnitude are called the [unit complex numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_complex_numbers "Unit complex numbers"). This is the set of [complex numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number")z such that ![Image 5: {\displaystyle |z|=1.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/abfbcdd6b1afc76fb8a016f500ba46153841a4c8) When broken into real and imaginary components ![Image 6: {\displaystyle z=x+iy,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8ee21a775fda65273b6e1a6786b7f3d51a85de65) this condition is ![Image 7: {\displaystyle |z|^{2}=z{\bar {z}}=x^{2}+y^{2}=1.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b3974daa6bb5176c5e3b709ccd0a0946872d9da0)

The complex unit circle can be parametrized by angle measure ![Image 8: {\displaystyle \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e5ab2664b422d53eb0c7df3b87e1360d75ad9af) from the positive real axis using the complex [exponential function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponential_function "Exponential function"), ![Image 9: {\displaystyle z=e^{i\theta }=\cos \theta +i\sin \theta .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d4d0745efb7e728dc6e5d566e49bc5c2894a2422) (See [Euler's formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler%27s_formula "Euler's formula").)

Under the complex multiplication operation, the unit complex numbers form a [group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_(mathematics) "Group (mathematics)") called the _[circle group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle\_group "Circle group")_, usually denoted ![Image 10: {\displaystyle \mathbb {T} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/23bfcd3d5e94ad3ec191037d3e330ea77943d9cc) In [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics"), a unit complex number is called a [phase factor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phase_factor "Phase factor").

Trigonometric functions on the unit circle
------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Unit_circle&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Trigonometric functions on the unit circle")]

[![Image 11](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Unit-circle_sin_cos_tan_cot_exsec_excsc_versin_vercos_coversin_covercos.svg/500px-Unit-circle_sin_cos_tan_cot_exsec_excsc_versin_vercos_coversin_covercos.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Unit-circle_sin_cos_tan_cot_exsec_excsc_versin_vercos_coversin_covercos.svg)

All of the trigonometric functions of the angle _θ_ (theta) can be constructed geometrically in terms of a unit circle centered at _O_.

[![Image 12](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Periodic_sine.svg/250px-Periodic_sine.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Periodic_sine.svg)

Sine function on unit circle (top) and its graph (bottom)

The [trigonometric functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trigonometric_functions "Trigonometric functions") cosine and sine of angle _θ_ may be defined on the unit circle as follows: If (_x_, _y_) is a point on the unit circle, and if the ray from the origin (0, 0) to (_x_, _y_) makes an [angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle "Angle")_θ_ from the positive _x_-axis, (where counterclockwise turning is positive), then ![Image 13: {\displaystyle \cos \theta =x\quad {\text{and}}\quad \sin \theta =y.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/94f54d9af620968ed7a309f243df9a718ca6243d)

The equation _x_ 2 + _y_ 2 = 1 gives the relation ![Image 14: {\displaystyle \cos ^{2}\theta +\sin ^{2}\theta =1.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5e79d40676a67b3463099809cd6bd9e046d5781f)

The unit circle also demonstrates that [sine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sine "Sine") and [cosine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosine "Cosine") are [periodic functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Periodic_function "Periodic function"), with the identities ![Image 15: {\displaystyle \cos \theta =\cos(2\pi k+\theta )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/65c6c880d7c4ed64cc3db288af123fec3f026e79)![Image 16: {\displaystyle \sin \theta =\sin(2\pi k+\theta )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/42ba79dfa8eb6f67f6fe249e49f13f1a59da8460) for any [integer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer")_k_.

Triangles constructed on the unit circle can also be used to illustrate the periodicity of the trigonometric functions. First, construct a radius OP from the origin O to a point P(_x_ 1,_y_ 1) on the unit circle such that an angle _t_ with 0 <_t_<⁠π/2⁠ is formed with the positive arm of the _x_-axis. Now consider a point Q(_x_ 1,0) and line segments PQ ⊥ OQ. The result is a right triangle △OPQ with ∠QOP = _t_. Because PQ has length _y_ 1, OQ length _x_ 1, and OP has length 1 as a radius on the unit circle, sin(_t_) = _y_ 1 and cos(_t_) = _x_ 1. Having established these equivalences, take another radius OR from the origin to a point R(−_x_ 1,_y_ 1) on the circle such that the same angle _t_ is formed with the negative arm of the _x_-axis. Now consider a point S(−_x_ 1,0) and line segments RS ⊥ OS. The result is a right triangle △ORS with ∠SOR = _t_. It can hence be seen that, because ∠ROQ = π − _t_, R is at (cos(π − _t_), sin(π − _t_)) in the same way that P is at (cos(_t_), sin(_t_)). The conclusion is that, since (−_x_ 1, _y_ 1) is the same as (cos(π − _t_), sin(π − _t_)) and (_x_ 1,_y_ 1) is the same as (cos(_t_),sin(_t_)), it is true that sin(_t_) = sin(π − _t_) and −cos(_t_) = cos(π − _t_). It may be inferred in a similar manner that tan(π − _t_) = −tan(_t_), since tan(_t_) = ⁠_y_ 1/_x_ 1⁠ and tan(π − _t_) = ⁠_y_ 1/−_x_ 1⁠. A simple demonstration of the above can be seen in the equality sin(⁠π/4⁠) = sin(⁠3π/4⁠) = ⁠1/√2⁠.

When working with right triangles, sine, cosine, and other trigonometric functions only make sense for angle measures more than zero and less than ⁠π/2⁠. However, when defined with the unit circle, these functions produce meaningful values for any [real](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number")-valued angle measure– even those greater than 2 π. In fact, all six standard trigonometric functions– sine, cosine, tangent, cotangent, secant, and cosecant, as well as archaic functions like [versine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Versine "Versine") and [exsecant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exsecant "Exsecant")– can be defined geometrically in terms of a unit circle, as shown at right.

Using the unit circle, the values of any trigonometric function for many angles other than those labeled can be easily calculated by hand using the [angle sum and difference formulas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trigonometric_identity#Angle_sum_and_difference_identities "Trigonometric identity").

Unit circle in complex dynamics

The [Julia set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Julia_set "Julia set") of [discrete nonlinear dynamical system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynamical_system_(definition) "Dynamical system (definition)") with [evolution function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evolution_function "Evolution function"): ![Image 19: {\displaystyle f_{0}(x)=x^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8a1e3fcab1bf54980595879e55fc3cbbba91952c) is a unit circle. It is a simplest case so it is widely used in the study of dynamical systems.
