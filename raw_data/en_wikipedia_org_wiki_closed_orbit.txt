In [astrodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrodynamics "Astrodynamics") or [celestial mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celestial_mechanics "Celestial mechanics"), an **elliptical orbit** or **eccentric orbit** is an [orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbit "Orbit") with an [eccentricity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity "Orbital eccentricity") of less than 1; this includes the special case of a [circular orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circular_orbit "Circular orbit"), with eccentricity equal to 0. Some orbits have been referred to as "elongated orbits" if the eccentricity is "high" but that is not an explanatory term. For the simple two body problem, all orbits are ellipses.

In a [gravitational two-body problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_two-body_problem "Gravitational two-body problem"), both bodies follow [similar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Similarity_(geometry) "Similarity (geometry)") elliptical orbits with the same [orbital period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_period "Orbital period") around their common [barycenter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barycenter "Barycenter"). The relative position of one body with respect to the other also follows an elliptic orbit.

Examples of elliptic orbits include [Hohmann transfer orbits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hohmann_transfer_orbit "Hohmann transfer orbit"), [Molniya orbits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molniya_orbit "Molniya orbit"), and [tundra orbits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tundra_orbit "Tundra orbit").

### From initial position and velocity

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Elliptic_orbit&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: From initial position and velocity")]

An [orbit equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbit_equation "Orbit equation") defines the path of an [orbiting body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbiting_body "Orbiting body")![Image 35: {\displaystyle m_{2}\,\!}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f380333b3c97879c3dc19d347ed5065a468d0858) around [central body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_body "Central body")![Image 36: {\displaystyle m_{1}\,\!}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/962961d14d550e1862cdfba5eee247e90eccfab9) relative to ![Image 37: {\displaystyle m_{1}\,\!}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/962961d14d550e1862cdfba5eee247e90eccfab9), without specifying position as a function of time. If the eccentricity is less than 1 then the equation of motion describes an elliptical orbit. Because [Kepler's equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler%27s_equation "Kepler's equation")![Image 38: {\displaystyle M=E-e\sin E}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/92f49670f292ad45dd2311f04742ed4c790a385a) has no general [closed-form solution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed-form_solution "Closed-form solution") for the [Eccentric anomaly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eccentric_anomaly "Eccentric anomaly") (E) [in terms of the Mean anomaly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler%27s_equation#Inverse_problem "Kepler's equation") (M), equations of motion as a function of time also have no closed-form solution (although [numerical solutions exist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mean_anomaly#Formulae "Mean anomaly") for both).

The state of an orbiting body at any given time is defined by the orbiting body's position and velocity with respect to the central body, which can be represented by the three-dimensional [Cartesian coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinates "Cartesian coordinates") (position of the orbiting body represented by x, y, and z) and the similar Cartesian components of the orbiting body's velocity. This set of six variables, together with time, are called the [orbital state vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_state_vectors "Orbital state vectors"). Given the masses of the two bodies they determine the full orbit. The two most general cases with these 6 degrees of freedom are the elliptic and the hyperbolic orbit. Special cases with fewer degrees of freedom are the circular and parabolic orbit.

Because at least six variables are absolutely required to completely represent an elliptic orbit with this set of parameters, then six variables are required to represent an orbit with any set of parameters. Another set of six parameters that are commonly used are the [orbital elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_elements "Orbital elements").

In the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System"), [planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planet "Planet"), [asteroids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asteroid "Asteroid"), most [comets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comet "Comet"), and some pieces of [space debris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_debris "Space debris") have approximately elliptical orbits around the Sun. Strictly speaking, both bodies revolve around the same focus of the ellipse, the one closer to the more massive body, but when one body is significantly more massive, such as the sun in relation to the earth, the focus may be contained within the larger massing body, and thus the smaller is said to revolve around it. The following chart of the [perihelion and aphelion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apsis "Apsis") of the [planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planet "Planet"), [dwarf planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dwarf_planet "Dwarf planet"), and [Halley's Comet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halley%27s_Comet "Halley's Comet") demonstrates the variation of the eccentricity of their elliptical orbits. For similar distances from the sun, wider bars denote greater eccentricity. Note the almost-zero eccentricity of Earth and Venus compared to the enormous eccentricity of Halley's Comet and [Eris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eris_(dwarf_planet) "Eris (dwarf planet)").

Radial elliptic trajectory
--------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Elliptic_orbit&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Radial elliptic trajectory")]

A [radial trajectory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radial_trajectory "Radial trajectory") can be a [double line segment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_segment#As_a_degenerate_ellipse "Line segment"), which is a [degenerate ellipse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degenerate_conic "Degenerate conic") with semi-minor axis = 0 and eccentricity = 1. Although the eccentricity is 1, this is not a parabolic orbit. Most properties and formulas of elliptic orbits apply. However, the orbit cannot be closed. It is an open orbit corresponding to the part of the degenerate ellipse from the moment the bodies touch each other and move away from each other until they touch each other again. In the case of point masses one full orbit is possible, starting and ending with a singularity. The velocities at the start and end are infinite in opposite directions and the potential energy is equal to minus infinity.

The radial elliptic trajectory is the solution of a two-body problem with at some instant zero speed, as in the case of [dropping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Falling_(physics) "Falling (physics)") an object (neglecting air resistance).

The [Babylonians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Babylonian_astronomy "Babylonian astronomy") were the first to realize that the Sun's motion along the [ecliptic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecliptic "Ecliptic") was not uniform, though they were unaware of why this was; it is today known that this is due to the Earth moving in an elliptic orbit around the Sun, with the Earth moving faster when it is nearer to the Sun at [perihelion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perihelion "Perihelion") and moving slower when it is farther away at [aphelion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aphelion "Aphelion").[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_orbit#cite_note-8)

In the 17th century, [Johannes Kepler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johannes_Kepler "Johannes Kepler") discovered that the orbits along which the planets travel around the Sun are ellipses with the Sun at one focus, and described this in his [first law of planetary motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler%27s_laws_of_planetary_motion "Kepler's laws of planetary motion"). Later, [Isaac Newton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaac_Newton "Isaac Newton") explained this as a corollary of his [law of universal gravitation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_law_of_universal_gravitation "Newton's law of universal gravitation").
