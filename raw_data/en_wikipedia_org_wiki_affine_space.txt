In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), an **affine space** is a [geometric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry")[structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Structure_(mathematics) "Structure (mathematics)") that generalizes some of the properties of [Euclidean spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space") in such a way that these are independent of the concepts of [distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distance_(mathematics) "Distance (mathematics)") and measure of [angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle "Angle"), keeping only the properties related to [parallelism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_(geometry) "Parallel (geometry)") and [ratio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ratio "Ratio") of lengths for parallel [line segments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_segment "Line segment"). Affine space is the setting for [affine geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_geometry "Affine geometry").

As in Euclidean space, the fundamental objects in an affine space are called _[points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point\_(geometry) "Point (geometry)")_, which can be thought of as locations in the space without any size or shape: zero-[dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension "Dimension"). Through any pair of points an infinite [straight line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Straight_line "Straight line") can be drawn, a one-dimensional set of points; through any three points that are not collinear, a two-dimensional [plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_(geometry) "Plane (geometry)") can be drawn; and, in general, through _k_ + 1 points in general position, a k-dimensional [flat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flat_(geometry) "Flat (geometry)") or affine subspace can be drawn. Affine space is characterized by a notion of pairs of parallel lines that lie within the same plane but never meet each-other (non-parallel lines within the same plane [intersect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intersection_(geometry) "Intersection (geometry)") in a point). Given any line, a line parallel to it can be drawn through any point in the space, and the [equivalence class](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equivalence_class "Equivalence class") of parallel lines are said to share a _direction_.

Unlike for vectors in a [vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space"), in an affine space there is no distinguished point that serves as an [origin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Origin_(mathematics) "Origin (mathematics)"). There is no predefined concept of adding or multiplying points together, or multiplying a point by a scalar number. However, for any affine space, an associated vector space can be constructed from the differences between start and end points, which are called _[free vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free\_vector "Free vector")_, _[displacement vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Displacement\_vector "Displacement vector")_, _[translation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Translation\_(geometry) "Translation (geometry)") vectors_ or simply _translations_.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_space#cite_note-1) Likewise, it makes sense to add a displacement vector to a point of an affine space, resulting in a new point translated from the starting point by that vector. While points cannot be arbitrarily added together, it is meaningful to take [affine combinations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_combination "Affine combination") of points: weighted sums with numerical coefficients summing to 1, resulting in another point. These coefficients define a [barycentric coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barycentric_coordinate_system "Barycentric coordinate system") for the flat through the points.

Any [vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space") may be viewed as an affine space; this amounts to "forgetting" the special role played by the [zero vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero_vector "Zero vector"). In this case, elements of the vector space may be viewed either as _points_ of the affine space or as _displacement vectors_ or _translations_. When considered as a point, the zero vector is called the _origin_. Adding a fixed vector to the elements of a [linear subspace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_subspace "Linear subspace") (vector subspace) of a [vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space") produces an _affine subspace_ of the vector space. One commonly says that this affine subspace has been obtained by translating (away from the origin) the linear subspace by the translation vector (the vector added to all the elements of the linear space). In finite dimensions, such an _affine subspace_ is the solution set of an [inhomogeneous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/System_of_linear_equations#Homogeneous_systems "System of linear equations") linear system. The displacement vectors for that affine space are the solutions of the corresponding _homogeneous_ linear system, which is a linear subspace. Linear subspaces, in contrast, always contain the origin of the vector space.

The _dimension_ of an affine space is defined as the [dimension of the vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension_(vector_space) "Dimension (vector space)") of its translations. An affine space of dimension one is an **affine line**. An affine space of dimension 2 is an _[affine plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine\_plane "Affine plane")_. An affine subspace of dimension _n_ – 1 in an affine space or a vector space of dimension _n_ is an [affine hyperplane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_hyperplane "Affine hyperplane").

Informal description
--------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Affine_space&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Informal description")]

[![Image 12](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Affine_origin.png/330px-Affine_origin.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Affine_origin.png)

Origins from Alice's and Bob's perspectives. Vector computation from Alice's perspective is in red, whereas that from Bob's is in blue.

The following [characterization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Characterization_(mathematics) "Characterization (mathematics)") may be easier to understand than the usual formal definition: an affine space is what is left of a [vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space") after one has forgotten which point is the origin (or, in the words of the French mathematician [Marcel Berger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marcel_Berger "Marcel Berger"), "An affine space is nothing more than a vector space whose origin we try to forget about, by adding [translations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Translation_(geometry) "Translation (geometry)") to the linear maps"[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_space#cite_note-2)). Imagine that Alice knows that a certain point is the actual origin, but Bob believes that another point—call it **p**—is the origin. Two vectors, **a** and **b**, are to be added. Bob draws an arrow from point **p** to point **a** and another arrow from point **p** to point **b**, and completes the parallelogram to find what Bob thinks is **a** + **b**, but Alice knows that he has actually computed

**p** + (**a** − **p**) + (**b** − **p**).
Similarly, [Alice and Bob](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alice_and_Bob "Alice and Bob") may evaluate any [linear combination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_combination "Linear combination") of **a** and **b**, or of any finite set of vectors, and will generally get different answers. However, if the sum of the coefficients in a linear combination is 1, then Alice and Bob will arrive at the same answer.

If Alice travels to

λ**a** + (1 − _λ_)**b**
then Bob can similarly travel to

**p** + _λ_(**a** − **p**) + (1 − _λ_)(**b** − **p**) = λ**a** + (1 − _λ_)**b**.
Under this condition, for all coefficients _λ_ + (1 − _λ_) = 1, Alice and Bob describe the same point with the same linear combination, despite using different origins.

While only Alice knows the "linear structure", both Alice and Bob know the "affine structure"—i.e. the values of [affine combinations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_combination "Affine combination"), defined as linear combinations in which the sum of the coefficients is 1. A set with an affine structure is an affine space.

While affine space can be defined axiomatically (see [§Axioms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_space#Axioms) below), analogously to the definition of Euclidean space implied by [Euclid's _Elements_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid%27s_Elements "Euclid's Elements"), for convenience most modern sources define affine spaces in terms of the well developed vector space theory.

An _affine space_ is a set _A_ together with a [vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space")![Image 13: {\displaystyle {\overrightarrow {A}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/79a63d1d5ad20924a9e908d0f805f2b16e745dcd), and a transitive and free [action](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_action_(mathematics) "Group action (mathematics)") of the [additive group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Additive_group "Additive group") of ![Image 14: {\displaystyle {\overrightarrow {A}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/79a63d1d5ad20924a9e908d0f805f2b16e745dcd) on the set _A_.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_space#cite_note-3) The elements of the affine space _A_ are called _points_. The vector space ![Image 15: {\displaystyle {\overrightarrow {A}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/79a63d1d5ad20924a9e908d0f805f2b16e745dcd) is said to be _associated_ to the affine space, and its elements are called _vectors_, _translations_, or sometimes _[free vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free\_vector "Free vector")_.

Explicitly, the definition above means that the action is a mapping, generally denoted as an addition,

![Image 16: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}A\times {\overrightarrow {A}}&\to A\\(a,v)\;&\mapsto a+v,\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/02f8aed9158a31e5ca4c74e142bbf8911d4bbd6a)
that has the following properties.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_space#cite_note-Berger1987-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_space#cite_note-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_space#cite_note-6)

1.   [Right identity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_identity "Right identity"): ![Image 17: {\displaystyle \forall a\in A,\;a+0=a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8fb70fd09bcf3008f5296884b6788d7f61f49b2f), where 0 is the zero vector in ![Image 18: {\displaystyle {\overrightarrow {A}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/79a63d1d5ad20924a9e908d0f805f2b16e745dcd)
2.   [Associativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Associativity "Associativity"): ![Image 19: {\displaystyle \forall v,w\in {\overrightarrow {A}},\forall a\in A,\;(a+v)+w=a+(v+w)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ea1f3c8ae46c9de65576544b3054b22ae5033b6d) (here the last + is the addition in ![Image 20: {\displaystyle {\overrightarrow {A}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/79a63d1d5ad20924a9e908d0f805f2b16e745dcd))
3.   [Free](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_action "Free action") and [transitive action](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transitive_action "Transitive action"): For every ![Image 21: {\displaystyle a\in A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a97387981adb5d65f74518e20b6785a284d7abd5), the mapping ![Image 22: {\displaystyle {\overrightarrow {A}}\to A\colon v\mapsto a+v}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/263fcca7383f4d2b57a93470c989f4f6a4aaf5c7) is a [bijection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bijection "Bijection").

The first two properties are simply defining properties of a (right) group action. The third property characterizes free and transitive actions, the [onto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Onto "Onto") character coming from transitivity, and then the [injective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Injective_function "Injective function") character follows from the action being free. There is a fourth property that follows from 1, 2 above:

2.   Existence of one-to-one [translations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Translation_(geometry) "Translation (geometry)")
For all ![Image 23: {\displaystyle v\in {\overrightarrow {A}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e55de186a95de885889df10b81e1d347aa1058f8), the mapping ![Image 24: {\displaystyle A\to A\colon a\mapsto a+v}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5de5b5b16c1a28c1b35e443cdd58be9904302d54) is a bijection.

Property 3 is often used in the following equivalent form (the 5th property).

2.   Subtraction:
For every _a_, _b_ in A, there exists a unique ![Image 25: {\displaystyle v\in {\overrightarrow {A}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e55de186a95de885889df10b81e1d347aa1058f8), denoted _b_ – _a_, such that ![Image 26: {\displaystyle b=a+v}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b0100ae48c18e183bef56cb0607d005c0f5b820b).

Another way to express the definition is that an affine space is a [principal homogeneous space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principal_homogeneous_space "Principal homogeneous space") for the action of the [additive group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Additive_group "Additive group") of a vector space. Homogeneous spaces are, by definition, endowed with a transitive group action, and for a principal homogeneous space, such a transitive action is, by definition, free.

### Subtraction and Weyl's axioms

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Affine_space&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Subtraction and Weyl's axioms")]

The properties of the group action allows for the definition of subtraction for any given ordered pair (_b_, _a_) of points in A, producing a vector of ![Image 27: {\displaystyle {\overrightarrow {A}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/79a63d1d5ad20924a9e908d0f805f2b16e745dcd). This vector, denoted ![Image 28: {\displaystyle b-a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ecca61f9c918fe1deb227ed79d4979d70c443ea4) or ![Image 29: {\displaystyle {\overrightarrow {ab}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6d9f5228fc1782efa416fdce3110c0200ef7bcc3), is defined to be the unique vector in ![Image 30: {\displaystyle {\overrightarrow {A}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/79a63d1d5ad20924a9e908d0f805f2b16e745dcd) such that

![Image 31: {\displaystyle a+(b-a)=b.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7d9b6fe8a03360c586b36ebff369555e40822d90)
Existence follows from the transitivity of the action, and uniqueness follows because the action is free.

This subtraction has the two following properties, called [Weyl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Weyl "Hermann Weyl")'s axioms:[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_space#cite_note-7)

1.   ![Image 32: {\displaystyle \forall a\in A,\;\forall v\in {\overrightarrow {A}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/85a01a67376d7f7bfcde79ff7deb526a17ca11db), there is a unique point ![Image 33: {\displaystyle b\in A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/26329067b4b3630a2bb6f7e6b3ee367b70918371) such that ![Image 34: {\displaystyle b-a=v.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a24264b1a0a5d4c764f65b84ed6549d5628b0e9e)
2.   ![Image 35: {\displaystyle \forall a,b,c\in A,\;(c-b)+(b-a)=c-a.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4ad4d9df1277d289d27d3a1a762b62deec9fcb1)

The [parallelogram property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equipollence_(geometry)#Parallelogram_property "Equipollence (geometry)") is satisfied in affine spaces, where it is expressed as: given four points ![Image 36: {\displaystyle a,b,c,d,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ec57da23813418a4c16caa6046dc2bf2f47806f3) the equalities ![Image 37: {\displaystyle b-a=d-c}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4a8ee54905a3968f6835ea87dc1d2f4463774f16) and ![Image 38: {\displaystyle c-a=d-b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a8eb4e4074fe07023e2c8457b80a823600577657) are equivalent. This results from the second Weyl's axiom, since ![Image 39: {\displaystyle d-a=(d-b)+(b-a)=(d-c)+(c-a).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/80277e7579caa6ad091db837c4bed15673819f89)

Affine spaces can be equivalently defined as a point set _A_, together with a vector space ![Image 40: {\displaystyle {\overrightarrow {A}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/79a63d1d5ad20924a9e908d0f805f2b16e745dcd), and a subtraction satisfying Weyl's axioms. In this case, the addition of a vector to a point is defined from the first of Weyl's axioms.

Affine subspaces and parallelism
--------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Affine_space&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Affine subspaces and parallelism")]

An **affine subspace** (also called, in some contexts, a _linear variety_, a [_flat_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flat_(geometry) "Flat (geometry)"), or, over the [real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number"), a _linear manifold_) _B_ of an affine space _A_ is a [subset](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subset "Subset") of _A_ such that, given a point ⁠![Image 41: {\displaystyle a\in B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/739c140e9ae7f2a90119c5b40507d1ad2bc3396b)⁠, the set of vectors ![Image 42: {\displaystyle {\overrightarrow {B}}=\{b-a\mid b\in B\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1fbc5b3170b8fe3e0ef2081944e07481aada02cc) is a [linear subspace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_subspace "Linear subspace") of ⁠![Image 43: {\displaystyle {\overrightarrow {A}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/79a63d1d5ad20924a9e908d0f805f2b16e745dcd)⁠. This property, which does not depend on the choice of _a_, implies that _B_ is an affine space, which has ![Image 44: {\displaystyle {\overrightarrow {B}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/31fb3faaa4a9e6eb6abcef4a41c4bbeb84cc3c90) as its associated vector space.

The affine subspaces of _A_ are the subsets of _A_ of the form

![Image 45: {\displaystyle a+V=\{a+w:w\in V\},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0e19c7ed4cbcabb6fe6b34c19717dae4ad0efadc)
where _a_ is a point of _A_, and _V_ a linear subspace of ⁠![Image 46: {\displaystyle {\overrightarrow {A}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/79a63d1d5ad20924a9e908d0f805f2b16e745dcd)⁠.

The linear subspace associated with an affine subspace is often called its _direction_, and two subspaces that share the same direction are said to be _parallel_.

This implies the following generalization of [Playfair's axiom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Playfair%27s_axiom "Playfair's axiom"): Given a direction _V_, for any point _a_ of _A_ there is one and only one affine subspace of direction _V_, which passes through _a_, namely the subspace _a_ + _V_.

Every translation ![Image 47: {\displaystyle A\to A:a\mapsto a+v}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a75490bd2c00651d0ad38f687eb5c3378382b8ed) maps any affine subspace to a parallel subspace.

The term _parallel_ is also used for two affine subspaces such that the direction of one is included in the direction of the other.

Given two affine spaces _A_ and _B_ whose associated vector spaces are ![Image 48: {\displaystyle {\overrightarrow {A}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/79a63d1d5ad20924a9e908d0f805f2b16e745dcd) and ⁠![Image 49: {\displaystyle {\overrightarrow {B}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/31fb3faaa4a9e6eb6abcef4a41c4bbeb84cc3c90)⁠, an _[affine map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine\_map "Affine map")_ or _affine homomorphism_ from _A_ to _B_ is a map

![Image 50: {\displaystyle f:A\to B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/20040a52d9391f2fe271f0aaa300bf7887a0c7b1)
such that

![Image 51: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}{\overrightarrow {f}}:{\overrightarrow {A}}&\to {\overrightarrow {B}}\\b-a&\mapsto f(b)-f(a)\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f7bc34f1be10b1d537bfb94c48f7aa7c6187eb69)
is a [well defined](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Well_defined "Well defined") linear map. By ![Image 52: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) being well defined is meant that _b_ – _a_ = _d_ – _c_ implies _f_(_b_) – _f_(_a_) = _f_(_d_) – _f_(_c_).

This implies that, for a point ![Image 53: {\displaystyle a\in A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a97387981adb5d65f74518e20b6785a284d7abd5) and a vector ![Image 54: {\displaystyle v\in {\overrightarrow {A}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e55de186a95de885889df10b81e1d347aa1058f8), one has

![Image 55: {\displaystyle f(a+v)=f(a)+{\overrightarrow {f}}(v).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/629be545e80a2dd87495904921cbb96fe9436de6)
Therefore, since for any given b in A, _b_ = _a_ + _v_ for a unique v, _f_ is completely defined by its value on a single point and the associated linear map ⁠![Image 56: {\displaystyle {\overrightarrow {f}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cfa88ab873934603aafa809d533b4c49fc16adda)⁠.

An _affine transformation_ or _[endomorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endomorphism "Endomorphism")_ of an affine space ![Image 57: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3) is an affine map from that space to itself. One important [family](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Family_of_sets "Family of sets") of examples is the translations: given a vector ⁠![Image 58: {\displaystyle {\overrightarrow {v}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cdbb1a68c861cbd0cfda4f71510f67eed27c7cb1)⁠, the translation map ![Image 59: {\displaystyle T_{\overrightarrow {v}}:A\rightarrow A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3ebeca4fbe60527653f977b904f92b489d43e3aa) that sends ![Image 60: {\displaystyle a\mapsto a+{\overrightarrow {v}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8c26f8b6adca71bbe522d3f9eb81e16ed0ee33b5) for every ![Image 61: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc) in ![Image 62: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3) is an affine map. Another important family of examples are the linear maps centred at an origin: given a point ![Image 63: {\displaystyle b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f11423fbb2e967f986e36804a8ae4271734917c3) and a linear map ![Image 64: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd), one may define an affine map ![Image 65: {\displaystyle L_{M,b}:A\rightarrow A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ad196ea8e4e01e4016de27e2a97775f1dc820ab5) by ![Image 66: {\displaystyle L_{M,b}(a)=b+M(a-b)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a729b56b1e43dfe0281b42b5fc4644f671a87948) for every ![Image 67: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc) in ⁠![Image 68: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3)⁠.

After making a choice of origin ⁠![Image 69: {\displaystyle b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f11423fbb2e967f986e36804a8ae4271734917c3)⁠, any affine map may be written uniquely as a combination of a translation and a linear map centred at ⁠![Image 70: {\displaystyle b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f11423fbb2e967f986e36804a8ae4271734917c3)⁠.

Vector spaces as affine spaces
------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Affine_space&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Vector spaces as affine spaces")]

Every vector space _V_ may be considered as an affine space over itself. This means that every element of _V_ may be considered either as a point or as a vector. This affine space is sometimes denoted (_V_, _V_) for emphasizing the double role of the elements of _V_. When considered as a point, the [zero vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero_vector "Zero vector") is commonly denoted _o_ (or _O_, when upper-case letters are used for points) and called the _origin_.

If _A_ is another affine space over the same vector space (that is ![Image 71: {\displaystyle V={\overrightarrow {A}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a59e4ac6e1149d31ede7eaf0a7ec295f0f6480bd)) the choice of any point _a_ in _A_ defines a unique affine isomorphism, which is the identity of _V_ and maps _a_ to _o_. In other words, the choice of an origin _a_ in _A_ allows us to identify _A_ and (_V_, _V_)[up to](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Up_to "Up to") a [canonical isomorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canonical_isomorphism "Canonical isomorphism"). The counterpart of this property is that the affine space _A_ may be identified with the vector space _V_ in which "the place of the origin has been forgotten".

Relation to Euclidean spaces
----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Affine_space&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Relation to Euclidean spaces")]

### Definition of Euclidean spaces

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Affine_space&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Definition of Euclidean spaces")]

[Euclidean spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_spaces "Euclidean spaces") (including the one-dimensional line, two-dimensional plane, and three-dimensional space commonly studied in elementary geometry, as well as higher-dimensional analogues) are affine spaces.

Indeed, in most modern definitions, a Euclidean space is defined to be an affine space, such that the associated vector space is a real [inner product space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inner_product_space "Inner product space") of finite dimension, that is a vector space over the reals with a [positive-definite quadratic form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positive-definite_quadratic_form "Positive-definite quadratic form")_q_(_x_). The inner product of two vectors x and y is the value of the [symmetric bilinear form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetric_bilinear_form "Symmetric bilinear form")

![Image 72: {\displaystyle x\cdot y={\frac {1}{2}}(q(x+y)-q(x)-q(y)).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/baa3faeb3da613de6bd56dde869bae11ec7c831f)
The usual [Euclidean distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_distance "Euclidean distance") between two points A and B is

![Image 73: {\displaystyle d(A,B)={\sqrt {q(B-A)}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a0bc59fc12a8f94498ce0eb52326f37fd6bff2b5)
In older definition of Euclidean spaces through [synthetic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synthetic_geometry "Synthetic geometry"), vectors are defined as [equivalence classes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equivalence_class "Equivalence class") of [ordered pairs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordered_pair "Ordered pair") of points under [equipollence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equipollence_(geometry) "Equipollence (geometry)") (the pairs (_A_, _B_) and (_C_, _D_) are _equipollent_ if the points _A_, _B_, _D_, _C_ (in this order) form a [parallelogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram "Parallelogram")). It is straightforward to verify that the vectors form a vector space, the square of the [Euclidean distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_distance "Euclidean distance") is a quadratic form on the space of vectors, and the two definitions of Euclidean spaces are equivalent.

In [Euclidean geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_geometry "Euclidean geometry"), the common phrase "**affine property**" refers to a property that can be proved in affine spaces, that is, it can be proved without using the quadratic form and its associated inner product. In other words, an affine property is a property that does not involve lengths and angles. Typical examples are [parallelism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_(geometry) "Parallel (geometry)"), and the definition of a [tangent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent "Tangent"). A non-example is the definition of a [normal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_(geometry) "Normal (geometry)").

Equivalently, an affine property is a property that is invariant under [affine transformations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_transformation "Affine transformation") of the Euclidean space.

Affine span and bases
---------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Affine_space&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Affine span and bases")]

For any non-empty subset _X_ of an affine space _A_, there is a smallest affine subspace that contains it, called the **affine span** of _X_. It is the intersection of all affine subspaces containing _X_, and its direction is the intersection of the directions of the affine subspaces that contain _X_.

The affine span of _X_ is the set of all (finite) affine combinations of points of _X_, and its direction is the [linear span](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_span "Linear span") of the _x_ − _y_ for _x_ and _y_ in _X_. If one chooses a particular point _x_ 0, the direction of the affine span of _X_ is also the linear span of the _x_ – _x_ 0 for _x_ in _X_.

One says also that the affine span of _X_ is **generated** by _X_ and that _X_ is a **generating set** of its affine span.

A set _X_ of points of an affine space is said to be **affinely independent** or, simply, **independent**, if the affine span of any [strict subset](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strict_subset "Strict subset") of _X_ is a strict subset of the affine span of _X_. An **affine basis** or **barycentric frame** (see [§Barycentric coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_space#Barycentric_coordinates), below) of an affine space is a generating set that is also independent (that is a [minimal generating set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minimal_generating_set "Minimal generating set")).

Recall that the _dimension_ of an affine space is the dimension of its associated vector space. The bases of an affine space of finite dimension _n_ are the independent subsets of _n_ + 1 elements, or, equivalently, the generating subsets of _n_ + 1 elements. Equivalently, {_x_ 0, ..., _x_ _n_} is an affine basis of an affine space [if and only if](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/If_and_only_if "If and only if"){_x_ 1 − _x_ 0, ..., _x_ _n_ − _x_ 0} is a [linear basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_basis "Linear basis") of the associated vector space.

There are two strongly related kinds of [coordinate systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_system "Coordinate system") that may be defined on affine spaces.

### Barycentric coordinates

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Affine_space&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: Barycentric coordinates")]

![Image 105: {\displaystyle \lambda _{0}+\dots +\lambda _{n}=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ff4410120f23a1e884b11a16847ccdbfd6ecb505)
and

![Image 106: {\displaystyle x=\lambda _{0}x_{0}+\dots +\lambda _{n}x_{n}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7c30ca97ef4b3771539ab3ed62bbe40c45e36851)
The ![Image 107: {\displaystyle \lambda _{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/72fde940918edf84caf3d406cc7d31949166820f) are called the **barycentric coordinates** of _x_ over the affine basis ![Image 108: {\displaystyle \{x_{0},\dots ,x_{n}\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/007e042ff4cda39d85d58e1876cda96a1e0658c8). If the _x i_ are viewed as bodies that have weights (or masses) ![Image 109: {\displaystyle \lambda _{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/72fde940918edf84caf3d406cc7d31949166820f), the point _x_ is thus the [barycenter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centroid "Centroid") of the _x i_, and this explains the origin of the term _barycentric coordinates_.

For affine spaces of infinite dimension, the same definition applies, using only finite sums. This means that for each point, only a finite number of coordinates are non-zero.

An **affine frame** is a [coordinate frame](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_frame "Coordinate frame") of an affine space, consisting of a point, called the _[origin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Origin\_(mathematics) "Origin (mathematics)")_, and a [linear basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_basis "Linear basis") of the associated vector space. More precisely, for an affine space _A_ with associated vector space ![Image 111: {\displaystyle {\overrightarrow {A}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/79a63d1d5ad20924a9e908d0f805f2b16e745dcd), the origin _o_ belongs to _A_, and the linear basis is a basis (_v_ 1, ..., _v_ _n_) of ![Image 112: {\displaystyle {\overrightarrow {A}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/79a63d1d5ad20924a9e908d0f805f2b16e745dcd) (for simplicity of the notation, we consider only the case of finite dimension, the general case is similar).

For each point _p_ of _A_, there is a unique sequence ![Image 113: {\displaystyle \lambda _{1},\dots ,\lambda _{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0980b08cb9c0fae51d9109a1c6319b22807e0eea) of elements of the ground field such that

![Image 114: {\displaystyle p=o+\lambda _{1}v_{1}+\dots +\lambda _{n}v_{n},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a241f4da37377a89d62ab447d54cd108ae6abc5b)
or equivalently

![Image 115: {\displaystyle {\overrightarrow {op}}=\lambda _{1}v_{1}+\dots +\lambda _{n}v_{n}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/73ab04c0b95b682d852ba8321591ee77e56c31a1)
The ![Image 116: {\displaystyle \lambda _{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/72fde940918edf84caf3d406cc7d31949166820f) are called the **affine coordinates** of _p_ over the affine frame (_o_, _v_ 1, ..., _v_ _n_).

**Example:** In [Euclidean geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_geometry "Euclidean geometry"), [Cartesian coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinates "Cartesian coordinates") are affine coordinates relative to an _[orthonormal frame](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthonormal\_frame\_(Euclidean\_geometry) "Orthonormal frame (Euclidean geometry)")_, that is an affine frame (_o_, _v_ 1, ..., _v_ _n_) such that (_v_ 1, ..., _v_ _n_) is an [orthonormal basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthonormal_basis "Orthonormal basis").

### Relationship between barycentric and affine coordinates

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Affine_space&action=edit&section=17 "Edit section: Relationship between barycentric and affine coordinates")]

Barycentric coordinates and affine coordinates are strongly related, and may be considered as equivalent.

In fact, given a barycentric frame

![Image 117: {\displaystyle (x_{0},\dots ,x_{n}),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fe79334b26a058149d8c84b726460377475e8bc6)
one deduces immediately the affine frame

![Image 118: {\displaystyle (x_{0},{\overrightarrow {x_{0}x_{1}}},\dots ,{\overrightarrow {x_{0}x_{n}}})=\left(x_{0},x_{1}-x_{0},\dots ,x_{n}-x_{0}\right),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/feb68180edc027a8c75c523530b68873e3140b6d)
and, if

![Image 119: {\displaystyle \left(\lambda _{0},\lambda _{1},\dots ,\lambda _{n}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0e976f8d679c43f97c3b6652c83e9686d4735d70)
are the barycentric coordinates of a point over the barycentric frame, then the affine coordinates of the same point over the affine frame are

![Image 120: {\displaystyle \left(\lambda _{1},\dots ,\lambda _{n}\right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0c5d8b5824a7f53bea98c66039b126a5f1ea00a0)
Conversely, if

![Image 121: {\displaystyle \left(o,v_{1},\dots ,v_{n}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/16b48eb98647af912da0588284ecbf32ba23de4c)
is an affine frame, then

![Image 122: {\displaystyle \left(o,o+v_{1},\dots ,o+v_{n}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ed716e47f7868a9cc9ef023561bfc06c0b8a4e8b)
is a barycentric frame. If

![Image 123: {\displaystyle \left(\lambda _{1},\dots ,\lambda _{n}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/56f7742a9f00ba87be1ef8a42c1af60f4b6b8fb7)
are the affine coordinates of a point over the affine frame, then its barycentric coordinates over the barycentric frame are

![Image 124: {\displaystyle \left(1-\lambda _{1}-\dots -\lambda _{n},\lambda _{1},\dots ,\lambda _{n}\right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d98f638ab4ee7de5cbd47586cacc4b4362d23507)
Therefore, barycentric and affine coordinates are almost equivalent. In most applications, affine coordinates are preferred, as involving less coordinates that are independent. However, in the situations where the important points of the studied problem are affinely independent, barycentric coordinates may lead to simpler computation, as in the following example.

#### Example of the triangle

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Affine_space&action=edit&section=18 "Edit section: Example of the triangle")]

The vertices of a non-flat [triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle "Triangle") form an affine basis of the [Euclidean plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_plane "Euclidean plane"). The barycentric coordinates allows easy characterization of the elements of the triangle that do not involve angles or distances:

The vertices are the points of barycentric coordinates (1, 0, 0), (0, 1, 0) and (0, 0, 1). The lines supporting the [edges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edge_(geometry) "Edge (geometry)") are the points that have a zero coordinate. The edges themselves are the points that have one zero coordinate and two nonnegative coordinates. The [interior](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interior_(mathematics) "Interior (mathematics)") of the triangle are the points whose coordinates are all positive. The [medians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Median_(geometry) "Median (geometry)") are the points that have two equal coordinates, and the [centroid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centroid "Centroid") is the point of coordinates (⁠1/3⁠, ⁠1/3⁠, ⁠1/3⁠).

Properties of affine homomorphisms
----------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Affine_space&action=edit&section=22 "Edit section: Properties of affine homomorphisms")]

= Matrix representation=
------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Affine_space&action=edit&section=23 "Edit section: = Matrix representation=")]

An affine transformation ![Image 149: {\displaystyle T}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ec7200acd984a1d3a3d7dc455e262fbe54f7f6e0) is executed on a projective space ![Image 150: {\displaystyle \mathbb {P} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7fb80175bc622b3936c7a0438fc690b2ec410b4f) of ![Image 151: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5), by a 4 by 4 matrix with a special[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_space#cite_note-8) fourth column:

![Image 152: {\displaystyle A={\begin{bmatrix}a_{11}&a_{12}&a_{13}&0\\a_{21}&a_{22}&a_{23}&0\\a_{31}&a_{32}&a_{33}&0\\a_{41}&a_{42}&a_{43}&1\end{bmatrix}}={\begin{bmatrix}T(1,0,0)&0\\T(0,1,0)&0\\T(0,0,1)&0\\T(0,0,0)&1\end{bmatrix}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4ba97b2892cb59be096579c1f25f2ea2ecf199a9)

The transformation is affine instead of linear due to the inclusion of point ![Image 153: {\displaystyle (0,0,0)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/28c6fd55d5621fd95ca93549660fbb355fd9bd22), the transformed output of which reveals the affine shift.

Let

![Image 154: {\displaystyle f\colon E\to F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/65e588635af63181c20287f8ea6f18389b5eaec2)
be an affine homomorphism, with

![Image 155: {\displaystyle {\overrightarrow {f}}\colon {\overrightarrow {E}}\to {\overrightarrow {F}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6627766bc0f5b60d7580e2debdad939ba6445953)
its associated linear map. The **image** of _f_ is the affine subspace ![Image 156: {\displaystyle f(E)=\{f(a)\mid a\in E\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/16483836fc5c9ce4bfe3d15fdb161a2733e032fe) of F, which has ![Image 157: {\displaystyle {\overrightarrow {f}}({\overrightarrow {E}})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e1498630d8ac79ab2081477c30721835073e156c) as associated vector space. As an affine space does not have a [zero element](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero_element "Zero element"), an affine homomorphism does not have a [kernel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kernel_(algebra) "Kernel (algebra)"). However, the linear map ![Image 158: {\displaystyle {\overrightarrow {f}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cfa88ab873934603aafa809d533b4c49fc16adda) does, and if we denote by ![Image 159: {\displaystyle K=\{v\in {\overrightarrow {E}}\mid {\overrightarrow {f}}(v)=0\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/aac122210a6f603ca4e9135006cb555ecd44cae0) its kernel, then for any point _x_ of ![Image 160: {\displaystyle f(E)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3465496148d29e6543003de4cdbac8e9ce6beda7), the [inverse image](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverse_image "Inverse image")![Image 161: {\displaystyle f^{-1}(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/46a543a3462cbb9effd5d0c514529426172c7d7b) of x is an affine subspace of _E_ whose direction is ![Image 162: {\displaystyle K}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b76fce82a62ed5461908f0dc8f037de4e3686b0). This affine subspace is called the [**fiber**](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiber_(mathematics) "Fiber (mathematics)") of _x_.

An important example is the projection parallel to some direction onto an affine subspace. The importance of this example lies in the fact that [Euclidean spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space") are affine spaces, and that these kinds of projections are fundamental in [Euclidean geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_geometry "Euclidean geometry").

More precisely, given an affine space _E_ with associated vector space ![Image 163: {\displaystyle {\overrightarrow {E}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5d664d9d5a70d17f42f3b274eb2e79037cf3bcae), let _F_ be an affine subspace of direction ![Image 164: {\displaystyle {\overrightarrow {F}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ac2b5875f3997034668b7b97ed82ae892697ba9b), and _D_ be a [complementary subspace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complementary_subspace "Complementary subspace") of ![Image 165: {\displaystyle {\overrightarrow {F}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ac2b5875f3997034668b7b97ed82ae892697ba9b) in ![Image 166: {\displaystyle {\overrightarrow {E}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5d664d9d5a70d17f42f3b274eb2e79037cf3bcae) (this means that every vector of ![Image 167: {\displaystyle {\overrightarrow {E}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5d664d9d5a70d17f42f3b274eb2e79037cf3bcae) may be decomposed in a unique way as the sum of an element of ![Image 168: {\displaystyle {\overrightarrow {F}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ac2b5875f3997034668b7b97ed82ae892697ba9b) and an element of _D_). For every point _x_ of _E_, its **projection** to _F_ parallel to _D_ is the unique point _p_(_x_) in _F_ such that

![Image 169: {\displaystyle p(x)-x\in D.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/53313df4053989e8d98b5b439ed6e49b0012c9a3)
This is an affine homomorphism whose associated linear map ![Image 170: {\displaystyle {\overrightarrow {p}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a48508462cc610fbb2c91ca441740f6b4c5478e6) is defined by

![Image 171: {\displaystyle {\overrightarrow {p}}(x-y)=p(x)-p(y),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/916e0b4c9df896231f026b58028771e7ddf9bba8)
for x and y in E.

The image of this projection is _F_, and its fibers are the subspaces of direction _D_.

Although kernels are not defined for affine spaces, [quotient spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quotient_space_(linear_algebra) "Quotient space (linear algebra)") are defined. This results from the fact that "belonging to the same fiber of an affine homomorphism" is an equivalence relation.

Let _E_ be an affine space, and _D_ be a [linear subspace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_subspace "Linear subspace") of the associated vector space ![Image 172: {\displaystyle {\overrightarrow {E}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5d664d9d5a70d17f42f3b274eb2e79037cf3bcae). The **quotient**_E_/_D_ of _E_ by _D_ is the [quotient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quotient_by_an_equivalence_relation "Quotient by an equivalence relation") of _E_ by the [equivalence relation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equivalence_relation "Equivalence relation") such that x and y are equivalent if

![Image 173: {\displaystyle x-y\in D.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/59a75d3c0914bc464c0dfcc8f5705af69b6eb2f4)
This quotient is an affine space, which has ![Image 174: {\displaystyle {\overrightarrow {E}}/D}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ce5db3f473ca606ea10bbe524def85ed268a0bd4) as associated vector space.

For every affine homomorphism ![Image 175: {\displaystyle E\to F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/84d9e3dcf392a3538226db6bda1b7e2dd73c32e3), the image is isomorphic to the quotient of _E_ by the kernel of the associated linear map. This is the [first isomorphism theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_isomorphism_theorem "First isomorphism theorem") for affine spaces.

Affine spaces are usually studied by [analytic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytic_geometry "Analytic geometry") using coordinates, or equivalently vector spaces. They can also be studied as [synthetic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synthetic_geometry "Synthetic geometry") by writing down axioms, though this approach is much less common. There are several different systems of axioms for affine space.

[Coxeter (1969](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_space#CITEREFCoxeter1969), p.192) axiomatizes the special case of [affine geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_geometry "Affine geometry") over the reals as [ordered geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordered_geometry "Ordered geometry") together with an affine form of [Desargues's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Desargues%27s_theorem "Desargues's theorem") and an axiom stating that in a plane there is at most one line through a given point not meeting a given line.

Affine planes satisfy the following axioms ([Cameron 1991](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_space#CITEREFCameron1991), chapter 2): (in which two lines are called parallel if they are equal or disjoint):

*   Any two distinct points lie on a unique line.
*   Given a point and line there is a unique line that contains the point and is parallel to the line
*   There exist three non-collinear points.

As well as affine planes over fields (or [division rings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_ring "Division ring")), there are also many [non-Desarguesian planes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-Desarguesian_plane "Non-Desarguesian plane") satisfying these axioms. [Cameron (1991](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_space#CITEREFCameron1991), chapter 3) gives axioms for higher-dimensional affine spaces.

Purely axiomatic affine geometry is more general than affine spaces and is treated in the article _[Affine geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine\_geometry "Affine geometry")_.

Relation to projective spaces
-----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Affine_space&action=edit&section=28 "Edit section: Relation to projective spaces")]

[![Image 176](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Affine_space%2C_projective_space%2C_vector_space.svg/250px-Affine_space%2C_projective_space%2C_vector_space.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Affine_space,_projective_space,_vector_space.svg)

An affine space is a subspace of a projective space, which is in turn the quotient of a vector space by an equivalence relation (not by a linear subspace)

Affine spaces are contained in [projective spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_space "Projective space"). For example, an affine plane can be obtained from any [projective plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_plane "Projective plane") by removing one line and all the points on it, and conversely any affine plane can be used to construct a projective plane as a [closure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closure_(mathematics) "Closure (mathematics)") by adding a [line at infinity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_at_infinity "Line at infinity") whose points correspond to equivalence classes of [parallel lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_lines "Parallel lines"). Similar constructions hold in higher dimensions.

Further, transformations of projective space that preserve affine space (equivalently, that leave the [hyperplane at infinity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperplane_at_infinity "Hyperplane at infinity")[invariant as a set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invariant_(mathematics)#Invariant_set "Invariant (mathematics)")) yield transformations of affine space. Conversely, any affine linear transformation extends uniquely to a [projective linear transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_linear_transformation "Projective linear transformation"), so the [affine group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_group "Affine group") is a [subgroup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subgroup "Subgroup") of the [projective group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_group "Projective group"). For instance, [Möbius transformations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M%C3%B6bius_transformation "Möbius transformation") (transformations of the [complex projective line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_projective_line "Complex projective line"), or [Riemann sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_sphere "Riemann sphere")) are affine (transformations of the [complex plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_plane "Complex plane")) if and only if they fix the [point at infinity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_at_infinity "Point at infinity").

Affine algebraic geometry
-------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Affine_space&action=edit&section=29 "Edit section: Affine algebraic geometry")]

In [algebraic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_geometry "Algebraic geometry"), an [affine variety](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_variety "Affine variety") (or, more generally, an [affine algebraic set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_algebraic_set "Affine algebraic set")) is defined as the subset of an affine space that is the set of the common zeros of a set of so-called _polynomial functions over the affine space_. For defining a _polynomial function over the affine space_, one has to choose an [affine frame](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_frame "Affine frame"). Then, a polynomial function is a function such that the image of any point is the value of some multivariate [polynomial function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial_function "Polynomial function") of the coordinates of the point. As a change of affine coordinates may be expressed by [linear functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_function "Linear function") (more precisely affine functions) of the coordinates, this definition is independent of a particular choice of coordinates.

The choice of a system of affine coordinates for an affine space ![Image 177: {\displaystyle \mathbb {A} _{k}^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0579559af277736057f7672ec4b75f9027f17dd2) of dimension _n_ over a [field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)")_k_ induces an affine [isomorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isomorphism "Isomorphism") between ![Image 178: {\displaystyle \mathbb {A} _{k}^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0579559af277736057f7672ec4b75f9027f17dd2) and the affine [coordinate space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_space "Coordinate space")_k_ _n_. This explains why, for simplification, many textbooks write ![Image 179: {\displaystyle \mathbb {A} _{k}^{n}=k^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b160394398931357ea654102a3f7352dd65321cf), and introduce affine [algebraic varieties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_varieties "Algebraic varieties") as the common zeros of polynomial functions over _k_ _n_.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_space#cite_note-9)

As the whole affine space is the set of the common zeros of the [zero polynomial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero_polynomial "Zero polynomial"), affine spaces are affine algebraic varieties.

### Ring of polynomial functions

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Affine_space&action=edit&section=30 "Edit section: Ring of polynomial functions")]

By the definition above, the choice of an affine frame of an affine space ![Image 180: {\displaystyle \mathbb {A} _{k}^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0579559af277736057f7672ec4b75f9027f17dd2) allows one to identify the polynomial functions on ![Image 181: {\displaystyle \mathbb {A} _{k}^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0579559af277736057f7672ec4b75f9027f17dd2) with polynomials in _n_ variables, the _i_ th variable representing the function that maps a point to its _i_ th coordinate. It follows that the set of polynomial functions over ![Image 182: {\displaystyle \mathbb {A} _{k}^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0579559af277736057f7672ec4b75f9027f17dd2) is a [_k_-algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra_over_a_field "Algebra over a field"), denoted ![Image 183: {\displaystyle k\left[\mathbb {A} _{k}^{n}\right]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cfc1e918756dde70dfb45492c3b82ae11b349a24), which is isomorphic to the [polynomial ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial_ring "Polynomial ring")![Image 184: {\displaystyle k\left[X_{1},\dots ,X_{n}\right]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/41000e80982a7309e905469c64ee71a87ea2c1af).

When one changes coordinates, the isomorphism between ![Image 185: {\displaystyle k\left[\mathbb {A} _{k}^{n}\right]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cfc1e918756dde70dfb45492c3b82ae11b349a24) and ![Image 186: {\displaystyle k[X_{1},\dots ,X_{n}]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b80417d281d3fa33df1c998af00fcb9a84702804) changes accordingly, and this induces an automorphism of ![Image 187: {\displaystyle k\left[X_{1},\dots ,X_{n}\right]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/41000e80982a7309e905469c64ee71a87ea2c1af), which maps each indeterminate to a polynomial of degree one. It follows that the [total degree](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Total_degree "Total degree") defines a [filtration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Filtration_(mathematics) "Filtration (mathematics)") of ![Image 188: {\displaystyle k\left[\mathbb {A} _{k}^{n}\right]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cfc1e918756dde70dfb45492c3b82ae11b349a24), which is independent from the choice of coordinates. The total degree defines also a [graduation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graded_ring "Graded ring"), but it depends on the choice of coordinates, as a change of affine coordinates may map indeterminates on non-[homogeneous polynomials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homogeneous_polynomial "Homogeneous polynomial").

Affine spaces over [topological fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_field "Topological field"), such as the real or the complex numbers, have a natural [topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology_(structure) "Topology (structure)"). The Zariski topology, which is defined for affine spaces over any field, allows use of topological methods in any case. Zariski topology is the unique topology on an affine space whose [closed sets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_set "Closed set") are [affine algebraic sets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_algebraic_set "Affine algebraic set") (that is sets of the common zeros of polynomial functions over the affine set). As, over a topological field, polynomial functions are continuous, every Zariski closed set is closed for the usual topology, if any. In other words, over a topological field, Zariski topology is [coarser](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coarser_topology "Coarser topology") than the natural topology.

There is a natural injective function from an affine space into the set of [prime ideals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_ideal "Prime ideal") (that is the [spectrum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectrum_of_a_ring "Spectrum of a ring")) of its ring of polynomial functions. When affine coordinates have been chosen, this function maps the point of coordinates ![Image 189: {\displaystyle \left(a_{1},\dots ,a_{n}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/852e439cb0c52c41773d190712883d61ed0b242d) to the [maximal ideal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maximal_ideal "Maximal ideal")![Image 190: {\displaystyle \left\langle X_{1}-a_{1},\dots ,X_{n}-a_{n}\right\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b143e2a1ac80f23993099727654a4557803e5fff). This function is a [homeomorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homeomorphism "Homeomorphism") (for the Zariski topology of the affine space and of the spectrum of the ring of polynomial functions) of the affine space onto the image of the function.

The case of an [algebraically closed ground field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraically_closed_field "Algebraically closed field") is especially important in algebraic geometry, because, in this case, the homeomorphism above is a map between the affine space and the set of all maximal ideals of the ring of functions (this is [Hilbert's Nullstellensatz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert%27s_Nullstellensatz "Hilbert's Nullstellensatz")).

This is the starting idea of [scheme theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scheme_theory "Scheme theory") of [Grothendieck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grothendieck "Grothendieck"), which consists, for studying algebraic varieties, of considering as "points", not only the points of the affine space, but also all the prime ideals of the spectrum. This allows [gluing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gluing_(topology) "Gluing (topology)") together algebraic varieties in a similar way as, for [manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manifold "Manifold"), [charts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chart_(topology) "Chart (topology)") are glued together for building a manifold.

Like all affine varieties, local data on an affine space can always be patched together globally: the [cohomology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cohomology "Cohomology") of affine space is trivial. More precisely, ![Image 191: {\displaystyle H^{i}\left(\mathbb {A} _{k}^{n},\mathbf {F} \right)=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a0b7c43b6719672ab32d34c6b3f07f2960b0ffa6) for all [coherent sheaves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coherent_sheaves "Coherent sheaves")**F**, and [integers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer")![Image 192: {\displaystyle i>0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1f49f2878fd68a89c3da37eb537198e887cf0293). This property is also enjoyed by all other [affine varieties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_variety "Affine variety") (see [Serre's theorem on affineness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Serre%27s_theorem_on_affineness "Serre's theorem on affineness")). But also all of the [étale cohomology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%89tale_cohomology "Étale cohomology") groups on affine space are trivial. In particular, every [line bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_bundle "Line bundle") is trivial. More generally, the [Quillen–Suslin theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quillen%E2%80%93Suslin_theorem "Quillen–Suslin theorem") implies that _every_ algebraic [vector bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_bundle "Vector bundle") over an affine space is trivial.
