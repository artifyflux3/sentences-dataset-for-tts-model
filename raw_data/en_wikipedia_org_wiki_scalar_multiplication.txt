In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), **scalar multiplication** is one of the basic operations defining a [vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space") in [linear algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_algebra "Linear algebra")[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_multiplication#cite_note-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_multiplication#cite_note-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_multiplication#cite_note-3) (or more generally, a [module](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Module_(mathematics) "Module (mathematics)") in [abstract algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abstract_algebra "Abstract algebra")[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_multiplication#cite_note-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_multiplication#cite_note-5)). In common geometrical contexts, scalar multiplication of a [real](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number")[Euclidean vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_vector "Euclidean vector") by a positive real number multiplies the magnitude of the vector without changing its [direction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direction_(geometry) "Direction (geometry)"). Scalar multiplication is the multiplication of a vector by a scalar (where the product is a vector), and is to be distinguished from [inner product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inner_product "Inner product") of two vectors (where the product is a scalar).

In general, if _K_ is a [field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(algebra) "Field (algebra)") and _V_ is a vector space over _K_, then scalar multiplication is a [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)") from _K_ × _V_ to _V_. The result of applying this function to _k_ in _K_ and **v** in _V_ is denoted _k_**v**.

Scalar multiplication obeys the following rules _(vector in [boldface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boldface "Boldface"))_:

*   [Additivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Additive_map "Additive map") in the scalar: (_c_ + _d_)**v** = _c_**v** + _d_**v**;
*   Additivity in the vector: _c_(**v** + **w**) = _c_**v** + _c_**w**;
*   Compatibility of product of scalars with scalar multiplication: (_cd_)**v** = _c_(_d_**v**);
*   Multiplying by 1 does not change a vector: 1**v** = **v**;
*   Multiplying by 0 gives the [zero vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero_vector "Zero vector"): 0**v** = **0**;
*   Multiplying by −1 gives the [additive inverse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Additive_inverse "Additive inverse"): (−1)**v** = −**v**.

Here, + is [addition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Addition "Addition") either in the field or in the vector space, as appropriate; and 0 is the additive identity in either. [Juxtaposition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication_by_juxtaposition "Multiplication by juxtaposition") indicates either scalar multiplication or the [multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication "Multiplication") operation in the field.

The space of vectors may be considered a [coordinate space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_space "Coordinate space") where elements are associated with a list of elements from _K_. The [units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_(ring_theory) "Unit (ring theory)") of the field form a group _K_ × and the scalar-vector multiplication is a [group action](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_action "Group action") on the coordinate space by _K_ ×. The zero of the field acts on the coordinate space to collapse it to the zero vector.

When _K_ is the field of real numbers there is a [geometric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric "Geometric") interpretation of scalar multiplication: it stretches or contracts vectors by a constant factor. As a result, it produces a vector in the same or opposite direction of the original vector but of a different length.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_multiplication#cite_note-6)

As a special case, _V_ may be taken to be _K_ itself and scalar multiplication may then be taken to be simply the multiplication in the field.

When _V_ is _K_ _n_, scalar multiplication is equivalent to multiplication of each component with the scalar, and may be defined as such.

The same idea applies if _K_ is a [commutative ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_ring "Commutative ring") and _V_ is a [module](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Module_(mathematics) "Module (mathematics)") over _K_. _K_ can even be a [rig](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rig_(algebra) "Rig (algebra)"), but then there is no additive inverse. If _K_ is not [commutative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative "Commutative"), the distinct operations _left scalar multiplication_ _c_**v** and _right scalar multiplication_**v**_c_ may be defined.

Scalar multiplication of matrices
---------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Scalar_multiplication&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Scalar multiplication of matrices")]

The **left scalar multiplication** of a matrix **A** with a scalar _λ_ gives another matrix of the same size as **A**. It is denoted by _λ_**A**, whose entries of _λ_**A** are defined by

![Image 3: {\displaystyle (\lambda \mathbf {A} )_{ij}=\lambda \left(\mathbf {A} \right)_{ij}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1d24514640b5608b11122af40082f89dfffad7f3)
explicitly:

![Image 4: {\displaystyle \lambda \mathbf {A} =\lambda {\begin{pmatrix}A_{11}&A_{12}&\cdots &A_{1m}\\A_{21}&A_{22}&\cdots &A_{2m}\\\vdots &\vdots &\ddots &\vdots \\A_{n1}&A_{n2}&\cdots &A_{nm}\\\end{pmatrix}}={\begin{pmatrix}\lambda A_{11}&\lambda A_{12}&\cdots &\lambda A_{1m}\\\lambda A_{21}&\lambda A_{22}&\cdots &\lambda A_{2m}\\\vdots &\vdots &\ddots &\vdots \\\lambda A_{n1}&\lambda A_{n2}&\cdots &\lambda A_{nm}\\\end{pmatrix}}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/249286d687770eadec5a02096535dcd814501b88)
Similarly, even though there is no widely accepted definition, the **right scalar multiplication** of a matrix **A** with a scalar _λ_ could be defined to be

![Image 5: {\displaystyle (\mathbf {A} \lambda )_{ij}=\left(\mathbf {A} \right)_{ij}\lambda \,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5a4eb610cb19c5a8d42d59bb3a919c8181759a00)
explicitly:

![Image 6: {\displaystyle \mathbf {A} \lambda ={\begin{pmatrix}A_{11}&A_{12}&\cdots &A_{1m}\\A_{21}&A_{22}&\cdots &A_{2m}\\\vdots &\vdots &\ddots &\vdots \\A_{n1}&A_{n2}&\cdots &A_{nm}\\\end{pmatrix}}\lambda ={\begin{pmatrix}A_{11}\lambda &A_{12}\lambda &\cdots &A_{1m}\lambda \\A_{21}\lambda &A_{22}\lambda &\cdots &A_{2m}\lambda \\\vdots &\vdots &\ddots &\vdots \\A_{n1}\lambda &A_{n2}\lambda &\cdots &A_{nm}\lambda \\\end{pmatrix}}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/58186145172bc4ad9bb672c620495f6508b88af3)
When the entries of the matrix and the scalars are from the same commutative field, for example, the real number field or the complex number field, these two multiplications are the same, and can be simply called _scalar multiplication_. For matrices over a more general [field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)") that is _not_ commutative, they may not be equal.

For a real scalar and matrix:

![Image 7: {\displaystyle \lambda =2,\quad \mathbf {A} ={\begin{pmatrix}a&b\\c&d\\\end{pmatrix}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8e3f4f43e50a1798d20948d2b33dccd8927f1182)![Image 8: {\displaystyle 2\mathbf {A} =2{\begin{pmatrix}a&b\\c&d\\\end{pmatrix}}={\begin{pmatrix}2\!\cdot \!a&2\!\cdot \!b\\2\!\cdot \!c&2\!\cdot \!d\\\end{pmatrix}}={\begin{pmatrix}a\!\cdot \!2&b\!\cdot \!2\\c\!\cdot \!2&d\!\cdot \!2\\\end{pmatrix}}={\begin{pmatrix}a&b\\c&d\\\end{pmatrix}}2=\mathbf {A} 2.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/07831fe515696fa0cb1a6e51c9112a7a60721b21)
For quaternion scalars and matrices:

![Image 9: {\displaystyle \lambda =i,\quad \mathbf {A} ={\begin{pmatrix}i&0\\0&j\\\end{pmatrix}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c6611631920bec00d95a7aec9ec63f94f108e040)![Image 10: {\displaystyle i{\begin{pmatrix}i&0\\0&j\\\end{pmatrix}}={\begin{pmatrix}i^{2}&0\\0&ij\\\end{pmatrix}}={\begin{pmatrix}-1&0\\0&k\\\end{pmatrix}}\neq {\begin{pmatrix}-1&0\\0&-k\\\end{pmatrix}}={\begin{pmatrix}i^{2}&0\\0&ji\\\end{pmatrix}}={\begin{pmatrix}i&0\\0&j\\\end{pmatrix}}i\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f850091591855f9c08ea5bb4b948c032220f9116)
where _i_, _j_, _k_ are the quaternion units. The non-commutativity of quaternion multiplication prevents the transition of changing _ij_ = +_k_ to _ji_ = −_k_.
