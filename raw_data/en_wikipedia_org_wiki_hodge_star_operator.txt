Markdown Content:
In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), the **Hodge star operator** or **Hodge star** is a [linear map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_map "Linear map") defined on the [exterior algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_algebra "Exterior algebra") of a [finite-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension_(vector_space) "Dimension (vector space)")[oriented](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orientation_(vector_space) "Orientation (vector space)")[vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space") endowed with a [nondegenerate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degenerate_bilinear_form "Degenerate bilinear form")[symmetric bilinear form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetric_bilinear_form "Symmetric bilinear form"). Applying the operator to an element of the algebra produces the **Hodge dual** of the element. This map was introduced by [W. V. D. Hodge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/W._V._D._Hodge "W. V. D. Hodge").

For example, in an oriented 3-dimensional Euclidean space, an oriented plane can be represented by the [exterior product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_product "Exterior product") of two basis vectors, and its Hodge dual is the [normal vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_vector "Normal vector") given by their [cross product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product "Cross product"); conversely, any vector is dual to the oriented plane perpendicular to it, endowed with a suitable bivector. Generalizing this to an _n_-dimensional vector space, the Hodge star is a one-to-one mapping of _k_-vectors to (_n – k_)-vectors; the dimensions of these spaces are the [binomial coefficients](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binomial_coefficient "Binomial coefficient")![Image 1: {\displaystyle {\tbinom {n}{k}}={\tbinom {n}{n-k}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/14dcb867ba1cc064d2ab0c85590a87c8511e17d8).

The [naturalness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_transformation "Natural transformation") of the star operator means it can play a role in differential geometry when applied to the cotangent [bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_bundle "Vector bundle") of a [pseudo-Riemannian manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudo-Riemannian_manifold "Pseudo-Riemannian manifold"), and hence to [differential _k_-forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_form "Differential form"). This allows the definition of the codifferential as the Hodge adjoint of the [exterior derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_derivative "Exterior derivative"), leading to the [Laplace–de Rham operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laplace%E2%80%93Beltrami_operator#Laplace%E2%80%93de_Rham_operator "Laplace–Beltrami operator"). This generalizes the case of 3-dimensional Euclidean space, in which [divergence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divergence "Divergence") of a vector field may be realized as the codifferential opposite to the [gradient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gradient "Gradient") operator, and the [Laplace operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laplace_operator "Laplace operator") on a function is the divergence of its gradient. An important application is the [Hodge decomposition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hodge_decomposition "Hodge decomposition") of differential forms on a [closed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_manifold "Closed manifold") Riemannian manifold.

Formal definition for _k_-vectors
---------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hodge_star_operator&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Formal definition for k-vectors")]

Let _V_ be an [_n_-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension_(vector_space) "Dimension (vector space)")[oriented](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orientation_(vector_space) "Orientation (vector space)")[vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space") with a nondegenerate symmetric bilinear form ![Image 2: {\displaystyle \langle \cdot ,\cdot \rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9a50080b735975d8001c9552ac2134b49ad534c0), referred to here as a scalar product. (In more general contexts such as pseudo-Riemannian manifolds and [Minkowski space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minkowski_space "Minkowski space"), the bilinear form may not be positive-definite.) This induces a [scalar product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_algebra#Inner_product "Exterior algebra") on [_k_-vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multivector "Multivector")![Image 3: {\textstyle \alpha ,\beta \in \bigwedge ^{\!k}V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b64f93be06d620225b65f1c7e8c588e1e789c0dd), for ![Image 4: {\displaystyle 0\leq k\leq n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e7b429c3c44b3dc10332285272eee6f754dbf985), by defining it on simple _k_-vectors ![Image 5: {\displaystyle \alpha =\alpha _{1}\wedge \cdots \wedge \alpha _{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0f6b9f9b93e21f2fef34febde6a45b2b5362da1c) and ![Image 6: {\displaystyle \beta =\beta _{1}\wedge \cdots \wedge \beta _{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c5d0875d169c82616df8de3ec7506b5d6cd5874f) to equal the [Gram determinant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gram_determinant "Gram determinant")[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hodge_star_operator#cite_note-HF-1): 14

![Image 7: {\displaystyle \langle \alpha ,\beta \rangle =\det \left(\left\langle \alpha _{i},\beta _{j}\right\rangle _{i,j=1}^{k}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d2101be0220a38cc9f29cafbebe621da3560581f)

In the Riemannian case, ![Image 40: {\displaystyle t=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/970dea4a5f5ec5355c4cdd62f6396fbc8b1baaa1).

Since Hodge star takes an orthonormal basis to an orthonormal basis, it is an [isometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isometry "Isometry") on the exterior algebra ![Image 41: {\textstyle \bigwedge V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81b19ed2a6f976174afb1038711ef92f260299a6).

Geometric explanation
---------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hodge_star_operator&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Geometric explanation")]

The Hodge star is motivated by the correspondence between a subspace _W_ of _V_ and its orthogonal subspace (with respect to the scalar product), where each space is endowed with an [orientation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orientation_(vector_space) "Orientation (vector space)") and a numerical scaling factor. Specifically, a non-zero decomposable _k_-vector ![Image 42: {\displaystyle w_{1}\wedge \cdots \wedge w_{k}\in \textstyle \bigwedge ^{\!k}V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/70b8f6f98dd1bc747e5dad6c950f34f2b1b9c096) corresponds by the [Plücker embedding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pl%C3%BCcker_embedding "Plücker embedding") to the subspace ![Image 43: {\displaystyle W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54a9c4c547f4d6111f81946cad242b18298d70b7) with oriented basis ![Image 44: {\displaystyle w_{1},\ldots ,w_{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4d05275871db4078540a0c24e70600a3657a9822), endowed with a scaling factor equal to the _k_-dimensional volume of the parallelepiped spanned by this basis (equal to the [Gramian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gramian_matrix "Gramian matrix"), the determinant of the matrix of scalar products ![Image 45: {\displaystyle \langle w_{i},w_{j}\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/27842e64005b495809ce27c7ed3dfe7ea2f5a396)). The Hodge star acting on a decomposable vector can be written as a decomposable (_n_ − _k_)-vector:

![Image 46: {\displaystyle {\star }(w_{1}\wedge \cdots \wedge w_{k})\,=\,u_{1}\wedge \cdots \wedge u_{n-k},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3b5d734ac683fa3bf9f1e0c3ddd4ba0edf8e948d)
where ![Image 47: {\displaystyle u_{1},\ldots ,u_{n-k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3c60e942bb07572a1fdda97243f712544f4613fb) form an oriented basis of the [orthogonal space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_complement "Orthogonal complement")![Image 48: {\displaystyle U=W^{\perp }\!}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3887b48f9daf43ca5de1b12710d2ea907e2ef09d). Furthermore, the (_n_ − _k_)-volume of the ![Image 49: {\displaystyle u_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/14f13cb025ff2e136dcbd2fc81ddf965b728e3d7)-parallelepiped must equal the _k_-volume of the ![Image 50: {\displaystyle w_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fe22f0329d3ecb2e1880d44d191aba0e5475db68)-parallelepiped, and ![Image 51: {\displaystyle w_{1},\ldots ,w_{k},u_{1},\ldots ,u_{n-k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d768fed8a8c30f57865a0b771f5ea90cbecfe074) must form an oriented basis of ![Image 52: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845).

A general _k_-vector is a linear combination of decomposable _k_-vectors, and the definition of Hodge star is extended to general _k_-vectors by defining it as being linear.

In two dimensions with the normalized Euclidean metric and orientation given by the ordering (_x_, _y_), the Hodge star on _k_-forms is given by ![Image 53: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}{\star }\,1&=dx\wedge dy\\{\star }\,dx&=dy\\{\star }\,dy&=-dx\\{\star }(dx\wedge dy)&=1.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5be87d22fc056707c211afb95656667acb76397d)

A common example of the Hodge star operator is the case _n_ = 3, when it can be taken as the correspondence between vectors and bivectors. Specifically, for [Euclidean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space")**R**3 with the basis ![Image 54: {\displaystyle dx,dy,dz}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c0df5a109b0087620b32449bd5ea7b4c1fffd9db) of [one-forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One-form "One-form") often used in [vector calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_calculus "Vector calculus"), one finds that ![Image 55: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}{\star }\,dx&=dy\wedge dz\\{\star }\,dy&=dz\wedge dx\\{\star }\,dz&=dx\wedge dy.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b281f94deaadd18ed9104a04bfd37f891dbac903)

The Hodge star relates the exterior and cross product in three dimensions:[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hodge_star_operator#cite_note-Lounesto-2)![Image 56: {\displaystyle {\star }(\mathbf {u} \wedge \mathbf {v} )=\mathbf {u} \times \mathbf {v} \qquad {\star }(\mathbf {u} \times \mathbf {v} )=\mathbf {u} \wedge \mathbf {v} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3703180e2291872b2c91282cc1e0fb166da2710) Applied to three dimensions, the Hodge star provides an [isomorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isomorphism "Isomorphism") between [axial vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axial_vector "Axial vector") and [bivectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bivector "Bivector"), so each axial vector **a** is associated with a bivector **A** and vice versa, that is:[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hodge_star_operator#cite_note-Lounesto-2)![Image 57: {\displaystyle \mathbf {A} ={\star }\mathbf {a} ,\ \ \mathbf {a} ={\star }\mathbf {A} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2d491d15e1d0868cbf2e36a17b3330a36ea992ec).

dx}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e25a3290ce75547b7e11b4ca778b47584eec2a34) is the skew-symmetric matrix ![Image 77: {\displaystyle \scriptscriptstyle \left[{\begin{array}{rrr}\,0\!\!&\!\!1&\!\!\!\!0\!\!\!\!\!\!\\[-.5em]\,\!-1\!\!&\!\!0\!\!&\!\!\!\!0\!\!\!\!\!\!\\[-.5em]\,0\!\!&\!\!0\!\!&\!\!\!\!0\!\!\!\!\!\!\end{array}}\!\!\!\right]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b19f2be27e2269b98bb1bd16bb8a13b6446f0109), etc. That is, we may interpret the star operator as: ![Image 78: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} =a\,dx+b\,dy+c\,dz\quad \longrightarrow \quad {\star }{\mathbf {v} }\ \cong \ L_{\mathbf {v} }\ =\left[{\begin{array}{rrr}0&c&-b\\-c&0&a\\b&-a&0\end{array}}\right].}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/be194eec58239b1f6e3cf434001b1062b10dcb69) Under this correspondence, cross product of vectors corresponds to the commutator [Lie bracket](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_algebra "Lie algebra") of linear operators: ![Image 79: {\displaystyle L_{\mathbf {u} \times \mathbf {v} }=L_{\mathbf {v} }L_{\mathbf {u} }-L_{\mathbf {u} }L_{\mathbf {v} }=-\left[L_{\mathbf {u} },L_{\mathbf {v} }\right]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3fd6c6072f45e6cbc7d703ecd072b526550804c0).

In case ![Image 80: {\displaystyle n=4}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d928ec15aeef83aade867992ee473933adb6139d), the Hodge star acts as an [endomorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endomorphism "Endomorphism") of the second exterior power (i.e. it maps 2-forms to 2-forms, since 4 − 2 = 2). If the signature of the [metric tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_tensor "Metric tensor") is all positive, i.e. on a [Riemannian manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_manifold "Riemannian manifold"), then the Hodge star is an [involution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Involution_(mathematics) "Involution (mathematics)"). If the signature is mixed, i.e., [pseudo-Riemannian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudo-Riemannian_manifold "Pseudo-Riemannian manifold"), then applying the operator twice will return the argument up to a sign – see _[§Duality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hodge\_star\_operator#Duality)_ below. This particular endomorphism property of 2-forms in four dimensions makes [self-dual and anti-self-dual two-forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yang%E2%80%93Mills_equations#Anti-self-duality_equations "Yang–Mills equations") natural geometric objects to study. That is, one can describe the space of 2-forms in four dimensions with a basis that "diagonalizes" the Hodge star operator with eigenvalues ![Image 81: {\displaystyle \pm 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0bfeaa85da53ad1947d8000926cfea33827ef1e0) (or ![Image 82: {\displaystyle \pm i}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c1b7df63745bc6839de7b7df413c192f5816ff2e), depending on the signature).

For concreteness, we discuss the Hodge star operator in Minkowski spacetime where ![Image 83: {\displaystyle n=4}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d928ec15aeef83aade867992ee473933adb6139d) with metric signature (− + + +) and coordinates ![Image 84: {\displaystyle (t,x,y,z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/899eb4e1d2776847a8a39f5a9e0354b2e1383c80). The [volume form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume_form "Volume form") is oriented as ![Image 85: {\displaystyle \varepsilon _{0123}=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cef23452f24c5b9b3027583700b3700c4d307fc8). For [one-forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One-form "One-form"), ![Image 86: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}{\star }dt&=-dx\wedge dy\wedge dz\,,\\{\star }dx&=-dt\wedge dy\wedge dz\,,\\{\star }dy&=-dt\wedge dz\wedge dx\,,\\{\star }dz&=-dt\wedge dx\wedge dy\,,\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a75409ee9ee1fae9aa18b1975f10fbbc3f99cac4) while for [2-forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2-form "2-form"), ![Image 87: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}{\star }(dt\wedge dx)&=-dy\wedge dz\,,\\{\star }(dt\wedge dy)&=-dz\wedge dx\,,\\{\star }(dt\wedge dz)&=-dx\wedge dy\,,\\{\star }(dx\wedge dy)&=dt\wedge dz\,,\\{\star }(dz\wedge dx)&=dt\wedge dy\,,\\{\star }(dy\wedge dz)&=dt\wedge dx\,.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/96403eb95c10cd28ba3a8fdc38c719745b1801f7)

These are summarized in the index notation as ![Image 88: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}{\star }(dx^{\mu })&=\eta ^{\mu \lambda }\varepsilon _{\lambda \nu \rho \sigma }{\frac {1}{3!}}dx^{\nu }\wedge dx^{\rho }\wedge dx^{\sigma }\,,\\{\star }(dx^{\mu }\wedge dx^{\nu })&=\eta ^{\mu \kappa }\eta ^{\nu \lambda }\varepsilon _{\kappa \lambda \rho \sigma }{\frac {1}{2!}}dx^{\rho }\wedge dx^{\sigma }\,.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a262b685eeb151a25b5b32db2bfe4749bc46588)

Hodge dual of three- and four-forms can be easily deduced from the fact that, in the Lorentzian signature, ![Image 89: {\displaystyle {\star }^{2}=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/63c11bdf68f845ba63a5d80ac6e07b2eb9a0b7d9) for odd-rank forms and ![Image 90: {\displaystyle {\star }^{2}=-1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f26087b238cd96c5c1993cc782ca9ad3209fba82) for even-rank forms. An easy rule to remember for these Hodge operations is that given a form ![Image 91: {\displaystyle \alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b79333175c8b3f0840bfb4ec41b8072c83ea88d3), its Hodge dual ![Image 92: {\displaystyle {\star }\alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1ff859ff419c6a994ca4a70d3a039d3892911da5) may be obtained by writing the components not involved in ![Image 93: {\displaystyle \alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b79333175c8b3f0840bfb4ec41b8072c83ea88d3) in an order such that ![Image 94: {\displaystyle \alpha \wedge ({\star }\alpha )=dt\wedge dx\wedge dy\wedge dz}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cd09809aaf02c817b8ccb3fd2aecbb46b1782efa).[_[verification needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability "Wikipedia:Verifiability")_] An extra minus sign will enter only if ![Image 95: {\displaystyle \alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b79333175c8b3f0840bfb4ec41b8072c83ea88d3) contains ![Image 96: {\displaystyle dt}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ebee76a835701fd1f26047a09855f2ea36bb08fc). (For (+ − − −), one puts in a minus sign only if ![Image 97: {\displaystyle \alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b79333175c8b3f0840bfb4ec41b8072c83ea88d3) involves an odd number of the space-associated forms ![Image 98: {\displaystyle dx}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/845c817e348381a13f3fad5184169ce0e021c685), ![Image 99: {\displaystyle dy}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5c5eda9ec854eb0076d43c147eb8956637a1003f) and ![Image 100: {\displaystyle dz}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6dfd26a4c7fe1595bf4629b5be9026dffd166956).)

Note that the combinations ![Image 101: {\displaystyle (dx^{\mu }\wedge dx^{\nu })^{\pm }:={\frac {1}{2}}{\big (}dx^{\mu }\wedge dx^{\nu }\mp i{\star }(dx^{\mu }\wedge dx^{\nu }){\big )}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dbc0dfb4677d576802f1303f0b9e95ff48303098) take ![Image 102: {\displaystyle \pm i}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c1b7df63745bc6839de7b7df413c192f5816ff2e) as the eigenvalue for Hodge star operator, i.e., ![Image 103: {\displaystyle {\star }(dx^{\mu }\wedge dx^{\nu })^{\pm }=\pm i(dx^{\mu }\wedge dx^{\nu })^{\pm },}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/58a964e8b01928f2bbe7c3a0809a07dabd14c195) and hence deserve the name self-dual and anti-self-dual two-forms. Understanding the geometry, or kinematics, of Minkowski spacetime in self-dual and anti-self-dual sectors turns out to be insightful in both [mathematical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Low-dimensional_topology#Four_dimensions "Low-dimensional topology") and [physical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chirality_(physics)#Chirality_and_helicity "Chirality (physics)") perspectives, making contacts to the use of the [two-spinor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weyl_equation#Weyl_spinors "Weyl equation") language in modern physics such as [spinor-helicity formalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Spinor-helicity_formalism&action=edit&redlink=1 "Spinor-helicity formalism (page does not exist)") or [twistor theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Twistor_theory "Twistor theory").

### Conformal invariance

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hodge_star_operator&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Conformal invariance")]

The Hodge star is conformally invariant on _n_-forms on a 2 _n_-dimensional vector space ![Image 104: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845), i.e. if ![Image 105: {\displaystyle g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3556280e66fe2c0d0140df20935a6f057381d77) is a metric on ![Image 106: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) and ![Image 107: {\displaystyle \lambda >0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eea25afc0351140f919cf791c49c1964b8b081de), then the induced Hodge stars ![Image 108: {\displaystyle {\star }_{g},{\star }_{\lambda g}:\Lambda ^{n}V\to \Lambda ^{n}V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/765ec2b5d8707f09f1f6f05d8fa4f5c1e9bf07ed) are the same.

### Example: Derivatives in three dimensions

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hodge_star_operator&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Example: Derivatives in three dimensions")]

The combination of the ![Image 109: {\displaystyle {\star }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dcda0fc423fda8f0a63f8a14aa707e813640b143) operator and the [exterior derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_derivative "Exterior derivative")_d_ generates the classical operators [grad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gradient "Gradient"), [curl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curl_(mathematics) "Curl (mathematics)"), and [div](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divergence "Divergence") on [vector fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_field "Vector field") in three-dimensional Euclidean space. This works out as follows: _d_ takes a 0-form (a function) to a 1-form, a 1-form to a 2-form, and a 2-form to a 3-form (and takes a 3-form to zero). For a 0-form ![Image 110: {\displaystyle f=f(x,y,z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/63495e3dffa38c2965b421aa22cafdd3d59071e4), the first case written out in components gives: ![Image 111: {\displaystyle df={\frac {\partial f}{\partial x}}\,dx+{\frac {\partial f}{\partial y}}\,dy+{\frac {\partial f}{\partial z}}\,dz.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/36009a863d7174a4803434815416276cb690e29a)

The scalar product [identifies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_space#Bilinear_products_and_dual_spaces "Dual space") 1-forms with vector fields as ![Image 112: {\displaystyle dx\mapsto (1,0,0)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7fbe6dba19b669f84c0385d7fe898ed8942b4289), etc., so that ![Image 113: {\displaystyle df}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/53181e2067a93b6bbf150042723cb059d9d2d26f) becomes ![Image 114: {\textstyle \operatorname {grad} f=\left({\frac {\partial f}{\partial x}},{\frac {\partial f}{\partial y}},{\frac {\partial f}{\partial z}}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9209ddd576fd000cb9a8fbb60cea23932bbbc2fc).

In the second case, a vector field ![Image 115: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} =(A,B,C)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fce59cdcb6f1535716faa8d83e9f12590c997628) corresponds to the 1-form ![Image 116: {\displaystyle \varphi =A\,dx+B\,dy+C\,dz}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/aebad21614cb68bf09331d1b972a91b461be149c), which has exterior derivative: ![Image 117: {\displaystyle d\varphi =\left({\frac {\partial C}{\partial y}}-{\frac {\partial B}{\partial z}}\right)dy\wedge dz+\left({\frac {\partial C}{\partial x}}-{\frac {\partial A}{\partial z}}\right)dx\wedge dz+\left({\partial B \over \partial x}-{\frac {\partial A}{\partial y}}\right)dx\wedge dy.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2109cc40a240c9edf185d155ff71fb2b05c158f2)

Applying the Hodge star gives the 1-form: ![Image 118: {\displaystyle {\star }d\varphi =\left({\partial C \over \partial y}-{\partial B \over \partial z}\right)\,dx-\left({\partial C \over \partial x}-{\partial A \over \partial z}\right)\,dy+\left({\partial B \over \partial x}-{\partial A \over \partial y}\right)\,dz,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b44256a768c342bd56711717c9159b7663f7bd94) which becomes the vector field ![Image 119: {\textstyle \operatorname {curl} \mathbf {F} =\left({\frac {\partial C}{\partial y}}-{\frac {\partial B}{\partial z}},\,-{\frac {\partial C}{\partial x}}+{\frac {\partial A}{\partial z}},\,{\frac {\partial B}{\partial x}}-{\frac {\partial A}{\partial y}}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6307672df9ce387e38d468e577ea888657d2d522).

In the third case, ![Image 120: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} =(A,B,C)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fce59cdcb6f1535716faa8d83e9f12590c997628) again corresponds to ![Image 121: {\displaystyle \varphi =A\,dx+B\,dy+C\,dz}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/aebad21614cb68bf09331d1b972a91b461be149c). Applying Hodge star, exterior derivative, and Hodge star again: ![Image 122: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}{\star }\varphi &=A\,dy\wedge dz-B\,dx\wedge dz+C\,dx\wedge dy,\\d{\star \varphi }&=\left({\frac {\partial A}{\partial x}}+{\frac {\partial B}{\partial y}}+{\frac {\partial C}{\partial z}}\right)dx\wedge dy\wedge dz,\\{\star }d{\star }\varphi &={\frac {\partial A}{\partial x}}+{\frac {\partial B}{\partial y}}+{\frac {\partial C}{\partial z}}=\operatorname {div} \mathbf {F} .\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ef5fc6ad719f6101daf18b37618ec2140da6c02e)

One advantage of this expression is that the identity _d_ 2 = 0, which is true in all cases, has as special cases two other identities: (1) curl grad _f_ = 0, and (2) div curl **F** = 0. In particular, [Maxwell's equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxwell%27s_equations#Relativistic_formulations "Maxwell's equations") take on a particularly simple and elegant form, when expressed in terms of the exterior derivative and the Hodge star. The expression ![Image 123: {\displaystyle {\star }d{\star }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3db5ba14dc989a347731fd5d84e8fc95e94b71f0) (multiplied by an appropriate power of −1) is called the _codifferential_; it is defined in full generality, for any dimension, further in the article below.

One can also obtain the [Laplacian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laplacian "Laplacian")Δ _f_ = div grad _f_ in terms of the above operations: ![Image 124: {\displaystyle \Delta f={\star }d{\star }df={\frac {\partial ^{2}f}{\partial x^{2}}}+{\frac {\partial ^{2}f}{\partial y^{2}}}+{\frac {\partial ^{2}f}{\partial z^{2}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9ff264a5c7617dd937baca4adab9fd28ca174b3f)

The Laplacian can also be seen as a special case of the more general [Laplace–deRham operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laplace%E2%80%93Beltrami_operator "Laplace–Beltrami operator")![Image 125: {\displaystyle \Delta =d\delta +\delta d}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1d92a393f3c3b5c6102a33f0f0b924476ba09e5c) where in three dimensions, ![Image 126: {\displaystyle \delta =(-1)^{k}{\star }d{\star }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/26792a13688ea1d69c3e095b3fe29bf08f19cd1b) is the codifferential for ![Image 127: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40)-forms. Any function ![Image 128: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) is a 0-form, and ![Image 129: {\displaystyle \delta f=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ff18e3e0399e3cbc94d39a9ca5fca904d8745df2) and so this reduces to the ordinary Laplacian. For the 1-form ![Image 130: {\displaystyle \varphi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/33ee699558d09cf9d653f6351f9fda0b2f4aaa3e) above, the codifferential is ![Image 131: {\displaystyle \delta =-{\star }d{\star }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/18e204871ab018a2426a36461c7edc395e09e584) and after some straightforward calculations one obtains the Laplacian acting on ![Image 132: {\displaystyle \varphi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/33ee699558d09cf9d653f6351f9fda0b2f4aaa3e).

Applying the Hodge star twice leaves a _k_-vector unchanged [up to](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Up_to "Up to") a sign: for ![Image 133: {\displaystyle \eta \in {\textstyle \bigwedge }^{k}V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ec28e2dc828f7b3dbad7ae5a799e8673fafe2c9a) in an n-dimensional space _V_, one has

![Image 134: {\displaystyle {\star }{\star }\eta =(-1)^{k(n-k)}s\,\eta ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/76b201a1588cc07f685215149b3c54d1ca879b55)
where s is the parity of the [signature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_signature "Metric signature") of the scalar product on _V_, that is, the sign of the [determinant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Determinant "Determinant") of the matrix of the scalar product with respect to any basis. For example, if _n_ = 4 and the signature of the scalar product is either (+ − − −) or (− + + +) then _s_ = −1. For Riemannian manifolds (including Euclidean spaces), we always have _s_ = 1.

The above identity implies that the inverse of ![Image 135: {\displaystyle {\star }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dcda0fc423fda8f0a63f8a14aa707e813640b143) can be given as

![Image 136: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}{\star }^{-1}:~{\textstyle \bigwedge }^{\!k}V&\to {\textstyle \bigwedge }^{\!n-k}V\\\eta &\mapsto (-1)^{k(n-k)}\!s\,{\star }\eta \end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4ac5c325ad4f4942b3a257a2def1297fd81ae49b)
If n is odd then _k_(_n_ − _k_) is even for any k, whereas if n is even then _k_(_n_ − _k_) has the parity of k. Therefore:

![Image 137: {\displaystyle {\star }^{-1}={\begin{cases}s\,{\star }&n{\text{ is odd}}\\(-1)^{k}s\,{\star }&n{\text{ is even}}\end{cases}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d15d17f788740a6eea09772da6bb526bf2b4341d)
where k is the degree of the element operated on.

For an _n_-dimensional oriented [pseudo-Riemannian manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudo-Riemannian_manifold "Pseudo-Riemannian manifold")_M_, we apply the construction above to each [cotangent space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cotangent_space "Cotangent space")![Image 138: {\displaystyle {\text{T}}_{p}^{*}M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e94bdc581d12068ae633a7a7348e95ac8a914cca) and its exterior powers ![Image 139: {\textstyle \bigwedge ^{k}{\text{T}}_{p}^{*}M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e8818f24b399c758a8fa68eea8535fc40ca707b1), and hence to the differential _k_[-forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_form "Differential form")![Image 140: {\textstyle \zeta \in \Omega ^{k}(M)=\Gamma \left(\bigwedge ^{k}{\text{T}}^{*}\!M\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/24f4e93ffff44113770cb62f1dc6d15e3335679a), the [global sections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sheaf_(mathematics) "Sheaf (mathematics)") of the [bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_bundle "Vector bundle")![Image 141: {\textstyle \bigwedge ^{k}\mathrm {T} ^{*}\!M\to M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/33313f908ea49dd83308bb4ae25ecb452952bb65). The Riemannian metric induces a scalar product on ![Image 142: {\textstyle \bigwedge ^{k}{\text{T}}_{p}^{*}M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e8818f24b399c758a8fa68eea8535fc40ca707b1) at each point ![Image 143: {\displaystyle p\in M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35ad2c18a15749505c928763cd4fdb56f4982816). We define the **Hodge dual** of a _k_[-form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_form "Differential form")![Image 144: {\displaystyle \zeta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d5c3916703cae7938143d38865f78f27faadd4ae), defining ![Image 145: {\displaystyle {\star }\zeta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/be076913b92bdba8e101733d082369bda844f123) as the unique (_n_ – _k_)-form satisfying ![Image 146: {\displaystyle \eta \wedge {\star }\zeta \ =\ \langle \eta ,\zeta \rangle \,\omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/90f5a42989b3aec8c7b01d257a165d7e141f9218) for every _k_-form ![Image 147: {\displaystyle \eta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e4d701857cf5fbec133eebaf94deadf722537f64), where ![Image 148: {\displaystyle \langle \eta ,\zeta \rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d8bf4bbf62b91216587545e6386ba7cc36133142) is a real-valued function on ![Image 149: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd), and the [volume form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume_form#Riemannian_volume_form "Volume form")![Image 150: {\displaystyle \omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/48eff443f9de7a985bb94ca3bde20813ea737be8) is induced by the pseudo-Riemannian metric. Integrating this equation over ![Image 151: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd), the right side becomes the ![Image 152: {\displaystyle L^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ba162c66ca85776c83557af5088cc6f8584d1912) ([square-integrable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sobolev_space "Sobolev space")) [scalar product on _k_-forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_algebra#Inner_product "Exterior algebra"), and we obtain: ![Image 153: {\displaystyle \int _{M}\eta \wedge {\star }\zeta \ =\ \int _{M}\langle \eta ,\zeta \rangle \ \omega .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0eb0e04f9aa9953e2740df2e90cf3f9967790e4a)

More generally, if ![Image 154: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) is non-orientable, one can define the Hodge star of a _k_-form as a (_n_ – _k_)-[pseudo differential form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudotensor "Pseudotensor"); that is, a differential form with values in the [canonical line bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canonical_bundle "Canonical bundle").

### Computation in index notation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hodge_star_operator&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Computation in index notation")]

We compute in terms of [tensor index notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_index_notation "Tensor index notation") with respect to a (not necessarily orthonormal) basis ![Image 155: {\textstyle \left\{{\frac {\partial }{\partial x_{1}}},\ldots ,{\frac {\partial }{\partial x_{n}}}\right\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a297353e9080edf27021e34d3f0202053de43213) in a tangent space ![Image 156: {\displaystyle V=T_{p}M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a009a75712f6c7278f5b0d936ea83d11619bb7d) and its dual basis ![Image 157: {\displaystyle \{dx_{1},\ldots ,dx_{n}\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b21acc613bb201c12ef684289b03095d004b8783) in ![Image 158: {\displaystyle V^{*}=T_{p}^{*}M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8328fa1c9a51ae3587c7930ff78a997e386c4330), having the metric matrix ![Image 159: {\textstyle (g_{ij})=\left(\left\langle {\frac {\partial }{\partial x_{i}}},{\frac {\partial }{\partial x_{j}}}\right\rangle \right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/98db7aa63c7b6fbfa4764d52a731d7a2cd2b63b6) and its inverse matrix ![Image 160: {\displaystyle (g^{ij})=(\langle dx^{i},dx^{j}\rangle )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c4d6b449a19bcab582fbc92c93e08ca5dc07d1e3). The Hodge dual of a decomposable _k_-form is: ![Image 161: {\displaystyle {\star }\left(dx^{i_{1}}\wedge \dots \wedge dx^{i_{k}}\right)\ =\ {\frac {\sqrt {\left|\det[g_{ij}]\right|}}{(n-k)!}}g^{i_{1}j_{1}}\cdots g^{i_{k}j_{k}}\varepsilon _{j_{1}\dots j_{n}}dx^{j_{k+1}}\wedge \dots \wedge dx^{j_{n}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ee6e01a526be0ccc2076a9091cce1909df485d06)

Here ![Image 162: {\displaystyle \varepsilon _{j_{1}\dots j_{n}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4c5ef2746edfa075fe7cb04907cf3e7c5d848b9f) is the [Levi-Civita symbol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_symbol "Levi-Civita symbol") with ![Image 163: {\displaystyle \varepsilon _{1\dots n}=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35c4510dd76725e6cfeeb8a129b0c720fb6e6be9), and we [implicitly take the sum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_notation "Einstein notation") over all values of the repeated indices ![Image 164: {\displaystyle j_{1},\ldots ,j_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/22f061be993b916c4cbf7b5a2495f6ccfdc3face). The factorial ![Image 165: {\displaystyle (n-k)!}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0291ef2aca31045420234df633ef2216412f8641) accounts for double counting, and is not present if the summation indices are restricted so that ![Image 166: {\displaystyle j_{k+1}<\dots <j_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/15c44f2b8090e2bf224c72af7ea32b600d0e4fc7). The absolute value of the determinant is necessary since it may be negative, as for tangent spaces to [Lorentzian manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudo-Riemannian_manifold#Lorentzian_manifold "Pseudo-Riemannian manifold").

An arbitrary differential form can be written as follows: ![Image 167: {\displaystyle \alpha \ =\ {\frac {1}{k!}}\alpha _{i_{1},\dots ,i_{k}}dx^{i_{1}}\wedge \dots \wedge dx^{i_{k}}\ =\ \sum _{i_{1}<\dots <i_{k}}\alpha _{i_{1},\dots ,i_{k}}dx^{i_{1}}\wedge \dots \wedge dx^{i_{k}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/992beac049d689089fcbd55fca43c11ec799ebfa)

The factorial ![Image 168: {\displaystyle k!}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/949e312c9bf9a9a5e641c1db1b1d7c6f0425b536) is again included to account for double counting when we allow non-increasing indices. We would like to define the dual of the component ![Image 169: {\displaystyle \alpha _{i_{1},\dots ,i_{k}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/956d7bb8c29d1a3cfc85071a8246aae3ebd71b1e) so that the Hodge dual of the form is given by ![Image 170: {\displaystyle {\star }\alpha ={\frac {1}{(n-k)!}}({\star }\alpha )_{i_{k+1},\dots ,i_{n}}dx^{i_{k+1}}\wedge \dots \wedge dx^{i_{n}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e8f71939cd7bcae1cdd18d5de477e71b01e0bdcb)

Using the above expression for the Hodge dual of ![Image 171: {\displaystyle dx^{i_{1}}\wedge \dots \wedge dx^{i_{k}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/71e8ba2302308404f2016d07a02e94dbf366bde0), we find:[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hodge_star_operator#cite_note-3)![Image 172: {\displaystyle ({\star }\alpha )_{j_{k+1},\dots ,j_{n}}={\frac {\sqrt {\left|\det[g_{ab}]\right|}}{k!}}\alpha _{i_{1},\dots ,i_{k}}\,g^{i_{1}j_{1}}\cdots g^{i_{k}j_{k}}\,\varepsilon _{j_{1},\dots ,j_{n}}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c76e792b7a03d0b2ac34e8299263f0616e1cba2d)

Although one can apply this expression to any tensor ![Image 173: {\displaystyle \alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b79333175c8b3f0840bfb4ec41b8072c83ea88d3), the result is antisymmetric, since contraction with the completely anti-symmetric Levi-Civita symbol cancels all but the totally antisymmetric part of the tensor. It is thus equivalent to antisymmetrization followed by applying the Hodge star.

The unit volume form ![Image 174: {\textstyle \omega ={\star }1\in \bigwedge ^{n}V^{*}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2eef3177f413e12c9ae596be78e4b9d9da9a4651) is given by: ![Image 175: {\displaystyle \omega ={\sqrt {\left|\det[g_{ij}]\right|}}\;dx^{1}\wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{n}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bd252222a98cd412c56f4ad544bdc5be40f7aa6b)

The most important application of the Hodge star on manifolds is to define the **codifferential**![Image 176: {\displaystyle \delta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c5321cfa797202b3e1f8620663ff43c4660ea03a) on ![Image 177: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40)-forms. Let ![Image 178: {\displaystyle \delta =(-1)^{n(k+1)+1}s\ {\star }d{\star }=(-1)^{k}\,{\star }^{-1}d{\star }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c14a255a07bc64f07954bd9320c2204280aac1ab) where ![Image 179: {\displaystyle d}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e85ff03cbe0c7341af6b982e47e9f90d235c66ab) is the [exterior derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_derivative "Exterior derivative") or differential, and ![Image 180: {\displaystyle s=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bac386d8f227fb823cede9b3e33d706cad3ed306) for Riemannian manifolds. Then ![Image 181: {\displaystyle d:\Omega ^{k}(M)\to \Omega ^{k+1}(M)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/50c50ee03c8998b0e97c4c1e6be3a5a418b49536) while ![Image 182: {\displaystyle \delta :\Omega ^{k}(M)\to \Omega ^{k-1}(M).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b5d0c0ef2ff95f79bbbf55c9383a06040db096e6)

The codifferential is not an [antiderivation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiderivation "Antiderivation") on the exterior algebra, in contrast to the exterior derivative.

The codifferential is the [adjoint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transpose_of_a_linear_map "Transpose of a linear map") of the exterior derivative with respect to the square-integrable scalar product: ![Image 183: {\displaystyle \langle \!\langle \eta ,\delta \zeta \rangle \!\rangle \ =\ \langle \!\langle d\eta ,\zeta \rangle \!\rangle ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/90f560556541cd3678e72e5f6e34d8c08a87dffd) where ![Image 184: {\displaystyle \zeta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d5c3916703cae7938143d38865f78f27faadd4ae) is a ![Image 185: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40)-form and ![Image 186: {\displaystyle \eta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e4d701857cf5fbec133eebaf94deadf722537f64) a ![Image 187: {\displaystyle (k\!-\!1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0ebc64bcbc812620d26457acb5082d1dad6e1c82)-form. This property is useful as it can be used to define the codifferential even when the manifold is non-orientable (and the Hodge star operator not defined). The identity can be proved from Stokes' theorem for smooth forms: ![Image 188: {\displaystyle 0\ =\ \int _{M}d(\eta \wedge {\star }\zeta )\ =\ \int _{M}\left(d\eta \wedge {\star }\zeta +(-1)^{k-1}\eta \wedge {\star }\,{\star }^{-1}d\,{\star }\zeta \right)\ =\ \langle \!\langle d\eta ,\zeta \rangle \!\rangle -\langle \!\langle \eta ,\delta \zeta \rangle \!\rangle ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0ed7a606225fad656e0f3a0bc98b0881703e583c) provided ![Image 189: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) has empty boundary, or ![Image 190: {\displaystyle \eta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e4d701857cf5fbec133eebaf94deadf722537f64) or ![Image 191: {\displaystyle {\star }\zeta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/be076913b92bdba8e101733d082369bda844f123) has zero boundary values. (The proper definition of the above requires specifying a [topological vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_vector_space "Topological vector space") that is closed and complete on the space of smooth forms. The [Sobolev space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sobolev_space "Sobolev space") is conventionally used; it allows the convergent sequence of forms ![Image 192: {\displaystyle \zeta _{i}\to \zeta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cfe8fccbb79a1c3f9e842c6fec77837bb5c05ae9) (as ![Image 193: {\displaystyle i\to \infty }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/70119755bca6d5271a646644ddd99cefd980d8fd)) to be interchanged with the combined differential and integral operations, so that ![Image 194: {\displaystyle \langle \!\langle \eta ,\delta \zeta _{i}\rangle \!\rangle \to \langle \!\langle \eta ,\delta \zeta \rangle \!\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d978781a0c4eebfb033c648ad4b601880dfc7a90) and likewise for sequences converging to ![Image 195: {\displaystyle \eta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e4d701857cf5fbec133eebaf94deadf722537f64).)

Since the differential satisfies ![Image 196: {\displaystyle d^{2}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1e9466655dcef8801f5d4e6f03da58b4fad2ab61), the codifferential has the corresponding property ![Image 197: {\displaystyle \delta ^{2}=(-1)^{n}s^{2}{\star }d{\star }{\star }d{\star }=(-1)^{nk+k+1}s^{3}{\star }d^{2}{\star }=0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/24750d414225bc628199ffa11d4bfedd7f3078f1)

The [Laplace–deRham](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laplace%E2%80%93Beltrami_operator "Laplace–Beltrami operator") operator is given by ![Image 198: {\displaystyle \Delta =(\delta +d)^{2}=\delta d+d\delta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/91d37a523b3e3dd5f677c46e131bc3e817a507d7) and lies at the heart of [Hodge theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hodge_theory "Hodge theory"). It is symmetric: ![Image 199: {\displaystyle \langle \!\langle \Delta \zeta ,\eta \rangle \!\rangle =\langle \!\langle \zeta ,\Delta \eta \rangle \!\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3cbe71983f2bb4421f09c1ca9ae82a6a9d2c7db) and non-negative: ![Image 200: {\displaystyle \langle \!\langle \Delta \eta ,\eta \rangle \!\rangle \geq 0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3517f6dd44ba43b617ff043ff977126395425264)

The Hodge star sends [harmonic forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harmonic_form "Harmonic form") to harmonic forms. As a consequence of [Hodge theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hodge_theory "Hodge theory"), the [de Rham cohomology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_Rham_cohomology "De Rham cohomology") is naturally isomorphic to the space of harmonic k-forms, and so the Hodge star induces an isomorphism of cohomology groups ![Image 201: {\displaystyle {\star }:H_{\Delta }^{k}(M)\to H_{\Delta }^{n-k}(M),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ddbc619817b74f042b3b31ed0d137a7d0c1efc6c) which in turn gives canonical identifications via [Poincaré duality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poincar%C3%A9_duality "Poincaré duality") of _H k_(_M_) with its [dual space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_space "Dual space").

In coordinates, with notation as above, the codifferential of the form ![Image 202: {\displaystyle \alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b79333175c8b3f0840bfb4ec41b8072c83ea88d3) may be written as ![Image 203: {\displaystyle \delta \alpha =\ -{\frac {1}{k!}}g^{ml}\left({\frac {\partial }{\partial x_{l}}}\alpha _{m,i_{1},\dots ,i_{k-1}}-\Gamma _{ml}^{j}\alpha _{j,i_{1},\dots ,i_{k-1}}\right)dx^{i_{1}}\wedge \dots \wedge dx^{i_{k-1}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d17af7328d9eca302b8151dfdd7057e98e3dde32) where here ![Image 204: {\displaystyle \Gamma _{ml}^{j}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a9fc09d789aab1effbb744a8ad24028e6441d3bc) denotes the [Christoffel symbols](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christoffel_symbols "Christoffel symbols") of ![Image 205: {\textstyle \left\{{\frac {\partial }{\partial x_{1}}},\ldots ,{\frac {\partial }{\partial x_{n}}}\right\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a297353e9080edf27021e34d3f0202053de43213).

#### Poincare lemma for codifferential

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hodge_star_operator&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Poincare lemma for codifferential")]

In analogy to the [Poincare lemma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poincare_lemma "Poincare lemma") for [exterior derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_derivative "Exterior derivative"), one can define its version for codifferential, which reads[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hodge_star_operator#cite_note-:0-4)

_If_![Image 206: {\displaystyle \delta \omega =0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0b6af8f14e117cafcd4be45970b50eacf4356319)_for_![Image 207: {\displaystyle \omega \in \Lambda ^{k}(U)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1bda8b27dc45daa61abea0a5d91b3ce3f840a4b7)_, where_![Image 208: {\displaystyle U}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/458a728f53b9a0274f059cd695e067c430956025)_is a [star domain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star\_domain "Star domain") on a manifold, then there is_![Image 209: {\displaystyle \alpha \in \Lambda ^{k+1}(U)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c6220acd1e329e0b8ab19c3f19b947d15e14ae61)_such that_![Image 210: {\displaystyle \omega =\delta \alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/41ecb5daa927161c8d98019e4537dca2d67aee67)_._
A practical way of finding ![Image 211: {\displaystyle \alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b79333175c8b3f0840bfb4ec41b8072c83ea88d3) is to use cohomotopy operator ![Image 212: {\displaystyle h}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b26be3e694314bc90c3215047e4a2010c6ee184a), that is a local inverse of ![Image 213: {\displaystyle \delta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c5321cfa797202b3e1f8620663ff43c4660ea03a). One has to define a [homotopy operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homotopy_operator "Homotopy operator")[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hodge_star_operator#cite_note-:0-4)

![Image 214: {\displaystyle H\beta =\int _{0}^{1}{\mathcal {K}}\lrcorner \beta |_{F(t,x)}t^{k}dt,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8742a1bf70ffed2d83b4b9d6f6887b4a4fb4726)
where ![Image 215: {\displaystyle F(t,x)=x_{0}+t(x-x_{0})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/95c00376ffdc712dcb0f978e13365cb40586078f) is the linear homotopy between its center ![Image 216: {\displaystyle x_{0}\in U}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2dee8010e46dd5891756fb4b02b43bc661f7c666) and a point ![Image 217: {\displaystyle x\in U}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c32ddcb2941216f2980b950ce969dc15cba26906), and the (Euler) vector ![Image 218: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {K}}=\sum _{i=1}^{n}(x-x_{0})^{i}\partial _{x^{i}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4136a75a63d993b6d03fd557da24304994c3da5f) for ![Image 219: {\displaystyle n=\dim(U)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fbf0925a789fee7b91c2da4d266502f6e960fabb) is inserted into the form ![Image 220: {\displaystyle \beta \in \Lambda ^{*}(U)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/77a59b127916c76b4b23de4e9eae5e5d0f003fd3). We can then define cohomotopy operator as[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hodge_star_operator#cite_note-:0-4)

![Image 221: {\displaystyle h:\Lambda (U)\rightarrow \Lambda (U),\quad h:=\eta {\star }^{-1}H\star }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/96e423802e607d27f212c3f0720a01dcf394c5a1),
where ![Image 222: {\displaystyle \eta \beta =(-1)^{k}\beta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f76a2b0150fb6637ce5a2a0b6813569a151cee9e) for ![Image 223: {\displaystyle \beta \in \Lambda ^{k}(U)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35856c67902d116495d5d6f9ce36b2e9045e2ec1).

The cohomotopy operator fulfills (co)homotopy invariance formula[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hodge_star_operator#cite_note-:0-4)

![Image 224: {\displaystyle \delta h+h\delta =I-S_{x_{0}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/97eea1c74efbc6eb364f41cb3f2d7320d2d797ce)
where ![Image 225: {\displaystyle S_{x_{0}}={\star }^{-1}s_{x_{0}}^{*}{\star }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/941533a4c52bced313fb5fed4cc616db7f78b487), and ![Image 226: {\displaystyle s_{x_{0}}^{*}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8b24f1dcaa83c82e78390b53e2e1ed101b8aab6d) is the [pullback](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pullback_(differential_geometry) "Pullback (differential geometry)") along the constant map ![Image 227: {\displaystyle s_{x_{0}}:x\rightarrow x_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6d3e4d290cff26ac91cb09d7cb3d289de6382fee).

Therefore, if we want to solve the equation ![Image 228: {\displaystyle \delta \omega =0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0b6af8f14e117cafcd4be45970b50eacf4356319), applying cohomotopy invariance formula we get

![Image 229: {\displaystyle \omega =\delta h\omega +S_{x_{0}}\omega ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/31e14b84287866f4aacff1640f3b1681fa11ce23) where ![Image 230: {\displaystyle h\omega \in \Lambda ^{k+1}(U)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/643eae1910601dcb0c39d06c91a70af50a6a9f15) is a differential form we are looking for, and "constant of integration" ![Image 231: {\displaystyle S_{x_{0}}\omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9e0f6b80107dc994eb2bdfce5be3fa9c79173265) vanishes unless ![Image 232: {\displaystyle \omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/48eff443f9de7a985bb94ca3bde20813ea737be8) is a top form.
Cohomotopy operator fulfills the following properties:[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hodge_star_operator#cite_note-:0-4)![Image 233: {\displaystyle h^{2}=0,\quad \delta h\delta =\delta ,\quad h\delta h=h}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/977673ba7940211a44ca0cd7e98a459225bd7c22). They make it possible to use it to define[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hodge_star_operator#cite_note-:0-4)_anticoexact_ forms on ![Image 234: {\displaystyle U}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/458a728f53b9a0274f059cd695e067c430956025) by ![Image 235: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {Y}}(U)=\{\omega \in \Lambda (U)|\omega =h\delta \omega \}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e49121255e32f63894381d5309c202f869060e79), which together with [exact forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exact_form "Exact form")![Image 236: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {C}}(U)=\{\omega \in \Lambda (U)|\omega =\delta h\omega \}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9344ef3525a3728789c04114c20e22aa1e723897) make a [direct sum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direct_sum "Direct sum") decomposition[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hodge_star_operator#cite_note-:0-4)

![Image 237: {\displaystyle \Lambda (U)={\mathcal {C}}(U)\oplus {\mathcal {Y}}(U)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f277224bfd42338550227efeca54a46b5eaa457e).
This direct sum is another way of saying that the cohomotopy invariance formula is a decomposition of unity, and the [projector operators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projector_operator "Projector operator") on the summands fulfills [idempotence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Idempotent_(ring_theory) "Idempotent (ring theory)") formulas:[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hodge_star_operator#cite_note-:0-4)![Image 238: {\displaystyle (h\delta )^{2}=h\delta ,\quad (\delta h)^{2}=\delta h}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cbb4604a07af2adfe8f0f53ca6d605d5004a3fc3).

These results are extension of similar results for exterior derivative.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hodge_star_operator#cite_note-5)
