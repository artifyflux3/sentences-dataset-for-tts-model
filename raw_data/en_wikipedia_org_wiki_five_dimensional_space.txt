A 2D [orthogonal projection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_projection "Orthogonal projection") of a [5-cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/5-cube "5-cube")

A **five-dimensional (5D) space** is a mathematical or physical concept referring to a [space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_(mathematics) "Space (mathematics)") that has five independent [dimensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension "Dimension"). In physics and geometry, such a space extends the familiar three spatial dimensions plus time (4D spacetime) by introducing an additional degree of freedom, which is often used to model advanced theories such as higher-dimensional gravity, extra spatial directions, or connections between different points in spacetime.

Concepts related to five-dimensional spaces include [super-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Superdimension&action=edit&redlink=1 "Superdimension (page does not exist)") or [hyper-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperspace "Hyperspace") spaces, which generally refer to any space with more than four dimensions. These ideas appear in [theoretical physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theoretical_physics "Theoretical physics"), [cosmology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmology "Cosmology"), and [science fiction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Science_fiction "Science fiction") to explore phenomena beyond ordinary perception.

Important related topics include:

*   [5-manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/5-manifold "5-manifold") — a generalization of a surface or volume to five dimensions.
*   [5-cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/5-cube "5-cube") — also called a penteract, a specific five-dimensional hypercube.
*   [Hypersphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypersphere "Hypersphere") — the generalization of a sphere to higher dimensions, including five-dimensional space.
*   [List of regular 5-polytopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_regular_polytopes#Five-dimensional "List of regular polytopes") — regular geometric shapes that exist in five-dimensional space.
*   [Four-dimensional space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Four-dimensional_space "Four-dimensional space") — a foundational step to understanding five-dimensional extensions.

Five-dimensional Euclidean geometry
-----------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Five-dimensional_space&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Five-dimensional Euclidean geometry")]

5D [Euclidean geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_geometry "Euclidean geometry") designated by the mathematical sign: [![Image 2: {\displaystyle \mathbb {E} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ad9faf1fd4a61d36d7f8a2f3204f3805a43c0d4a)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blackboard_bold#Usage "Blackboard bold")5[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Five-dimensional_space#cite_note-1) is dimensions beyond two ([planar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_geometry "Plane geometry")) and three ([solid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_geometry "Solid geometry")). Shapes studied in five dimensions include counterparts of [regular polyhedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polyhedron "Regular polyhedron") and of the [sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere "Sphere").

In five or more dimensions, only three [regular polytopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polytope "Regular polytope") exist. In five dimensions, they are:

*   The **[5-simplex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/5-simplex "5-simplex")** of the [simplex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simplex "Simplex") family, {3,3,3,3}, with 6 vertices, 15 edges, 20 faces (each an [equilateral triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equilateral_triangle "Equilateral triangle")), 15 cells (each a regular [tetrahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron "Tetrahedron")), and 6 hypercells (each a [5-cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/5-cell "5-cell")).
*   The **[5-cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/5-cube "5-cube")** of the [hypercube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypercube "Hypercube") family, {4,3,3,3}, with 32 vertices, 80 edges, 80 faces (each a [square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_(geometry) "Square (geometry)")), 40 cells (each a [cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube "Cube")), and 10 hypercells (each a [tesseract](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tesseract "Tesseract")).
*   The **[5-orthoplex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/5-orthoplex "5-orthoplex")** of the [cross polytope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_polytope "Cross polytope") family, {3,3,3,4}, with 10 vertices, 40 edges, 80 faces (each a [triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle "Triangle")), 80 cells (each a [tetrahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron "Tetrahedron")), and 32 hypercells (each a [5-cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/5-cell "5-cell")).

An important uniform 5-polytope is the [5-demicube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/5-demicube "5-demicube"), h{4,3,3,3} has half the vertices of the 5-cube (16), bounded by alternating [5-cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/5-cell "5-cell") and [16-cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/16-cell "16-cell") hypercells. The [expanded](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expansion_(geometry) "Expansion (geometry)") or [stericated 5-simplex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stericated_5-simplex "Stericated 5-simplex") is the vertex figure of the [A 5 lattice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A5_lattice "A5 lattice"), ![Image 3](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/bd/CDel_node_1.png)![Image 4](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a1/CDel_split1.png)![Image 5](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/1f/CDel_nodes.png)![Image 6](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d5/CDel_3ab.png)![Image 7](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/1f/CDel_nodes.png)![Image 8](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/32/CDel_split2.png)![Image 9](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/5e/CDel_node.png). It and has a doubled symmetry from its symmetric Coxeter diagram. The kissing number of the lattice, 30, is represented in its vertices.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Five-dimensional_space#cite_note-2) The [rectified 5-orthoplex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectified_5-orthoplex "Rectified 5-orthoplex") is the vertex figure of the [D 5 lattice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D5_lattice "D5 lattice"), ![Image 10](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/fc/CDel_nodes_10ru.png)![Image 11](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/32/CDel_split2.png)![Image 12](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/5e/CDel_node.png)![Image 13](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c3/CDel_3.png)![Image 14](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/5e/CDel_node.png)![Image 15](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a1/CDel_split1.png)![Image 16](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/1f/CDel_nodes.png). Its 40 vertices represent the [kissing number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kissing_number "Kissing number") of the lattice and the highest for dimension 5.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Five-dimensional_space#cite_note-3)

Other five-dimensional geometries
---------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Five-dimensional_space&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Other five-dimensional geometries")]

The theory of [special relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_relativity "Special relativity") makes use of [Minkowski spacetime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minkowski_spacetime "Minkowski spacetime"), a type of geometry that locates events in both space and time. The time dimension is mathematically distinguished from the spatial dimensions by a modification in the formula for computing the "distance" between events. Ordinary Minkowski spacetime has four dimensions in all, three of space and one of time. However, higher-dimensional generalizations of the concept have been employed in various proposals. [Kaluza–Klein theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory "Kaluza–Klein theory"), a speculative attempt to develop a unified theory of [gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity "Gravity") and [electromagnetism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetism "Electromagnetism"), relied upon a spacetime with four dimensions of space and one of time.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Five-dimensional_space#cite_note-4)

Geometries can also be constructed in which the coordinates are something other than real numbers. For example, one can define a space in which the points are labeled by [tuples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tuple "Tuple") of 5 [complex numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number"). This is often denoted ![Image 77: {\displaystyle \mathbb {C} ^{5}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/15f23664bd1ceae52ec57c391feb9f96ff81a003). In [quantum information theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_information_theory "Quantum information theory"), quantum systems described by [quantum states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_state "Quantum state") belonging to ![Image 78: {\displaystyle \mathbb {C} ^{5}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/15f23664bd1ceae52ec57c391feb9f96ff81a003) are sometimes called _ququints._[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Five-dimensional_space#cite_note-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Five-dimensional_space#cite_note-6)
