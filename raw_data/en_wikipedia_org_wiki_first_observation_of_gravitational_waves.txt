The **first direct observation of gravitational waves** was made on 14 September 2015 and was announced by the [LIGO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LIGO "LIGO") and [Virgo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgo_interferometer "Virgo interferometer") collaborations on 11 February 2016.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-PRL-20160211-4)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-Nature_11Feb16-5)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-NYT-20160216-6) Previously, [gravitational waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_wave "Gravitational wave") had been inferred only indirectly, via their effect on the timing of [pulsars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulsars "Pulsars") in [binary star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_star "Binary star") systems. The [waveform](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Waveform "Waveform"), detected by both LIGO observatories,[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-BBC_11Feb16-7) matched the predictions of [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity")[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-Pretorius2005-8)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-CampanelliLousto2006-9)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-BakerCentrella2006-10) for a [gravitational wave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_wave "Gravitational wave") emanating from the [inward spiral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inspiral "Inspiral") and [merger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_collision "Stellar collision") of [two black holes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_black_hole "Binary black hole") (of 36 [M☉](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_mass "Solar mass") and 29 M☉) and the subsequent [ringdown](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_black_hole#Ringdown "Binary black hole")[[b]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-12) of a single, 62 M☉ black hole remnant. The signal was named **GW150914** (from _gravitational wave_ and the date of observation 2015-09-14).[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-PRL-20160211-4)[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-Naeye-13) It was also the first observation of a binary black hole merger, demonstrating both the existence of binary [stellar-mass black hole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar-mass_black_hole "Stellar-mass black hole") systems and the fact that such mergers could occur within the current [age of the universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Age_of_the_universe "Age of the universe").

This first direct observation was reported around the world as a remarkable accomplishment for many reasons. Efforts to directly prove the existence of such waves had been ongoing for over fifty years, and the waves are so minuscule that [Albert Einstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Einstein "Albert Einstein") himself doubted that they could ever be detected.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-14)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-renn-15) The waves given off by the cataclysmic merger of GW150914 reached Earth as a ripple in [spacetime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spacetime "Spacetime") that changed the length of a 1,120 km LIGO effective span by a thousandth of the width of a [proton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton "Proton"),[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-Naeye-13) proportionally equivalent to changing the distance to [the nearest star outside the Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proxima_Centauri "Proxima Centauri") by one hair's width.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-Guardian_11Feb16-16)[[c]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-17) The energy released by the binary as it spiralled together and merged was immense, with the energy of 3.0+0.5

−0.5 _c_ 2 M☉ (5.3+0.9

−0.8×10 47 joules or 5300+900

−800[foes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foe_(unit) "Foe (unit)")) in total radiated as gravitational waves, reaching a peak emission rate in its final few milliseconds of about 3.6+0.5

−0.4×10 49[watts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Watt "Watt") – a level greater than the combined [power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_(physics) "Power (physics)") of all light radiated by all the stars in the [observable universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe "Observable universe").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-PRL-20160211-4)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-Nature_11Feb16-5)[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-CBS2016-02-11-18)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-Drake-19)[[d]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-21)

The observation confirmed the last remaining directly undetected prediction of [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity") and corroborated its predictions of space-time distortion in the context of large scale cosmic events (known as [strong field tests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity "Tests of general relativity")). It was heralded as inaugurating a new era of [gravitational-wave astronomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational-wave_astronomy "Gravitational-wave astronomy"), which enables observations of violent astrophysical events that were not previously possible and allows for the direct observation of the earliest [history of the universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chronology_of_the_universe "Chronology of the universe").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-PRL-20160211-4)[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-tests-22)[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-astrophysics-23)[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-CNN-A-24)[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-BBC-A-25) On 15 June 2016, two more detections of gravitational waves, made in late 2015, were announced.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-NYT-20160615-26)[Eight more observations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_gravitational_wave_observations "List of gravitational wave observations") were made in 2017, including [GW170817](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GW170817 "GW170817"), the first observed merger of binary [neutron stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron_star "Neutron star"), which was also observed in [electromagnetic radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation "Electromagnetic radiation").

Gravitational waves
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=First_observation_of_gravitational_waves&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Gravitational waves")]

Video simulation showing the warping of [space-time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space-time "Space-time") and gravitational waves produced, during the final inspiral, merge, and ringdown of black hole binary system GW150914[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-SXSproject-27)

[Albert Einstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Einstein "Albert Einstein") predicted the existence of gravitational waves in 1916,[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-28)[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-29) on the basis of his theory of [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity").[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-30) General relativity interprets [gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity "Gravity") as a consequence of distortions in [spacetime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spacetime "Spacetime") caused by the presence of [mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass "Mass"), and further entails that [certain movements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrupole_formula "Quadrupole formula") or acceleration of these masses will cause distortions – or "ripples" – in spacetime which spread outward from the source at the speed of light. Einstein considered this mostly a curiosity, since he understood that these ripples would be far too minuscule to detect using any technology foreseen at that time.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-renn-15) As a further consequence following from the [conservation of energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservation_of_energy "Conservation of energy"), the energy radiated away by gravitational waves from a system of two objects in mutual orbit would cause them to slowly spiral inwards, although again, this effect would be extremely minute and thus challenging to observe.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-31)

One case where gravitational waves would be strongest is during the final moments of the merger of two [compact objects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compact_star "Compact star") such as [neutron stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron_star "Neutron star") or [black holes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_hole "Black hole"). Over a span of millions of years, [binary neutron stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_star "Binary star"), and [binary black holes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_black_hole "Binary black hole") lose energy, largely through gravitational waves, and as a result, they [spiral in](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_decay#Gravitational_radiation "Orbital decay") towards each other. At the very end of this process, the two objects will reach extreme velocities, and in the final fraction of a second of their merger a substantial amount of their mass would theoretically be converted into gravitational energy, and travel outward as gravitational waves,[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-PhysicsWorld-32) allowing a greater than usual chance for detection. However, since little was known about the number of compact binaries in the universe and reaching that final stage can be very slow, there was little certainty as to how often such events might happen.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-33)

Slow motion computer simulation of the black hole binary system GW150914 as seen by a nearby observer, during 0.33 s of its final inspiral, merge, and ringdown. The star field behind the black holes is being heavily distorted and appears to rotate and move, due to extreme [gravitational lensing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_lensing "Gravitational lensing"), as [space-time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space-time "Space-time") itself is distorted and dragged around by the rotating black holes.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-SXSproject-27)

Gravitational waves can be detected indirectly – by observing celestial phenomena caused by gravitational waves – or more directly by means of instruments such as the Earth-based [LIGO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LIGO "LIGO") or the planned space-based [LISA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laser_Interferometer_Space_Antenna "Laser Interferometer Space Antenna") instrument.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-34)

#### Indirect observation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=First_observation_of_gravitational_waves&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Indirect observation")]

Evidence of gravitational waves was first deduced in 1974 through the motion of the double neutron star system [PSR B1913+16](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PSR_B1913%2B16 "PSR B1913+16"), in which one of the stars is a [pulsar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulsar "Pulsar") that emits electro-magnetic pulses at radio frequencies at precise, regular intervals as it rotates. [Russell Hulse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russell_Alan_Hulse "Russell Alan Hulse") and [Joseph Taylor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Hooton_Taylor,_Jr. "Joseph Hooton Taylor, Jr."), who discovered the stars, also showed that over time, the frequency of pulses shortened, and that the stars were gradually spiralling towards each other with an energy loss that agreed closely with the predicted energy that would be radiated by gravitational waves.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-35)[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-36) For this work, Hulse and Taylor were awarded the [Nobel Prize in Physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physics "Nobel Prize in Physics") in 1993.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-37) Further observations of this pulsar and others in multiple systems (such as the [double pulsar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double_pulsar "Double pulsar") system [PSR J0737-3039](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PSR_J0737-3039 "PSR J0737-3039")) also agree with General Relativity to high precision.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-38)[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-39)

[![Image 3](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Northern_leg_of_LIGO_interferometer_on_Hanford_Reservation.JPG/250px-Northern_leg_of_LIGO_interferometer_on_Hanford_Reservation.JPG)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Northern_leg_of_LIGO_interferometer_on_Hanford_Reservation.JPG)

Northern arm of the LIGO Hanford [Gravitational-wave observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational-wave_observatory "Gravitational-wave observatory")

Direct observation of gravitational waves was not possible for many decades following their prediction, due to the minuscule effect that would need to be detected and separated from the background of vibrations present everywhere on Earth. A technique called [interferometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interferometry "Interferometry") was suggested in the 1960s and eventually technology developed sufficiently for this technique to become feasible.

In the present approach used by LIGO, a [laser beam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laser_beam "Laser beam") is split and the two halves are recombined after traveling different paths. Changes to the length of the paths or the time taken for the two split beams, caused by the effect of passing gravitational waves, to reach the point where they recombine are revealed as "[beats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beat_(acoustics) "Beat (acoustics)")". Such a technique is extremely sensitive to tiny changes in the distance or time taken to traverse the two paths. In theory, an interferometer with arms about 4 km long would be capable of revealing the change of space-time – a tiny fraction of the size of a single [proton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton "Proton") – as a gravitational wave of sufficient strength passed through Earth from elsewhere. This effect would be perceptible only to other interferometers of a similar size, such as the [Virgo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgo_interferometer "Virgo interferometer"), [GEO 600](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GEO_600 "GEO 600") and planned [KAGRA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/KAGRA "KAGRA") and [INDIGO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/INDIGO "INDIGO") detectors. In practice at least two interferometers would be needed because any gravitational wave would be detected at both of these, but other kinds of disturbances would generally not be present at both. This technique allows the sought-after signal to be distinguished from [noise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noise "Noise"). This project was eventually founded in 1992 as the [Laser Interferometer Gravitational-Wave Observatory (LIGO)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LIGO "LIGO"). The original instruments were upgraded between 2010 and 2015 (to Advanced LIGO), giving an increase of around 10 times their original sensitivity.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-40)

LIGO operates two [gravitational-wave observatories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational-wave_observatory "Gravitational-wave observatory") in unison, located 3,002 km (1,865 mi) apart: the LIGO Livingston Observatory ([30°33′46.42″N 90°46′27.27″W / 30.5628944°N 90.7742417°W](https://geohack.toolforge.org/geohack.php?pagename=First_observation_of_gravitational_waves&params=30_33_46.42_N_90_46_27.27_W_)) in [Livingston, Louisiana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Livingston,_Louisiana "Livingston, Louisiana"), and the LIGO Hanford Observatory, on the [DOE Hanford Site](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hanford_Site "Hanford Site") ([46°27′18.52″N 119°24′27.56″W / 46.4551444°N 119.4076556°W](https://geohack.toolforge.org/geohack.php?pagename=First_observation_of_gravitational_waves&params=46_27_18.52_N_119_24_27.56_W_)) near [Richland, Washington](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richland,_Washington "Richland, Washington"). The tiny shifts in the length of their arms are continually compared and significant patterns which appear to arise synchronously are followed up to determine whether a gravitational wave may have been detected or if some other cause was responsible.

Initial LIGO operations between 2002 and 2010 did not detect any statistically significant events that could be confirmed as gravitational waves. This was followed by a multi-year shut-down while the detectors were replaced by much improved "Advanced LIGO" versions.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-41)In February 2015, the two advanced detectors were brought into engineering mode, in which the instruments are operating fully for the purpose of testing and confirming they are functioning correctly before being used for research,[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-42) with formal science observations due to begin on 18 September 2015.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-43)

Throughout the development and initial observations by LIGO, several "blind injections" of fake gravitational wave signals were introduced to test the ability of the researchers to identify such signals. To protect the efficacy of blind injections, only four LIGO scientists knew when such injections occurred, and that information was revealed only after a signal had been thoroughly analyzed by researchers.[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-firstperson-44) On 14 September 2015, while LIGO was running in engineering mode but without any blind data injections, the instrument reported a possible gravitational wave detection. The detected event was given the name GW150914.[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-Nature12Jan16-45)

GW150914 was detected by the LIGO detectors in [Hanford, Washington](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hanford,_Washington "Hanford, Washington") state, and [Livingston, Louisiana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Livingston,_Louisiana "Livingston, Louisiana"), USA, at 9:50:45 [UTC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UTC "UTC") on 14 September 2015.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-Nature_11Feb16-5)[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-Naeye-13) The LIGO detectors were operating in "engineering mode", meaning that they were operating fully but had not yet begun a formal "research" phase (which was due to commence three days later on 18 September), so initially there was a question as to whether the signals had been real detections or simulated data for testing purposes before it was ascertained that they were not tests.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-Nature16-46)

The [chirp signal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chirp "Chirp") lasted over 0.2 seconds, and increased in frequency and amplitude in about 8 cycles from 35 Hz to 250 Hz.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-PRL-20160211-4) The signal is in the [audible range](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Audible_range "Audible range") and has been described as resembling the ["chirp" of a bird](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bird_vocalization "Bird vocalization");[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-Nature_11Feb16-5) astrophysicists and other interested parties the world over excitedly responded by imitating the signal on [social media](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_media "Social media") upon the announcement of the discovery.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-Nature_11Feb16-5)[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-NYT-20160211-mr-47)[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-48)[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-49) (The frequency increases because each [orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbit "Orbit") is noticeably faster than the one before during the final moments before merging.)

The trigger that indicated a possible detection was reported within three minutes of acquisition of the signal, using rapid ('online') search methods that provide a quick, initial analysis of the data from the detectors.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-PRL-20160211-4) After the initial automatic alert at 9:54 UTC, a sequence of internal emails confirmed that no scheduled or unscheduled injections had been made, and that the data looked clean.[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-firstperson-44)[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-newyorker-50) After this, the rest of the collaborating team was quickly made aware of the tentative detection and its parameters.[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-mpg1-51)

More detailed statistical analysis of the signal, and of 16 days of surrounding data from 12 September to 20 October 2015, identified GW150914 as a real event, with an estimated significance of at least [5.1 sigma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_deviation "Standard deviation")[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-PRL-20160211-4) or a [confidence level](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confidence_level "Confidence level") of 99.99994%.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-52) Corresponding wave peaks were seen at Livingston seven milliseconds before they arrived at Hanford. Gravitational waves propagate at the [speed of light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light "Speed of light"), and the disparity is consistent with the light travel time between the two sites.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-PRL-20160211-4) The waves had traveled at the speed of light for more than a billion years.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-SA-20160212-53)

At the time of the event, the [Virgo gravitational wave detector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgo_interferometer "Virgo interferometer") (near Pisa, [Italy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italy "Italy")) was offline and undergoing an upgrade; had it been online it would likely have been sensitive enough to also detect the signal, which would have greatly improved the positioning of the event.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-Nature_11Feb16-5)[GEO600](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GEO600 "GEO600") (near [Hannover](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hannover "Hannover"), Germany) was not sensitive enough to detect the signal.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-PRL-20160211-4) Consequently, neither of those detectors was able to confirm the signal measured by the LIGO detectors.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-Nature_11Feb16-5)

### Astrophysical origin

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=First_observation_of_gravitational_waves&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Astrophysical origin")]

[![Image 4](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/MergingBlackHoles_V2.jpg/250px-MergingBlackHoles_V2.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:MergingBlackHoles_V2.jpg)

Simulation of merging black holes radiating gravitational waves

The event happened at a [luminosity distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luminosity_distance "Luminosity distance") of 440+160

−180[megaparsecs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Megaparsec "Megaparsec")[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-Improved-1): 6  (determined by the amplitude of the signal),[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-Nature_11Feb16-5) or 1.4±0.6 billion [light years](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_year "Light year"), corresponding to a cosmological [redshift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift "Redshift") of 0.093+0.030

−0.036 (90% [credible intervals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Credible_interval "Credible interval")). Analysis of the signal along with the inferred redshift suggested that it was produced by the merger of two [black holes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_hole "Black hole") with masses of 35+5

−3 times and 30+3

−4 times the mass of the [Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun "Sun") (in the source frame), resulting in a post-merger black hole of 62+4

−3 M☉.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-Improved-1): 6  The [mass–energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass%E2%80%93energy_equivalence "Mass–energy equivalence") of the missing 3.0±0.5 M☉ was radiated away in the form of gravitational waves.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-PRL-20160211-4)

During the final 20 milliseconds of the merger, the power of the radiated gravitational waves peaked at about 3.6×10 49 watts or 526 [dBm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DBm "DBm") – 50 times greater[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-54) than the combined power of all light radiated by all the stars in the [observable universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe "Observable universe").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-PRL-20160211-4)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-Nature_11Feb16-5)[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-CBS2016-02-11-18)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-Drake-19) The amount of this energy that was received by the entire planet Earth was about 36 billion joules, of which only a small amount was absorbed.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-55)

Across the 0.2-second duration of the detectable signal, the relative tangential (orbiting) velocity of the black holes increased from 30% to 60% of the [speed of light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light "Speed of light"). The orbital frequency of 75 Hz (half the gravitational wave frequency) means that the objects were orbiting each other at a distance of only 350 km by the time they merged. The [phase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phase_(waves) "Phase (waves)") changes to the signal's [polarization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polarization_(waves) "Polarization (waves)") allowed calculation of the objects' orbital frequency, and taken together with the [amplitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amplitude "Amplitude") and pattern of the signal, allowed calculation of their masses and therefore their extreme final velocities and orbital separation (distance apart) when they merged. That information showed that the objects had to be black holes, as any other kind of known objects with these masses would have been physically larger and therefore merged before that point, or would not have reached such velocities in such a small orbit. The highest observed neutron star mass is 2 M☉, with a conservative [upper limit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tolman%E2%80%93Oppenheimer%E2%80%93Volkoff_limit "Tolman–Oppenheimer–Volkoff limit") for the mass of a stable neutron star of 3 M☉, so that a pair of neutron stars would not have had sufficient mass to account for the merger (unless exotic alternatives exist, for example, [boson stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exotic_star#Boson_stars "Exotic star")),[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-Properties-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-PRL-20160211-4) while a black hole-[neutron star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron_star "Neutron star") pair would have merged sooner, resulting in a final orbital frequency that was not so high.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-PRL-20160211-4)

The decay of the waveform after it peaked was consistent with the damped oscillations of a black hole as it relaxed to a final merged configuration.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-PRL-20160211-4) Although the inspiral motion of compact binaries can be described well from [post-Newtonian calculations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Post-Newtonian_expansion "Post-Newtonian expansion"),[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-56) the strong gravitational field merger stage can only be solved in full generality by large-scale [numerical relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Numerical_relativity "Numerical relativity") simulations.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-57)[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-58)[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-59)

In the improved model and analysis, the post-merger object is found to be a [rotating Kerr black hole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotating_black_hole "Rotating black hole") with a spin parameter of 0.68+0.05

−0.06,[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-Improved-1) i.e. one with 2/3 of the [maximum possible angular momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_hole#Physical_properties "Black hole") for its mass.

The two stars which formed the two black holes were likely formed about 2 billion years after the [Big Bang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang "Big Bang") with masses of between 40 and 100 times the mass of the [Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun "Sun").[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-:0-60)[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-:1-61)

### Location in the sky

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=First_observation_of_gravitational_waves&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Location in the sky")]

Gravitational wave instruments are whole-sky monitors with little ability to resolve signals spatially. A network of such instruments is needed to locate the source in the sky through [triangulation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangulation "Triangulation"). With only the two LIGO instruments in observational mode, GW150914's source location could only be confined to an arc on the sky. This was done via analysis of the 6.9+0.5

−0.4 ms time-delay, along with amplitude and phase consistency across both detectors. This analysis produced a credible region of 150 deg 2 with a probability of 50% or 610 deg 2 with a probability of 90% located mainly in the [Southern Celestial Hemisphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southern_Celestial_Hemisphere "Southern Celestial Hemisphere"),[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-Properties-2): 7 : fig 4  in the rough direction of (but much farther than) the [Magellanic Clouds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magellanic_Clouds "Magellanic Clouds").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-Nature_11Feb16-5)[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-Naeye-13)

For [comparison, the area of the constellation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/88_modern_constellations_by_area "88 modern constellations by area")[Orion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(constellation) "Orion (constellation)") is 594 deg 2.[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-RASCconstellations-62)

### Coincident gamma-ray observation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=First_observation_of_gravitational_waves&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Coincident gamma-ray observation")]

The [Fermi Gamma-ray Space Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermi_Gamma-ray_Space_Telescope "Fermi Gamma-ray Space Telescope") reported that its Gamma-Ray Burst Monitor (GBM) instrument detected a weak [gamma-ray burst](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamma-ray_burst "Gamma-ray burst") above 50 keV, starting 0.4 seconds after the LIGO event and with a positional uncertainty region overlapping that of the LIGO observation. The Fermi team calculated the odds of such an event being the result of a coincidence or noise at 0.22%.[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-fermi_arxiv-63) However a gamma ray burst would not have been expected, and observations from the [INTEGRAL](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/INTEGRAL "INTEGRAL") telescope's all-sky SPI-ACS instrument indicated that any energy emission in gamma-rays and hard X-rays from the event was less than one millionth of the energy emitted as gravitational waves, which "excludes the possibility that the event is associated with substantial gamma-ray radiation, directed towards the observer". If the signal observed by the Fermi GBM was genuinely astrophysical, INTEGRAL would have indicated a clear detection at a significance of 15 sigma above background radiation.[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-Integral-64) The [AGILE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AGILE_(satellite) "AGILE (satellite)") space telescope also did not detect a gamma-ray counterpart of the event.[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-AGILE_4Apr16-65)

A follow-up analysis by an independent group, released in June 2016, developed a different statistical approach to estimate the spectrum of the gamma-ray transient. It concluded that Fermi GBM's data did not show evidence of a gamma ray burst, and was either background radiation or an Earth albedo transient on a 1-second timescale.[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-siegel-66)[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-greiner-67) A rebuttal of this follow-up analysis, however, pointed out that the independent group misrepresented the analysis of the original Fermi GBM Team paper and therefore misconstrued the results of the original analysis. The rebuttal reaffirmed that the false coincidence probability is calculated empirically and is not refuted by the independent analysis.[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-Connaughton2018-68)[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-Siegel2018-69)

Black hole mergers of the type thought to have produced the gravitational wave event are not expected to produce gamma-ray bursts, as stellar-mass black hole binaries are not expected to have large amounts of orbiting matter. [Avi Loeb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Avi_Loeb "Avi Loeb") has theorised that if a massive star is rapidly rotating, the centrifugal force produced during its collapse will lead to the formation of a rotating bar that breaks into two dense clumps of matter with a dumbbell configuration that becomes a black hole binary, and at the end of the star's collapse it triggers a gamma-ray burst.[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-fermi_woo-70)[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-Loeb-71) Loeb suggests that the 0.4 second delay is the time it took the gamma-ray burst to cross the star, relative to the gravitational waves.[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-Loeb-71)[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-GRB_on_BBH-72)

### Other follow-up observations

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=First_observation_of_gravitational_waves&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Other follow-up observations")]

The reconstructed source area was targeted by follow-up observations covering [radio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radio_astronomy "Radio astronomy"), [optical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visible_light_astronomy "Visible light astronomy"), [near infra-red](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infrared_astronomy "Infrared astronomy"), [X-ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray_astronomy "X-ray astronomy"), and [gamma-ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamma-ray_astronomy "Gamma-ray astronomy") wavelengths along with searches for coincident [neutrinos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutrino "Neutrino").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-Properties-2) However, because LIGO had not yet started its science run, notice to other telescopes was delayed.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

The [ANTARES](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ANTARES_(telescope) "ANTARES (telescope)") telescope detected no neutrino candidates within ±500 seconds of GW150914. The [IceCube Neutrino Observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IceCube_Neutrino_Observatory "IceCube Neutrino Observatory") detected three neutrino candidates within ±500 seconds of GW150914. One event was found in the southern sky and two in the northern sky. This was consistent with the expectation of background detection levels. None of the candidates were compatible with the 90% confidence area of the merger event.[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-LIGO-P1500271-73) Although no neutrinos were detected, the lack of such observations provided a limit on neutrino emission from this type of gravitational wave event.[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-LIGO-P1500271-73)

Observations by the [Swift Gamma-Ray Burst Mission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swift_Gamma-Ray_Burst_Mission "Swift Gamma-Ray Burst Mission") of nearby galaxies in the region of the detection, two days after the event, did not detect any new X-ray, optical or ultraviolet sources.[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-arXiv:1602.03868-74)

The observation was heralded as inaugurating a revolutionary era of [gravitational-wave astronomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational-wave_astronomy "Gravitational-wave astronomy").[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-Black_Holes,_Cosmic_Collisions_and_the_Rippling_of_Spacetime-85) Prior to this detection, astrophysicists and cosmologists were only able to make observations based upon [electromagnetic radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation "Electromagnetic radiation") (including visible light, X-rays, microwave, radio waves, gamma rays) and particle-like entities ([cosmic rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_ray "Cosmic ray"), [stellar winds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_wind "Stellar wind"), [neutrinos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutrino "Neutrino"), and so on). These have significant limitations – light and other radiation may not be emitted by many kinds of objects, and can also be obscured or hidden behind other objects. Objects such as galaxies and nebulae can also absorb, re-emit, or modify light generated within or behind them, and [compact stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compact_star "Compact star") or [exotic stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exotic_star "Exotic star") may contain material which is dark and radio silent, and as a result there is little evidence of their presence other than through their gravitational interactions.[[82]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-86)[[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-87)

### Expectations for detection of future binary merger events

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=First_observation_of_gravitational_waves&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Expectations for detection of future binary merger events")]

On 15 June 2016, the [LIGO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LIGO "LIGO") group announced an observation of another gravitational wave signal, named [GW151226](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GW151226 "GW151226").[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-88) The Advanced LIGO was predicted to detect five more black hole mergers like GW150914 in its next observing campaign from November 2016 until August 2017 (it turned out to be [seven](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_gravitational_wave_observations "List of gravitational wave observations")), and then 40 binary star mergers each year, in addition to an unknown number of more exotic gravitational wave sources, some of which may not be anticipated by current theory.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-Naeye-13)

Planned upgrades are expected to double the [signal-to-noise ratio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Signal-to-noise_ratio "Signal-to-noise ratio"), expanding the volume of space in which events like GW150914 can be detected by a factor of ten. Additionally, Advanced Virgo, [KAGRA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/KAGRA "KAGRA"), and a possible third LIGO detector in India will extend the network and significantly improve the position reconstruction and parameter estimation of sources.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-PRL-20160211-4)

[Laser Interferometer Space Antenna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laser_Interferometer_Space_Antenna "Laser Interferometer Space Antenna") (LISA) is a proposed space based observation mission to detect gravitational waves. With the proposed sensitivity range of LISA, merging binaries like GW150914 would be detectable about 1000 years before they merge, providing for a class of previously unknown sources for this observatory if they exist within about 10 megaparsecs.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-astrophysics-23)[LISA Pathfinder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LISA_Pathfinder "LISA Pathfinder"), LISA's technology development mission, was launched in December 2015 and it demonstrated that the LISA mission is feasible.[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-89)

A 2016 model predicted LIGO would detect approximately 1000 black hole mergers per year when it reached full sensitivity following upgrades.[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-:0-60)[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-:1-61)

### Lessons for stellar evolution and astrophysics

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=First_observation_of_gravitational_waves&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: Lessons for stellar evolution and astrophysics")]

The masses of the two pre-merger black holes provide information about [stellar evolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_evolution "Stellar evolution"). Both black holes were more massive than previously discovered [stellar-mass black holes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_black_hole "Stellar black hole"), which were inferred from [X-ray binary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray_binary "X-ray binary") observations. This implies that the [stellar winds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_wind "Stellar wind") from their progenitor stars must have been relatively weak, and therefore that the [metallicity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metallicity "Metallicity") (mass fraction of chemical elements heavier than hydrogen and helium) must have been less than about half the solar value.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-astrophysics-23)

The fact that the pre-merger black holes were present in a [binary star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_star "Binary star") system, as well as the fact that the system was compact enough to merge within the age of the universe, constrains either binary star evolution or [dynamical formation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_dynamics "Stellar dynamics") scenarios, depending on how the black hole binary was formed. A significant number of black holes must receive low [natal kicks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_black_hole#Black_hole_kicks "Stellar black hole") (the velocity a black hole gains at its formation in a [core-collapse supernova](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Core-collapse_supernova "Core-collapse supernova") event), otherwise the black hole forming in a binary star system would be ejected and an event like GW would be prevented.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-astrophysics-23) The survival of such binaries, through common envelope phases of high rotation in massive progenitor stars, may be necessary for their survival.[_[clarification needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Please\_clarify "Wikipedia:Please clarify")_] The majority of the latest black hole model predictions comply with these added constraints.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

The discovery of the GW merger event increases the lower limit on the rate of such events, and rules out certain theoretical models that predicted very low rates of less than 1 Gpc−3 yr−1 (one event per cubic gigaparsec per year).[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-PRL-20160211-4)[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-astrophysics-23) Analysis resulted in lowering the previous upper limit rate on events like GW150914 from ~140 Gpc−3 yr−1 to 17+39

−13 Gpc−3 yr−1.[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-BlackHoleRate-90)

### Impact on future cosmological observation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=First_observation_of_gravitational_waves&action=edit&section=16 "Edit section: Impact on future cosmological observation")]

Measurement of the waveform and amplitude of the gravitational waves from a black hole merger event makes accurate determination of its distance possible. The accumulation of black hole merger data from cosmologically distant events may help to create more precise models of the history of the expansion of the universe and the nature of the [dark energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_energy "Dark energy") that influences it.[[87]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-O'Neill2016-91)[[88]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-Cooper2016-92)

The [earliest universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recombination_(cosmology) "Recombination (cosmology)") is opaque since the cosmos was so energetic then that most matter was ionized and photons were scattered by free electrons.[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-93) However, this opacity would not affect gravitational waves from that time, so if they occurred at levels strong enough to be detected at this distance, it would allow a window to observe the cosmos beyond the current [visible universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visible_universe "Visible universe"). Gravitational-wave astronomy therefore may some day allow direct observation of the earliest [history of the universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chronology_of_the_universe "Chronology of the universe").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-PRL-20160211-4)[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-tests-22)[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-astrophysics-23)[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-CNN-A-24)[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-BBC-A-25)

### Tests of general relativity

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=First_observation_of_gravitational_waves&action=edit&section=17 "Edit section: Tests of general relativity")]

The inferred fundamental properties, mass and spin, of the post-merger black hole were consistent with those of the two pre-merger black holes, following the predictions of general relativity.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-Pretorius2005-8)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-CampanelliLousto2006-9)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-BakerCentrella2006-10) This is the first test of general relativity in the very [strong-field regime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#Strong_field_tests:_Binary_pulsars "Tests of general relativity").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-PRL-20160211-4)[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-tests-22) No evidence could be established against the predictions of general relativity.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-tests-22)

The opportunity was limited in this signal to investigate the more complex general relativity interactions, such as tails produced by interactions between the gravitational wave and curved space-time background. Although a moderately strong signal, it is much smaller than that produced by binary-pulsar systems. In the future stronger signals, in conjunction with more sensitive detectors, could be used to explore the intricate interactions of gravitational waves as well as to improve the constraints on deviations from general relativity.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-tests-22)

### Speed of gravitational waves and limit on possible mass of graviton

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=First_observation_of_gravitational_waves&action=edit&section=18 "Edit section: Speed of gravitational waves and limit on possible mass of graviton")]

The speed of gravitational waves (_**v g**_) is predicted by general relativity to be the speed of light (_**c**_).[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-94) The extent of any deviation from this relationship can be parameterized in terms of the mass of the hypothetical [graviton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graviton "Graviton"). The graviton is the name given to an [elementary particle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementary_particle "Elementary particle") that would act as the [force carrier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Force_carrier "Force carrier") for gravity, in [quantum theories about gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_gravity "Quantum gravity"). It is expected to be massless if, as it appears, gravitation has an infinite range. (This is because the more massive a [gauge boson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauge_boson "Gauge boson") is, the shorter is the range of the associated force; as with the infinite range of [electromagnetism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetism "Electromagnetism"), which is due to the massless [photon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon "Photon"), the infinite range of gravity implies that any associated force-carrying particle would also be massless.) If the graviton were not massless, gravitational waves would propagate below lightspeed, with lower frequencies (_**ƒ**_) being slower than higher frequencies, leading to dispersion of the waves from the merger event.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-tests-22) No such dispersion was observed.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-tests-22)[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-PhysicsWorld-32) The observations of the inspiral slightly improve (lower) the upper limit on the mass of the graviton from Solar System observations to 2.1×10−58 kg, corresponding to 1.2×10−22[eV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronvolt "Electronvolt")/_c_ 2 or a [Compton wavelength](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compton_wavelength "Compton wavelength") (_**λ g**_) of greater than 10 13 km, roughly 1 light-year.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-PRL-20160211-4)[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-tests-22) Using the lowest observed frequency of 35 Hz, this translates to a lower limit on _**v g**_ such that the upper limit on **1-_v g_ /_c_** is ~4×10−19.[[e]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves#cite_note-95)
