Chemical compound

**Tricarbon** (systematically named **1λ 2,3λ 2-propadiene** and **_catena_-tricarbon**) is an [inorganic compound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inorganic_compound "Inorganic compound") with the [chemical formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_formula "Chemical formula")C

2(μ-C) (also written [C(μ-C)C] or C

3). It is a colourless gas that only persists in dilution or solution as an [adduct](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adduct "Adduct"). It is one of the simplest [unsaturated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saturated_and_unsaturated_compounds "Saturated and unsaturated compounds")[carbenes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbene "Carbene"). Tricarbon can be found in interstellar space and can be produced in the laboratory by a process called [laser ablation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laser_ablation "Laser ablation").

Natural occurrence
------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tricarbon&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Natural occurrence")]

Tricarbon is a small [carbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon "Carbon") cluster first [spectroscopically](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopy "Spectroscopy") observed in the early 20th century in the tail of a comet by [William Huggins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Huggins "William Huggins") and subsequently identified in [stellar atmospheres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_atmosphere "Stellar atmosphere"). Small carbon clusters like tricarbon and [dicarbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dicarbon "Dicarbon") are regarded as soot precursors and are implicated in the formation of certain industrial [diamonds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diamond "Diamond") and in the formation of [fullerenes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fullerene "Fullerene").

C 3 has also been identified as a transient species in various [combustion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Combustion "Combustion") reactions.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tricarbon#cite_note-1): 218–220, plate 20

Properties
----------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tricarbon&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Properties")]

### Chemical properties

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tricarbon&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Chemical properties")]

The chemical properties of C 3 was investigated in the 1960s by Professor Emeritus [Philip S. Skell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philip_Skell "Philip Skell") of [Pennsylvania State University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pennsylvania_State_University "Pennsylvania State University"), who showed that certain reactions of carbon vapor indicated its generation, such as the reaction with isobutylene to produce 1,1,1',1'-tetramethyl-bis-ethanoallene.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tricarbon#cite_note-Skell-2)

### Physical properties

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tricarbon&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Physical properties")]

The ground state [molecular geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_geometry "Molecular geometry") of tricarbon has been identified as linear via its characteristic symmetric and antisymmetric stretching and bending vibrational modes and bears [bond lengths](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bond_length "Bond length") of 129 to 130 [picometer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Picometer "Picometer") corresponding to those of [alkenes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkene "Alkene"). The [ionization potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ionization_potential "Ionization potential") is determined experimentally at 11 to 13.5 [electronvolts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronvolt "Electronvolt").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tricarbon#cite_note-Nicolas-3) In contrast to the linear tricarbon molecule, the C+

3[cation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cation "Cation") is bent.

Nomenclature
------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tricarbon&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Nomenclature")]

The systematic names _1λ 2,3λ 2-propadiene_, and _μ-carbidodicarbon_, valid [IUPAC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IUPAC "IUPAC") names, are constructed according to the substitutive and additive nomenclatures, respectively.

In appropriate contexts, tricarbon can be viewed as [propadiene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propadiene "Propadiene") with four hydrogen atoms removed, or as propane with eight hydrogen atoms removed; and as such, _propadienediylidene_ or _propanetetraylidene_, respectively, may be used as a context-specific systematic names, according to substitutive nomenclature. By default, these names pay no regard to the radicality of the tricarbon molecule. In even more specific context, these can also name the non-radical singlet state, whereas the diradical state is named _propadienediylylidene_, or _propanediyldiylidene_, and the tetraradical state is named _propedienetetrayl_ or _propanetetraylylidene_.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tricarbon#cite_note-Skell-2)
