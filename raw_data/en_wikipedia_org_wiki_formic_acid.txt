**Formic acid** (from [Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin_language "Latin language")_formica_'[ant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ant "Ant")'), [systematically named](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Preferred_IUPAC_name "Preferred IUPAC name")**methanoic acid**, is the simplest [carboxylic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carboxylic_acid "Carboxylic acid"). It has the [chemical formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_formula "Chemical formula") HCOOH and structure H−C(=O)−O−H. This acid is an important intermediate in [chemical synthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_synthesis "Chemical synthesis") and occurs naturally, most notably in some ants. [Esters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ester "Ester"), [salts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salt_(chemistry) "Salt (chemistry)"), and the [anion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anion "Anion") derived from formic acid are called [formates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formate "Formate"). Industrially, formic acid is produced from [methanol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol "Methanol").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-5)

Formic acid, which has a pungent, penetrating odor, is found naturally in insects, weeds, fruits and vegetables, and forest emissions. It appears in most [ants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ants "Ants") and in [stingless bees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stingless_bee "Stingless bee") of the genus _[Oxytrigona](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxytrigona "Oxytrigona")_.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-7)[Wood ants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formica_rufa_species_group "Formica rufa species group") from the genus _[Formica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formica "Formica")_ can spray formic acid on their prey or to defend the nest. The [puss moth caterpillar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cerura_vinula "Cerura vinula") (_Cerura vinula_) will spray it as well when threatened by predators. It is also found in the [trichomes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trichome "Trichome") of [stinging nettle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stinging_nettle "Stinging nettle") (_Urtica dioica_). Apart from that, this acid is incorporated in many fruits such as pineapple (0.21 mg per 100 g), apple (2 mg per 100 g) and kiwi (1 mg per 100 g), as well as in many vegetables, namely onion (45 mg per 100 g), eggplant (1.34 mg per 100 g) and, in extremely low concentrations, cucumber (0.11 mg per 100 g).[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-8) Formic acid is a naturally occurring component of the [atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth "Atmosphere of Earth") primarily due to forest emissions.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-9)

As early as the 15th century, some [alchemists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alchemy "Alchemy") and [naturalists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_history "Natural history") were aware that ant hills give off an acidic vapor. The first person to describe the isolation of this substance (by the distillation of large numbers of ants) was the English naturalist [John Ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Ray "John Ray"), in 1671.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-10)[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-11) Ants secrete the formic acid for attack and defense purposes. Formic acid was first synthesized from [hydrocyanic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrocyanic_acid "Hydrocyanic acid") by the French chemist [Joseph Gay-Lussac](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Gay-Lussac "Joseph Gay-Lussac"). In 1855, another French chemist, [Marcellin Berthelot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marcellin_Berthelot "Marcellin Berthelot"), developed a synthesis from [carbon monoxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide "Carbon monoxide") similar to the process used today.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-Monarch-12)

Formic acid was long considered a [chemical compound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_compound "Chemical compound") of only minor interest in the chemical industry. In the late 1960s, significant quantities became available as a byproduct of [acetic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid "Acetic acid") production. It now finds increasing use as a preservative and antibacterial in [livestock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Livestock "Livestock") feed.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-Monarch-12)

Cyclic dimer of formic acid; dashed **green** lines represent hydrogen bonds

Formic acid is a colorless liquid having a pungent, penetrating odor[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-13) at room temperature, comparable to the related [acetic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid "Acetic acid"). Formic acid is about ten times stronger than [acetic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid "Acetic acid") having a (logarithmic) dissociation constant of 3.745 compared to 4.756 for acetic acid.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-Smith_R-3)

It is [miscible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Miscibility "Miscibility") with water and most polar [organic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_chemistry "Organic chemistry")[solvents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solvent "Solvent"), and is somewhat soluble in [hydrocarbons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrocarbon "Hydrocarbon"). In hydrocarbons and in the vapor phase, it consists of [hydrogen-bonded](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_bond "Hydrogen bond")[dimers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimer_(chemistry) "Dimer (chemistry)") rather than individual molecules.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-Ullmann_2009-14)[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-Balabin_2009-15) Owing to its tendency to hydrogen-bond, gaseous formic acid does not obey the [ideal gas law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ideal_gas_law "Ideal gas law").[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-Balabin_2009-15) Solid formic acid, which can exist in either of two [polymorphs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polymorphism_(materials_science) "Polymorphism (materials science)"), consists of an effectively endless network of hydrogen-bonded formic acid molecules. Formic acid forms a high-boiling [azeotrope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Azeotrope "Azeotrope") with water (107.3°C; 77.5% formic acid). Liquid formic acid tends to [supercool](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supercooling "Supercooling").

Formic acid readily decomposes by dehydration in the presence of concentrated [sulfuric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfuric_acid "Sulfuric acid") to form [carbon monoxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide "Carbon monoxide") and water:

HCO 2 H → H 2 O + CO
Treatment of formic acid with sulfuric acid is a convenient laboratory source of CO.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-koch-16)[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-coleman-17)

In the presence of [platinum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platinum "Platinum"), it decomposes with a release of [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen") and [carbon dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_dioxide "Carbon dioxide").

HCO 2 H → H 2 + CO 2
Soluble [ruthenium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ruthenium "Ruthenium") catalysts are also effective for producing carbon monoxide-free hydrogen.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-Fellay2008-18)

Formic acid shares most of the chemical properties of other [carboxylic acids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carboxylic_acid "Carboxylic acid"). Because of its high acidity, solutions in alcohols form esters spontaneously; in [Fischer esterifications](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fischer_esterification "Fischer esterification") of formic acid, it self-catalyzes the reaction and no additional acid catalyst is needed.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-19) Formic acid shares some of the [reducing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redox "Redox") properties of [aldehydes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aldehyde "Aldehyde"), reducing solutions of metal oxides to their respective metal.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-20)

Formic acid is a source for a [formyl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formyl "Formyl") group for example in the [formylation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formylation "Formylation") of [_N_-methylaniline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/N-Methylaniline "N-Methylaniline") to _N_-methylformanilide in [toluene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toluene "Toluene").[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-21)

In [synthetic organic chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_synthesis "Organic synthesis"), formic acid is often used as a source of [hydride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydride "Hydride") ion, as in the [Eschweiler–Clarke reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eschweiler%E2%80%93Clarke_reaction "Eschweiler–Clarke reaction"):

[![Image 22: The Eschweiler–Clark reaction](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Eschweiler-Clarke_Reaction.svg/330px-Eschweiler-Clarke_Reaction.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Eschweiler-Clarke_Reaction.svg "The Eschweiler–Clark reaction")

The Eschweiler–Clark reaction

It is used as a source of hydrogen in [transfer hydrogenation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transfer_hydrogenation "Transfer hydrogenation"), as in the [Leuckart reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leuckart_reaction "Leuckart reaction") to make [amines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amine "Amine"), and (in aqueous solution or in its [azeotrope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Azeotrope "Azeotrope") with [triethylamine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triethylamine "Triethylamine")) for hydrogenation of [ketones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ketone "Ketone").[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-Zhou2012-22)

### Addition to alkenes

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Formic_acid&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Addition to alkenes")]

Formic acid is unique among the carboxylic acids in its ability to participate in addition reactions with [alkenes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkene "Alkene"). Formic acids and alkenes readily react to form formate [esters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ester "Ester"). In the presence of certain acids, including [sulfuric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfuric_acid "Sulfuric acid") and [hydrofluoric acids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrofluoric_acid "Hydrofluoric acid"), however, a variant of the [Koch reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Koch_reaction "Koch reaction") occurs instead, and formic acid adds to the alkene to produce a larger carboxylic acid.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-23)

### Formic acid anhydride

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Formic_acid&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Formic acid anhydride")]

An unstable [formic anhydride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_anhydride "Formic anhydride"), H(C=O)−O−(C=O)H, can be obtained by dehydration of formic acid with [_N_,_N′_-dicyclohexylcarbodiimide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/N,N%27-Dicyclohexylcarbodiimide "N,N'-Dicyclohexylcarbodiimide") in ether at low temperature.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-gwu-24)

In 2009, the worldwide capacity for producing formic acid was 720 thousand tonnes (1.6 billion pounds) per year, roughly equally divided between Europe (350 thousand tonnes or 770 million pounds, mainly in Germany) and Asia (370 thousand tonnes or 820 million pounds, mainly in China) while production was below 1 thousand tonnes or 2.2 million pounds per year in all other continents.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-CEH-25) It is commercially available in solutions of various concentrations between 85 and 99 w/w%.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-Ullmann_2009-14) As of 2009, the largest producers are [BASF](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BASF "BASF"), [Eastman Chemical Company](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastman_Chemical_Company "Eastman Chemical Company"), [LC Industrial](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=LC_Industrial&action=edit&redlink=1 "LC Industrial (page does not exist)"), and [Feicheng Acid Chemicals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feicheng_Acid_Chemicals "Feicheng Acid Chemicals"), with the largest production facilities in [Ludwigshafen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ludwigshafen "Ludwigshafen") (200 thousand tonnes or 440 million pounds per year, BASF, Germany), [Oulu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oulu "Oulu") (105 thousand tonnes or 230 million pounds, Eastman, Finland), [Nakhon Pathom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nakhon_Pathom "Nakhon Pathom") (n/a, LC Industrial), and [Feicheng](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feicheng "Feicheng") (100 thousand tonnes or 220 million pounds, Feicheng, China). 2010 prices ranged from around €650/tonne (equivalent to around $800/tonne) in Western Europe to $1250/tonne in the United States.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-CEH-25)

Regenerating CO 2 to make useful products, that displace incumbent fossil fuel based pathways is a more impactful process than CO 2 sequestration.

Both formic acid and CO (carbon monoxide) are C1 (one carbon molecules). Formic is a hydrogen-rich liquid which can be transported and easily donates its hydrogen to enable a variety of condensation and esterification reactions to make a wide variety of derivative molecules. CO, while more difficult to transport as a gas, is also one of the primary constituents of syngas useful in synthesizing a wide variety of molecules.

CO 2 electrolysis is distinct from photosynthesis and offers a promising alternative to accelerate decarbonization. By converting CO 2 into products using clean electricity, we reduce CO 2 emissions in two ways: first and most simply by the amount of CO 2 that is regenerated, but the second way is less obvious but even more consequential by avoiding the CO 2 emissions otherwise generated by making these same products from fossil fuels. This is known as carbon displacement or abatement.

CO 2 electrolysis holds promise for reducing atmospheric CO 2 levels and providing a sustainable method for producing chemicals, materials, and fuels. Its efficiency and scalability are active areas of research, but now also commercialization, aiming to make it a viable commercial technology for both carbon management and molecule production.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-26)

### From methyl formate and formamide

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Formic_acid&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: From methyl formate and formamide")]

When [methanol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol "Methanol") and [carbon monoxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide "Carbon monoxide") are combined in the presence of a strong [base](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Base_(chemistry) "Base (chemistry)"), the result is [methyl formate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methyl_formate "Methyl formate"), according to the [chemical equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_equation "Chemical equation"):[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-Ullmann_2009-14)

CH 3 OH + CO → HCO 2 CH 3
In industry, this reaction is performed in the liquid phase at elevated pressure. Typical reaction conditions are 80°C and 40 atm. The most widely used base is [sodium methoxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_methoxide "Sodium methoxide"). [Hydrolysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrolysis "Hydrolysis") of the methyl formate produces formic acid:

HCO 2 CH 3 + H 2 O → HCOOH + CH 3 OH
Efficient hydrolysis of methyl formate requires a large excess of water. Some routes proceed indirectly by first treating the methyl formate with [ammonia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia "Ammonia") to give [formamide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formamide "Formamide"), which is then hydrolyzed with [sulfuric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfuric_acid "Sulfuric acid"):

HCO 2 CH 3 + NH 3 → HC(O)NH 2 + CH 3 OH 2 HC(O)NH 2 + 2H 2 O + H 2 SO 4 → 2HCO 2 H + (NH 4)2 SO 4
A disadvantage of this approach is the need to dispose of the [ammonium sulfate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium_sulfate "Ammonium sulfate") byproduct. This problem has led some manufacturers to develop energy-efficient methods of separating formic acid from the excess water used in direct hydrolysis. In one of these processes, used by [BASF](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BASF "BASF"), the formic acid is removed from the water by [liquid-liquid extraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liquid-liquid_extraction "Liquid-liquid extraction") with an organic base.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

### Niche and obsolete chemical routes

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Formic_acid&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Niche and obsolete chemical routes")]

#### By-product of acetic acid production

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Formic_acid&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: By-product of acetic acid production")]

A significant amount of formic acid is produced as a byproduct in the manufacture of other chemicals. At one time, [acetic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid "Acetic acid") was produced on a large scale by oxidation of [alkanes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkane "Alkane"), by a process that cogenerates significant formic acid.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-Ullmann_2009-14) This oxidative route to acetic acid has declined in importance so that the aforementioned dedicated routes to formic acid have become more important.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

#### Hydrogenation of carbon dioxide

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Formic_acid&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Hydrogenation of carbon dioxide")]

The catalytic [hydrogenation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogenation "Hydrogenation") of [CO 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_dioxide "Carbon dioxide") to formic acid has long been studied. This reaction can be conducted homogeneously.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-27)[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-28)[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-29)

#### Oxidation of biomass

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Formic_acid&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Oxidation of biomass")]

Formic acid can also be obtained by aqueous catalytic partial oxidation of wet biomass by the [OxFA process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OxFA_process "OxFA process").[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-30)[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-31) A [Keggin-type](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Keggin_structure "Keggin structure") polyoxometalate (H 5 PV 2 Mo 10 O 40) is used as the homogeneous catalyst to convert sugars, wood, waste paper, or cyanobacteria to formic acid and CO 2 as the sole byproduct. Yields of up to 53% formic acid can be achieved.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

In the laboratory, formic acid can be obtained by heating [oxalic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxalic_acid "Oxalic acid") in [glycerol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycerol "Glycerol") followed by steam distillation.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-chattaway-32) Glycerol acts as a catalyst, as the reaction proceeds through a glyceryl oxalate intermediate. If the reaction mixture is heated to higher temperatures, [allyl alcohol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allyl_alcohol "Allyl alcohol") results. The net reaction is thus:

C 2 O 4 H 2 → HCO 2 H + CO 2
Another illustrative method involves the reaction between [lead formate](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Lead_formate&action=edit&redlink=1 "Lead formate (page does not exist)") and [hydrogen sulfide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide "Hydrogen sulfide"), driven by the formation of [lead sulfide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lead_sulfide "Lead sulfide").[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-33)

Pb(HCOO)2 + H 2 S → 2HCOOH + PbS

Formic acid is named after ants which have high concentrations of the compound in their venom, derived from [serine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Serine "Serine") through a [5,10-methenyltetrahydrofolate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/5,10-methenyltetrahydrofolate "5,10-methenyltetrahydrofolate") intermediate.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-35) The conjugate base of formic acid, formate, also occurs widely in nature. An [assay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Assay "Assay") for formic acid in body fluids, designed for determination of formate after methanol poisoning, is based on the reaction of formate with bacterial [formate dehydrogenase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formate_dehydrogenase "Formate dehydrogenase").[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-36)

A major use of formic acid is as a [preservative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Preservative "Preservative") and [antibacterial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacteria "Bacteria") agent in livestock feed. It arrests certain decay processes and causes the feed to retain its nutritive value longer,

In Europe, it is applied on [silage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silage "Silage"), including fresh hay, to promote the fermentation of [lactic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lactic_acid "Lactic acid") and to suppress the formation of [butyric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Butyric_acid "Butyric acid"); it also allows fermentation to occur quickly, and at a lower temperature, reducing the loss of nutritional value.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-Ullmann_2009-14) It is widely used to preserve winter feed for [cattle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cattle "Cattle"),[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-37) and is sometimes added to [poultry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poultry "Poultry") feed to kill _[E. coli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Escherichia\_coli "Escherichia coli")_ bacteria.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-38)[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-39) Use as a preservative for silage and other animal feed constituted 30% of the global consumption in 2009.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-CEH-25)

[Beekeepers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beekeeper "Beekeeper") use formic acid as a [miticide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Miticide "Miticide") against the tracheal mite (_[Acarapis woodi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acarapis\_woodi "Acarapis woodi")_) and the [_Varroa destructor_ mite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Varroa_destructor "Varroa destructor") and [_Varroa jacobsoni_ mite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Varroa_jacobsoni "Varroa jacobsoni").[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-40)

Formic acid can be used directly in [formic acid fuel cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid_fuel_cell "Formic acid fuel cell") or indirectly in hydrogen [fuel cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fuel_cell "Fuel cell").[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-41)[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-42)

Electrolytic conversion of electrical energy to chemical fuel has been proposed as a large-scale source of formate by various groups.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-43) The formate could be used as feed to modified _[E. coli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Escherichia\_coli "Escherichia coli")_ bacteria for producing [biomass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biomass "Biomass").[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-44)[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-45) Natural [methylotroph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methylotroph "Methylotroph") microbes can feed on formic acid or formate.

Formic acid has been considered as a means of [hydrogen storage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_storage "Hydrogen storage").[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-46) The co-product of this decomposition, carbon dioxide, can be rehydrogenated back to formic acid in a second step. Formic acid contains 53 g/L hydrogen at room temperature and atmospheric pressure, which is three and a half times as much as compressed hydrogen gas can attain at 350 bar pressure (14.7 g/L). Pure formic acid is a liquid with a [flash point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flash_point "Flash point") of 69°C, much higher than that of gasoline (−40°C) or ethanol (13°C).[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

It is possible to use formic acid as an intermediary to produce [isobutanol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isobutanol "Isobutanol") from CO 2 using microbes.[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-47)[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-48)

Formic acid has a potential application in [soldering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soldering "Soldering"). Due to its capacity to reduce oxide layers, formic acid gas can be blasted at an oxide surface to increase solder [wettability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soldering#Flux "Soldering").[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

Formic acid is used as a volatile pH modifier in [HPLC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-performance_liquid_chromatography "High-performance liquid chromatography") and [capillary electrophoresis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capillary_electrophoresis "Capillary electrophoresis"). Formic acid is often used as a component of mobile phase in [reversed-phase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reversed-phase_chromatography "Reversed-phase chromatography")[high-performance liquid chromatography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-performance_liquid_chromatography "High-performance liquid chromatography") (RP-HPLC) analysis and separation techniques for the separation of hydrophobic macromolecules, such as peptides, proteins and more complex structures including intact viruses. Especially when paired with [mass spectrometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass_spectrometry "Mass spectrometry") detection, formic acid offers several advantages over the more traditionally used [phosphoric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphoric_acid "Phosphoric acid").[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-49)[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-50)

Formic acid is also significantly used in the production of leather, including [tanning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tanning_(leather) "Tanning (leather)") (23% of the global consumption in 2009[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-CEH-25)), and in dyeing and finishing textiles (9% of the global consumption in 2009[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-CEH-25)) because of its acidic nature. Use as a coagulant in the [production of rubber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Production_of_rubber "Production of rubber")[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-Ullmann_2009-14) consumed 6% of the global production in 2009.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-CEH-25)

Formic acid is also used in place of mineral acids for various cleaning products,[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-Ullmann_2009-14) such as [limescale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limescale "Limescale") remover and [toilet bowl cleaner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Household_cleaner "Household cleaner"). Some formate [esters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Esters "Esters") are artificial flavorings and perfumes.

Formic acid application has been reported to be an effective treatment for [warts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wart "Wart").[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-pmid11589750-51)

Formic acid has low toxicity (hence its use as a food additive), with an [LD 50](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Median_lethal_dose "Median lethal dose") of 1.8 g/kg (tested orally on mice). The concentrated acid is corrosive to the skin.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-Ullmann_2009-14)

Formic acid is readily metabolized and eliminated by the body. Nonetheless, it has specific [toxic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toxic "Toxic") effects; the formic acid and [formaldehyde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde "Formaldehyde") produced as metabolites of [methanol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol "Methanol") are responsible for the [optic nerve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optic_nerve "Optic nerve") damage, causing blindness, seen in [methanol poisoning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol_poisoning "Methanol poisoning").[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-52) Some chronic effects of formic acid exposure have been documented. Some experiments on bacterial species have demonstrated it to be a [mutagen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mutagen "Mutagen").[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-osha.gov-53) Chronic exposure in humans may cause kidney damage.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid#cite_note-osha.gov-53) Another possible effect of chronic exposure is development of a skin [allergy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allergy "Allergy") that manifests upon re-exposure to the chemical.

Concentrated formic acid slowly decomposes to carbon monoxide and water, leading to pressure buildup in the containing vessel. For this reason, 98% formic acid is shipped in plastic bottles with self-venting caps.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

The hazards of solutions of formic acid depend on the concentration.
