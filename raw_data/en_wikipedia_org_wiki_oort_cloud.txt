This article is about the outer Oort cloud. For the inner Oort cloud, see [Hills cloud](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hills_cloud "Hills cloud").

[![Image 3](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/PIA17046_-_Voyager_1_Goes_Interstellar.jpg/330px-PIA17046_-_Voyager_1_Goes_Interstellar.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:PIA17046_-_Voyager_1_Goes_Interstellar.jpg)

The distance from the Oort cloud to the interior of the Solar System, and two of the nearest stars, is measured in [astronomical units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_unit "Astronomical unit"). The scale is [logarithmic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logarithmic_scale "Logarithmic scale"): each indicated distance is ten times farther out than the previous distance. The red arrow indicates the projected location, in 2025–2027, of the [space probe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_probe "Space probe")_[Voyager 1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voyager\_1 "Voyager 1")_, which may reach the Oort cloud about 300 years later.

The **Oort cloud** (pronounced [_AWT_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key "Help:Pronunciation respelling key") or [_OORT_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key "Help:Pronunciation respelling key")),[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-1) sometimes called the **Öpik–Oort cloud**,[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-Whipple-2) is [theorized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_theory "Scientific theory") to be a cloud of billions of [icy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volatile_(astrogeology) "Volatile (astrogeology)")[planetesimals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetesimal "Planetesimal") surrounding the [Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun "Sun") at distances ranging from 2,000 to 200,000 [AU](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_unit "Astronomical unit") (0.03 to 3.2 [light-years](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light-years "Light-years")).[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-Morbidelli2006-4) The cloud was proposed in 1950 by the Dutch [astronomer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomer "Astronomer")[Jan Oort](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jan_Oort "Jan Oort"), in whose honor the idea was named.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-6) Oort proposed that the bodies in this cloud replenish and keep constant the number of [long-period comets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comet "Comet") entering the [inner Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inner_Solar_System "Inner Solar System")—where they are eventually consumed and destroyed during close approaches to the Sun.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-SPC-20181004-7)

The cloud is thought to encompass two regions: a [disc-shaped](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumstellar_disc "Circumstellar disc") inner Oort cloud aligned with the [solar ecliptic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecliptic "Ecliptic") (also called its [Hills cloud](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hills_cloud "Hills cloud")) and a [spherical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumstellar_envelope "Circumstellar envelope") outer Oort cloud enclosing the entire [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System"). Both regions lie well beyond the [heliosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heliosphere "Heliosphere") and are in [interstellar space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_space#Interstellar_space "Outer space").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-Morbidelli2006-4)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-jpl.PIA17046-8) The innermost portion of the Oort cloud is more than a thousand times farther from the Sun than the [Kuiper belt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kuiper_belt "Kuiper belt"), the [scattered disc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scattered_disc "Scattered disc") and the [detached objects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Detached_object "Detached object")—three nearer reservoirs of [trans-Neptunian objects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trans-Neptunian_object "Trans-Neptunian object").[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-9)

The outer limit of the Oort cloud defines the [cosmographic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmography "Cosmography") boundary of the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System"). This area is defined by the Sun's [Hill sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hill_sphere "Hill sphere"), and hence lies at the interface between solar and galactic gravitational dominion.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-NASA_SSE_oort-10) The outer Oort cloud is only loosely bound to the Solar System and its constituents are easily affected by the gravitational pulls of [passing stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_nearest_stars#Distant_future_and_past_encounters "List of nearest stars"), the [Milky Way](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milky_Way "Milky Way") itself and the cloud's own microgravity.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-11) These forces served to moderate and render more circular the highly eccentric orbits of material ejected from the inner Solar System during its [early phases of development](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formation_and_evolution_of_the_Solar_System "Formation and evolution of the Solar System"). The circular orbits of material in the Oort disc are largely thanks to this galactic gravitational torquing.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-12)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-13) By the same token, galactic interference in the motion of Oort bodies occasionally dislodges [comets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comet "Comet") from their orbits within the cloud, sending them into the [inner Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inner_Solar_System "Inner Solar System").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-Morbidelli2006-4) Based on their orbits, most but not all of the [short-period comets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Short-period_comet "Short-period comet") appear to have come from the Oort disc. Other short-period comets may have originated from the far larger spherical cloud.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-Morbidelli2006-4)[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-emel2007-14)

Astronomers hypothesize that the material presently in the Oort cloud formed much closer to the Sun, in the [protoplanetary disc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protoplanetary_disc "Protoplanetary disc"), and was then scattered far into space through the gravitational influence of the [giant planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giant_planet "Giant planet").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-Morbidelli2006-4) No direct observation of the Oort cloud is possible with present imaging technology.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-15) Nevertheless, the cloud is thought to be the source that replenishes most [long-period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Long-period_comet "Long-period comet") and [Halley-type](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halley%27s_Comet "Halley's Comet") comets, which are eventually consumed by their close approaches to the Sun after entering the inner Solar System. The cloud may also serve the same function for many of the [centaurs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centaur_(small_Solar_System_body) "Centaur (small Solar System body)") and [Jupiter-family comets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category:Jupiter-family_comets "Category:Jupiter-family comets").[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-emel2007-14)

Development of theory
---------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Oort_cloud&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Development of theory")]

By the early 20th century, astronomers had identified two main types of comets: short-period comets (also called _[ecliptic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecliptic "Ecliptic")_ comets) and long-period comets (also called _nearly [isotropic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotropy "Isotropy")_ comets).[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-16) Ecliptic comets have relatively small orbits aligned near the [ecliptic plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_of_the_ecliptic "Plane of the ecliptic") and are not found much farther than the [Kuiper cliff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kuiper_cliff "Kuiper cliff") around 50 AU from the Sun (the orbit of [Neptune](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune "Neptune") averages about 30 AU and [177P/Barnard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/177P/Barnard "177P/Barnard") has aphelion around 48 AU). Long-period comets, on the other hand, travel in very large orbits thousands of AU from the Sun and are isotropically distributed. This means long-period comets appear from every direction in the sky, both above and below the ecliptic plane.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-book-17) The origin of these comets was not well understood, and many long-period comets were initially assumed to be on parabolic trajectories, making them one-time visitors to the Sun from interstellar space.

In 1907, [Armin Otto Leuschner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Armin_Otto_Leuschner "Armin Otto Leuschner") suggested that many of the comets then thought to have parabolic orbits in fact moved along extremely large elliptical orbits that would return them to the inner Solar System after long intervals during which they were invisible to Earth-based astronomy.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-ley1967204-18) In 1932, the [Estonian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Estonia "Estonia") astronomer [Ernst Öpik](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ernst_%C3%96pik "Ernst Öpik") proposed a reservoir of long-period comets in the form of an orbiting cloud at the outermost edge of the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System").[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-%C3%96pik-1932-19)[Dutch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dutch_people "Dutch people") astronomer [Jan Oort](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jan_Oort "Jan Oort") revived this idea in 1950 to resolve a paradox about the origin of comets. The following facts are not easily reconcilable with the highly elliptical orbits in which long-period comets are always found:

*   Over millions and billions of years the orbits of Oort cloud comets are unstable. [Celestial dynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynamics_(physics) "Dynamics (physics)") will eventually dictate that a comet must be pulled away by a passing star, collide with the Sun or a planet, or be ejected from the Solar System through planetary [perturbations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perturbation_(astronomy) "Perturbation (astronomy)").
*   Moreover, the volatile composition of comets means that as they repeatedly approach the Sun [radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation "Electromagnetic radiation") gradually boils the volatiles off until the comet splits or develops an insulating crust that prevents further [outgassing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outgassing "Outgassing").[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-Oort-20)

Oort reasoned that comets with orbits that closely approach the Sun cannot have been doing so since the condensation of the protoplanetary disc, more than 4.5 billion years ago. Hence long-period comets could not have formed in the current orbits in which they are always discovered and must have been held in an outer reservoir for nearly all of their existence.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-Oort-20)[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-dave-21)[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-book-17)

Oort also studied tables of [ephemerides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ephemeris "Ephemeris") for long-period comets and discovered that there is a curious concentration of long-period comets whose farthest retreat from the Sun (their [aphelia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aphelion "Aphelion")) cluster around 20,000 AU. This suggested a reservoir at that distance with a spherical, [isotropic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotropy "Isotropy") distribution. He also proposed that the relatively rare comets with orbits of about 10,000 AU probably went through one or more orbits into the inner Solar System and there had their orbits drawn inward by the [gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity "Gravity") of the planets.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-book-17)

Structure and composition
-------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Oort_cloud&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Structure and composition")]

[![Image 4](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Oort_cloud_Sedna_orbit.svg/330px-Oort_cloud_Sedna_orbit.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Oort_cloud_Sedna_orbit.svg)

The presumed distance of the Oort cloud compared to the rest of the Solar System

The Oort cloud is thought to occupy a vast space somewhere between 2,000 and 5,000 AU (0.03 and 0.08 ly)[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-book-17) from the Sun to as far out as 50,000 AU (0.79 ly) or even 100,000 to 200,000 AU (1.58 to 3.16 ly).[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-Morbidelli2006-4)[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-book-17) The region can be subdivided into a spherical outer Oort cloud with a radius of some 20,000–50,000 AU (0.32–0.79 ly) and a [torus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torus "Torus")-shaped inner Oort cloud with a radius of 2,000–20,000 AU (0.03–0.32 ly).

The inner Oort cloud is sometimes known as the [Hills cloud](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hills_cloud "Hills cloud"), named for [Jack G. Hills](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jack_G._Hills "Jack G. Hills"), who proposed its existence in 1981.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-hills1981-22) Models predict the inner cloud to be much the denser of the two, having tens or hundreds of times as many cometary nuclei as the outer cloud.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-hills1981-22)[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-levison2001-23)[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-Donahue1991-24) The Hills cloud is thought to be necessary to explain the continued existence of the Oort cloud after billions of years.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-Julio1997-25)

Because it lies at the interface between the dominion of Solar and galactic gravitation, the objects comprising the outer Oort cloud are only weakly bound to the Sun. This in turn allows small perturbations from nearby stars or the Milky Way itself to inject long-period (and possibly [Halley-type](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_Halley-type_comets "List of Halley-type comets")) comets inside the orbit of [Neptune](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune "Neptune").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-Morbidelli2006-4) This process ought to have depleted the sparser, outer cloud and yet long-period comets with orbits well above or below the ecliptic continue to be observed. The Hills cloud is thought to be a secondary reservoir of cometary nuclei and the source of replenishment for the tenuous outer cloud as the latter's numbers are gradually depleted through losses to the inner Solar System.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-26)

The outer Oort cloud may have trillions of objects larger than 1 km (0.6 mi),[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-Morbidelli2006-4) and billions with diameters of 20-kilometre (12 mi). This corresponds to an [absolute magnitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_magnitude#Solar_System_bodies_(H) "Absolute magnitude") of more than 11.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-27) On this analysis, "neighboring" objects in the outer cloud are separated by a significant fraction of 1 AU, tens of millions of kilometres.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-emel2007-14)[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-28) The outer cloud's total mass is not known, but assuming that [Halley's Comet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halley%27s_Comet "Halley's Comet") is a suitable proxy for the nuclei composing the outer Oort cloud, their combined mass would be roughly 3×10 25 kilograms (6.6×10 25 lb), or five Earth masses.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-Morbidelli2006-4)[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-29) Formerly the outer cloud was thought to be more massive by two orders of magnitude, containing up to 380 Earth masses,[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-30) but improved knowledge of the size distribution of long-period comets has led to lower estimates. No estimates of the mass of the inner Oort cloud have been published as of 2023.

If analyses of comets are representative of the whole, the vast majority of Oort-cloud objects consist of [ices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volatile_(astrogeology) "Volatile (astrogeology)") such as [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ice "Ice"), [methane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane "Methane"), [ethane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethane "Ethane"), [carbon monoxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide "Carbon monoxide") and [hydrogen cyanide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide "Hydrogen cyanide").[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-31) However, the discovery of the object [1996 PW](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1996_PW "1996 PW"), an object whose appearance was consistent with a [D-type asteroid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D-type_asteroid "D-type asteroid")[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-32)[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-33) in an orbit typical of a long-period comet, prompted theoretical research that suggests that the Oort cloud population consists of roughly one to two percent asteroids.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-34) Analysis of the carbon and nitrogen [isotope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotope "Isotope") ratios in both the long-period and Jupiter-family comets shows little difference between the two, despite their presumably vastly separate regions of origin. This suggests that both originated from the original protosolar cloud,[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-35) a conclusion also supported by studies of granular size in Oort-cloud comets[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-36) and by the recent impact study of Jupiter-family comet [Tempel 1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tempel_1 "Tempel 1").[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-37)

The Oort cloud is thought to have developed after the [formation of planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formation_and_evolution_of_the_Solar_System "Formation and evolution of the Solar System") from the primordial [protoplanetary disc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protoplanetary_disc "Protoplanetary disc") approximately 4.6 billion years ago.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-Morbidelli2006-4) The most widely accepted hypothesis is that the Oort cloud's objects initially coalesced much closer to the Sun as part of the same process that formed the [planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planet "Planet") and [minor planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minor_planet "Minor planet"). After formation, strong gravitational interactions with young gas giants, such as Jupiter, scattered the objects into extremely wide [elliptical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptic_orbit "Elliptic orbit") or [parabolic orbits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parabolic_orbit "Parabolic orbit") that were subsequently modified by perturbations from passing stars and giant molecular clouds into long-lived orbits detached from the gas giant region.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-Morbidelli2006-4)[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-38)

Recent research has been cited by NASA hypothesizing that a large number of Oort cloud objects are the product of an exchange of materials between the Sun and its sibling stars as they formed and drifted apart and it is suggested that many—possibly the majority—of Oort cloud objects did not form in close proximity to the Sun.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-nasax-39) Simulations of the evolution of the Oort cloud from the beginnings of the Solar System to the present suggest that the cloud's mass peaked around 800 million years after formation, as the pace of accretion and collision slowed and depletion began to overtake supply.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-Morbidelli2006-4)

Models by [Julio Ángel Fernández](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Julio_%C3%81ngel_Fern%C3%A1ndez "Julio Ángel Fernández") suggest that the [scattered disc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scattered_disc "Scattered disc"), which is the main source for [periodic comets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Periodic_comet "Periodic comet") in the Solar System, might also be the primary source for Oort cloud objects. According to the models, about half of the objects scattered travel outward toward the Oort cloud, whereas a quarter are shifted inward to Jupiter's orbit, and a quarter are ejected on [hyperbolic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbola "Hyperbola") orbits. The scattered disc might still be supplying the Oort cloud with material.[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-40)[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-41) A third of the scattered disc's population is likely to end up in the Oort cloud after 2.5 billion years.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-42)

Computer models suggest that collisions of cometary debris during the formation period play a far greater role than was previously thought. According to these models, the number of collisions early in the Solar System's history was so great that most comets were destroyed before they reached the Oort cloud. Therefore, the current cumulative mass of the Oort cloud is far less than was once suspected.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-43) The estimated mass of the cloud is only a small part of the 50–100 Earth masses of ejected material.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-Morbidelli2006-4)

Gravitational interaction with nearby stars and [galactic tides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galactic_tide "Galactic tide") modified cometary orbits to make them more circular. This explains the nearly spherical shape of the outer Oort cloud.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-Morbidelli2006-4) On the other hand, the [Hills cloud](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hills_cloud "Hills cloud"), which is bound more strongly to the Sun, has not acquired a spherical shape. Recent studies have shown that the formation of the Oort cloud is broadly compatible with the hypothesis that the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System") formed as part of an embedded [cluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_cluster "Star cluster") of 200–400 stars. These early stars likely played a role in the cloud's formation, since the number of close stellar passages within the cluster was much higher than today, leading to far more frequent perturbations.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-44)

In June 2010 [Harold F. Levison](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harold_F._Levison "Harold F. Levison") and others suggested on the basis of enhanced computer simulations that the Sun "captured comets from other stars while it was in its [birth cluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_cluster "Open cluster")." Their results imply that "a substantial fraction of the Oort cloud comets, perhaps exceeding 90%, are from the protoplanetary discs of other stars."[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-45)[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-46) In July 2020 Amir Siraj and [Avi Loeb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Avi_Loeb "Avi Loeb") found that a captured origin for the Oort Cloud in the Sun's [birth cluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_cluster "Open cluster") could address the theoretical tension in explaining the observed ratio of outer Oort cloud to [scattered disc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scattered_disc "Scattered disc") objects, and in addition could increase the chances of a captured [Planet Nine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planet_Nine "Planet Nine").[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-47)[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-48)[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-49)

[Comets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comet "Comet") are remnants from the formation of the Solar system around 4 billion years ago, stored in two separate areas, the [Kuiper belt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kuiper_belt "Kuiper belt") and the Oort cloud.[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-50) Short-period comets (those with orbits of up to 200 years) are generally accepted to have emerged from either the Kuiper belt or the scattered disc, which are two linked flat discs of icy debris beyond Neptune's orbit at 30 AU and jointly extending out beyond 100 AU. Very long-period comets, such as [C/1999 F1 (Catalina)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C/1999_F1_(Catalina) "C/1999 F1 (Catalina)"), whose orbits last for millions of years, are thought to originate directly from the outer Oort cloud.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-C1999F1-51) Other comets modeled to have come directly from the outer Oort cloud include [C/2006 P1 (McNaught)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C/2006_P1_(McNaught) "C/2006 P1 (McNaught)"), [C/2010 X1 (Elenin)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C/2010_X1_(Elenin) "C/2010 X1 (Elenin)"), [Comet ISON](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comet_ISON "Comet ISON"), [C/2013 A1 (Siding Spring)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C/2013_A1_(Siding_Spring) "C/2013 A1 (Siding Spring)"), [C/2017 K2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C/2017_K2 "C/2017 K2"), and [C/2017 T2 (PANSTARRS)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C/2017_T2_(PANSTARRS) "C/2017 T2 (PANSTARRS)"). The orbits within the Kuiper belt are relatively stable, so very few comets are thought to originate there. The scattered disc, however, is dynamically active and is far more likely to be the place of origin for comets.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-book-17) Comets pass from the scattered disc into the realm of the outer planets, becoming what are known as [centaurs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centaur_(minor_planet) "Centaur (minor planet)").[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-52)[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-53) These centaurs are then sent farther inward to become the short-period comets.[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-54)

There are two main types of short-period comets: Jupiter-family comets (with orbits smaller than 5 AU) and Halley-family comets. Halley-family comets, named after [Halley's Comet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halley%27s_Comet "Halley's Comet"), are distinct because, even though they are short-period comets, they are thought to come from the Oort Cloud rather than the scattered disc.[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-55)[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-56) Based on their orbits, it is suggested they were long-period comets that were captured by the gravity of the giant planets and sent into the inner Solar System.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-dave-21) This process may have also created the present orbits of a significant fraction of the Jupiter-family comets, although the majority of such comets are thought to have originated in the scattered disc.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-emel2007-14)

Oort noted that the number of returning comets was far less than his model predicted, and this issue, known as "cometary fading", has yet to be resolved.[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-57) No dynamical process is known to explain the smaller number of observed comets than Oort estimated. Hypotheses for this discrepancy include the destruction of comets due to tidal stresses, impact or heating; the loss of all [volatiles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volatile_(astrogeology) "Volatile (astrogeology)"), rendering some comets invisible, or the formation of a non-volatile crust on the surface.[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-58) Dynamical studies of hypothetical Oort cloud comets have estimated that their occurrence in the [outer-planet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_planets "Outer planets") region would be several times higher than in the inner-planet region. This discrepancy may be due to the gravitational attraction of [Jupiter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jupiter "Jupiter"), which acts as a kind of barrier, trapping incoming comets and causing them to collide with it, just as it did with [Comet Shoemaker–Levy 9](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comet_Shoemaker%E2%80%93Levy_9 "Comet Shoemaker–Levy 9") in 1994.[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-julio-59) An example of a typical dynamically old comet with an origin in the Oort cloud could be C/2018 F4.[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-Oort_Cloud_origin-60)

Sedna and similar objects
-------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Oort_cloud&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Sedna and similar objects")]

Several observed objects have been proposed as members of the inner Oort cloud.[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-Dones-2015-61) Sedna, first reported in 2004, has a highly eccentric orbit with a perihelion distances of 76AU.[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-62)[2012 VP 113](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2012_VP113 "2012 VP113"), observed in 2012, has a larger perihelion (closest approach to the Sun) but its aphelion is half of Sedna's.[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-63)[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-64) Other candidate objects[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-65) include [2010 GB 174](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2010_GB174 "2010 GB174")[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-66) and [474640 Alicanto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/474640_Alicanto "474640 Alicanto") (originally 2004 VN 112).[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-67)

Most of the comets seen close to the Sun seem to have reached their current positions through gravitational perturbation of the Oort cloud by the [tidal force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tidal_force "Tidal force") exerted by the [Milky Way](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milky_Way "Milky Way"). Just as the [Moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moon "Moon")'s tidal force deforms Earth's oceans, causing the tides to rise and fall, the galactic tide also distorts the orbits of bodies in the [outer Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_Solar_System "Outer Solar System").[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-68) In the charted regions of the Solar System, these effects are negligible compared to the gravity of the Sun, but in the outer reaches of the system, the Sun's gravity is weaker and the gradient of the Milky Way's gravitational [Galactic Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galactic_Center "Galactic Center") compresses it along the other two axes; these small perturbations can shift orbits in the Oort cloud to bring objects close to the Sun.[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-69) The point at which the Sun's gravity concedes its influence to the galactic tide is called the tidal truncation radius. It lies at a radius of 100,000 to 200,000 AU, and marks the outer boundary of the Oort cloud.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-book-17)

Some scholars theorize that the galactic tide may have contributed to the formation of the Oort cloud by increasing the [perihelia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perihelion_and_aphelion "Perihelion and aphelion") (smallest distances to the Sun) of [planetesimals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetesimal "Planetesimal") with large aphelia (largest distances to the Sun).[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-70) The effects of the galactic tide are quite complex, and depend heavily on the behaviour of individual objects within a planetary system. Cumulatively, however, the effect can be quite significant: up to 90% of all comets originating from the Oort cloud may be the result of the galactic tide.[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-71) Statistical models of the observed orbits of long-period comets argue that the galactic tide is the principal means by which their orbits are perturbed toward the inner Solar System.[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-72)

Stellar perturbations and stellar companion hypotheses
------------------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Oort_cloud&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Stellar perturbations and stellar companion hypotheses")]

Besides the [galactic tide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galactic_tide "Galactic tide"), the main trigger for sending comets into the inner Solar System is thought to be interaction between the Sun's Oort cloud and the gravitational fields of nearby stars[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-Morbidelli2006-4) or giant [molecular clouds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_cloud "Molecular cloud").[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-julio-59) The orbit of the Sun through the plane of the Milky Way sometimes brings it in relatively [close proximity to other stellar systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_nearest_stars#Future_and_past "List of nearest stars"). For example, it is hypothesized that 70,000 years ago [Scholz's Star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scholz%27s_Star "Scholz's Star") passed through the outer Oort cloud (although its low mass and high relative velocity limited its effect).[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-73)[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-74) During the next 10 million years the known star with the greatest possibility of perturbing the Oort cloud is [Gliese 710](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gliese_710 "Gliese 710").[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-algol-75) This process could also scatter Oort cloud objects out of the ecliptic plane, potentially also explaining its spherical distribution.[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-algol-75)[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-76)

In 1984, [physicist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physicist "Physicist")[Richard A. Muller](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_A._Muller "Richard A. Muller") postulated that the Sun has an as-yet undetected companion, either a [brown dwarf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brown_dwarf "Brown dwarf") or a [red dwarf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_dwarf "Red dwarf"), in an elliptical orbit within the Oort cloud.[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-77) This object, known as [Nemesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nemesis_(hypothetical_star) "Nemesis (hypothetical star)"), was hypothesized to pass through a portion of the Oort cloud approximately every 26 million years, bombarding the inner Solar System with comets. However, to date no evidence of Nemesis has been found, and many lines of evidence (such as [crater counts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crater_counting "Crater counting")), have thrown its existence into doubt.[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-78)[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-79) Recent scientific analysis no longer supports the idea that extinctions on Earth happen at regular, repeating intervals.[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-Tyche2011-060-80) Thus, the Nemesis hypothesis is no longer needed to explain current assumptions.[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-Tyche2011-060-80)

A somewhat similar hypothesis was advanced by astronomer [John J. Matese](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=John_J._Matese&action=edit&redlink=1 "John J. Matese (page does not exist)") of the [University of Louisiana at Lafayette](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Louisiana_at_Lafayette "University of Louisiana at Lafayette") in 2002. He contends that more comets are arriving in the inner Solar System from a particular region of the postulated Oort cloud than can be explained by the galactic tide or stellar perturbations alone, and that the most likely cause would be a [Jupiter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jupiter "Jupiter")-mass object in a distant orbit.[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-81) This hypothetical [gas giant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas_giant "Gas giant") was nicknamed [Tyche](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tyche_(hypothetical_planet) "Tyche (hypothetical planet)"). The [WISE mission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WISE_mission "WISE mission"), an [all-sky survey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/All-sky_survey "All-sky survey") using [parallax](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallax "Parallax") measurements in order to clarify local star distances, was capable of proving or disproving the Tyche hypothesis.[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-Tyche2011-060-80) In 2014, NASA announced that the WISE survey had ruled out any object as they had defined it.[[82]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-NASA-20140307-82)

[![Image 5](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Voyager_spacecraft.jpg/250px-Voyager_spacecraft.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Voyager_spacecraft.jpg)

[Artist's impression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artist%27s_impression "Artist's impression") of the [_Voyager_ spacecraft](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voyager_program "Voyager program")

Space probes have yet to reach the area of the Oort cloud. _[Voyager 1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voyager\_1 "Voyager 1")_, the fastest[[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-New_Horizons2006-83) and farthest[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-g.2013sep13-84)[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-85) of the interplanetary space probes currently leaving the Solar System, will reach the Oort cloud in about 300 years[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-jpl.PIA17046-8)[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-ut.104717-86) and would take about 30,000 years to pass through it.[[87]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-Ghose2013-87)[[88]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-How_We_Know-88) However, around 2025, the [radioisotope thermoelectric generators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radioisotope_thermoelectric_generator "Radioisotope thermoelectric generator") on _Voyager 1_ will no longer supply enough power to operate any of its scientific instruments, preventing any further exploration by _Voyager 1._ The [other four probes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category:Spacecraft_escaping_the_Solar_System "Category:Spacecraft escaping the Solar System") currently escaping the Solar System have either already stopped functioning (_Pioneer 10_, _Pioneer 11_) or are predicted to also stop functioning before they reach the Oort cloud (_Voyager 2_, _New Horizons_).

In the 1980s, there was a concept for a probe that could reach 1,000 AU in 50 years, called _[TAU](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TAU\_(spacecraft) "TAU (spacecraft)")_; among its missions would be to look for the Oort cloud.[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-89)

In the 2014 Announcement of Opportunity for the [Discovery program](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discovery_program "Discovery program"), an observatory to detect the objects in the Oort cloud (and Kuiper belt) called the ["Whipple Mission"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whipple_(spacecraft) "Whipple (spacecraft)") was proposed.[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-whipple.cfa.harvard.edu-90) It would monitor distant stars with a photometer, looking for transits up to 10,000 AU away.[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-whipple.cfa.harvard.edu-90) The observatory was proposed for halo orbiting around L2 with a suggested 5-year mission.[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-whipple.cfa.harvard.edu-90) It was also suggested that the [Kepler space telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler_space_telescope "Kepler space telescope") could have been capable of detecting objects in the Oort cloud.[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_note-91)

27.   **[^](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oort_cloud#cite_ref-27)**Absolute magnitude is a measure of how bright an object would be if it were 1 au from the Sun and Earth; as opposed to [apparent magnitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apparent_magnitude "Apparent magnitude"), which measures how bright an object appears from Earth. Because all measurements of absolute magnitude assume the same distance, absolute magnitude is in effect a measurement of an object's brightness. The lower an object's absolute magnitude, the brighter it is.
