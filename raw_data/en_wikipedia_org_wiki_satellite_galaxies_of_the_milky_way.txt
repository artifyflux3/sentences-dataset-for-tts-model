Galaxies bound to the Milky Way

The [Milky Way](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milky_Way "Milky Way") has several smaller [galaxies gravitationally bound to it](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satellite_galaxy "Satellite galaxy"), as part of the Milky Way [subgroup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_group "Galaxy group"), which is part of the local galaxy cluster, the [Local Group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_Group "Local Group").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satellite_galaxies_of_the_Milky_Way#cite_note-1)

There are 61 small galaxies confirmed to be within 420 [kiloparsecs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parsec#Parsecs_and_kiloparsecs "Parsec") (1.4 million [light-years](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light-year "Light-year")) of the Milky Way,[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satellite_galaxies_of_the_Milky_Way#cite_note-satellitecensus-2) but not all of them are necessarily in orbit, and some may themselves be in orbit of other satellite galaxies. The only ones visible to the naked eye are the [Large](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Large_Magellanic_Cloud "Large Magellanic Cloud") and [Small](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Small_Magellanic_Cloud "Small Magellanic Cloud") Magellanic Clouds, which have been observed since prehistory. Measurements with the Hubble Space Telescope in 2006 suggest the Magellanic Clouds may be moving too fast to be orbiting the Milky Way.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satellite_galaxies_of_the_Milky_Way#cite_note-3) Of the galaxies confirmed to be in orbit, the largest is the [Sagittarius Dwarf Spheroidal Galaxy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sagittarius_Dwarf_Spheroidal_Galaxy "Sagittarius Dwarf Spheroidal Galaxy"), which has a diameter of 2.6 kiloparsecs (8,500 ly)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satellite_galaxies_of_the_Milky_Way#cite_note-Karachentsev_et_al._2004-4) or roughly a twentieth that of the Milky Way.

Characteristics
---------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Satellite_galaxies_of_the_Milky_Way&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Characteristics")]

Satellite galaxies that orbit from 1,000[ly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light-year "Light-year") (310[pc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parsec "Parsec")) of the edge of the [disc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disc_(galaxy) "Disc (galaxy)") of the Milky Way Galaxy to the edge of the [dark matter halo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter_halo "Dark matter halo") of the Milky Way at 980,000 ly (300 kpc) from the center of the galaxy,[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satellite_galaxies_of_the_Milky_Way#cite_note-5) are generally depleted in hydrogen gas compared to those that orbit more distantly. This is because of their interactions with the dense hot gas halo of the Milky Way that strip cold gas from the satellites. Satellites beyond that region still retain copious quantities of gas.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satellite_galaxies_of_the_Milky_Way#cite_note-6)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satellite_galaxies_of_the_Milky_Way#cite_note-7)

Streams
-------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Satellite_galaxies_of_the_Milky_Way&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Streams")]

See also: [List of stellar streams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_stellar_streams "List of stellar streams")

The Sagittarius Dwarf Spheroidal Galaxy is currently in the process of being [consumed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interacting_galaxy "Interacting galaxy") by the Milky Way and is expected to pass through it within the next 100 million years. The [Sagittarius Stream](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sagittarius_Stream "Sagittarius Stream") is a stream of stars in polar orbit around the Milky Way leeched from the Sagittarius Dwarf. The [Virgo Stellar Stream](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgo_Stellar_Stream "Virgo Stellar Stream") is a stream of stars that is believed to have once been an orbiting dwarf galaxy that has been completely distended by the Milky Way's gravity.
