A normal to a surface at a point is the same as a normal to the tangent plane to the surface at the same point.

In [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry"), a **normal** is an [object](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_object "Mathematical object") (e.g. a [line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_(geometry) "Line (geometry)"), [ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ray_(geometry) "Ray (geometry)"), or [vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_vector "Euclidean vector")) that is [perpendicular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular "Perpendicular") to a given object. For example, the **normal line** to a [plane curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_curve "Plane curve") at a given point is the infinite straight line perpendicular to the [tangent line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_line "Tangent line") to the curve at the point.

A **normal vector** is a [vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_(geometry) "Vector (geometry)") perpendicular to a given object at a particular point. A normal [vector of length one](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_vector "Unit vector") is called a **unit normal vector** or **normal direction**. A [curvature vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvature_vector "Curvature vector") is a normal vector whose length is the [curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvature "Curvature") of the object. Multiplying a normal vector by −1 results in the [opposite vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Opposite_vector "Opposite vector"), which may be used for indicating sides (e.g., interior or exterior).

In [three-dimensional space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_space "Three-dimensional space"), a **surface normal**, or simply **normal**, to a [surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_(topology) "Surface (topology)") at point _P_ is a vector perpendicular to the [tangent plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_plane "Tangent plane") of the surface at _P_. The [vector field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_field "Vector field") of normal directions to a surface is known as _[Gauss map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauss\_map "Gauss map")_. The word "normal" is also used as an adjective: a line _normal_ to a [plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_plane "Euclidean plane"), the _normal_ component of a [force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Force "Force"), etc. The concept of normality generalizes to [orthogonality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonality "Orthogonality") ([right angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_angle "Right angle")).

The concept has been generalized to [differentiable manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differentiable_manifold "Differentiable manifold") of arbitrary dimension embedded in a [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space"). The **normal vector space** or **normal space** of a manifold at point ![Image 3: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a) is the set of vectors which are orthogonal to the [tangent space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_space "Tangent space") at ![Image 4: {\displaystyle P.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/49f4f085fcd14302f4f7a9bbdf77e816cccb3bc9) Normal vectors are of special interest in the case of [smooth curves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry_of_curves "Differential geometry of curves") and [smooth surfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry_of_surfaces "Differential geometry of surfaces").

The normal is often used in [3D computer graphics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3D_computer_graphics "3D computer graphics") (notice the singular, as only one normal will be defined) to determine a surface's orientation toward a [light source](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_source "Light source") for [flat shading](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flat_shading "Flat shading"), or the orientation of each of the surface's corners ([vertices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_(geometry) "Vertex (geometry)")) to mimic a curved surface with [Phong shading](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phong_shading "Phong shading").

The **foot** of a normal at a point of interest _Q_ (analogous to the [foot of a perpendicular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foot_of_a_perpendicular "Foot of a perpendicular")) can be defined at the point _P_ on the surface where the normal vector contains _Q_. The _[normal distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal\_distance "Normal distance")_ of a point _Q_ to a curve or to a surface is the [Euclidean distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_distance "Euclidean distance") between _Q_ and its foot _P_.

Normal to general surfaces in 3D space
--------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Normal_(geometry)&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Normal to general surfaces in 3D space")]

[![Image 21](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Normal_vectors_on_a_curved_surface.svg/500px-Normal_vectors_on_a_curved_surface.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Normal_vectors_on_a_curved_surface.svg)

If a (possibly non-flat) surface ![Image 22: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2) in 3D space ![Image 23: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5) is [parameterized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_system "Coordinate system") by a system of [curvilinear coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvilinear_coordinates "Curvilinear coordinates")![Image 24: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} (s,t)=(x(s,t),y(s,t),z(s,t)),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f2172cb5b4fc6c6baa1abd025bd0360eed0d0889) with ![Image 25: {\displaystyle s}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/01d131dfd7673938b947072a13a9744fe997e632) and ![Image 26: {\displaystyle t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/65658b7b223af9e1acc877d848888ecdb4466560)[real](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number") variables, then a normal to _S_ is by definition a normal to a tangent plane, given by the cross product of the [partial derivatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_derivative "Partial derivative")![Image 27: {\displaystyle \mathbf {n} ={\frac {\partial \mathbf {r} }{\partial s}}\times {\frac {\partial \mathbf {r} }{\partial t}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ddaa2aae8df40ad6085f117068fa0f7d6f26f770)

If a surface ![Image 28: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2) is given [implicitly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Implicit_function "Implicit function") as the set of points ![Image 29: {\displaystyle (x,y,z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/22a8c93372e8f8b6e24d523bd5545aed3430baf4) satisfying ![Image 30: {\displaystyle F(x,y,z)=0,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/51dd0fd6845d9439591f64ff063527a58a5f0487) then a normal at a point ![Image 31: {\displaystyle (x,y,z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/22a8c93372e8f8b6e24d523bd5545aed3430baf4) on the surface is given by the [gradient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gradient "Gradient")![Image 32: {\displaystyle \mathbf {n} =\nabla F(x,y,z).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/448e560770e02dab889cc242bf9f910d36ff6b3d) since [the gradient at any point is perpendicular to the level set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Level_set#Level_sets_versus_the_gradient "Level set")![Image 33: {\displaystyle S.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/23bbb1f0f6ebdfa78b4fed06049640f7386bb44b)

For a surface ![Image 34: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2) in ![Image 35: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5) given as the graph of a function ![Image 36: {\displaystyle z=f(x,y),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1269bfd7a8dbf5a109363ce2a7992efdf8e406a9) an upward-pointing normal can be found either from the parametrization ![Image 37: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} (x,y)=(x,y,f(x,y)),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/30c6dacf41660068cbb8746141574184d5396652) giving ![Image 38: {\displaystyle \mathbf {n} ={\frac {\partial \mathbf {r} }{\partial x}}\times {\frac {\partial \mathbf {r} }{\partial y}}=\left(1,0,{\tfrac {\partial f}{\partial x}}\right)\times \left(0,1,{\tfrac {\partial f}{\partial y}}\right)=\left(-{\tfrac {\partial f}{\partial x}},-{\tfrac {\partial f}{\partial y}},1\right);}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e7a9ea0f1aed9c3b5e1f2eb6c7b9366c65d2d98) or more simply from its implicit form ![Image 39: {\displaystyle F(x,y,z)=z-f(x,y)=0,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5e742c57736d0a009746b70ba0536ba8d0bc5ecb) giving ![Image 40: {\displaystyle \mathbf {n} =\nabla F(x,y,z)=\left(-{\tfrac {\partial f}{\partial x}},-{\tfrac {\partial f}{\partial y}},1\right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a94ec76d612889e94ca7e181f805c0390879c3ef) Since a surface does not have a tangent plane at a [singular point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Singularity_(mathematics) "Singularity (mathematics)"), it has no well-defined normal at that point: for example, the vertex of a [cone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cone_(geometry) "Cone (geometry)"). In general, it is possible to define a normal almost everywhere for a surface that is [Lipschitz continuous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lipschitz_continuous "Lipschitz continuous").

[![Image 41](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Surface_normals.svg/330px-Surface_normals.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Surface_normals.svg)

A vector field of normals to a surface

The normal to a (hyper)surface is usually scaled to have [unit length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_vector "Unit vector"), but it does not have a unique direction, since its opposite is also a unit normal. For a surface which is the [topological boundary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boundary_(topology) "Boundary (topology)") of a set in three dimensions, one can distinguish between two **normal orientations**, the **inward-pointing normal** and **outer-pointing normal**. For an [oriented surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orientability "Orientability"), the normal is usually determined by the [right-hand rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right-hand_rule "Right-hand rule") or its analog in higher dimensions.

If the normal is constructed as the cross product of tangent vectors (as described in the text above), it is a [pseudovector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudovector "Pseudovector").

### Transforming normals

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Normal_(geometry)&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Transforming normals")]

in this section we only use the upper ![Image 42: {\displaystyle 3\times 3}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ddc0d4d6106875f8006be1d898512ca5843bad8e) matrix, as translation is irrelevant to the calculation

When applying a transform to a surface it is often useful to derive normals for the resulting surface from the original normals.

Specifically, given a 3×3 transformation matrix ![Image 43: {\displaystyle \mathbf {M} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38d39eeedc5ff95e3716415596fd9899bdf60eee) we can determine the matrix ![Image 44: {\displaystyle \mathbf {W} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/04749f1e87cca59c094da23c79cc64b085b0df12) that transforms a vector ![Image 45: {\displaystyle \mathbf {n} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4a720c341f39f52fd96028dab83edd34d400be46) perpendicular to the tangent plane ![Image 46: {\displaystyle \mathbf {t} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/16ff63cc74a931900e79b3caacbae3fa8cc66845) into a vector ![Image 47: {\displaystyle \mathbf {n} ^{\prime }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ff165a10ab2dcffcc173f7ca30a49767b66d1335) perpendicular to the transformed tangent plane ![Image 48: {\displaystyle \mathbf {Mt} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/61acc24dac0ba26affde652670ef76bfbc9138d2) by the following logic:

Write **n′** as ![Image 49: {\displaystyle \mathbf {Wn} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/986cbd5c237fb3db13d5c3d864035759daa45320) We must find ![Image 50: {\displaystyle \mathbf {W} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a23c4b1486482a8ea21de862b7141c9b423938cc)![Image 51: {\displaystyle {\begin{alignedat}{5}W\mathbb {n} {\text{ is perpendicular to }}M\mathbb {t} \quad \,&{\text{ if and only if }}\quad 0=(W\mathbb {n} )\cdot (M\mathbb {t} )\\&{\text{ if and only if }}\quad 0=(W\mathbb {n} )^{\mathrm {T} }(M\mathbb {t} )\\&{\text{ if and only if }}\quad 0=\left(\mathbb {n} ^{\mathrm {T} }W^{\mathrm {T} }\right)(M\mathbb {t} )\\&{\text{ if and only if }}\quad 0=\mathbb {n} ^{\mathrm {T} }\left(W^{\mathrm {T} }M\right)\mathbb {t} \\\end{alignedat}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f1c81df10a1620b69e53a92c3922be61a0b1fca1)

Choosing ![Image 52: {\displaystyle \mathbf {W} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/04749f1e87cca59c094da23c79cc64b085b0df12) such that ![Image 53: {\displaystyle W^{\mathrm {T} }M=I,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e89e87dac99b47e1b4b50b9951dbe3864be0b15d) or ![Image 54: {\displaystyle W=(M^{-1})^{\mathrm {T} },}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9674baeb53f2ef978b1148ac3a13d60390621961) will satisfy the above equation, giving a ![Image 55: {\displaystyle W\mathbb {n} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/65c53eed6135e9c624f559b453e8ec9ac7f3d452) perpendicular to ![Image 56: {\displaystyle M\mathbb {t} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e1f6951e17d54d7c18c181d391b3e8c872052a4) or an ![Image 57: {\displaystyle \mathbf {n} ^{\prime }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ff165a10ab2dcffcc173f7ca30a49767b66d1335) perpendicular to ![Image 58: {\displaystyle \mathbf {t} ^{\prime },}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6947fbc4201b771a4fd4533c14a750e52bbc3afa) as required.

Therefore, one should use the inverse transpose of the linear transformation when transforming surface normals. The inverse transpose is equal to the original matrix if the matrix is orthonormal, that is, purely rotational with no scaling or shearing.

Alternatively, if the hyperplane is defined as the solution set of a single linear equation ⁠![Image 69: {\displaystyle a_{1}x_{1}+\cdots +a_{n}x_{n}=c}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bb4540a22f46a349cfa193a37e1c6a2fdb6dd501)⁠, then the vector ![Image 70: {\displaystyle \mathbf {n} =\left(a_{1},\ldots ,a_{n}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1e0b8907b6496c2fbedf1922c2da373fed964e72) is a normal.

The definition of a normal to a surface in three-dimensional space can be extended to ![Image 71: {\displaystyle (n-1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/df88c6333caaf6471cf277f24b802ff9931b133e)-dimensional [hypersurfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypersurface "Hypersurface") in ⁠![Image 72: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d)⁠. A hypersurface may be [locally](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_property "Local property") defined implicitly as the set of points ![Image 73: {\displaystyle (x_{1},x_{2},\ldots ,x_{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47d8b3ec62633086002489cad8df4214e9585880) satisfying an equation ⁠![Image 74: {\displaystyle F(x_{1},x_{2},\ldots ,x_{n})=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dbde5e99d3972b079fdfeba6742d06fbf6374a97)⁠, where ![Image 75: {\displaystyle F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/545fd099af8541605f7ee55f08225526be88ce57) is a given [scalar function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_field "Scalar field"). If ![Image 76: {\displaystyle F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/545fd099af8541605f7ee55f08225526be88ce57) is [continuously differentiable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuously_differentiable "Continuously differentiable") then the hypersurface is a [differentiable manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differentiable_manifold "Differentiable manifold") in the [neighbourhood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neighbourhood_(mathematics) "Neighbourhood (mathematics)") of the points where the [gradient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gradient "Gradient") is not zero. At these points a normal vector is given by the gradient: ![Image 77: {\displaystyle \mathbb {n} =\nabla F\left(x_{1},x_{2},\ldots ,x_{n}\right)=\left({\tfrac {\partial F}{\partial x_{1}}},{\tfrac {\partial F}{\partial x_{2}}},\ldots ,{\tfrac {\partial F}{\partial x_{n}}}\right)\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2836f448c5e183fd7ff4c2bdd816a8e3a12fc476)

The **normal line** is the one-dimensional subspace with basis ![Image 78: {\displaystyle \{\mathbf {n} \}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a1cfb306c65b75d9d5a533c1df0c911363736e4b)

A vector that is normal to the space spanned by the linearly independent vectors **v**1, ..., **v**_r_−1 and falls within the r-dimensional space spanned by the linearly independent vectors **v**1, ..., **v**_r_ is given by the r-th column of the matrix Λ = _V_(_V_ T _V_)−1, where the matrix _V_ = (**v**1, ..., **v**_r_) is the juxtaposition of the r column vectors. (Proof: _V_ T Λ = _I_ so each of **v**1, ..., **v**_r_−1 is perpendicular to the last column of Λ.) This formula works even when r is less than the dimension of the Euclidean space n. The formula simplifies to Λ = (_V_ T)−1 when _r_ = _n_.

----------------------------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Normal_(geometry)&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Varieties defined by implicit equations in n-dimensional space")]

A **[differential variety](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytic_variety "Analytic variety")** defined by implicit equations in the ![Image 79: {\displaystyle ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d) is the set of the common zeros of a finite set of differentiable functions in ![Image 81: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) variables ![Image 82: {\displaystyle f_{1}\left(x_{1},\ldots ,x_{n}\right),\ldots ,f_{k}\left(x_{1},\ldots ,x_{n}\right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9d0ce85dd36aa0483511003fad8dc9cdf59a0929) The [Jacobian matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacobian_matrix "Jacobian matrix") of the variety is the ![Image 83: {\displaystyle k\times n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8e173885ca0f29dd406df781144cd635276c68e1) matrix whose ![Image 84: {\displaystyle The **normal (affine) space** at a point ![Image 91: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a) of the variety is the [affine subspace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_subspace "Affine subspace") passing through ![Image 92: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a) and generated by the normal vector space at ![Image 93: {\displaystyle P.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/49f4f085fcd14302f4f7a9bbdf77e816cccb3bc9)

These definitions may be extended _verbatim_ to the points where the variety is not a manifold.

Let _V_ be the variety defined in the 3-dimensional space by the equations ![Image 94: {\displaystyle x\,y=0,\quad z=0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/79aad191142ac3be688c36de767bfa4941a0da8c) This variety is the union of the ![Image 95: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4)-axis and the ![Image 96: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d)-axis.

At a point ![Image 97: {\displaystyle (a,0,0),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c15831931b8ce05012e962ac347c457270e6cc4a) where ![Image 98: {\displaystyle a\neq 0,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2068d811f09c9ca1437234192c052786eae4e41f) the rows of the Jacobian matrix are ![Image 99: {\displaystyle (0,0,1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/25b35543ef5f96df2e9aea6cced9bdd57dab7e1a) and ![Image 100: {\displaystyle (0,a,0).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d31ff300662cd6f2b2880ff859174cb229b18ee2) Thus the normal affine space is the plane of equation ![Image 101: {\displaystyle x=a.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e917ab4f4e8b2fa913a9ec28880e3ee4a5a00765) Similarly, if ![Image 102: {\displaystyle b\neq 0,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/69238caa691e75f448aae578c1d7d93e8c9840b9) the _[normal plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal\_plane\_(geometry) "Normal plane (geometry)")_ at ![Image 103: {\displaystyle (0,b,0)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2f0cc4529c22456d00199991960eb7ebf2b0da41) is the plane of equation ![Image 104: {\displaystyle y=b.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4ef1bd48463dbb0709b15f72779baf4c80f980a2)

At the point ![Image 105: {\displaystyle (0,0,0)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/28c6fd55d5621fd95ca93549660fbb355fd9bd22) the rows of the Jacobian matrix are ![Image 106: {\displaystyle (0,0,1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/25b35543ef5f96df2e9aea6cced9bdd57dab7e1a) and ![Image 107: {\displaystyle (0,0,0).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/56b5c983e8388a9ad4a6dab868671f87a98f5bb1) Thus the normal vector space and the normal affine space have dimension 1 and the normal affine space is the ![Image 108: {\displaystyle z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf368e72c009decd9b6686ee84a375632e11de98)-axis.

*   Surface normals are useful in defining [surface integrals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_integral "Surface integral") of [vector fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_field "Vector field").
*   Surface normals are commonly used in [3D computer graphics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3D_computer_graphics "3D computer graphics") for [lighting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_graphics_lighting "Computer graphics lighting") calculations (see [Lambert's cosine law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lambert%27s_cosine_law "Lambert's cosine law")), often adjusted by [normal mapping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_mapping "Normal mapping").
*   [Render layers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Render_layers "Render layers") containing surface normal information may be used in [digital compositing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digital_compositing "Digital compositing") to change the apparent lighting of rendered elements.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]
*   In [computer vision](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_vision "Computer vision"), the shapes of 3D objects are estimated from surface normals using [photometric stereo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photometric_stereo "Photometric stereo").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_(geometry)#cite_note-1)
*   The normal vector may be obtained as the gradient of the [signed distance function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Signed_distance_function "Signed distance function").

Normal in geometric optics
--------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Normal_(geometry)&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Normal in geometric optics")]

[![Image 109](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Reflection_angles.svg/170px-Reflection_angles.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Reflection_angles.svg)

Diagram of specular reflection

The **normal ray** is the outward-pointing ray [perpendicular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular "Perpendicular") to the surface of an [optical medium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optical_medium "Optical medium") at a given point.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_(geometry)#cite_note-2) In [reflection of light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflection_of_light "Reflection of light"), the [angle of incidence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle_of_incidence_(optics) "Angle of incidence (optics)") and the [angle of reflection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle_of_reflection "Angle of reflection") are respectively the angle between the normal and the [incident ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Incident_ray "Incident ray") (on the [plane of incidence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_of_incidence "Plane of incidence")) and the angle between the normal and the [reflected ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflected_ray "Reflected ray").
