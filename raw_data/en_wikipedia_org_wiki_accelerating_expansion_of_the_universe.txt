Lambda-CDM, accelerated [expansion of the universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expansion_of_the_universe "Expansion of the universe"). The timeline in this schematic diagram extends from the [Big Bang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang "Big Bang")/inflation era 13.8 billion years ago to the present cosmological time.

[Observations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observational_astronomy "Observational astronomy") show that the [expansion of the universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expansion_of_the_universe "Expansion of the universe") is [accelerating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acceleration_(differential_geometry) "Acceleration (differential geometry)"), such that the [velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity "Velocity") at which a distant [galaxy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy "Galaxy") recedes from the observer is continuously increasing with time.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-NYT-20170220-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-AM-20170818-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-ES-20180311-3) The accelerated expansion of the [universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe "Universe") was discovered in 1998 by two independent projects, the [Supernova Cosmology Project](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supernova_Cosmology_Project "Supernova Cosmology Project") and the [High-Z Supernova Search Team](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-Z_Supernova_Search_Team "High-Z Supernova Search Team"), which used distant [type Ia supernovae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Type_Ia_supernovae "Type Ia supernovae") to measure the acceleration.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-BBC-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-peebles-6) The idea was that as type Ia supernovae have almost the same intrinsic brightness (a [standard candle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_candle "Standard candle")), and since objects that are further away appear dimmer, the observed brightness of these supernovae can be used to measure the distance to them. The distance can then be compared to the supernovae's cosmological [redshift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift "Redshift"), which measures how much the universe has expanded since the supernova occurred; the [Hubble law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble_law "Hubble law") established that the further away an object is, the faster it is receding. The unexpected result was that objects in the universe are moving away from one another at an accelerating rate. Cosmologists at the time expected that recession velocity would always be decelerating, due to the gravitational attraction of the matter in the universe. Three members of these two groups have subsequently been awarded [Nobel Prizes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nobel_Prize "Nobel Prize") for their discovery.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-7) Confirmatory evidence has been found in [baryon acoustic oscillations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baryon_acoustic_oscillations "Baryon acoustic oscillations"), and in analyses of the [clustering of galaxies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_cluster "Galaxy cluster").

The accelerated expansion of the universe is thought to have begun since the universe entered its [dark-energy-dominated era](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark-energy-dominated_era "Dark-energy-dominated era") roughly 5 billion years ago.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-Frieman-8)[[notes 1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-9) Within the framework of [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity"), an accelerated expansion can be accounted for by a positive value of the [cosmological constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmological_constant "Cosmological constant")Λ, equivalent to the presence of a positive [vacuum energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vacuum_energy "Vacuum energy"), dubbed "[dark energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_energy "Dark energy")". While there are alternative possible explanations, the description assuming dark energy (positive Λ) is used in the standard model of [cosmology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_cosmology "Physical cosmology"), which also includes [cold dark matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cold_dark_matter "Cold dark matter") (CDM) and is known as the [Lambda-CDM model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lambda-CDM_model "Lambda-CDM model").

In the decades since the detection of [cosmic microwave background](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background "Cosmic microwave background") (CMB) in 1965,[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-Penzias&Wilson-10) the [Big Bang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang "Big Bang") model has become the most accepted model explaining the evolution of our universe. The [Friedmann equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedmann_equations "Friedmann equations") defines how the [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy "Energy") in the universe drives its expansion.

![Image 2: {\displaystyle H^{2}={\left({\frac {\dot {a}}{a}}\right)}^{2}={\frac {8{\pi }G}{3}}\rho -{\frac {{\kappa }c^{2}}{a^{2}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/22754c070dcdeddad2e5a688c625367eb3c4ff03) where κ represents the [curvature of the universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvature_of_the_universe "Curvature of the universe"), _a_(_t_) is the [scale factor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scale_factor_(cosmology) "Scale factor (cosmology)"), ρ is the total energy density of the universe, and H is the [Hubble parameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble_parameter "Hubble parameter").[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-11)

The [critical density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Critical_density_(cosmology) "Critical density (cosmology)") is defined as ![Image 3: {\displaystyle \rho _{c}={\frac {3H^{2}}{8{\pi }G}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/36acb8b36227aa8d09fda30545f8adf633f1c87e) and the [density parameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Density_parameter "Density parameter")![Image 4: {\displaystyle \Omega ={\frac {\rho }{\rho _{c}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f3238ba0776acb8b2a990100d816acf45306b129)

The Hubble parameter can then be rewritten as ![Image 5: {\displaystyle H(a)=H_{0}{\sqrt {{\Omega _{k}a^{-2}+\Omega }_{m}a^{-3}+\Omega _{r}a^{-4}+\Omega _{\mathrm {DE} }a^{-3(1+w)}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/98885008697d8b814e3a9c7ca4632fb96778a7af) where the four currently hypothesized contributors to the energy density of the universe are [curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shape_of_the_universe "Shape of the universe"), [matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matter "Matter"), [radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiation "Radiation") and [dark energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_energy "Dark energy").[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-Bassett-12) Each of the components decreases with the expansion of the universe (increasing scale factor), except perhaps the dark energy term. It is the values of these cosmological parameters which physicists use to determine the acceleration of the universe.

The [acceleration equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedmann_equations "Friedmann equations") describes the evolution of the scale factor with time ![Image 6: {\displaystyle {\frac {\ddot {a}}{a}}=-{\frac {4{\pi }G}{3}}\left(\rho +{\frac {3P}{c^{2}}}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/74de0bee62377fd5e7395bcaf00deda188ecc880) where the [pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pressure "Pressure")P is defined by the cosmological model chosen (see also: [Explanatory models](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#Explanatory_models)).

Physicists at one time were so assured of the deceleration of the universe's expansion that they introduced a so-called [deceleration parameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deceleration_parameter "Deceleration parameter")_q_ 0.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-Ryden-13) Recent observations indicate this deceleration parameter is negative.

### Relation to inflation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Relation to inflation")]

According to the theory of [cosmic inflation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inflation_(cosmology) "Inflation (cosmology)"), the very early universe underwent a period of very rapid, quasi-exponential expansion. While the time-scale for this period of expansion was far shorter than that of the existing expansion, this was a period of accelerated expansion with some similarities to the current epoch.

### Technical definition

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Technical definition")]

The definition of "accelerating expansion" is that the second time derivative of the cosmic scale factor, ![Image 7: {\displaystyle {\ddot {a}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6b4d8db669dea9bee5d348bbecf6434914a406ac), is positive, which is equivalent to the [deceleration parameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deceleration_parameter "Deceleration parameter"), ![Image 8: {\displaystyle q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/06809d64fa7c817ffc7e323f85997f783dbdf71d), being negative. However, note this does **not** imply that the [Hubble parameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble_constant "Hubble constant") is increasing with time. Since the Hubble parameter is defined as ![Image 9: {\displaystyle H(t)\equiv {\dot {a}}(t)/a(t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d249ba1a079d982bf9459dcd81a006b1087ff858), it follows from the definitions that the derivative of the Hubble parameter is given by ![Image 10: {\displaystyle {\frac {dH}{dt}}=-H^{2}(1+q)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6074f6135894d0c22f4a25365aae3a755f9624ba) so the Hubble parameter is decreasing with time unless ![Image 11: {\displaystyle q<-1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/11bb76dbc25dd5c71d6341ee15ace9745bbd004c). Observations prefer ![Image 12: {\displaystyle q\approx -0.55}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cf2d1fe57cfccd164572779e02cef42ddadc1900), which implies that ![Image 13: {\displaystyle {\ddot {a}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6b4d8db669dea9bee5d348bbecf6434914a406ac) is positive but ![Image 14: {\displaystyle dH/dt}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/353ae1998e149fee5da0029c488ffa5a56b7ef76) is negative. Essentially, this implies that the cosmic recession velocity of any one particular galaxy is increasing with time, but its velocity/distance ratio is still decreasing; thus different galaxies expanding across a sphere of fixed radius cross the sphere more slowly at later times.

It is seen from above that the case of "zero acceleration/deceleration" corresponds to ![Image 15: {\displaystyle a(t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bb7931a26b0d360eaf90aa45247d2de5c984d5d8) is a linear function of ![Image 16: {\displaystyle t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/65658b7b223af9e1acc877d848888ecdb4466560), ![Image 17: {\displaystyle q=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/654c2d5dc1a26e0af36dc0deb5fd252c6178977a), ![Image 18: {\displaystyle {\dot {a}}=const}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6abff0855b028fb3d93330a4604750a6254d1b61), and ![Image 19: {\displaystyle H(t)=1/t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b5d8a9afae16ce95eb5860af77bc5b6fd3596fd3).

Evidence for acceleration
-------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Evidence for acceleration")]

The rate of expansion of the universe can be analyzed using the [magnitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnitude_(astronomy) "Magnitude (astronomy)")-redshift relationship of astronomical objects using [standard candles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_distance_ladder#Standard_candles "Cosmic distance ladder"), or their distance-redshift relationship using [standard rulers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_ruler "Standard ruler"). Also a factor is the growth of [large-scale structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Large-scale_structure_of_the_universe "Large-scale structure of the universe"), finding that the observed values of the cosmological parameters are best described by models which include an accelerating expansion.

### Supernova observation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Supernova observation")]

[![Image 20](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Asymmetric_Ashes_%28artist%27s_impression%29.jpg/250px-Asymmetric_Ashes_%28artist%27s_impression%29.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Asymmetric_Ashes_(artist%27s_impression).jpg)

Artist's impression of a Type Ia supernova, as revealed by spectro-polarimetry observations

In 1998, the first evidence for acceleration came from the observation of [Type Ia supernovae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Type_Ia_supernova "Type Ia supernova"), which are exploding [white dwarf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_dwarf "White dwarf") stars that have exceeded their [stability limit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chandrasekhar_limit "Chandrasekhar limit"). Because they all have similar masses, their intrinsic [luminosity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luminosity "Luminosity") can be standardized. Repeated imaging of selected areas of the sky is used to discover the supernovae, then follow-up observations give their peak brightness, which is converted into a quantity known as luminosity distance (see [distance measures in cosmology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distance_measures_in_cosmology "Distance measures in cosmology") for details).[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-14)[Spectral lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectral_line "Spectral line") of their light can be used to determine their [redshift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift "Redshift").

For supernovae at redshift less than around 0.1, or light travel time less than 10 percent of the age of the universe, this gives a nearly linear distance–redshift relation due to [Hubble's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble%27s_law "Hubble's law"). At larger distances, since the expansion rate of the universe has changed over time, the distance-redshift relation deviates from linearity, and this deviation depends on how the expansion rate has changed over time. The full calculation requires computer integration of the Friedmann equation, but a simple derivation can be given as follows: the redshift z directly gives the [cosmic scale factor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_scale_factor "Cosmic scale factor") at the time the supernova exploded.

![Image 21: {\displaystyle a(t)={\frac {1}{1+z}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3fc50f64b94c0a8d7356a0726ca0a303db1c8b95)

So a supernova with a measured redshift _z_ = 0.5 implies the universe was ⁠1/1 + 0.5⁠=⁠2/3⁠ of its present size when the supernova exploded. In the case of accelerated expansion, ![Image 22: {\displaystyle {\ddot {a}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6b4d8db669dea9bee5d348bbecf6434914a406ac) is positive; therefore, ![Image 23: {\displaystyle {\dot {a}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c9167337d4bf7b72b9099d7d5714497cda8643ca) was smaller in the past than today. Thus, an accelerating universe took a longer time to expand from 2/3 to 1 times its present size, compared to a non-accelerating universe with constant ![Image 24: {\displaystyle {\dot {a}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c9167337d4bf7b72b9099d7d5714497cda8643ca) and the same present-day value of the Hubble constant. This results in a larger light-travel time, larger distance and fainter supernovae, which corresponds to the actual observations. [Adam Riess](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adam_Riess "Adam Riess")_et al._ found that "the distances of the high-redshift SNe Ia were, on average, 10% to 15% further than expected in a low mass density _Ω_ M = 0.2 universe without a cosmological constant".[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-Riess-15) This means that the measured high-redshift distances were too large, compared to nearby ones, for a decelerating universe.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-Pain-16)

Several researchers have questioned the majority opinion on the acceleration or the assumption of the "[cosmological principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmological_principle "Cosmological principle")" (that the universe is homogeneous and isotropic).[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-17) For example, a 2019 paper analyzed the [Joint Light-curve Analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Joint_Light-curve_Analysis&action=edit&redlink=1 "Joint Light-curve Analysis (page does not exist)") catalog of Type Ia supernovas, containing ten times as many supernova as were used in the 1998 analyses, and concluded that there was little evidence for a "monopole", that is, for an isotropic acceleration in all directions[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-18)[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-19)(see also: [§Alternative theories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#Alternative_theories), below).

### Baryon acoustic oscillations

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Baryon acoustic oscillations")]

In the early universe before [recombination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recombination_(cosmology) "Recombination (cosmology)") and [decoupling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoupling_(cosmology) "Decoupling (cosmology)") took place, [photons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon "Photon") and matter existed in a [primordial plasma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Structure_formation#Primordial_plasma "Structure formation"). Points of higher density in the photon-baryon plasma would contract, being compressed by gravity until the pressure became too large and they expanded again.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-Ryden-13) This contraction and expansion created vibrations in the plasma analogous to [sound waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sound_waves "Sound waves"). Since [dark matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter "Dark matter") only interacts [gravitationally](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitationally "Gravitationally"), it stayed at the centre of the sound wave, the origin of the original overdensity. When decoupling occurred, approximately 380,000 years after the Big Bang,[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-20) photons separated from matter and were able to [stream freely](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_streaming "Free streaming") through the universe, creating the [cosmic microwave background](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background "Cosmic microwave background") as we know it. This left shells of [baryonic matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baryonic_matter "Baryonic matter") at a fixed radius from the overdensities of dark matter, a distance known as the sound horizon. As time passed and the universe expanded, it was at these inhomogeneities of matter density where galaxies started to form. So by looking at the distances at which galaxies at different redshifts tend to cluster, it is possible to determine a standard [angular diameter distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_diameter_distance "Angular diameter distance") and use that to compare to the distances predicted by different cosmological models.

Peaks have been found in the correlation function (the probability that two galaxies will be a certain distance apart) at 100 _h_−1[Mpc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parsec#Megaparsecs_and_gigaparsecs "Parsec"),[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-Bassett-12) (where _h_ is the [dimensionless Hubble constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimensionless_Hubble_constant "Dimensionless Hubble constant")) indicating that this is the size of the sound horizon today, and by comparing this to the sound horizon at the time of decoupling (using the CMB), we can confirm the accelerated expansion of the universe.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-21)

### Clusters of galaxies

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Clusters of galaxies")]

Measuring the mass functions of [galaxy clusters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_cluster "Galaxy cluster"), which describe the [number density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_density "Number density") of the clusters above a threshold mass, also provides evidence for dark energy [_[further explanation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Please\_clarify "Wikipedia:Please clarify")_].[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-22) By comparing these mass functions at high and low redshifts to those predicted by different cosmological models, values for w and Ω m are obtained which confirm a low matter density and a non-zero amount of dark energy.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-Pain-16)

### Age of the universe

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Age of the universe")]

Given a cosmological model with certain values of the cosmological density parameters, it is possible to integrate the [Friedmann equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedmann_equations "Friedmann equations") and derive the age of the universe.

![Image 25: {\displaystyle t_{0}=\int _{0}^{1}{\frac {da}{\dot {a}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c8a6c606cee51951b7a6ff0d95c64228fb1dd9b8)

By comparing this to actual measured values of the cosmological parameters, we can confirm the validity of a model which is accelerating now, and had a slower expansion in the past.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-Pain-16)

### Gravitational waves as standard sirens

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Gravitational waves as standard sirens")]

Recent discoveries of [gravitational waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_wave "Gravitational wave") through [LIGO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LIGO "LIGO") and [VIRGO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgo_interferometer "Virgo interferometer")[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-:0-23)[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-24)[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-:1-25) not only confirmed Einstein's predictions but also opened a new window into the universe. These gravitational waves can work as sort of [standard sirens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_siren "Standard siren") to measure the expansion rate of the universe. Abbot et al. 2017 measured the Hubble constant value to be approximately 70 kilometres per second per megaparsec.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-:0-23) The amplitudes of the strain 'h' is dependent on the masses of the objects causing waves, distances from observation point and gravitational waves detection frequencies. The associated distance measures are dependent on the cosmological parameters like the Hubble Constant for nearby objects[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-:0-23) and will be dependent on other cosmological parameters like the dark energy density, matter density, etc. for distant sources.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-26)[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-:1-25)

[![Image 26](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Dark_Energy.jpg/500px-Dark_Energy.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Dark_Energy.jpg)

The expansion of the Universe accelerating. Time flows from bottom to top

The most important property of dark energy is that it has negative pressure (repulsive action) which is distributed relatively homogeneously in space.

![Image 27: {\displaystyle P=wc^{2}\rho }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5644db7370eb0fa9aa322c4e322c9c3f86483c37) where c is the speed of light and ρ is the energy density. Different theories of dark energy suggest different values of w, with _w_< −⁠1/3⁠ for cosmic acceleration (this leads to a positive value of ä in the [acceleration equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#Background) above).

The simplest explanation for dark energy is that it is a cosmological constant or [vacuum energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vacuum_energy "Vacuum energy"); in this case _w_ = −1. This leads to the [Lambda-CDM model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lambda-CDM_model "Lambda-CDM model"), which has generally been known as the Standard Model of Cosmology from 2003 through the present, since it is the simplest model in good agreement with a variety of recent observations. Riess _et al._ found that their results from supernova observations favoured expanding models with positive cosmological constant (_Ω λ_> 0) and an accelerated expansion (_q_ 0< 0).[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-Riess-15)

These observations allow the possibility of a cosmological model containing a dark energy component with equation of state _w_< −1. This phantom energy density would become infinite in finite time, causing such a huge gravitational repulsion that the universe would lose all structure and end in a [Big Rip](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Rip "Big Rip").[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-27) For example, for _w_ = −⁠3/2⁠ and _H_ 0=70 km·s−1·Mpc−1, the time remaining before the universe ends in this Big Rip is 22 billion years.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-28)

### Alternative theories

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Alternative theories")]

There are many alternative explanations for the accelerating universe. Some examples are [quintessence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quintessence_(physics) "Quintessence (physics)"), a proposed form of dark energy with a non-constant state equation, whose density decreases with time. A [negative mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Negative_mass "Negative mass") cosmology does not assume that the mass density of the universe is positive (as is done in supernova observations), and instead finds a negative cosmological constant. [Occam's razor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Occam%27s_razor "Occam's razor") also suggests that this is the 'more parsimonious hypothesis'.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-EA-20181205-29)[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-ARX-2018-30)[Dark fluid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_fluid "Dark fluid") is an alternative explanation for accelerating expansion which attempts to unite dark matter and dark energy into a single framework.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-31) Alternatively, some authors have argued that the accelerated expansion of the universe could be due to a repulsive [gravitational interaction of antimatter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_interaction_of_antimatter "Gravitational interaction of antimatter")[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-benoit-levy-32)[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-Hajdukovic-33)[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-villata13-34) or a deviation of the gravitational laws from general relativity, such as [massive gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Massive_gravity "Massive gravity"), meaning that gravitons themselves have mass.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-35) The measurement of the speed of gravity with the gravitational wave event [GW170817](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GW170817 "GW170817") ruled out many modified gravity theories as alternative explanations to dark energy.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-36)[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-37)[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-38) Another type of model, the backreaction conjecture,[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-39)[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-40) was proposed by cosmologist Syksy Räsänen:[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-NS2007-41) the rate of expansion is not homogenous, but Earth is in a region where expansion is faster than the background. Inhomogeneities in the early universe cause the formation of walls and bubbles, where the inside of a bubble has less matter than on average. According to general relativity, space is less curved than on the walls, and thus appears to have more volume and a higher expansion rate. In the denser regions, the expansion is slowed by a higher gravitational attraction. Therefore, the inward collapse of the denser regions looks the same as an accelerating expansion of the bubbles, leading us to conclude that the universe is undergoing an accelerated expansion.[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-42) The benefit is that it does not require any new physics such as dark energy. Räsänen does not consider the model likely, but without any falsification, it must remain a possibility. It would require rather large density fluctuations (20%) to work.[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-NS2007-41)

[Shockwave cosmology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shockwave_cosmology "Shockwave cosmology"), proposed by Joel Smoller and Blake Temple in 2003, has the “big bang” as an explosion inside a black hole, producing the expanding volume of space and matter that includes the observable universe.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-43) A related theory by Smoller, Temple, and Vogler proposes that this shockwave may have resulted in our part of the universe having a lower density than that surrounding it, causing the accelerated expansion normally attributed to dark energy.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-:3-44)[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-:2-45) They also propose that this related theory could be tested: a universe with dark energy should give a figure for the cubic correction to redshift versus luminosity C = −0.180 at a = a whereas for Smoller, Temple, and Vogler's alternative C should be positive rather than negative. They give a more precise calculation for their shockwave model alternative as: the cubic correction to redshift versus luminosity at a = a is C = 0.359.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-:2-45)

Although shockwave cosmology produces a universe that "looks essentially identical to the aftermath of the big bang",[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-46) cosmologists consider that it needs further development before it could be considered as a more advantageous model than the big bang theory (or standard model) in explaining the universe. In particular, and especially for the proposed alternative to dark energy, it would need to explain big bang nucleosynthesis, the quantitative details of the microwave background anisotropies, the Lyman-alpha forest, and galaxy surveys.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-:3-44)

A final possibility is that dark energy is an illusion caused by some bias in measurements. For example, if we are located in an emptier-than-average region of space, the observed cosmic expansion rate could be mistaken for a variation in time, or acceleration.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-47)[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-48)[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-49)[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-50) A different approach uses a cosmological extension of the [equivalence principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equivalence_principle "Equivalence principle") to show how space might appear to be expanding more rapidly in the voids surrounding our local cluster. While weak, such effects considered cumulatively over billions of years could become significant, creating the illusion of cosmic acceleration, and making it appear as if we live in a [Hubble bubble](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble_Bubble_(astronomy) "Hubble Bubble (astronomy)").[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-51)[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-52)[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-53) Yet other possibilities are that the accelerated expansion of the universe is an illusion caused by the relative motion of us to the rest of the universe,[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-54)[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-55) or that the supernova sample size used wasn't large enough.[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-sarkar-56)[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-ox.ac.uk-57)

Consequences for the universe
-----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Consequences for the universe")]

As the universe expands, the density of radiation and ordinary [dark matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter "Dark matter") declines more quickly than the density of [dark energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_energy "Dark energy") (see [equation of state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equation_of_state_(cosmology) "Equation of state (cosmology)")) and, eventually, dark energy dominates. Specifically, when the scale of the universe doubles, the density of matter is reduced by a factor of 8, but the density of dark energy is nearly unchanged (it is exactly constant if the dark energy is the [cosmological constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmological_constant "Cosmological constant")).[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-Ryden-13)

In models where dark energy is the cosmological constant, the universe will expand exponentially with time in the far future, coming closer and closer to a [de Sitter universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_Sitter_universe "De Sitter universe"). This will eventually lead to all evidence for the Big Bang disappearing, as the cosmic microwave background is redshifted to lower intensities and longer wavelengths. Eventually, its frequency will be low enough that it will be absorbed by the [interstellar medium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_medium "Interstellar medium"), and so be screened from any observer within the galaxy. This will occur when the universe is less than 50 times its existing age, leading to the end of any life as the distant universe turns dark.[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-58)

A constantly expanding universe with a non-zero cosmological constant has mass density decreasing over time. Under such a scenario, it is understood that all matter will ionize and disintegrate into isolated stable particles such as [electrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrons "Electrons") and [neutrinos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutrinos "Neutrinos"), with all complex structures dissipating.[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe#cite_note-59) This is called "[heat death of the universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat_death_of_the_universe "Heat death of the universe")" (or the [Big Freeze](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Freeze "Big Freeze")).

Alternatives for the [ultimate fate of the universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultimate_fate_of_the_universe "Ultimate fate of the universe") include the [Big Rip](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Rip "Big Rip") mentioned above, a [Big Bounce](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bounce "Big Bounce"), or a [Big Crunch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Crunch "Big Crunch").
