The [Roman surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_surface "Roman surface") is non-orientable.

In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), **orientability** is a property of some [topological spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_space "Topological space") such as [real vector spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_vector_space "Real vector space"), [Euclidean spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space"), [surfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_(topology) "Surface (topology)"), and more generally [manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manifold "Manifold") that allows a consistent definition of "clockwise" and "anticlockwise".[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orientability#cite_note-1) A space is **orientable** if such a consistent definition exists. In this case, there are two possible definitions, and a choice between them is an **orientation** of the space. Real vector spaces, Euclidean spaces, and [spheres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere "Sphere") are orientable. A space is **non-orientable** if "clockwise" is changed into "counterclockwise" after running through some [loops](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Loop_(topology) "Loop (topology)") in it, and coming back to the starting point. This means that a [geometric shape](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_shape "Geometric shape"), such as [![Image 4](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Small_pie.svg/20px-Small_pie.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Small_pie.svg), that moves continuously along such a loop is changed into its own [mirror image](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mirror_image "Mirror image")[![Image 5](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Pie_2.svg/20px-Pie_2.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Pie_2.svg). A [Möbius strip](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M%C3%B6bius_strip "Möbius strip") is an example of a non-orientable space.

Various equivalent formulations of orientability can be given, depending on the desired application and level of generality. Formulations applicable to general topological manifolds often employ methods of [homology theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homology_theory "Homology theory"), whereas for [differentiable manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differentiable_manifolds "Differentiable manifolds") more structure is present, allowing a formulation in terms of [differential forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_form "Differential form"). A generalization of the notion of orientability of a space is that of orientability of a family of spaces parameterized by some other space (a [fiber bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiber_bundle "Fiber bundle")) for which an orientation must be selected in each of the spaces which varies continuously with respect to changes in the parameter values.

Orientable surfaces
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Orientability&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Orientable surfaces")]

[![Image 6](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Surface_orientation.gif/330px-Surface_orientation.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Surface_orientation.gif)

In this animation, a simple analogy is made using a gear that rotates according to the right-hand rule on a surface's normal vector. The orientation of the curves given by the boundaries is given by the direction in which the dots move as they are pushed by the moving gear. On a non-orientable surface, such as the Möbius strip, the boundary would have to move in both directions at once, which is not possible.

A surface ![Image 7: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2) in the [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space")![Image 8: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5) is _**orientable**_ if a [chiral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chirality_(mathematics) "Chirality (mathematics)") two-dimensional figure (for example, [![Image 9](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Small_pie.svg/20px-Small_pie.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Small_pie.svg)) cannot be moved around the surface and back to where it started so that it looks like its own mirror image ([![Image 10](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Pie_2.svg/20px-Pie_2.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Pie_2.svg)). Otherwise the surface is _**non-orientable**_. An abstract surface (i.e., a two-dimensional [manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manifold "Manifold")) is orientable if a consistent concept of clockwise rotation can be defined on the surface in a continuous manner. That is to say that a loop going around one way on the surface can never be continuously deformed (without overlapping itself) to a loop going around the opposite way. This turns out to be equivalent to the question of whether the surface contains no subset that is [homeomorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homeomorphic "Homeomorphic") to the [Möbius strip](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M%C3%B6bius_strip "Möbius strip"). Thus, for surfaces, the Möbius strip may be considered the source of all non-orientability.

For an orientable surface, a consistent choice of "clockwise" (as opposed to counter-clockwise) is called an _**orientation**_, and the surface is called _**oriented**_. For surfaces embedded in Euclidean space, an orientation is specified by the choice of a continuously varying [surface normal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_normal "Surface normal")![Image 11: {\displaystyle \mathbf {n} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4a720c341f39f52fd96028dab83edd34d400be46) at every point. If such a normal exists at all, then there are always two ways to select it: ![Image 12: {\displaystyle \mathbf {n} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4a720c341f39f52fd96028dab83edd34d400be46) or ![Image 13: {\displaystyle -\mathbf {n} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a4e701c8949cabaf6d6285c2f01e2e653cd0a439). More generally, an orientable surface admits exactly two orientations, and the distinction between an orient _ed_ surface and an orient _able_ surface is subtle and frequently blurred. An orientable surface is an abstract surface that admits an orientation, while an oriented surface is a surface that is abstractly orientable, and has the additional datum of a choice of one of the two possible orientations.

Most surfaces encountered in the physical world are orientable. [Spheres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere "Sphere"), [planes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_(mathematics) "Plane (mathematics)"), and [tori](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torus "Torus") are orientable, for example. But [Möbius strips](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M%C3%B6bius_strip "Möbius strip"), [real projective planes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_projective_plane "Real projective plane"), and [Klein bottles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Klein_bottle "Klein bottle") are non-orientable. They, as visualized in ![Image 14: {\displaystyle 3}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/991e33c6e207b12546f15bdfee8b5726eafbbb2f)-dimensions, all have just one side. The real projective plane and Klein bottle cannot be embedded in ![Image 15: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5), only [immersed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immersion_(mathematics) "Immersion (mathematics)") with nice intersections.

Note that locally an embedded surface always has two sides, so a near-sighted ant crawling on a one-sided surface would think there is an "other side". The essence of one-sidedness is that the ant can crawl from one side of the surface to the "other" without going through the surface or flipping over an edge, but simply by crawling far enough.

In general, the property of being orientable is not equivalent to being two-sided; however, this holds when the ambient space (such as ![Image 16: {\displaystyle R^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0ab2376f6a3a3b77ddc94ade4f6fbc96e85ca29b) above) is orientable. For example, a torus embedded in

![Image 17: {\displaystyle K^{2}\times S^{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9f5ed22969a1699f0b781701c3bc189c0404c6eb)
can be one-sided, and a Klein bottle in the same space can be two-sided; here ![Image 18: {\displaystyle K^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f123baef27cc7331ccc361c0c4a18c2faedfa011) refers to the Klein bottle.

### Orientation by triangulation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Orientability&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Orientation by triangulation")]

Any surface has a [triangulation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangulation_(topology) "Triangulation (topology)"): a decomposition into triangles such that each edge on a triangle is glued to at most one other edge. Each triangle is oriented by choosing a direction around the perimeter of the triangle, associating a direction to each edge of the triangle. If this is done in such a way that, when glued together, neighboring edges are pointing in the opposite direction, then this determines an orientation of the surface. Such a choice is only possible if the surface is orientable, and in this case there are exactly two different orientations.

If the figure [![Image 19](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Small_pie.svg/20px-Small_pie.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Small_pie.svg) can be consistently positioned at all points of the surface without turning into its mirror image, then this will induce an orientation in the above sense on each of the triangles of the triangulation by selecting the direction of each of the triangles based on the order red-green-blue of colors of any of the figures in the interior of the triangle.

This approach generalizes to any ![Image 20: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)-manifold having a triangulation. However, some 4-manifolds do not have a triangulation, and in general for ![Image 21: {\displaystyle n>4}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3c6b13dc8b113121cdaf76a723a61aa4f8be1468) some ![Image 22: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)-manifolds have triangulations that are inequivalent.

More precisely, if ![Image 27: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2) is orientable then ![Image 28: {\displaystyle H_{1}(S)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/696e4a4afcc1ddeff9d42849833d4cf9b6bedbe1) is a [free abelian group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_abelian_group "Free abelian group"), and if not then ![Image 29: {\displaystyle H_{1}(S)=F+\mathbb {Z} /2\mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e43a41b11aa76a25c98a51ee0bb2e6f1ed97b62f) where ![Image 30: {\displaystyle F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/545fd099af8541605f7ee55f08225526be88ce57) is free abelian, and the ![Image 31: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} /2\mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1b3bb21abe942aa9c0c63bae35a0c38905e1712c) factor is generated by the middle curve in a [Möbius band](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M%C3%B6bius_band "Möbius band") embedded in ![Image 32: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2).

Orientability of manifolds
--------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Orientability&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Orientability of manifolds")]

Let _M_ be a connected topological _n_-[manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manifold_(mathematics) "Manifold (mathematics)"). There are several possible definitions of what it means for _M_ to be orientable. Some of these definitions require that _M_ has extra structure, like being differentiable. Occasionally, _n_ = 0 must be made into a special case. When more than one of these definitions applies to _M_, then _M_ is orientable under one definition if and only if it is orientable under the others.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orientability#cite_note-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orientability#cite_note-3)

### Orientability of differentiable manifolds

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Orientability&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Orientability of differentiable manifolds")]

The most intuitive definitions require that ![Image 33: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) be a differentiable manifold. This means that the transition functions in the atlas of ![Image 34: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) are ![Image 35: {\displaystyle C^{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bd24bae0d7570018e828e19851902c09c618af91)-functions. Such a function admits a [Jacobian determinant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacobian_determinant "Jacobian determinant"). When the Jacobian determinant is positive, the transition function is said to be **orientation preserving**. An **oriented atlas** on ![Image 36: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) is an atlas for which all transition functions are orientation preserving. ![Image 37: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) is **orientable** if it admits an oriented atlas. When ![Image 38: {\displaystyle n>0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/27a6a5d982d54202a14f111cb8a49210501b2c96), an **orientation** of ![Image 39: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) is a maximal oriented atlas. (When ![Image 40: {\displaystyle n=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/26819344e55f5e671c76c07c18eb4291fcec85ae), i.e. ![Image 41: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) is a point, an orientation of ![Image 42: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) is a function ![Image 43: {\displaystyle M\to \{\pm 1\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3a40ce6b989010e414478c0eda307b14009634f8).)

Orientability and orientations can also be expressed in terms of the tangent bundle. The tangent bundle is a [vector bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_bundle "Vector bundle"), so it is a [fiber bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiber_bundle "Fiber bundle") with [structure group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Structure_group "Structure group")![Image 44: {\displaystyle \operatorname {GL} (n,\mathbb {R} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/27624b52908a0452895ef7b7798a6660bf239e36). That is, the transition functions of the manifold induce transition functions on the tangent bundle which are fiberwise linear transformations. If the structure group can be reduced to the group ![Image 45: {\displaystyle \operatorname {GL} ^{+}(n,\mathbb {R} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a982982fd9d14f91384f4b072c0eb09086873a1a) of positive determinant matrices, or equivalently if there exists an atlas whose transition functions determine an orientation preserving linear transformation on each tangent space, then the manifold ![Image 46: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) is orientable. Conversely, ![Image 47: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) is orientable if and only if the structure group of the tangent bundle can be reduced in this way. Similar observations can be made for the frame bundle.

Another way to define orientations on a differentiable manifold is through [volume forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume_form "Volume form"). A volume form is a nowhere vanishing section ![Image 48: {\displaystyle \omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/48eff443f9de7a985bb94ca3bde20813ea737be8) of ![Image 49: {\displaystyle {\bigwedge }^{\!n}T^{*}M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/22f43ad269f6229029d25698ac3c77975bf3f506), the top exterior power of the cotangent bundle of ![Image 50: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd). For example, ![Image 51: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d) has a standard volume form given by ![Image 52: {\displaystyle dx^{1}\wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b19af8d61fe8d24eaac5cd66a603dd32357b2033). Given a volume form on ![Image 53: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd), the collection of all charts ![Image 54: {\displaystyle U\to \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f400295637439de6e8f3acab12c306b180aa817f) for which the standard volume form pulls back to a positive multiple of ![Image 55: {\displaystyle \omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/48eff443f9de7a985bb94ca3bde20813ea737be8) is an oriented atlas. The existence of a volume form is therefore equivalent to orientability of the manifold.

Volume forms and tangent vectors can be combined to give yet another description of orientability. If ![Image 56: {\displaystyle X_{1},\ldots ,X_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ac794f5521dcce89913085a6d566e7cdb615dbb0) is a basis of tangent vectors at a point ![Image 57: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36), then the basis is said to be **right-handed** if ![Image 58: {\displaystyle \omega (X_{1},\ldots ,X_{n})>0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0235a8c97072b307825dd531945fb9fae2c5b996). A transition function is orientation preserving if and only if it sends right-handed bases to right-handed bases. The existence of a volume form implies a reduction of the structure group of the tangent bundle or the frame bundle to ![Image 59: {\displaystyle \operatorname {GL} ^{+}(n,\mathbb {R} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a982982fd9d14f91384f4b072c0eb09086873a1a). As before, this implies the orientability of ![Image 60: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd). Conversely, if ![Image 61: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) is orientable, then local volume forms can be patched together to create a global volume form, orientability being necessary to ensure that the global form is nowhere vanishing.

### Homology and the orientability of general manifolds

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Orientability&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Homology and the orientability of general manifolds")]

At the heart of all the above definitions of orientability of a differentiable manifold is the notion of an orientation preserving transition function. This raises the question of what exactly such transition functions are preserving. They cannot be preserving an orientation of the manifold because an orientation of the manifold is an atlas, and it makes no sense to say that a transition function preserves or does not preserve an atlas of which it is a member.

This question can be resolved by defining local orientations. On a one-dimensional manifold, a local orientation around a point ![Image 62: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) corresponds to a choice of left and right near that point. On a two-dimensional manifold, it corresponds to a choice of clockwise and counter-clockwise. These two situations share the common feature that they are described in terms of top-dimensional behavior near ![Image 63: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) but not at ![Image 64: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36). For the general case, let ![Image 65: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) be a topological ![Image 66: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)-manifold. A **local orientation** of ![Image 67: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) around a point ![Image 68: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) is a choice of generator of the group

![Image 69: {\displaystyle H_{n}\left(M,M\setminus \{p\};\mathbb {Z} \right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/51f0323c2b6efa004f37f7e30d431a64fa9ec321)
To see the geometric significance of this group, choose a chart around ![Image 70: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36). In that chart there is a neighborhood of ![Image 71: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) which is an open ball ![Image 72: {\displaystyle B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47136aad860d145f75f3eed3022df827cee94d7a) around the origin ![Image 73: {\displaystyle O}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9d70e1d0d87e2ef1092ea1ffe2923d9933ff18fc). By the [excision theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Excision_theorem "Excision theorem"), ![Image 74: {\displaystyle H_{n}\left(M,M\setminus \{p\};\mathbb {Z} \right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5229176ea5030595017700b9362433cce72d2d46) is isomorphic to ![Image 75: {\displaystyle H_{n}\left(B,B\setminus \{O\};\mathbb {Z} \right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d740630c7bcfebf9979dcab6e606778ed7344e80). The ball ![Image 76: {\displaystyle B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47136aad860d145f75f3eed3022df827cee94d7a) is contractible, so its homology groups vanish except in degree zero, and the space ![Image 77: {\displaystyle B\setminus O}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/77526495ae4e4daccba710e4db32ec3934467710) is an ![Image 78: {\displaystyle (n-1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/df88c6333caaf6471cf277f24b802ff9931b133e)-sphere, so its homology groups vanish except in degrees ![Image 79: {\displaystyle n-1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fbd0b0f32b28f51962943ee9ede4fb34198a2521) and ![Image 80: {\displaystyle 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2aae8864a3c1fec9585261791a809ddec1489950). A computation with the [long exact sequence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Long_exact_sequence "Long exact sequence") in [relative homology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relative_homology "Relative homology") shows that the above homology group is isomorphic to ![Image 81: {\displaystyle H_{n-1}\left(S^{n-1};\mathbb {Z} \right)\cong \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a4a942d3e8257e5815a70dc91914f92a96c098ac). A choice of generator therefore corresponds to a decision of whether, in the given chart, a sphere around ![Image 82: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) is positive or negative. A reflection of ![Image 83: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d) through the origin acts by negation on ![Image 84: {\displaystyle H_{n-1}\left(S^{n-1};\mathbb {Z} \right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bd52d60f4519b156677e0a86bcb7d1bfdf447514), so the geometric significance of the choice of generator is that it distinguishes charts from their reflections.

On a topological manifold, a transition function is **orientation preserving** if, at each point ![Image 85: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) in its domain, it fixes the generators of ![Image 86: {\displaystyle H_{n}\left(M,M\setminus \{p\};\mathbb {Z} \right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5229176ea5030595017700b9362433cce72d2d46). From here, the relevant definitions are the same as in the differentiable case. An **oriented atlas** is one for which all transition functions are orientation preserving, ![Image 87: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) is **orientable** if it admits an oriented atlas, and when ![Image 88: {\displaystyle n>0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/27a6a5d982d54202a14f111cb8a49210501b2c96), an **orientation** of ![Image 89: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) is a maximal oriented atlas.

Intuitively, an orientation of ![Image 90: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) ought to define a unique local orientation of ![Image 91: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) at each point. This is made precise by noting that any chart in the oriented atlas around ![Image 92: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) can be used to determine a sphere around ![Image 93: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36), and this sphere determines a generator of ![Image 94: {\displaystyle H_{n}\left(M,M\setminus \{p\};\mathbb {Z} \right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5229176ea5030595017700b9362433cce72d2d46). Moreover, any other chart around ![Image 95: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) is related to the first chart by an orientation preserving transition function, and this implies that the two charts yield the same generator, whence the generator is unique.

Purely homological definitions are also possible. Assuming that ![Image 96: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) is closed and connected, ![Image 97: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) is **orientable** if and only if the ![Image 98: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)th homology group ![Image 99: {\displaystyle H_{n}(M;\mathbb {Z} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f18a062d9d47af591fc4d3cd04e72bcd262a1bcf) is isomorphic to the integers ![Image 100: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/449494a083e0a1fda2b61c62b2f09b6bee4633dc). An **orientation** of ![Image 101: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) is a choice of generator ![Image 102: {\displaystyle \alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b79333175c8b3f0840bfb4ec41b8072c83ea88d3) of this group. This generator determines an oriented atlas by fixing a generator of the infinite cyclic group ![Image 103: {\displaystyle H_{n}(M;\mathbb {Z} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f18a062d9d47af591fc4d3cd04e72bcd262a1bcf) and taking the oriented charts to be those for which ![Image 104: {\displaystyle \alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b79333175c8b3f0840bfb4ec41b8072c83ea88d3) pushes forward to the fixed generator. Conversely, an oriented atlas determines such a generator as compatible local orientations can be glued together to give a generator for the homology group ![Image 105: {\displaystyle H_{n}(M;\mathbb {Z} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f18a062d9d47af591fc4d3cd04e72bcd262a1bcf).[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orientability#cite_note-4)

### Orientation and cohomology

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Orientability&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Orientation and cohomology")]

A manifold ![Image 106: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) is orientable if and only if the first [Stiefel–Whitney class](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stiefel%E2%80%93Whitney_class "Stiefel–Whitney class")![Image 107: {\displaystyle w_{1}(M)\in H^{1}(M;\mathbb {Z} /2\mathbb {Z} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d17b70fccb3eb1b9912d1182103efafa66067bfe) vanishes. In particular, if the first cohomology group with ![Image 108: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} /2\mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1b3bb21abe942aa9c0c63bae35a0c38905e1712c) coefficients is zero, then the manifold is orientable. Moreover, if ![Image 109: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) is orientable and ![Image 110: {\displaystyle w_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2f6728d2b30f42f88b52281be5ae0584fdc9df64) vanishes, then ![Image 111: {\displaystyle H^{0}(M;\mathbb {Z} /2\mathbb {Z} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b6d51e54b48624e3c8c932288882e0eb44651566) parametrizes the choices of orientations.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orientability#cite_note-5) This characterization of orientability extends to [orientability of general vector bundles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orientation_of_a_vector_bundle "Orientation of a vector bundle") over ![Image 112: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd), not just the tangent bundle.

### The orientation double cover

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Orientability&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: The orientation double cover")]

Around each point of ![Image 113: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) there are two local orientations. Intuitively, there is a way to move from a local orientation at a point ![Image 114: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) to a local orientation at a nearby point ![Image 115: {\displaystyle p^{\prime }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7d46d13620db1b84c69bf1ff5d1290d8d5e7de6c): when the two points lie in the same coordinate chart ![Image 116: {\displaystyle U\to \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f400295637439de6e8f3acab12c306b180aa817f), that coordinate chart defines compatible local orientations at ![Image 117: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) and ![Image 118: {\displaystyle p^{\prime }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7d46d13620db1b84c69bf1ff5d1290d8d5e7de6c). The set of local orientations can therefore be given a topology, and this topology makes it into a manifold.

More precisely, let ![Image 119: {\displaystyle O}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9d70e1d0d87e2ef1092ea1ffe2923d9933ff18fc) be the set of all local orientations of ![Image 120: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd). To topologize ![Image 121: {\displaystyle O}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9d70e1d0d87e2ef1092ea1ffe2923d9933ff18fc) we will specify a subbase for its topology. Let ![Image 122: {\displaystyle U}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/458a728f53b9a0274f059cd695e067c430956025) be an open subset of ![Image 123: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) chosen such that ![Image 124: {\displaystyle H_{n}(M,M\setminus U;\mathbb {Z} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d138e5ab4dd45cf3f5e2dde3bc9274a79329deda) is isomorphic to ![Image 125: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/449494a083e0a1fda2b61c62b2f09b6bee4633dc). Assume that ![Image 126: {\displaystyle \alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b79333175c8b3f0840bfb4ec41b8072c83ea88d3) is a generator of this group. For each ![Image 127: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) in ![Image 128: {\displaystyle U}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/458a728f53b9a0274f059cd695e067c430956025), there is a pushforward function ![Image 129: {\displaystyle H_{n}(M,M\setminus U;\mathbb {Z} )\to H_{n}\left(M,M\setminus \{p\};\mathbb {Z} \right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7cd188f0b0091cd5442fc5dfa60035b9b722d12f). The codomain of this group has two generators, and ![Image 130: {\displaystyle \alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b79333175c8b3f0840bfb4ec41b8072c83ea88d3) maps to one of them. The topology on ![Image 131: {\displaystyle O}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9d70e1d0d87e2ef1092ea1ffe2923d9933ff18fc) is defined so that

![Image 132: {\displaystyle \{{\text{Image of }}\alpha {\text{ in }}H_{n}\left(M,M\setminus \{p\};\mathbb {Z} \right)\colon p\in U\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cbaf7bff30d11194f3d1072771f2d68c61110dec)
is open.

There is a canonical map ![Image 133: {\displaystyle \pi :O\to M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9406bb0a37d494f2f975da4e8960ea70dce5c0a3) that sends a local orientation at ![Image 134: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) to ![Image 135: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36). It is clear that every point of ![Image 136: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) has precisely two preimages under ![Image 137: {\displaystyle \pi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9be4ba0bb8df3af72e90a0535fabcc17431e540a). In fact, ![Image 138: {\displaystyle \pi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9be4ba0bb8df3af72e90a0535fabcc17431e540a) is even a local homeomorphism, because the preimages of the open sets ![Image 139: {\displaystyle U}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/458a728f53b9a0274f059cd695e067c430956025) mentioned above are homeomorphic to the disjoint union of two copies of ![Image 140: {\displaystyle U}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/458a728f53b9a0274f059cd695e067c430956025). If ![Image 141: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) is orientable, then ![Image 142: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) itself is one of these open sets, so ![Image 143: {\displaystyle O}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9d70e1d0d87e2ef1092ea1ffe2923d9933ff18fc) is the disjoint union of two copies of ![Image 144: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd). If ![Image 145: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) is non-orientable, however, then ![Image 146: {\displaystyle O}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9d70e1d0d87e2ef1092ea1ffe2923d9933ff18fc) is connected and orientable. The manifold ![Image 147: {\displaystyle O}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9d70e1d0d87e2ef1092ea1ffe2923d9933ff18fc) is called the **orientation double cover**.

### Manifolds with boundary

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Orientability&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Manifolds with boundary")]

If ![Image 148: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) is a manifold with boundary, then an orientation of ![Image 149: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) is defined to be an orientation of its interior. Such an orientation induces an orientation of ![Image 150: {\displaystyle \partial M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8456299e2b600f44f5aa08920be090af1b35e013). Indeed, suppose that an orientation of ![Image 151: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) is fixed. Let ![Image 152: {\displaystyle U\to \mathbb {R} _{+}^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4bf9e875e7c172db0f76320a0888040f7923ab99) be a chart at a boundary point of ![Image 153: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) which, when restricted to the interior of ![Image 154: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd), is in the chosen oriented atlas. The restriction of this chart to ![Image 155: {\displaystyle \partial M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8456299e2b600f44f5aa08920be090af1b35e013) is a chart of ![Image 156: {\displaystyle \partial M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8456299e2b600f44f5aa08920be090af1b35e013). Such charts form an oriented atlas for ![Image 157: {\displaystyle \partial M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8456299e2b600f44f5aa08920be090af1b35e013).

When ![Image 158: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) is smooth, at each point ![Image 159: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) of ![Image 160: {\displaystyle \partial M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8456299e2b600f44f5aa08920be090af1b35e013), the restriction of the tangent bundle of ![Image 161: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) to ![Image 162: {\displaystyle \partial M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8456299e2b600f44f5aa08920be090af1b35e013) is isomorphic to ![Image 163: {\displaystyle T_{p}\partial M\oplus \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7ce8c1be68b59b57d4cf5c0fae11563ee83093f2), where the factor of ![Image 164: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc) is described by the inward pointing normal vector. The orientation of ![Image 165: {\displaystyle T_{p}\partial M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ef346867f9f59fc19d37274a0c6d304f247e4a37) is defined by the condition that a basis of ![Image 166: {\displaystyle T_{p}\partial M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ef346867f9f59fc19d37274a0c6d304f247e4a37) is positively oriented if and only if it, when combined with the inward pointing normal vector, defines a positively oriented basis of ![Image 167: {\displaystyle T_{p}M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/71b20e0304a65ead2cafb33412a383b34fe527ca).

Orientable double cover
-----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Orientability&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Orientable double cover")]

Animation of the orientable double cover of the [Möbius strip](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M%C3%B6bius_strip "Möbius strip").

A closely related notion uses the idea of [covering space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covering_space "Covering space"). For a connected manifold ![Image 168: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) take ![Image 169: {\displaystyle M^{*}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b6d760e72a9f5f578f8ba166127f0713d56dc589), the set of pairs ![Image 170: {\displaystyle (x,o)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/48364008d671fe14d50a23cac2c182cb282d4439) where ![Image 171: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) is a point of ![Image 172: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) and ![Image 173: {\displaystyle o}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0c1031f61947aa3d1cf3a70ec3e4904df2c3675d) is an orientation at ![Image 174: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4); here we assume ![Image 175: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) is either smooth so we can choose an orientation on the tangent space at a point or we use [singular homology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Singular_homology "Singular homology") to define orientation. Then for every open, oriented subset of ![Image 176: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) we consider the corresponding set of pairs and define that to be an open set of ![Image 177: {\displaystyle M^{*}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b6d760e72a9f5f578f8ba166127f0713d56dc589). This gives ![Image 178: {\displaystyle M^{*}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b6d760e72a9f5f578f8ba166127f0713d56dc589) a topology and the projection sending ![Image 179: {\displaystyle (x,o)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/48364008d671fe14d50a23cac2c182cb282d4439) to ![Image 180: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) is then a 2-to-1 covering map. This covering space is called the **orientable double cover**, as it is orientable. ![Image 181: {\displaystyle M^{*}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b6d760e72a9f5f578f8ba166127f0713d56dc589) is connected if and only if ![Image 182: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) is not orientable.

Another way to construct this cover is to divide the loops based at a basepoint into either orientation-preserving or orientation-reversing loops. The orientation preserving loops generate a subgroup of the fundamental group which is either the whole group or of [index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Index_of_a_subgroup "Index of a subgroup") two. In the latter case (which means there is an orientation-reversing path), the subgroup corresponds to a connected double covering; this cover is orientable by construction. In the former case, one can simply take two copies of ![Image 183: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd), each of which corresponds to a different orientation.

Orientation of vector bundles
-----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Orientability&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Orientation of vector bundles")]

A real [vector bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_bundle "Vector bundle"), which _a priori_ has a [![Image 184: {\displaystyle \operatorname {GL} (n)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7033760b4d9a933782f9809f79b5c17e952453f2)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GL(n) "GL(n)")[structure group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Structure_group "Structure group"), is called _orientable_ when the [structure group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Structure_group "Structure group") may be [reduced](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reduction_of_the_structure_group "Reduction of the structure group") to ![Image 185: {\displaystyle \operatorname {GL} ^{+}(n)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d72962173c88613f4068a4936d1540102d6fcd53), the group of [matrices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_(mathematics) "Matrix (mathematics)") with positive [determinant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Determinant "Determinant"). For the [tangent bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_bundle "Tangent bundle"), this reduction is always possible if the underlying base manifold is orientable and in fact this provides a convenient way to define the orientability of a [smooth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smooth_function "Smooth function") real [manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manifold "Manifold"): a smooth manifold is defined to be orientable if its [tangent bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_bundle "Tangent bundle") is orientable (as a vector bundle). Note that as a manifold in its own right, the tangent bundle is _always_ orientable, even over nonorientable manifolds.

### Lorentzian geometry

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Orientability&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Lorentzian geometry")]

In [Lorentzian geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentzian_geometry "Lorentzian geometry"), there are two kinds of orientability: [space orientability](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Space_orientability&action=edit&redlink=1 "Space orientability (page does not exist)") and [time orientability](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Time_orientability&action=edit&redlink=1 "Time orientability (page does not exist)"). These play a role in the [causal structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Causal_structure "Causal structure") of spacetime.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orientability#cite_note-6) In the context of [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity"), a [spacetime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spacetime "Spacetime") manifold is space orientable if, whenever two right-handed observers head off in rocket ships starting at the same spacetime point, and then meet again at another point, they remain right-handed with respect to one another. If a spacetime is time-orientable then the two observers will always agree on the direction of time at both points of their meeting. In fact, a spacetime is time-orientable if and only if any two observers can agree which of the two meetings preceded the other.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orientability#cite_note-7)

Formally, the [pseudo-orthogonal group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudo-orthogonal_group "Pseudo-orthogonal group")![Image 186: {\displaystyle \operatorname {O} (p,q)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1384853ed7c40b56e3c73020a38952b88e94d011) has a pair of [characters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Character_theory "Character theory"): the space orientation character ![Image 187: {\displaystyle \sigma _{+}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/05c6199f312fda9064ce3a84290fe253e2e46282) and the time orientation character ![Image 188: {\displaystyle \sigma _{-}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8b73c2ff6ddb417e0f33c8c91f4608f7a936a211),

![Image 189: {\displaystyle \sigma _{\pm }:\operatorname {O} (p,q)\to \{-1,+1\}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0b609df21d7cad8cba2681a6181f0065854c9ba1)
Their product ![Image 190: {\displaystyle \sigma =\sigma _{+}\sigma _{-}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ddad2b3373b97150e9ffb0f5242a820275261c84) is the determinant, which gives the orientation character. A space-orientation of a pseudo-Riemannian manifold is identified with a [section](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Section_(fiber_bundle) "Section (fiber bundle)") of the [associated bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Associated_bundle "Associated bundle")

![Image 191: {\displaystyle \operatorname {O} (M)\times _{\sigma _{+}}\{-1,+1\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3d0424bfea9efbe53d81d6228d42aedf4899537b)
where ![Image 192: {\displaystyle \operatorname {O} (M)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6dd4824165ac1eed6d235056c3856b3b9a6d2286) is the bundle of pseudo-orthogonal frames. Similarly, a time orientation is a section of the associated bundle
