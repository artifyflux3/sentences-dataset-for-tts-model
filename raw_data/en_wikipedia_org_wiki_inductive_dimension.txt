From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

In the mathematical field of [topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology "Topology"), the **inductive dimension** of a [topological space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_space "Topological space")_X_ is either of two values, the **small inductive dimension** ind(_X_) or the **large inductive dimension** Ind(_X_). These are based on the observation that, in _n_-dimensional [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space")_R_ _n_, (_n_−1)-dimensional [spheres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere "Sphere") (that is, the [boundaries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boundary_(topology) "Boundary (topology)") of _n_-dimensional balls) have dimension _n_−1. Therefore it should be possible to define the dimension of a space [inductively](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_induction "Mathematical induction") in terms of the dimensions of the boundaries of suitable [open sets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_set "Open set").

The small and large inductive dimensions are two of the three most usual ways of capturing the notion of "dimension" for a topological space, in a way that depends only on the topology (and not, say, on the properties of a [metric space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space "Metric space")). The other is the [Lebesgue covering dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebesgue_covering_dimension "Lebesgue covering dimension"). The term "topological dimension" is ordinarily understood to refer to the Lebesgue covering dimension. For "sufficiently nice" spaces, the three measures of dimension are equal.

We want the dimension of a point to be 0, and a point has empty boundary, so we start with

![Image 1: {\displaystyle \operatorname {ind} (\varnothing )=\operatorname {Ind} (\varnothing )=-1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/53d8b88549d1edc3c0ec6881e9a3831326584d4e)
Then inductively, ind(_X_) is the smallest _n_ such that, for every _![Image 2: {\displaystyle x\in X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest\_v1/media/math/render/svg/3e580967f68f36743e894aa7944f032dda6ea01d)_ and every open set _U_ containing _x_, there is an open set _V_ containing _x_, such that the [closure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closure_(topology) "Closure (topology)") of _V_ is a [subset](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subset "Subset") of _U_, and the boundary of _V_ has small inductive dimension less than or equal to _n_−1. (If _X_ is a Euclidean _n_-dimensional space, _V_ can be chosen to be an _n_-dimensional ball centered at _x_.)

For the large inductive dimension, we restrict the choice of _V_ still further; Ind(_X_) is the smallest _n_ such that, for every [closed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_set "Closed set") subset _F_ of every open subset _U_ of _X_, there is an open _V_ in between (that is, _F_ is a subset of _V_ and the closure of _V_ is a subset of _U_), such that the boundary of _V_ has large inductive dimension less than or equal to _n_−1.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inductive_dimension#cite_note-1)

Relationship between dimensions
-------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Inductive_dimension&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Relationship between dimensions")]

Let ![Image 3: {\displaystyle \dim }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/66115c83c4bb19068adb45849f0f596647c18f2e) be the Lebesgue covering dimension. For any [topological space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_space "Topological space")_X_, we have

![Image 4: {\displaystyle \dim X=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f791015b08019c26cd66f6a9d28e447637cb038c) if and only if ![Image 5: {\displaystyle \operatorname {Ind} X=0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/de8f4b04ef87f93f6b145c16a6e7e875c3f1c569)
**Urysohn's theorem** states that when _X_ is a [normal space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_space "Normal space") with a [countable base](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second-countable_space "Second-countable space"), then

![Image 6: {\displaystyle \dim X=\operatorname {Ind} X=\operatorname {ind} X.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4bfbb2c90cae957c6acf40c8ad50e8ac34898d42)
Such spaces are exactly the [separable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separable_space "Separable space") and [metrizable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metrizable "Metrizable")_X_ (see [Urysohn's metrization theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urysohn%27s_metrization_theorem "Urysohn's metrization theorem")).

The **Nöbeling–Pontryagin theorem** then states that such spaces with finite dimension are characterised up to homeomorphism as the subspaces of the [Euclidean spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space"), with their usual topology. The **Menger–Nöbeling theorem** (1932) states that if ![Image 7: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) is compact metric separable and of dimension ![Image 8: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b), then it embeds as a subspace of Euclidean space of dimension ![Image 9: {\displaystyle 2n+1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8ca410f731fe4c7c444330343afb1d1850eadaea). ([Georg Nöbeling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georg_N%C3%B6beling "Georg Nöbeling") was a student of [Karl Menger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karl_Menger "Karl Menger"). He introduced **Nöbeling space**, the subspace of ![Image 10: {\displaystyle \mathbf {R} ^{2n+1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3b5e8ef45a42d0f2453469e3ef7f87148fa14bda) consisting of points with at least ![Image 11: {\displaystyle n+1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2a135e65a42f2d73cccbfc4569523996ca0036f1) co-ordinates being [irrational numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irrational_number "Irrational number"), which has universal properties for embedding spaces of dimension ![Image 12: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b).)

Assuming only _X_ metrizable we have ([Miroslav Katětov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Miroslav_Kat%C4%9Btov "Miroslav Katětov"))

ind _X_ ≤ Ind _X_ = dim _X_;
or assuming _X_[compact](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compact_space "Compact space") and [Hausdorff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hausdorff_space "Hausdorff space") ([P. S. Aleksandrov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/P._S._Aleksandrov "P. S. Aleksandrov"))

dim _X_ ≤ ind _X_ ≤ Ind _X_.
Either inequality here may be strict; an example of Vladimir V. Filippov shows that the two inductive dimensions may differ.

A separable metric space _X_ satisfies the inequality ![Image 13: {\displaystyle \operatorname {Ind} X\leq n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a65603c166c4af7e71a7ef70162c48391a9d5a3a) if and only if for every closed sub-space ![Image 14: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3) of the space ![Image 15: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) and each continuous mapping ![Image 16: {\displaystyle f:A\to S^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8394ee6815e09a6c5d3458f26c26156df1d2f02e) there exists a continuous extension ![Image 17: {\displaystyle {\bar {f}}:X\to S^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3b06ca97b5fda02318e7302e1e1043d5fc7d12ab).
