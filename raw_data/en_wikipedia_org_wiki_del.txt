**Del**, or **nabla**, is an [operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operator_(mathematics) "Operator (mathematics)") used in mathematics (particularly in [vector calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_calculus "Vector calculus")) as a [vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_(geometry) "Vector (geometry)")[differential operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_operator "Differential operator"), usually represented by **∇** (the _[nabla symbol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nabla\_symbol "Nabla symbol")_). When applied to a [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)") defined on a [one-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension_(mathematics) "Dimension (mathematics)") domain, it denotes the standard [derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative "Derivative") of the function as defined in [calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus "Calculus"). When applied to a _field_ (a function defined on a multi-dimensional domain), it may denote any one of three operations depending on the way it is applied: the [gradient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gradient "Gradient") or (locally) steepest slope of a [scalar field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_field "Scalar field") (or sometimes of a [vector field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_field "Vector field"), as in the [Navier–Stokes equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Navier%E2%80%93Stokes_equations#Interpretation_as_v%C2%B7(%E2%88%87v) "Navier–Stokes equations")); the [divergence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divergence "Divergence") of a vector field; or the [curl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curl_(mathematics) "Curl (mathematics)") (rotation) of a vector field.

Del is a very convenient [mathematical notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_notation "Mathematical notation") for those three operations (gradient, divergence, and curl) that makes many equations easier to write and remember. The del symbol (or nabla) can be [formally](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formal_calculation "Formal calculation") defined as a vector operator whose components are the corresponding [partial derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_derivative "Partial derivative") operators. As a vector operator, it can act on scalar and vector fields in three different ways, giving rise to three different differential operations: first, it can act on scalar fields by a formal scalar multiplication—to give a vector field called the gradient; second, it can act on vector fields by a formal [dot product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dot_product "Dot product")—to give a scalar field called the divergence; and lastly, it can act on vector fields by a formal [cross product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product "Cross product")—to give a vector field called the curl. These formal products do not necessarily [commute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_operation "Commutative operation") with other operators or products. These three uses are summarized as:

![Image 10: {\displaystyle \nabla =\sum _{i=1}^{n}\mathbf {e} _{i}{\partial  \over \partial x_{i}}=\left({\partial  \over \partial x_{1}},\ldots ,{\partial  \over \partial x_{n}}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3cbe31df1ef0f70f083d4137a993bf3f960a2ad3)

In [three-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional "Three-dimensional") Cartesian coordinate system ![Image 11: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5) with coordinates ![Image 12: {\displaystyle (x,y,z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/22a8c93372e8f8b6e24d523bd5545aed3430baf4) and standard basis or unit vectors of axes ![Image 13: {\displaystyle \{\mathbf {e} _{x},\mathbf {e} _{y},\mathbf {e} _{z}\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/84589fe1107dc80af0233db9847cf54e28e7c3bf), del is written as:

![Image 14: {\displaystyle \nabla =\mathbf {e} _{x}{\partial  \over \partial x}+\mathbf {e} _{y}{\partial  \over \partial y}+\mathbf {e} _{z}{\partial  \over \partial z}=\left({\partial  \over \partial x},{\partial  \over \partial y},{\partial  \over \partial z}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f76d82c11031984f27bec14e8f32c6e70943afc2)
As a vector operator, del naturally acts on scalar fields via [scalar multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_multiplication "Scalar multiplication"), and naturally acts on vector fields via dot products and cross products.

and

Del can also be expressed in other coordinate systems, see for example [del in cylindrical and spherical coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Del_in_cylindrical_and_spherical_coordinates "Del in cylindrical and spherical coordinates").

Del is used as a shorthand form to simplify many long mathematical expressions. It is most commonly used to simplify expressions for the [gradient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gradient "Gradient"), [divergence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divergence "Divergence"), [curl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curl_(mathematics) "Curl (mathematics)"), [directional derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Directional_derivative "Directional derivative"), and [Laplacian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laplacian "Laplacian").

The vector derivative of a [scalar field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_field "Scalar field")![Image 28: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) is called the [gradient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gradient "Gradient"), and it can be represented as:

![Image 29: {\displaystyle \operatorname {grad} f={\partial f \over \partial x}{\hat {\mathbf {x} }}+{\partial f \over \partial y}{\hat {\mathbf {y} }}+{\partial f \over \partial z}{\hat {\mathbf {z} }}=\nabla f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a06554fe16b9ac73b42d64542888e5be4ed28a15)
It always points in the [direction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direction_(geometry) "Direction (geometry)") of greatest increase of ![Image 30: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61), and it has a [magnitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnitude_(mathematics) "Magnitude (mathematics)") equal to the maximum rate of increase at the point—just like a standard derivative. In particular, if a hill is defined as a [height function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Height_function "Height function") over a plane ![Image 31: {\displaystyle h(x,y)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7235c6799c7d4112231c9941bd428fe6a4111fe4), the gradient at a given location will be a vector in the xy-plane (visualizable as an arrow on a map) pointing along the steepest direction. The magnitude of the gradient is the value of this steepest slope.

In particular, this notation is powerful because the gradient [product rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Product_rule "Product rule") looks very similar to the 1d-derivative case:

![Image 32: {\displaystyle \nabla (fg)=f\nabla g+g\nabla f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7d27f6e61a59a516822d5fab9c8adbf3b4fe2612)
However, the rules for [dot products](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dot_product "Dot product") do not turn out to be simple, as illustrated by:

![Image 33: {\displaystyle \nabla (\mathbf {u} \cdot \mathbf {v} )=(\mathbf {u} \cdot \nabla )\mathbf {v} +(\mathbf {v} \cdot \nabla )\mathbf {u} +\mathbf {u} \times (\nabla \times \mathbf {v} )+\mathbf {v} \times (\nabla \times \mathbf {u} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f7eebaef43ae4007156eb7bc6ab979b29f698840)
The [divergence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divergence "Divergence") of a [vector field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_field "Vector field")![Image 34: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} (x,y,z)=v_{x}{\hat {\mathbf {x} }}+v_{y}{\hat {\mathbf {y} }}+v_{z}{\hat {\mathbf {z} }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/10b4e06cd52dcd7bfc0c41a823495529c055c220) is a [scalar field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_field "Scalar field") that can be represented as:

![Image 35: {\displaystyle \operatorname {div} \mathbf {v} ={\partial v_{x} \over \partial x}+{\partial v_{y} \over \partial y}+{\partial v_{z} \over \partial z}=\nabla \cdot \mathbf {v} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eefd05ad90ca9c5663be1f53b0e4a7804598c485)
The divergence is roughly a measure of a vector field's increase in the direction it points; but more accurately, it is a measure of that field's tendency to converge toward or diverge from a point.

The power of the del notation is shown by the following product rule:

![Image 36: {\displaystyle \nabla \cdot (f\mathbf {v} )=(\nabla f)\cdot \mathbf {v} +f(\nabla \cdot \mathbf {v} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fcf11ad2dd2a7fb966d6cb88a0be6094bc0452a3)
The formula for the [vector product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_product "Vector product") is slightly less intuitive, because this product is not commutative:

![Image 37: {\displaystyle \nabla \cdot (\mathbf {u} \times \mathbf {v} )=(\nabla \times \mathbf {u} )\cdot \mathbf {v} -\mathbf {u} \cdot (\nabla \times \mathbf {v} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4e16989946642f58f29d7dba97f3948ee0ac6a79)
The [curl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curl_(mathematics) "Curl (mathematics)") of a vector field ![Image 38: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} (x,y,z)=v_{x}{\hat {\mathbf {x} }}+v_{y}{\hat {\mathbf {y} }}+v_{z}{\hat {\mathbf {z} }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/10b4e06cd52dcd7bfc0c41a823495529c055c220) is a [vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_field "Vector field") function that can be represented as:

![Image 39: {\displaystyle \operatorname {curl} \mathbf {v} =\left({\partial v_{z} \over \partial y}-{\partial v_{y} \over \partial z}\right){\hat {\mathbf {x} }}+\left({\partial v_{x} \over \partial z}-{\partial v_{z} \over \partial x}\right){\hat {\mathbf {y} }}+\left({\partial v_{y} \over \partial x}-{\partial v_{x} \over \partial y}\right){\hat {\mathbf {z} }}=\nabla \times \mathbf {v} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/059cf5650923c41ba44fccd43d5030d76cc38efe)
The curl at a point is proportional to the on-axis torque that a tiny pinwheel would be subjected to if it were centered at that point.

The vector product operation can be visualized as a pseudo-[determinant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Determinant "Determinant"):

![Image 40: {\displaystyle \nabla \times \mathbf {v} =\left|{\begin{matrix}{\hat {\mathbf {x} }}&{\hat {\mathbf {y} }}&{\hat {\mathbf {z} }}\\[2pt]{\frac {\partial }{\partial x}}&{\frac {\partial }{\partial y}}&{\frac {\partial }{\partial z}}\\[2pt]v_{x}&v_{y}&v_{z}\end{matrix}}\right|}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2c22add6c547b447848574525afdfe35578f1f62)
Again the power of the notation is shown by the product rule:

![Image 41: {\displaystyle \nabla \times (f\mathbf {v} )=(\nabla f)\times \mathbf {v} +f(\nabla \times \mathbf {v} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a00d526a83adcd232599b48a3f4b484f54ccab18)
The rule for the vector product does not turn out to be simple:

![Image 42: {\displaystyle \nabla \times (\mathbf {u} \times \mathbf {v} )=\mathbf {u} \,(\nabla \cdot \mathbf {v} )-\mathbf {v} \,(\nabla \cdot \mathbf {u} )+(\mathbf {v} \cdot \nabla )\,\mathbf {u} -(\mathbf {u} \cdot \nabla )\,\mathbf {v} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c89a8cf9a1de32fd5d9f464d53054c51d4d88b77)

### Directional derivative

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Del&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Directional derivative")]

The [directional derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Directional_derivative "Directional derivative") of a scalar field ![Image 43: {\displaystyle f(x,y,z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5d48dce2c4341575269f1709237a2e18923237a) in the direction ![Image 44: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} (x,y,z)=a_{x}{\hat {\mathbf {x} }}+a_{y}{\hat {\mathbf {y} }}+a_{z}{\hat {\mathbf {z} }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/062fcc09f1c251589149a25909e1fa249587320e) is defined as:

![Image 45: {\displaystyle (\mathbf {a} \cdot \nabla )f=\lim _{h\to 0}{\frac {f(x+a_{x}h,y+a_{y}h,z+a_{z}h)-f(x,y,z)}{h}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5a28c4ebfd48336df992c1cc223a91d420bea838)
Which is equal to the following when the gradient exists

![Image 46: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} \cdot \operatorname {grad} f=a_{x}{\partial f \over \partial x}+a_{y}{\partial f \over \partial y}+a_{z}{\partial f \over \partial z}=\mathbf {a} \cdot (\nabla f)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b76b1bcec0bb257c2f0b2d4119e48cb217c78e3)
This gives the rate of change of a field ![Image 47: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) in the direction of ![Image 48: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1a957216653a9ee0d0133dcefd13fb75e36b8b9d), scaled by the magnitude of ![Image 49: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1a957216653a9ee0d0133dcefd13fb75e36b8b9d). In operator notation, the element in parentheses can be considered a single coherent unit; [fluid dynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluid_dynamics "Fluid dynamics") uses this convention extensively, terming it the [convective derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convective_derivative "Convective derivative")—the "moving" derivative of the fluid.

Note that ![Image 50: {\displaystyle (\mathbf {a} \cdot \nabla )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f359b483581016360ef91d2b1b1e8a193e10baac) is an operator that maps scalars to scalars. It can be extended to act on a vector field by applying the operator component-wise to each component of the vector.

The [Laplace operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laplace_operator "Laplace operator") is a scalar operator that can be applied to either vector or scalar fields; for cartesian coordinate systems it is defined as:

![Image 51: {\displaystyle \Delta ={\partial ^{2} \over \partial x^{2}}+{\partial ^{2} \over \partial y^{2}}+{\partial ^{2} \over \partial z^{2}}=\nabla \cdot \nabla =\nabla ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f07578e67b23dba5c9583d405fd975e85348b696)
and the definition for more general coordinate systems is given in [vector Laplacian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_Laplacian "Vector Laplacian").

The Laplacian is ubiquitous throughout modern [mathematical physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_physics "Mathematical physics"), appearing for example in [Laplace's equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laplace%27s_equation "Laplace's equation"), [Poisson's equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poisson%27s_equation "Poisson's equation"), the [heat equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat_equation "Heat equation"), the [wave equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wave_equation "Wave equation"), and the [Schrödinger equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schr%C3%B6dinger_equation "Schrödinger equation").

While ![Image 52: {\displaystyle \nabla ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f4be87ad083e5ead48d92b0c82f2d4e719cb34a6) usually represents the [Laplacian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laplacian "Laplacian"), sometimes ![Image 53: {\displaystyle \nabla ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f4be87ad083e5ead48d92b0c82f2d4e719cb34a6) also represents the [Hessian matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hessian_matrix "Hessian matrix"). The former refers to the inner product of ![Image 54: {\displaystyle \nabla }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a3d0e93b78c50237f9ea83d027e4ebbdaef354b2), while the latter refers to the [dyadic product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dyadic_product "Dyadic product") of ![Image 55: {\displaystyle \nabla }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a3d0e93b78c50237f9ea83d027e4ebbdaef354b2):

![Image 56: {\displaystyle \nabla ^{2}=\nabla \cdot \nabla ^{T}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/66bb7115488b7c9662e1df1ade5dc9165f50fa71).
So whether ![Image 57: {\displaystyle \nabla ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f4be87ad083e5ead48d92b0c82f2d4e719cb34a6) refers to a Laplacian or a Hessian matrix depends on the context.

Del can also be applied to a vector field with the result being a [tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor "Tensor"). The [tensor derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_derivative "Tensor derivative") of a vector field ![Image 58: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35c1866e359fbfd2e0f606c725ba5cc37a5195d6) (in three dimensions) is a 9-term second-rank tensor – that is, a 3×3 matrix – but can be denoted simply as ![Image 59: {\displaystyle \nabla \otimes \mathbf {v} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f19c83421802bfd7c50332c34ff4bee23dbdf217), where ![Image 60: {\displaystyle \otimes }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/de29098f5a34ee296a505681a0d5e875070f2aea) represents the [dyadic product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dyadic_product "Dyadic product"). This quantity is equivalent to the transpose of the [Jacobian matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacobian_matrix "Jacobian matrix") of the vector field with respect to space. The divergence of the vector field can then be expressed as the [trace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trace_(linear_algebra) "Trace (linear algebra)") of this matrix.

For a small displacement ![Image 61: {\displaystyle \delta \mathbf {r} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/316af5a88d44e3de8269a3bfc89367414edeb9a3), the change in the vector field is given by:

![Image 62: {\displaystyle \delta \mathbf {v} =(\nabla \otimes \mathbf {v} )^{T}\cdot \delta \mathbf {r} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f2fa911ddd72e96e203e4840d1ecd894e67d6f7a)
For [vector calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_calculus "Vector calculus"):

![Image 63: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\nabla (fg)&=f\nabla g+g\nabla f\\\nabla (\mathbf {u} \cdot \mathbf {v} )&=\mathbf {u} \times (\nabla \times \mathbf {v} )+\mathbf {v} \times (\nabla \times \mathbf {u} )+(\mathbf {u} \cdot \nabla )\mathbf {v} +(\mathbf {v} \cdot \nabla )\mathbf {u} \\\nabla \cdot (f\mathbf {v} )&=f(\nabla \cdot \mathbf {v} )+\mathbf {v} \cdot (\nabla f)\\\nabla \cdot (\mathbf {u} \times \mathbf {v} )&=\mathbf {v} \cdot (\nabla \times \mathbf {u} )-\mathbf {u} \cdot (\nabla \times \mathbf {v} )\\\nabla \times (f\mathbf {v} )&=(\nabla f)\times \mathbf {v} +f(\nabla \times \mathbf {v} )\\\nabla \times (\mathbf {u} \times \mathbf {v} )&=\mathbf {u} \,(\nabla \cdot \mathbf {v} )-\mathbf {v} \,(\nabla \cdot \mathbf {u} )+(\mathbf {v} \cdot \nabla )\,\mathbf {u} -(\mathbf {u} \cdot \nabla )\,\mathbf {v} \end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a6cc7515bdc1acdc88b00be5906ae2a6ab7f143d)
For [matrix calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_calculus "Matrix calculus") (for which ![Image 64: {\displaystyle \mathbf {u} \cdot \mathbf {v} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b3161cc5faca46f8bd209de2beb24159a50bc66e) can be written ![Image 65: {\displaystyle \mathbf {u} ^{\text{T}}\mathbf {v} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5a7fe855c58f7ac464e607d7369f971019f80c8f)):

![Image 66: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\left(\mathbf {A} \nabla \right)^{\text{T}}\mathbf {u} &=\nabla ^{\text{T}}\left(\mathbf {A} ^{\text{T}}\mathbf {u} \right)-\left(\nabla ^{\text{T}}\mathbf {A} ^{\text{T}}\right)\mathbf {u} \end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/45a43f3cdda201d3a119e98e6253f063d517f6e3)
Another relation of interest (see e.g. _[Euler equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler\_equations "Euler equations")_) is the following, where ![Image 67: {\displaystyle \mathbf {u} \otimes \mathbf {v} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5d579ce37084592ce1baaaddbd0f50592934783) is the [outer product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_product "Outer product") tensor:

![Image 68: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\nabla \cdot (\mathbf {u} \otimes \mathbf {v} )=(\nabla \cdot \mathbf {u} )\mathbf {v} +(\mathbf {u} \cdot \nabla )\mathbf {v} \end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/399413288555112b0775b3c30c708897db6839fd)[![Image 69](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/DCG_chart.svg/250px-DCG_chart.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:DCG_chart.svg)

DCG chart: A simple chart depicting all rules pertaining to second derivatives. D, C, G, L and CC stand for divergence, curl, gradient, Laplacian and curl of curl, respectively. Arrows indicate existence of second derivatives. Blue circle in the middle represents curl of curl, whereas the other two red circles (dashed) mean that DD and GG do not exist. 

When del operates on a scalar or vector, either a scalar or vector is returned. Because of the diversity of vector products (scalar, dot, cross) one application of del already gives rise to three major derivatives: the gradient (scalar product), divergence (dot product), and curl (cross product). Applying these three sorts of derivatives again to each other gives five possible second derivatives, for a scalar field _f_ or a vector field _**v**_; the use of the scalar [Laplacian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laplacian "Laplacian") and [vector Laplacian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_Laplacian "Vector Laplacian") gives two more:

![Image 70: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\operatorname {div} (\operatorname {grad} f)&=\nabla \cdot (\nabla f)=\nabla ^{2}f\\\operatorname {curl} (\operatorname {grad} f)&=\nabla \times (\nabla f)\\\operatorname {grad} (\operatorname {div} \mathbf {v} )&=\nabla (\nabla \cdot \mathbf {v} )\\\operatorname {div} (\operatorname {curl} \mathbf {v} )&=\nabla \cdot (\nabla \times \mathbf {v} )\\\operatorname {curl} (\operatorname {curl} \mathbf {v} )&=\nabla \times (\nabla \times \mathbf {v} )\\\Delta f&=\nabla ^{2}f\\\Delta \mathbf {v} &=\nabla ^{2}\mathbf {v} \end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/94af07d422714768a170cdc8c379c959ad6c07a1)
These are of interest principally because they are not always unique or independent of each other. As long as the functions are well-behaved (![Image 71: {\displaystyle C^{\infty }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/971ed05871d69309df32efdfd2020128c9cf69d8) in most cases), two of them are always zero:

![Image 72: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\operatorname {curl} (\operatorname {grad} f)&=\nabla \times (\nabla f)=0\\\operatorname {div} (\operatorname {curl} \mathbf {v} )&=\nabla \cdot (\nabla \times \mathbf {v} )=0\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bdcdec8546fe66be7f6247ff963ce6d7996be6a5)
Two of them are always equal:

![Image 73: {\displaystyle \operatorname {div} (\operatorname {grad} f)=\nabla \cdot (\nabla f)=\nabla ^{2}f=\Delta f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8f3a762325091088033a0380cd92adb712ec6cb0)
The 3 remaining vector derivatives are related by the equation:

![Image 74: {\displaystyle \nabla \times \left(\nabla \times \mathbf {v} \right)=\nabla (\nabla \cdot \mathbf {v} )-\nabla ^{2}\mathbf {v} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c4f949de15a0864a5765bfec3362d9b4dd00a072)
And one of them can even be expressed with the tensor product, if the functions are well-behaved:

![Image 75: {\displaystyle \nabla (\nabla \cdot \mathbf {v} )=\nabla \cdot (\mathbf {v} \otimes \nabla )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ebf5e8a47989462bb99bfbf8afea47f8d433a078)
Most of the above vector properties (except for those that rely explicitly on del's differential properties—for example, the product rule) rely only on symbol rearrangement, and must necessarily hold if the del symbol is replaced by any other vector. This is part of the value to be gained in notationally representing this operator as a vector.

Though one can often replace del with a vector and obtain a vector identity, making those identities mnemonic, the reverse is _not_ necessarily reliable, because del does not commute in general.

A counterexample that demonstrates the divergence (![Image 76: {\displaystyle \nabla \cdot \mathbf {v} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5b4fee0cfc620e59850feaf72a21ea92720ff6ac)) and the [advection operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Advection#Mathematics_of_advection "Advection") (![Image 77: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} \cdot \nabla }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/184c16d2563ad2c4f39da769912dd267b1ec5874)) are not commutative:

![Image 78: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}(\mathbf {u} \cdot \mathbf {v} )f&\equiv (\mathbf {v} \cdot \mathbf {u} )f\\(\nabla \cdot \mathbf {v} )f&=\left({\frac {\partial v_{x}}{\partial x}}+{\frac {\partial v_{y}}{\partial y}}+{\frac {\partial v_{z}}{\partial z}}\right)f={\frac {\partial v_{x}}{\partial x}}f+{\frac {\partial v_{y}}{\partial y}}f+{\frac {\partial v_{z}}{\partial z}}f\\(\mathbf {v} \cdot \nabla )f&=\left(v_{x}{\frac {\partial }{\partial x}}+v_{y}{\frac {\partial }{\partial y}}+v_{z}{\frac {\partial }{\partial z}}\right)f=v_{x}{\frac {\partial f}{\partial x}}+v_{y}{\frac {\partial f}{\partial y}}+v_{z}{\frac {\partial f}{\partial z}}\\\Rightarrow (\nabla \cdot \mathbf {v} )f&\neq (\mathbf {v} \cdot \nabla )f\\\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/75513abf8ce9fbf6a803ab3106ec423b7def562c)
A counterexample that relies on del's differential properties:

![Image 79: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}(\nabla x)\times (\nabla y)&=\left(\mathbf {e} _{x}{\frac {\partial x}{\partial x}}+\mathbf {e} _{y}{\frac {\partial x}{\partial y}}+\mathbf {e} _{z}{\frac {\partial x}{\partial z}}\right)\times \left(\mathbf {e} _{x}{\frac {\partial y}{\partial x}}+\mathbf {e} _{y}{\frac {\partial y}{\partial y}}+\mathbf {e} _{z}{\frac {\partial y}{\partial z}}\right)\\&=(\mathbf {e} _{x}\cdot 1+\mathbf {e} _{y}\cdot 0+\mathbf {e} _{z}\cdot 0)\times (\mathbf {e} _{x}\cdot 0+\mathbf {e} _{y}\cdot 1+\mathbf {e} _{z}\cdot 0)\\&=\mathbf {e} _{x}\times \mathbf {e} _{y}\\&=\mathbf {e} _{z}\\(\mathbf {u} x)\times (\mathbf {u} y)&=xy(\mathbf {u} \times \mathbf {u} )\\&=xy\mathbf {0} \\&=\mathbf {0} \end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/53468dc139c3fdd4a23c4d6ab35d001d8b60263a)
Central to these distinctions is the fact that del is not simply a vector; it is a [vector operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_operator "Vector operator"). Whereas a vector is an object with both a magnitude and direction, del has neither a magnitude nor a direction until it operates on a function.

For that reason, identities involving del must be derived with care, using both vector identities and _differentiation_ identities such as the product rule.
