**Geography** (from [Ancient Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek "Ancient Greek")γεωγραφία _geōgraphía_; combining _gê_ 'Earth' and _gráphō_ 'write', literally 'Earth writing') is the study of the lands, features, inhabitants, and phenomena of [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth "Earth").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-intro1-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Springer2017-2) Geography is an all-encompassing discipline that seeks an understanding of Earth and [its human and natural complexities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World "World")—not merely where objects are, but also how they have changed and come to be. While geography is specific to Earth, many [concepts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concept "Concept") can be applied more broadly to other [celestial bodies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_object "Astronomical object") in the field of [planetary science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_science "Planetary science").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Burt-3) Geography has been called "a bridge between [natural science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_science "Natural science") and [social science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_science "Social science") disciplines."[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Sala1-4)

Origins of many of the concepts in geography can be traced to Greek [Eratosthenes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eratosthenes "Eratosthenes") of Cyrene, who may have coined the term "geographia" (c. 276 BC– c. 195/194 BC).[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Roller-5) The first recorded use of the word [γεωγραφία](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography_(Ptolemy) "Geography (Ptolemy)") was as the title of a book by Greek scholar [Claudius Ptolemy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Claudius_Ptolemy "Claudius Ptolemy") (100 – 170 AD).[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-intro1-1) This work created the so-called "Ptolemaic tradition" of geography, which included "Ptolemaic cartographic theory."[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Brentjes2009-6) However, the concepts of geography (such as [cartography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartography "Cartography")) date back to the earliest attempts to understand the world spatially, with the earliest example of an attempted world map dating to the 9th century BCE in ancient [Babylon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Babylon "Babylon").[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Kurt-2009-7) The [history of geography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_geography "History of geography") as a discipline spans cultures and millennia, being independently developed by multiple groups, and cross-pollinated by trade between these groups. The core concepts of geography consistent between all approaches are a focus on space, place, time, and scale.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Thrift1-8)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Kent1-9)[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Tuan1-10)[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Tuan2-11)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Castree1-12)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Gregory1-13) Today, geography is an extremely broad discipline with multiple approaches and modalities. There have been multiple attempts to organize the discipline, including the four traditions of geography, and into branches.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Traditions1-14)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Sala1-4)[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Tambassi-15) Techniques employed can generally be broken down into [quantitative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantitative_geography "Quantitative geography")[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Fotheringham1-16) and [qualitative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qualitative_geography "Qualitative geography")[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-RyanBurns1-17) approaches, with many studies taking [mixed-methods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mixed-methods_research "Mixed-methods research") approaches.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Diriw%C3%A4chter1-18) Common techniques include [cartography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartography "Cartography"), [remote sensing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Remote_sensing "Remote sensing"), [interviews](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interview "Interview"), and [surveying](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surveying "Surveying").

Fundamentals
------------

[![Image 1](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/17th_Century_map_of_Pembrokeshire.jpg/250px-17th_Century_map_of_Pembrokeshire.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:17th_Century_map_of_Pembrokeshire.jpg)

Maps, like this 17th Century depiction of [Pembrokeshire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pembrokeshire "Pembrokeshire"), are a central element in the study of geography.

Geography is a systematic study of the Earth (other celestial bodies are specified, such as "geography of Mars", or given another name, such as [areography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography_of_Mars "Geography of Mars") in the case of Mars, or [selenography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Selenography "Selenography") in the case of the Moon, or [planetography](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Planetography&action=edit&redlink=1 "Planetography (page does not exist)") for the general case), its features, and phenomena that take place on it.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Areography1-19)[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Areoography2-20)[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-WilliamSheeham2014-21) For something to fall into the domain of geography, it generally needs some sort of spatial component that can be placed on a [map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Map "Map"), such as [coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geographic_coordinate_system "Geographic coordinate system"), place names, or [addresses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Address "Address"). This has led to geography being associated with [cartography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartography "Cartography") and place names. Although many geographers are trained in [toponymy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toponymy "Toponymy") and cartology, this is not their main preoccupation. [Geographers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geographers "Geographers") study the Earth's spatial and temporal distribution of phenomena, processes, and features as well as the interaction of humans and their [environment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_environment "Natural environment").[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Hayes1-22) Because space and place affect a variety of topics, such as economics, health, [climate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate "Climate"), plants, and animals, geography is highly interdisciplinary. The interdisciplinary nature of the geographical approach depends on an attentiveness to the relationship between physical and human phenomena and their spatial patterns.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Baker-23)[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Hornby11-24)

While narrowing down geography to a few key concepts is extremely challenging, and subject to tremendous debate within the discipline, several sources have approached the topic.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-KeyConcepts2014-25) The 1st edition of the book "Key Concepts in Geography" broke down this into chapters focusing on "Space," "Place," "Time," "Scale," and "Landscape."[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-KeyConcepts2003-26) The 2nd edition of the book expanded on these key concepts by adding "Environmental systems," "Social Systems," "Nature," "[Globalization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Globalization "Globalization")," "Development," and "Risk," demonstrating how challenging narrowing the field can be.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-KeyConcepts2014-25) Another approach used extensively in teaching geography are the [Five themes of geography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Five_themes_of_geography "Five themes of geography") established by "Guidelines for Geographic Education: Elementary and Secondary Schools," published jointly by the [National Council for Geographic Education](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Council_for_Geographic_Education "National Council for Geographic Education") and the [Association of American Geographers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Association_of_American_Geographers "Association of American Geographers") in 1984.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Guidelines1984-27)[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Natoli1994-28) These themes are Location, place, relationships within places (often summarized as Human-Environment Interaction), movement, and regions.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Natoli1994-28)[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Brown2015-29) The five themes of geography have shaped how American education approaches the topic in the years since.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Natoli1994-28)[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Brown2015-29)

### Space

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/3D_coordinate_system.svg/250px-3D_coordinate_system.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:3D_coordinate_system.svg)

A right-handed three-dimensional [Cartesian coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system "Cartesian coordinate system") used to indicate positions in space

> Just as all phenomena exist in time and thus have a history, they also exist in space and have a geography.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-GeographyPerspective1-30)

For something to exist in the realm of geography, it must be able to be described spatially.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-GeographyPerspective1-30)[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Matthews1-31) Thus, space is the most fundamental concept at the foundation of geography.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Thrift1-8)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Kent1-9) The concept is so basic, that geographers often have difficulty defining exactly what it is. [Absolute space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_space_and_time "Absolute space and time") is the exact site, or spatial coordinates, of objects, persons, places, or phenomena under investigation.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Thrift1-8) We exist in space.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Tuan1-10) Absolute space leads to the view of the world as a photograph, with everything frozen in place when the coordinates were recorded. Today, geographers are trained to recognize the world as a dynamic space where all processes interact and take place, rather than a static image on a map.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Thrift1-8)[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Chen1-32)

### Place

[![Image 3](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Yi-Fu_Tuan-Festival_international_de_g%C3%A9ographie_2012_%281%29.jpg/250px-Yi-Fu_Tuan-Festival_international_de_g%C3%A9ographie_2012_%281%29.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Yi-Fu_Tuan-Festival_international_de_g%C3%A9ographie_2012_(1).jpg)

Yi-Fu Tuan, geographer who foregrounded the importance of language in the making of place.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-33)

Place is one of the most complex and important terms in geography.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Tuan1-10)[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Tuan2-11)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Castree1-12)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Gregory1-13) In human geography, place is the synthesis of the coordinates on the Earth's surface, the activity and use that occurs, has occurred, and will occur at the coordinates, and the meaning ascribed to the space by human individuals and groups.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Matthews1-31)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Castree1-12) This can be extraordinarily complex, as different spaces may have different uses at different times and mean different things to different people. In physical geography, a place includes all of the physical phenomena that occur in space, including the [lithosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lithosphere "Lithosphere"), [atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere "Atmosphere"), [hydrosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrosphere "Hydrosphere"), and [biosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere "Biosphere").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Gregory1-13) Places do not exist in a vacuum and instead have complex spatial relationships with each other, and place is concerned how a location is situated in relation to all other locations.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Tobler1-34)[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Tobler2-35) As a discipline then, the term place in geography includes all spatial phenomena occurring at a location, the diverse uses and meanings humans ascribe to that location, and how that location impacts and is impacted by all other locations on Earth.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Castree1-12)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Gregory1-13) In one of [Yi-Fu Tuan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yi-Fu_Tuan "Yi-Fu Tuan")'s papers, he explains that in his view, geography is the study of Earth as a home for humanity, and thus place and the complex meaning behind the term is central to the discipline of geography.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Tuan2-11)

### Time

[![Image 4: A space-time cube is a three-axis graph where one axis represents the time dimension and the other axes represent two spatial dimensions](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Sample_of_time_geographical_description.png/340px-Sample_of_time_geographical_description.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Sample_of_time_geographical_description.png)

Examples of the [visual language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visual_language "Visual language") of time geography: space-time cube, path, prism, bundle, and other concepts.

Time is usually thought to be within the domain of [history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History "History"), however, it is of significant concern in the discipline of geography.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Thrift2-36)[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Thornes1-37)[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Taylor2-38) In physics, space and time are not separated, and are combined into the concept of [spacetime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spacetime "Spacetime").[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Galison-39) Geography is subject to the laws of physics, and in studying things that occur in space, time must be considered. Time in geography is more than just the historical record of events that occurred at various discrete coordinates; but also includes modeling the dynamic movement of people, organisms, and things through space.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Tuan1-10) Time facilitates movement through space, ultimately allowing things to flow through a system.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Thrift2-36) The amount of time an individual, or group of people, spends in a place will often shape their attachment and perspective to that place.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Tuan1-10) Time constrains the possible paths that can be taken through space, given a starting point, possible routes, and rate of travel.[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Miller1-40) Visualizing time over space is challenging in terms of cartography, and includes Space-Prism, advanced 3D geovisualizations, and [animated maps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Animated_mapping "Animated mapping").[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Tobler1-34)[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Miller1-40)[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-41)[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Chen1-32)

### Scale

[![Image 5](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Map_scale_-_8km%2C_5mi.png/250px-Map_scale_-_8km%2C_5mi.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Map_scale_-_8km,_5mi.png)

A graphical or bar scale. A map would also usually give its scale numerically ("1:50,000", for instance, means that one cm on the map represents 50,000 cm of real space, which is 500 meters).

Scale in the context of a map is the ratio between a distance measured on the map and the corresponding distance as measured on the ground.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Burt-3)[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Herod-42) This concept is fundamental to the discipline of geography, not just cartography, in that phenomena being investigated appear different depending on the scale used.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Arbia1-43)[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-SmithLaws-44) Scale is the frame that geographers use to measure space, and ultimately to understand a place.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Herod-42)

### Laws of geography

During the quantitative revolution, geography shifted to an empirical law-making ([nomothetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nomothetic "Nomothetic")) approach.[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-DeLyser1-45)[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Yano1-46) Several **laws of geography** have been proposed since then, most notably by [Waldo Tobler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Waldo_Tobler "Waldo Tobler") and can be viewed as a product of the quantitative revolution.[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Walker1-47) In general, some dispute the entire concept of laws in geography and the social sciences.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Tobler1-34)[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Tobler3-48)[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Goodchild1-49) These criticisms have been addressed by Tobler and others, such as [Michael Frank Goodchild](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michael_Frank_Goodchild "Michael Frank Goodchild").[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Tobler3-48)[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Goodchild1-49) However, this is an ongoing source of debate in geography and is unlikely to be resolved anytime soon. Several laws have been proposed, and Tobler's first law of geography is the most generally accepted in geography. Some have argued that geographic laws do not need to be numbered. The existence of a first invites a second, and many have proposed themselves as that. It has also been proposed that Tobler's first law of geography should be moved to the second and replaced with another.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Goodchild1-49) A few of the proposed laws of geography are below:

*   [Tobler's first law of geography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tobler%27s_first_law_of_geography "Tobler's first law of geography"): "Everything is related to everything else, but near things are more related than distant."[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Tobler1-34)[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Tobler3-48)[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Goodchild1-49)
*   [Tobler's second law of geography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tobler%27s_second_law_of_geography "Tobler's second law of geography"): "The phenomenon external to a geographic area of interest affects what goes on inside."[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Tobler3-48)[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Tobler4-50)
*   [Arbia's law of geography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arbia%27s_law_of_geography "Arbia's law of geography"): "Everything is related to everything else, but things observed at a coarse spatial resolution are more related than things observed at a finer resolution."[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Arbia1-43)[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Tobler3-48)[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-SmithLaws-44)[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Hecht1-51)[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Otto1-52)
*   [Spatial heterogeneity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spatial_heterogeneity#Law_of_geography "Spatial heterogeneity"): Geographic variables exhibit uncontrolled variance.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Goodchild1-49)[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Zou2022-53)[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Zhang2023-54)
*   The uncertainty principle: "That the geographic world is infinitely complex and that any representation must therefore contain elements of uncertainty, that many definitions used in acquiring geographic data contain elements of vagueness, and that it is impossible to measure location on the Earth's surface exactly."[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Goodchild1-49)

Additionally, several variations or amendments to these laws exist within the literature, although not as well supported. For example, one paper proposed an amended version of Tobler's first law of geography, referred to in the text as the _Tobler–von Thünen law_,[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Walker1-47) which states: "Everything is related to everything else, but near things are more related than distant things, as a consequence of accessibility."[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Walker1-47)

Sub-disciplines
---------------

Geography is a branch of inquiry that focuses on spatial information on Earth. It is an extremely broad topic and can be broken down multiple ways.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Tambassi-15) There have been several approaches to doing this spanning at least several centuries, including "four traditions of geography" and into distinct branches.[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-cave1-55)[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Traditions1-14) The Four traditions of geography are often used to divide the different historical approach theories geographers have taken to the discipline.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Traditions1-14) In contrast, geography's branches describe contemporary applied geographical approaches.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Sala1-4)

### Four traditions

Geography is an extremely broad field. Because of this, many view the various definitions of geography proposed over the decades as inadequate. To address this, [William D. Pattison](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=William_D._Pattison&action=edit&redlink=1 "William D. Pattison (page does not exist)") proposed the concept of the "Four traditions of Geography" in 1964.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Traditions1-14)[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Traditions2-56)[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Traditions3-57) These traditions are the [Spatial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spatial_analysis "Spatial analysis") or Locational Tradition, the Man-Land or Human-Environment Interaction Tradition (sometimes referred to as [Integrated geography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integrated_geography "Integrated geography")), the [Area Studies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area_Studies "Area Studies") or [Regional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regional_geography "Regional geography") Tradition, and the [Earth Science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth_Science "Earth Science") Tradition.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Traditions1-14)[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Traditions2-56)[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Traditions3-57) These concepts are broad sets of geography philosophies bound together within the discipline. They are one of many ways geographers organize the major sets of thoughts and philosophies within the discipline.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Traditions1-14)[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Traditions2-56)[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Traditions3-57)

### Branches

[![Image 6](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/CIA_World_Physical_Map_%282023%29.pdf/page1-440px-CIA_World_Physical_Map_%282023%29.pdf.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:CIA_World_Physical_Map_(2023).pdf)

Map of Earth showing both physical features and [political boundaries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Border#Classification "Border") from the 2023 CIA World Factbook

In another approach to the abovementioned four traditions, geography is organized into applied branches.[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Sala2-58)[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Dada1-59) The UNESCO [Encyclopedia of Life Support Systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Encyclopedia_of_Life_Support_Systems "Encyclopedia of Life Support Systems") organizes geography into the three categories of [human geography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_geography "Human geography"), [physical geography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_geography "Physical geography"), and [technical geography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Technical_geography "Technical geography").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Sala1-4)[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Ormeling1-60)[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Sala2-58)[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Tambassi-15) Some publications limit the number of branches to physical and human, describing them as the principal branches.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Matthews1-31) Human geography largely focuses on the [built environment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Built_environment "Built environment") and how humans create, view, manage, and influence space.[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Hough1-61) Human geographers study people and their communities, cultures, economies, and environmental interactions by studying their relations with and across space and place.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Matthews1-31) Physical geography examines the natural environment and how [organisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism "Organism"), climate, [soil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soil "Soil"), water, and [landforms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Landform "Landform") produce and interact, studying spatial patterns in the natural environment, [atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere "Atmosphere"), [hydrosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrosphere "Hydrosphere"), [biosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere "Biosphere"), and [geosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosphere "Geosphere").[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Matthews1-31)[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Cotterill-62) The difference between these approaches led to the development of [integrated geography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integrated_geography "Integrated geography"), which combines physical and human geography and concerns the interactions between the environment and humans.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Hayes1-22) Technical geography involves studying and developing the tools and techniques used by geographers, such as remote sensing, cartography, and geographic information system.[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Haidu1-63)[Technical geography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Technical_geography "Technical geography") is interested in studying and applying techniques and methods to store, process, analyze, visualize, and use spatial data.[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Dada1-59) It is the newest of the branches, the most controversial, and often other terms are used in the literature to describe the emerging category. These branches use similar geographic philosophies, concepts, and tools and often overlap significantly. Geographers rarely focus on just one of these topics, often using one as their primary focus and then incorporating data and methods from the other branches. Often, geographers are asked to describe what they do by individuals outside the discipline[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Tuan2-11) and are likely to identify closely with a specific branch, or sub-branch when describing themselves to lay people.

#### Physical

Physical geography (or physiography) focuses on geography as an [Earth science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth_science "Earth science").[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-64)[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-65)[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-66) It aims to understand the physical problems and the issues of [lithosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lithosphere "Lithosphere"), [hydrosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrosphere "Hydrosphere"), [atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere "Earth's atmosphere"), [pedosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pedosphere "Pedosphere"), and global [flora](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flora "Flora") and [fauna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fauna "Fauna") patterns ([biosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere "Biosphere")). Physical geography is the study of earth's seasons, [climate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate "Climate"), [atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere "Atmosphere"), [soil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soil "Soil"), streams, landforms, and oceans.[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Lockyer1-67) Physical geographers will often work in identifying and monitoring the use of natural resources.

#### Technical

Technical geography concerns studying and developing tools, techniques, and statistical methods employed to collect, analyze, use, and understand spatial data.[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Haidu1-63)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Sala1-4)[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Sala2-58)[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Ormeling1-60) Technical geography is the most recently recognized, and controversial, of the branches. Its use dates back to 1749, when a book published by [Edward Cave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edward_Cave "Edward Cave") organized the discipline into a section containing content such as cartographic techniques and globes.[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-cave1-55) There are several other terms, often used interchangeably with technical geography to subdivide the discipline, including "techniques of geographic analysis,"[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-69) "Geographic Information Technology,"[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-intro1-1) "Geography method's and techniques,"[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-70) "[Geographic Information Science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geographic_Information_Science "Geographic Information Science"),"[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Ron1-71) "[geoinformatics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geoinformatics "Geoinformatics")," "[geomatics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geomatics "Geomatics")," and "information geography". There are subtle differences to each concept and term; however, technical geography is one of the broadest, is consistent with the naming convention of the other two branches, has been in use since the 1700s, and has been used by the [UNESCO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UNESCO "UNESCO")[Encyclopedia of Life Support Systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Encyclopedia_of_Life_Support_Systems "Encyclopedia of Life Support Systems") to divide geography into themes.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Sala1-4)[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Sala2-58)[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-cave1-55) As academic fields increasingly specialize in their nature, technical geography has emerged as a branch of geography specializing in geographic methods and thought.[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Haidu1-63) The emergence of technical geography has brought new relevance to the broad discipline of geography by serving as a set of unique methods for managing the interdisciplinary nature of the phenomena under investigation. While human and physical geographers use the techniques employed by technical geographers, technical geography is more concerned with the fundamental spatial concepts and technologies than the nature of the data.[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Haidu1-63)[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Dada1-59) It is therefore closely associated with the spatial tradition of geography while being applied to the other two major branches. A technical geographer might work as a GIS analyst, a GIS developer working to make new software tools, or create general reference maps incorporating human and natural features.[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Kretzschmar1-72)

Methods
-------

[![Image 42](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Cook_chart_of_New_Zealand.jpg/250px-Cook_chart_of_New_Zealand.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Cook_chart_of_New_Zealand.jpg)

[James Cook](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Cook "James Cook")'s 1770 chart of New Zealand

All geographic research and analysis start with asking the question "where," followed by "why there." Geographers start with the fundamental assumption set forth in [Tobler's first law of geography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tobler%27s_first_law_of_geography "Tobler's first law of geography"), that "everything is related to everything else, but near things are more related than distant things."[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Tobler1-34)[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Tobler2-35) As spatial interrelationships are key to this synoptic science, maps are a key tool. Classical [cartography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartography "Cartography") has been joined by a more modern approach to geographical analysis, computer-based [geographic information systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geographic_information_system "Geographic information system") (GIS).

In their study, geographers use four interrelated approaches:

*   Analytical– Asks _why_ we find features and populations in a specific geographic area.
*   Descriptive– Simply specifies the locations of features and populations.
*   Regional– Examines systematic relationships between categories for a specific region or location on the planet.
*   Systematic– Groups geographical knowledge into categories that can be explored globally.

### Quantitative methods

Quantitative methods in geography became particularly influential in the discipline during the quantitative revolution of the 1950s and 60s.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Fotheringham1-16) These methods revitalized the discipline in many ways, allowing scientific testing of hypotheses and proposing scientific geographic theories and laws.[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-73) The quantitative revolution heavily influenced and revitalized technical geography, and lead to the development of the subfield of quantitative geography.[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Haidu1-63)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Fotheringham1-16)

#### Quantitative cartography

Cartography is the art, science, and technology of making maps.[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Kainz1-74) Cartographers study the Earth's surface representation with abstract symbols (map making). Although other subdisciplines of geography rely on maps for presenting their analyses, the actual making of maps is abstract enough to be regarded separately.[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Jenks1-75) Cartography has grown from a collection of drafting techniques into an actual science.

Cartographers must learn [cognitive psychology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cognitive_psychology "Cognitive psychology") and [ergonomics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ergonomics "Ergonomics") to understand which symbols convey information about the Earth most effectively and [behavioural psychology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Behaviorism "Behaviorism") to induce the readers of their maps to act on the information. They must learn [geodesy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesy "Geodesy") and fairly advanced mathematics to understand how the [shape of the Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Figure_of_the_Earth "Figure of the Earth") affects the distortion of map symbols projected onto a flat surface for viewing. It can be said, without much controversy, that cartography is the seed from which the larger field of geography grew.

#### Geographic information systems

Geographic information systems (GIS) deal with storing information about the Earth for automatic retrieval by a computer in an accurate manner appropriate to the information's purpose.[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Demers1-76) In addition to all of the other subdisciplines of geography, GIS specialists must understand [computer science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_science "Computer science") and [database](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Database "Database") systems. GIS has revolutionized the field of cartography: nearly all mapmaking is now done with the assistance of some form of [GIS software](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_GIS_software "List of GIS software"). The science of using GIS software and GIS techniques to represent, analyse, and predict the spatial relationships is called _[geographic information science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geographic\_information\_science "Geographic information science")_ (GISc).[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Monmonier1-77)

#### Remote sensing

[![Image 43](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Death-valley-sar.jpg/250px-Death-valley-sar.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Death-valley-sar.jpg)

[Synthetic aperture radar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synthetic_aperture_radar "Synthetic aperture radar") image of [Death Valley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Death_Valley "Death Valley") colored using [polarimetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polarimetry "Polarimetry")

Remote sensing is the art, science, and technology of obtaining information about Earth's features from measurements made at a distance.[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Jensen1-78) Remotely sensed data can be either passive, such as traditional [photography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photography "Photography"), or active, such as [LiDAR](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LiDAR "LiDAR").[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Jensen1-78) A variety of platforms can be used for remote sensing, including [satellite imagery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satellite_imagery "Satellite imagery"), [aerial photography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aerial_photography "Aerial photography") (including consumer drones), and data obtained from hand-held sensors.[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Jensen1-78) Products from remote sensing include [Digital elevation model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digital_elevation_model "Digital elevation model") and cartographic base maps. Geographers increasingly use remotely sensed data to obtain information about the Earth's [land surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Land_surface "Land surface"), ocean, and atmosphere, because it: (a) supplies objective information at a variety of spatial scales (local to global), (b) provides a synoptic view of the area of interest, (c) allows access to distant and inaccessible sites, (d) provides spectral information outside the visible portion of the [electromagnetic spectrum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum "Electromagnetic spectrum"), and (e) facilitates studies of how features/areas change over time. Remotely sensed data may be analyzed independently or in conjunction with other digital data layers (e.g., in a geographic information system). Remote sensing aids in land use, land cover (LULC) mapping, by helping to determine both what is naturally occurring on a piece of land and what human activities are taking place on it.[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-79)

#### Geostatistics

[Geostatistics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geostatistics "Geostatistics") deal with [quantitative data](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Numerical_data "Numerical data") analysis, specifically the application of a statistical methodology to the exploration of geographic phenomena.[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-80) Geostatistics is used extensively in a variety of fields, including [hydrology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrology "Hydrology"), geology, petroleum exploration, weather analysis, [urban planning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urban_planning "Urban planning"), logistics, and [epidemiology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epidemiology "Epidemiology"). The mathematical basis for geostatistics derives from [cluster analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cluster_analysis "Cluster analysis"), [linear discriminant analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_discriminant_analysis "Linear discriminant analysis") and [non-parametric statistical tests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-parametric_statistics "Non-parametric statistics"), and a variety of other subjects. Applications of geostatistics rely heavily on [geographic information systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geographic_information_system "Geographic information system"), particularly for the [interpolation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interpolation "Interpolation") (estimate) of unmeasured points. Geographers are making notable contributions to the method of quantitative techniques.

### Qualitative methods

Qualitative methods in geography are descriptive rather than numerical or statistical in nature.[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-81)[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-RyanBurns1-17)[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-DeLyser1-45) They add context to concepts, and explore human concepts like beliefs and perspective that are difficult or impossible to quantify.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-RyanBurns1-17) Human geography is much more likely to employ qualitative methods than physical geography. Increasingly, technical geographers are attempting to employ GIS methods to qualitative datasets.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-RyanBurns1-17)[[82]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Suchan1-82)

#### Qualitative cartography

[![Image 44](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Indo-Aryan_language_map.svg/330px-Indo-Aryan_language_map.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Indo-Aryan_language_map.svg)

A compound chorochromatic map of [Indo-Aryan (Indic) languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indo-Aryan_languages "Indo-Aryan languages")

Qualitative cartography employs many of the same software and techniques as quantitative cartography.[[82]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Suchan1-82) It may be employed to inform on map practices, or to visualize perspectives and ideas that are not strictly quantitative in nature.[[82]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Suchan1-82)[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-RyanBurns1-17) An example of a form of qualitative cartography is a [Chorochromatic map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chorochromatic_map "Chorochromatic map") of nominal data, such as land cover or dominant language group in an area.[[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Brewer1994-83) Another example is a [deep map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deep_map "Deep map"), or maps that combine geography and storytelling to produce a product with greater information than a two-dimensional image of places, names, and topography.[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Bodenhamer1-84)[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Butts1-85) This approach offers more inclusive strategies than more traditional cartographic approaches for connecting the complex layers that makeup places.[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Butts1-85)

#### Ethnography

Ethnographical research techniques are used by human geographers.[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-86) In [cultural geography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cultural_geography "Cultural geography"), there is a tradition of employing [qualitative research](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qualitative_research "Qualitative research") techniques, also used in [anthropology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anthropology "Anthropology") and sociology. [Participant observation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Participant_observation "Participant observation") and in-depth interviews provide human geographers with qualitative data.

#### Geopoetics

Geopoetics is an interdisciplinary approach that combines geography and [poetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poetry "Poetry") to explore the interconnectedness between humans, space, place, and the environment.[[87]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Magrane1-87)[[88]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Russo1-88) Geopoetics is employed as a [mixed methods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mixed_methods "Mixed methods") tool to explain the implications of geographic research.[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Magrane2-89) It is often employed to address and communicate the implications of complex topics, such as the [anthropocene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anthropocene "Anthropocene").[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Dialogs1-90)[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Dialogs2-91)[[92]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Dialogs3-92)[[93]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Dialogs4-93)[[94]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Dialogs5-94)

#### Interviews

Geographers employ interviews to gather data and acquire valuable understandings from individuals or groups regarding their encounters, outlooks, and opinions concerning spatial phenomena.[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Dixon1-95)[[96]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Dixon2-96) Interviews can be carried out through various mediums, including face-to-face interactions, phone conversations, online platforms, or written exchanges.[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-DeLyser1-45) Geographers typically adopt a structured or semi-structured approach during interviews involving specific questions or discussion points when utilized for research purposes.[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Dixon1-95) These questions are designed to extract focused information about the research topic while being flexible enough to allow participants to express their experiences and viewpoints, such as through open-ended questions.[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Dixon1-95)

Origin and history
------------------

The concept of geography is present in all cultures, and therefore the history of the discipline is a series of competing narratives, with concepts emerging at various points across space and time.[[97]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-97) The oldest known [world maps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_map "World map") date back to [ancient Babylon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Babylon "Ancient Babylon") from the 9th century BC.[[98]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Raaflaub1-98) The best known [Babylonian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Babylonia "Babylonia") world map, however, is the _[Imago Mundi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Babylonian\_Map\_of\_the\_World "Babylonian Map of the World")_ of 600 BC.[[99]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Siebold1-99) The map as reconstructed by [Eckhard Unger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eckhard_Unger "Eckhard Unger") shows [Babylon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Babylon "Babylon") on the [Euphrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euphrates "Euphrates"), surrounded by a circular landmass showing [Assyria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Assyria "Assyria"), [Urartu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urartu "Urartu"), and several cities, in turn surrounded by a "bitter river" ([Oceanus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oceanus "Oceanus")), with seven islands arranged around it so as to form a seven-pointed star.[[100]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Delano1-100) The accompanying text mentions seven outer regions beyond the encircling ocean. The descriptions of five of them have survived.[[101]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Finkel1-101) In contrast to the _Imago Mundi_, an earlier Babylonian [world map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_map "World map") dating back to the 9th century BC depicted Babylon as being further north from the center of the world, though it is not certain what that center was supposed to represent.[[98]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Raaflaub1-98)

[![Image 45](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Eratosthenes_profile.png/250px-Eratosthenes_profile.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Eratosthenes_profile.png)

Etching of an ancient seal identified as Eratosthenes. [Philipp Daniel Lippert](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Philipp_Daniel_Lippert&action=edit&redlink=1 "Philipp Daniel Lippert (page does not exist)"), _Dactyliothec_, 1767.

The ideas of [Anaximander](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anaximander "Anaximander") (c. 610–545 BC): considered by later Greek writers to be the true founder of geography, come to us through fragments quoted by his successors.[[102]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Kish1-102) Anaximander is credited with the invention of the [gnomon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gnomon "Gnomon"), the simple, yet efficient Greek instrument that allowed the early measurement of [latitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latitude "Latitude").[[102]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Kish1-102)[Thales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thales_of_Miletus "Thales of Miletus") is also credited with the prediction of eclipses. The foundations of geography can be traced to ancient cultures, such as the ancient, medieval, and early modern [Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_China "History of China"). The [Greeks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greece "Ancient Greece"), who were the first to explore geography as both art and science, achieved this through [Cartography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_cartography "History of cartography"), [Philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_philosophy "Greek philosophy"), and [Literature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek_literature "Ancient Greek literature"), or through [Mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_mathematics "History of mathematics"). There is some debate about who was the first person to assert that the [Earth is spherical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_Earth "Spherical Earth") in shape, with the credit going either to [Parmenides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parmenides "Parmenides") or [Pythagoras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras "Pythagoras"). [Anaxagoras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anaxagoras "Anaxagoras") was able to demonstrate that the profile of the Earth was circular by explaining [eclipses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eclipse "Eclipse"). However, he still believed that the Earth was a flat disk, as did many of his contemporaries. One of the first estimates of the radius of the Earth was made by [Eratosthenes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eratosthenes "Eratosthenes").[[103]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Tassoul1-103)

The first rigorous system of [latitude and longitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geographic_coordinate_system "Geographic coordinate system") lines is credited to [Hipparchus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hipparchus "Hipparchus"). He employed a [sexagesimal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sexagesimal "Sexagesimal") system that was derived from [Babylonian mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Babylonian_mathematics "Babylonian mathematics"). The meridians were subdivided into 360°, with each degree further subdivided into 60 ([minutes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minute_of_arc "Minute of arc")). To measure the longitude at different locations on Earth, he suggested using eclipses to determine the relative difference in time.[[104]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Smith1-104) The extensive mapping by the [Romans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Empire "Roman Empire") as they explored new lands would later provide a high level of information for [Ptolemy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ptolemy "Ptolemy") to construct detailed [atlases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atlas "Atlas"). He extended the work of [Hipparchus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hipparchus "Hipparchus"), using a grid system on his maps and adopting a length of 56.5 miles for a degree.[[105]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Sullivan1-105)

From the 3rd century onwards, [Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_China "History of China") methods of geographical study and writing of geographical literature became much more comprehensive than what was found in Europe at the time (until the 13th century).[[106]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Needham1-106) Chinese geographers such as [Liu An](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liu_An "Liu An"), [Pei Xiu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pei_Xiu "Pei Xiu"), [Jia Dan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jia_Dan "Jia Dan"), [Shen Kuo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shen_Kuo "Shen Kuo"), [Fan Chengda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fan_Chengda "Fan Chengda"), [Zhou Daguan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zhou_Daguan "Zhou Daguan"), and [Xu Xiake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xu_Xiake "Xu Xiake") wrote important treatises, yet by the 17th century advanced ideas and methods of Western-style geography were adopted in China.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

[![Image 46](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/PtolemyWorldMap.jpg/250px-PtolemyWorldMap.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:PtolemyWorldMap.jpg)

The [Ptolemy world map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ptolemy_world_map "Ptolemy world map"), reconstituted from [Ptolemy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ptolemy "Ptolemy")'s _Geographia_, written c. 150

During the [Middle Ages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_Ages "Middle Ages"), the [fall of the Roman empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decline_of_the_Roman_Empire "Decline of the Roman Empire") led to a shift in the evolution of geography from Europe to the [Islamic world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muslim_world "Muslim world").[[106]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Needham1-106)[Muslim geographers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography_in_medieval_Islam "Geography in medieval Islam") such as [Muhammad al-Idrisi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muhammad_al-Idrisi "Muhammad al-Idrisi") produced detailed world maps (such as [Tabula Rogeriana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tabula_Rogeriana "Tabula Rogeriana")), while other geographers such as [Yaqut al-Hamawi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yaqut_al-Hamawi "Yaqut al-Hamawi"), [Abu Rayhan Biruni](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abu_Rayhan_Biruni "Abu Rayhan Biruni"), [Ibn Battuta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ibn_Battuta "Ibn Battuta"), and [Ibn Khaldun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ibn_Khaldun "Ibn Khaldun") provided detailed accounts of their journeys and the geography of the regions they visited. Turkish geographer [Mahmud al-Kashgari](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mahmud_al-Kashgari "Mahmud al-Kashgari") drew a world map on a linguistic basis, and later so did [Piri Reis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Piri_Reis "Piri Reis") ([Piri Reis map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Piri_Reis_map "Piri Reis map")). Further, Islamic scholars translated and [interpreted](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interpreting "Interpreting") the earlier works of the [Romans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Rome "Ancient Rome") and the [Greeks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greece "Ancient Greece") and established the [House of Wisdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/House_of_Wisdom "House of Wisdom") in [Baghdad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baghdad "Baghdad") for this purpose.[[107]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Nawwab1-107)[Abū Zayd al-Balkhī](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ahmed_ibn_Sahl_al-Balkhi "Ahmed ibn Sahl al-Balkhi"), originally from [Balkh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Balkh "Balkh"), founded the "Balkhī school" of terrestrial mapping in [Baghdad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baghdad "Baghdad").[[108]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Edson1-108) Suhrāb, a late tenth century Muslim geographer accompanied a book of geographical coordinates, with instructions for making a rectangular world map with [equirectangular projection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equirectangular_projection "Equirectangular projection") or cylindrical equidistant projection.[[109]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Tibbetts1-109)

[Abu Rayhan Biruni](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abu_Rayhan_Biruni "Abu Rayhan Biruni") (976–1048) first described a polar equi-[azimuthal equidistant projection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Azimuthal_equidistant_projection "Azimuthal equidistant projection") of the [celestial sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celestial_sphere "Celestial sphere").[[110]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-King1-110) He was regarded as the most skilled when it came to mapping cities and measuring the distances between them, which he did for many cities in the Middle East and the [Indian subcontinent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_subcontinent "Indian subcontinent"). He often combined astronomical readings and mathematical equations to develop methods of pin-pointing locations by recording degrees of [latitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latitude "Latitude") and [longitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Longitude "Longitude"). He also developed similar techniques when it came to measuring the heights of mountains, depths of the [valleys](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Valley "Valley"), and expanse of the [horizon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horizon "Horizon"). He also discussed [human geography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_geography "Human geography") and the [planetary habitability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_habitability "Planetary habitability") of the Earth. He also calculated the [latitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latitude "Latitude") of Kath, [Khwarezm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khwarezm "Khwarezm"), using the maximum altitude of the Sun, and solved a complex [geodesic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesy "Geodesy") equation to accurately compute the [Earth's circumference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_circumference "Earth's circumference"), which was close to modern values of the Earth's circumference.[[111]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Aber1-111) His estimate of 6,339.9 km for the [Earth radius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth_radius "Earth radius") was only 16.8 km less than the modern value of 6,356.7 km. In contrast to his predecessors, who measured the Earth's circumference by sighting the Sun simultaneously from two different locations, [al-Biruni](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Biruni "Al-Biruni") developed a new method of using [trigonometric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trigonometry "Trigonometry") calculations based on the angle between a [plain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plain "Plain") and mountain top, which yielded more accurate measurements of the Earth's circumference, and made it possible for it to be measured by a single person from a single location.[[112]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Goodman1-112)

[![Image 47](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Geographia_Generalis_1733_Figure_30.jpg/250px-Geographia_Generalis_1733_Figure_30.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Geographia_Generalis_1733_Figure_30.jpg)

 Map of southern Atlantic Ocean from 1733 edition of the _[Geographia Generalis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geographia\_Generalis "Geographia Generalis")_

The European [Age of Discovery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Age_of_Discovery "Age of Discovery") during the 16th and the 17th centuries, where many new lands were discovered and accounts by European explorers such as [Christopher Columbus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christopher_Columbus "Christopher Columbus"), [Marco Polo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marco_Polo "Marco Polo"), and [James Cook](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Cook "James Cook") revived a desire for both accurate geographic detail and more solid theoretical foundations in Europe. In 1650, the first edition of the _[Geographia Generalis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geographia\_Generalis "Geographia Generalis")_ was published by [Bernhardus Varenius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bernhardus_Varenius "Bernhardus Varenius"), which was later edited and republished by others including [Isaac Newton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaac_Newton "Isaac Newton").[[113]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Baker1955-113)[[114]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Warntz1989-114) This textbook sought to integrate new scientific discoveries and principles into classical geography and approach the discipline like the other sciences emerging, and is seen by some as the division between ancient and modern geography in the West.[[113]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Baker1955-113)[[114]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Warntz1989-114)

The _Geographia Generalis_ contained both theoretical background and practical applications related to ship navigation.[[114]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Warntz1989-114) The remaining problem facing both explorers and geographers was finding the latitude and longitude of a geographic location. While the problem of latitude was solved long ago, but that of longitude remained; agreeing on what zero meridians should be was only part of the problem. It was left to [John Harrison](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Harrison "John Harrison") to solve it by inventing the chronometer [H-4](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harrison_Number_Four "Harrison Number Four") in 1760, and later in 1884 for the [International Meridian Conference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Meridian_Conference "International Meridian Conference") to adopt by convention the [Greenwich meridian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenwich_meridian "Greenwich meridian") as zero meridians.[[111]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Aber1-111)

The 18th and 19th centuries were the times when geography became recognized as a discrete [academic discipline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_academic_disciplines "List of academic disciplines"), and became part of a typical university curriculum in Europe (especially Paris and [Berlin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berlin "Berlin")). The development of many geographic societies also occurred during the 19th century, with the foundations of the [Société de Géographie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soci%C3%A9t%C3%A9_de_G%C3%A9ographie "Société de Géographie") in 1821, the [Royal Geographical Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Geographical_Society "Royal Geographical Society") in 1830, [Russian Geographical Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_Geographical_Society "Russian Geographical Society") in 1845, [American Geographical Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Geographical_Society "American Geographical Society") in 1851, the [Royal Danish Geographical Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Danish_Geographical_Society "Royal Danish Geographical Society") in 1876 and the [National Geographic Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Geographic_Society "National Geographic Society") in 1888.[[115]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-t118-115) The influence of [Immanuel Kant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immanuel_Kant "Immanuel Kant"), [Alexander von Humboldt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_von_Humboldt "Alexander von Humboldt"), [Carl Ritter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Ritter "Carl Ritter"), and [Paul Vidal de la Blache](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Vidal_de_la_Blache "Paul Vidal de la Blache") can be seen as a major turning point in geography from philosophy to an academic subject.[[116]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Societe1-116)[[117]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-117)[[118]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-118)[[119]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-119)[[120]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-120) Geographers such as [Richard Hartshorne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Hartshorne "Richard Hartshorne") and [Joseph Kerski](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Kerski "Joseph Kerski") have regarded both Humboldt and Ritter as the founders of modern geography, as Humboldt and Ritter were the first to establish geography as an independent scientific discipline.[[121]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-121)[[122]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-122)

[![Image 48](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Waldo_Tobler_2007.jpg/250px-Waldo_Tobler_2007.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Waldo_Tobler_2007.jpg)

Waldo Tobler in front of the Newberry Library. Chicago, November 2007

Over the past two centuries, the advancements in technology with computers have led to the development of [geomatics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geomatics "Geomatics") and new practices such as participant observation and geostatistics being incorporated into geography's portfolio of tools. In the West during the 20th century, the discipline of geography went through four major phases: [environmental determinism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Environmental_determinism "Environmental determinism"), [regional geography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regional_geography "Regional geography"), the [quantitative revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantitative_revolution "Quantitative revolution"), and [critical geography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Critical_geography "Critical geography"). The strong interdisciplinary links between geography and the sciences of geology and [botany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Botany "Botany"), as well as economics, sociology, and [demographics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Demographics "Demographics"), have also grown greatly, especially as a result of [earth system science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth_system_science "Earth system science") that seeks to understand the world in a holistic view. New concepts and philosophies have emerged from the rapid advancement of computers, quantitative methods, and interdisciplinary approaches. The 1962 book _[Theoretical Geography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theoretical\_Geography "Theoretical Geography")_ by [William Bunge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Bunge "William Bunge"), which argued for a [nomothetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nomothetic "Nomothetic") approach to geography and that from a purely spatial perspective there was no real difference between human and physical geography, has been described by [Kevin R. Cox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kevin_R._Cox "Kevin R. Cox") as "perhaps the seminal text of the spatial-quantitative revolution."[[123]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Goodchild2008-123)[[124]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Cox2001-124) In 1970, [Waldo Tobler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Waldo_Tobler "Waldo Tobler") proposed the [first law of geography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tobler%27s_first_law_of_geography "Tobler's first law of geography"), "everything is related to everything else, but near things are more related than distant things."[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Tobler1-34)[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Tobler2-35) This law summarizes the first assumption geographers make about the world.

### Geology

[![Image 49](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Cycle_of_rocks_2.png/250px-Cycle_of_rocks_2.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Cycle_of_rocks_2.png)

The [rock cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rock_cycle "Rock cycle") shows the relationship between [igneous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Igneous "Igneous"), [sedimentary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sedimentary "Sedimentary"), and [metamorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metamorphic "Metamorphic")[rocks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rock_(geology) "Rock (geology)").

The discipline of geography, especially physical geography, and geology have significant overlap. In the past, the two have often shared academic departments at universities, a point that has led to conflict over resources.[[125]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Smith2-125) Both disciplines do seek to understand the [rocks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rocks "Rocks") on the Earth's surface and the processes that change them over time. Geology employs many of the tools and techniques of technical geographers, such as GIS and remote sensing to aid in [geological mapping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geological_mapping "Geological mapping").[[126]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Compton1-126) However, geology includes research that goes beyond the spatial component, such as the chemical analysis of rocks and [biogeochemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biogeochemistry "Biogeochemistry").[[127]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Gorham_1-127)

### History

The discipline of History has significant overlap with geography, especially human geography.[[128]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Bryce1-128)[[129]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Darby1-129) Like geology, history and geography have shared university departments. Geography provides the spatial context within which historical events unfold.[[128]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Bryce1-128) The physical geographic features of a region, such as its landforms, climate, and resources, shape human settlements, trade routes, and economic activities, which in turn influence the course of historical events.[[128]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Bryce1-128) Thus, a historian must have a strong foundation in geography.[[128]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Bryce1-128)[[129]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Darby1-129) Historians employ the techniques of technical geographers to create [historical atlases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Historical_atlas "Historical atlas") and maps.

### Planetary science

[![Image 50](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Apollo15DunaTisza.jpg/330px-Apollo15DunaTisza.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Apollo15DunaTisza.jpg)

Photograph from the [Apollo 15](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apollo_15 "Apollo 15") command module _Endeavour_ of the [rilles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rille "Rille") in the vicinity of the crater [Aristarchus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristarchus_(crater) "Aristarchus (crater)") on the [Moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moon "Moon").

While the discipline of geography is normally concerned with the Earth, the term can also be informally used to describe the study of other worlds, such as the [planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planet "Planet") of the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System") and even beyond.[[130]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Kereszturi2012-130) The study of systems larger than the Earth itself usually forms part of [Astronomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy "Astronomy") or [Cosmology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmology "Cosmology"), while the study of other planets is usually called [planetary science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_science "Planetary science"). Alternative terms such as [areography (geography of Mars)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Areography_(geography_of_Mars) "Areography (geography of Mars)") have been employed to describe the study of other celestial objects.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Areography1-19)[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Areoography2-20)[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-WilliamSheeham2014-21) Ultimately, geography may be considered a subdiscipline within planetary science, and planetary science link geography with fields like astronomy and physics.[[130]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography#cite_note-Kereszturi2012-130)
