**Caesium** ([IUPAC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IUPAC "IUPAC") spelling;[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-8) also spelled **cesium** in [American English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_English "American English")) is a [chemical element](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_element "Chemical element"); it has [symbol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symbol_(chemistry) "Symbol (chemistry)")**Cs** and [atomic number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_number "Atomic number") 55. It is a soft, silvery-golden [alkali metal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkali_metal "Alkali metal") with a melting point of 28.5°C (83.3°F; 301.6 K), which makes it one of only five elemental [metals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metal "Metal") that are [liquid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liquid "Liquid") at or near [room temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Room_temperature "Room temperature"). Caesium has physical and chemical properties similar to those of [rubidium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rubidium "Rubidium") and [potassium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium "Potassium"). It is [pyrophoric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrophoricity "Pyrophoricity") and reacts with [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water "Water") even at −116°C (−177°F). It is the least [electronegative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronegativity "Electronegativity") stable element, with a value of 0.79 on the [Pauling scale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pauling_scale "Pauling scale"). It has only one stable [isotope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotope "Isotope"), [caesium-133](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium-133 "Caesium-133"). Caesium is mined mostly from [pollucite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pollucite "Pollucite"). [Caesium-137](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium-137 "Caesium-137"), a [fission product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fission_product "Fission product"), is extracted from waste produced by [nuclear reactors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_reactor_technology "Nuclear reactor technology"). It has the largest [atomic radius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_radius "Atomic radius") of all elements whose radii have been measured or calculated, at about 260 [picometres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Picometre "Picometre").

The German chemist [Robert Bunsen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Bunsen "Robert Bunsen") and physicist [Gustav Kirchhoff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gustav_Kirchhoff "Gustav Kirchhoff") discovered caesium in 1860 by the newly developed method of [flame spectroscopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_emission_spectroscopy#Flame_emission_spectroscopy "Atomic emission spectroscopy"). The first small-scale applications for caesium were as a "[getter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Getter "Getter")" in [vacuum tubes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vacuum_tube "Vacuum tube") and in [photoelectric cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_cell "Solar cell"). Caesium is widely used in highly accurate [atomic clocks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_clock "Atomic clock"). In 1967, the [International System of Units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Units "International System of Units") began using a specific [hyperfine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperfine_structure "Hyperfine structure") transition of neutral caesium-133 atoms to define the [basic unit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SI_base_unit "SI base unit") of time, the [second](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second "Second").

Since the 1990s, the largest [application of the element](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#Applications) has been as [caesium formate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium_formate "Caesium formate") for [drilling fluids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drilling_fluid "Drilling fluid"), but it has a range of applications in the production of electricity, in electronics, and in chemistry. The radioactive isotope caesium-137 has a [half-life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Half-life "Half-life") of about 30 years and is used in medical applications, industrial gauges, and hydrology. Nonradioactive caesium compounds are only mildly [toxic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toxicity "Toxicity"), but the pure metal's tendency to react explosively with water means that it is considered a hazardous material, and the [radioisotopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radioisotopes "Radioisotopes") present a significant health and environmental hazard.

_Caesium_ is the spelling recommended by the [International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Union_of_Pure_and_Applied_Chemistry "International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry") (IUPAC).[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-9) The [American Chemical Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Chemical_Society "American Chemical Society") (ACS) has used the spelling _cesium_ since 1921,[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-10)[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-11) following _Webster's New International Dictionary_. The element was named after the Latin word _[caesius](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/caesius#Adjective "wikt:caesius")_, meaning "bluish grey".[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-12) In medieval and early modern writings _caesius_ was spelled with the [ligature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Typographic_ligature "Typographic ligature")_[æ](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%86#Latin\_and\_Greek "Æ")_ as _cæsius_; hence, an alternative but now old-fashioned orthography is _cæsium_. More spelling explanation at [ae/oe vs e](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_and_British_English_spelling_differences#ae_and_oe "American and British English spelling differences").

### Physical properties

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Caesium&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Physical properties")]

[![Image 5: Y-shaped yellowish crystal in glass ampoule, looking like the branch of a pine tree](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/CsCrystals.JPG/250px-CsCrystals.JPG)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:CsCrystals.JPG)

High-purity caesium stored in [argon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Argon "Argon").

Of all elements that are solid at room temperature, caesium is the softest: it has a hardness of 0.2 [Mohs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mohs_scale "Mohs scale"). It is a very [ductile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ductility "Ductility"), pale metal, which darkens in the presence of trace amounts of [oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen "Oxygen").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-USGS-13)[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-14)[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-15) When in the presence of [mineral oil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mineral_oil "Mineral oil") (where it is best kept during transport), it loses its metallic [lustre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lustre_(mineralogy) "Lustre (mineralogy)") and takes on a duller, grey appearance. It has a [melting point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Melting_point "Melting point") of 28.5°C (83.3°F), making it one of the few elemental metals that are liquid near [room temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Room_temperature "Room temperature"). The others are [rubidium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rubidium "Rubidium") (39°C [102°F]), [francium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Francium "Francium") (estimated at 27°C [81°F]), [mercury](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mercury_(element) "Mercury (element)") (−39°C [−38°F]), and [gallium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gallium "Gallium") (30°C [86°F]); bromine is also liquid at room temperature (melting at −7.2°C [19.0°F]), but it is a [halogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halogen "Halogen") and not a metal. [Mercury](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mercury_(element) "Mercury (element)") is the only stable elemental metal with a known melting point lower than caesium.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-KanerACS-16) In addition, the metal has a rather low [boiling point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boiling_point "Boiling point"), 641°C (1,186°F), the [lowest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_elements_by_boiling_point "List of elements by boiling point") of all stable metals other than mercury.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-RSC-17)[Copernicium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Copernicium "Copernicium") and [flerovium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flerovium "Flerovium") have been predicted to have lower boiling points than mercury and caesium, but they are extremely radioactive and it is not certain if they are metals.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-CRNL-18)[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-liquid-19)

[![Image 6](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Rb%26Cs_crystals.jpg/250px-Rb%26Cs_crystals.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Rb%26Cs_crystals.jpg)

Caesium crystals (golden) compared to [rubidium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rubidium "Rubidium") crystals (silvery)

Caesium forms [alloys](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alloy "Alloy") with the other alkali metals, [gold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gold "Gold"), and mercury ([amalgams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amalgam_(chemistry) "Amalgam (chemistry)")). At temperatures below 650°C (1,202°F), it does not alloy with [cobalt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cobalt "Cobalt"), [iron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron "Iron"), [molybdenum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molybdenum "Molybdenum"), [nickel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nickel "Nickel"), [platinum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platinum "Platinum"), [tantalum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tantalum "Tantalum"), or [tungsten](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tungsten "Tungsten"). It forms well-defined [intermetallic compounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intermetallics "Intermetallics") with [antimony](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antimony "Antimony"), [gallium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gallium "Gallium"), [indium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indium "Indium"), and [thorium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thorium "Thorium"), which are [photosensitive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photosensitive "Photosensitive").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-USGS-13) It mixes with all the other alkali metals (except lithium); the alloy with a [molar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molar_concentration "Molar concentration") distribution of 41% caesium, 47% [potassium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium "Potassium"), and 12% [sodium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium "Sodium") has the lowest melting point of any known metal alloy, at −78°C (−108°F).[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-KanerACS-16)[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-20) A few amalgams have been studied: CsHg

2 is black with a purple metallic [lustre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lustre_(mineralogy) "Lustre (mineralogy)"), while CsHg is golden-coloured, also with a metallic lustre.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-21)

The golden colour of caesium comes from the decreasing frequency of light required to excite electrons of the alkali metals as the group is descended. For lithium through rubidium this frequency is in the ultraviolet, but for caesium it enters the blue–violet end of the spectrum; in other words, the [plasmonic frequency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasma_oscillation "Plasma oscillation") of the alkali metals becomes lower from lithium to caesium. Thus caesium transmits and partially absorbs violet light preferentially while other colours (having lower frequency) are reflected; hence it appears yellowish.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-22) Its compounds burn with a blue[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-CRC74-23)[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-flametests-24) or violet[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-flametests-24) colour.

Caesium exists in the form of different [allotropes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allotropy "Allotropy"), one of them a dimer called dicaesium.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-25)

### Chemical properties

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Caesium&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Chemical properties")]

Addition of a small amount of caesium to cold water is explosive.

Caesium metal is highly reactive and [pyrophoric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrophoricity "Pyrophoricity"). It ignites spontaneously in air, and reacts explosively with water even at low temperatures, more so than the other [alkali metals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkali_metal "Alkali metal").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-USGS-13) It reacts with ice at temperatures as low as −116°C (−177°F).[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-KanerACS-16) Because of this high reactivity, caesium metal is classified as a [hazardous material](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hazardous_material "Hazardous material"). It is stored and shipped in dry, saturated hydrocarbons such as [mineral oil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mineral_oil "Mineral oil"). It can be handled only under [inert gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inert_gas "Inert gas"), such as [argon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Argon "Argon"). However, a caesium-water explosion is often less powerful than a [sodium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium "Sodium")-water explosion with a similar amount of sodium. This is because caesium explodes instantly upon contact with water, leaving little time for [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen") to accumulate.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-26) Caesium can be stored in vacuum-sealed [borosilicate glass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Borosilicate_glass "Borosilicate glass")[ampoules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ampoule "Ampoule"). In quantities of more than about 100 grams (3.5 oz), caesium is shipped in hermetically sealed, stainless steel containers.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-USGS-13)

The chemistry of caesium is similar to that of other alkali metals, in particular [rubidium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rubidium "Rubidium"), the element above caesium in the periodic table.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-greenwood-27) As expected for an alkali metal, the only common oxidation state is +1. It differs from this value in caesides, which contain the Cs− anion and thus have caesium in the −1 oxidation state.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-caeside2-5) Under conditions of extreme pressure (greater than 30[GPa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pascal_(unit) "Pascal (unit)")), theoretical studies indicate that the inner 5p electrons could form chemical bonds, where caesium would behave as the seventh 5p element, suggesting that higher caesium fluorides with caesium in oxidation states from +2 to +6 could exist under such conditions.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-28)[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-29) Some slight differences arise from the fact that it has a higher [atomic mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_mass "Atomic mass") and is more [electropositive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronegativity "Electronegativity") than other (nonradioactive) alkali metals.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-HollemanAF-30) Caesium is the most electropositive chemical element.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-KanerACS-16) The caesium ion is also larger and [less "hard"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HSAB_theory "HSAB theory") than those of the lighter [alkali metals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkali_metals "Alkali metals").

[![Image 7: 27 small grey spheres in 3 evenly spaced layers of nine. 8 spheres form a regular cube and 8 of those cubes form a larger cube. The grey spheres represent the caesium atoms. The center of each small cube is occupied by a small green sphere representing a chlorine atom. Thus, every chlorine is in the middle of a cube formed by caesium atoms and every caesium is in the middle of a cube formed by chlorine.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/CsCl_polyhedra.png/250px-CsCl_polyhedra.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:CsCl_polyhedra.png)

Ball-and-stick model of the cubic coordination of Cs and Cl in CsCl

Most caesium compounds contain the element as the [cation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cation "Cation")Cs+

, which [binds ionically](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ionic_bond "Ionic bond") to a wide variety of [anions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anion "Anion"). One noteworthy exception is the [caeside](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkalide "Alkalide") anion (Cs−

),[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-caeside2-5) and others are the several suboxides (see section on oxides below). More recently, caesium is predicted to behave as a [p-block](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/P-block "P-block") element and capable of forming higher fluorides with higher [oxidation states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxidation_state "Oxidation state") (i.e., CsF n with n>1) under high pressure.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-31) This prediction needs to be validated by further experiments.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-32)

Salts of Cs+ are usually colourless unless the anion itself is coloured. Many of the simple salts are [hygroscopic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hygroscopic "Hygroscopic"), but less so than the corresponding salts of lighter alkali metals. The [phosphate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphate "Phosphate"),[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-33)[acetate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetate "Acetate"), [carbonate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonate "Carbonate"), [halides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halide "Halide"), [oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxide "Oxide"), [nitrate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrate "Nitrate"), and [sulfate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfate "Sulfate") salts are water-soluble. Its [double salts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double_salt "Double salt") are often less soluble, and the low solubility of caesium aluminium sulfate is exploited in refining Cs from ores. The double salts with antimony (such as CsSbCl

4), [bismuth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bismuth "Bismuth"), [cadmium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cadmium "Cadmium"), [copper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Copper "Copper"), [iron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron "Iron"), and [lead](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lead "Lead") are also poorly [soluble](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dissolution_(chemistry) "Dissolution (chemistry)").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-USGS-13)

[Caesium hydroxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium_hydroxide "Caesium hydroxide") (CsOH) is [hygroscopic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hygroscopic "Hygroscopic") and strongly [basic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Base_(chemistry) "Base (chemistry)").[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-greenwood-27) It rapidly [etches](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Etching "Etching") the surface of [semiconductors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semiconductor "Semiconductor") such as [silicon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon "Silicon").[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-34) CsOH has been previously regarded by chemists as the "strongest base", reflecting the relatively weak attraction between the large Cs+ ion and OH−;[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-CRC74-23) it is indeed the strongest [Arrhenius base](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arrhenius_base "Arrhenius base"); however, a number of compounds such as [_n_-butyllithium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/N-butyllithium "N-butyllithium"), [sodium amide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_amide "Sodium amide"), [sodium hydride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_hydride "Sodium hydride"), [caesium hydride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium_hydride "Caesium hydride"), etc., which cannot be dissolved in water as reacting violently with it but rather only used in some [anhydrous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anhydrous "Anhydrous")[polar aprotic solvents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_aprotic_solvents "Polar aprotic solvents"), are far more basic on the basis of the [Brønsted–Lowry acid–base theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Br%C3%B8nsted%E2%80%93Lowry_acid%E2%80%93base_theory "Brønsted–Lowry acid–base theory").[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-greenwood-27)

A [stoichiometric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stoichiometry "Stoichiometry") mixture of caesium and gold will react to form yellow [caesium auride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium_auride "Caesium auride") (Cs+Au−) upon heating. The auride anion here behaves as a [pseudohalogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudohalogen "Pseudohalogen"). The compound reacts violently with water, yielding [caesium hydroxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium_hydroxide "Caesium hydroxide"), metallic gold, and hydrogen gas; it dissolves in liquid ammonia and then can be reacted with a caesium-specific ion exchange resin to produce [tetramethylammonium auride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetramethylammonium_auride "Tetramethylammonium auride"). The analogous [platinum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platinum "Platinum") compound, red caesium platinide (Cs 2 Pt), contains the platinide ion that behaves as a pseudo[chalcogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chalcogen "Chalcogen").[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-35)

Like all metal cations, Cs+ forms complexes with [Lewis bases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lewis_base "Lewis base") in solution. Because of its large size, Cs+ usually adopts [coordination numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordination_number "Coordination number") greater than 6, the number typical for the smaller alkali metal cations. This difference is apparent in the 8-coordination of CsCl. This high coordination number and [softness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HSAB "HSAB") (tendency to form covalent bonds) are properties exploited in separating Cs+ from other cations in the remediation of nuclear wastes, where 137 Cs+ must be separated from large amounts of nonradioactive K+.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-36)

Monatomic caesium halide wires grown inside double-wall [carbon nanotubes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_nanotube "Carbon nanotube") ([TEM image](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transmission_electron_microscopy "Transmission electron microscopy")).[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-chains-37)

[Caesium fluoride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium_fluoride "Caesium fluoride") (CsF) is a [hygroscopic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hygroscopic "Hygroscopic") white solid that is widely used in [organofluorine chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organofluorine_chemistry "Organofluorine chemistry") as a source of [fluoride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluoride "Fluoride") anions.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-38) Caesium fluoride has the halite structure, which means that the Cs+ and F− pack in a [cubic closest packed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cubic_closest_packed "Cubic closest packed") array as do Na+ and Cl− in [sodium chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride "Sodium chloride").[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-greenwood-27) Notably, caesium and fluorine have the lowest and highest [electronegativities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronegativity "Electronegativity"), respectively, among all the known elements.

[Caesium chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium_chloride "Caesium chloride") (CsCl) crystallizes in the simple [cubic crystal system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cubic_crystal_system "Cubic crystal system"). Also called the "caesium chloride structure",[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-HollemanAF-30) this structural motif is composed of a [primitive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primitive_cell "Primitive cell") cubic lattice with a two-atom basis, each with an eightfold [coordination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordination_number "Coordination number"); the chloride atoms lie upon the lattice points at the edges of the cube, while the caesium atoms lie in the holes in the centre of the cubes. This structure is shared with [CsBr](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium_bromide "Caesium bromide") and [CsI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium_iodide "Caesium iodide"), and many other compounds that do not contain Cs. In contrast, most other alkaline halides have the [sodium chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride "Sodium chloride") (NaCl) structure.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-HollemanAF-30) The CsCl structure is preferred because Cs+ has an [ionic radius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ionic_radius "Ionic radius") of 174[pm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Picometer "Picometer") and Cl−

 181 pm.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-39)

Caesium has 41 known [isotopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotope "Isotope"), ranging in [mass number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass_number "Mass number") from 112 to 152. The only [stable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stable_nuclide "Stable nuclide") caesium isotope is 133 Cs, with 78 [neutrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron "Neutron"). The radioactive [135 Cs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium-135 "Caesium-135") has a very long half-life of about 1.33 million years, the longest of all radioactive isotopes of caesium. [137 Cs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium-137 "Caesium-137") and [134 Cs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium-134 "Caesium-134") have half-lives of 30.04 and 2.065 years, respectively. The isotopes with mass numbers of 129, 131, 132 and 136, have half-lives between a day and two weeks, while most of the other isotopes have half-lives from a few seconds to fractions of a second. At least 21 metastable [nuclear isomers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_isomer "Nuclear isomer") exist. Other than 134m Cs (with a half-life of just under 3 hours), all are very unstable and decay with half-lives of a few minutes or less.[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-50)

The isotope 135 Cs is one of the [long-lived fission products](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Long-lived_fission_product "Long-lived fission product") of [uranium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uranium "Uranium") produced in [nuclear reactors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_reactor_technology "Nuclear reactor technology").[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-51) However, this [fission product yield](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fission_product_yield "Fission product yield") is reduced in most reactors because the predecessor, [135 Xe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xenon-135 "Xenon-135"), is a potent [neutron poison](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron_poison "Neutron poison") and frequently transmutes to stable [136 Xe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xenon-136 "Xenon-136") before it can decay to 135 Cs.[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-52)[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-53)

The [beta decay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beta_decay "Beta decay") from 137 Cs to 137m Ba results in [gamma radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamma_ray "Gamma ray") as the 137m Ba relaxes to ground state 137 Ba, with the emitted photons having an energy of 0.6617 MeV.[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-54)137 Cs and [90 Sr](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strontium-90 "Strontium-90") are the principal [medium-lived](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medium-lived_fission_product "Medium-lived fission product") products of [nuclear fission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_fission "Nuclear fission"), and the prime sources of [radioactivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radioactive_decay "Radioactive decay") from [spent nuclear fuel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spent_nuclear_fuel "Spent nuclear fuel") from several years to several hundred years after removal.[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-55) Those two isotopes are the largest source of residual radioactivity in the area of the [Chernobyl disaster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chernobyl_disaster "Chernobyl disaster").[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-56) Because of the low capture rate, disposing of 137 Cs through [neutron capture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron_capture "Neutron capture") is not feasible and the only current solution is to allow it to decay over time.[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-57)

Almost all caesium produced from nuclear fission comes from the [beta decay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beta_decay "Beta decay") of originally more neutron-rich fission products, passing through various [isotopes of iodine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotopes_of_iodine "Isotopes of iodine") and [xenon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotopes_of_xenon "Isotopes of xenon").[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-58) Because iodine and xenon are volatile and can diffuse through nuclear fuel or air, radioactive caesium is often created far from the original site of fission.[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-59) With [nuclear weapons testing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_weapons_testing "Nuclear weapons testing") in the 1950s through the 1980s, 137 Cs was released into the [atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth "Atmosphere of Earth") and returned to the surface of the earth as a component of [radioactive fallout](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_fallout "Nuclear fallout"), becoming a marker of the movement of soil and sediment from those times.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-USGS-13)

Although it has a large [nuclear spin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_spin "Nuclear spin") (⁠7/2⁠+), [nuclear magnetic resonance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_magnetic_resonance "Nuclear magnetic resonance") can use the stable 133 Cs isotope.[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-NMR-60)

Caesium is a relatively rare element, estimated to average 3[parts per million](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parts_per_million "Parts per million") in the [Earth's crust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abundance_of_elements_in_Earth%27s_crust "Abundance of elements in Earth's crust").[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-61) It is the 45th most abundant element and 36th among the metals.[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-62) Caesium is 30 times less abundant than [rubidium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rubidium "Rubidium"), with which it is closely associated, chemically.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-USGS-13)

Mining and refining pollucite ore is a selective process and is conducted on a smaller scale than for most other metals. The ore is crushed, hand-sorted, but not usually concentrated, and then ground. Caesium is then extracted from pollucite primarily by three methods: acid digestion, alkaline decomposition, and direct reduction.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-USGS-13)[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-Burt-67)

All wear long jackets, and the shorter man on the left has a beard.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Kirchhoff_Bunsen_Roscoe.jpg/250px-Kirchhoff_Bunsen_Roscoe.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Kirchhoff_Bunsen_Roscoe.jpg)

[Gustav Kirchhoff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gustav_Kirchhoff "Gustav Kirchhoff") (left) and [Robert Bunsen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Bunsen "Robert Bunsen") (centre) discovered caesium with their newly invented spectroscope.

In 1860, [Robert Bunsen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Bunsen "Robert Bunsen") and [Gustav Kirchhoff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gustav_Kirchhoff "Gustav Kirchhoff") discovered caesium in the [mineral water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mineral_water "Mineral water") from [Dürkheim](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bad_D%C3%BCrkheim "Bad Dürkheim"), Germany. Because of the bright blue lines in the [emission spectrum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emission_spectrum "Emission spectrum"), they derived the name from the [Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin "Latin") word _caesius_, meaning 'bluish grey'.[[note 1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-70)[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-71)[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-BuKi1861-72)[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-Weeks-73) Caesium was the first element to be discovered with a [spectroscope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopy "Spectroscopy"), which had been invented by Bunsen and Kirchhoff only a year previously.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-KanerACS-16)

To obtain a pure sample of caesium, 44,000 litres (9,700 imp gal; 12,000 US gal) of mineral water had to be evaporated to yield 240 kilograms (530 lb) of concentrated salt solution. The [alkaline earth metals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkaline_earth_metal "Alkaline earth metal") were precipitated either as sulfates or [oxalates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxalate "Oxalate"), leaving the alkali metal in the solution. After conversion to the [nitrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrate "Nitrate") and extraction with [ethanol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethanol "Ethanol"), a sodium-free mixture was obtained. From this mixture, the lithium was precipitated by [ammonium carbonate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium_carbonate "Ammonium carbonate"). Potassium, rubidium, and caesium form insoluble salts with [chloroplatinic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chloroplatinic_acid "Chloroplatinic acid"), but these salts show a slight difference in solubility in hot water, and the less-soluble caesium and rubidium hexachloroplatinate ((Cs,Rb)2 PtCl 6) were obtained by [fractional crystallization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractional_crystallization_(chemistry) "Fractional crystallization (chemistry)"). After reduction of the hexachloroplatinate with [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen"), caesium and rubidium were separated by the difference in solubility of their carbonates in alcohol. The process yielded 9.2 grams (0.32 oz) of [rubidium chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rubidium_chloride "Rubidium chloride") and 7.3 grams (0.26 oz) of caesium chloride from the initial 44,000 litres of mineral water.[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-BuKi1861-72)

From the caesium chloride, the two scientists estimated the [atomic weight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_weight "Atomic weight") of the new element at 123.35 (compared to the currently accepted one of 132.9).[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-BuKi1861-72) They tried to generate elemental caesium by electrolysis of molten caesium chloride, but instead of a metal, they obtained a blue homogeneous substance which "neither under the naked eye nor under the microscope showed the slightest trace of metallic substance"; as a result, they assigned it as a [subchloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-stoichiometric_compound "Non-stoichiometric compound") (Cs

2 Cl). In reality, the product was probably a [colloidal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colloid "Colloid") mixture of the metal and caesium chloride.[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-74) The electrolysis of the aqueous solution of chloride with a mercury cathode produced a caesium amalgam which readily decomposed under the aqueous conditions.[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-BuKi1861-72) The pure metal was eventually isolated by the Swedish chemist [Carl Setterberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Setterberg "Carl Setterberg") while working on his doctorate with [Kekulé](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_August_Kekul%C3%A9_von_Stradonitz "Friedrich August Kekulé von Stradonitz") and Bunsen.[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-Weeks-73) In 1882, he produced caesium metal by electrolysing [caesium cyanide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium_cyanide "Caesium cyanide"), avoiding the problems with the chloride.[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-Sett-75)

Historically, the most important use for caesium has been in research and development, primarily in chemical and electrical fields. Very few applications existed for caesium until the 1920s, when it came into use in radio [vacuum tubes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vacuum_tube "Vacuum tube"), where it had two functions; as a [getter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Getter "Getter"), it removed excess oxygen after manufacture, and as a coating on the heated [cathode](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cathode "Cathode"), it increased the [electrical conductivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrical_conductivity "Electrical conductivity"). Caesium was not recognized as a high-performance industrial metal until the 1950s.[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-76) Applications for nonradioactive caesium included [photoelectric cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_cell "Solar cell"), [photomultiplier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photomultiplier "Photomultiplier") tubes, optical components of [infrared spectrophotometers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infrared_spectroscopy "Infrared spectroscopy"), catalysts for several organic reactions, crystals for [scintillation counters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scintillation_counter "Scintillation counter"), and in [magnetohydrodynamic power generators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MHD_generator "MHD generator").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-USGS-13) Caesium is also used as a source of positive ions in [secondary ion mass spectrometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Secondary_ion_mass_spectrometry "Secondary ion mass spectrometry") (SIMS).

Since 1967, the [International System of Measurements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Units "International System of Units") has based the primary unit of time, the second, on the properties of caesium. The International System of Units (SI) defines the second as the duration of 9,192,631,770 cycles at the [microwave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microwave "Microwave")[frequency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frequency "Frequency") of the [spectral line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectral_line "Spectral line") corresponding to the transition between two [hyperfine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperfine_structure "Hyperfine structure")[energy levels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_level "Energy level") of the [ground state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ground_state "Ground state") of [caesium-133](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium-133 "Caesium-133").[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-USNO-77) The 13th [General Conference on Weights and Measures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Conference_on_Weights_and_Measures "General Conference on Weights and Measures") of 1967 defined a second as: "the duration of 9,192,631,770 cycles of microwave light absorbed or emitted by the hyperfine transition of caesium-133 atoms in their ground state undisturbed by external fields".

### Petroleum exploration

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Caesium&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: Petroleum exploration")]

The largest present-day use of nonradioactive caesium is in [caesium formate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formate "Formate")[drilling fluids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drilling_fluid "Drilling fluid") for the [extractive oil industry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extractive_oil_industry "Extractive oil industry").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-USGS-13) Aqueous solutions of caesium formate (HCOO−Cs+)—made by reacting caesium hydroxide with [formic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid "Formic acid")—were developed in the mid-1990s for use as oil well drilling and [completion fluids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Completion_(oil_and_gas_wells) "Completion (oil and gas wells)"). The function of a drilling fluid is to lubricate drill bits, to bring rock cuttings to the surface, and to maintain pressure on the formation during drilling of the well. Completion fluids assist the emplacement of control hardware after drilling but prior to production by maintaining the pressure.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-USGS-13)

The high density of the caesium formate brine (up to 2.3 g/cm 3, or 19.2 pounds per gallon),[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-Down-78) coupled with the relatively benign nature of most caesium compounds, reduces the requirement for toxic high-density suspended solids in the drilling fluid—a significant technological, engineering and environmental advantage. Unlike the components of many other heavy liquids, caesium formate is relatively environment-friendly.[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-Down-78) Caesium formate brine can be blended with potassium and sodium formates to decrease the density of the fluids to that of water (1.0 g/cm 3, or 8.3 pounds per gallon). Furthermore, it is biodegradable and may be recycled, which is important in view of its high cost (about $4,000 per [barrel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barrel_(volume)#Oil_barrel "Barrel (volume)") in 2001).[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-79) Alkali formates are safe to handle and do not damage the producing formation or downhole metals as corrosive alternative, high-density brines (such as [zinc bromide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zinc_bromide "Zinc bromide")ZnBr

2 solutions) sometimes do; they also require less cleanup and reduce disposal costs.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-USGS-13)

Most, but not all, of the cabinets are filled with white boxes.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Usno-mc.jpg/250px-Usno-mc.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Usno-mc.jpg)

Atomic clock ensemble at the U.S. Naval Observatory

Caesium-based [atomic clocks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_clock "Atomic clock") use the [electromagnetic transitions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation "Electromagnetic radiation") in the [hyperfine structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperfine_structure "Hyperfine structure") of caesium-133 atoms as a reference point. The first accurate caesium clock was built by [Louis Essen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis_Essen "Louis Essen") in 1955 at the [National Physical Laboratory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Physical_Laboratory,_UK "National Physical Laboratory, UK") in the UK.[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-80) Caesium clocks have improved over the past half-century and are regarded as "the most accurate realization of a unit that mankind has yet achieved."[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-USNO-77) These clocks measure frequency with an error of 2 to 3 parts in 10 14, which corresponds to an accuracy of 2[nanoseconds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nanosecond "Nanosecond") per day, or one second in 1.4 million years. The latest versions are more accurate than 1 part in 10 15, about 1 second in 20 million years.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-USGS-13) The [caesium standard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium_standard "Caesium standard") is the primary standard for standards-compliant time and frequency measurements.[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-81) Caesium clocks regulate the timing of cell phone networks and the Internet.[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-82)

#### Definition of the second

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Caesium&action=edit&section=17 "Edit section: Definition of the second")]

The second, symbol _s_, is the SI unit of time. The [BIPM](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BIPM "BIPM") restated its definition at its 26th conference in 2018: "[The second] is defined by taking the fixed numerical value of the caesium frequency Δ _ν_ Cs, the unperturbed ground-state hyperfine transition frequency of the caesium-133 atom, to be 9 192 631 770 when expressed in the unit [Hz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hz "Hz"), which is equal to s−1."[[82]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-83)

### Electric power and electronics

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Caesium&action=edit&section=18 "Edit section: Electric power and electronics")]

Caesium vapour [thermionic generators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermionic_converter "Thermionic converter") are low-power devices that convert heat energy to electrical energy. In the two-electrode [vacuum tube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vacuum_tube "Vacuum tube") converter, caesium neutralizes the space charge near the cathode and enhances the current flow.[[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-84)

Caesium is also important for its [photoemissive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect "Photoelectric effect") properties, converting light to electron flow. It is used in [photoelectric cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_cell "Solar cell") because caesium-based cathodes, such as the intermetallic compound K

2 CsSb, have a low threshold voltage for emission of [electrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron "Electron").[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-85) The range of photoemissive devices using caesium include [optical character recognition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optical_character_recognition "Optical character recognition") devices, [photomultiplier tubes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photomultiplier "Photomultiplier"), and [video camera tubes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Video_camera_tube "Video camera tube").[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-86)[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-87) Nevertheless, [germanium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germanium "Germanium"), rubidium, selenium, silicon, tellurium, and several other elements can be substituted for caesium in photosensitive materials.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-USGS-13)

[Caesium iodide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium_iodide "Caesium iodide") (CsI), [bromide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium_bromide "Caesium bromide") (CsBr) and [fluoride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium_fluoride "Caesium fluoride") (CsF) crystals are employed for [scintillators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scintillator "Scintillator") in [scintillation counters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scintillation_counter "Scintillation counter") widely used in mineral exploration and particle physics research to detect [gamma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamma_ray "Gamma ray") and [X-ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray "X-ray") radiation. Being a heavy element, caesium provides good stopping power with better detection. Caesium compounds may provide a faster response (CsF) and be less hygroscopic (CsI).

Caesium vapour is used in many common [magnetometers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetometer "Magnetometer").[[87]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-88)

The element is used as an [internal standard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internal_standard "Internal standard") in [spectrophotometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectrophotometry "Spectrophotometry").[[88]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-89) Like other [alkali metals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkali_metal "Alkali metal"), caesium has a great affinity for [oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen "Oxygen") and is used as a "[getter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Getter "Getter")" in [vacuum tubes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vacuum_tube "Vacuum tube").[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-90) Other uses of the metal include high-energy [lasers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laser "Laser"), [vapour glow lamps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluorescent_lamp "Fluorescent lamp"), and vapour [rectifiers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectifier "Rectifier").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-USGS-13)

### Centrifugation fluids

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Caesium&action=edit&section=19 "Edit section: Centrifugation fluids")]

The high density of the caesium ion makes solutions of caesium chloride, caesium sulfate, and caesium [trifluoroacetate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trifluoroacetic_acid "Trifluoroacetic acid") (Cs(O

2 CCF

3)) useful in molecular biology for density gradient [ultracentrifugation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_centrifugation "Differential centrifugation").[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-91) This technology is used primarily in the isolation of [viral particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virus "Virus"), subcellular [organelles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organelle "Organelle") and fractions, and [nucleic acids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleic_acid "Nucleic acid") from biological samples.[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-92)

### Chemical and medical use

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Caesium&action=edit&section=20 "Edit section: Chemical and medical use")]

[![Image 14: Some fine white powder on a laboratory watch glass](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Caesium_chloride.jpg/250px-Caesium_chloride.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Caesium_chloride.jpg)

Caesium chloride powder

Relatively few chemical applications use caesium.[[92]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-93) Doping with caesium compounds enhances the effectiveness of several metal-ion catalysts for chemical synthesis, such as [acrylic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acrylic_acid "Acrylic acid"), [anthraquinone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anthraquinone "Anthraquinone"), [ethylene oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene_oxide "Ethylene oxide"), [methanol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol "Methanol"), [phthalic anhydride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phthalic_anhydride "Phthalic anhydride"), [styrene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Styrene "Styrene"), [methyl methacrylate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methyl_methacrylate "Methyl methacrylate") monomers, and various [olefins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkene "Alkene"). It is also used in the catalytic conversion of [sulfur dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide "Sulfur dioxide") into [sulfur trioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_trioxide "Sulfur trioxide") in the production of [sulfuric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfuric_acid "Sulfuric acid").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-USGS-13)

[Caesium fluoride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium_fluoride "Caesium fluoride") enjoys a niche use in [organic chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_chemistry "Organic chemistry") as a [base](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Base_(chemistry) "Base (chemistry)")[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-greenwood-27) and as an [anhydrous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anhydrous "Anhydrous") source of [fluoride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluoride "Fluoride") ion.[[93]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-94) Caesium salts sometimes replace potassium or sodium salts in [organic synthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_synthesis "Organic synthesis"), such as [cyclization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclic_compound "Cyclic compound"), [esterification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Esterification "Esterification"), and [polymerization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polymerization "Polymerization"). Caesium has also been used in thermoluminescent radiation [dosimetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dosimetry "Dosimetry")(TLD): When exposed to radiation, it acquires crystal defects that, when heated, revert with emission of light proportionate to the received dose. Thus, measuring the light pulse with a [photomultiplier tube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photomultiplier_tube "Photomultiplier tube") can allow the accumulated radiation dose to be quantified.

### Nuclear and isotope applications

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Caesium&action=edit&section=21 "Edit section: Nuclear and isotope applications")]

[Caesium-137](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium-137 "Caesium-137") is a [radioisotope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radionuclide "Radionuclide") commonly used as a [gamma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamma_ray "Gamma ray")-emitter in industrial applications. Its advantages include a half-life of roughly 30 years, its availability from the [nuclear fuel cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_fuel_cycle "Nuclear fuel cycle"), and having [137 Ba](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotopes_of_barium "Isotopes of barium") as a stable end product. The high water solubility is a disadvantage which makes it incompatible with large pool irradiators for food and medical supplies.[[94]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-Takeshi-95) It has been used in agriculture, cancer treatment, and the [sterilization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sterilization_(microbiology) "Sterilization (microbiology)") of food, sewage sludge, and surgical equipment.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-USGS-13)[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-96) Radioactive [isotopes of caesium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotopes_of_caesium "Isotopes of caesium") in [radiation devices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiation_therapy "Radiation therapy") were used in the medical field to treat certain types of cancer,[[96]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-97) but emergence of better alternatives and the use of water-soluble caesium chloride in the sources, which could create wide-ranging contamination, gradually put some of these caesium sources out of use.[[97]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-98)[[98]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-99) Caesium-137 has been employed in a variety of industrial measurement gauges, including moisture, density, levelling, and thickness gauges.[[99]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-gauges-100) It has also been used in [well logging](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Well_logging "Well logging") devices for measuring the [electron density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_density "Electron density") of the rock formations, which is analogous to the bulk density of the formations.[[100]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-101)

Caesium-137 has been used in [hydrologic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrology "Hydrology") studies analogous to those with [tritium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tritium "Tritium"). As a daughter product of fission bomb testing from the 1950s through the mid-1980s, caesium-137 was released into the atmosphere, where it was absorbed readily into solution. Known year-to-year variation within that period allows correlation with soil and sediment layers. Caesium-134, and to a lesser extent caesium-135, have also been used in hydrology to measure the caesium output by the nuclear power industry. While they are less prevalent than either caesium-133 or caesium-137, these bellwether isotopes are produced solely from anthropogenic sources.[[101]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-102)

[![Image 15: Electrons beamed from an electron gun hit and ionize neutral fuel atoms; in a chamber surrounded by magnets, the positive ions are directed toward a negative grid that accelerates them. The force of the engine is created by expelling the ions from the rear at high velocity. On exiting, the positive ions are neutralized from another electron gun, ensuring that neither the ship nor the exhaust is electrically charged and are not attracted.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Electrostatic_ion_thruster-en.svg/500px-Electrostatic_ion_thruster-en.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Electrostatic_ion_thruster-en.svg)

Schematics of an electrostatic ion thruster developed for use with caesium or mercury fuel

Caesium and mercury were used as a propellant in early [ion engines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ion_thruster "Ion thruster") designed for [spacecraft propulsion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spacecraft_propulsion "Spacecraft propulsion") on very long interplanetary or extraplanetary missions. The fuel was ionized by contact with a charged [tungsten](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tungsten "Tungsten") electrode. But corrosion by caesium on spacecraft components has pushed development in the direction of inert gas propellants, such as [xenon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xenon "Xenon"), which are easier to handle in ground-based tests and do less potential damage to the spacecraft.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-USGS-13) Xenon was used in the experimental spacecraft [Deep Space 1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deep_Space_1 "Deep Space 1") launched in 1998.[[102]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-103)[[103]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-104) Nevertheless, [field-emission electric propulsion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field-emission_electric_propulsion "Field-emission electric propulsion") thrusters that accelerate liquid metal ions such as caesium have been built.[[104]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-105)

[Caesium nitrate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium_nitrate "Caesium nitrate") is used as an [oxidizer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxidizing_agent "Oxidizing agent") and [pyrotechnic colorant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrotechnic_colorant "Pyrotechnic colorant") to burn [silicon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon "Silicon") in [infrared](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infrared "Infrared")[flares](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flare_(pyrotechnic) "Flare (pyrotechnic)"),[[105]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-106) such as the LUU-19 flare,[[106]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-107) because it emits much of its light in the [near infrared](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infrared "Infrared") spectrum.[[107]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-108) Caesium compounds may have been used as fuel additives to reduce the [radar signature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radar_cross-section "Radar cross-section") of [exhaust plumes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exhaust_gas "Exhaust gas") in the [Lockheed A-12](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lockheed_A-12 "Lockheed A-12")[CIA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CIA "CIA") reconnaissance aircraft.[[108]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-109) Caesium and rubidium have been added as a [carbonate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonate "Carbonate") to glass because they reduce electrical conductivity and improve stability and durability of [fibre optics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optical_fiber "Optical fiber") and [night vision](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Night_vision "Night vision") devices. Caesium fluoride or caesium aluminium fluoride are used in fluxes formulated for brazing [aluminium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aluminium "Aluminium") alloys that contain [magnesium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnesium "Magnesium").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-USGS-13)

[Magnetohydrodynamic (MHD) power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MHD_generator "MHD generator")-generating systems were researched, but failed to gain widespread acceptance.[[109]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-110) Caesium metal has also been considered as the working fluid in high-temperature [Rankine cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rankine_cycle "Rankine cycle") turboelectric generators.[[110]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-111)

Caesium salts have been evaluated as antishock reagents following the administration of [arsenical drugs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arsenic_toxicity "Arsenic toxicity"). Because of their effect on heart rhythms, however, they are less likely to be used than potassium or rubidium salts. They have also been used to treat [epilepsy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epilepsy "Epilepsy").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-USGS-13)

Caesium-133 can be [laser cooled](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laser_cooling "Laser cooling") and used to probe fundamental and [technological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_technology "Quantum technology") problems in [quantum physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics"). It has a particularly convenient [Feshbach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feshbach_resonance "Feshbach resonance") spectrum to enable studies of [ultracold atoms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultracold_atom "Ultracold atom") requiring tunable interactions.[[111]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-112)

The portion of the total radiation dose (in air) contributed by each isotope plotted against time after the [Chernobyl disaster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chernobyl_disaster "Chernobyl disaster"). Nonradioactive caesium compounds are only mildly toxic, and nonradioactive caesium is not a significant environmental hazard. Because biochemical processes can confuse and substitute caesium with [potassium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium "Potassium"), excess caesium can lead to [hypokalemia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypokalemia "Hypokalemia"), [arrhythmia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heart_arrhythmia "Heart arrhythmia"), and acute [cardiac arrest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardiac_arrest "Cardiac arrest"), but such amounts would not ordinarily be encountered in natural sources.[[114]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-115)[[115]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-116)

The [median lethal dose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Median_lethal_dose "Median lethal dose") (LD 50) for [caesium chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium_chloride "Caesium chloride") in mice is 2.3 g per kilogram, which is comparable to the LD 50 values of [potassium chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_chloride "Potassium chloride") and [sodium chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride "Sodium chloride").[[116]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-117) The principal use of nonradioactive caesium is as caesium formate in petroleum [drilling fluids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drilling_fluid "Drilling fluid") because it is much less toxic than alternatives, though it is more costly.[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-Down-78)

Elemental caesium is one of the most reactive elements and is highly [explosive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Explosive_material "Explosive material") in the presence of water. The hydrogen gas produced by the reaction is heated by the thermal energy released at the same time, causing ignition and a violent explosion. This can occur with other alkali metals, but caesium is so potent that this explosive reaction can be triggered even by cold water.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-USGS-13)

It is highly [pyrophoric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrophoricity "Pyrophoricity"): the [autoignition temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autoignition_temperature "Autoignition temperature") of caesium is −116°C (−177°F), and it ignites explosively in air to form [caesium hydroxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium_hydroxide "Caesium hydroxide") and various oxides. Caesium hydroxide is a very strong [base](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Base_(chemistry) "Base (chemistry)"), and will rapidly corrode glass.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-RSC-17)

The [isotopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotope "Isotope")[134](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium-134 "Caesium-134") and 137 are present in the [biosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere "Biosphere") in small amounts from human activities, differing by location. Radiocaesium does not accumulate in the body as readily as other fission products (such as radioiodine and radiostrontium). About 10% of absorbed radiocaesium washes out of the body relatively quickly in sweat and urine. The remaining 90% has a biological half-life between 50 and 150 days.[[117]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-118) Radiocaesium follows potassium and tends to accumulate in plant tissues, including fruits and vegetables.[[118]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-119)[[119]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-120)[[120]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-121) Plants vary widely in the absorption of caesium, sometimes displaying great resistance to it. It is also well-documented that mushrooms from contaminated forests accumulate radiocaesium (caesium-137) in the fungal [sporocarps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sporocarp_(fungi) "Sporocarp (fungi)").[[121]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-122) Accumulation of caesium-137 in lakes has been a great concern after the [Chernobyl disaster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chernobyl_disaster "Chernobyl disaster").[[122]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-smithber05-123)[[123]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-124) Experiments with dogs showed that a single dose of 3.8 [millicuries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curie_(unit) "Curie (unit)") (140[MBq](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Becquerel "Becquerel"), 4.1 μg of caesium-137) per kilogram is lethal within three weeks;[[124]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-125) smaller amounts may cause infertility and cancer.[[125]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-126) The [International Atomic Energy Agency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Atomic_Energy_Agency "International Atomic Energy Agency") and other sources have warned that radioactive materials, such as caesium-137, could be used in radiological dispersion devices, or "[dirty bombs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dirty_bomb "Dirty bomb")".[[126]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium#cite_note-127)
