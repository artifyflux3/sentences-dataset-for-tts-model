From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

**Digital geometry** deals with [discrete](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discrete_space "Discrete space") sets (usually discrete [point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_(geometry) "Point (geometry)") sets) considered to be [digitized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digitizing "Digitizing")[models](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scale_model "Scale model") or [images](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Image "Image") of objects of the 2D or 3D [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space"). Simply put, _digitizing_ is replacing an object by a discrete set of its points. The images we see on the TV screen, the [raster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raster_graphics "Raster graphics") display of a computer, or in newspapers are in fact [digital](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digital_data "Digital data") images.

Its main application areas are [computer graphics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_graphics "Computer graphics") and [image analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Image_analysis "Image analysis").

Main aspects of study are:

*   Constructing digitized representations of objects, with the emphasis on precision and efficiency (either by means of synthesis, see, for example, [Bresenham's line algorithm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bresenham%27s_line_algorithm "Bresenham's line algorithm") or digital disks, or by means of digitization and subsequent processing of digital images).
*   Study of properties of digital sets; see, for example, [Pick's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pick%27s_theorem "Pick's theorem"), digital convexity, [digital straightness](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Digital_straightness&action=edit&redlink=1 "Digital straightness (page does not exist)"), or digital planarity.
*   Transforming digitized representations of objects, for example (A) into simplified shapes such as (i) skeletons, by repeated removal of simple points such that the [digital topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digital_topology "Digital topology") of an image does not change, or (ii) medial axis, by calculating local maxima in a distance transform of the given digitized object representation, or (B) into modified shapes using [mathematical morphology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_morphology "Mathematical morphology").
*   Reconstructing "real" objects or their properties (area, length, curvature, volume, surface area, and so forth) from digital images.
*   Study of digital curves, digital surfaces, and [digital manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digital_manifold "Digital manifold").
*   Designing tracking algorithms for digital objects.
*   Functions on digital space.
*   Curve sketching, a method of drawing a curve pixel by pixel.

Tracing a curve on a triangular mesh

Digital geometry heavily overlaps with [discrete geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discrete_geometry "Discrete geometry") and may be considered as a part thereof.

A 2D digital space usually means a 2D grid space that only contains integer points in 2D Euclidean space. A 2D image is a function on a 2D digital space (See [image processing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Image_processing "Image processing")).

In Rosenfeld and Kak's book, digital connectivity are defined as the relationship among elements in digital space. For example, 4-connectivity and 8-connectivity in 2D. Also see [pixel connectivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pixel_connectivity "Pixel connectivity"). A digital space and its (digital-)connectivity determine a [digital topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digital_topology "Digital topology").

In digital space, the digitally continuous function (A. Rosenfeld, 1986) and the [gradually varied function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gradually_varied_surface "Gradually varied surface") (L. Chen, 1989) were proposed, independently.

A digitally continuous function means a function in which the value (an integer) at a digital point is the same or off by at most 1 from its neighbors. In other words, if _x_ and _y_ are two adjacent points in a digital space, |_f_(_x_)âˆ’_f_(_y_)|â‰¤1.
