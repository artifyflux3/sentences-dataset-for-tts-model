**Acetylene** ([systematic name](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_nomenclature "Chemical nomenclature"): **ethyne**) is a [chemical compound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_compound "Chemical compound") with the formula C 2 H 2 and structure HC≡CH. It is a [hydrocarbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrocarbon "Hydrocarbon") and the simplest [alkyne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkyne "Alkyne").[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-8) This colorless gas is widely used as a fuel and a chemical building block. It is unstable in its pure form and thus is usually handled as a solution.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-Ullmann-9) Pure acetylene is odorless, but commercial grades usually have a marked odor due to impurities such as [divinyl sulfide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divinyl_sulfide "Divinyl sulfide") and [phosphine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine "Phosphine").[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-Ullmann-9)[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-msds-10)

As an alkyne, acetylene is [unsaturated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saturated_and_unsaturated_compounds "Saturated and unsaturated compounds") because its two carbon atoms are [bonded](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_bond "Chemical bond") together in a [triple bond](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triple_bond "Triple bond"). The carbon–carbon triple bond places all four atoms in the same straight line, with CCH bond angles of 180°.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-11) The triple bond in acetylene results in a high energy content that is released when acetylene is burned.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-Myers-12)

Acetylene was discovered in 1836 by [Edmund Davy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edmund_Davy "Edmund Davy"), who identified it as a "new carburet of hydrogen".[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-13)[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-14) It was an accidental discovery while attempting to isolate [potassium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium "Potassium") metal. By heating [potassium carbonate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_carbonate "Potassium carbonate") with carbon at very high temperatures, he produced a residue of what is now known as [potassium carbide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_carbide "Potassium carbide"), (K 2 C 2), which reacted with water to release the new gas.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-Myers-12) It was rediscovered in 1860 by French chemist [Marcellin Berthelot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marcellin_Berthelot "Marcellin Berthelot"), who coined the name _acétylène_.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-15) Berthelot's empirical formula for acetylene (C 4 H 2), as well as the alternative name "_quadricarbure d'hydrogène_" (hydrogen quadricarbide), were incorrect because many chemists at that time used the wrong atomic mass for carbon (6 instead of 12).[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-16) Berthelot was able to prepare this gas by passing vapours of organic compounds (methanol, ethanol, etc.) through a red hot tube and collecting the [effluent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Effluent "Effluent"). He also found that acetylene was formed by sparking electricity through mixed [cyanogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyanogen "Cyanogen") and [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen") gases. Berthelot later obtained acetylene directly by passing hydrogen between the poles of a [carbon arc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arc_lamp "Arc lamp").[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-17)[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-18)

### Partial combustion of hydrocarbons

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Acetylene&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Partial combustion of hydrocarbons")]

Since the 1950s, acetylene has mainly been manufactured by the partial combustion of [methane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane "Methane") in the US, much of the EU, and many other countries:[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-Ullmann-9)[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-19)[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-20)

3 CH 4 + 3 O 2 → C 2 H 2 + CO + 5 H 2 O
It is a recovered side product in production of [ethylene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene "Ethylene") by [cracking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cracking_(chemistry) "Cracking (chemistry)") of [hydrocarbons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrocarbon "Hydrocarbon"). Approximately 400,000 tonnes were produced by this method in 1983.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-Ullmann-9) Its presence in ethylene is usually undesirable because of its explosive character and its ability to poison [Ziegler–Natta catalysts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ziegler%E2%80%93Natta_catalyst "Ziegler–Natta catalyst"). It is selectively hydrogenated into ethylene, usually using [Pd](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palladium "Palladium")–[Ag](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silver "Silver") catalysts.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-21)

### Dehydrogenation of alkanes

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Acetylene&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Dehydrogenation of alkanes")]

The heaviest alkanes in petroleum and natural gas are cracked into lighter molecules which are dehydrogenated at high temperature:

C 2 H 6 → C 2 H 2 + 2 H 2 2 CH 4 → C 2 H 2 + 3 H 2
This last reaction is implemented in the process of anaerobic decomposition of methane by microwave plasma.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-22)

### Carbochemical method

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Acetylene&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Carbochemical method")]

The first acetylene produced was by Edmund Davy in 1836, via potassium carbide.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-23) Acetylene was historically produced by hydrolysis (reaction with water) of calcium carbide:[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-Myers-12)

CaC 2 + 2 H 2 O → Ca(OH)2 + C 2 H 2
This reaction was discovered by [Friedrich Wöhler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_W%C3%B6hler "Friedrich Wöhler") in 1862,[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-24) but a suitable commercial scale production method which allowed acetylene to be put into wider scale use was not found until 1892 by the Canadian inventor [Thomas Willson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Willson "Thomas Willson") while searching for a viable commercial production method for aluminum.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-Willson-25)

As late as the early 21st century, China, Japan, and Eastern Europe produced acetylene primarily by this method.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-26)

The use of this technology has since declined worldwide with the notable exception of China, with its emphasis on coal-based chemical industry, as of 2013. Otherwise [oil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petroleum "Petroleum") has increasingly supplanted [coal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coal "Coal") as the chief source of [reduced](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redox "Redox") carbon.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-27)

Calcium carbide production requires high temperatures, ~2000°C, necessitating the use of an [electric arc furnace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_arc_furnace "Electric arc furnace"). In the US, this process was an important part of the late-19th century revolution in chemistry enabled by the massive [hydroelectric power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroelectricity "Hydroelectricity") project at [Niagara Falls](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Niagara_Falls "Niagara Falls").[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-28)

[![Image 21](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Structure_of_acetylene_with_bond_lengths_and_angles_labeled.svg/100px-Structure_of_acetylene_with_bond_lengths_and_angles_labeled.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Structure_of_acetylene_with_bond_lengths_and_angles_labeled.svg)

Bond lengths and angles in acetylene

In terms of [valence bond theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Valence_bond_theory "Valence bond theory"), in each carbon atom the 2s [orbital](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_orbital "Atomic orbital")[hybridizes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_hybridisation "Orbital hybridisation") with one 2p orbital thus forming an sp hybrid. The other two 2p orbitals remain unhybridized. The two ends of the two sp hybrid [orbital overlap](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_overlap "Orbital overlap") to form a strong [σ valence bond](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sigma_bond "Sigma bond") between the carbons, while on each of the other two ends hydrogen atoms attach also by σ bonds. The two unchanged 2p orbitals form a pair of weaker [π bonds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi_bond "Pi bond").[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-29)

Since acetylene is a linear [symmetrical molecule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_symmetry "Molecular symmetry"), it possesses the D∞h[point group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_symmetry#Point_group "Molecular symmetry").[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-30)

Physical properties
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Acetylene&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Physical properties")]

At atmospheric pressure, acetylene cannot exist as a liquid and does not have a melting point. The [triple point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triple_point "Triple point") on the [phase diagram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phase_diagram "Phase diagram") corresponds to the melting point (−80.8°C) at the minimal pressure at which liquid acetylene can exist (1.27 atm). At temperatures below the triple point, solid acetylene can change directly to the [vapour](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vapour "Vapour") (gas) by [sublimation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sublimation_(phase_transition) "Sublimation (phase transition)"). The sublimation point at atmospheric pressure is −84.0°C.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-31)

At room temperature and atmospheric pressure, the solubility of acetylene in [acetone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone "Acetone") is 27.9 g per kg. For the same amount of [dimethylformamide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimethylformamide "Dimethylformamide") (DMF), the solubility is 51 g. At 20.26 bar, the solubility increases to 689.0 and 628.0 g for acetone and DMF, respectively. These solvents are used in pressurized gas cylinders.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-Ull-32)

Approximately 20% of acetylene is supplied by the [industrial gases industry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Industrial_gas "Industrial gas") for [oxyacetylene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxyacetylene "Oxyacetylene")[gas welding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas_welding "Gas welding") and [cutting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxy-fuel_welding_and_cutting "Oxy-fuel welding and cutting") due to the high temperature of the flame. Combustion of acetylene with oxygen produces a flame of over 3,600 K (3,330°C; 6,020°F), releasing 11.8[kJ](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kilojoule "Kilojoule")/g. Oxygen with acetylene is the hottest burning common gas mixture.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-Linde2013-33) Acetylene is the third-hottest natural chemical flame after [dicyanoacetylene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dicyanoacetylene "Dicyanoacetylene")'s 5,260 K (4,990°C; 9,010°F) and [cyanogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyanogen "Cyanogen") at 4,798 K (4,525°C; 8,177°F). [Oxy-acetylene welding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxy-fuel_welding_and_cutting "Oxy-fuel welding and cutting") was a popular welding process in previous decades. The development and advantages of [arc-based welding processes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arc_welding "Arc welding") have made oxy-fuel welding nearly extinct for many applications. Acetylene usage for welding has dropped significantly. On the other hand, oxy-acetylene welding _equipment_ is quite versatile – not only because the torch is preferred for some sorts of iron or steel welding (as in certain artistic applications), but also because it lends itself easily to brazing, braze-welding, metal heating (for annealing or tempering, bending or forming), the loosening of corroded nuts and bolts, and other applications. [Bell Canada](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bell_Canada "Bell Canada") cable-repair technicians still use portable acetylene-fuelled torch kits as a [soldering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soldering "Soldering") tool for sealing lead sleeve splices in [manholes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manhole "Manhole") and in some aerial locations. Oxyacetylene welding may also be used in areas where electricity is not readily accessible. Oxyacetylene cutting is used in many metal fabrication shops. For use in welding and cutting, the working pressures must be controlled by a regulator, since above 15 psi (100 kPa), if subjected to a shockwave (caused, for example, by a [flashback](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flashback_(welding) "Flashback (welding)")), acetylene [decomposes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decompose "Decompose") explosively into [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen") and [carbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon "Carbon").[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-34)

[![Image 22](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Laskarbit.jpg/250px-Laskarbit.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Laskarbit.jpg)

Acetylene fuel container/burner as used on the island of [Bali](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bali "Bali")

Acetylene is useful for many processes, but few are conducted on a commercial scale.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-trot-35)

One of the major chemical applications is [ethynylation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethynylation "Ethynylation") of formaldehyde.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-Ullmann-9) Acetylene adds to [aldehydes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aldehyde "Aldehyde") and [ketones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ketone "Ketone") to form α-ethynyl alcohols:

[![Image 23](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Reppe-chemistry-endiol-V1.svg/330px-Reppe-chemistry-endiol-V1.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Reppe-chemistry-endiol-V1.svg)
The reaction gives [butynediol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1,4-Butynediol "1,4-Butynediol"), with [propargyl alcohol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propargyl_alcohol "Propargyl alcohol") as the by-product. [Copper acetylide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Copper(I)_acetylide "Copper(I) acetylide") is used as the catalyst.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-36)[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-37)

In addition to ethynylation, acetylene reacts with [carbon monoxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide "Carbon monoxide") to give [acrylic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acrylic_acid "Acrylic acid"), or acrylic esters. Metal catalysts are required. These derivatives form products such as [acrylic fibers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acrylic_fiber "Acrylic fiber"), [glasses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acrylic_glass "Acrylic glass"), [paints](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acrylic_paint "Acrylic paint"), [resins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acrylic_resin "Acrylic resin"), and [polymers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acrylate_polymer "Acrylate polymer"). Except in China, use of acetylene as a chemical feedstock has declined by 70% from 1965 to 2007 owing to cost and environmental considerations.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-38) In China, acetylene is a major precursor to [vinyl chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vinyl_chloride "Vinyl chloride").[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-trot-35)

Prior to the widespread use of petrochemicals, coal-derived acetylene was a building block for several industrial chemicals. Thus acetylene can be hydrated to give [acetaldehyde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde "Acetaldehyde"), which in turn can be oxidized to acetic acid. Processes leading to acrylates were also commercialized. Almost all of these processes became obsolete with the availability of petroleum-derived ethylene and propylene.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-39)

In 1881, the Russian chemist Mikhail Kucherov[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-40) described the [hydration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydration_reaction "Hydration reaction") of acetylene to [acetaldehyde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde "Acetaldehyde") using catalysts such as [mercury(II) bromide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mercury(II)_bromide "Mercury(II) bromide"). Before the advent of the [Wacker process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wacker_process "Wacker process"), this reaction was conducted on an industrial scale.[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-41)

The [polymerization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polymerization "Polymerization") of acetylene with [Ziegler–Natta catalysts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ziegler%E2%80%93Natta_catalyst "Ziegler–Natta catalyst") produces [polyacetylene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyacetylene "Polyacetylene") films. Polyacetylene, a chain of CH centres with alternating single and double bonds, was one of the first discovered [organic semiconductors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_semiconductor "Organic semiconductor"). Its reaction with [iodine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iodine "Iodine") produces a highly electrically conducting material. Although such materials are not useful, these discoveries led to the developments of [organic semiconductors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_semiconductor "Organic semiconductor"), as recognized by the [Nobel Prize in Chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Chemistry "Nobel Prize in Chemistry") in 2000 to [Alan J. Heeger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alan_J._Heeger "Alan J. Heeger"), [Alan G MacDiarmid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alan_G_MacDiarmid "Alan G MacDiarmid"), and [Hideki Shirakawa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hideki_Shirakawa "Hideki Shirakawa").[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-Ullmann-9)

In the 1920s, pure acetylene was experimentally used as an [inhalation anesthetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inhalation_anesthetic "Inhalation anesthetic").[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-42)

Acetylene is sometimes used for [carburization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carburization "Carburization") (that is, hardening) of steel when the object is too large to fit into a furnace.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-BOC2006-43)

Acetylene is used to volatilize carbon in [radiocarbon dating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiocarbon_dating "Radiocarbon dating"). The carbonaceous material in an archeological sample is treated with [lithium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lithium "Lithium") metal in a small specialized research furnace to form [lithium carbide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lithium_carbide "Lithium carbide") (also known as lithium acetylide). The carbide can then be reacted with water, as usual, to form acetylene gas to feed into a [mass spectrometer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass_spectrometer "Mass spectrometer") to measure the isotopic ratio of carbon-14 to carbon-12.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-44)

Acetylene combustion produces a strong, bright light and the ubiquity of [carbide lamps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbide_lamp "Carbide lamp") drove much acetylene commercialization in the early 20th century. Common applications included coastal [lighthouses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lighthouse "Lighthouse"),[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-45)[street lights](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Street_light "Street light"), [[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-Myers-12) and [automobile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Headlamp "Headlamp")[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-46) and [mining](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Miner%27s_helmet "Miner's helmet")[headlamps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Headlamp_(outdoor) "Headlamp (outdoor)").[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-Thorpe-2005-47) In most of these applications, direct combustion is a [fire hazard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fire_hazard "Fire hazard"), and so acetylene has been replaced, first by [incandescent lighting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Incandescent_light_bulb "Incandescent light bulb") and many years later by low-power/high-lumen LEDs. Nevertheless, acetylene lamps remain in limited use in remote or otherwise inaccessible areas and in countries with a weak or unreliable central [electric grid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrical_grid "Electrical grid").[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-Thorpe-2005-47)

The energy richness of the C≡C triple bond and the rather high solubility of acetylene in water make it a suitable substrate for bacteria, provided an adequate source is available.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-48) A number of bacteria living on acetylene have been identified. The [enzyme](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enzyme "Enzyme")[acetylene hydratase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene_hydratase "Acetylene hydratase") catalyzes the hydration of acetylene to give [acetaldehyde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde "Acetaldehyde"):[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-49)

C 2 H 2 + H 2 O → CH 3 CHO
Acetylene is a moderately common chemical in the universe, often associated with the atmospheres of [gas giants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas_giant "Gas giant").[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-50) One curious discovery of acetylene is on [Enceladus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enceladus "Enceladus"), a moon of [Saturn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saturn "Saturn"). Natural acetylene is believed to form from [catalytic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catalysis "Catalysis") decomposition of long-chain hydrocarbons at temperatures of 1,700 K (1,430°C; 2,600°F) and above. Since such temperatures are highly unlikely on such a small distant body, this discovery is potentially suggestive of catalytic reactions within that moon, making it a promising site to search for prebiotic chemistry.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-51)[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-52)

### Vinylation reactions

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Acetylene&action=edit&section=17 "Edit section: Vinylation reactions")]

In [vinylation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vinylation "Vinylation") reactions, H−X compounds add across the triple bond. [Alcohols](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alcohol_(chemistry) "Alcohol (chemistry)") and [phenols](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phenols "Phenols") add to acetylene to give [vinyl ethers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enol_ether "Enol ether"). [Thiols](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thiol "Thiol") give vinyl thioethers. Similarly, [vinylpyrrolidone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vinylpyrrolidone "Vinylpyrrolidone") and [vinylcarbazole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vinylcarbazole "Vinylcarbazole") are produced industrially by vinylation of [2-pyrrolidone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2-Pyrrolidone "2-Pyrrolidone") and [carbazole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbazole "Carbazole").[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-Ull-32)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-Ullmann-9)

[![Image 24](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Reppe-chemnistry-vinylization.png/330px-Reppe-chemnistry-vinylization.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Reppe-chemnistry-vinylization.png)
The hydration of acetylene is a vinylation reaction, but the resulting vinyl alcohol isomerizes to [acetaldehyde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde "Acetaldehyde"). The reaction is catalyzed by [mercury](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mercury_(element) "Mercury (element)") salts. This reaction once was the dominant technology for acetaldehyde production, but it has been displaced by the [Wacker process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wacker_process "Wacker process"), which affords acetaldehyde by oxidation of [ethylene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene "Ethylene"), a cheaper feedstock. A similar situation applies to the conversion of acetylene to the valuable [vinyl chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vinyl_chloride "Vinyl chloride") by [hydrochlorination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrochlorination "Hydrochlorination") versus the [oxychlorination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxychlorination "Oxychlorination") of ethylene.

[Vinyl acetate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vinyl_acetate "Vinyl acetate") is used instead of acetylene for some vinylations, which are more accurately described as [transvinylations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transvinylation "Transvinylation").[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-53) Higher esters of vinyl acetate have been used in the synthesis of [vinyl formate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vinyl_formate "Vinyl formate").

### Organometallic chemistry

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Acetylene&action=edit&section=18 "Edit section: Organometallic chemistry")]

Acetylene and its derivatives (2-butyne, diphenylacetylene, etc.) form [complexes with transition metals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transition_metal_alkyne_complex "Transition metal alkyne complex"). Its bonding to the metal is somewhat similar to that of ethylene complexes. These complexes are intermediates in many catalytic reactions such as [alkyne trimerisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkyne_trimerisation "Alkyne trimerisation") to benzene, tetramerization to [cyclooctatetraene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclooctatetraene "Cyclooctatetraene"),[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-Ullmann-9) and carbonylation to [hydroquinone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroquinone "Hydroquinone"):[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-Cyclization-54)

[![Image 25](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Reppe-chemistry-benzene.png/250px-Reppe-chemistry-benzene.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Reppe-chemistry-benzene.png)[![Image 26](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Reppe-chemistry-cyclooctatetraene.png/250px-Reppe-chemistry-cyclooctatetraene.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Reppe-chemistry-cyclooctatetraene.png)Fe(CO)5 + 4 C 2 H 2 + 2 H 2 O → 2 C 6 H 4(OH)2 + FeCO 3 at basic conditions (50–80°C, 20–25 atm).
Metal [acetylides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylide "Acetylide"), species of the formula L _n_ M−C 2 R, are also common. [Copper(I) acetylide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Copper(I)_acetylide "Copper(I) acetylide") and [silver acetylide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silver_acetylide "Silver acetylide") can be formed in [aqueous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aqueous "Aqueous") solutions with ease due to a favorable [solubility equilibrium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solubility_equilibrium "Solubility equilibrium").[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-Viehe-55)

### Acid-base reactions

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Acetylene&action=edit&section=19 "Edit section: Acid-base reactions")]

Acetylene has a [p _K_ a](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acid_dissociation_constant "Acid dissociation constant") of 25. Acetylene can be [deprotonated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deprotonation "Deprotonation") by a [superbase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superbase "Superbase") to form an [acetylide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylide "Acetylide"):[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-Viehe-55)

HC≡CH + RM → RH + HC≡CM
Various [organometallic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organometallic "Organometallic")[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-Midland1990-56) and [inorganic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inorganic_compound "Inorganic compound")[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-Coffman-57) reagents are effective.

[![Image 27](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/BASF_Nsw.jpg/250px-BASF_Nsw.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:BASF_Nsw.jpg)

The _new acetylene plant_ of [BASF](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BASF "BASF"), commissioned in 2020

Acetylene can be [semihydrogenated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semihydrogenation "Semihydrogenation") to [ethylene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene "Ethylene"), providing a feedstock for a variety of [polyethylene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyethylene "Polyethylene") plastics. Halogens add to the triple bond.

Safety and handling
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Acetylene&action=edit&section=21 "Edit section: Safety and handling")]

Acetylene is not especially toxic, but when generated from [calcium carbide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calcium_carbide "Calcium carbide"), or CaC 2, it can contain toxic impurities such as traces of [phosphine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine "Phosphine") and [arsine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arsine "Arsine"), which gives it a distinct [garlic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Garlic "Garlic")-like smell. It is also highly flammable, as are most light hydrocarbons, hence its use in welding. Its most singular hazard is associated with its intrinsic instability, especially when it is pressurized: under certain conditions acetylene can react in an [exothermic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exothermic "Exothermic") addition-type reaction to form a number of products, typically [benzene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene "Benzene") and/or [vinylacetylene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vinylacetylene "Vinylacetylene"), possibly in addition to [carbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon "Carbon") and [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen").[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] Although it is stable at normal pressures and temperatures, if it is subjected to pressures as low as 15 psig it can explode.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-Myers-12) The safe limit for acetylene therefore is 101 kPa gage, or 15 psig.[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-58)[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-law-59) Additionally, if acetylene is initiated by intense heat or a shockwave, it can decompose explosively if the absolute pressure of the gas exceeds about 200 kilopascals (29 psi). It is therefore supplied and stored dissolved in [acetone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone "Acetone") or [dimethylformamide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimethylformamide "Dimethylformamide") (DMF),[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-law-59)[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-Industrial_Gases-60)[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-61) contained in a [gas cylinder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas_cylinder "Gas cylinder") with [a porous filling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agamassan "Agamassan"), which renders it safe to transport and use, given proper handling. Acetylene cylinders should be used in the upright position to avoid withdrawing acetone during use.[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-EIGA-62)

Information on safe storage of acetylene in upright cylinders is provided by the OSHA,[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-63)[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-OSHA-64) Compressed Gas Association,[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-law-59) United States Mine Safety and Health Administration (MSHA),[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-65) EIGA,[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-EIGA-62) and other agencies.

[Copper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Copper "Copper") catalyses the decomposition of acetylene, and as a result acetylene should not be transported in copper pipes.[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-brown-66)

Cylinders should be stored in an area segregated from oxidizers to avoid exacerbated reaction in case of fire/leakage.[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-law-59)[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-OSHA-64) Acetylene cylinders should not be stored in confined spaces, enclosed vehicles, garages, and buildings, to avoid unintended leakage leading to explosive atmosphere.[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-law-59)[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-OSHA-64) In the US, National Electric Code (NEC) requires consideration for hazardous areas including those where acetylene may be released during accidents or leaks.[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-NFPA-67) Consideration may include electrical classification and use of listed Group A electrical components in US.[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-NFPA-67) Further information on determining the areas requiring special consideration is in NFPA 497.[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-68) In Europe, ATEX also requires consideration for hazardous areas where flammable gases may be released during accidents or leaks.[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene#cite_note-EIGA-62)
