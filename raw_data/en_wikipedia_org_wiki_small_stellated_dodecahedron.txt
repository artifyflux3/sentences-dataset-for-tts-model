3D model of a small stellated dodecahedron

In [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry"), the **small stellated dodecahedron** is a [Kepler–Poinsot polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler%E2%80%93Poinsot_polyhedron "Kepler–Poinsot polyhedron"), named by [Arthur Cayley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arthur_Cayley "Arthur Cayley"), and with [Schläfli symbol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schl%C3%A4fli_symbol "Schläfli symbol") {⁠5/2⁠,5}. It is one of four [nonconvex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nonconvex "Nonconvex")[regular polyhedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_regular_polytopes#Non-convex_2 "List of regular polytopes"). It is composed of 12 [pentagrammic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentagram "Pentagram") faces, with five pentagrams meeting at each vertex.

It shares the same [vertex arrangement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_arrangement "Vertex arrangement") as the convex regular [icosahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Icosahedron "Icosahedron"). It also shares the same [edge arrangement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edge_arrangement "Edge arrangement") with the [great icosahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_icosahedron "Great icosahedron"), with which it forms [a degenerate uniform compound figure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_complex_icosidodecahedron "Great complex icosidodecahedron").

It is the [second of four stellations of the dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_Wenninger_polyhedron_models#Stellations_of_dodecahedron "List of Wenninger polyhedron models") (including the original dodecahedron itself).

The small stellated dodecahedron can be constructed analogously to the pentagram, its two-dimensional analogue, via the extension of the edges (1-faces) of the core polytope until a point is reached where they intersect.

Construction and properties
---------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Small_stellated_dodecahedron&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Construction and properties")]

The small stellated dodecahedron is constructed by attaching twelve [pentagonal pyramids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentagonal_pyramid "Pentagonal pyramid") onto a [regular dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron "Regular dodecahedron")'s faces.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Small_stellated_dodecahedron#cite_note-kappraff-1) Suppose the [pentagrammic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentagram "Pentagram") faces are considered as five triangular faces. In that case, it shares the same surface topology as the [pentakis dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentakis_dodecahedron "Pentakis dodecahedron"), but with much taller [isosceles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isosceles "Isosceles") triangle faces, with the height of the pentagonal pyramids adjusted so that the five triangles in the pentagram become coplanar. The critical angle is atan(2) above the dodecahedron face.

Regarding the small stellated dodecahedron has 12 pentagrams as faces, with these pentagrams meeting at 30 edges and 12 vertices, one can compute its [genus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genus_(mathematics) "Genus (mathematics)") using [Euler's formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler_characteristic "Euler characteristic")![Image 4: {\displaystyle V-E+F=2-2g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9152905ba746cc6ed498254285f4ec3b242870cc) and conclude that the small stellated dodecahedron has genus 4.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Small_stellated_dodecahedron#cite_note-2) This observation, made by [Louis Poinsot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis_Poinsot "Louis Poinsot"), was initially confusing, but [Felix Klein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Felix_Klein "Felix Klein") showed in 1877 that the small stellated dodecahedron could be seen as a [branched covering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Branched_covering "Branched covering") of the [Riemann sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_sphere "Riemann sphere") by a [Riemann surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_surface "Riemann surface") of genus 4, with [branch points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Branch_points "Branch points") at the center of each pentagram. This Riemann surface, called [Bring's curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bring%27s_curve "Bring's curve"), has the greatest number of symmetries of any Riemann surface of genus 4: the [symmetric group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetric_group "Symmetric group")![Image 5: {\displaystyle S_{5}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7d113353a42f71fc5e7154ddef2257079ab2e25a) acts as automorphisms.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Small_stellated_dodecahedron#cite_note-3)

The [dual polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_polyhedron "Dual polyhedron") of a small stellated dodecahedron is the [great dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_dodecahedron "Great dodecahedron") which shares the same number of vertices, edges, and faces.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Small_stellated_dodecahedron#cite_note-4)

Its convex hull is the regular convex [icosahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Icosahedron "Icosahedron"). It also shares its edges with the [great icosahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_icosahedron "Great icosahedron"); the compound with both is the [great complex icosidodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_complex_icosidodecahedron "Great complex icosidodecahedron").

There are four related uniform polyhedra, constructed as degrees of truncation. The dual is a [great dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_dodecahedron "Great dodecahedron"). The [dodecadodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dodecadodecahedron "Dodecadodecahedron") is a rectification, where edges are truncated down to points.

The _[truncated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Truncation\_(geometry) "Truncation (geometry)") small stellated dodecahedron_ can be considered a [degenerate uniform polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degeneracy_(mathematics) "Degeneracy (mathematics)") since edges and vertices coincide, but it is included for completeness. Visually, it looks like a [regular dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dodecahedron "Dodecahedron") on the surface, but it has 24 faces in overlapping pairs. The spikes are truncated until they reach the plane of the pentagram beneath them. The 24 faces are 12 [pentagons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentagons "Pentagons") from the truncated vertices and 12 decagons taking the form of doubly-wound pentagons overlapping the first 12 pentagons. The latter faces are formed by truncating the original pentagrams. When an {⁠_n_/_d_⁠}-gon is truncated, it becomes a {⁠2 _n_/_d_⁠}-gon. For example, a truncated pentagon {⁠5/1⁠} becomes a decagon {⁠10/1⁠}, so truncating a pentagram {⁠5/2⁠} becomes a doubly-wound pentagon {⁠10/2⁠} (the common factor between 10 and 2 mean we visit each vertex twice to complete the polygon).
