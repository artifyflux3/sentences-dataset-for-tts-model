In [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics"), **angular velocity** (symbol **ω** or ![Image 3: {\displaystyle {\vec {\omega }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f4e066a68ceb355e3314fb2b97f1c0c421ca6074), the lowercase Greek letter [omega](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Omega "Omega")), also known as the **angular frequency vector**,[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_velocity#cite_note-UP1-1) is a [pseudovector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudovector "Pseudovector") representation of how the [angular position](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_position "Angular position") or [orientation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orientation_(geometry) "Orientation (geometry)") of an object changes with time, i.e. how quickly an object [rotates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotate "Rotate") (spins or revolves) around an axis of rotation and how fast the axis itself changes [direction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direction_(geometry) "Direction (geometry)").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_velocity#cite_note-2)

The magnitude of the pseudovector, ![Image 4: {\displaystyle \omega =\|{\boldsymbol {\omega }}\|}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/67af9f7e99fa06db934d8f7bbfb299f4dcf09c5d), represents the _[angular speed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular\_speed "Angular speed")_ (or _angular frequency_), the angular rate at which the object rotates (spins or revolves). The pseudovector direction ![Image 5: {\displaystyle {\hat {\boldsymbol {\omega }}}={\boldsymbol {\omega }}/\omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35cfe6376e2785b9409c9765b49877e4ce199346) is [normal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_(geometry) "Normal (geometry)") to the instantaneous [plane of rotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_of_rotation "Plane of rotation") or [angular displacement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_displacement "Angular displacement").

There are two types of angular velocity:

*   **Orbital angular velocity** refers to how fast a point object [revolves about a fixed origin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation_around_a_fixed_axis "Rotation around a fixed axis"), i.e. the time rate of change of its angular position relative to the [origin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Origin_(mathematics) "Origin (mathematics)"). [_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]
*   **Spin angular velocity** refers to how fast a rigid body rotates around a fixed axis of rotation, and is independent of the choice of origin, in contrast to orbital angular velocity.

Angular velocity has [dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension_(physics) "Dimension (physics)") of angle per unit time; this is analogous to linear [velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity "Velocity"), with angle replacing [distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distance "Distance"), with time in common. The [SI unit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SI_unit "SI unit") of angular velocity is [radians per second](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radians_per_second "Radians per second"),[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_velocity#cite_note-3) although [degrees per second](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degrees_per_second "Degrees per second") (°/s) is also common. The [radian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radian "Radian") is a [dimensionless quantity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimensionless_quantity "Dimensionless quantity"), thus the SI units of angular velocity are dimensionally equivalent to [reciprocal seconds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reciprocal_seconds "Reciprocal seconds"), s−1, although rad/s is preferable to avoid confusion with **rotation velocity** in units of [hertz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hertz "Hertz") (also equivalent to s−1).[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_velocity#cite_note-4)

The sense of angular velocity is conventionally specified by the [right-hand rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right-hand_rule "Right-hand rule"), implying [clockwise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clockwise "Clockwise") rotations (as viewed on the plane of rotation); [negation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Negation_(arithmetic) "Negation (arithmetic)") (multiplication by −1) leaves the magnitude unchanged but flips the axis in the [opposite direction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Opposite_direction_(geometry) "Opposite direction (geometry)").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_velocity#cite_note-EM1-5)

For example, a [geostationary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit "Geosynchronous orbit") satellite completes one orbit per day above the [equator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equator "Equator") (360 degrees per 24 hours)[a](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_velocity#endnote_sidereal) has angular velocity magnitude (angular speed) _ω_ = 360°/24 h = 15°/h (or 2π rad/24 h ≈ 0.26 rad/h) and angular velocity direction (a [unit vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_vector "Unit vector")) parallel to [Earth's rotation axis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_rotation_axis "Earth's rotation axis") (![Image 6: {\displaystyle {\hat {\omega }}={\hat {Z}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1e0c5f4da9accf14ab83e123609796d0f2ab2eec), in the [geocentric coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geocentric_coordinate_system "Geocentric coordinate system")). If angle is measured in radians, the linear velocity is the radius times the angular velocity, ![Image 7: {\displaystyle v=r\omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/23b5b5c066f5be8dee84c4cb33dea2383de8b012). With orbital radius 42,000 km from the Earth's center, the satellite's [tangential speed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangential_speed "Tangential speed") through space is thus _v_ = 42,000 km × 0.26/h ≈ 11,000 km/h. The angular velocity is positive since the satellite travels [prograde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Retrograde_and_prograde_motion "Retrograde and prograde motion") with the Earth's rotation (the same direction as the rotation of Earth).

**[^a](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_velocity#ref_sidereal)** Geosynchronous satellites actually orbit based on a sidereal day which is 23h 56m 04s, but 24h is assumed in this example for simplicity.

Orbital angular velocity of a point particle
--------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Angular_velocity&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Orbital angular velocity of a point particle")]

### Particle in two dimensions

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Angular_velocity&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Particle in two dimensions")]

[![Image 8](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Angular_velocity1.svg/330px-Angular_velocity1.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Angular_velocity1.svg)

The angular velocity of the particle at _P_ with respect to the origin _O_ is determined by the [perpendicular component](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular_component "Perpendicular component") of the velocity vector **v**.

In the simplest case of circular motion at radius ![Image 9: {\displaystyle r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d1ecb613aa2984f0576f70f86650b7c2a132538), with position given by the angular displacement ![Image 10: {\displaystyle \phi (t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/23781b983d21d78467b65e7e32b9e7bc05d625f8) from the x-axis, the orbital angular velocity is the rate of change of angle with respect to time: ![Image 11: {\textstyle \omega ={\frac {d\phi }{dt}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/79ee8c6924c79b015b5f921a272fa208e781d848). If ![Image 12: {\displaystyle \phi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/72b1f30316670aee6270a28334bdf4f5072cdde4) is measured in [radians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radian "Radian"), the arc-length from the positive x-axis around the circle to the particle is ![Image 13: {\displaystyle \ell =r\phi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1c7a0183de3910197375c12f2da661b14cee50b1), and the linear velocity is ![Image 14: {\textstyle v(t)={\frac {d\ell }{dt}}=r\omega (t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/62dae89bf837a43f993e118bb7174111e9f70785), so that ![Image 15: {\textstyle \omega ={\frac {v}{r}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eee739b6740669a50e767dcce1efcddea2bf549d).

In the general case of a particle moving in the plane, the orbital angular velocity is the rate at which the position vector relative to a chosen origin "sweeps out" angle. The diagram shows the position vector ![Image 16: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eca0f46511c4c986c48b254073732c0bd98ae0c1) from the origin ![Image 17: {\displaystyle O}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9d70e1d0d87e2ef1092ea1ffe2923d9933ff18fc) to a particle ![Image 18: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a), with its [polar coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_coordinates "Polar coordinates")![Image 19: {\displaystyle (r,\phi )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/389994f381dc6fcbbf41cef5bb3f694b9bfef296). (All variables are functions of time ![Image 20: {\displaystyle t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/65658b7b223af9e1acc877d848888ecdb4466560).) The particle has linear velocity splitting as ![Image 21: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} =\mathbf {v} _{\|}+\mathbf {v} _{\perp }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/efcf4de42cdfa8f917d3175ad2e8037ea8106aa8), with the radial component ![Image 22: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} _{\|}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/13affcf189cfe3a5cab69f0926b68a36c49d9530) parallel to the radius, and the cross-radial (or tangential) component ![Image 23: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} _{\perp }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3f52e365b25af2de60f34a144fad912ead6fa563) perpendicular to the radius. When there is no radial component, the particle moves around the origin in a circle; but when there is no cross-radial component, it moves in a straight line from the origin. Since radial motion leaves the angle unchanged, only the cross-radial component of linear velocity contributes to angular velocity.

The angular velocity _ω_ is the rate of change of angular position with respect to time, which can be computed from the cross-radial velocity as:

[![Image 24: {\displaystyle \omega ={\frac {d\phi }{dt}}={\frac {v_{\perp }}{r}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/528e7183358eea2c29304795db03278acc30603f)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:MathWikibase&qid=Q240105)

Here the cross-radial speed ![Image 25: {\displaystyle v_{\perp }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f80a8cf80254aa3ef2640555e94986487d5cba0b) is the signed magnitude of ![Image 26: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} _{\perp }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3f52e365b25af2de60f34a144fad912ead6fa563), positive for counter-clockwise motion, negative for clockwise. Taking polar coordinates for the linear velocity ![Image 27: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35c1866e359fbfd2e0f606c725ba5cc37a5195d6) gives magnitude ![Image 28: {\displaystyle v}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e07b00e7fc0847fbd16391c778d65bc25c452597) (linear speed) and angle ![Image 29: {\displaystyle \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e5ab2664b422d53eb0c7df3b87e1360d75ad9af) relative to the radius vector; in these terms, ![Image 30: {\displaystyle v_{\perp }=v\sin(\theta )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/163f5f644793a0f6eb62286bb838848317b0a8cb), so that

[![Image 31: {\displaystyle \omega ={\frac {v\sin(\theta )}{r}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d86e1b45e12305f9246b28c3af54ab57f63edb69)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:MathWikibase&qid=Q161635)

These formulas may be derived doing ![Image 32: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} =(r\cos(\varphi ),r\sin(\varphi ))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/59620e79aafd3489b6fa09f68556a22aad2ebdfd), being ![Image 33: {\displaystyle r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d1ecb613aa2984f0576f70f86650b7c2a132538) a function of the distance to the origin with respect to time, and ![Image 34: {\displaystyle \varphi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/33ee699558d09cf9d653f6351f9fda0b2f4aaa3e) a function of the angle between the vector and the x axis. Then: ![Image 35: {\displaystyle {\frac {d\mathbf {r} }{dt}}=({\dot {r}}\cos(\varphi )-r{\dot {\varphi }}\sin(\varphi ),{\dot {r}}\sin(\varphi )+r{\dot {\varphi }}\cos(\varphi )),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5a87d9633a8a8de4cacdc867dec9e6086d4305a3) which is equal to: ![Image 36: {\displaystyle {\dot {r}}(\cos(\varphi ),\sin(\varphi ))+r{\dot {\varphi }}(-\sin(\varphi ),\cos(\varphi ))={\dot {r}}{\hat {r}}+r{\dot {\varphi }}{\hat {\varphi }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/acd9fd5f34cf740a853f1a896e3dae5860f09454) (see [Unit vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_vector "Unit vector") in cylindrical coordinates).

Knowing ![Image 37: {\textstyle {\frac {d\mathbf {r} }{dt}}=\mathbf {v} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5093b0cea19eb93a5841fa7a80d0b7b00abd912e), we conclude that the radial component of the velocity is given by ![Image 38: {\displaystyle {\dot {r}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/99b51a39764cf6daa615ef2144ac420ecff01cfc), because ![Image 39: {\displaystyle {\hat {r}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1009619964ce33a4a02aaa7cf82adc0fb0a50f23) is a radial unit vector; and the perpendicular component is given by ![Image 40: {\displaystyle r{\dot {\varphi }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/16732ba43c11b2e31c3e6a7ca15aaa60d3c0ab74) because ![Image 41: {\displaystyle {\hat {\varphi }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c29e64043bc6702ec51a4c08ba1a94dc3b7412e8) is a perpendicular unit vector.

In two dimensions, angular velocity is a number with plus or minus sign indicating orientation, but not pointing in a direction. The sign is conventionally taken to be positive if the radius vector turns counter-clockwise, and negative if clockwise. Angular velocity then may be termed a [pseudoscalar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudoscalar "Pseudoscalar"), a numerical quantity which changes sign under a [parity inversion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parity_(physics) "Parity (physics)"), such as inverting one axis or switching the two axes.

### Particle in three dimensions

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Angular_velocity&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Particle in three dimensions")]

[![Image 42](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Angular_velocity.svg/250px-Angular_velocity.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Angular_velocity.svg)

The orbital angular velocity vector encodes the time rate of change of angular position, as well as the instantaneous plane of angular displacement. In this case (counter-clockwise circular motion) the vector points up.

In [three-dimensional space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_space "Three-dimensional space"), we again have the position vector **r** of a moving particle. Here, orbital angular velocity is a [pseudovector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudovector "Pseudovector") whose magnitude is the rate at which **r** sweeps out angle (in radians per unit of time), and whose direction is perpendicular to the instantaneous plane in which **r** sweeps out angle (i.e. the plane spanned by **r** and **v**). However, as there are _two_ directions perpendicular to any plane, an additional condition is necessary to uniquely specify the direction of the angular velocity; conventionally, the [right-hand rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right-hand_rule "Right-hand rule") is used.

Let the pseudovector ![Image 43: {\displaystyle \mathbf {u} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/261e20fe101de02a771021d9d4466c0ad3e352d7) be the unit vector perpendicular to the plane spanned by **r** and **v**, so that the right-hand rule is satisfied (i.e. the instantaneous direction of angular displacement is counter-clockwise looking from the top of ![Image 44: {\displaystyle \mathbf {u} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/261e20fe101de02a771021d9d4466c0ad3e352d7)). Taking polar coordinates ![Image 45: {\displaystyle (r,\phi )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/389994f381dc6fcbbf41cef5bb3f694b9bfef296) in this plane, as in the two-dimensional case above, one may define the orbital angular velocity vector as:

![Image 46: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {\omega }}=\omega \mathbf {u} ={\frac {d\phi }{dt}}\mathbf {u} ={\frac {v\sin(\theta )}{r}}\mathbf {u} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a4b7382758abdba0858e2386f0fc929abcb68325)
where _θ_ is the angle between **r** and **v**. In terms of the cross product, this is:

![Image 47: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {\omega }}={\frac {\mathbf {r} \times \mathbf {v} }{r^{2}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cb08b50b5ed23de26c5be3fae30a56040cd6bb90)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_velocity#cite_note-6)
From the above equation, one can recover the tangential velocity as:

![Image 48: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} _{\perp }={\boldsymbol {\omega }}\times \mathbf {r} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/727e2660507afd37e8157ee1a81b74cffcc4f2de)

Spin angular velocity of a rigid body or reference frame
--------------------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Angular_velocity&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Spin angular velocity of a rigid body or reference frame")]

Given a rotating frame of three linearly independent unit coordinate vectors, at each instant in time, there always exists a common axis (called the axis of rotation) around which all three vectors rotate with the same angular speed and in the same angular direction (clockwise or counterclockwise). In such a frame, each vector may be considered as a moving particle with constant scalar radius. A collection of such particles is called a rigid body.

[Euler's rotation theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler%27s_rotation_theorem "Euler's rotation theorem") says that in a rotating frame, the axis of rotation one obtains from one choice of three linearly independent unit vectors is the same as that for any other choice; that is, there is one _single_[instantaneous axis of rotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Instantaneous_axis_of_rotation "Instantaneous axis of rotation") to the frame, around which all points rotate at the same angular speed and in the same angular direction (clockwise or counterclockwise). The spin angular velocity of a frame or rigid body is defined to be the pseudovector whose magnitude is this common angular speed, and whose direction is along the common axis of rotation in accordance with the right-hand rule (that is, for counterclockise rotation, it points "upward" along the axis, while for clockwise rotation, it points "downward").

In larger than 3 spatial dimensions, the interpretation of spin angular velocity as a pseudovector is not valid; however, it may be characterized by a more general type of object known as an antisymmetric rank-2 [tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor "Tensor").

The addition of angular velocity vectors for frames is also defined by the usual vector addition (composition of linear movements), and can be useful to decompose the rotation as in a [gimbal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gimbal "Gimbal"). All components of the vector can be calculated as derivatives of the parameters defining the moving frames (Euler angles or rotation matrices). As in the general case, addition is commutative: ![Image 49: {\displaystyle \omega _{1}+\omega _{2}=\omega _{2}+\omega _{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/24202680abd0394e617714943efe183b8c961bb6).

If we choose a reference point ![Image 50: {\displaystyle {{\boldsymbol {r}}_{0}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d09ca5d68634353522d7fb3e8a17c063ad3a3c6b) fixed in a rotating frame, the velocity ![Image 51: {\displaystyle {\dot {\boldsymbol {r}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7417b4d4033f20326c0276fa50739a2cf1bb9338) of any point in the frame is given by

![Image 52: {\displaystyle {\dot {\boldsymbol {r}}}={\dot {{\boldsymbol {r}}_{0}}}+{\boldsymbol {\omega }}\times ({\boldsymbol {r}}-{{\boldsymbol {r}}_{0}})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d0bac8e81380460e5f69fd6b15feeffcf94a6d22)

### Components from the basis vectors of a body-fixed frame

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Angular_velocity&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Components from the basis vectors of a body-fixed frame")]

Consider a rigid body rotating about a fixed point O. Construct a reference frame in the body consisting of an orthonormal set of vectors ![Image 53: {\displaystyle \mathbf {e} _{1},\mathbf {e} _{2},\mathbf {e} _{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6b830763a1f552a9c87e7a97e3fb34924c92f3ba) fixed to the body and with their common origin at O. The spin angular velocity vector of both frame and body about O is then

![Image 54: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {\omega }}=\left({\dot {\mathbf {e} }}_{1}\cdot \mathbf {e} _{2}\right)\mathbf {e} _{3}+\left({\dot {\mathbf {e} }}_{2}\cdot \mathbf {e} _{3}\right)\mathbf {e} _{1}+\left({\dot {\mathbf {e} }}_{3}\cdot \mathbf {e} _{1}\right)\mathbf {e} _{2},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/13364bd5520f9314b0b357acad4e9c0b2df1d43b)
where ![Image 55: {\displaystyle {\dot {\mathbf {e} }}_{i}={\frac {d\mathbf {e} _{i}}{dt}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5370d4e6c24e68caa0f1c97021681b804678d916) is the time rate of change of the frame vector ![Image 56: {\displaystyle \mathbf {e} _{i},i=1,2,3,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e02c393dca3251539e0558bec0fdf5a84c8ce1dc) due to the rotation.

This formula is incompatible with the expression for _orbital_ angular velocity

![Image 57: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {\omega }}={\frac {{\boldsymbol {r}}\times {\boldsymbol {v}}}{r^{2}}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b9242df57593d0e5e0b9bf0089dd63f8da6cee66)
as that formula defines angular velocity for a _single point_ about O, while the formula in this section applies to a frame or rigid body. In the case of a rigid body a _single_![Image 58: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {\omega }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7cb8af7a2f64af348e559652b6b1f0d2415ba444) has to account for the motion of _all_ particles in the body.

### Components from Euler angles

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Angular_velocity&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Components from Euler angles")]

[![Image 59](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Eulerframe.svg/250px-Eulerframe.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Eulerframe.svg)

Diagram showing Euler frame in green

The components of the spin angular velocity pseudovector were first calculated by [Leonhard Euler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leonhard_Euler "Leonhard Euler") using his [Euler angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler_angles "Euler angles") and the use of an intermediate frame:

*   One axis of the reference frame (the precession axis)
*   The line of nodes of the moving frame with respect to the reference frame (nutation axis)
*   One axis of the moving frame (the intrinsic rotation axis)

Euler proved that the projections of the angular velocity pseudovector on each of these three axes is the derivative of its associated angle (which is equivalent to decomposing the instantaneous rotation into three instantaneous [Euler rotations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler_rotations "Euler rotations")). Therefore:[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_velocity#cite_note-7)

This basis is not orthonormal and it is difficult to use, but now the velocity vector can be changed to the fixed frame or to the moving frame with just a change of bases. For example, changing to the mobile frame:
