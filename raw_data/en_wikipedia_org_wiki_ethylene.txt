**Ethylene** ([IUPAC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IUPAC "IUPAC") name: **ethene**) is a [hydrocarbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrocarbon "Hydrocarbon") which has the formula C 2 H 4 or H 2 C=CH 2. It is a colourless, [flammable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flammable "Flammable") gas with a faint "sweet and [musky](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Musk "Musk")" odour when pure.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene#cite_note-UllmannEthylene-7) It is the simplest [alkene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkene "Alkene") (a hydrocarbon with [carbon–carbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon%E2%80%93carbon_bond "Carbon–carbon bond")[double bonds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double_bond "Double bond")).

Ethylene is widely used in the chemical industry, and its worldwide production (over 150 million [tonnes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tonne "Tonne") in 2016[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene#cite_note-8)) exceeds that of any other [organic compound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_compound "Organic compound").[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene#cite_note-cenews-9)[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene#cite_note-Technology_Economics_Program-10) Much of this production goes toward creating [polyethylene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyethylene "Polyethylene"), which is a widely used [plastic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plastic "Plastic") containing [polymer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polymer "Polymer") chains of ethylene units in various chain lengths. Production [emits greenhouse gases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenhouse_gas_emissions "Greenhouse gas emissions"), including [methane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane "Methane") from [feedstock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feedstock "Feedstock") production and [carbon dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_dioxide "Carbon dioxide") from any non-[sustainable energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sustainable_energy "Sustainable energy") used.

Ethylene is also an important natural [plant hormone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant_hormone "Plant hormone") and is used in agriculture to induce [ripening](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ripening "Ripening") of [fruits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fruit "Fruit").[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene#cite_note-Wang_2002-11) The [hydrate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrate "Hydrate") of ethylene is [ethanol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethanol "Ethanol").

Structure and properties
------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ethylene&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Structure and properties")]

[![Image 20](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Dewar-Chatt-Duncanson_model.png/330px-Dewar-Chatt-Duncanson_model.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Dewar-Chatt-Duncanson_model.png)

Orbital description of bonding between ethylene and a transition metal

This [hydrocarbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrocarbon "Hydrocarbon") has four [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen")[atoms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atom "Atom") bound to a pair of [carbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon "Carbon") atoms that are connected by a [double bond](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double_bond "Double bond"). All six atoms that comprise ethylene are [coplanar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coplanar "Coplanar"). The H-C-H [angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle "Angle") is 117.4°, close to the 120° for ideal sp² [hybridized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hybridization_(chemistry) "Hybridization (chemistry)") carbon. The molecule is also relatively weak: rotation about the C-C bond is a very low energy process that requires breaking the [π-bond](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi_bond "Pi bond") by supplying heat at 50°C.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

The [π-bond](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi_bond "Pi bond") in the ethylene molecule is responsible for its useful reactivity. The double bond is a region of high [electron density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_density "Electron density"), thus it is susceptible to attack by [electrophiles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrophiles "Electrophiles"). Many reactions of ethylene are catalyzed by transition metals, which bind transiently to the ethylene using both the π and π* orbitals.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

Being a simple molecule, ethylene is spectroscopically simple. Its UV-vis [spectrum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopy "Spectroscopy") is still used as a test of theoretical methods.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene#cite_note-NIST_Webbook-12)

[![Image 21](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Uses_of_Ethene.tif/lossy-page1-250px-Uses_of_Ethene.tif.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Uses_of_Ethene.tif)

Diagram of uses of ethene

Major industrial reactions of ethylene include in order of scale: 1) [polymerization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polymerization "Polymerization"), 2) [oxidation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxidation "Oxidation"), 3) [halogenation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halogenation "Halogenation") and [hydrohalogenation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrohalogenation "Hydrohalogenation"), 4) [alkylation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkylation "Alkylation"), 5) [hydration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydration_reaction "Hydration reaction"), 6) [oligomerization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oligomerization "Oligomerization"), and 7) [hydroformylation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroformylation "Hydroformylation"). In the [United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States "United States") and [Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Europe "Europe"), approximately 90% of ethylene is used to produce [ethylene oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene_oxide "Ethylene oxide"), [ethylene dichloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene_dichloride "Ethylene dichloride"), [ethylbenzene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylbenzene "Ethylbenzene") and [polyethylene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyethylene "Polyethylene").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene#cite_note-inchem-13) Most of the reactions with ethylene are [electrophilic addition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrophilic_addition "Electrophilic addition").[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

[![Image 22](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/C2H4uses.png/960px-C2H4uses.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:C2H4uses.png)

Main industrial uses of ethylene. Clockwise from the upper right: its conversions to [ethylene oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene_oxide "Ethylene oxide"), precursor to [ethylene glycol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene_glycol "Ethylene glycol"); to [ethylbenzene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylbenzene "Ethylbenzene"), precursor to [styrene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Styrene "Styrene"); to various kinds of [polyethylene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyethylene "Polyethylene"); to [ethylene dichloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene_dichloride "Ethylene dichloride"), precursor to [vinyl chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vinyl_chloride "Vinyl chloride").

Polyethylene production uses more than half of the world's ethylene supply. Polyethylene, also called _polyethene_ and _polythene_, is the world's most widely used plastic. It is primarily used to make films in [packaging](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Packaging "Packaging"), [carrier bags](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carrier_bags "Carrier bags") and trash [liners](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bin_bag "Bin bag"). Linear [alpha-olefins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha-olefins "Alpha-olefins"), produced by [oligomerization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oligomerization "Oligomerization") (formation of short-chain molecules) are used as [precursors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Precursor_(chemistry) "Precursor (chemistry)"), [detergents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Detergents "Detergents"), [plasticisers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasticisers "Plasticisers"), [synthetic lubricants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synthetic_lubricants "Synthetic lubricants"), additives, and also as co-monomers in the production of polyethylenes.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene#cite_note-inchem-13)

Ethylene is [oxidized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxidation "Oxidation") to produce [ethylene oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene_oxide "Ethylene oxide"), a key raw material in the production of [surfactants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surfactant "Surfactant") and [detergents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Detergent "Detergent") by [ethoxylation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethoxylation "Ethoxylation"). Ethylene oxide is also hydrolyzed to produce [ethylene glycol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene_glycol "Ethylene glycol"), widely used as an automotive antifreeze as well as higher molecular weight glycols, [glycol ethers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycol_ethers "Glycol ethers"), and [polyethylene terephthalate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyethylene_terephthalate "Polyethylene terephthalate").[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene#cite_note-14)[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene#cite_note-15)

Ethylene oxidation in the presence of a palladium catalyst can form [acetaldehyde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde "Acetaldehyde"). This conversion remains a major industrial process (10M kg/y).[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene#cite_note-16) The process proceeds via the initial complexation of ethylene to a Pd(II) center.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

### Halogenation and hydrohalogenation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ethylene&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Halogenation and hydrohalogenation")]

Major intermediates from the [halogenation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halogenation "Halogenation") and [hydrohalogenation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrohalogenation "Hydrohalogenation") of ethylene include [ethylene dichloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene_dichloride "Ethylene dichloride"), [ethyl chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethyl_chloride "Ethyl chloride"), and [ethylene dibromide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene_dibromide "Ethylene dibromide"). The addition of chlorine entails "[oxychlorination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxychlorination "Oxychlorination")", i.e. chlorine itself is not used. Some products derived from this group are [polyvinyl chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyvinyl_chloride "Polyvinyl chloride"), [trichloroethylene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trichloroethylene "Trichloroethylene"), [perchloroethylene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perchloroethylene "Perchloroethylene"), [methyl chloroform](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methyl_chloroform "Methyl chloroform"), [polyvinylidene chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyvinylidene_chloride "Polyvinylidene chloride") and [copolymers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Copolymer "Copolymer"), and [ethyl bromide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethyl_bromide "Ethyl bromide").[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene#cite_note-Keystone-17)

Major chemical intermediates from the [alkylation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkylation "Alkylation") with ethylene is [ethylbenzene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylbenzene "Ethylbenzene"), precursor to [styrene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Styrene "Styrene"). Styrene is used principally in [polystyrene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polystyrene "Polystyrene") for packaging and insulation, as well as in [styrene-butadiene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Styrene-butadiene "Styrene-butadiene") rubber for tires and footwear. On a smaller scale, [ethyltoluene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethyltoluene "Ethyltoluene"), ethylanilines, 1,4-hexadiene, and [aluminium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aluminium "Aluminium") alkyls. Products of these intermediates include [polystyrene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polystyrene "Polystyrene"), [unsaturated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saturated_and_unsaturated_compounds "Saturated and unsaturated compounds")[polyesters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyester "Polyester") and ethylene-propylene [terpolymers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Copolymer "Copolymer").[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene#cite_note-Keystone-17)

The [hydroformylation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroformylation "Hydroformylation") (oxo reaction) of ethylene results in [propionaldehyde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propionaldehyde "Propionaldehyde"), a precursor to [propionic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propionic_acid "Propionic acid") and [n-propyl alcohol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/N-propyl_alcohol "N-propyl alcohol").[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene#cite_note-Keystone-17)

Ethylene has long represented the major nonfermentative precursor to [ethanol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethanol "Ethanol"). The original method entailed its conversion to [diethyl sulfate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diethyl_sulfate "Diethyl sulfate"), followed by hydrolysis. The main method practiced since the mid-1990s is the direct hydration of ethylene catalyzed by [solid acid catalysts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_acid_catalyst "Solid acid catalyst"):[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene#cite_note-18)

C 2 H 4 + H 2 O → CH 3 CH 2 OH

### Dimerization to butenes

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ethylene&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Dimerization to butenes")]

Ethylene is [dimerized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimer_(chemistry) "Dimer (chemistry)") by [hydrovinylation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrovinylation "Hydrovinylation") to give _n_-butenes using processes licensed by Lummus or [IFP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Institute_of_Petroleum "French Institute of Petroleum"). The Lummus process produces mixed _n_-butenes (primarily [2-butenes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2-butene "2-butene")) while the IFP process produces [1-butene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1-butene "1-butene"). 1-Butene is used as a [comonomer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comonomer "Comonomer") in the production of certain kinds of [polyethylene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyethylene "Polyethylene").[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene#cite_note-19)

### Fruit and flowering

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ethylene&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Fruit and flowering")]

Ethylene is a hormone that affects the ripening and flowering of many plants. It is widely used to control freshness in [horticulture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horticulture "Horticulture") and [fruits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fruit "Fruit").[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene#cite_note-20) The scrubbing of naturally occurring ethylene delays ripening.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene#cite_note-21) Adsorption of ethylene by nets coated in [titanium dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Titanium_dioxide "Titanium dioxide") gel has also been shown to be effective.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene#cite_note-22)

An example of a niche use is as an [anesthetic agent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anesthesiology "Anesthesiology") (in an 85% ethylene/15% oxygen ratio).[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene#cite_note-23) It is also used as a refrigerant gas for low temperature applications under the name R-1150.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene#cite_note-24)

Global ethylene production was 107 million tonnes in 2005,[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene#cite_note-cenews-9) 109 million tonnes in 2006,[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene#cite_note-25) 138 million tonnes in 2010, and 141 million tonnes in 2011.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene#cite_note-26) By 2013, ethylene was produced by at least 117 companies in 32 countries. To meet the ever-increasing demand for ethylene, sharp increases in production facilities are added globally, particularly in the [Mideast](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mideast "Mideast") and in [China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China "China").[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene#cite_note-Ceresana-27) Production [emits greenhouse gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenhouse_gas_emissions "Greenhouse gas emissions"), namely significant amounts of carbon dioxide.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene#cite_note-28)

Ethylene is produced by several methods in the [petrochemical industry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petrochemical_industry "Petrochemical industry"). A primary method is [steam cracking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steam_cracking "Steam cracking") (SC) where hydrocarbons and steam are heated to 750–950°C. This process converts large hydrocarbons into smaller ones and introduces unsaturation. When [ethane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethane "Ethane") is the feedstock, ethylene is the product. Ethylene is separated from the resulting mixture by repeated [compression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compression_(physical) "Compression (physical)") and [distillation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distillation "Distillation").[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene#cite_note-Keystone-17) In Europe and Asia, ethylene is obtained mainly from cracking naphtha, gasoil and condensates with the coproduction of propylene, C4 olefins and aromatics (pyrolysis gasoline).[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene#cite_note-29) Other procedures employed for the production of ethylene include [Fischer-Tropsch synthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fischer-Tropsch_synthesis "Fischer-Tropsch synthesis") and [methanol-to-olefins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol-to-olefin "Methanol-to-olefin") (MTO).[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene#cite_note-30)

### Laboratory synthesis

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ethylene&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Laboratory synthesis")]

Although of great value industrially, ethylene is rarely synthesized in the laboratory and is ordinarily purchased.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene#cite_note-31) It can be produced via dehydration of [ethanol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethanol "Ethanol") with [sulfuric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfuric_acid "Sulfuric acid") or in the gas phase with [aluminium oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aluminium_oxide "Aluminium oxide") or [activated alumina](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Activated_alumina "Activated alumina").[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene#cite_note-32)

Ethylene is produced from [methionine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methionine "Methionine") in nature. The immediate precursor is [1-aminocyclopropane-1-carboxylic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1-Aminocyclopropane-1-carboxylic_acid "1-Aminocyclopropane-1-carboxylic acid").[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene#cite_note-Yang_1984-33)

[![Image 23](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Rh2Cl2_C2H4_4.svg/250px-Rh2Cl2_C2H4_4.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Rh2Cl2_C2H4_4.svg)

[Chlorobis(ethylene)rhodium dimer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chlorobis(ethylene)rhodium_dimer "Chlorobis(ethylene)rhodium dimer") is a well-studied complex of ethylene.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene#cite_note-34)

Ethylene is a fundamental [ligand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ligand "Ligand") in [transition metal alkene complexes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transition_metal_alkene_complexes "Transition metal alkene complexes"). One of the first organometallic compounds, [Zeise's salt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zeise%27s_salt "Zeise's salt") is a complex of ethylene. Useful reagents containing ethylene include Pt(PPh 3)2(C 2 H 4) and Rh 2 Cl 2(C 2 H 4)4. The Rh-catalysed [hydroformylation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroformylation "Hydroformylation") of ethylene is conducted on an industrial scale to provide [propionaldehyde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propionaldehyde "Propionaldehyde").[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene#cite_note-35)

Some geologists and scholars believe that the famous Greek Oracle at [Delphi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Delphi "Delphi") (the [Pythia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythia "Pythia")) went into her trance-like state as an effect of ethylene rising from ground faults.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene#cite_note-Roach-36)

Ethylene appears to have been discovered by [Johann Joachim Becher](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johann_Joachim_Becher "Johann Joachim Becher"), who obtained it by heating [ethanol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethanol "Ethanol") with sulfuric acid;[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene#cite_note-37) he mentioned the gas in his _Physica Subterranea_ (1669).[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene#cite_note-38)[Joseph Priestley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Priestley "Joseph Priestley") also mentions the gas in his _Experiments and observations relating to the various branches of natural philosophy: with a continuation of the observations on air_ (1779), where he reports that [Jan Ingenhousz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jan_Ingenhousz "Jan Ingenhousz") saw ethylene synthesized in the same way by a Mr. Enée in Amsterdam in 1777 and that Ingenhousz subsequently produced the gas himself.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene#cite_note-39) The properties of ethylene were studied in 1795 by four [Dutch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Netherlands "Netherlands") chemists, Johann Rudolph Deimann, Adrien Paets van Troostwyck, Anthoni Lauwerenburgh and Nicolas Bondt, who found that it differed from [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen") gas and that it contained both carbon and hydrogen.[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene#cite_note-40) This group also discovered that ethylene could be combined with [chlorine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chlorine "Chlorine") to produce the _Dutch oil_, [1,2-dichloroethane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1,2-Dichloroethane "1,2-Dichloroethane"); this discovery gave ethylene the name used for it at that time, _olefiant gas_ (oil-making gas.)[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene#cite_note-41) The term olefiant gas is in turn the etymological origin of the modern word "olefin", the class of hydrocarbons in which ethylene is the first member.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

In the mid-19th century, the suffix _-ene_ (an Ancient Greek root added to the end of female names meaning "daughter of") was widely used to refer to a molecule or part thereof that contained one fewer hydrogen atoms than the molecule being modified. Thus, _ethylene_ (C

2 H

4) was the "daughter of [ethyl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethyl_group "Ethyl group")" (C

2 H

5). The name ethylene was used in this sense as early as 1852.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene#cite_note-42)

In 1866, the [German](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germany "Germany") chemist [August Wilhelm von Hofmann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/August_Wilhelm_von_Hofmann "August Wilhelm von Hofmann") proposed a system of hydrocarbon nomenclature in which the suffixes -ane, -ene, -ine, -one, and -une were used to denote the hydrocarbons with 0, 2, 4, 6, and 8 fewer hydrogens than their parent [alkane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkane "Alkane").[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene#cite_note-43) In this system, ethylene became _ethene_. Hofmann's system eventually became the basis for the Geneva nomenclature approved by the International Congress of Chemists in 1892, which remains at the core of the [IUPAC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IUPAC "IUPAC") nomenclature. However, by that time, the name ethylene was deeply entrenched, and it remains in wide use today, especially in the chemical industry.

Following experimentation by Luckhardt, Crocker, and Carter at the University of Chicago,[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene#cite_note-44) ethylene was used as an anesthetic.[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene#cite_note-45)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene#cite_note-UllmannEthylene-7) It remained in use through the 1940s, even while chloroform was being phased out. Its pungent odor and its explosive nature limit its use today.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene#cite_note-46)

The 1979 IUPAC nomenclature rules made an exception for retaining the non-systematic name _ethylene_;[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene#cite_note-47) however, this decision was reversed in the 1993 rules,[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene#cite_note-48) and it remains unchanged in the newest 2013 recommendations,[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene#cite_note-49) so the IUPAC name is now _ethene_. In the IUPAC system, the name _ethylene_ is reserved for the [divalent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divalent "Divalent") group -CH 2 CH 2-. Hence, names like _ethylene oxide_ and _ethylene dibromide_ are permitted, but the use of the name _ethylene_ for the two-carbon alkene is not. Nevertheless, use of the name _ethylene_ for H 2 C=CH 2 (and propylene for H 2 C=CHCH 3) is still prevalent among chemists in North America.[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene#cite_note-50)

Like all hydrocarbons, ethylene is a combustible [asphyxiant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asphyxiant_gas "Asphyxiant gas"). It is listed as an [IARC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Agency_for_Research_on_Cancer "International Agency for Research on Cancer")[group 3 agent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IARC_Group_3 "IARC Group 3"), since there is no current evidence that it causes cancer in humans.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene#cite_note-51)
