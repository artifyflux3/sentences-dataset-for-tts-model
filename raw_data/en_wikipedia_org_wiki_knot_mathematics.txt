In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), a **knot** is an [embedding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Embedding "Embedding") of the [circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle#Topological_definition "Circle") ([_S_ 1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/N-sphere "N-sphere")) into three-dimensional [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space"), **R**3 (also known as **E**3). Often two knots are considered equivalent if they are [ambient isotopic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ambient_isotopy "Ambient isotopy"), that is, if there exists a continuous deformation of **R**3 which takes one knot to the other.

A crucial difference between the standard mathematical and conventional notions of a [knot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knot "Knot") is that mathematical knots are closed — there are no ends to tie or untie on a mathematical knot. Physical properties such as friction and thickness also do not apply, although there are mathematical definitions of a knot that take such properties into account. The term _knot_ is also applied to embeddings of _S_ _j_ in _S_ _n_, especially in the case _j_ = _n_ − 2. The branch of mathematics that studies knots is known as [knot theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knot_theory "Knot theory") and has many relations to [graph theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_theory "Graph theory").

A knot is an [embedding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Embedding#General_topology "Embedding") of the [circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle "Circle") (_S_ 1) into [three-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_space "Three-dimensional space")[Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space") (**R**3),[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knot_(mathematics)#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArmstrong1983213-1) or the [3-sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3-sphere "3-sphere") (_S_ 3), since the 3-sphere is [compact](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compact_space "Compact space").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knot_(mathematics)#cite_note-2)[[Note 1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knot_(mathematics)#cite_note-3) Two knots are defined to be equivalent if there is an [ambient isotopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ambient_isotopy "Ambient isotopy") between them.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knot_(mathematics)#cite_note-FOOTNOTECromwell20045-4)

A knot in [**R**3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space") (or alternatively in the [3-sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3-sphere "3-sphere"),_S_ 3), can be projected onto a plane[**R**2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_plane "Euclidean plane") (respectively a [sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere "Sphere")_S_ 2). This projection is almost always **regular**, meaning that it is [injective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Injective "Injective") everywhere, except at a _finite number_ of crossing points, which are the projections of _only two points_ of the knot, and these points are not [collinear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_(geometry) "Line (geometry)"). In this case, by choosing a projection side, one can completely encode the [isotopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_isotopy "Regular isotopy") class of the knot by its regular projection by recording a simple over/under information at these crossings. In graph theory terms, a regular projection of a knot, or [knot diagram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knot_diagram "Knot diagram") is thus a quadrivalent [planar graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planar_graph "Planar graph") with over/under-decorated vertices. The local modifications of this graph which allow to go from one diagram to any other diagram of the same knot (up to ambient [isotopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_isotopy "Regular isotopy") of the plane) are called [Reidemeister moves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reidemeister_moves "Reidemeister moves").

The simplest knot, called the [unknot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unknot "Unknot") or trivial knot, is a round circle embedded in [**R**3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knot_(mathematics)#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAdams19942-5) In the ordinary sense of the word, the unknot is not "knotted" at all. The simplest nontrivial knots are the [trefoil knot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trefoil_knot "Trefoil knot") (3 1 in the table), the [figure-eight knot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Figure-eight_knot_(mathematics) "Figure-eight knot (mathematics)") (4 1) and the [cinquefoil knot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cinquefoil_knot "Cinquefoil knot") (5 1).[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knot_(mathematics)#cite_note-6)

Several knots, linked or tangled together, are called [links](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Link_(knot_theory) "Link (knot theory)"). Knots are links with a single component.

### Tame vs. wild knots

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Knot_(mathematics)&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Tame vs. wild knots")]

[![Image 9](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Wild_knot.svg/250px-Wild_knot.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Wild_knot.svg)

A wild knot

A _polygonal_ knot is a knot whose [image](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Image_(mathematics) "Image (mathematics)") in **R**3 is the [union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Union_(set_theory) "Union (set theory)") of a [finite set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_set "Finite set") of [line segments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_segments "Line segments").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knot_(mathematics)#cite_note-Armstrong-7) A _tame_ knot is any knot equivalent to a polygonal knot.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knot_(mathematics)#cite_note-Armstrong-7)[[Note 2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knot_(mathematics)#cite_note-8) Knots which are not tame are called _[wild](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wild\_knot "Wild knot")_,[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knot_(mathematics)#cite_note-wild-9) and can have [pathological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pathological_(mathematics) "Pathological (mathematics)") behavior.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knot_(mathematics)#cite_note-wild-9) In knot theory and [3-manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3-manifold "3-manifold") theory, often the adjective "tame" is omitted. Smooth knots, for example, are always tame.

A _framed knot_ is the extension of a tame knot to an embedding of the [solid torus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_torus "Solid torus")_D_ 2 × _S_ 1 in _S_ 3.

The _framing_ of the knot is the [linking number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linking_number "Linking number") of the image of the ribbon _I_ × _S_ 1 with the knot. A framed knot can be seen as the embedded ribbon and the framing is the (signed) number of twists.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knot_(mathematics)#cite_note-10) This definition generalizes to an analogous one for _framed links_. Framed links are said to be _equivalent_ if their extensions to solid tori are ambient isotopic.

Framed link _diagrams_ are link diagrams with each component marked, to indicate framing, by an [integer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer") representing a slope with respect to the meridian and preferred longitude. A standard way to view a link diagram without markings as representing a framed link is to use the _blackboard framing_. This framing is obtained by converting each component to a ribbon lying flat on the plane. A type I [Reidemeister move](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reidemeister_move "Reidemeister move") clearly changes the blackboard framing (it changes the number of twists in a ribbon), but the other two moves do not. Replacing the type I move by a modified type I move gives a result for link diagrams with blackboard framing similar to the Reidemeister theorem: Link diagrams, with blackboard framing, represent equivalent framed links if and only if they are connected by a sequence of (modified) type I, II, and III moves. Given a knot, one can define infinitely many framings on it. Suppose that we are given a knot with a fixed framing. One may obtain a new framing from the existing one by cutting a ribbon and twisting it an integer multiple of 2π around the knot and then glue back again in the place we did the cut. In this way one obtains a new framing from an old one, up to the equivalence relation for framed knots„ leaving the knot fixed. [[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knot_(mathematics)#cite_note-11) The framing in this sense is associated to the number of twists the vector field performs around the knot. Knowing how many times the vector field is twisted around the knot allows one to determine the vector field up to diffeomorphism, and the equivalence class of the framing is determined completely by this integer called the framing integer.

[![Image 10](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/d7/Knot_with_borromean_rings_in_jsj_decomp_small.png/250px-Knot_with_borromean_rings_in_jsj_decomp_small.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Knot_with_borromean_rings_in_jsj_decomp_small.png)

A knot whose complement has a non-trivial JSJ decomposition

Given a knot in the 3-sphere, the [knot complement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knot_complement "Knot complement") is all the points of the 3-sphere not contained in the knot. A major [theorem of Gordon and Luecke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gordon%E2%80%93Luecke_theorem "Gordon–Luecke theorem") states that at most two knots have homeomorphic complements (the original knot and its mirror reflection). This in effect turns the study of knots into the study of their complements, and in turn into [3-manifold theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3-manifolds "3-manifolds").[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knot_(mathematics)#cite_note-12)

The [JSJ decomposition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/JSJ_decomposition "JSJ decomposition") and [Thurston's hyperbolization theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometrization_conjecture "Geometrization conjecture") reduces the study of knots in the 3-sphere to the study of various geometric manifolds via _splicing_ or _[satellite operations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satellite\_knot "Satellite knot")_. In the pictured knot, the JSJ-decomposition splits the complement into the union of three manifolds: two [trefoil complements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trefoil_knot "Trefoil knot") and the complement of the [Borromean rings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Borromean_rings "Borromean rings"). The trefoil complement has the geometry of **H**2 × **R**, while the Borromean rings complement has the geometry of **H**3.

Parametric representations of knots are called harmonic knots. Aaron Trautwein compiled parametric representations for all knots up to and including those with a crossing number of 8 in his PhD thesis.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knot_(mathematics)#cite_note-13)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knot_(mathematics)#cite_note-14)

Another convenient representation of knot diagrams [[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knot_(mathematics)#cite_note-15)[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knot_(mathematics)#cite_note-16) was introduced by [Peter Tait](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Tait_(physicist) "Peter Tait (physicist)") in 1877.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knot_(mathematics)#cite_note-17)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knot_(mathematics)#cite_note-18)

Any knot diagram defines a [plane graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planar_graph "Planar graph") whose vertices are the crossings and whose edges are paths in between successive crossings. Exactly one face of this planar graph is unbounded; each of the others is [homeomorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homeomorphic "Homeomorphic") to a 2-dimensional [disk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disk_(mathematics) "Disk (mathematics)"). Color these faces black or white so that the unbounded face is black and any two faces that share a boundary edge have opposite colors. The [Jordan curve theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jordan_curve_theorem "Jordan curve theorem") implies that there is exactly one such coloring.

We construct a new plane graph whose vertices are the white faces and whose edges correspond to crossings. We can label each edge in this graph as a left edge or a right edge, depending on which thread appears to go over the other as we view the corresponding crossing from one of the endpoints of the edge. Left and right edges are typically indicated by labeling left edges + and right edges –, or by drawing left edges with solid lines and right edges with dashed lines.

The original knot diagram is the [medial graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medial_graph "Medial graph") of this new plane graph, with the type of each crossing determined by the sign of the corresponding edge. Changing the sign of _every_ edge corresponds to reflecting [the knot in a mirror](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chiral_knot "Chiral knot").

### Linkless and knotless embedding

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Knot_(mathematics)&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Linkless and knotless embedding")]

[![Image 16](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Petersen_family.svg/250px-Petersen_family.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Petersen_family.svg)

The seven graphs in the [Petersen family](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petersen_family "Petersen family"). No matter how these graphs are embedded into three-dimensional space, some two cycles will have nonzero [linking number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linking_number "Linking number").

In two dimensions, only the [planar graphs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planar_graphs "Planar graphs") may be embedded into the Euclidean plane without crossings, but in three dimensions, any [undirected graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Undirected_graph "Undirected graph") may be embedded into space without crossings. However, a spatial analogue of the planar graphs is provided by the graphs with [linkless embeddings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linkless_embedding "Linkless embedding") and [knotless embeddings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knotless_embedding "Knotless embedding"). A linkless embedding is an embedding of the graph with the property that any two cycles are [unlinked](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unlink "Unlink"); a knotless embedding is an embedding of the graph with the property that any single cycle is [unknotted](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unknot "Unknot"). The graphs that have linkless embeddings have a [forbidden graph characterization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forbidden_graph_characterization "Forbidden graph characterization") involving the [Petersen family](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petersen_family "Petersen family"), a set of seven graphs that are intrinsically linked: no matter how they are embedded, some two cycles will be linked with each other.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knot_(mathematics)#cite_note-19) A full characterization of the graphs with knotless embeddings is not known, but the [complete graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complete_graph "Complete graph")_K_ 7 is one of the minimal forbidden graphs for knotless embedding: no matter how _K_ 7 is embedded, it will contain a cycle that forms a [trefoil knot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trefoil_knot "Trefoil knot").[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knot_(mathematics)#cite_note-20)

In contemporary mathematics the term _knot_ is sometimes used to describe a more general phenomenon related to embeddings. Given a manifold _M_ with a submanifold _N_, one sometimes says _N_ can be knotted in _M_ if there exists an embedding of _N_ in _M_ which is not isotopic to _N_. Traditional knots form the case where _N_ = _S_ 1 and _M_ = **R**3 or _M_ = _S_ 3.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knot_(mathematics)#cite_note-21)[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knot_(mathematics)#cite_note-22)

The [Schoenflies theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schoenflies_theorem "Schoenflies theorem") states that the circle does not knot in the 2-sphere: every topological circle in the 2-sphere is isotopic to a geometric circle.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knot_(mathematics)#cite_note-23)[Alexander's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander%27s_theorem "Alexander's theorem") states that the 2-sphere does not smoothly (or PL or tame topologically) knot in the 3-sphere.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knot_(mathematics)#cite_note-24) In the tame topological category, it's known that the _n_-sphere does not knot in the _n_ + 1-sphere for all _n_. This is a theorem of [Morton Brown](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morton_Brown "Morton Brown"), [Barry Mazur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barry_Mazur "Barry Mazur"), and [Marston Morse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marston_Morse "Marston Morse").[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knot_(mathematics)#cite_note-25) The [Alexander horned sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_horned_sphere "Alexander horned sphere") is an example of a knotted 2-sphere in the 3-sphere which is not tame.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knot_(mathematics)#cite_note-26) In the smooth category, the _n_-sphere is known not to knot in the _n_ + 1-sphere provided _n_ ≠ 3. The case _n_ = 3 is a long-outstanding problem closely related to the question: does the 4-ball admit an [exotic smooth structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exotic_sphere "Exotic sphere")?

[André Haefliger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Haefliger "André Haefliger") proved that there are no smooth _j_-dimensional knots in _S_ _n_ provided 2 _n_ − 3 _j_ − 3 > 0, and gave further examples of knotted spheres for all _n_>_j_ ≥ 1 such that 2 _n_ − 3 _j_ − 3 = 0. _n_ − _j_ is called the [codimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Codimension "Codimension") of the knot. An interesting aspect of Haefliger's work is that the isotopy classes of embeddings of _S_ _j_ in _S_ _n_ form a group, with group operation given by the connect sum, provided the co-dimension is greater than two. Haefliger based his work on [Stephen Smale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stephen_Smale "Stephen Smale")'s [_h_-cobordism theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/H-cobordism "H-cobordism"). One of Smale's theorems is that when one deals with knots in co-dimension greater than two, even inequivalent knots have diffeomorphic complements. This gives the subject a different flavour than co-dimension 2 knot theory. If one allows topological or PL-isotopies, [Christopher Zeeman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christopher_Zeeman "Christopher Zeeman") proved that spheres do not knot when the co-dimension is greater than 2. See a [generalization to manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whitney_embedding_theorem#Isotopy_versions "Whitney embedding theorem").
