**Pyrene** is a [polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polycyclic_aromatic_hydrocarbon "Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbon") (PAH) consisting of four fused [benzene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene "Benzene") rings, resulting in a flat [aromatic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aromaticity "Aromaticity") system. The chemical formula is C 16 H 10. This yellow-green solid is the smallest peri-fused PAH (one where the rings are fused through more than one face). Pyrene forms during [incomplete combustion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Combustion#Incomplete "Combustion") of organic compounds.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrene#cite_note-10)

Occurrence and properties
-------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Pyrene&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Occurrence and properties")]

Pyrene was first isolated from [coal tar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coal_tar "Coal tar"), where it occurs up to 2% by weight. As a peri-fused PAH, pyrene is much more [resonance-stabilized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resonance_(chemistry) "Resonance (chemistry)") than its five-member-ring containing isomer [fluoranthene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluoranthene "Fluoranthene"). Therefore, it is produced in a wide range of combustion conditions. For example, automobiles produce about 1 μg/km.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrene#cite_note-Ullmann-11)

Oxidation with [chromate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chromate_ion "Chromate ion") affords perinaphthenone and then naphthalene-1,4,5,8-tetracarboxylic acid. Pyrene undergoes a series of [hydrogenation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogenation "Hydrogenation") reactions and is susceptible to halogenation, [Diels-Alder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diels-Alder "Diels-Alder") additions, and nitration, all with varying degrees of selectivity.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrene#cite_note-Ullmann-11) Bromination occurs at one of the 3-positions.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrene#cite_note-12)

Reduction with sodium affords the radical anion. From this anion, a variety of pi-arene complexes can be prepared.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrene#cite_note-13)

Pyrene and its derivatives are used commercially to make [dyes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dye "Dye") and dye precursors, for example [pyranine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyranine "Pyranine") and naphthalene-1,4,5,8-tetracarboxylic acid. It has strong absorbance in UV-Vis in three sharp bands at 330 nm in DCM. The emission is close to the absorption, but moving at 375 nm.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrene#cite_note-:0-14) The morphology of the signals change with the solvent. Its derivatives are also valuable molecular probes via [fluorescence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluorescence "Fluorescence") spectroscopy, having a high quantum yield and lifetime (0.65 and 410 nanoseconds, respectively, in [ethanol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethanol "Ethanol") at 293 K). Pyrene was the first molecule for which [excimer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Excimer "Excimer") behavior was discovered.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrene#cite_note-15) Such excimer appears around 450 nm. [Theodor Förster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theodor_F%C3%B6rster "Theodor Förster") reported this in 1954.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrene#cite_note-16)

[![Image 21](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Br4Py_self-assembly_on_Au_2.jpg/250px-Br4Py_self-assembly_on_Au_2.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Br4Py_self-assembly_on_Au_2.jpg)

[STM](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scanning_tunneling_microscopy "Scanning tunneling microscopy") image of self-assembled Br 4 Py molecules on Au(111) surface (top) and its model (bottom; pink spheres are Br atoms).[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrene#cite_note-17)

Pyrene's fluorescence [emission spectrum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emission_spectrum "Emission spectrum") is very sensitive to solvent polarity, so pyrene has been used as a probe to determine solvent environments. This is due to its excited state having a different, non-planar structure than the ground state. Certain emission bands are unaffected, but others vary in intensity due to the strength of interaction with a solvent.

[![Image 22](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Pyrene-numbering.svg/250px-Pyrene-numbering.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Pyrene-numbering.svg)

Diagram showing the numbering and ring fusion locations of pyrene according to [IUPAC nomenclature of organic chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IUPAC_nomenclature_of_organic_chemistry "IUPAC nomenclature of organic chemistry").

Pyrenes are strong electron donor materials and can be combined with several materials in order to make electron donor-acceptor systems which can be used in energy conversion and light harvesting applications.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrene#cite_note-:0-14)

Safety and environmental factors
--------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Pyrene&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Safety and environmental factors")]

Although it is not as problematic as [benzopyrene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzopyrene "Benzopyrene"), [animal studies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Animal_studies "Animal studies") have shown pyrene is [toxic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toxic "Toxic") to the [kidneys](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kidneys "Kidneys") and [liver](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liver "Liver"). It is now known that pyrene affects several living functions in fish and algae.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrene#cite_note-18)[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrene#cite_note-19)[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrene#cite_note-20)[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrene#cite_note-21)

Its biodegradation has been heavily examined. The process commences with dihydroxylation at each of two kinds of CH=CH linkages.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrene#cite_note-22) Experiments in pigs show that urinary [1-hydroxypyrene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1-hydroxypyrene "1-hydroxypyrene") is a metabolite of pyrene, when given orally.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrene#cite_note-23)
