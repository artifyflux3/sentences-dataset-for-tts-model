**Joseph Liouville**[FRS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Society "Fellow of the Royal Society")[FRSE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Society_of_Edinburgh "Fellow of the Royal Society of Edinburgh")[FAS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Academy_of_Sciences "French Academy of Sciences") ( [_LEE-oo-VIL_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key "Help:Pronunciation respelling key"); French:[[ʒozɛf ljuvil]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/French "Help:IPA/French"); 24 March 1809 – 8 September 1882)[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Liouville#cite_note-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Liouville#cite_note-2) was a French mathematician and engineer.

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Journal_math_liouville.jpg/250px-Journal_math_liouville.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Journal_math_liouville.jpg)

Title page of the first volume of _Journal de Mathématiques Pures et Appliquées_ in 1836.

He was born in [Saint-Omer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saint-Omer "Saint-Omer") in France on 24 March 1809.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Liouville#cite_note-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Liouville#cite_note-4) His parents were Claude-Joseph Liouville (an army officer) and Thérèse Liouville (née Balland).

Liouville gained admission to the [École Polytechnique](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%89cole_Polytechnique "École Polytechnique") in 1825 and graduated in 1827. Just like [Augustin-Louis Cauchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Augustin-Louis_Cauchy "Augustin-Louis Cauchy") before him, Liouville studied engineering at [École des Ponts et Chaussées](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%89cole_des_Ponts_et_Chauss%C3%A9es "École des Ponts et Chaussées") after graduating from the Polytechnique, but opted instead for a career in mathematics. After some years as an assistant at various institutions including the [École Centrale Paris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%89cole_Centrale_Paris "École Centrale Paris"), he was appointed as professor at the École Polytechnique in 1838. He obtained a chair in mathematics at the [Collège de France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coll%C3%A8ge_de_France "Collège de France") in 1850 and a chair in mechanics at the Faculté des Sciences in 1857.

Besides his academic achievements, he was very talented in organisational matters. Liouville founded the _[Journal de Mathématiques Pures et Appliquées](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Journal\_de\_Math%C3%A9matiques\_Pures\_et\_Appliqu%C3%A9es "Journal de Mathématiques Pures et Appliquées")_ which retains its high reputation up to today, in order to promote other mathematicians' work. He was the first to read, and to recognize the importance of, the unpublished work of [Évariste Galois](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%89variste_Galois "Évariste Galois") which appeared in his journal in 1846. Liouville was also involved in politics for some time, and he became a member of the [Constituting Assembly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Assembly_of_France "National Assembly of France") in 1848. However, after his defeat in the legislative elections in 1849, he turned away from politics.

Liouville worked in a number of different fields in mathematics, including [number theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_theory "Number theory"), [complex analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_analysis "Complex analysis"), [differential geometry and topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry_and_topology "Differential geometry and topology"), but also [mathematical physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_physics "Mathematical physics") and even [astronomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy "Astronomy"). He is remembered particularly for [Liouville's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liouville%27s_theorem_(complex_analysis) "Liouville's theorem (complex analysis)") in complex analysis. In number theory, he was the first to prove the existence of [transcendental numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transcendental_number "Transcendental number") by a construction using [continued fractions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continued_fraction "Continued fraction") ([Liouville numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liouville_number "Liouville number")).[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Liouville#cite_note-5) In mathematical physics, Liouville made two fundamental contributions: the [Sturm–Liouville theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sturm%E2%80%93Liouville_theory "Sturm–Liouville theory"), which was joint work with [Charles François Sturm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Fran%C3%A7ois_Sturm "Charles François Sturm"), and is now a standard procedure to solve certain types of [integral equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral_equation "Integral equation") by developing into eigenfunctions, and the fact (also known as [Liouville's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liouville%27s_theorem_(Hamiltonian) "Liouville's theorem (Hamiltonian)")) that time evolution is measure preserving for a [Hamiltonian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamiltonian_mechanics "Hamiltonian mechanics") system. In Hamiltonian dynamics, Liouville also introduced the notion of [action-angle variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action-angle_variables "Action-angle variables") as a description of completely [integrable systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integrable_systems "Integrable systems"). The modern formulation of this is sometimes called the **Liouville–Arnold theorem**, and the underlying concept of integrability is referred to as **Liouville integrability**. Additionally, Liouville developed the [Riemann-Liouville integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann-Liouville_integral "Riemann-Liouville integral") to consider differentiation and integration of a [fractional order](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractional_calculus "Fractional calculus").

In 1851, he was elected a foreign member of the [Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Swedish_Academy_of_Sciences "Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences"). In 1853, he was elected as a member of the [American Philosophical Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Philosophical_Society "American Philosophical Society").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Liouville#cite_note-6)

The crater [Liouville](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liouville_(crater) "Liouville (crater)") on the [Moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moon "Moon") is named after him. So is the [Liouville function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liouville_function "Liouville function"), an important function in number theory.
