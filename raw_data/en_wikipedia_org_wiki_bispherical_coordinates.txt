Illustration of bispherical coordinates, which are obtained by rotating a two-dimensional [bipolar coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bipolar_coordinates "Bipolar coordinates") about the axis joining its two foci. The foci are located at distance 1 from the vertical _z_-axis. The red self-intersecting torus is the σ=45° isosurface, the blue sphere is the τ=0.5 isosurface, and the yellow half-plane is the φ=60° isosurface. The green half-plane marks the _x_-_z_ plane, from which φ is measured. The black point is located at the intersection of the red, blue and yellow isosurfaces, at Cartesian coordinates roughly (0.841, −1.456, 1.239).

**Bispherical coordinates** are a three-dimensional [orthogonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_coordinates "Orthogonal coordinates")[coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_system "Coordinate system") that results from rotating the two-dimensional [bipolar coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bipolar_coordinates "Bipolar coordinates") about the axis that connects the two foci. Thus, the two [foci](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Focus_(geometry) "Focus (geometry)")![Image 2: {\displaystyle F_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/100c7fbf174fe8b06eacc2a6b0bb2e1badd1c7ce) and ![Image 3: {\displaystyle F_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0fd17e0779153d765b40ebef91533489b87b2e37) in [bipolar coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bipolar_coordinates "Bipolar coordinates") remain points (on the ![Image 4: {\displaystyle z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf368e72c009decd9b6686ee84a375632e11de98)-axis, the axis of rotation) in the bispherical coordinate system.

The most common definition of bispherical coordinates ![Image 5: {\displaystyle (\tau ,\sigma ,\phi )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/53db56e7bd342a436ee2db6ab2690b360f190313) is

![Image 6: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}x&=a\ {\frac {\sin \sigma }{\cosh \tau -\cos \sigma }}\cos \phi ,\\y&=a\ {\frac {\sin \sigma }{\cosh \tau -\cos \sigma }}\sin \phi ,\\z&=a\ {\frac {\sinh \tau }{\cosh \tau -\cos \sigma }},\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dd0301f0bd780fdbd15deee7064af8be335d9662)
where the ![Image 7: {\displaystyle \sigma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/59f59b7c3e6fdb1d0365a494b81fb9a696138c36) coordinate of a point ![Image 8: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a) equals the angle ![Image 9: {\displaystyle F_{1}PF_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9cda32c101507b35c2f715808ecce6b648c0ebff) and the ![Image 10: {\displaystyle \tau }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38a7dcde9730ef0853809fefc18d88771f95206c) coordinate equals the [natural logarithm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_logarithm "Natural logarithm") of the ratio of the distances ![Image 11: {\displaystyle d_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4cccb5a6a2f1acab4ca255e0be86c224ed82282a) and ![Image 12: {\displaystyle d_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9276f8f68c5c23329de74ad76e69f6801358fb1f) to the foci

![Image 13: {\displaystyle \tau =\ln {\frac {d_{1}}{d_{2}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/18362215eee1a1e4d0695ab357d50c66cbbd687b)
The coordinates ranges are −∞ <![Image 14: {\displaystyle \tau }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38a7dcde9730ef0853809fefc18d88771f95206c)< ∞, 0 ≤ ![Image 15: {\displaystyle \sigma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/59f59b7c3e6fdb1d0365a494b81fb9a696138c36) ≤ ![Image 16: {\displaystyle \pi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9be4ba0bb8df3af72e90a0535fabcc17431e540a) and 0 ≤ ![Image 17: {\displaystyle \phi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/72b1f30316670aee6270a28334bdf4f5072cdde4) ≤ 2![Image 18: {\displaystyle \pi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9be4ba0bb8df3af72e90a0535fabcc17431e540a).

### Coordinate surfaces

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bispherical_coordinates&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Coordinate surfaces")]

Surfaces of constant ![Image 19: {\displaystyle \sigma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/59f59b7c3e6fdb1d0365a494b81fb9a696138c36) correspond to intersecting tori of different radii

![Image 20: {\displaystyle z^{2}+\left({\sqrt {x^{2}+y^{2}}}-a\cot \sigma \right)^{2}={\frac {a^{2}}{\sin ^{2}\sigma }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cb0af3e02d4d0155942c2d69d55811de579d73f6)
that all pass through the foci but are not concentric. The surfaces of constant ![Image 21: {\displaystyle \tau }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38a7dcde9730ef0853809fefc18d88771f95206c) are non-intersecting spheres of different radii

![Image 22: {\displaystyle \left(x^{2}+y^{2}\right)+\left(z-a\coth \tau \right)^{2}={\frac {a^{2}}{\sinh ^{2}\tau }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c58e8116b375a22d76984048f9fd5a24e44c5c45)
that surround the foci. The centers of the constant-![Image 23: {\displaystyle \tau }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38a7dcde9730ef0853809fefc18d88771f95206c) spheres lie along the ![Image 24: {\displaystyle z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf368e72c009decd9b6686ee84a375632e11de98)-axis, whereas the constant-![Image 25: {\displaystyle \sigma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/59f59b7c3e6fdb1d0365a494b81fb9a696138c36) tori are centered in the ![Image 26: {\displaystyle xy}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c72eb345e496513fb8b2fa4aa8c4d89b855f9a01) plane.

The formulae for the inverse transformation are:

![Image 27: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\sigma &=\arccos \left({\dfrac {R^{2}-a^{2}}{Q}}\right),\\\tau &=\operatorname {arsinh} \left({\dfrac {2az}{Q}}\right),\\\phi &=\arctan \left({\dfrac {y}{x}}\right),\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9721ef9795a0fb19bd5939020b19dd112eb5c1d2)
where ![Image 28: {\textstyle R={\sqrt {x^{2}+y^{2}+z^{2}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1a514b50d05a2b2bc68849417171015fc7a2f056) and ![Image 29: {\textstyle Q={\sqrt {\left(R^{2}+a^{2}\right)^{2}-\left(2az\right)^{2}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/76eadaebc3cc995de5e6e253a62ff4178468ff09)

The scale factors for the bispherical coordinates ![Image 30: {\displaystyle \sigma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/59f59b7c3e6fdb1d0365a494b81fb9a696138c36) and ![Image 31: {\displaystyle \tau }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38a7dcde9730ef0853809fefc18d88771f95206c) are equal

![Image 32: {\displaystyle h_{\sigma }=h_{\tau }={\frac {a}{\cosh \tau -\cos \sigma }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1f1dde0bb87db13aeafe2e17c842d6fc2d337de2)
whereas the azimuthal scale factor equals

![Image 33: {\displaystyle h_{\phi }={\frac {a\sin \sigma }{\cosh \tau -\cos \sigma }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/23ffce1877f23224a9bd4f9c7ed20e5ce5ee8ea5)
Thus, the infinitesimal volume element equals

![Image 34: {\displaystyle dV={\frac {a^{3}\sin \sigma }{\left(\cosh \tau -\cos \sigma \right)^{3}}}\,d\sigma \,d\tau \,d\phi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e3cc7f5d721bbf32b0057fb8006cc4287d1a5cf6)
and the Laplacian is given by

![Image 35: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\nabla ^{2}\Phi ={\frac {\left(\cosh \tau -\cos \sigma \right)^{3}}{a^{2}\sin \sigma }}&\left[{\frac {\partial }{\partial \sigma }}\left({\frac {\sin \sigma }{\cosh \tau -\cos \sigma }}{\frac {\partial \Phi }{\partial \sigma }}\right)\right.\\[8pt]&{}\quad +\left.\sin \sigma {\frac {\partial }{\partial \tau }}\left({\frac {1}{\cosh \tau -\cos \sigma }}{\frac {\partial \Phi }{\partial \tau }}\right)+{\frac {1}{\sin \sigma \left(\cosh \tau -\cos \sigma \right)}}{\frac {\partial ^{2}\Phi }{\partial \phi ^{2}}}\right]\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3e64490d05a1f7b1450d8c53ad607f30c522b63)
Other differential operators such as ![Image 36: {\displaystyle \nabla \cdot \mathbf {F} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1f4f5b9e6560d7fdaf1a69d9fdeff6beb1f327cc) and ![Image 37: {\displaystyle \nabla \times \mathbf {F} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c6e2429b7e3cb6965d1a6651703ff5ef0c88b378) can be expressed in the coordinates ![Image 38: {\displaystyle (\sigma ,\tau )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c2d60596d44a9bc5043b60b76c1fac959eb65eb0) by substituting the scale factors into the general formulae found in [orthogonal coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_coordinates "Orthogonal coordinates").

The classic applications of bispherical coordinates are in solving [partial differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_differential_equations "Partial differential equations"), e.g., [Laplace's equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laplace%27s_equation "Laplace's equation"), for which bispherical coordinates allow a [separation of variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separation_of_variables "Separation of variables"). However, the [Helmholtz equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helmholtz_equation "Helmholtz equation") is not separable in bispherical coordinates. A typical example would be the [electric field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_field "Electric field") surrounding two conducting spheres of different radii.
