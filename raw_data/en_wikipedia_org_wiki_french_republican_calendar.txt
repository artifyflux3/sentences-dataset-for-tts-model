The **French Republican calendar** ([French](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_language "French language"): _calendrier républicain français_), also commonly called the **French Revolutionary calendar** (_calendrier révolutionnaire français_), was a [calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calendar "Calendar") created and implemented during the [French Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Revolution "French Revolution") and used by the French government for about 12 years from late 1793 to 1805, and for 18 days by the [Paris Commune](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paris_Commune "Paris Commune") in 1871, meant to replace the [Gregorian calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gregorian_calendar "Gregorian calendar").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Republican_Calendar#cite_note-1) The calendar consisted of twelve 30-day months, each divided into three 10-day cycles similar to weeks, plus five or six [intercalary days](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intercalation_(timekeeping) "Intercalation (timekeeping)") at the end to fill out the balance of a [solar year](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tropical_year "Tropical year"). It was designed in part to remove all religious and [royalist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royalist "Royalist") influences from the calendar, and it was part of a larger attempt at [dechristianisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dechristianization_of_France_during_the_French_Revolution "Dechristianization of France during the French Revolution") and [decimalisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimalisation "Decimalisation") in France (which also included [decimal time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_time "Decimal time") of day, [decimalisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimalisation "Decimalisation") of currency, and [metrication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metrication "Metrication")). It was used in government records in France and other areas under French rule, including [Belgium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_Belgium#French_control "History of Belgium"), [Luxembourg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_Luxembourg#French_invasion "History of Luxembourg"), and parts of the [Netherlands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Netherlands "Netherlands"), [Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germany "Germany"), [Switzerland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Switzerland "Switzerland"), [Malta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malta "Malta"), and [Italy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italy "Italy").

The [National Constituent Assembly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Constituent_Assembly_(France) "National Constituent Assembly (France)") at first intended to create a new calendar marking the "era of Liberty", beginning on 14 July 1789, the date of the [storming of the Bastille](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Storming_of_the_Bastille "Storming of the Bastille"). However, on 2 January 1792 its successor the [Legislative Assembly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legislative_Assembly_(France) "Legislative Assembly (France)") decided that Year IV of Liberty had begun the day before. Year I had therefore begun on 1 January 1789.

On 21 September 1792 the [French First Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_First_Republic "French First Republic") was [proclaimed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proclamation_of_the_abolition_of_the_monarchy "Proclamation of the abolition of the monarchy"), and the new [National Convention](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Convention "National Convention") decided that 1792 was to be known as Year I of the French Republic. It decreed on 2 January 1793 that Year II of the Republic had begun the day before. However, the new calendar as adopted by the Convention in October 1793 made 22 September 1792 the first day of Year I. The [Common Era](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Common_Era "Common Era"), commemorating the birth of [Jesus Christ](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jesus "Jesus"), was abolished and replaced with _l'ère républicaine_, the Republican Era, signifying the "age of reason" overcoming superstition, as part of the [campaign of dechristianisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dechristianization_of_France_during_the_French_Revolution "Dechristianization of France during the French Revolution").

The calendar is frequently named the "French Revolutionary Calendar" because it was created during the revolution, but this is a slight misnomer. In France, it is known as the _calendrier républicain_ as well as the _calendrier révolutionnaire_. There was initially a debate as to whether the calendar should celebrate the revolution, which began in July 1789, or the Republic, which was established in 1792.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Republican_Calendar#cite_note-2) Immediately following 14 July 1789, papers and pamphlets started calling 1789 year I of Liberty and the following years II and III. It was in 1792, with the practical problem of dating financial transactions, that the legislative assembly was confronted with the problem of the calendar. Originally, the choice of epoch was either 1 January 1789 or 14 July 1789. After some hesitation the assembly decided on 2 January 1792 that all official documents would use the "era of Liberty" and that the year IV of Liberty started on 1 January 1792. This usage was modified on 22 September 1792 when the Republic was proclaimed and the Convention decided that all public documents would be dated Year I of the French Republic. The decree of 2 January 1793 stipulated that the year II of the Republic began on 1 January 1793; this was revoked with the introduction of the calendar, which set 22 September 1793 as the beginning of year II. The establishment of the Republic was used as the epochal date for the calendar; therefore, the calendar commemorates the Republic, and not the Revolution.

The [Concordat of 1801](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concordat_of_1801 "Concordat of 1801") re-established the Roman Catholic Church as an official institution in France, although not as the state religion of France.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Republican_Calendar#cite_note-3) The concordat took effect from Easter Sunday, 28 Germinal, Year XI (8 April 1802); it restored the names of the days of the week to the ones from the [Gregorian calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gregorian_calendar "Gregorian calendar"), and fixed [Sunday](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sunday "Sunday") as the official day of rest and religious celebration.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Republican_Calendar#cite_note-4) However, the other attributes of the republican calendar, the months, and years, remained as they were.

The First Republic ended with the [coronation of Napoleon I as emperor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coronation_of_Napoleon "Coronation of Napoleon") on 11 Frimaire, Year XIII, or 2 December 1804. Despite this, the republican calendar continued to be used until 1 January 1806, when Napoleon declared it abolished. It was used again briefly in the _[Journal officiel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Journal\_officiel "Journal officiel")_ for some dates during a short period of the [Paris Commune](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paris_Commune "Paris Commune"), 6–23 May 1871 (16 Floréal – 3 Prairial Year LXXIX).[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Republican_Calendar#cite_note-5)

Overview and origins
--------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=French_Republican_calendar&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Overview and origins")]

The prominent atheist essayist and philosopher [Sylvain Maréchal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sylvain_Mar%C3%A9chal "Sylvain Maréchal") published the first edition of his _Almanach des Honnêtes-gens_ (Almanac of Honest People) in 1788.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Republican_Calendar#cite_note-Marechal-6) The first month in the almanac is "Mars, ou Princeps" (March, or First), the last month is "Février, ou Duodécembre" (February, or Twelfth). The lengths of the months are the same as those in the Gregorian calendar; however, the 10th, 20th, and 30th days are singled out of each month as the end of a _décade_ (group of ten days). Individual days were assigned, instead of to the traditional saints, to people noteworthy for mostly secular achievements. Later editions of the almanac would switch to the Republican Calendar.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Republican_Calendar#cite_note-7)

### Development and usage

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=French_Republican_calendar&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Development and usage")]

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Musee-historique-lausanne-img_0143.jpg/250px-Musee-historique-lausanne-img_0143.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Musee-historique-lausanne-img_0143.jpg)

A copy of the French Republican Calendar in the Historical Museum of Lausanne

The days of the [French Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Revolution "French Revolution") and First French Republic saw many efforts to sweep away various trappings of the [_ancien régime_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancien_r%C3%A9gime "Ancien régime") (the old [feudal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feudalism "Feudalism") monarchy); some of these were more successful than others. The new Republican government sought to institute, among other reforms, a new social and legal system, a new system of weights and measures (which became the [metric system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_system "Metric system")), and a new calendar.

Amid nostalgia for the ancient [Roman Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Republic "Roman Republic"), the theories of the [Age of Enlightenment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment "Age of Enlightenment") were at their peak, and the devisers of the new systems looked to nature for their inspiration. Natural constants, multiples of ten, and [Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin "Latin") as well as [Ancient Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek "Ancient Greek") derivations formed the fundamental blocks from which the systems were built.

The calendar was created by a commission under the direction of the politician [Gilbert Romme](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gilbert_Romme "Gilbert Romme") seconded by [Claude Joseph Ferry](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Claude_Joseph_Ferry&action=edit&redlink=1 "Claude Joseph Ferry (page does not exist)")[[fr](https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Claude_Joseph_Ferry "fr:Claude Joseph Ferry")] and [Charles-François Dupuis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles-Fran%C3%A7ois_Dupuis "Charles-François Dupuis"). They associated with their work chemist [Louis-Bernard Guyton de Morveau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis-Bernard_Guyton_de_Morveau "Louis-Bernard Guyton de Morveau"), mathematician and astronomer [Joseph-Louis Lagrange](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph-Louis_Lagrange "Joseph-Louis Lagrange"), astronomer [Jérôme Lalande](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J%C3%A9r%C3%B4me_Lalande "Jérôme Lalande"), mathematician [Gaspard Monge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaspard_Monge "Gaspard Monge"), astronomer and naval geographer [Alexandre Guy Pingré](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexandre_Guy_Pingr%C3%A9 "Alexandre Guy Pingré"), and poet, actor and playwright [Fabre d'Églantine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fabre_d%27%C3%89glantine "Fabre d'Églantine") (who invented the names of the months) with the help of [André Thouin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Thouin "André Thouin") (gardener at the [Jardin des plantes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jardin_des_plantes "Jardin des plantes") of the [Muséum National d'Histoire Naturelle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mus%C3%A9um_National_d%27Histoire_Naturelle "Muséum National d'Histoire Naturelle") in Paris). As the [rapporteur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rapporteur "Rapporteur") of the commission, Romme presented the calendar to the [Jacobin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacobin "Jacobin")-controlled National Convention on 23 September 1793, which adopted it on 24 October 1793 and also extended it [proleptically](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/proleptic "wikt:proleptic") to its [epoch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epoch "Epoch") of 22 September 1792. It is because of his position as rapporteur of the commission that the creation of the republican calendar is attributed to Romme.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Republican_Calendar#cite_note-8)

French coins of the period used the calendar. Many show the year ([French](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_language "French language"): _an_) in [Arabic numerals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabic_numerals "Arabic numerals"), although [Roman numerals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_numerals "Roman numerals") were used on some issues. Year 11 coins typically have a "XI" date to avoid confusion with the Roman "II".

L AN 2 DE LA REPUBLIQUE FR (Year 2 of the French Republic) on a barn near Geneva, dating to 1793 or 1794

[![Image 4](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/PereDuchesneIllustre1_1_0.png/250px-PereDuchesneIllustre1_1_0.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:PereDuchesneIllustre1_1_0.png)

1 Floréal, Year 79 issue of _[Le Fils du Père Duchêne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Le\_Fils\_du\_P%C3%A8re\_Duch%C3%AAne "Le Fils du Père Duchêne")_, a newspaper published during the [Paris Commune](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paris_Commune "Paris Commune").

Years usually appear in writing as Roman numerals. Roman numeral I indicates the first year of the republic, that is, the year before the calendar actually came into use. By law, the beginning of each year was set at midnight, beginning on the day the apparent [autumnal equinox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/September_equinox "September equinox") falls at the Paris Observatory.

There were twelve months, each divided into three 10-day weeks called _décades_. The tenth day, _décadi_, replaced Sunday as the day of rest and festivity. The five or six extra days needed to approximate the solar or [tropical year](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tropical_year "Tropical year") were placed after the final month of each year and called [complementary days](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sansculottides "Sansculottides"). This arrangement was an almost exact copy of the [calendar used by the Ancient Egyptians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egyptian_calendar "Egyptian calendar"), though in their case the year did not begin and end on the autumnal equinox.

A period of four years ending on a leap day was to be called a "Franciade". The name "[Olympique](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Olympiad "Olympiad")" was originally proposed[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Republican_Calendar#cite_note-Rapportsur-9) but changed to Franciade to commemorate the fact that it had taken the revolution four years to establish a republican government in France.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Republican_Calendar#cite_note-10) The leap year was called _Sextile_, an allusion to the "[bissextile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bissextus "Bissextus")" [leap years](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leap_year "Leap year") of the Julian and Gregorian calendars, because it contained a sixth complementary day.

Each day was divided into ten hours, each hour into 100 decimal minutes, and each decimal minute into 100 decimal seconds. Thus an hour was 144 conventional minutes (2.4 times as long as a conventional hour), a minute was 86.4 conventional seconds (44% longer than a conventional minute), and a second was 0.864 conventional seconds (13.6% shorter than a conventional second). [Clocks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clock "Clock") were manufactured to display this [decimal time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_time "Decimal time"), but it did not catch on. Mandatory use of decimal time was officially suspended 7 April 1795, although some cities continued to use decimal time as late as 1801.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Republican_Calendar#cite_note-Carrigan-11) The numbering of years by Roman numerals ran counter to this general decimalisation tendency.

The month names were based on nature, principally having to do with the prevailing weather in and around Paris and sometimes evoking the Medieval [Labours of the Months](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Labours_of_the_Months "Labours of the Months"). The extra five or six days in the year were not given a month designation but considered [Sansculottides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sansculottides "Sansculottides") or [complementary days](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Republican_Calendar#Complementary_days). Most of the month names were new words coined from French, Latin, or Greek. The endings of the names were grouped by season. _-dor_ comes from δῶρον, _dō̂ron_ means 'giving' in Greek.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Republican_Calendar#cite_note-Carlyle-12)

*   Autumn: 
    *   [Vendémiaire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vend%C3%A9miaire "Vendémiaire") (from French _vendange_, which means 'grape harvest', derived from Latin _vindemia_ 'vintage'), starting 22, 23, or 24 September
    *   [Brumaire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brumaire "Brumaire") (from French _brume_ 'mist', from Latin _brūma_ 'winter solstice; winter; winter cold'), starting 22, 23, or 24 October
    *   [Frimaire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frimaire "Frimaire") (from French _frimas_ 'frost'), starting 21, 22, or 23 November

*   Winter: 
    *   [Nivôse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Niv%C3%B4se "Nivôse") (from Latin _nivosus_ 'snowy'), starting 21, 22, or 23 December
    *   [Pluviôse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pluvi%C3%B4se "Pluviôse") (from French _pluvieux_, derived from Latin _pluvius_ 'rainy'), starting 20, 21, or 22 January
    *   [Ventôse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vent%C3%B4se "Ventôse") (from French _venteux_, derived from Latin _ventosus_ 'windy'), starting 19, 20, or 21 February

*   Spring: 
    *   [Germinal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germinal_(month) "Germinal (month)") (from French _germination_), starting 21 or 22 March
    *   [Floréal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flor%C3%A9al "Floréal") (from French _fleur_, derived from Latin _flos_ 'flower'), starting 20 or 21 April
    *   [Prairial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prairial "Prairial") (from French _prairie_ 'meadow'), starting 20 or 21 May

*   Summer: 
    *   [Messidor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Messidor "Messidor") (from Latin _messis_ 'harvest'), starting 19 or 20 June
    *   [Thermidor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermidor "Thermidor") (from Greek θέρμη, _thermē_, 'summer heat'), starting 19 or 20 July; on many printed calendars of Year II (1793–94), the month of _Thermidor_ was named _Fervidor_ (from Latin _fervidus_, "burning hot")
    *   [Fructidor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fructidor "Fructidor") (from Latin _fructus_ 'fruit'), starting 18 or 19 August

In Britain, a contemporary wit mocked the calendar by calling the months: [Wheezy](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/wheezy "wikt:wheezy"), [Sneezy](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/sneezy "wikt:sneezy"), and [Freezy](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/freezy "wikt:freezy"); [Slippy](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/slippy "wikt:slippy"), [Drippy](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/drippy "wikt:drippy"), and [Nippy](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/nippy "wikt:nippy"); [Showery](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/showery "wikt:showery"), [Flowery](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/flowery "wikt:flowery"), and [Bowery](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/bowery "wikt:bowery"); [Hoppy](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/hoppy "wikt:hoppy"), [Croppy](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/croppy "wikt:croppy"), and [Poppy](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/poppy "wikt:poppy").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Republican_Calendar#cite_note-13)[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Republican_Calendar#cite_note-14) Historian [Thomas Carlyle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Carlyle "Thomas Carlyle") suggests somewhat more serious English names in his 1837 work _[The French Revolution: A History](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The\_French\_Revolution:\_A\_History "The French Revolution: A History")_,[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Republican_Calendar#cite_note-Carlyle-12) namely Vintagearious, Fogarious, Frostarious, Snowous, Rainous, Windous, Buddal, Floweral, Meadowal, Reapidor, Heatidor, and Fruitidor. Like the French originals, they are [neologisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neologism "Neologism") suggesting a meaning related to the season.

French Revolutionary [pocket watch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pocket_watch "Pocket watch") showing ten-day _décade_ names and thirty-day month numbers from the Republican Calendar, but with duodecimal time. On display at the [Musée d'Art et d'Histoire (Neuchâtel)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mus%C3%A9e_d%27Art_et_d%27Histoire_(Neuch%C3%A2tel) "Musée d'Art et d'Histoire (Neuchâtel)") In Switzerland.

Each month was divided into three _décades_ or "weeks" of ten days each, named:

*   _primidi_ (first day)
*   _duodi_ (second day)
*   _tridi_ (third day)
*   _quartidi_ (fourth day)
*   _quintidi_ (fifth day)
*   _sextidi_ (sixth day)
*   _septidi_ (seventh day)
*   _octidi_ (eighth day)
*   _nonidi_ (ninth day)
*   _décadi_ (tenth day)

Décadis became an official day of rest instead of Sunday, in order to diminish the influence of the Roman [Catholic Church](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catholic_Church "Catholic Church"). They were used for the festivals of a succession of new religions meant to replace Catholicism: the [Cult of Reason](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cult_of_Reason "Cult of Reason"), the [Cult of the Supreme Being](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cult_of_the_Supreme_Being "Cult of the Supreme Being"), the [Decadary Cult](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decadary_Cult "Decadary Cult"), and [Theophilanthropy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theophilanthropy "Theophilanthropy"). [Christian holidays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liturgical_year "Liturgical year") were officially abolished in favor of revolutionary holidays. The law of 13 Fructidor year VI (30 August 1798) required that marriages must only be celebrated on décadis. This law was applied from the 1st Vendémiaire year VII (22 September 1798) to 28 Pluviôse year VIII (17 February 1800).[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

Five extra days – six in leap years – were national holidays at the end of every year. These were originally known as _[les sans-culottides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sansculottides "Sansculottides")_ (after _[sans-culottes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sans-culottes "Sans-culottes")_), but after year III (1795) as _les jours complémentaires_:

*   1st complementary day: _La Fête de la Vertu_, "Celebration of Virtue", on 17 or 18 September
*   2nd complementary day: _La Fête du Génie_, "Celebration of Talent", on 18 or 19 September
*   3rd complementary day: _La Fête du Travail_, "Celebration of Labour", on 19 or 20 September
*   4th complementary day: _La Fête de l'Opinion_, "Celebration of Convictions", on 20 or 21 September
*   5th complementary day: _La Fête des Récompenses_, "Celebration of Honours (Awards)", on 21 or 22 September
*   6th complementary day: _La Fête de la Révolution_, "Celebration of the Revolution", on 22 or 23 September (on leap years only)

The Roman Catholic Church used a [calendar of saints](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calendar_of_saints "Calendar of saints"), which named each day of the year after an associated [saint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saint "Saint"). To reduce the influence of the Church, [Fabre d'Églantine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fabre_d%27%C3%89glantine "Fabre d'Églantine") introduced a rural calendar in which each day of the year had a unique name associated with the [rural economy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rural_economics "Rural economics"), stated to correspond to the time of year. Every _décadi_ (ending in 0) was named after an agricultural tool. Each _quintidi_ (ending in 5) was named for a common animal. The rest of the days were named for "grain, pasture, trees, roots, flowers, fruits" and other plants, except for the first month of winter, Nivôse, during which the rest of the days were named after minerals.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Republican_Calendar#cite_note-15)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Republican_Calendar#cite_note-16)

> Our starting point was the idea of celebrating, through the calendar, the agricultural system, and of leading the nation back to it, marking the times and the fractions of the year by intelligible or visible signs taken from agriculture and the rural economy. (...)
> 
> 
> As the calendar is something that we use so often, we must take advantage of this frequency of use to put elementary notions of agriculture before the people – to show the richness of nature, to make them love the fields, and to methodically show them the order of the influences of the heavens and of the products of the earth.
> 
> 
> The priests assigned the commemoration of a so-called saint to each day of the year: this catalogue exhibited neither utility nor method; it was a collection of lies, of deceit or of charlatanism.
> 
> 
> We thought that the nation, after having kicked out this canonised mob from its calendar, must replace it with the objects that make up the true riches of the nation, worthy objects not from a cult, but from agriculture – useful products of the soil, the tools that we use to cultivate it, and the domesticated animals, our faithful servants in these works; animals much more precious, without doubt, to the eye of reason, than the beatified skeletons pulled from the catacombs of Rome.
> 
> 
> So we have arranged in the column of each month, the names of the real treasures of the rural economy. The grains, the pastures, the trees, the roots, the flowers, the fruits, the plants are arranged in the calendar, in such a way that the place and the day of the month that each product occupies is precisely the season and the day that Nature presents it to us.

— Fabre d'Églantine, "Rapport fait à la Convention nationale au nom de la Commission chargée de la confection du Calendrier",[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Republican_Calendar#cite_note-Rapport_Fabre-17) Imprimerie nationale, 1793

Criticism and shortcomings
--------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=French_Republican_calendar&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Criticism and shortcomings")]

[![Image 6](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Horloge-republicaine1.jpg/250px-Horloge-republicaine1.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Horloge-republicaine1.jpg)

Clock dial displaying both decimal and duodecimal time

Leap years in the calendar are a point of great dispute, due to the contradicting statements in the establishing decree[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Republican_Calendar#cite_note-gefrance.com-18) stating:

> Each year begins at midnight, with the day on which the true autumnal equinox falls for the [Paris Observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paris_Observatory "Paris Observatory").

and:

> The four-year period, after which the addition of a day is usually necessary, is called the _Franciade_ in memory of the revolution which, after four years of effort, led France to republican government. The fourth year of the _Franciade_ is called _Sextile_.

These two specifications are incompatible, as leap years defined by the autumnal equinox in Paris do not recur on a regular four-year schedule. It was erroneously believed that one leap day would be skipped automatically every 129 years,[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Republican_Calendar#cite_note-19) on average, but actually five years would sometimes pass between leap years, about three times per century. Thus, the years III, VII, and XI were observed as leap years, and the years XV and XX were also planned as such, even though they were five years apart.

[![Image 7](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Horloge-republicaine2.jpg/250px-Horloge-republicaine2.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Horloge-republicaine2.jpg)

Clock dial displaying both decimal (inside the circle) and duodecimal time (on the outer rim)

A fixed arithmetic rule for determining leap years was proposed by Delambre and presented to the Committee of Public Education by Romme on 19 Floréal An III (8 May 1795). The proposed rule was to determine leap years by applying the rules of the Gregorian calendar to the years of the French Republic (years IV, VIII, XII, etc. were to be leap years) except that year 4000 (the last year of ten 400-year periods) should be a common year instead of a leap year. Shortly thereafter, Romme was sentenced to the guillotine and committed suicide, and the proposal was never adopted, although [Jérôme Lalande](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J%C3%A9r%C3%B4me_Lalande "Jérôme Lalande") repeatedly proposed it for a number of years. The proposal was intended to avoid uncertain future leap years caused by the inaccurate astronomical knowledge of the 1790s (even today, this statement is still valid due to the uncertainty in [ΔT](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%CE%94T_(timekeeping) "ΔT (timekeeping)")). In particular, the committee noted that the autumnal equinox of year 144 was predicted to occur at 11:59:40 pm [local apparent time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_apparent_time "Local apparent time") in Paris, which was closer to midnight than its inherent 3 to 4 minute uncertainty.

The calendar was abolished by an act dated 22 Fructidor an XIII (9 September 1805) and signed by [Napoleon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Napoleon "Napoleon"), which referred to a report by [Michel-Louis-Étienne Regnaud de Saint-Jean d'Angély](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michel-Louis-%C3%89tienne_Regnaud_de_Saint-Jean_d%27Ang%C3%A9ly "Michel-Louis-Étienne Regnaud de Saint-Jean d'Angély") and [Jean Joseph Mounier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_Joseph_Mounier "Jean Joseph Mounier"), listing two fundamental flaws.

1.   The rule for leap years depended upon the uneven course of the sun, rather than fixed intervals, so that one must consult astronomers to determine when each year started, especially when the equinox happened close to midnight, as the exact moment could not be predicted with certainty.
2.   Both the era and the beginning of the year were chosen to commemorate a historical event that occurred on the first day of autumn in France, whereas the other European nations began the year near the beginning of winter or spring, thus being impediments to the calendar's adoption in Europe and America, and even a part of the French nation, where the Gregorian calendar continued to be used, as it was required for religious purposes.

The report also notes that the 10-day décade was unpopular and had already been suppressed three years earlier in favor of the seven-day week, removing what was considered by some as one of the calendar's main benefits.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Republican_Calendar#cite_note-20) The 10-day décade was unpopular with laborers because they received only one full day of rest out of ten, instead of one in seven, although they also got a half-day off on the fifth day (thus 36 full days and 36 half days in a year, for a total of 54 free days, compared to the usual 52 or 53 Sundays). It also, by design, conflicted with Sunday religious observances.

Another criticism of the calendar was that despite the poetic names of its months, they were tied to the climate and agriculture of [metropolitan France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metropolitan_France "Metropolitan France") and therefore not applicable to [France's overseas territories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Overseas_departments_and_territories_of_France "Overseas departments and territories of France").[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Republican_Calendar#cite_note-21)

The Republican Calendar was abolished in the year XIV (1805). After this year, there are two historically attested calendars which may be used to determine dates. Both calendars gave the same dates for years 17 to 52 (1808–1844), always beginning on 23 September, and it was suggested, but never adopted, that the reformed calendar be implemented during this period, before the Republican Calendar was abolished.

*   _Republican Calendar:_ The only legal calendar during the Republic. The first day of the year, 1 Vendémiaire, is always the day the autumn equinox occurs in Paris. About every 30 years, leap years are 5 years apart instead of 4, as happened between the leap years 15 and 20.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Republican_Calendar#cite_note-24) The lengths of the first 524 years were calculated by [Jean Baptiste Joseph Delambre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_Baptiste_Joseph_Delambre "Jean Baptiste Joseph Delambre").
*   _Reformed Republican Calendar:_ Following a proposal by Delambre in order to make leap years regular and predictable, with leap years being every year divisible by 4, except years divisible by 100 and not by 400. Years divisible by 4000 would also be ordinary years. Intended to be implemented in year 3, the reformed calendar was abandoned after the death of Romme, the head of the calendar committee. This calendar also has the benefit that every year in the third century of the Republican Era (1992–2091) begins on 22 September.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Republican_Calendar#cite_note-25)

### Current date and time

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=French_Republican_calendar&action=edit&section=18 "Edit section: Current date and time")]

For this calendar, Delambre's revised method of calculating leap years is used. Other methods may differ by one day. Time may be cached and therefore not accurate. Decimal time is according to Paris mean time, which is 9 minutes 21 seconds (6.49 decimal minutes) ahead of [Greenwich Mean Time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenwich_Mean_Time "Greenwich Mean Time"). _Décret de la Convention 9 Brumaire An III_ above the entrance to the [ENS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%89cole_Normale_Sup%C3%A9rieure "École Normale Supérieure")

The "[Coup of 18 Brumaire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coup_of_18_Brumaire "Coup of 18 Brumaire")" or "Brumaire" was the [coup d'état](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coup_d%27%C3%A9tat "Coup d'état") of [Napoleon Bonaparte](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Napoleon_Bonaparte "Napoleon Bonaparte") on 18 Brumaire An VIII (9 November 1799), which many historians consider to be the end of the French Revolution. [Karl Marx](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karl_Marx "Karl Marx")'s 1852 essay _[The Eighteenth Brumaire of Louis Bonaparte](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The\_Eighteenth\_Brumaire\_of\_Louis\_Bonaparte "The Eighteenth Brumaire of Louis Bonaparte")_ compares [the coup d'état of 1851](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1851_French_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat "1851 French coup d'état") of [Louis Napoléon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis_Napol%C3%A9on "Louis Napoléon") unfavorably to his uncle's earlier coup, with the statement "History repeats ... first as tragedy, then as farce".

Another famous revolutionary date is [9 Thermidor An II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermidorian_Reaction "Thermidorian Reaction") (27 July 1794), the date the Convention turned against [Maximilien Robespierre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maximilien_Robespierre "Maximilien Robespierre"), who, along with others associated with [the Mountain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Mountain "The Mountain"), was [guillotined](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guillotine "Guillotine") the following day.

[Émile Zola](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%89mile_Zola "Émile Zola")'s novel _[Germinal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germinal\_(novel) "Germinal (novel)")_ takes its name from the calendar's month of Germinal.

The seafood dish [Lobster Thermidor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lobster_Thermidor "Lobster Thermidor") was named after the 1891 play _[Thermidor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermidor\_(play) "Thermidor (play)")_, set during the Revolution.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Republican_Calendar#cite_note-James2006-26)[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Republican_Calendar#cite_note-27)

The French frigates of the [_Floréal_ class](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flor%C3%A9al-class_frigate "Floréal-class frigate") all bear names of Republican months.

The months in [J. R. R. Tolkien](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J._R._R._Tolkien "J. R. R. Tolkien")'s Nûmenorean Calendar are those of the Republican Calendar, translated into Elvish language.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Republican_Calendar#cite_note-28)
