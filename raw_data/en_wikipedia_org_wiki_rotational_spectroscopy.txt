Part of the rotational spectrum of [trifluoroiodomethane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trifluoroiodomethane "Trifluoroiodomethane"), CF

3 I.[[notes 1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy#cite_note-1) Each rotational transition is labeled with the quantum numbers, _J_, of the final and initial states, and is extensively split by the effects of [nuclear quadrupole coupling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_quadrupole_resonance "Nuclear quadrupole resonance") with the 127 I nucleus.

**Rotational spectroscopy** is concerned with the measurement of the energies of transitions between quantized rotational states of [molecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecule "Molecule") in the [gas phase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas_phase "Gas phase"). The **rotational spectrum** ([power spectral density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_spectral_density "Power spectral density") vs. [rotational frequency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_frequency "Rotational frequency")) of [polar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_polarity "Chemical polarity") molecules can be measured in [absorption](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absorption_(optics) "Absorption (optics)") or [emission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emission_(electromagnetic_radiation) "Emission (electromagnetic radiation)") by [microwave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microwave "Microwave") spectroscopy[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy#cite_note-2) or by [far infrared](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Far_infrared "Far infrared") spectroscopy. The rotational spectra of non-polar molecules cannot be observed by those methods, but can be observed and measured by [Raman spectroscopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raman_spectroscopy "Raman spectroscopy"). Rotational spectroscopy is sometimes referred to as _pure_ rotational spectroscopy to distinguish it from [rotational-vibrational spectroscopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational-vibrational_spectroscopy "Rotational-vibrational spectroscopy") where changes in rotational energy occur together with changes in vibrational energy, and also from ro-vibronic spectroscopy (or just [vibronic spectroscopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vibronic_spectroscopy "Vibronic spectroscopy")) where rotational, vibrational and electronic energy changes occur simultaneously.

For rotational spectroscopy, molecules are classified according to symmetry into spherical tops, linear molecules, and symmetric tops; analytical expressions can be derived for the rotational energy terms of these molecules. Analytical expressions can be derived for the fourth category, asymmetric top, for rotational levels up to J=3, but higher [energy levels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_level "Energy level") need to be determined using numerical methods. The rotational energies are derived theoretically by considering the molecules to be [rigid rotors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rigid_rotor "Rigid rotor") and then applying extra terms to account for [centrifugal distortion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centrifugal_force "Centrifugal force"), [fine structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fine_structure "Fine structure"), [hyperfine structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperfine_structure "Hyperfine structure") and [Coriolis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coriolis_force "Coriolis force")[coupling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational%E2%80%93vibrational_coupling "Rotational–vibrational coupling"). Fitting the spectra to the theoretical expressions gives numerical values of the angular [moments of inertia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_of_inertia "Moment of inertia") from which very precise values of molecular bond lengths and angles can be derived in favorable cases. In the presence of an electrostatic field there is [Stark splitting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stark_effect "Stark effect") which allows molecular [electric dipole moments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_dipole_moment "Electric dipole moment") to be determined.

An important application of rotational spectroscopy is in exploration of the chemical composition of the [interstellar medium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_medium "Interstellar medium") using [radio telescopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radio_telescope "Radio telescope").

Rotational spectroscopy has primarily been used to investigate fundamental aspects of molecular physics. It is a uniquely precise tool for the determination of molecular structure in gas-phase molecules. It can be used to establish barriers to internal rotation such as that associated with the rotation of the CH

3 group relative to the C

6 H

4 Cl group in [chlorotoluene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chlorotoluene "Chlorotoluene") (C

7 H

7 Cl).[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy#cite_note-3) When fine or hyperfine structure can be observed, the technique also provides information on the electronic structures of molecules. Much of current understanding of the nature of weak molecular interactions such as [van der Waals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_der_Waals_force "Van der Waals force"), [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_bond "Hydrogen bond") and [halogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halogen_bond "Halogen bond") bonds has been established through rotational spectroscopy. In connection with radio astronomy, the technique has a key role in exploration of the chemical composition of the interstellar medium. Microwave transitions are measured in the laboratory and matched to emissions from the interstellar medium using a [radio telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radio_telescope "Radio telescope"). [NH 3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia "Ammonia") was the first stable [polyatomic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyatomic "Polyatomic") molecule to be identified in the interstellar medium.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy#cite_note-4) The measurement of [chlorine monoxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chlorine_monoxide "Chlorine monoxide")[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy#cite_note-5) is important for [atmospheric chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmospheric_chemistry "Atmospheric chemistry"). Current projects in astrochemistry involve both laboratory microwave spectroscopy and observations made using modern radiotelescopes such as the [Atacama Large Millimeter/submillimeter Array](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atacama_Large_Millimeter_Array "Atacama Large Millimeter Array") (ALMA).[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy#cite_note-6)

A molecule in the [gas phase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas_phase "Gas phase") is free to rotate relative to a set of mutually [orthogonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal "Orthogonal") axes of fixed orientation in space, centered on the [center of mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Center_of_mass "Center of mass") of the molecule. Free rotation is not possible for molecules in liquid or solid phases due to the presence of [intermolecular forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intermolecular_force "Intermolecular force"). Rotation about each unique axis is associated with a set of quantized energy levels dependent on the moment of inertia about that axis and a quantum number. Thus, for linear molecules the energy levels are described by a single moment of inertia and a single quantum number, ![Image 2: {\displaystyle J}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/359e4f407b49910e02c27c2f52e87a36cd74c053), which defines the magnitude of the rotational angular momentum.

For nonlinear molecules which are symmetric rotors (or symmetric tops - see next section), there are two moments of inertia and the energy also depends on a second rotational quantum number, ![Image 3: {\displaystyle K}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b76fce82a62ed5461908f0dc8f037de4e3686b0), which defines the vector component of rotational angular momentum along the [principal symmetry axis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_symmetry "Molecular symmetry").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy#cite_note-7) Analysis of spectroscopic data with the expressions detailed below results in quantitative determination of the value(s) of the moment(s) of inertia. From these precise values of the molecular structure and dimensions may be obtained.

For a linear molecule, analysis of the rotational spectrum provides values for the [rotational constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rigid_rotor#Quantum_mechanical_linear_rigid_rotor "Rigid rotor")[[notes 2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy#cite_note-8) and the moment of inertia of the molecule, and, knowing the atomic masses, can be used to determine the [bond length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bond_length "Bond length") directly. For [diatomic molecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diatomic "Diatomic") this process is straightforward. For linear molecules with more than two atoms it is necessary to measure the spectra of two or more [isotopologues](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotopologue "Isotopologue"), such as 16 O 12 C 32 S and 16 O 12 C 34 S. This allows a set of [simultaneous equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simultaneous_equations "Simultaneous equations") to be set up and solved for the [bond lengths](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bond_length "Bond length")).[[notes 3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy#cite_note-9) A bond length obtained in this way is slightly different from the equilibrium bond length. This is because there is [zero-point energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero-point_energy "Zero-point energy") in the vibrational ground state, to which the rotational states refer, whereas the equilibrium bond length is at the minimum in the potential energy curve. The relation between the rotational constants is given by

![Image 4: {\displaystyle B_{v}=B-\alpha \left(v+{\frac {1}{2}}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d93df412f1dc6b11c5094dff3684c2aea277ae88)
where v is a vibrational quantum number and α is a vibration-rotation interaction constant which can be calculated if the B values for two different vibrational states can be found.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy#cite_note-10)

For other molecules, if the spectra can be resolved and individual transitions assigned both [bond lengths](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bond_length "Bond length") and [bond angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_geometry "Molecular geometry") can be deduced. When this is not possible, as with most asymmetric tops, all that can be done is to fit the spectra to three moments of inertia calculated from an assumed molecular structure. By varying the molecular structure the fit can be improved, giving a qualitative estimate of the structure. Isotopic substitution is invaluable when using this approach to the determination of molecular structure.

### Classification of molecular rotors

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Rotational_spectroscopy&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Classification of molecular rotors")]

In [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics") the free rotation of a molecule is [quantized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum_quantization "Angular momentum quantization"), so that the [rotational energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_energy "Rotational energy") and the [angular momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum "Angular momentum") can take only certain fixed values, which are related simply to the [moment of inertia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_of_inertia "Moment of inertia"), ![Image 5: {\displaystyle I}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/535ea7fc4134a31cbe2251d9d3511374bc41be9f), of the molecule. For any molecule, there are three moments of inertia: ![Image 6: {\displaystyle I_{A}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fad11afeef762471a89c8a258282a40af27d6516), ![Image 7: {\displaystyle I_{B}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2d40597cfee42fba5f2d94723c8332dbc4b3f1c5) and ![Image 8: {\displaystyle I_{C}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fcad0811b1e635343b4ac72fe5b75ac4a160b318) about three mutually orthogonal axes _A_, _B_, and _C_ with the origin at the [center of mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Center_of_mass "Center of mass") of the system. The general convention, used in this article, is to define the axes such that ![Image 9: {\displaystyle I_{A}\leq I_{B}\leq I_{C}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/de7598a10acafbfbfbbaf762b62e58f7d5802658), with axis ![Image 10: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3) corresponding to the smallest moment of inertia. Some authors, however, define the ![Image 11: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3) axis as the molecular [rotation axis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_symmetry#Elements "Molecular symmetry") of highest order.

The particular pattern of [energy levels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_level "Energy level") (and, hence, of transitions in the rotational spectrum) for a molecule is determined by its symmetry. A convenient way to look at the molecules is to divide them into four different classes, based on the symmetry of their structure. These are

Spherical tops (spherical rotors) All three moments of inertia are equal to each other: ![Image 12: {\displaystyle I_{A}=I_{B}=I_{C}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/620546f820349b7dc52961c301d2491ba79944b6). Examples of spherical tops include [phosphorus tetramer (P 4)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allotropes_of_phosphorus#White_phosphorus "Allotropes of phosphorus"), [carbon tetrachloride (CCl 4)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_tetrachloride "Carbon tetrachloride") and other tetrahalides, [methane (CH 4)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane "Methane"), [silane, (SiH 4)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silane "Silane"), [sulfur hexafluoride (SF 6)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_hexafluoride "Sulfur hexafluoride") and other hexahalides. The molecules all belong to the cubic [point groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_point_group "Molecular point group") T d or O h. Linear molecules For a linear molecule the moments of inertia are related by ![Image 13: {\displaystyle I_{A}\ll I_{B}=I_{C}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3bb678a96c9c5fbcd13db08ad5384b7d502b841e). For most purposes, ![Image 14: {\displaystyle I_{A}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fad11afeef762471a89c8a258282a40af27d6516) can be taken to be zero. Examples of linear molecules include [dioxygen (O 2)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen "Oxygen"), [dinitrogen (N 2)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen "Nitrogen"), [carbon monoxide (CO)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide "Carbon monoxide"), [hydroxy radical (OH)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroxyl_radical "Hydroxyl radical"), [carbon dioxide (CO 2)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_dioxide "Carbon dioxide"), [hydrogen cyanide (HCN)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide "Hydrogen cyanide"), [carbonyl sulfide (OCS)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonyl_sulfide "Carbonyl sulfide"), [acetylene (ethyne (HC≡CH)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene "Acetylene") and dihaloethynes. These molecules belong to the point groups C∞v or D∞h. Symmetric tops (symmetric rotors) A symmetric top is a molecule in which two moments of inertia are the same, ![Image 15: {\displaystyle I_{A}=I_{B}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d32cde53707e3c0985c38eb32b0e15cff8dcd87f) or ![Image 16: {\displaystyle I_{B}=I_{C}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/edd813a2fdca36647de4b680f7e9422bd51bd215). By definition a symmetric top must have a 3-fold or higher order [rotation axis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_symmetry#Elements "Molecular symmetry"). As a matter of convenience, spectroscopists divide molecules into two classes of symmetric tops, _[Oblate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oblate\_spheroid "Oblate spheroid") symmetric tops_ (saucer or disc shaped) with ![Image 17: {\displaystyle I_{A}=I_{B}<I_{C}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4d04c34cd7542e648fd0d89eec89c0ed848efeeb) and _[Prolate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prolate "Prolate") symmetric tops_ (rugby football, or cigar shaped) with ![Image 18: {\displaystyle I_{A}<I_{B}=I_{C}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d6beae4408475f38988dc3b831b7417f8c8199d1). The spectra look rather different, and are instantly recognizable. Examples of symmetric tops include [Oblate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oblate_spheroid "Oblate spheroid")[Benzene, C 6 H 6](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene "Benzene"); [ammonia, NH 3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia "Ammonia"); [xenon tetrafluoride, XeF 4](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xenon_tetrafluoride "Xenon tetrafluoride")[Prolate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prolate "Prolate")[Chloromethane, CH 3 Cl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chloromethane "Chloromethane"), [propyne, CH 3 C≡CH](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methylacetylene "Methylacetylene") As a detailed example, ammonia has a moment of inertia _I_ C = 4.4128 × 10−47 kg m 2 about the 3-fold rotation axis, and moments _I_ A = _I_ B = 2.8059 × 10−47 kg m 2 about any axis perpendicular to the C 3 axis. Since the unique moment of inertia is larger than the other two, the molecule is an oblate symmetric top.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy#cite_note-11)Asymmetric tops (asymmetric rotors) The three moments of inertia have different values. Examples of small molecules that are asymmetric tops include [water, H 2 O](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water_(molecule) "Water (molecule)") and [nitrogen dioxide, NO 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen_dioxide "Nitrogen dioxide") whose symmetry axis of highest order is a 2-fold rotation axis. Most large molecules are asymmetric tops. 

#### Microwave and far-infrared spectra

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Rotational_spectroscopy&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Microwave and far-infrared spectra")]

Transitions between rotational states can be observed in molecules with a permanent [electric dipole moment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_dipole_moment "Electric dipole moment").[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy#cite_note-12)[[notes 4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy#cite_note-13) A consequence of this rule is that no microwave spectrum can be observed for centrosymmetric linear molecules such as N

2 ([dinitrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dinitrogen "Dinitrogen")) or HCCH ([ethyne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethyne "Ethyne")), which are non-polar. Tetrahedral molecules such as CH

4 ([methane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane "Methane")), which have both a zero dipole moment and isotropic polarizability, would not have a pure rotation spectrum but for the effect of centrifugal distortion; when the molecule rotates about a 3-fold symmetry axis a small dipole moment is created, allowing a weak rotation spectrum to be observed by microwave spectroscopy.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy#cite_note-14)

With symmetric tops, the selection rule for electric-dipole-allowed pure rotation transitions is Δ _K_ = 0, Δ _J_ = ±1. Since these transitions are due to absorption (or emission) of a single photon with a spin of one, [conservation of angular momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservation_of_angular_momentum "Conservation of angular momentum") implies that the molecular angular momentum can change by at most one unit.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy#cite_note-15) Moreover, the quantum number _K_ is limited to have values between and including +_J_ to -_J_.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy#cite_note-16)

For [Raman spectra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raman_spectroscopy "Raman spectroscopy") the molecules undergo transitions in which an _incident_ photon is absorbed and another _scattered_ photon is emitted. The general selection rule for such a transition to be allowed is that the molecular [polarizability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polarizability "Polarizability") must be [anisotropic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anisotropic "Anisotropic"), which means that it is not the same in all directions.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy#cite_note-17) Polarizability is a 3-dimensional [tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor "Tensor") that can be represented as an ellipsoid. The polarizability ellipsoid of spherical top molecules is in fact spherical so those molecules show no rotational Raman spectrum. For all other molecules both [Stokes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stokes_line "Stokes line") and anti-Stokes lines[[notes 5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy#cite_note-18) can be observed and they have similar intensities due to the fact that many rotational states are thermally populated. The selection rule for linear molecules is ΔJ = 0, ±2. The reason for the values ±2 is that the polarizability returns to the same value twice during a rotation.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy#cite_note-19) The value ΔJ = 0 does not correspond to a molecular transition but rather to [Rayleigh scattering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rayleigh_scattering "Rayleigh scattering") in which the incident photon merely changes direction.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy#cite_note-Banwell_1994_loc=Section_4.2,_p._105,_Pure_Rotational_Raman_Spectra-20)

The selection rule for symmetric top molecules is

Δ _K_ = 0 If _K_ = 0, then Δ _J_ = ±2 If _K_ ≠ 0, then Δ _J_ = 0, ±1, ±2
Transitions with Δ _J_ = +1 are said to belong to the _R_ series, whereas transitions with Δ _J_ = +2 belong to an _S_ series.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy#cite_note-Banwell_1994_loc=Section_4.2,_p._105,_Pure_Rotational_Raman_Spectra-20) Since Raman transitions involve two photons, it is possible for the molecular angular momentum to change by two units.

The units used for rotational constants depend on the type of measurement. With infrared spectra in the [wavenumber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wavenumber "Wavenumber") scale (![Image 19: {\displaystyle {\tilde {\nu }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d6b66a3de6d14b026bff87c519315d260be4c97)), the unit is usually the [inverse centimeter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverse_centimeter "Inverse centimeter"), written as cm−1, which is literally the number of waves in one centimeter, or the reciprocal of the wavelength in centimeters (![Image 20: {\displaystyle {\tilde {\nu }}=1/\lambda }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/83da212fdf6fc43ea48c1863d9f648187727291a)). On the other hand, for microwave spectra in the frequency scale (![Image 21: {\displaystyle \nu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c15bbbb971240cf328aba572178f091684585468)), the unit is usually the [gigahertz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gigahertz "Gigahertz"). The relationship between these two units is derived from the expression

![Image 22: {\displaystyle \nu \cdot \lambda =c,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/808420776e74fac25f1eedcb4fa7b411a6c62c5c)
where ν is a [frequency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frequency "Frequency"), λ is a [wavelength](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wavelength "Wavelength") and _c_ is the [velocity of light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity_of_light "Velocity of light"). It follows that

### Effect of vibration on rotation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Rotational_spectroscopy&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Effect of vibration on rotation")]

The population of vibrationally excited states follows a [Boltzmann distribution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boltzmann_distribution "Boltzmann distribution"), so low-frequency vibrational states are appreciably populated even at room temperatures. As the moment of inertia is higher when a vibration is excited, the rotational constants (_B_) decrease. Consequently, the rotation frequencies in each vibration state are different from each other. This can give rise to "satellite" lines in the rotational spectrum. An example is provided by [cyanodiacetylene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyanodiacetylene "Cyanodiacetylene"), H−C≡C−C≡C−C≡N.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy#cite_note-21)

Further, there is a [fictitious force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fictitious_force "Fictitious force"), [Coriolis coupling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coriolis_effect "Coriolis effect"), between the vibrational motion of the nuclei in the rotating (non-inertial) frame. However, as long as the vibrational quantum number does not change (i.e., the molecule is in only one state of vibration), the effect of vibration on rotation is not important, because the time for vibration is much shorter than the time required for rotation. The Coriolis coupling is often negligible, too, if one is interested in low vibrational and rotational quantum numbers only.

### Effect of rotation on vibrational spectra

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Rotational_spectroscopy&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Effect of rotation on vibrational spectra")]

Historically, the theory of rotational energy levels was developed to account for observations of vibration-rotation spectra of gases in [infrared spectroscopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infrared_spectroscopy "Infrared spectroscopy"), which was used before microwave spectroscopy had become practical. To a first approximation, the rotation and vibration can be treated as [separable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separable_partial_differential_equation "Separable partial differential equation"), so the energy of rotation is added to the energy of vibration. In this approximation, the vibration-rotation wavenumbers of transitions are

where ![Image 27: {\displaystyle B''}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c2036173fa79837095a1b1ca4f95138837d9b1f4) and ![Image 28: {\displaystyle B'}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8b144e466a3757b4706daf24a45d4f1266700016) are rotational constants for the upper and lower vibrational state respectively, while ![Image 29: {\displaystyle J''}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ec133a827a3cecaf5299d1fcad5053868439056a) and ![Image 30: {\displaystyle J'}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c76d5f08e40c83bfc959e27a75dd387278214e9c) are the rotational quantum numbers of the upper and lower levels. In reality, this expression has to be modified for the effects of anharmonicity of the vibrations, for centrifugal distortion and for Coriolis coupling.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy#cite_note-22)

For the so-called _R_ branch of the spectrum, ![Image 31: {\displaystyle J'=J''+1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/84304bb0b016ef8be1971a3f6617d2331dc0cfcb) so that there is simultaneous excitation of both vibration and rotation. For the _P_ branch, ![Image 32: {\displaystyle J'=J''-1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b27d326aaebff1e637d43e839614fabc927e2a16) so that a quantum of rotational energy is lost while a quantum of vibrational energy is gained. The purely vibrational transition, ![Image 33: {\displaystyle \Delta J=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4e75f16531aead77f8f6cb649637b30188f0b570), gives rise to the _Q_ branch of the spectrum. Because of the thermal population of the rotational states the _P_ branch is slightly less intense than the _R_ branch.

Rotational constants obtained from infrared measurements are in good accord with those obtained by microwave spectroscopy, while the latter usually offers greater precision.

Structure of rotational spectra
-------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Rotational_spectroscopy&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Structure of rotational spectra")]

Spherical top molecules have no net dipole moment. A pure rotational spectrum cannot be observed by absorption or emission spectroscopy because there is no permanent dipole moment whose rotation can be accelerated by the electric field of an incident photon. Also the polarizability is isotropic, so that pure rotational transitions cannot be observed by Raman spectroscopy either. Nevertheless, rotational constants can be obtained by [ro–vibrational spectroscopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational%E2%80%93vibrational_coupling "Rotational–vibrational coupling"). This occurs when a molecule is polar in the vibrationally excited state. For example, the molecule [methane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane "Methane") is a spherical top but the asymmetric C-H stretching band shows rotational fine structure in the infrared spectrum, illustrated in [rovibrational coupling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational%E2%80%93vibrational_coupling "Rotational–vibrational coupling"). This spectrum is also interesting because it shows clear evidence of [Coriolis coupling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coriolis_effect "Coriolis effect") in the asymmetric structure of the band.

[![Image 34](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Rotational_spectrum_example.png/330px-Rotational_spectrum_example.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Rotational_spectrum_example.png)

Energy levels and line positions calculated in the rigid rotor approximation

The [rigid rotor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rigid_rotor "Rigid rotor") is a good starting point from which to construct a model of a rotating molecule. It is assumed that component atoms are [point masses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_particle "Point particle") connected by rigid bonds. A linear molecule lies on a single axis and each atom moves on the surface of a sphere around the centre of mass. The two degrees of rotational freedom correspond to the [spherical coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_coordinates "Spherical coordinates") θ and φ which describe the direction of the molecular axis, and the quantum state is determined by two quantum numbers J and M. J defines the magnitude of the rotational angular momentum, and M its component about an axis fixed in space, such as an external electric or magnetic field. In the absence of external fields, the energy depends only on J. Under the [rigid rotor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rigid_rotor "Rigid rotor") model, the rotational energy levels, _F_(J), of the molecule can be expressed as,

where ![Image 36: {\displaystyle B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47136aad860d145f75f3eed3022df827cee94d7a) is the rotational constant of the molecule and is related to the moment of inertia of the molecule. In a linear molecule the moment of inertia about an axis perpendicular to the molecular axis is unique, that is, ![Image 37: {\displaystyle I_{B}=I_{C},I_{A}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2fa2a25f86c376c76530bb91eeeef13a1e5342a5), so

![Image 38: {\displaystyle B={h \over {8\pi ^{2}cI_{B}}}={h \over {8\pi ^{2}cI_{C}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5002140bf4c7609a4321add2ca825f5d0f815175)
For a diatomic molecule

Thus, the locations of the lines in a rotational spectrum will be given by

The probability of a transition taking place is the most important factor influencing the intensity of an observed rotational line. This probability is proportional to the population of the initial state involved in the transition. The population of a rotational state depends on two factors. The number of molecules in an excited state with quantum number _J_, relative to the number of molecules in the ground state, _N J_/_N_ 0 is given by the [Boltzmann distribution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boltzmann_distribution "Boltzmann distribution") as

![Image 47: {\displaystyle {\frac {N_{J}}{N_{0}}}=e^{-{\frac {E_{J}}{kT}}}=e^{-{\frac {BhcJ(J+1)}{kT}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf926de4a4927526674dfaa7a9c0b25d42213e32),

This factor decreases as _J_ increases. The second factor is the [degeneracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degenerate_energy_levels "Degenerate energy levels") of the rotational state, which is equal to 2J + 1. This factor increases as _J_ increases. Combining the two factors[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy#cite_note-23)

#### Centrifugal distortion

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Rotational_spectroscopy&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Centrifugal distortion")]

When a molecule rotates, the [centrifugal force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centrifugal_force "Centrifugal force") pulls the atoms apart. As a result, the moment of inertia of the molecule increases, thus decreasing the value of ![Image 50: {\displaystyle B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47136aad860d145f75f3eed3022df827cee94d7a), when it is calculated using the expression for the rigid rotor. To account for this a centrifugal distortion correction term is added to the rotational energy levels of the diatomic molecule.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy#cite_note-Ban45-26)

![Image 51: {\displaystyle F\left(J\right)=BJ\left(J+1\right)-DJ^{2}\left(J+1\right)^{2}\qquad J=0,1,2,...}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c5d12a27d6d31aadadd95b252ec61352c7621c3d)
where ![Image 52: {\displaystyle D}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f34a0c600395e5d4345287e21fb26efd386990e6) is the centrifugal distortion constant.

Therefore, the line positions for the rotational mode change to

![Image 53: {\displaystyle {\tilde {\nu }}_{J^{\prime }\leftrightarrow J^{\prime \prime }}=2B\left(J^{\prime \prime }+1\right)-4D\left(J^{\prime \prime }+1\right)^{3}\qquad J^{\prime \prime }=0,1,2,...}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/affefab6c140b11d3721d02fc4338f8d7c9f67fb)
In consequence, the spacing between lines is not constant, as in the rigid rotor approximation, but decreases with increasing rotational quantum number.

An assumption underlying these expressions is that the molecular vibration follows [simple harmonic motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simple_harmonic_motion "Simple harmonic motion"). In the harmonic approximation the centrifugal constant ![Image 54: {\displaystyle D}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f34a0c600395e5d4345287e21fb26efd386990e6) can be derived as

where _k_ is the vibrational [force constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Force_constant "Force constant"). The relationship between ![Image 56: {\displaystyle B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47136aad860d145f75f3eed3022df827cee94d7a) and ![Image 57: {\displaystyle D}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f34a0c600395e5d4345287e21fb26efd386990e6)

![Image 58: {\displaystyle D={\frac {4B^{3}}{{\tilde {\omega }}^{2}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ee1ade666616d3485ce5c2aa97fc8d5c9cbba547)
where ![Image 59: {\displaystyle {\tilde {\omega }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e60cbeebb31255ddce04c7526d2631bfd49fb8bf) is the harmonic vibration frequency, follows. If anharmonicity is to be taken into account, terms in higher powers of J should be added to the expressions for the energy levels and line positions.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy#cite_note-Ban45-26) A striking example concerns the rotational spectrum of [hydrogen fluoride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_fluoride "Hydrogen fluoride") which was fitted to terms up to _[J(J+1)]5_.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy#cite_note-27)

The electric dipole moment of the dioxygen molecule, O

2 is zero, but the molecule is [paramagnetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paramagnetic "Paramagnetic") with two unpaired electrons so that there are magnetic-dipole allowed transitions which can be observed by microwave spectroscopy. The unit electron spin has three spatial orientations with respect to the given molecular rotational angular momentum vector, K, so that each rotational level is split into three states, J = K + 1, K, and K - 1, each J state of this so-called p-type triplet arising from a different orientation of the spin with respect to the rotational motion of the molecule. The energy difference between successive J terms in any of these triplets is about 2 cm−1 (60 GHz), with the single exception of J = 1←0 difference which is about 4 cm−1. Selection rules for magnetic dipole transitions allow transitions between successive members of the triplet (ΔJ = ±1) so that for each value of the rotational angular momentum quantum number K there are two allowed transitions. The 16 O nucleus has zero nuclear spin angular momentum, so that symmetry considerations demand that K have only odd values.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy#cite_note-28)[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy#cite_note-29)

For symmetric rotors a quantum number _J_ is associated with the total angular momentum of the molecule. For a given value of J, there is a 2 _J_+1- fold degeneracy with the quantum number, _M_ taking the values +_J_ ...0 ... -_J_. The third quantum number, _K_ is associated with rotation about the [principal rotation axis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_symmetry "Molecular symmetry") of the molecule. In the absence of an external electrical field, the rotational energy of a symmetric top is a function of only J and K and, in the rigid rotor approximation, the energy of each rotational state is given by

![Image 60: {\displaystyle F\left(J,K\right)=BJ\left(J+1\right)+\left(A-B\right)K^{2}\qquad J=0,1,2,\ldots \quad {\mbox{and}}\quad K=+J,\ldots ,0,\ldots ,-J}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1f8ab557deff582c6c27409a2a65b760a23cb444)
where ![Image 61: {\displaystyle B={h \over {8\pi ^{2}cI_{B}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6d7067b19d02b50800007964dd5127c216037298) and ![Image 62: {\displaystyle A={h \over {8\pi ^{2}cI_{A}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b59e8b45a069f1a21d5309f8246fdbc86a50af53) for a _prolate_ symmetric top molecule or ![Image 63: {\displaystyle A={h \over {8\pi ^{2}cI_{C}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/52d0f07e17fe35afe4dcd041267ee9905f9a0882) for an _oblate_ molecule.

The quantum number _J_ refers to the total angular momentum, as before. Since there are three independent moments of inertia, there are two other independent quantum numbers to consider, but the term values for an asymmetric rotor cannot be derived in closed form. They are obtained by individual [matrix diagonalization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diagonalizable_matrix#Diagonalization "Diagonalizable matrix") for each _J_ value. Formulae are available for molecules whose shape approximates to that of a symmetric top.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy#cite_note-32)

The water molecule is an important example of an asymmetric top. It has an intense pure rotation spectrum in the far infrared region, below about 200 cm−1. For this reason far infrared spectrometers have to be freed of atmospheric water vapour either by purging with a dry gas or by evacuation. The spectrum has been analyzed in detail.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy#cite_note-33)

Quadrupole splitting
--------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Rotational_spectroscopy&action=edit&section=18 "Edit section: Quadrupole splitting")]

When a nucleus has a [spin quantum number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_quantum_number "Spin quantum number"), _I_, greater than 1/2 it has a [quadrupole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrupole "Quadrupole") moment. In that case, coupling of nuclear spin angular momentum with rotational angular momentum causes splitting of the rotational energy levels. If the [quantum number _J_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Total_angular_momentum "Total angular momentum") of a rotational level is greater than _I_, 2 _I_ + 1 levels are produced; but if _J_ is less than _I_, 2 _J_ + 1 levels result. The effect is one type of [hyperfine splitting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperfine_splitting "Hyperfine splitting"). For example, with 14 N (_I_ = 1) in HCN, all levels with J > 0 are split into 3. The energies of the sub-levels are proportional to the [nuclear quadrupole moment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrupole_moment "Quadrupole moment") and a function of _F_ and _J_. where _F_ = _J_ + _I_, _J_ + _I_ − 1, …, |_J_ − _I_|. Thus, observation of nuclear quadrupole splitting permits the magnitude of the nuclear quadrupole moment to be determined.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy#cite_note-34) This is an alternative method to the use of [nuclear quadrupole resonance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_quadrupole_resonance "Nuclear quadrupole resonance") spectroscopy. The selection rule for rotational transitions becomes[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy#cite_note-35)

![Image 67: {\displaystyle \Delta J=\pm 1,\Delta F=0,\pm 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3e2e79aafd0b905f859682ec6013d2bcc7d701c)

Stark and Zeeman effects
------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Rotational_spectroscopy&action=edit&section=19 "Edit section: Stark and Zeeman effects")]

In the presence of a static external [electric field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_field "Electric field") the 2 _J_ + 1 degeneracy of each rotational state is partly removed, an instance of a [Stark effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stark_effect "Stark effect"). For example, in linear molecules each energy level is split into _J_ + 1 components. The extent of splitting depends on the square of the electric field strength and the square of the dipole moment of the molecule.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy#cite_note-36) In principle this provides a means to determine the value of the molecular dipole moment with high precision. Examples include [carbonyl sulfide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonyl_sulfide "Carbonyl sulfide"), OCS, with μ = 0.71521 ± 0.00020 [debye](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Debye_(unit) "Debye (unit)"). However, because the splitting depends on μ 2, the orientation of the dipole must be deduced from quantum mechanical considerations.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy#cite_note-37)

A similar removal of degeneracy will occur when a [paramagnetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paramagnetic "Paramagnetic") molecule is placed in a magnetic field, an instance of the [Zeeman effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zeeman_effect "Zeeman effect"). Most species which can be observed in the gaseous state are [diamagnetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diamagnetic "Diamagnetic") . Exceptions are odd-electron molecules such as [nitric oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitric_oxide "Nitric oxide"), NO, [nitrogen dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen_dioxide "Nitrogen dioxide"), NO

2, some [chlorine oxides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chlorine_oxide "Chlorine oxide") and the [hydroxyl radical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroxyl_radical "Hydroxyl radical"). The Zeeman effect has been observed with [dioxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dioxygen "Dioxygen"), O

2[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy#cite_note-38)

Rotational Raman spectroscopy
-----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Rotational_spectroscopy&action=edit&section=20 "Edit section: Rotational Raman spectroscopy")]

Molecular rotational transitions can also be observed by [Raman spectroscopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raman_spectroscopy "Raman spectroscopy"). Rotational transitions are Raman-allowed for any molecule with an [anisotropic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anisotropic "Anisotropic")[polarizability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polarizability "Polarizability") which includes all molecules except for spherical tops. This means that rotational transitions of molecules with no permanent dipole moment, which cannot be observed in absorption or emission, can be observed, by scattering, in Raman spectroscopy. Very high resolution Raman spectra can be obtained by adapting a [Fourier Transform Infrared Spectrometer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourier_transform_infrared_spectroscopy "Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy"). An example is the spectrum of 15

N

2. It shows the effect of nuclear spin, resulting in intensities variation of 3:1 in adjacent lines. A bond length of 109.9985 ± 0.0010 pm was deduced from the data.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy#cite_note-39)

Instruments and methods
-----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Rotational_spectroscopy&action=edit&section=21 "Edit section: Instruments and methods")]

The great majority of contemporary spectrometers use a mixture of commercially available and bespoke components which users integrate according to their particular needs. Instruments can be broadly categorised according to their general operating principles. Although rotational transitions can be found across a very broad region of the [electromagnetic spectrum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum "Electromagnetic spectrum"), fundamental physical constraints exist on the operational bandwidth of instrument components. It is often impractical and costly to switch to measurements within an entirely different frequency region. The instruments and operating principals described below are generally appropriate to microwave spectroscopy experiments conducted at frequencies between 6 and 24 GHz.

### Absorption cells and Stark modulation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Rotational_spectroscopy&action=edit&section=22 "Edit section: Absorption cells and Stark modulation")]

A microwave spectrometer can be most simply constructed using a source of microwave radiation, an absorption cell into which sample gas can be introduced and a detector such as a [superheterodyne receiver](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superheterodyne_receiver "Superheterodyne receiver"). A spectrum can be obtained by sweeping the frequency of the source while detecting the intensity of transmitted radiation. A simple section of [waveguide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Waveguide "Waveguide") can serve as an absorption cell. An important variation of the technique in which an alternating current is applied across electrodes within the absorption cell results in a modulation of the frequencies of rotational transitions. This is referred to as Stark modulation and allows the use of [phase-sensitive detection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lock-in_amplifier "Lock-in amplifier") methods offering improved sensitivity. Absorption spectroscopy allows the study of samples that are thermodynamically stable at room temperature. The first study of the [microwave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microwave "Microwave") spectrum of a molecule (NH

3) was performed by Cleeton & Williams in 1934.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy#cite_note-Cleeton-40) Subsequent experiments exploited powerful sources of [microwaves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microwave "Microwave") such as the [klystron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Klystron "Klystron"), many of which were developed for [radar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radar "Radar") during the [Second World War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II "World War II"). The number of experiments in microwave spectroscopy surged immediately after the war. By 1948, [Walter Gordy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Walter_Gordy "Walter Gordy") was able to prepare a review of the results contained in approximately 100 research papers.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy#cite_note-41) Commercial versions[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy#cite_note-42) of microwave absorption spectrometer were developed by [Hewlett-Packard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hewlett-Packard "Hewlett-Packard") in the 1970s and were once widely used for fundamental research. Most research laboratories now exploit either Balle-[Flygare](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Willis_H._Flygare "Willis H. Flygare") or chirped-pulse Fourier transform microwave (FTMW) spectrometers.

### Fourier transform microwave (FTMW) spectroscopy

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Rotational_spectroscopy&action=edit&section=23 "Edit section: Fourier transform microwave (FTMW) spectroscopy")]

The theoretical framework[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy#cite_note-43) underpinning FTMW spectroscopy is analogous to that used to describe [FT-NMR spectroscopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NMR_spectroscopy "NMR spectroscopy"). The behaviour of the evolving system is described by optical [Bloch equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bloch_equations "Bloch equations"). First, a short (typically 0-3 microsecond duration) microwave pulse is introduced on resonance with a rotational transition. Those molecules that absorb the energy from this pulse are induced to rotate coherently in phase with the incident radiation. De-activation of the polarisation pulse is followed by microwave emission that accompanies decoherence of the molecular ensemble. This [free induction decay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_induction_decay "Free induction decay") occurs on a timescale of 1-100 microseconds depending on instrument settings. Following pioneering work by Dicke and co-workers in the 1950s,[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy#cite_note-44) the first FTMW spectrometer was constructed by Ekkers and [Flygare](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Willis_H._Flygare "Willis H. Flygare") in 1975.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy#cite_note-45)

#### Balle–Flygare FTMW spectrometer

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Rotational_spectroscopy&action=edit&section=24 "Edit section: Balle–Flygare FTMW spectrometer")]

Balle, Campbell, Keenan and Flygare demonstrated that the FTMW technique can be applied within a "free space cell" comprising an evacuated chamber containing a [Fabry-Perot cavity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optical_cavity "Optical cavity").[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy#cite_note-46) This technique allows a sample to be probed only milliseconds after it undergoes rapid cooling to only a few [kelvins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvin "Kelvin") in the throat of an expanding gas jet. This was a revolutionary development because (i) cooling molecules to low temperatures concentrates the available population in the lowest rotational energy levels. Coupled with benefits conferred by the use of a Fabry-Perot cavity, this brought a great enhancement in the sensitivity and resolution of spectrometers along with a reduction in the complexity of observed spectra; (ii) it became possible to isolate and study molecules that are very weakly bound because there is insufficient energy available for them to undergo fragmentation or chemical reaction at such low temperatures. [William Klemperer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Klemperer "William Klemperer") was a pioneer in using this instrument for the exploration of weakly bound interactions. While the Fabry-Perot cavity of a Balle-Flygare FTMW spectrometer can typically be tuned into resonance at any frequency between 6 and 18 GHz, the bandwidth of individual measurements is restricted to about 1 MHz. An animation illustrates the operation of this instrument which is currently the most widely used tool for microwave spectroscopy.[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy#cite_note-47)

#### Chirped-Pulse FTMW spectrometer

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Rotational_spectroscopy&action=edit&section=25 "Edit section: Chirped-Pulse FTMW spectrometer")]

Noting that digitisers and related electronics technology had significantly progressed since the inception of FTMW spectroscopy, [B.H. Pate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brooks_Pate "Brooks Pate") at the University of Virginia[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy#cite_note-48) designed a spectrometer[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy#cite_note-49) which retains many advantages of the Balle-Flygare FT-MW spectrometer while innovating in (i) the use of a high speed (>4 GS/s) arbitrary waveform generator to generate a "chirped" microwave polarisation pulse that sweeps up to 12 GHz in frequency in less than a microsecond and (ii) the use of a high speed (>40 GS/s) oscilloscope to digitise and Fourier transform the molecular free induction decay. The result is an instrument that allows the study of weakly bound molecules but which is able to exploit a measurement bandwidth (12 GHz) that is greatly enhanced compared with the Balle-Flygare FTMW spectrometer. Modified versions of the original CP-FTMW spectrometer have been constructed by a number of groups in the United States, Canada and Europe.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy#cite_note-50)[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy#cite_note-51) The instrument offers a broadband capability that is highly complementary to the high sensitivity and resolution offered by the Balle-Flygare design.
