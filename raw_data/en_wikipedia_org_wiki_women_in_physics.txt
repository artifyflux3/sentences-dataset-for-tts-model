This article discusses women who have made an important contribution to the field of [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics").

International physics awards
----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Women_in_physics&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: International physics awards")]

Five women have won the [Nobel Prize in Physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physics "Nobel Prize in Physics"), awarded annually since 1901 by the [Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Swedish_Academy_of_Sciences "Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-TeshWade2017-1) These are:[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-2)

*   1903 [Marie Curie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marie_Curie "Marie Curie"): "in recognition of the extraordinary services they have rendered by their joint researches on the [radiation phenomena](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radioactive_decay "Radioactive decay") discovered by Professor [Henri Becquerel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Becquerel "Henri Becquerel")" [[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-3)
*   1963 [Maria Goeppert Mayer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maria_Goeppert_Mayer "Maria Goeppert Mayer"): "for their discoveries concerning [nuclear shell structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_shell_model "Nuclear shell model")" [[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-4)
*   2018 [Donna Strickland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Donna_Strickland "Donna Strickland"): "for their method [high-intensity, ultra-short optical pulses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chirped_pulse_amplification "Chirped pulse amplification")" [[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-5)
*   2020 [Andrea Ghez](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andrea_Ghez "Andrea Ghez"): "for the discovery of a [supermassive compact object](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sagittarius_A* "Sagittarius A*") at the centre of our galaxy."[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-6)
*   2023 [Anne L'Huillier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anne_L%27Huillier "Anne L'Huillier") "for experimental methods that generate attosecond pulses of light for the study of electron dynamics in matter."[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-7)

Marie Curie was the first woman to be nominated in 1902 and to receive the prize in 1903 and shared 1/2 of the prize with her husband [Pierre Curie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre_Curie "Pierre Curie") for their joint work on [radioactivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radioactive_decay "Radioactive decay"), discovered by Henri Becquerel who got the other half of the prize. Marie Curie was the first woman to also receive the [Nobel Prize in Chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Chemistry "Nobel Prize in Chemistry") in 1911, making her the first person to win two Nobel prizes and, as of 2023, the only person to be awarded two Nobel prizes in two different scientific categories.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-nobelprize1911-8)

Maria Goeppert Mayer became the second woman to win the prize in 1963, for the theoretical development of the [nuclear shell model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_shell_model "Nuclear shell model"), a half of the prize shared with [J. Hans D. Jensen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J._Hans_D._Jensen "J. Hans D. Jensen") (the other half given to [Eugene Wigner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eugene_Wigner "Eugene Wigner")). Donna Strickland shared half of the prize in 2018 with [Gérard Mourou](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G%C3%A9rard_Mourou "Gérard Mourou"), for their work in [chirped pulse amplification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chirped_pulse_amplification "Chirped pulse amplification") beginning in the 1980s (the other half given to [Arthur Ashkin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arthur_Ashkin "Arthur Ashkin")). Andrea Ghez was the fourth female Nobel laureate in 2020, she shared one half of the prize with [Reinhard Genzel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reinhard_Genzel "Reinhard Genzel") for the discovery of the supermassive compact object [Sagittarius A*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sagittarius_A* "Sagittarius A*") at the center of our galaxy (the other half given to [Roger Penrose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roger_Penrose "Roger Penrose")). In 2023, Anne L'Huillier shared the prize in equal parts with [Pierre Agostini](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre_Agostini "Pierre Agostini") and [Ferenc Krausz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ferenc_Krausz "Ferenc Krausz") for their experimental contribution and development of [attosecond physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Attosecond_physics "Attosecond physics"). L'Huillier is the first female laureate to receive 1/3 of monetary award of the Nobel Prize in Physics (Curie, Goeppert–Mayer, Strickland and Ghez received 1/4).

Physicists and physicochemists that won a Nobel Prize in Chemistry include Marie Curie,[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-Nomination_Archive_%E2%80%93_Marie_Curie-9)[Irène Joliot-Curie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ir%C3%A8ne_Joliot-Curie "Irène Joliot-Curie"), daughter of Marie Curie, in 1935,[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-nobelprize.org2-10) and [Dorothy Hodgkin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dorothy_Hodgkin "Dorothy Hodgkin") in 1964.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-ReferenceA2-11) Nuclear physicist [Rosalyn Sussman Yalow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rosalyn_Sussman_Yalow "Rosalyn Sussman Yalow") was the second female scientist to win the [Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physiology_or_Medicine "Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine") in 1977 for the development of [radioimmunoassays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radioimmunoassay "Radioimmunoassay").[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-12) Human right activist and [2023 Nobel Peace Prize](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2023_Nobel_Peace_Prize "2023 Nobel Peace Prize"), [Narges Mohammadi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Narges_Mohammadi "Narges Mohammadi"), was trained in [nuclear physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_physics "Nuclear physics").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-13)

#### Nobel nominees and nominators

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Women_in_physics&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Nobel nominees and nominators")]

According to the Nobel archives (updated up to 1970), other [physicists that were nominated to the Nobel Prize in Physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_female_nominees_for_the_Nobel_Prize "List of female nominees for the Nobel Prize") but did not receive it, include:

*   [Lise Meitner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lise_Meitner "Lise Meitner"), nominated 21 times;[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-Nomination_Archive_%E2%80%93_Lise_Meitner-14)
*   [Chien-Shiung Wu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chien-Shiung_Wu "Chien-Shiung Wu"), nominated 5 times;[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-15)
*   [Marietta Blau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marietta_Blau "Marietta Blau"), nominated 3 times;[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-Nomination_Archive_%E2%80%93_Marietta_Blau-16)
*   and [Hertha Wambacher](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hertha_Wambacher "Hertha Wambacher"),[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-17)[Margaret Burbidge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Margaret_Burbidge "Margaret Burbidge")[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-18) and [Janine Connes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Janine_Connes "Janine Connes"), nominated once.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-19)

Irène Joliot-Curie[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-nobelprize.org2-10) and Dorothy Hodgkin[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-ReferenceA2-11) were also nominated for the Nobel Prize in Physics, but received a Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 1935 and 1964, respectively. Lise Meitner is the female physicist the most nominated, 16 times for Physics and 14 times for Chemistry.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-:4-20) About 1.7% of the Nobel nominations in Physics up to 1970 were women.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-:4-20)

Aside from the named above, other physicists and [physicochemists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_chemistry "Physical chemistry") that were nominated to the Nobel Prize in Chemistry but dit not receive it, include [Ida Noddack](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ida_Noddack "Ida Noddack"),[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-21)[Marguerite Perey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marguerite_Perey "Marguerite Perey"),[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-22)[Alberte Pullman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alberte_Pullman "Alberte Pullman"),[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-23) and [Erika Cremer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erika_Cremer "Erika Cremer").[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-24)

Up to 1970, eight female scientists have participated as nominators for the Nobel Prize in Physics. These are Marie Curie, [Hertha Sponer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hertha_Sponer "Hertha Sponer"), [Marie-Antoinette Tonnelat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marie-Antoinette_Tonnelat "Marie-Antoinette Tonnelat"), [Anne Barbara Underhill](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anne_Barbara_Underhill "Anne Barbara Underhill"), [Katharina Boll-Dornberger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Katharina_Boll-Dornberger "Katharina Boll-Dornberger"), Maria Goeppert Mayer, Dorothy Hodgkin, and Margaret Burbidge.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-25)

Several women have been selected as [Clarivate Citation laureates in Physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_Clarivate_Citation_laureates_in_Physics "List of Clarivate Citation laureates in Physics"), which makes an annual list of possible candidates for the Nobel Prize in Physics based on citation statistics, these include:

*   2008 [Vera Rubin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vera_Rubin "Vera Rubin") † "for her pioneering research indicating the existence of [dark matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter "Dark matter") in the [universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe "Universe")."[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-26)
*   2012 [Lene Hau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lene_Hau "Lene Hau") "for the experimental demonstration of [electromagnetically induced transparency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetically_induced_transparency "Electromagnetically induced transparency") '[slow light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slow_light "Slow light")' (with [Stephen E. Harris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stephen_E._Harris "Stephen E. Harris"))."[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-27)
*   2015 [Deborah S. Jin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deborah_S._Jin "Deborah S. Jin") † "for pioneering research on atomic gases at ultra-cold temperatures and the creation of the first [fermionic condensate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermionic_condensate "Fermionic condensate")."[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-28)
*   2018 [Sandra Faber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sandra_Faber "Sandra Faber") "for pioneering methods to determine the [age, size and distance of galaxies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_formation_and_evolution "Galaxy formation and evolution") and for other contributions to [cosmology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmology "Cosmology")."[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-29)
*   2023 [Sharon Glotzer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sharon_Glotzer "Sharon Glotzer") "for demonstrating the role of entropy in the self-assembly of matter and for introducing strategies to control the assembly process to engineer new materials."[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-30)

†: deceased, no longer eligible.

Two women have been awarded the [Wolf Prize in Physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolf_Prize_in_Physics "Wolf Prize in Physics"), awarded by the [Wolf Foundation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolf_Foundation "Wolf Foundation") in Israel since 1978. They are:

*   1978 [Chien-Shiung Wu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chien-Shiung_Wu "Chien-Shiung Wu"), "for her explorations of the [weak interaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weak_interaction "Weak interaction"), helping establish the precise form and the [non-conservation of parity for this natural force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parity_(physics)#Parity_violation "Parity (physics)")."[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-31)
*   2022 Anne L'Huillier, "for pioneering contributions to ultrafast laser science and attosecond physics".[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-32)

Women who have been awarded the [Breakthrough Prize in Fundamental Physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Breakthrough_Prize_in_Fundamental_Physics "Breakthrough Prize in Fundamental Physics") since 2012, include:

*   2018 [WMAP Probe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe "Wilkinson Microwave Anisotropy Probe") team, 27 listed members, including [Hiranya Peiris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hiranya_Peiris "Hiranya Peiris"), [Licia Verde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Licia_Verde "Licia Verde"), [Janet L. Weiland](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Janet_L._Weiland&action=edit&redlink=1 "Janet L. Weiland (page does not exist)") and [Joanna Dunkley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joanna_Dunkley "Joanna Dunkley") for "For detailed maps of the early universe that greatly improved our knowledge of the evolution of the cosmos and the fluctuations that seeded the formation of galaxies."[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-33)
*   2018 Special recognition to [Jocelyn Bell Burnell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jocelyn_Bell_Burnell "Jocelyn Bell Burnell") for "For fundamental contributions to the discovery of [pulsars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulsars "Pulsars"), and a lifetime of inspiring leadership in the scientific community."[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-34)

### Prizes only for female physicists

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Women_in_physics&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Prizes only for female physicists")]

*   [L'Oréal-UNESCO For Women in Science Awards](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L%27Or%C3%A9al-UNESCO_For_Women_in_Science_Awards "L'Oréal-UNESCO For Women in Science Awards"), awarded bi-annually to one laureate per continent for outstanding contributions to the physical sciences.
*   [Maria Goeppert-Mayer Award](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maria_Goeppert-Mayer_Award "Maria Goeppert-Mayer Award") of the [American Physical Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Physical_Society "American Physical Society") awarded annually in recognition of an outstanding contribution to physics research.
*   [Jocelyn Bell Burnell Medal and Prize](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Institute_of_Physics_Awards "Institute of Physics Awards") by the [Institute of Physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Institute_of_Physics "Institute of Physics") in UK, for contributions to physics by a very early career physicist.
*   [Annie Jump Cannon Award in Astronomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Annie_J._Cannon_Award_in_Astronomy "Annie J. Cannon Award in Astronomy") awarded annually for outstanding contributions to astronomy within five years of earning a doctorate degree.

Topics named after female scientists
------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Women_in_physics&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Topics named after female scientists")]

[![Image 6](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Noether.jpg/250px-Noether.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Noether.jpg)

[Emmy Noether](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emmy_Noether "Emmy Noether") who published the [Noether's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noether%27s_theorem "Noether's theorem") in 1918. The theorem relates symmetries to conserved quantities in physics.

Female scientist have sometimes not been recognized in the naming of topics they discovered due to [Matilda effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matilda_effect "Matilda effect"). Some physics phenomena that are named after female scientists include:

### Physical models and theories

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Women_in_physics&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Physical models and theories")]

*   [Birge–Sponer method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Birge%E2%80%93Sponer_method "Birge–Sponer method"), in molecular physics, partially named after [Hertha Sponer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hertha_Sponer "Hertha Sponer").
*   [Fermi–Pasta–Ulam–Tsingou problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermi%E2%80%93Pasta%E2%80%93Ulam%E2%80%93Tsingou_problem "Fermi–Pasta–Ulam–Tsingou problem") in [chaos theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chaos_theory "Chaos theory"), partially named after [Mary Tsingou](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mary_Tsingou "Mary Tsingou").
*   [Frenkel–Kontorova model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frenkel%E2%80%93Kontorova_model "Frenkel–Kontorova model"), in non-linear physics, partially named after [Tatiana Kontorova](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tatiana_Kontorova&action=edit&redlink=1 "Tatiana Kontorova (page does not exist)")[[ru](https://ru.wikipedia.org/wiki/%D0%9A%D0%BE%D0%BD%D1%82%D0%BE%D1%80%D0%BE%D0%B2%D0%B0,_%D0%A2%D0%B0%D1%82%D1%8C%D1%8F%D0%BD%D0%B0_%D0%90%D0%B1%D1%80%D0%B0%D0%BC%D0%BE%D0%B2%D0%BD%D0%B0 "ru:Конторова, Татьяна Абрамовна")].
*   [Kovalevskaya top](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrange,_Euler,_and_Kovalevskaya_tops "Lagrange, Euler, and Kovalevskaya tops") in rotational dynamics, named after [Sofya Kovalevskaya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sofya_Kovalevskaya "Sofya Kovalevskaya").
*   [Pasterski–Strominger–Zhiboedov triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pasterski%E2%80%93Strominger%E2%80%93Zhiboedov_triangle "Pasterski–Strominger–Zhiboedov triangle") in [quantum gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_gravity "Quantum gravity"), is partially named after [Sabrina Gonzalez Pasterski](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sabrina_Gonzalez_Pasterski "Sabrina Gonzalez Pasterski").
*   [Peccei–Quinn theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peccei%E2%80%93Quinn_theory "Peccei–Quinn theory") in [particle physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_physics "Particle physics"), partially named after [Helen Quinn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helen_Quinn "Helen Quinn").
*   [Pöschl–Teller potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/P%C3%B6schl%E2%80%93Teller_potential "Pöschl–Teller potential") in quantum mechanics, partially named after Herta Pöschl.
*   [Randall–Sundrum model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Randall%E2%80%93Sundrum_model "Randall–Sundrum model") in theoretical physics, partially named after [Lisa Randall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lisa_Randall "Lisa Randall").
*   [Falkner–Skan boundary layer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Falkner%E2%80%93Skan_boundary_layer "Falkner–Skan boundary layer") in fluid mechanics, partially named after [Sylvia Skan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sylvia_Skan "Sylvia Skan")
*   Van Vleck–Pauli–Morette determinant in quantum mechanics, partially named after [Cécile DeWitt-Morette](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C%C3%A9cile_DeWitt-Morette "Cécile DeWitt-Morette").

### Physical phenomena and empirical laws

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Women_in_physics&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Physical phenomena and empirical laws")]

*   [Faber–Jackson relation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Faber%E2%80%93Jackson_relation "Faber–Jackson relation"), in astronomomy, partially named after [Sandra Faber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sandra_Faber "Sandra Faber").
*   [Goos–Hänchen effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goos%E2%80%93H%C3%A4nchen_effect "Goos–Hänchen effect") in optics, partially named after [Hilda Hänchen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilda_H%C3%A4nchen "Hilda Hänchen").
*   [Leavitt's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Leavitt_Law "The Leavitt Law") in astronomy, named after [Henrietta Swan Leavitt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henrietta_Swan_Leavitt "Henrietta Swan Leavitt").
*   Pockels point in surface physics, named after [Agnes Pockels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agnes_Pockels "Agnes Pockels").
*   [Rubin–Ford effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rubin%E2%80%93Ford_effect "Rubin–Ford effect") in [cosmology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmology "Cosmology"), partially named after [Vera Rubin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vera_Rubin "Vera Rubin").

*   [Bohr–Van Leeuwen theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bohr%E2%80%93Van_Leeuwen_theorem "Bohr–Van Leeuwen theorem") in thermodynamics, partially named after [Hendrika Johanna van Leeuwen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hendrika_Johanna_van_Leeuwen "Hendrika Johanna van Leeuwen")
*   [Coffman–Kundu–Wootters inequality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monogamy_of_entanglement "Monogamy of entanglement"), in quantum information, partially named after Valerie Coffman
*   [Noether's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noether%27s_theorem "Noether's theorem") in [modern physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modern_physics "Modern physics"), named after [Emmy Noether](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emmy_Noether "Emmy Noether")

### Experiments and equipment

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Women_in_physics&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Experiments and equipment")]

*   [Langmuir–Blodgett film](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Langmuir%E2%80%93Blodgett_film "Langmuir–Blodgett film"), partially named after [Katharine Burr Blodgett](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Katharine_Burr_Blodgett "Katharine Burr Blodgett")

*   1938: Tatiana Kontorova, in collaboration with [Yakov Frenkel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yakov_Frenkel "Yakov Frenkel"), develops the [Frenkel-Kontorova model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frenkel-Kontorova_model "Frenkel-Kontorova model") to describe the structure and nonlinear dynamics of a crystal lattice in the vicinity of the dislocation core.[[101]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-101)

    *   Nuclear physicist [Marguerite Perey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marguerite_Perey "Marguerite Perey") discovers [francium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Francium "Francium").[[103]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-103)

[Leona Woods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leona_Woods "Leona Woods") was the only woman in the team and she was instrumental in the construction and then use of geiger counters for analysis during experimentation.

*   1944: [Curium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curium "Curium") (atomic number 96, symbol Cm) gets discovered a gets named after Marie and Pierre Curie, the "m" in Cm as a reference to Marie.[[110]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-110)
*   1945: American physicists and mathematicians [Frances Spence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frances_Spence "Frances Spence"), [Ruth Teitelbaum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ruth_Teitelbaum "Ruth Teitelbaum"), [Marlyn Meltzer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marlyn_Meltzer "Marlyn Meltzer"), [Betty Holberton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Betty_Holberton "Betty Holberton"), [Jean Bartik](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_Bartik "Jean Bartik") and [Kathleen Antonelli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kathleen_Antonelli "Kathleen Antonelli") programmed the electronic general-purpose computer [ENIAC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ENIAC "ENIAC"), becoming some of the world's first computer programmers.[[111]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-111)
*   1947: [Hilda Hänchen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilda_H%C3%A4nchen "Hilda Hänchen"), in collaboration with [Fritz Goos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fritz_Goos "Fritz Goos"), demonstrates a new optical phenomena, now known as the [Goos–Hänchen effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goos%E2%80%93H%C3%A4nchen_effect "Goos–Hänchen effect").[[112]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-112)
*   1949: [Rosemary Brown (later Fowler)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Rosemary_Brown_(later_Fowler)&action=edit&redlink=1 "Rosemary Brown (later Fowler) (page does not exist)"), a student of [C.F. Powell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C.F._Powell "C.F. Powell") at the [University of Bristol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Bristol "University of Bristol"), discovers the k-meson in what Heisenberg calls "most beautiful" pictures of cosmic ray tracks from the Jungfraujoch (the 'k' track in Brown, R. et al. Nature, 163, 47 (1949). This discovery and the prior finding of a very similar particle in 1947 led to the "τ–θ puzzle", the discovery of parity violation in weak interactions, and hence the Standard Model.

*   1951: [Cécile DeWitt-Morette](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C%C3%A9cile_DeWitt-Morette "Cécile DeWitt-Morette") founds the [École de physique des Houches](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%89cole_de_physique_des_Houches "École de physique des Houches"), one of the most prestigious scientific centers for international physics summer schools in Europe.[[113]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-113)

Teller"), led by [Nicholas Metropolis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nicholas_Metropolis "Nicholas Metropolis"), write the paper titled "[Equation of State Calculations by Fast Computing Machines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equation_of_State_Calculations_by_Fast_Computing_Machines "Equation of State Calculations by Fast Computing Machines")" that introduced the [Metropolis–Hastings algorithm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metropolis%E2%80%93Hastings_algorithm "Metropolis–Hastings algorithm").[[121]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-121)

Fowler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Alfred_Fowler "William Alfred Fowler"), and [Fred Hoyle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fred_Hoyle "Fred Hoyle"). *   1978: Chien-Shiung Wu becomes the inaugural laureate of the [Wolf Prize in Physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolf_Prize_in_Physics "Wolf Prize in Physics") for her help with the development of the [Standard Model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model "Standard Model").
*   1980: Nigerian geophysicist [Deborah Ajakaiye](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deborah_Ajakaiye "Deborah Ajakaiye") became the first woman in any West African country to be appointed a full professor of physics.[[148]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-148)[[149]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-149) Over the course of her scientific career, she became the first female Fellow elected to the [Nigerian Academy of Science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nigerian_Academy_of_Science "Nigerian Academy of Science"), and the first female dean of science in Nigeria.[[150]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-150)
*   1980: [Mary K. Gaillard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mary_K._Gaillard "Mary K. Gaillard") produces a report at [CERN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CERN "CERN") (European Organization for Nuclear Research) addressing the fact that just 3% of the staff were women. She called for the elimination of gender discrimination through equality in promotion, maternity leave and full-day child care.[[144]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-:0-144)

*   1981: Mary K. Gaillard becomes the first woman with a tenured position in the physics faculty at the [University of California, Berkeley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_California,_Berkeley "University of California, Berkeley").[[144]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-:0-144)
*   1985: [Mildred Dresselhaus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mildred_Dresselhaus "Mildred Dresselhaus") was appointed the first women [Institute Professor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_Institute_Professors_at_the_Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology "List of Institute Professors at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology") at [MIT](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology "Massachusetts Institute of Technology")[[151]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-151)
*   1986: [Maria Goeppert Mayer Award](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maria_Goeppert_Mayer_Award "Maria Goeppert Mayer Award") was awarded for the first time to honor young female physicists at the beginning of their careers[[152]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-152)
*   1986 [Jean M. Bennett](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_M._Bennett "Jean M. Bennett") became the first woman president of [The Optical Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Optical_Society "The Optical Society") founded in 1916.[[153]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-153)

*   1991: Ana María López, graduate student of [Eduardo Fradkin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eduardo_Fradkin "Eduardo Fradkin"), develops the first [Chern–Simons theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chern%E2%80%93Simons_theory "Chern–Simons theory") for [composite fermions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Composite_fermion "Composite fermion") to explain the [fractional quantum Hall effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractional_quantum_Hall_effect "Fractional quantum Hall effect").[[154]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-154)
*   1992: [Claudine Hermann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Claudine_Hermann "Claudine Hermann") first woman to be appointed professor at [École Polytechnique](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%89cole_Polytechnique "École Polytechnique").
*   1995: [Reva Williams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reva_Williams "Reva Williams") works out the [Penrose process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Penrose_process "Penrose process") for [rotating black holes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotating_black_hole "Rotating black hole").
*   1997: Chemical element with atomic number 278 is officially named [meitnerium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meitnerium "Meitnerium"), after Lise Meitner.[[155]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-155)
*   1999: [Lisa Randall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lisa_Randall "Lisa Randall") published the [Randall–Sundrum model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Randall%E2%80%93Sundrum_model "Randall–Sundrum model"), with [Raman Sundrum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raman_Sundrum "Raman Sundrum").[[156]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-156)
*   2000 
    *   [Mildred Dresselhaus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mildred_Dresselhaus "Mildred Dresselhaus") became the director of the Office of Science at the [United States Department of Energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Energy "United States Department of Energy").
    *   Helen Quinn becomes the first woman to receive the [Dirac Medal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dirac_Medal_(ICTP) "Dirac Medal (ICTP)") of the [International Centre for Theoretical Physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Centre_for_Theoretical_Physics "International Centre for Theoretical Physics") (ICTP) "pioneering contributions to the quest for a unified theory of quarks and leptons and the strong, weak and electromagnetic interactions."[[157]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-157)
    *   Valerie Coffman, working with Joydip Kundu and [William Wootters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Wootters "William Wootters") establish the concept of [monogamy of entanglement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monogamy_of_entanglement "Monogamy of entanglement") for tripartite systems, using their Coffman–Kundu–Wooters inequality.[[158]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-158)

*   2001: [Lene Hau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lene_Hau "Lene Hau") stopped a beam of light completely[[159]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-159)
*   2001: [Wendy Freedman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wendy_Freedman "Wendy Freedman") and her team published the measured [Hubble constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble_constant "Hubble constant") from measurements of the [Hubble Space Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope "Hubble Space Telescope").[[160]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-160)
*   2003: 
    *   Geophysicist [Claudia Alexander](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Claudia_Alexander "Claudia Alexander") oversaw the final stages of [Project Galileo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Project_Galileo "Project Galileo"), a space exploration mission that ended at the planet Jupiter.[[161]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-161)
    *   [Deborah S. Jin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deborah_S._Jin "Deborah S. Jin") and her team were the first to condense pairs of [fermionic atoms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermionic_condensate "Fermionic condensate")[[162]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-162)
    *   Physicists [Ayşe Erzan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ay%C5%9Fe_Erzan "Ayşe Erzan"), [Karimat El-Sayed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karimat_El-Sayed "Karimat El-Sayed"), [Li Fanghua](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Li_Fanghua "Li Fanghua"), [Mariana Weissmann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mariana_Weissmann "Mariana Weissmann") and [Anneke Levelt Sengers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anneke_Levelt_Sengers "Anneke Levelt Sengers") win the first [L'Oréal-UNESCO For Women in Science Awards](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L%27Or%C3%A9al-UNESCO_For_Women_in_Science_Awards "L'Oréal-UNESCO For Women in Science Awards") in Physical Sciences.[[163]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-163)

*   2005: [Myriam Sarachik](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Myriam_Sarachik "Myriam Sarachik") becomes the first woman to win the [Oliver E. Buckley Condensed Matter Prize](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oliver_E._Buckley_Condensed_Matter_Prize "Oliver E. Buckley Condensed Matter Prize") for her contributions to quantum spin dynamics and spin coherence in condensed matter systems, along with [David Awschalom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Awschalom "David Awschalom") and [Gabriel Aeppli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gabriel_Aeppli "Gabriel Aeppli").[[164]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-:2-164)
*   2007: Physicist [Ibtesam Badhrees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ibtesam_Badhrees "Ibtesam Badhrees") was the first Saudi Arabian woman to become a member of the [European Organization for Nuclear Research (CERN)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CERN "CERN").[[165]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-165)
*   2009: [Margaret Reid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Margaret_Reid_(scientist) "Margaret Reid (scientist)") becomes the first woman to win the Moyal Medal fromm [Macquarie University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macquarie_University "Macquarie University"), for her In 2019, her work on how to demonstrate the [Einstein-Podolsky-Rosen paradox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein%E2%80%93Podolsky%E2%80%93Rosen_paradox "Einstein–Podolsky–Rosen paradox") using [squeezing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Squeezed_states_of_light "Squeezed states of light") and [parametric down conversion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spontaneous_parametric_down-conversion "Spontaneous parametric down-conversion").[[166]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-166)

[![Image 12](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Deborah_S._Jin.jpg/250px-Deborah_S._Jin.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Deborah_S._Jin.jpg)

Deborah S. Jin known for creating the first fermionic condensate

*   2011: Taiwanese-American astrophysicist [Chung-Pei Ma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chung-Pei_Ma "Chung-Pei Ma") led a team of scientists in discovering two of the largest [black holes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_hole "Black hole") ever observed.[[167]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-167)
*   2012: Mildred Dresselhaus becomes the first female laureate of the [Kavli Prize](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kavli_Prize "Kavli Prize") in Nanosciences "for her pioneering contributions to the study of [phonons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phonons "Phonons"), electron-phonon interactions, and [thermal transport in nanostructures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermal_transport_in_nanostructures "Thermal transport in nanostructures")".[[168]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-168)
*   2013: [Nashwa Eassa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nashwa_Eassa "Nashwa Eassa") founded the NGO [Sudanese Women in Sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sudanese_Women_in_Sciences&action=edit&redlink=1 "Sudanese Women in Sciences (page does not exist)").
*   2014: American theoretical physicist [Shirley Anne Jackson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shirley_Ann_Jackson "Shirley Ann Jackson") was awarded the [National Medal of Science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Medal_of_Science "National Medal of Science"). Jackson had been the first African-American woman to receive a PhD from the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT) during the early 1970s, and the first woman to chair the [U.S. Nuclear Regulatory Commission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U.S._Nuclear_Regulatory_Commission "U.S. Nuclear Regulatory Commission").[[169]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-169)[[170]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-170)
*   2014: [Amanda Barnard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amanda_Barnard "Amanda Barnard") becomes the first woman to win the [Feynman Prize in Nanotechnology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feynman_Prize_in_Nanotechnology "Feynman Prize in Nanotechnology") for her computational simulations on diamond nanoparticles.[[171]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-171)
*   2015: [Sabrina Gonzalez Pasterski](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sabrina_Gonzalez_Pasterski "Sabrina Gonzalez Pasterski"), working with [Andrew Strominger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andrew_Strominger "Andrew Strominger") and Alexander Zhiboedov, develops the [Pasterski–Strominger–Zhiboedov triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pasterski%E2%80%93Strominger%E2%80%93Zhiboedov_triangle "Pasterski–Strominger–Zhiboedov triangle") relating soft particle theorems of quantum field theory, symmetries of space-time and [memory effects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_memory_effect "Gravitational memory effect") in gravitational waves.[[172]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-172)
*   2016: [Fabiola Gianotti](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fabiola_Gianotti "Fabiola Gianotti") became the first woman [Director-General](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_Directors_General_of_CERN "List of Directors General of CERN") of CERN (European Organization for Nuclear Research)[[173]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-173)
*   2018: 
    *   Astrophysicists [Hiranya Peiris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hiranya_Peiris "Hiranya Peiris") and [Joanna Dunkley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joanna_Dunkley "Joanna Dunkley") and Italian cosmologist [Licia Verde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Licia_Verde "Licia Verde") were among 27 scientists awarded the [Breakthrough Prize in Fundamental Physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Breakthrough_Prize_in_Fundamental_Physics "Breakthrough Prize in Fundamental Physics") for their contributions to "detailed maps of the early universe that greatly improved our knowledge of the evolution of the cosmos and the fluctuations that seeded the formation of galaxies".[[174]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-174)
    *   Astrophysicist [Jocelyn Bell Burnell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jocelyn_Bell_Burnell "Jocelyn Bell Burnell") received the special Breakthrough Prize in Fundamental Physics for her scientific achievements and “inspiring leadership”, worth $3 million. She donated the entirety of the prize money towards the creation of scholarships to assist women, underrepresented minorities and refugees who are pursuing the study of physics.[[175]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-175)
    *   Physicist Donna Strickland received the Nobel Prize in Physics "for groundbreaking inventions in the field of [laser physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laser_physics "Laser physics")"; she shared it with [Arthur Ashkin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arthur_Ashkin "Arthur Ashkin") and [Gérard Mourou](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G%C3%A9rard_Mourou "Gérard Mourou").[[176]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-N18-176)[[177]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-177)
    *   For the first time in history, women received the Nobel Prize in Chemistry and the Nobel Prize in Physics in the same year.[[178]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-178)
    *   Human right activist and physicist [Narges Mohammadi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Narges_Mohammadi "Narges Mohammadi") wins the [Andrei Sakharov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andrei_Sakharov "Andrei Sakharov") prize by the [American Physical Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Physical_Society "American Physical Society"), "_for her leadership in campaigning for peace, justice, and the abolition of the [death penalty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Death-penalty "Death-penalty") and for her unwavering efforts to promote the human rights and freedoms of the Iranian people, despite persecution that has forced her to suspend her scientific pursuits and endure lengthy incarceration."_[[179]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-179)
    *   [Ewine van Dishoeck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ewine_van_Dishoeck "Ewine van Dishoeck") becomes the first female laureate of the [Kavli Prize](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kavli_Prize "Kavli Prize") in Astrophysics for "for her combined contributions to observational, theoretical, and laboratory [astrochemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrochemistry "Astrochemistry"), elucidating the life cycle of [interstellar clouds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_cloud "Interstellar cloud") and the formation of [stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star "Star") and [planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planet "Planet")"[[180]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-Astro2018-180)[[181]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-:02-181)

*   2019: Mathematician [Karen Uhlenbeck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karen_Uhlenbeck "Karen Uhlenbeck") became the first woman to win the [Abel Prize](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abel_Prize "Abel Prize") for "her pioneering achievements in geometric [partial differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_differential_equations "Partial differential equations"), [gauge theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauge_theory "Gauge theory"), and [integrable systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integrable_system "Integrable system"), and for the fundamental impact of her work on [analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_analysis "Mathematical analysis"), [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry") and [mathematical physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_physics "Mathematical physics")."[[182]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-citation-182)
*   2020: 
    *   Andrea M. Ghez received the Nobel Prize in Physics "for the discovery of a supermassive compact object at the centre of our galaxy." She shared half of the prize with [Reinhard Genzel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reinhard_Genzel "Reinhard Genzel"), while the other half was awarded to [Roger Penrose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roger_Penrose "Roger Penrose").[[183]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-183)
    *   Geoscientist [Ingeborg Levin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ingeborg_Levin "Ingeborg Levin") was the first woman to receive the [Alfred Wegener](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alfred_Wegener "Alfred Wegener") medal from the [European Geosciences Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Geosciences_Union "European Geosciences Union") "for fundamental contributions to our present knowledge and understanding of greenhouse gases in the atmosphere, including the global carbon cycle."[[184]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-184)
    *   [Françoise Combes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fran%C3%A7oise_Combes "Françoise Combes") becomes the first female astrophysicist to win the [CNRS Gold Medal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CNRS_Gold_Medal "CNRS Gold Medal"), highest degree in research by the French government.[[185]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-185)

*   2022: Anne L’Huillier becomes the second female scientist to receive the Wolf Prize in Physics “for pioneering contributions to ultrafast laser science and attosecond physics”.[[186]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-186)
*   2022: Astronomer [Ewine van Dishoeck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ewine_van_Dishoeck "Ewine van Dishoeck") is awarded the [UNESCO Niels Bohr Medal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UNESCO_Niels_Bohr_Medal "UNESCO Niels Bohr Medal").[[187]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-187)
*   2023: Professor [Polina Bayvel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polina_Bayvel "Polina Bayvel") becomes the first woman to win the [Rumford Medal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rumford_Medal "Rumford Medal") by the [Royal Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Society "Royal Society").[[188]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women_in_physics#cite_note-188)
*   2023: Anne l'Huillier receives the 2023 Nobel Prize in Physics for "for experimental methods that generate attosecond pulses of light for the study of electron dynamics in matter" shared with [Pierre Agostini](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre_Agostini "Pierre Agostini") and [Ferenc Krausz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ferenc_Krausz "Ferenc Krausz").
