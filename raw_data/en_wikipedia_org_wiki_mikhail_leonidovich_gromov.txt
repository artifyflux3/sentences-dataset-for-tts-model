**Mikhael Leonidovich Gromov** (also **Mikhail Gromov**, **Michael Gromov** or **Misha Gromov**; Russian: Михаи́л Леони́дович Гро́мов; born 23 December 1943) is a Russian-French mathematician known for his work in [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry"), [analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_analysis "Mathematical analysis") and [group theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_theory "Group theory"). He is a permanent member of [Institut des Hautes Études Scientifiques](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Institut_des_Hautes_%C3%89tudes_Scientifiques "Institut des Hautes Études Scientifiques") in France and a professor of mathematics at [New York University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_York_University "New York University").

Gromov has won several prizes, including the [Abel Prize](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abel_Prize "Abel Prize") in 2009 "for his revolutionary contributions to geometry".

Early years, education and career
---------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Mikhael_Gromov_(mathematician)&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Early years, education and career")]

Mikhail Gromov was born on 23 December 1943 in [Boksitogorsk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boksitogorsk "Boksitogorsk"), [Soviet Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Union "Soviet Union"). His father Leonid Gromov was [Russian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russians "Russians")-Slavic and his mother Lea was of [Jewish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ashkenazi_Jews "Ashkenazi Jews") heritage. Both were [pathologists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pathologist "Pathologist").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#cite_note-1) His mother was the cousin of World Chess Champion [Mikhail Botvinnik](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Botvinnik "Mikhail Botvinnik"), as well as of the mathematician Isaak Moiseevich Rabinovich.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#cite_note-2) Gromov was born during [World War II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II "World War II"), and his mother, who worked as a medical doctor in the Soviet Army, had to leave the front line in order to give birth to him.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#cite_note-EMS-3) When Gromov was nine years old,[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#cite_note-lemonde-4) his mother gave him the book _[The Enjoyment of Mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=The\_Enjoyment\_of\_Mathematics&action=edit&redlink=1 "The Enjoyment of Mathematics (page does not exist)")_ by [Hans Rademacher](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hans_Rademacher "Hans Rademacher") and [Otto Toeplitz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Otto_Toeplitz "Otto Toeplitz"), a book that piqued his curiosity and had a great influence on him.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#cite_note-EMS-3)

Gromov studied mathematics at [Leningrad State University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leningrad_State_University "Leningrad State University") where he obtained a master's degree in 1965, a doctorate in 1969 and defended his postdoctoral thesis in 1973. His thesis advisor was [Vladimir Rokhlin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vladimir_Rokhlin_(Soviet_mathematician) "Vladimir Rokhlin (Soviet mathematician)").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#cite_note-5)

Gromov married in 1967. In 1970, he was invited to give a presentation at the [International Congress of Mathematicians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Congress_of_Mathematicians "International Congress of Mathematicians") in [Nice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nice "Nice"), France. However, he was not allowed to leave the USSR. Still, his lecture was published in the conference proceedings.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#cite_note-simons-6)

Disagreeing with the Soviet system, he had been thinking of emigrating since the age of 14. In the early 1970s he ceased publication, hoping that this would help his application to [move to Israel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aliyah "Aliyah").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#cite_note-lemonde-4)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#cite_note-7) He changed his last name to that of his mother.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#cite_note-lemonde-4) He received a coded letter saying that, if he could get out of the Soviet Union, he could go to [Stony Brook](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stony_Brook_University "Stony Brook University"), where a position had been arranged for him. When the request was granted in 1974, he moved directly to New York and worked at Stony Brook.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#cite_note-simons-6)

In 1981 he left [Stony Brook University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stony_Brook_University "Stony Brook University") to join the faculty of [University of Paris VI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre_and_Marie_Curie_University "Pierre and Marie Curie University") and in 1982 he became a permanent professor at the [Institut des Hautes Études Scientifiques](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Institut_des_Hautes_%C3%89tudes_Scientifiques "Institut des Hautes Études Scientifiques") where he remains today. At the same time, he has held professorships at the [University of Maryland, College Park](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Maryland,_College_Park "University of Maryland, College Park") from 1991 to 1996, and at the [Courant Institute of Mathematical Sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Courant_Institute_of_Mathematical_Sciences "Courant Institute of Mathematical Sciences") in New York since 1996.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#cite_note-mactutor-8) He adopted French citizenship in 1992.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#cite_note-9)

Gromov's style of geometry often features a "coarse" or "soft" viewpoint, analyzing asymptotic or large-scale properties.[[G00]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#CITEREFG00) He is also interested in [mathematical biology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_biology "Mathematical biology"),[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#cite_note-:0-10) the structure of the brain and the thinking process, and the way scientific ideas evolve.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#cite_note-simons-6)

Motivated by [Nash and Kuiper's isometric embedding theorems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nash_embedding_theorem "Nash embedding theorem") and the results on [immersions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immersion_(mathematics) "Immersion (mathematics)") by [Morris Hirsch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morris_Hirsch "Morris Hirsch") and [Stephen Smale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stephen_Smale "Stephen Smale"),[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#cite_note-:0-10) Gromov introduced the [h-principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/H-principle "H-principle") in various formulations. Modeled upon the special case of the Hirsch–Smale theory, he introduced and developed the general theory of _microflexible sheaves_, proving that they satisfy an h-principle on [open manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_manifold "Open manifold").[[G69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#CITEREFG69) As a consequence (among other results) he was able to establish the existence of positively curved and negatively curved [Riemannian metrics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_metric "Riemannian metric") on any [open manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_manifold "Open manifold") whatsoever. His result is in counterpoint to the well-known topological restrictions (such as the [Cheeger–Gromoll soul theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soul_theorem "Soul theorem") or [Cartan–Hadamard theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartan%E2%80%93Hadamard_theorem "Cartan–Hadamard theorem")) on _[geodesically complete](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesically\_complete "Geodesically complete")_ Riemannian manifolds of positive or negative curvature. After this initial work, he developed further h-principles partly in collaboration with [Yakov Eliashberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yakov_Eliashberg "Yakov Eliashberg"), including work building upon Nash and Kuiper's theorem and the [Nash–Moser implicit function theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nash-Moser_theorem "Nash-Moser theorem"). There are many applications of his results, including topological conditions for the existence of [exact Lagrangian immersions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symplectic_manifold#Lagrangian_mapping "Symplectic manifold") and similar objects in [symplectic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symplectic_geometry "Symplectic geometry") and [contact geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contact_geometry "Contact geometry").[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#cite_note-11)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#cite_note-12) His well-known book _Partial Differential Relations_ collects most of his work on these problems.[[G86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#CITEREFG86) Later, he applied his methods to [complex geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_geometry "Complex geometry"), proving certain instances of the _Oka principle_ on deformation of [continuous maps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_map "Continuous map") to [holomorphic maps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holomorphic_map "Holomorphic map").[[G89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#CITEREFG89) His work initiated a renewed study of the Oka–Grauert theory, which had been introduced in the 1950s.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#cite_note-13)[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#cite_note-14)

Gromov and [Vitali Milman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vitali_Milman "Vitali Milman") gave a formulation of the [concentration of measure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concentration_of_measure "Concentration of measure") phenomena.[[GM83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#CITEREFGM83) They defined a "Lévy family" as a sequence of normalized metric measure spaces in which any asymptotically nonvanishing sequence of sets can be metrically thickened to include almost every point. This closely mimics the phenomena of the [law of large numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law_of_large_numbers "Law of large numbers"), and in fact the law of large numbers can be put into the framework of Lévy families. Gromov and Milman developed the basic theory of Lévy families and identified a number of examples, most importantly coming from sequences of [Riemannian manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_manifold "Riemannian manifold") in which the lower bound of the [Ricci curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_curvature "Ricci curvature") or the first eigenvalue of the [Laplace–Beltrami operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laplace%E2%80%93Beltrami_operator "Laplace–Beltrami operator") diverge to infinity. They also highlighted a feature of Lévy families in which any sequence of continuous functions must be asymptotically almost constant. These considerations have been taken further by other authors, such as [Michel Talagrand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michel_Talagrand "Michel Talagrand").[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#cite_note-15)

Since the seminal 1964 publication of [James Eells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Eells "James Eells") and [Joseph Sampson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_H._Sampson "Joseph H. Sampson") on [harmonic maps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harmonic_map "Harmonic map"), various rigidity phenomena had been deduced from the combination of an existence theorem for harmonic mappings together with a vanishing theorem asserting that (certain) harmonic mappings must be totally geodesic or holomorphic.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#cite_note-16)[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#cite_note-17)[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#cite_note-18) Gromov had the insight that the extension of this program to the setting of mappings into [metric spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space "Metric space") would imply new results on [discrete groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discrete_group "Discrete group"), following [Margulis superrigidity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superrigidity "Superrigidity"). [Richard Schoen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Schoen "Richard Schoen") carried out the analytical work to extend the harmonic map theory to the metric space setting; this was subsequently done more systematically by Nicholas Korevaar and Schoen, establishing extensions of most of the standard [Sobolev space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sobolev_space "Sobolev space") theory.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#cite_note-19) A sample application of Gromov and Schoen's methods is the fact that [lattices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lattice_(discrete_subgroup) "Lattice (discrete subgroup)") in the isometry group of the [quaternionic hyperbolic space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_quaternion "Hyperbolic quaternion") are [arithmetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic_group "Arithmetic group").[[GS92]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#CITEREFGS92)

### Riemannian geometry

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Mikhael_Gromov_(mathematician)&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Riemannian geometry")]

In 1978, Gromov introduced the notion of [almost flat manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Almost_flat_manifold "Almost flat manifold").[[G78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#CITEREFG78) The famous [quarter-pinched sphere theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere_theorem "Sphere theorem") in [Riemannian geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_geometry "Riemannian geometry") says that if a complete Riemannian manifold has [sectional curvatures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sectional_curvature "Sectional curvature") which are all sufficiently close to a given positive constant, then M must be finitely covered by a sphere. In contrast, it can be seen by scaling that every [closed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_manifold "Closed manifold") Riemannian manifold has Riemannian metrics whose sectional curvatures are arbitrarily close to zero. Gromov showed that if the scaling possibility is broken by only considering Riemannian manifolds of a fixed diameter, then a closed manifold admitting such a Riemannian metric, with sectional curvatures sufficiently close to zero, must be finitely covered by a [nilmanifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nilmanifold "Nilmanifold"). The proof works by replaying the proofs of the [Bieberbach theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flat_manifold "Flat manifold") and [Margulis lemma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Margulis_lemma "Margulis lemma"). Gromov's proof was given a careful exposition by [Peter Buser](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J%C3%BCrg_Peter_Buser "Jürg Peter Buser") and Hermann Karcher.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#cite_note-20)[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#cite_note-21)[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#cite_note-22)

In 1979, [Richard Schoen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Schoen "Richard Schoen") and [Shing-Tung Yau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shing-Tung_Yau "Shing-Tung Yau") showed that the class of [smooth manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smooth_manifolds "Smooth manifolds") which admit Riemannian metrics of positive [scalar curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_curvature "Scalar curvature") is topologically rich. In particular, they showed that this class is closed under the operation of [connected sum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connected_sum "Connected sum") and of [surgery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surgery_theory "Surgery theory") in codimension at least three.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#cite_note-23) Their proof used elementary methods of [partial differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_differential_equation "Partial differential equation"), in particular to do with the [Green's function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green%27s_function "Green's function"). Gromov and [Blaine Lawson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blaine_Lawson "Blaine Lawson") gave another proof of Schoen and Yau's results, making use of elementary geometric constructions.[[GL80b]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#CITEREFGL80b) They also showed how purely topological results such as [Stephen Smale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stephen_Smale "Stephen Smale")'s [h-cobordism theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/H-cobordism "H-cobordism") could then be applied to draw conclusions such as the fact that every [closed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_manifold "Closed manifold") and [simply-connected](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simply-connected "Simply-connected") smooth manifold of dimension 5, 6, or 7 has a Riemannian metric of positive scalar curvature. They further introduced the new class of _enlargeable manifolds_, distinguished by a condition in [homotopy theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homotopy_theory "Homotopy theory").[[GL80a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#CITEREFGL80a) They showed that Riemannian metrics of positive scalar curvature _cannot_ exist on such manifolds. A particular consequence is that the [torus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torus "Torus") cannot support any Riemannian metric of positive scalar curvature, which had been a major conjecture previously resolved by Schoen and Yau in low dimensions.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#cite_note-24)

In 1981, Gromov identified topological restrictions, based upon [Betti numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Betti_number "Betti number"), on manifolds which admit Riemannian metrics of [nonnegative sectional curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sectional_curvature "Sectional curvature").[[G81a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#CITEREFG81a) The principal idea of his work was to combine [Karsten Grove](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karsten_Grove "Karsten Grove") and Katsuhiro Shiohama's Morse theory for the Riemannian distance function, with control of the distance function obtained from the [Toponogov comparison theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toponogov%27s_theorem "Toponogov's theorem"), together with the [Bishop–Gromov inequality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bishop%E2%80%93Gromov_inequality "Bishop–Gromov inequality") on volume of geodesic balls.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#cite_note-25) This resulted in topologically controlled covers of the manifold by geodesic balls, to which [spectral sequence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectral_sequence "Spectral sequence") arguments could be applied to control the topology of the underlying manifold. The topology of lower bounds on sectional curvature is still not fully understood, and Gromov's work remains as a primary result. As an application of [Hodge theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hodge_theory "Hodge theory"), [Peter Li](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Li_(mathematician) "Peter Li (mathematician)") and Yau were able to apply their gradient estimates to find similar Betti number estimates which are weaker than Gromov's but allow the manifold to have convex boundary.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#cite_note-LiYau-26)

In [Jeff Cheeger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jeff_Cheeger "Jeff Cheeger")'s fundamental compactness theory for Riemannian manifolds, a key step in constructing coordinates on the limiting space is an [injectivity radius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Injectivity_radius "Injectivity radius") estimate for [closed manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_manifold "Closed manifold").[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#cite_note-27) Cheeger, Gromov, and [Michael Taylor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michael_E._Taylor "Michael E. Taylor") localized Cheeger's estimate, showing how to use [Bishop−Gromov volume comparison](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bishop%E2%80%93Gromov_inequality "Bishop–Gromov inequality") to control the injectivity radius in absolute terms by curvature bounds and volumes of geodesic balls.[[CGT82]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#CITEREFCGT82) Their estimate has been used in a number of places where the construction of coordinates is an important problem.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#cite_note-28)[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#cite_note-29)[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#cite_note-30) A particularly well-known instance of this is to show that [Grigori Perelman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grigori_Perelman "Grigori Perelman")'s "noncollapsing theorem" for [Ricci flow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_flow "Ricci flow"), which controls volume, is sufficient to allow applications of [Richard Hamilton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_S._Hamilton "Richard S. Hamilton")'s compactness theory.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#cite_note-31)[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#cite_note-32)[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#cite_note-33) Cheeger, Gromov, and Taylor applied their injectivity radius estimate to prove [Gaussian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaussian_function "Gaussian function") control of the [heat kernel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat_kernel "Heat kernel"), although these estimates were later improved by Li and Yau as an application of their gradient estimates.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#cite_note-LiYau-26)

Gromov made foundational contributions to [systolic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Systolic_geometry "Systolic geometry"). Systolic geometry studies the relationship between size invariants (such as volume or diameter) of a manifold M and its topologically non-trivial submanifolds (such as non-contractible curves). In his 1983 paper "Filling Riemannian manifolds"[[G83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#CITEREFG83) Gromov [proved](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gromov%27s_systolic_inequality_for_essential_manifolds "Gromov's systolic inequality for essential manifolds") that every [essential manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Essential_manifold "Essential manifold")![Image 2: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) with a Riemannian metric contains a closed non-contractible [geodesic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic "Geodesic") of length at most ![Image 3: {\displaystyle C(n)\operatorname {Vol} (M)^{1/n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/213065cf56ed96ca177600f623bf83e7c5edb209).[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#cite_note-34)

### Gromov−Hausdorff convergence and geometric group theory

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Mikhael_Gromov_(mathematician)&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Gromov−Hausdorff convergence and geometric group theory")]

In 1981, Gromov introduced the [Gromov–Hausdorff metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gromov%E2%80%93Hausdorff_metric "Gromov–Hausdorff metric"), which endows the set of all [metric spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space "Metric space") with the structure of a metric space.[[G81b]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#CITEREFG81b) More generally, one can define the Gromov-Hausdorff distance between two metric spaces, relative to the choice of a point in each space. Although this does not give a metric on the space of all metric spaces, it is sufficient in order to define "Gromov-Hausdorff convergence" of a sequence of pointed metric spaces to a limit. Gromov formulated an important compactness theorem in this setting, giving a condition under which a sequence of pointed and "proper" metric spaces must have a subsequence which converges. This was later reformulated by Gromov and others into the more flexible notion of an [ultralimit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultralimit "Ultralimit").[[G93]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#CITEREFG93)

Gromov's compactness theorem had a deep impact on the field of [geometric group theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_group_theory "Geometric group theory"). He applied it to understand the asymptotic geometry of the [word metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Word_metric "Word metric") of a [group of polynomial growth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Growth_rate_(group_theory) "Growth rate (group theory)"), by taking the limit of well-chosen rescalings of the metric. By tracking the limits of isometries of the word metric, he was able to show that the limiting metric space has unexpected continuities, and in particular that its isometry group is a [Lie group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_group "Lie group").[[G81b]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#CITEREFG81b) As a consequence he was able to settle the [Milnor-Wolf conjecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gromov%27s_theorem_on_groups_of_polynomial_growth "Gromov's theorem on groups of polynomial growth") as posed in the 1960s, which asserts that any such group is [virtually nilpotent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virtually_nilpotent "Virtually nilpotent"). Using ultralimits, similar asymptotic structures can be studied for more general metric spaces.[[G93]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#CITEREFG93) Important developments on this topic were given by [Bruce Kleiner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bruce_Kleiner "Bruce Kleiner"), Bernhard Leeb, and [Pierre Pansu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre_Pansu "Pierre Pansu"), among others.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#cite_note-35)[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#cite_note-36)

Another consequence is [Gromov's compactness theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gromov%27s_compactness_theorem_(geometry) "Gromov's compactness theorem (geometry)"), stating that the set of compact [Riemannian manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_manifold "Riemannian manifold") with [Ricci curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_curvature "Ricci curvature") ≥ _c_ and [diameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diameter "Diameter") ≤ _D_ is [relatively compact](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relatively_compact "Relatively compact") in the Gromov–Hausdorff metric.[[G81b]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#CITEREFG81b) The possible limit points of sequences of such manifolds are [Alexandrov spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexandrov_space "Alexandrov space") of curvature ≥ _c_, a class of [metric spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space "Metric space") studied in detail by [Burago](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yuri_Burago "Yuri Burago"), Gromov and [Perelman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grigori_Perelman "Grigori Perelman") in 1992.[[BGP92]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#CITEREFBGP92)

Along with [Eliyahu Rips](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eliyahu_Rips "Eliyahu Rips"), Gromov introduced the notion of [hyperbolic groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_group "Hyperbolic group").[[G87]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#CITEREFG87)

### Symplectic geometry

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Mikhael_Gromov_(mathematician)&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Symplectic geometry")]

Gromov's theory of [pseudoholomorphic curves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudoholomorphic_curves "Pseudoholomorphic curves") is one of the foundations of the modern study of [symplectic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symplectic_geometry "Symplectic geometry").[[G85]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#CITEREFG85) Although he was not the first to consider pseudo-holomorphic curves, he uncovered a "bubbling" phenomena paralleling [Karen Uhlenbeck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karen_Uhlenbeck "Karen Uhlenbeck")'s earlier work on [Yang–Mills connections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yang%E2%80%93Mills_connection "Yang–Mills connection"), and Uhlenbeck and Jonathan Sack's work on [harmonic maps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harmonic_map "Harmonic map").[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#cite_note-37)[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#cite_note-38) In the time since Sacks, Uhlenbeck, and Gromov's work, such bubbling phenomena has been found in a number of other geometric contexts. The corresponding [compactness theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gromov%27s_compactness_theorem_(topology) "Gromov's compactness theorem (topology)") encoding the bubbling allowed Gromov to arrive at a number of analytically deep conclusions on existence of pseudo-holomorphic curves. A particularly famous result of Gromov's, arrived at as a consequence of the existence theory and the monotonicity formula for [minimal surfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minimal_surface "Minimal surface"), is the "[non-squeezing theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-squeezing_theorem "Non-squeezing theorem")," which provided a striking qualitative feature of symplectic geometry. Following ideas of [Edward Witten](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edward_Witten "Edward Witten"), Gromov's work is also fundamental for [Gromov-Witten theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gromov-Witten_theory "Gromov-Witten theory"), which is a widely studied topic reaching into [string theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/String_theory "String theory"), [algebraic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_geometry "Algebraic geometry"), and [symplectic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symplectic_geometry "Symplectic geometry").[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#cite_note-39)[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#cite_note-40)[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#cite_note-41) From a different perspective, Gromov's work was also inspirational for much of [Andreas Floer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andreas_Floer "Andreas Floer")'s work.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#cite_note-42)

[Yakov Eliashberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yakov_Eliashberg "Yakov Eliashberg") and Gromov developed some of the basic theory for symplectic notions of convexity.[[EG91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#CITEREFEG91) They introduce various specific notions of convexity, all of which are concerned with the existence of one-parameter families of diffeomorphisms which contract the symplectic form. They show that convexity is an appropriate context for an [h-principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/H-principle "H-principle") to hold for the problem of constructing certain [symplectomorphisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symplectomorphism "Symplectomorphism"). They also introduced analogous notions in [contact geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contact_geometry "Contact geometry"); the existence of convex contact structures was later studied by [Emmanuel Giroux](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emmanuel_Giroux "Emmanuel Giroux").[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov#cite_note-43)
