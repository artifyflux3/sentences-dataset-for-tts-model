**Eric Wolfgang Weisstein** (born March 18, 1969) is an American scientist, mathematician, and [encyclopedist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Encyclopedia "Encyclopedia") who created and maintains the encyclopedias _[MathWorld](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MathWorld "MathWorld")_ and _ScienceWorld_. In addition, he is the author of the _[CRC Concise Encyclopedia of Mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CRC\_Concise\_Encyclopedia\_of\_Mathematics "CRC Concise Encyclopedia of Mathematics")_. He works for [Wolfram Research](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfram_Research "Wolfram Research").

Education
---------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Eric_W._Weisstein&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Education")]

Weisstein holds a Ph.D. in [planetary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_science "Planetary science")[astronomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy "Astronomy"), which he obtained from the [California Institute of Technology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/California_Institute_of_Technology "California Institute of Technology")'s Division of Geological and Planetary Sciences in 1996 as well as an M.S. in planetary astronomy in 1993 also from Caltech. Weisstein graduated cum laude from [Cornell University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cornell_University "Cornell University") with a B.A. in [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics") and a minor in astronomy in 1990. During his summers away from Cornell, Weisstein participated in research at the [Arecibo Observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arecibo_Observatory "Arecibo Observatory"), a radio telescope facility in [Puerto Rico](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Puerto_Rico "Puerto Rico") operated by Cornell. As a graduate student, Weisstein also participated in research at [Goddard Space Flight Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goddard_Space_Flight_Center "Goddard Space Flight Center") in [Greenbelt, Maryland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenbelt,_Maryland "Greenbelt, Maryland"). During his time at Goddard, Weisstein participated in the development of hurricane visualization software. In 1996 Weisstein published his doctoral thesis titled _Millimeter/Submillimeter Fourier Transform Spectroscopy of Jovian Planet Atmospheres_, which was completed under faculty advisor Dewey Muhleman and in association with Eugene Serabyn, who later became a member of the Caltech [Jet Propulsion Laboratory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jet_Propulsion_Laboratory "Jet Propulsion Laboratory").

Career
------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Eric_W._Weisstein&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Career")]

### Academic research

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Eric_W._Weisstein&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Academic research")]

Upon completion of his doctorate, Weisstein became a [research](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Research "Research")[scientist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientist "Scientist") at Caltech in January 1996 and continued work in the field of submillimeter [spectroscopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopy "Spectroscopy"). He collaborated with Eugene Serabyn and published several papers. Six months later, he moved to the [University of Virginia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Virginia "University of Virginia")'s Department of Astronomy in [Charlottesville](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charlottesville,_Virginia "Charlottesville, Virginia") where he remained for three years to continue his research.

### MathWorld, ScienceWorld, and Wolfram Research

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Eric_W._Weisstein&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: MathWorld, ScienceWorld, and Wolfram Research")]

See also: [MathWorld](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MathWorld "MathWorld") and [Wolfram Research](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfram_Research "Wolfram Research")

In 1995, Weisstein converted a [Microsoft Word](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microsoft_Word "Microsoft Word") document of over 200 pages to hypertext format and uploaded it to his webspace at Caltech under the title _Eric's Treasure Trove of Sciences._ After Weisstein transferred to the University of Virginia to continue his work in astronomy, he continued to refine his fledgling encyclopedia. In November 1998, Weisstein made a deal with the [CRC Press](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CRC_Press "CRC Press") to publish his encyclopedia in book format as the _[CRC Concise Encyclopedia of Mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CRC\_Concise\_Encyclopedia\_of\_Mathematics "CRC Concise Encyclopedia of Mathematics")_. A year later, in 1999, Weisstein accepted the position of encyclopedist at [Wolfram Research](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfram_Research "Wolfram Research"), Inc. (WRI). _MathWorld_ was ready to be unveiled in December 1999 with nearly 13,000 entries, most of them written by Weisstein, encompassing a variety of disciplines, including [algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra "Algebra"), [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry"), [calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus "Calculus"), [discrete mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discrete_mathematics "Discrete mathematics"), [topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology "Topology"), [number theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_theory "Number theory"), [statistics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistics "Statistics"), and the [foundations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foundations_of_mathematics "Foundations of mathematics").

_MathWorld_ became involved in a legal dispute with the CRC Press in March 2000. The CRC Press claimed _MathWorld_ violated the copyright on the _[CRC Concise Encyclopedia of Mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CRC\_Concise\_Encyclopedia\_of\_Mathematics "CRC Concise Encyclopedia of Mathematics")_. During the dispute, a court order shut down _MathWorld_ for over a year starting October 23, 2000. According to Eric Weisstein's personal site, he restarted MathWorld on November 6, 2001.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eric_W._Weisstein#cite_note-1) This ultimately led to the creation of [PlanetMath](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PlanetMath "PlanetMath").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eric_W._Weisstein#cite_note-2) Wolfram Research, [Stephen Wolfram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stephen_Wolfram "Stephen Wolfram"), and Eric Weisstein settled with the CRC Press for an undisclosed financial award and several benefits. Among these benefits are the inclusion of a copyright notice of the CRC Press at the bottom of all webpages in _MathWorld_ and legal rights to reproduce _MathWorld_ in a book format again.

_ScienceWorld_, also known as _Eric Weisstein's World of Science_, was unveiled to the general public in January 2002. _ScienceWorld_ includes more than 1,000 entries in many fields of science including [astronomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy "Astronomy"), [chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemistry "Chemistry"), [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics") and [biographies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biographies "Biographies") of scientists.

### Further scientific activities

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Eric_W._Weisstein&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Further scientific activities")]

In 2014, he became a member of the [Global Digital Mathematics Library](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_Digital_Mathematics_Library "Global Digital Mathematics Library") Working Group of the [International Mathematical Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Mathematical_Union "International Mathematical Union").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eric_W._Weisstein#cite_note-3)
