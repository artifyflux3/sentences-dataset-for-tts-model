In [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry"), the **great dodecahedron** is one of four [Kepler–Poinsot polyhedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler%E2%80%93Poinsot_polyhedra "Kepler–Poinsot polyhedra"). It is composed of 12 [pentagonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentagon "Pentagon") faces (six pairs of parallel pentagons), intersecting each other making a [pentagrammic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentagram "Pentagram") path, with five pentagons meeting at each vertex.

One way to construct a great dodecahedron is by [faceting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Faceting "Faceting") the [regular icosahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron "Regular icosahedron"). In other words, it is constructed from the regular icosahedron by removing its polygonal faces without changing or creating new vertices.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_dodecahedron#cite_note-inchbald-1) For each vertex of the icosahedron, the five neighboring vertices become those of a regular pentagon face of the great dodecahedron. The resulting shape has a [pentagram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentagram "Pentagram") as its [vertex figure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_figure "Vertex figure"),[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_dodecahedron#cite_note-pugh-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_dodecahedron#cite_note-barnes-3) so its [Schläfli symbol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schl%C3%A4fli_symbol "Schläfli symbol") is ![Image 5: {\displaystyle \{5,5/2\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f34b529c77ac38fb5e7ab5802f996b16c5dd76a9).[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_dodecahedron#cite_note-kappraff-4)

The great dodecahedron may also be interpreted as the _second stellation of dodecahedron_. The construction started from a [regular dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron "Regular dodecahedron") by attaching 12 pentagonal pyramids onto each of its faces, known as the _first stellation_. The second stellation appears when 30 [wedges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wedge_(geometry) "Wedge (geometry)") are attached to it.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_dodecahedron#cite_note-cromwell-5)

Historically, the great dodecahedron is one of two solids discovered by [Louis Poinsot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis_Poinsot "Louis Poinsot") in 1810, with some people named it after him, _Poinsot solid_. As for the background, Poinsot rediscovered two other solids that were already discovered by [Johannes Kepler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johannes_Kepler "Johannes Kepler")—the [small stellated dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Small_stellated_dodecahedron "Small stellated dodecahedron") and the [great stellated dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_stellated_dodecahedron "Great stellated dodecahedron").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_dodecahedron#cite_note-barnes-3) However, the great dodecahedron appeared in the 1568 _[Perspectiva Corporum Regularium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perspectiva\_Corporum\_Regularium "Perspectiva Corporum Regularium")_ by [Wenzel Jamnitzer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wenzel_Jamnitzer "Wenzel Jamnitzer"), although its drawing is somewhat similar.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_dodecahedron#cite_note-ss-6)

The great dodecahedron appeared in popular culture and toys. An example is [Alexander's Star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander%27s_Star "Alexander's Star") puzzle, a [twisty puzzle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Twisty_puzzle "Twisty puzzle") that is based on a great dodecahedron.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_dodecahedron#cite_note-7)

The _compound of small stellated dodecahedron and great dodecahedron_ is a [polyhedron compound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyhedron_compound "Polyhedron compound") where the great dodecahedron is internal to its [dual](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_polyhedron "Dual polyhedron"), the [small stellated dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Small_stellated_dodecahedron "Small stellated dodecahedron"). This can be seen as one of the two three-dimensional equivalents of the compound of two pentagrams ({⁠10/4⁠} "[decagram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decagram_(geometry) "Decagram (geometry)")"); this series continues into the fourth dimension as [compounds of star 4-polytopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polytope_compound#Compounds_with_regular_star_4-polytopes "Polytope compound").

A [truncation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Truncation_(geometry) "Truncation (geometry)") process applied to the great dodecahedron produces a series of [nonconvex uniform polyhedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nonconvex_uniform_polyhedron "Nonconvex uniform polyhedron"). Truncating edges down to points produces the [dodecadodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dodecadodecahedron "Dodecadodecahedron") as a rectified great dodecahedron. The process completes as a birectification, reducing the original faces down to points, and producing the [small stellated dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Small_stellated_dodecahedron "Small stellated dodecahedron").

It shares the same [edge arrangement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edge_arrangement "Edge arrangement") as the convex regular [icosahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Icosahedron "Icosahedron"); the compound with both is the [small complex icosidodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Small_complex_icosidodecahedron "Small complex icosidodecahedron").
