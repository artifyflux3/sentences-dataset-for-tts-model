The unit hyperbola is blue, its conjugate is green, and the asymptotes are red.

In [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry"), the **unit hyperbola** is the set of points (_x_,_y_) in the [Cartesian plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_plane "Cartesian plane") that satisfy the [implicit equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Implicit_equation "Implicit equation")![Image 2: {\displaystyle x^{2}-y^{2}=1.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/24f2ba746546937e0027e7cebcae383f226c6125) In the study of [indefinite orthogonal groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indefinite_orthogonal_group "Indefinite orthogonal group"), the unit hyperbola forms the basis for an _alternative radial length_

![Image 3: {\displaystyle r={\sqrt {x^{2}-y^{2}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ae8d3bf78b377938e6383ab1dd5bc6010a2d8a25)
Whereas the [unit circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_circle "Unit circle") surrounds its center, the unit hyperbola requires the [conjugate hyperbola](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conjugate_hyperbola "Conjugate hyperbola")![Image 4: {\displaystyle y^{2}-x^{2}=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2abebd04178a7a604de7086b4be7140469194ae0) to complement it in the plane. This pair of [hyperbolas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbola "Hyperbola") share the [asymptotes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asymptote "Asymptote")_y_ = _x_ and _y_ = −_x_. When the conjugate of the unit hyperbola is in use, the alternative radial length is ![Image 5: {\displaystyle r={\sqrt {y^{2}-x^{2}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0f79e3af87b7b6356af289797afc7b8f543937c0)

The unit hyperbola is a special case of the [rectangular hyperbola](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangular_hyperbola "Rectangular hyperbola"), with a particular [orientation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orientation_(geometry) "Orientation (geometry)"), [location](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Translation_(geometry) "Translation (geometry)"), and [scale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scaling_(geometry) "Scaling (geometry)"). As such, its [eccentricity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eccentricity_(mathematics) "Eccentricity (mathematics)") equals ![Image 6: {\displaystyle {\sqrt {2}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6f4b9a6710637a4b6fac61388a46180a132d66c4)[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_hyperbola#cite_note-1)

The unit hyperbola finds applications where the circle must be replaced with the hyperbola for purposes of analytic geometry. A prominent instance is the depiction of [spacetime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spacetime "Spacetime") as a [pseudo-Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudo-Euclidean_space "Pseudo-Euclidean space"). There the asymptotes of the unit hyperbola form a [light cone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_cone "Light cone"). Further, the attention to areas of [hyperbolic sectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_sector "Hyperbolic sector") by [Gregoire de Saint-Vincent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gregoire_de_Saint-Vincent "Gregoire de Saint-Vincent") led to the [logarithm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logarithm "Logarithm") function and the modern parametrization of the hyperbola by sector areas. When the notions of conjugate hyperbolas and hyperbolic angles are understood, then the classical [complex numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number"), which are built around the unit circle, can be replaced with numbers built around the unit hyperbola.

Generally asymptotic lines to a curve are said to converge toward the curve. In [algebraic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_geometry "Algebraic geometry") and the theory of [algebraic curves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_curves "Algebraic curves") there is a different approach to asymptotes. The curve is first interpreted in the [projective plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_plane "Projective plane") using [homogeneous coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homogeneous_coordinates "Homogeneous coordinates"). Then the asymptotes are lines that are tangent to the projective curve at a [point at infinity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_at_infinity "Point at infinity"), thus circumventing any need for a distance concept and convergence. In a common framework (_x, y, z_) are homogeneous coordinates with the [line at infinity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_at_infinity "Line at infinity") determined by the equation _z_ = 0. For instance, C. G. Gibson wrote:[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_hyperbola#cite_note-2)

For the standard rectangular hyperbola ![Image 7: {\displaystyle f=x^{2}-y^{2}-1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4a3440ee636cf769fed5d383b3d140ad3a5ec418) in ![Image 8: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e150115ab9f63023215109595b76686a1ff890fd), the corresponding projective curve is ![Image 9: {\displaystyle F=x^{2}-y^{2}-z^{2},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/66154515b60cfe136bca3d0b10b7d0373e51a20d) which meets _z_ = 0 at the points _P_ = (1: 1: 0) and _Q_ = (1: −1: 0). Both _P_ and _Q_ are [simple](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero_(complex_analysis)#Multiplicity_of_a_zero "Zero (complex analysis)") on _F_, with tangents _x_ + _y_ = 0, _x_ − _y_ = 0; thus we recover the familiar 'asymptotes' of elementary geometry.
The [Minkowski diagram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spacetime_diagram "Spacetime diagram") is drawn in a spacetime plane where the spatial aspect has been restricted to a single dimension. The units of distance and time on such a plane are

*   units of 30 centimetres length and [nanoseconds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nanosecond "Nanosecond"), or
*   [astronomical units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_unit "Astronomical unit") and intervals of 8 minutes and 20 seconds, or
*   [light years](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_year "Light year") and [years](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Year "Year").

Each of these scales of coordinates results in [photon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon "Photon") connections of events along diagonal lines of [slope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slope "Slope") plus or minus one. Five elements constitute the diagram [Hermann Minkowski](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Minkowski "Hermann Minkowski") used to describe the relativity transformations: the unit hyperbola, its [conjugate hyperbola](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conjugate_hyperbola "Conjugate hyperbola"), the axes of the hyperbola, a diameter of the unit hyperbola, and the [conjugate diameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conjugate_diameters "Conjugate diameters"). The plane with the axes refers to a resting [frame of reference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frame_of_reference "Frame of reference"). The diameter of the unit hyperbola represents a frame of reference in motion with [rapidity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rapidity "Rapidity")_a_ where tanh _a_ = _y_/_x_ and (_x_,_y_) is the endpoint of the diameter on the unit hyperbola. The conjugate diameter represents the _spatial hyperplane of simultaneity_ corresponding to rapidity _a_. In this context the unit hyperbola is a _calibration hyperbola_[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_hyperbola#cite_note-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_hyperbola#cite_note-4) Commonly in relativity study the hyperbola with vertical axis is taken as primary:

The arrow of time goes from the bottom to top of the figure — a convention adopted by [Richard Feynman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Feynman "Richard Feynman") in his famous diagrams. Space is represented by planes perpendicular to the time axis. The here and now is a singularity in the middle.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_hyperbola#cite_note-5)
The vertical time axis convention stems from Minkowski in 1908, and is also illustrated on page 48 of Eddington's _The Nature of the Physical World_ (1928).

The branches of the unit hyperbola evolve as the points ![Image 11: {\displaystyle (\cosh a,\sinh a)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fd85fe3138dd55340cdd24631795aaa6828bd84f) and ![Image 12: {\displaystyle (-\cosh a,-\sinh a)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1614ce46f02505354c45cd7ba837897247dea789) depending on the hyperbolic angle parameter ![Image 13: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc).

A direct way to parameterizing the unit hyperbola starts with the hyperbola _xy_ = 1 parameterized with the [exponential function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponential_function "Exponential function"): ![Image 14: {\displaystyle (e^{t},\ e^{-t}).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a928a97102a13b40353855a19c4baa86cfffbb6f)

This hyperbola is transformed into the unit hyperbola by a [linear mapping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_mapping "Linear mapping") having the matrix ![Image 15: {\displaystyle A={\tfrac {1}{2}}{\begin{pmatrix}1&1\\1&-1\end{pmatrix}}\ :}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c091a0a9a086530b196dc1bba6f5dd71c639d2e5)

![Image 16: {\displaystyle (e^{t},\ e^{-t})\ A=({\frac {e^{t}+e^{-t}}{2}},\ {\frac {e^{t}-e^{-t}}{2}})=(\cosh t,\ \sinh t).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b5574897d10da5af7f059d89b7a2d34a9614fe0a)
This parameter _t_ is the _hyperbolic angle_, which is the [argument](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Argument_of_a_function "Argument of a function") of the [hyperbolic functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_function "Hyperbolic function").

One finds an early expression of the parametrized unit hyperbola in [Elements of Dynamic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elements_of_Dynamic "Elements of Dynamic") (1878) by [W. K. Clifford](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Kingdon_Clifford "William Kingdon Clifford"). He describes quasi-harmonic motion in a hyperbola as follows:

The motion ![Image 17: {\displaystyle \rho =\alpha \cosh(nt+\epsilon )+\beta \sinh(nt+\epsilon )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/463901fe7857044b628b0b8ed15c7f8f3abe5f0b) has some curious analogies to elliptic harmonic motion. ... The acceleration ![Image 18: {\displaystyle {\ddot {\rho }}=n^{2}\rho \ ;}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/583b19730d11542de73cd4e5b3aebc875ab632a7) thus it is always proportional to the distance from the centre, as in elliptic harmonic motion, but directed _away_ from the centre.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_hyperbola#cite_note-6)
As a particular [conic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conic_section "Conic section"), the hyperbola can be parametrized by the process of addition of points on a conic. The following description was given by Russian analysts:

Fix a point _E_ on the conic. Consider the points at which the straight line drawn through _E_ parallel to _AB_ intersects the conic a second time to be the _sum of the points A and B_.For the hyperbola ![Image 19: {\displaystyle x^{2}-y^{2}=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/66b6dc015284f725f6537eb50fe4b20348cf0a5a) with the fixed point _E_ = (1,0) the sum of the points ![Image 20: {\displaystyle (x_{1},\ y_{1})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8fa9a04dc844b7a6e1e987593b7365c0c794f817) and ![Image 21: {\displaystyle (x_{2},\ y_{2})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5e0395e65f4b5acac2263017f8d5a58d652737a6) is the point ![Image 22: {\displaystyle (x_{1}x_{2}+y_{1}y_{2},\ y_{1}x_{2}+y_{2}x_{1})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/74d84bd3f08a5f2ea50a341313b2d5fcc101404e) under the parametrization ![Image 23: {\displaystyle x=\cosh \ t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/40f70ad405460a9723eb6c6221aaa378c07adacf) and ![Image 24: {\displaystyle y=\sinh \ t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5a24ffe86139f2c15e2dae8583a909d58080f39) this addition corresponds to the addition of the parameter _t_.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_hyperbola#cite_note-7)

Complex plane algebra
---------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Unit_hyperbola&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Complex plane algebra")]

Whereas the unit circle is associated with [complex numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number"), the unit hyperbola is key to the _split-complex number plane_ consisting of _z_ = _x_ + _yj_, where _j_ 2 = +1. Then _jz = y + xj_, so the action of _j_ on the plane is to swap the coordinates. In particular, this action swaps the unit hyperbola with its conjugate and swaps pairs of [conjugate diameters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conjugate_diameters "Conjugate diameters") of the hyperbolas.

In terms of the [hyperbolic angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_angle "Hyperbolic angle") parameter _a_, the unit hyperbola consists of points

![Image 25: {\displaystyle \pm (\cosh a+j\sinh a)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/36868d17084865a0f41fcd623006d7cc1eea8697), where _j_ = (0,1).
The right branch of the unit hyperbola corresponds to the positive coefficient. In fact, this branch is the image of the [exponential map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponential_map_(Lie_theory) "Exponential map (Lie theory)") acting on the _j_-axis. Thus this branch is the curve ![Image 26: {\displaystyle f(a)=\exp(aj).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/28935655cbba6b920d1ff10882305264b91e69e8) The [slope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slope "Slope") of the curve at _a_ is given by the [derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative "Derivative")

![Image 27: {\displaystyle f^{\prime }(a)=\sinh a+j\cosh a=jf(a).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/01d2f8e6e08b6da90fb822f2c12a5f734289f2b6) For any _a_, ![Image 28: {\displaystyle f^{\prime }(a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/555732385ee5db0682bfc075f378a487011d100b)) is [hyperbolic-orthogonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic-orthogonal "Hyperbolic-orthogonal") to ![Image 29: {\displaystyle f(a)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/368cb4b81ba5754d7a354a4ce49c2f1084bdaace). This relation is analogous to the perpendicularity of exp(_a_ i) and i exp(_a_ i) when i 2 = − 1.
Since ![Image 30: {\displaystyle \exp(aj)\exp(bj)=\exp((a+b)j)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3cecbeac4daad10b550ca2b7357118c1bdf91fdb), the branch is a [group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_(mathematics) "Group (mathematics)") under multiplication.

Unlike the [circle group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle_group "Circle group"), this unit hyperbola group is _not_[compact](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compact_space "Compact space"). Similar to the ordinary complex plane, a point not on the diagonals has a [polar decomposition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_decomposition#Alternative_planar_decompositions "Polar decomposition") using the parametrization of the unit hyperbola and the alternative radial length.
