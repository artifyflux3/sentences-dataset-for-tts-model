From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Form of writing

[![Image 8](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Carolineminuscel.jpg/250px-Carolineminuscel.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Carolineminuscel.jpg)

Carolingian minuscule alphabet

[![Image 9](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/91/CarolingianMinuscule.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:CarolingianMinuscule.jpg)

Example from 10th-century manuscript, [Vulgate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vulgate "Vulgate")[Luke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gospel_of_Luke "Gospel of Luke") 1:5–8.

**Carolingian minuscule** or **Caroline minuscule** is a [script](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hand_(handwriting) "Hand (handwriting)") which developed as a calligraphic standard in the [medieval European](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_Ages "Middle Ages") period so that the [Latin alphabet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin_alphabet "Latin alphabet") of [Jerome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerome "Jerome")'s [Vulgate Bible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vulgate_Bible "Vulgate Bible") could be easily recognized by the [literate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Literate "Literate") class from one region to another. It is thought to have originated before 778 CE at the [scriptorium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scriptorium "Scriptorium") of the [Benedictine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benedictine "Benedictine") monks of [Corbie Abbey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corbie_Abbey "Corbie Abbey"), about 150 kilometres (95 miles) north of Paris, and then developed by [Alcuin of York](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alcuin_of_York "Alcuin of York") for wide use in the [Carolingian Renaissance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carolingian_Renaissance "Carolingian Renaissance").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carolingian_minuscule#cite_note-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carolingian_minuscule#cite_note-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carolingian_minuscule#cite_note-Marcia-3) Alcuin himself still wrote in a script which was a precursor to the Carolingian minuscule, which slowly developed over three centuries.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carolingian_minuscule#cite_note-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carolingian_minuscule#cite_note-5) He was most likely responsible for copying and preserving the manuscripts[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carolingian_minuscule#cite_note-6) and upkeep of the script.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carolingian_minuscule#cite_note-7) It was used in the [Holy Roman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire "Holy Roman Empire") between approximately 800 and 1200. [Codices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Codex "Codex"), [Christian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christianity "Christianity") texts, and educational material were written in Carolingian minuscule.

After [blackletter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blackletter "Blackletter") developed out of it, the Carolingian minuscule became obsolete, until the 14th century [Italian Renaissance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italian_Renaissance "Italian Renaissance"), when the [humanist minuscule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Humanist_minuscule "Humanist minuscule") script was also developed from it. By this latter line, the Carolingian minuscule is a direct ancestor of most modern-day [Latin letter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin_script "Latin script") scripts and typefaces.

Creation
--------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Carolingian_minuscule&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Creation")]

[![Image 10](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Minuscule_caroline.jpg/250px-Minuscule_caroline.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Minuscule_caroline.jpg)

Page of text (folio 160v) from a Carolingian Gospel Book ([British Library](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Library "British Library"), Add MS 11848), written in Carolingian minuscule. Text is [Vulgate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vulgate "Vulgate")[Luke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gospel_of_Luke "Gospel of Luke") 23:15–26.

The script is derived partly from various continental minuscule scripts ultimately descended from late [Roman cursive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_cursive "Roman cursive") and partly from [Roman half uncial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uncial_script "Uncial script") and its cursive version.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carolingian_minuscule#cite_note-8) It also adopted certain features from the [Insular scripts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Insular_script "Insular script") that were being used in Irish and English monasteries.

Carolingian minuscule was created partly under the patronage of the Emperor [Charlemagne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charlemagne "Charlemagne") (hence Carolingian). Charlemagne had a keen interest in learning, according to his biographer [Einhard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einhard "Einhard") (here with [apices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apex_(diacritic) "Apex (diacritic)")):

> Temptábat et scríbere, tabulásque et códicellós ad hoc in lectó sub cervícálibus circumferre solébat, ut, cum vacuum tempus esset, manum litterís effigiendís adsuésceret, sed parum successit labor praeposterus ac séró incohátus.

> He also tried to write, and used to keep tablets and blanks in bed under his pillow, that at leisure hours he might accustom his hand to form the letters; however, as he did not begin his efforts in due season, but late in life, they met with ill success.

This new script was significantly more legible than the ones used in earlier periods of the Middle Ages and was also superior to the book scripts used in the Roman period, because it provided word spacing, more punctuation, and conventions such as the usage of upper-case for titles, a mix of upper and lower case for subtitles, and lower case for the body of a text.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carolingian_minuscule#cite_note-Marcia-3) Although Charlemagne was never fully literate, he understood the value of literacy and a uniform script in running his empire. Charlemagne sent for the English scholar [Alcuin of York](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alcuin_of_York "Alcuin of York") to run his palace school and [scriptorium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scriptorium "Scriptorium") at his capital, [Aachen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aachen "Aachen"). Efforts to supplant [Gallo-Roman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Merovingian_script "Merovingian script") and Germanic scripts had been under way before Alcuin arrived at Aachen, where he was master from 782 to 796, with a two-year break. The new minuscule was disseminated first from Aachen, of which the [Ada Gospels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ada_Gospels "Ada Gospels") provided classic models, and later from the influential scriptorium at [Marmoutier Abbey (Tours)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marmoutier_Abbey_(Tours) "Marmoutier Abbey (Tours)"), where Alcuin withdrew from court service as an [abbot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abbot "Abbot") in 796 and restructured the scriptorium.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carolingian_minuscule#cite_note-Rand1929-9)

Characteristics
---------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Carolingian_minuscule&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Characteristics")]

Carolingian minuscule was uniform with rounded shapes in clearly distinguishable [glyphs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glyph "Glyph"), disciplined and above all, legible. Clear capital letters and spaces between words became standard in Carolingian minuscule, which was one result of a campaign to achieve a culturally unifying standardization across the [Carolingian Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carolingian_Empire "Carolingian Empire").[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carolingian_minuscule#cite_note-:0-10)

Traditional charters, however, continued to be written in a [Merovingian "chancery hand"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Merovingian_script "Merovingian script") long after manuscripts of Scripture and classical literature were being produced in the minuscule hand. Documents written in a local language, like [Gothic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gothic_language "Gothic language") or [Anglo-Saxon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Old_English_language "Old English language") rather than Latin, tended to be expressed in traditional local script.

Carolingian script generally has fewer [ligatures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ligature_(typography) "Ligature (typography)") than other contemporary [scripts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Script_(styles_of_handwriting) "Script (styles of handwriting)"), although the [_et_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ampersand "Ampersand") (_&_), _æ_, _rt_, _st_, and _ct_ ligatures are common. The letter _d_ often appears in an uncial form with an [ascender](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ascender_(typography) "Ascender (typography)") slanting to the left, but the letter _g_ is essentially the same as the modern minuscule letter, rather than the previously common uncial _ᵹ_. Ascenders are usually "clubbed" – they become thicker near the top.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carolingian_minuscule#cite_note-:0-10)

The early period of the script, during Charlemagne's reign in the late 8th century and early 9th, still has widely varying letter forms in different regions. The uncial form of the letter _a_, similar to a double _c_ (_cc_), was still used in manuscripts from this period. There was also use of punctuation such as the [question mark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Question_mark "Question mark"), as in [Beneventan script](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beneventan_script "Beneventan script") of the same period. The script flourished during the 9th century, when regional hands developed into an international standard, with less variation of letter forms. Modern [glyphs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glyph "Glyph"), such as _s_ and _v_, began to appear (as opposed to the "[long _s_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Long_s "Long s")" _ſ_ and _u_), and ascenders, after thickening at the top, were finished with a three-cornered wedge. The script began to evolve slowly after the 9th century. In the 10th and 11th centuries, ligatures were rare and ascenders began to slant to the right and were finished with a fork. The letter _w_ also began to appear. By the 12th century, Carolingian letters had become more angular and were written closer together, less legibly than in previous centuries; at the same time, the modern [dotted](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tittle "Tittle")_i_ appeared.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

[![Image 11](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Freising_manuscript.jpg/250px-Freising_manuscript.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Freising_manuscript.jpg)

A page of the [Freising manuscripts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freising_manuscripts "Freising manuscripts"), showing 10th-century [Slovene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slovene_language "Slovene language") text written in Carolingian minuscule. [Bavarian State Library](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bavarian_State_Library "Bavarian State Library"), Munich.

Spread
------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Carolingian_minuscule&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Spread")]

The new script spread through Western Europe most widely where Carolingian influence was strongest. In luxuriously produced [lectionaries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lectionary "Lectionary") that now began to be produced for princely patronage of abbots and bishops, legibility was essential. It reached far afield: the 10th century [Freising manuscripts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freising_manuscripts "Freising manuscripts"), which contain the oldest [Slovene language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slovene_language "Slovene language"), the first [Roman-script](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin_alphabet "Latin alphabet") record of any [Slavic language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slavic_language "Slavic language"), are written in Carolingian minuscule. In [Switzerland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Switzerland "Switzerland"), Carolingian was used in the Rhaetian and Alemannic minuscule types. Manuscripts written in **Rhaetian minuscule** tend to have slender letters, resembling Insular script, with the letters ⟨a⟩ and ⟨t⟩, and ligatures such as ⟨ri⟩, showing similar to Visigothic and Beneventan. **Alemannic minuscule**, used for a short time in the early 9th century, is usually larger, broader, and very vertical in comparison to the slanting Rhaetian type. It was developed by the monk [Wolfcoz I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfcoz_I "Wolfcoz I") at the [Abbey of Saint Gall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abbey_of_Saint_Gall "Abbey of Saint Gall").[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carolingian_minuscule#cite_note-hls-11) In the [Holy Roman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire "Holy Roman Empire"), Carolingian script flourished in [Salzburg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salzburg "Salzburg"), [Austria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Austria "Austria"), as well as in [Fulda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fulda "Fulda"), [Mainz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mainz "Mainz"), and [Würzburg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/W%C3%BCrzburg "Würzburg"), all of which were major centers of the script. German minuscule tends to be oval-shaped, very slender, and slanted to the right. It has uncial features as well, such as the ascender of the letter ⟨d⟩ slanting to the left, and vertical initial strokes of ⟨m⟩ and ⟨n⟩.

In northern Italy, the monastery at [Bobbio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bobbio "Bobbio") used Carolingian minuscule beginning in the 9th century. Outside the sphere of influence of Charlemagne and his successors, however, the new legible hand was resisted by the [Roman Curia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Curia "Roman Curia"); nevertheless the Romanesca type was developed in [Rome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rome "Rome") after the 10th century. The script was not taken up in England and Ireland until ecclesiastic reforms in the middle of the 10th century; in Spain a traditionalist [Visigothic hand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visigothic_script "Visigothic script") survived; and in southern Italy a '[Beneventan minuscule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beneventan_script "Beneventan script")' survived in the lands of the [Lombard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lombards "Lombards")[duchy of Benevento](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duchy_of_Benevento "Duchy of Benevento") through the 13th century, although Romanesca eventually also appeared in southern Italy.

Role in cultural transmission
-----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Carolingian_minuscule&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Role in cultural transmission")]

Scholars during the [Carolingian Renaissance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carolingian_Renaissance "Carolingian Renaissance") sought out and copied in the new legible standardized hand many Roman texts that had been wholly forgotten. Most of contemporary knowledge of classical literature derives from copies made in the [scriptoria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scriptorium "Scriptorium") of [Charlemagne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charlemagne "Charlemagne"). Over 7000 manuscripts written in Carolingian script survive from the 8th and 9th centuries alone.

Though the Carolingian minuscule was superseded by [Gothic blackletter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blackletter "Blackletter") hands, in retrospect, it seemed so thoroughly 'classic' to the [humanists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Renaissance_humanism "Renaissance humanism") of the early [Renaissance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Renaissance "Renaissance") that they took these old Carolingian manuscripts to be [ancient Roman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Rome "Ancient Rome") originals, and used them as bases for their Renaissance hand, the "[humanist minuscule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Humanist_minuscule "Humanist minuscule")".[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carolingian_minuscule#cite_note-Ullman1960-12) From there the script passed to the 15th- and 16th-century printers of books, such as [Aldus Manutius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aldus_Manutius "Aldus Manutius") of Venice. In this way it forms the basis of our modern lowercase typefaces. Indeed, 'Carolingian minuscule' is a style of [typeface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Typeface "Typeface"), which approximates this historical hand, eliminating the nuances of size of capitals, long descenders, and so on.
