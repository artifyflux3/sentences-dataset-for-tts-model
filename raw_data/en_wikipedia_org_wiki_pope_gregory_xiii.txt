**Pope Gregory XIII** ([Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin_language "Latin language"): _Gregorius XIII_, [Italian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italian_language "Italian language"): _Gregorio XIII_, born **Ugo Boncompagni**; 7 January 1502 – 10 April 1585[[b]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pope_Gregory_XIII#cite_note-2)) was head of the [Catholic Church](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catholic_Church "Catholic Church") and ruler of the [Papal States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Papal_States "Papal States") from 13 May 1572 to his death in April 1585. He is best known for commissioning and being the namesake for the [Gregorian calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gregorian_calendar "Gregorian calendar"), which remains the internationally accepted [civil calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civil_calendar "Civil calendar") to this day.

Early biography
---------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Pope_Gregory_XIII&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Early biography")]

### Youth

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Pope_Gregory_XIII&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Youth")]

Ugo Boncompagni was born the son of Cristoforo [Boncompagni](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boncompagni "Boncompagni") (10 July 1470 – 1546) and Angela Marescalchi, and paternal grandson of Giacomo Boncompagni and Camilla Piattesi, in [Bologna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bologna "Bologna"),[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pope_Gregory_XIII#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBorromeo2002663-3) where he studied law and graduated in 1530.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pope_Gregory_XIII#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrowther19998-4) He later taught jurisprudence for some years, and his students included notable figures such as Cardinals [Alexander Farnese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alessandro_Farnese_(cardinal) "Alessandro Farnese (cardinal)"), [Reginald Pole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reginald_Pole "Reginald Pole") and [Charles Borromeo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Borromeo "Charles Borromeo"). He had an illegitimate son after an affair with Maddalena Fulchini, [Giacomo Boncompagni](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giacomo_Boncompagni "Giacomo Boncompagni"), but before he took holy orders, making him the last Pope to have left issue.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pope_Gregory_XIII#cite_note-5)

### Career before papacy

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Pope_Gregory_XIII&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Career before papacy")]

At the age of 36 he was summoned to Rome by [Pope Paul III](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pope_Paul_III "Pope Paul III") (1534–1549), under whom he held successive appointments as first judge of the capital, [abbreviator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abbreviator "Abbreviator"), and vice-chancellor of the [Campagna e Marittima](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Campagna_e_Marittima "Campagna e Marittima"). [Pope Paul IV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pope_Paul_IV "Pope Paul IV") (1555–1559) attached him as _[datarius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Datarius "Datarius")_ to the suite of Cardinal [Carlo Carafa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carlo_Carafa "Carlo Carafa"). [Pope Pius IV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pope_Pius_IV "Pope Pius IV") (1559–1565) made him [Cardinal-Priest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardinal-Priest "Cardinal-Priest") of [San Sisto Vecchio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Sisto_Vecchio "San Sisto Vecchio") and sent him to the [Council of Trent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Council_of_Trent "Council of Trent").

In the year 1552, Ugo Boncompagni confirmed the paternity of his son Giacomo (or Jacopo). As stated in the online Archivio Digitale Boncompagni Ludovisi: "One of the most valuable items to emerge from the new archival finds from the [Villa Aurora](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Villa_Aurora_(Rome) "Villa Aurora (Rome)") is an autograph declaration in Latin and Italian dated 22 December 1552 by Ugo Boncompagni (1502–1585, from 1572 Pope Gregory XIII). Here Ugo confirms his paternity of [Giacomo (or Jacopo) Boncompagni](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giacomo_Boncompagni "Giacomo Boncompagni") (1548–1612) by Maddalena de' Fucchinis, a servant in the employ of his sister-in-law Laura Ferro. The future Pope explains in detail the circumstances of the boy's conception, which took place in 1547 in [Bologna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bologna "Bologna") after the Council of Trent had moved to that city; his motive was to assure his inheritance rights following the death (in 1546) of his father Cristoforo Boncompagni."[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pope_Gregory_XIII#cite_note-6)

He also served as a legate to [Philip II of Spain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philip_II_of_Spain "Philip II of Spain") (1556–1598), being sent by the Pope to investigate the [Archbishop of Toledo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Archdiocese_of_Toledo "Roman Catholic Archdiocese of Toledo")[Bartolomé Carranza](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bartolom%C3%A9_Carranza "Bartolomé Carranza"). He formed a lasting and close relationship with the Spanish king, which aided his foreign policy aims as Pope.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pope_Gregory_XIII#cite_note-7)

### Election as pope

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Pope_Gregory_XIII&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Election as pope")]

Main article: [1572 papal conclave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1572_papal_conclave "1572 papal conclave")

After Pope Pius V (1566–1572) died, the [conclave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Papal_conclave,_1572 "Papal conclave, 1572") chose Cardinal Boncompagni, who assumed the name of Gregory XIII in homage to [Gregory the Great](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gregory_the_Great "Gregory the Great"), a 6th-century reforming pope. It was a very brief conclave, lasting less than 24 hours. Many historians have attributed this to the influence and backing of the Spanish king. [Charles Borromeo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Borromeo "Charles Borromeo") and the cardinals wishing reform accepted Boncompagni's candidature and so supported him in the conclave, while the Spanish faction also deemed him acceptable due to his success as a [nuncio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuncio "Nuncio") in Spain.

Pontificate
-----------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Pope_Gregory_XIII&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Pontificate")]

### Reform of the Church

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Pope_Gregory_XIII&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Reform of the Church")]

[![Image 19](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Lavinia_Fontana_-_Portrait_of_Pope_Gregory_XIII.jpg/250px-Lavinia_Fontana_-_Portrait_of_Pope_Gregory_XIII.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Lavinia_Fontana_-_Portrait_of_Pope_Gregory_XIII.jpg)

_Portrait of Gregory XIII_ by [Lavinia Fontana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lavinia_Fontana "Lavinia Fontana") (oil on canvas, private collection)

Once in the chair of [Saint Peter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saint_Peter "Saint Peter"), Gregory XIII dedicated himself to reform of the Catholic Church. He implemented the recommendations of the [Council of Trent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Council_of_Trent "Council of Trent"). He mandated that cardinals reside in their [sees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Episcopal_see "Episcopal see") without exception, and designated a committee to update the [Index of Forbidden Books](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Index_of_Forbidden_Books "Index of Forbidden Books"). Gregory XIII was also the patron of a new and greatly improved edition of the _Corpus juris canonici_. In a time of considerable centralization of power, Gregory XIII abolished the Cardinals [Consistories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Papal_consistory "Papal consistory"), replacing them with Colleges and appointing specific tasks for these colleges to work on. He was renowned for fierce independence; some confidants noted that he neither welcomed interventions nor sought advice. The power of the papacy increased under him, whereas the influence and power of the cardinals substantially decreased.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

Gregory XIII also established the [Discalced Carmelites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discalced_Carmelites "Discalced Carmelites"), an offshoot of the [Carmelite Order](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carmelite_Order "Carmelite Order"), as a distinct unit or "province" within the former by the decree "Pia consideratione" dated 22 June 1580,[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pope_Gregory_XIII#cite_note-8) ending a period of great difficulty between them and enabling the former to become a significant religious order in the Catholic Church.

### Formation of clergy and promotion of the arts and sciences

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Pope_Gregory_XIII&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Formation of clergy and promotion of the arts and sciences")]

Gregory XIII was a generous patron of the [Jesuit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jesuit "Jesuit") colleges in Rome.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pope_Gregory_XIII#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrowther19998-4) The Roman College of the Jesuits grew substantially under his patronage, and became the most important centre of learning in Europe for a time. It is now named the [Pontifical Gregorian University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pontifical_Gregorian_University "Pontifical Gregorian University"). Pope Gregory XIII also founded numerous [seminaries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seminaries "Seminaries") for training priests, beginning with the [German College](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_College "German College") at [Rome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rome "Rome"), and put them in the charge of the Jesuits.

In 1575, he gave official status to the [Congregation of the Oratory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oratory_of_Saint_Philip_Neri "Oratory of Saint Philip Neri"), a community of priests without vows, dedicated to prayer and preaching (founded by Saint [Philip Neri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philip_Neri "Philip Neri")). In 1580 he commissioned artists, including [Ignazio Danti](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ignazio_Danti "Ignazio Danti"), to complete works to decorate the [Vatican](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apostolic_Palace "Apostolic Palace") and commissioned [The Gallery of Maps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Gallery_of_Maps "The Gallery of Maps").

Gregory also transformed the Dominican studium founded in the 13th century at Rome into the College of St. Thomas in 1580, as recommended by the Council of Trent. This college was the precursor of the [Pontifical University of Saint Thomas Aquinas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pontifical_University_of_Saint_Thomas_Aquinas "Pontifical University of Saint Thomas Aquinas")_Angelicum_.

### The Gregorian calendar

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Pope_Gregory_XIII&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: The Gregorian calendar")]

Main article: [Gregorian calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gregorian_calendar "Gregorian calendar")

[![Image 20](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Gregorianscher_Kalender_Petersdom.jpg/330px-Gregorianscher_Kalender_Petersdom.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Gregorianscher_Kalender_Petersdom.jpg)

Detail of the [tomb of Pope Gregory XIII](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_extant_papal_tombs "List of extant papal tombs") celebrating the introduction of the Gregorian calendar.

Pope Gregory XIII is best known for commissioning the [Gregorian calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gregorian_calendar "Gregorian calendar"), initially authored by the physician/astronomer [Aloysius Lilius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aloysius_Lilius "Aloysius Lilius")[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pope_Gregory_XIII#cite_note-9)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pope_Gregory_XIII#cite_note-10) and aided by Jesuit priest/astronomer [Christopher Clavius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christopher_Clavius "Christopher Clavius"), who made the final modifications. This calendar is more accurate than the [Julian calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Julian_calendar "Julian calendar"), which treats each year as 365 days and 6 hours in length, even though the actual length of a year is slightly less (365 days, 5 hours, and 49 minutes). As a result, the date of the [vernal equinox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/March_equinox "March equinox") had slowly, over the course of 13 centuries, slipped to 10 March, while the [computus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computus "Computus") (calculation) of the date of Easter still followed the traditional date of 21 March. Clavius verified this phenomenon.

Gregory subsequently decreed, by the [papal bull](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Papal_bull "Papal bull")_[Inter gravissimas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inter\_gravissimas "Inter gravissimas")_ of 24 February 1582, that the day after Thursday, 4 October 1582 would be the fifteenth, not the fifth, of October. The new calendar replaced the Julian calendar, which had been used since 45 BC. Because of Gregory's involvement, the new calendar came to be known as the Gregorian calendar and has been almost universally adopted.

Much of the populace bitterly opposed this reform; they feared it was an attempt by landlords to cheat them out of a week and a half's rent. However, the Catholic countries of [Spain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habsburg_Spain "Habsburg Spain"), [Portugal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Portugal "Kingdom of Portugal"), [Poland-Lithuania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polish%E2%80%93Lithuanian_Commonwealth "Polish–Lithuanian Commonwealth"), and the Italian states complied. [France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_France "Kingdom of France"), some states of the [Dutch Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dutch_Republic "Dutch Republic") and various Catholic states in the [Holy Roman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire "Holy Roman Empire") and [Switzerland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Old_Swiss_Confederacy "Old Swiss Confederacy") (both countries were religiously split) followed suit within a year or two. [Austria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archduchy_of_Austria "Archduchy of Austria") and [Hungary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Hungary_(1526-1867) "Kingdom of Hungary (1526-1867)") followed in 1587.

However, more than a century passed before [Protestant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protestant "Protestant") Europe accepted the new calendar. [Denmark-Norway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Denmark-Norway "Denmark-Norway"), the remaining states of the Dutch Republic, and the Protestant states of the Holy Roman Empire and Switzerland adopted the Gregorian reform in 1700–01. By that time, the calendar trailed the seasons by 11 days. [Great Britain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Great_Britain "Kingdom of Great Britain"), its [American colonies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_North_America "British North America") and [Ireland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Ireland "Kingdom of Ireland") adopted the reformed calendar in 1752, where Wednesday 2 September 1752 was immediately followed by Thursday 14 September 1752; they were joined by the last Protestant holdout, [Sweden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sweden "Sweden"), on 1 March 1753.

The Gregorian calendar was not accepted in [eastern Christendom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastern_Christendom "Eastern Christendom") for several hundred years, and then only as the civil calendar.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pope_Gregory_XIII#cite_note-11)

### Foreign policy

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Pope_Gregory_XIII&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Foreign policy")]

[![Image 21](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Japanese_Tensho_Embassy_with_Pope_Gregory_XIII_1585.png/250px-Japanese_Tensho_Embassy_with_Pope_Gregory_XIII_1585.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Japanese_Tensho_Embassy_with_Pope_Gregory_XIII_1585.png)

The Japanese ambassadors of Tennsho, Keisho, headed by [Itō Mancio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/It%C5%8D_Mancio "Itō Mancio") meet with Pope Gregory XIII in 1585.

Though he feared the invasion of Europe by the [Turks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ottoman_Empire "Ottoman Empire"), Gregory XIII's attentions were more consistently directed to the dangers of the [Protestants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protestants "Protestants"). He encouraged the plans of Philip II to dethrone [Elizabeth I of England](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elizabeth_I_of_England "Elizabeth I of England") (reigned from 1558 to 1603), resulting in English Protestants suspecting [Catholics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catholic_Church "Catholic Church") as potential traitors and subversives.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

In 1578, to further the plans of exiled English and Irish Catholics such as [Nicholas Sanders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nicholas_Sanders "Nicholas Sanders"), [William Allen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Allen_(cardinal) "William Allen (cardinal)"), and [James Fitzmaurice FitzGerald](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Fitzmaurice_FitzGerald "James Fitzmaurice FitzGerald"), Gregory outfitted adventurer [Thomas Stukeley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Stukeley "Thomas Stukeley") with a ship and an army of 800 men to land in [Ireland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ireland "Ireland") to aid the Catholics against the Protestant plantations.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pope_Gregory_XIII#cite_note-12) To his dismay, Stukeley joined his forces with those of King [Sebastian of Portugal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sebastian_of_Portugal "Sebastian of Portugal") against Sultan [Abdul Malik of Morocco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abu_Marwan_Abd_al-Malik_I_Saadi "Abu Marwan Abd al-Malik I Saadi") instead.

Another papal expedition sailed to Ireland in 1579 with a mere 50 soldiers under the command of Fitzmaurice, accompanied by Sanders as [papal legate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Papal_legate "Papal legate").[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] They took part in the [Second Desmond Rebellion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_Desmond_Rebellion "Second Desmond Rebellion"). All of the soldiers and sailors on board, as well as the women and children who accompanied them, were beheaded or hanged on landing in Kerry in the [Smerwick Massacre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siege_of_Smerwick "Siege of Smerwick").

In 1580, he was persuaded by English [Jesuits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jesuits "Jesuits") to moderate or suspend the Bull _[Regnans in Excelsis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regnans\_in\_Excelsis "Regnans in Excelsis")_ (1570), which had excommunicated Queen Elizabeth I of England. Catholics were advised to obey the queen outwardly in all civil matters until such time as a suitable opportunity presented itself for her overthrow.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pope_Gregory_XIII#cite_note-13)

[![Image 22](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Gregory_XIII_medal.jpg/250px-Gregory_XIII_medal.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Gregory_XIII_medal.jpg)

_Ugonottorum Strages_ medal

After the [St. Bartholomew's Day Massacres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/St._Bartholomew%27s_Day_Massacre "St. Bartholomew's Day Massacre") of [Huguenots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Huguenot "Huguenot") in France in 1572, Pope Gregory signaled his approval and celebrated a _[Te Deum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Te\_Deum "Te Deum")_ mass. Three frescoes in the [Sala Regia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sala_Regia_(Vatican) "Sala Regia (Vatican)") hall of the Vatican depicting the events were commissioned and painted by [Giorgio Vasari](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giorgio_Vasari "Giorgio Vasari"). A commemorative medal was issued with Gregory's portrait and on the obverse a chastising angel, sword in hand and the legend UGONOTTORUM STRAGES ("Overthrow of the Huguenots").[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pope_Gregory_XIII#cite_note-History-volVII-14)

Gregory XIII was visited by the [Tenshō embassy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensh%C5%8D_embassy "Tenshō embassy") of Japan, becoming the first Pope to have received such an embassy. On behalf of the Japanese ruler [Oda Nobunaga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oda_Nobunaga "Oda Nobunaga"), they gifted him with the so-called [Azuchi Screens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Azuchi_Screens "Azuchi Screens"), which were put on display within the Vatican.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pope_Gregory_XIII#cite_note-15)

### Cultural patronage

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Pope_Gregory_XIII&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Cultural patronage")]

In Rome, Gregory XIII built the Gregorian chapel in the [Basilica of St. Peter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basilica_of_St._Peter "Basilica of St. Peter") and extended the [Quirinal Palace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quirinal_Palace "Quirinal Palace") in 1580. He also turned the [Baths of Diocletian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baths_of_Diocletian "Baths of Diocletian") into a granary in 1575.

He appointed his illegitimate son [Giacomo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giacomo_Boncompagni "Giacomo Boncompagni"),[[c]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pope_Gregory_XIII#cite_note-16) born to his mistress at [Bologna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bologna "Bologna") before his papacy, [castellan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Castellan "Castellan") of [Sant'Angelo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Castel_Sant%27Angelo "Castel Sant'Angelo") and [Gonfalonier of the Church](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gonfalonier_of_the_Church "Gonfalonier of the Church"); [Venice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_Venice "Republic of Venice"), anxious to please the Pope, enrolled his son among its nobles, and Philip II of Spain appointed him general in his army. Gregory also helped his son to become a powerful feudatory through the acquisition of the [Duchy of Sora](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duchy_of_Sora "Duchy of Sora"), on the border between the [Papal States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Papal_States "Papal States") and the [Kingdom of Naples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Naples "Kingdom of Naples").

To raise funds for his endeavours, Gregory confiscated a large proportion of the houses and properties throughout the states of the Church. This measure enriched his treasury for a time, but alienated a great body of the nobility and gentry, revived old factions, and created new ones, and caused economic and social chaos in the Papal States.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pope_Gregory_XIII#cite_note-17)

### Canonizations and beatifications

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Pope_Gregory_XIII&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Canonizations and beatifications")]

The pope canonized four saints during his pontificate and in 1584 beatified his predecessor [Pope Gregory VII](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pope_Gregory_VII "Pope Gregory VII").

### Consistories

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Pope_Gregory_XIII&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Consistories")]

Main article: [Cardinals created by Gregory XIII](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardinals_created_by_Gregory_XIII "Cardinals created by Gregory XIII")

[![Image 23](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Tomb_of_Gregory_XIII.jpg/250px-Tomb_of_Gregory_XIII.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Tomb_of_Gregory_XIII.jpg)

Gregory XIII tomb in the [St Peter Basilica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/St._Peter_Basilica "St. Peter Basilica") in [Vatican City](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vatican_City "Vatican City")

During his pontificate, the pope created 34 cardinals in eight consistories; this included naming his nephew [Filippo Boncompagni](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Filippo_Boncompagni "Filippo Boncompagni") to the cardinalate in the pope's first consistory in 1572. Gregory XIII also named four of his successors as cardinals all in 1583: [Giovanni Battista Castagna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pope_Urban_VII "Pope Urban VII") (Urban VII), [Niccolò Sfondrati](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pope_Gregory_XIV "Pope Gregory XIV") (Gregory XIV), [Giovanni Antonio Facchinetti](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pope_Innocent_IX "Pope Innocent IX") (Innocent IX), and [Alessandro de' Medici](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pope_Leo_XI "Pope Leo XI") (Leo XI).

Death
-----

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Pope_Gregory_XIII&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Death")]

The pope suffered from a fever on 5 April 1585 and on 7 April said his usual private Mass, still in ill health. He seemed to recover enough that he was able to conduct meetings throughout 8 and 9 April, although it was observed he did not feel well. But a sudden change on 10 April saw him confined in his bed and it was observed that he had a cold sweat and a weak pulse; he received [Extreme Unction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extreme_Unction "Extreme Unction") moments before he died.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pope_Gregory_XIII#cite_note-18)
