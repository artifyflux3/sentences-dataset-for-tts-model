The **cosmic microwave background** (**CMB**, **CMBR**), or **relic radiation**, is [microwave radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microwave_radiation "Microwave radiation") that fills all space in the [observable universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe "Observable universe"). With a standard [optical telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optical_telescope "Optical telescope"), the background space between stars and galaxies is almost completely dark. However, a sufficiently sensitive [radio telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radio_telescope "Radio telescope") detects a faint background glow that is almost [uniform](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotropic "Isotropic") and is not associated with any star, galaxy, or other [object](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_object "Astronomical object"). This glow is strongest in the [microwave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microwave "Microwave") region of the electromagnetic spectrum. Its total energy density exceeds that of all the photons emitted by all the stars in the history of the universe. The accidental [discovery of the CMB](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discovery_of_cosmic_microwave_background_radiation "Discovery of cosmic microwave background radiation") in 1965 by American radio astronomers [Arno Allan Penzias](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arno_Allan_Penzias "Arno Allan Penzias") and [Robert Woodrow Wilson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Woodrow_Wilson "Robert Woodrow Wilson") was the culmination of work initiated in the 1940s.

The CMB is landmark evidence of the [Big Bang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang "Big Bang")[theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_theory "Scientific theory") for the origin of the universe. In the Big Bang [cosmological models](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmological_model "Cosmological model"), during the earliest periods, the universe was filled with an [opaque](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Opacity_(optics) "Opacity (optics)") fog of dense, hot [plasma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasma_(physics) "Plasma (physics)") of [sub-atomic particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sub-atomic_particle "Sub-atomic particle"). As the universe expanded, this plasma cooled to the point where protons and electrons combined to form neutral atoms of mostly hydrogen. Unlike the plasma, these atoms could not scatter thermal radiation by [Thomson scattering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomson_scattering "Thomson scattering"), and so the universe became transparent. Known as the [recombination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recombination_(cosmology) "Recombination (cosmology)") epoch, this [decoupling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoupling_(cosmology) "Decoupling (cosmology)") event released [photons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photons "Photons") to travel freely through space. However, the photons have grown less [energetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon_energy "Photon energy") due to the [cosmological redshift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmological_redshift "Cosmological redshift") associated with the [expansion of the universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expansion_of_the_universe "Expansion of the universe"). The _surface of last scattering_ refers to a shell at the right distance in space so photons are now received that were originally emitted at the time of decoupling.

The CMB is very smooth and uniform, but maps by sensitive detectors detect small but important temperature variations. Ground and space-based experiments such as [COBE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_Background_Explorer "Cosmic Background Explorer"), [WMAP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe "Wilkinson Microwave Anisotropy Probe") and [Planck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_(spacecraft) "Planck (spacecraft)") have been used to measure these temperature inhomogeneities. The anisotropy structure is influenced by various interactions of matter and photons up to the point of decoupling, which results in a characteristic pattern of tiny ripples that varies with angular scale. The [distribution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distribution_(mathematics) "Distribution (mathematics)") of the anisotropy across the sky has [frequency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frequency "Frequency") components that can be represented by a [power spectrum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_spectrum "Power spectrum") displaying a sequence of peaks and valleys. The peak values of this spectrum hold important information about the physical properties of the early universe: the first peak determines the overall [curvature of the universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvature_of_the_universe "Curvature of the universe"), while the second and third peak detail the density of normal matter and so-called [dark matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter "Dark matter"), respectively. Extracting fine details from the CMB data can be challenging, since the emission has undergone modification by foreground features such as [galaxy clusters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_cluster "Galaxy cluster").

Graph of cosmic microwave background spectrum around its peak in the [microwave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microwave "Microwave") frequency range,[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-Komatsu2022Review-1) as measured by the FIRAS instrument on the [COBE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_Background_Explorer "Cosmic Background Explorer").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-3) While vastly exaggerated "[error bars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_error_of_estimation "Standard error of estimation")" were included here to show the measured data points, the true error bars are too small to be seen even in an enlarged image, and it is impossible to distinguish the observed data from the [blackbody](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blackbody "Blackbody") spectrum for 2.725[K](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvin "Kelvin").

The cosmic microwave background radiation is an emission of uniform [black body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_body "Black body")[thermal energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermal_energy "Thermal energy") coming from all directions. Intensity of the CMB is expressed in [kelvin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvin "Kelvin") (K), the [SI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/System_International "System International") unit of temperature. The CMB has a thermal [black body spectrum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_body_spectrum "Black body spectrum") at a temperature of 2.725 48±0.000 57 K.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-apj707_2_916-4) Variations in intensity are expressed as variations in temperature. The blackbody temperature uniquely characterizes the intensity of the radiation at all wavelengths; a measured [brightness temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brightness_temperature "Brightness temperature") at any wavelength can be converted to a blackbody temperature.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-WrightUCLASite-5)

The radiation is remarkably uniform across the sky, very unlike the almost point-like structure of stars or clumps of stars in galaxies.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-HuDodelsonReview-6) The radiation is [isotropic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotropic "Isotropic") to roughly one part in 25,000: the [root mean square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Root_mean_square "Root mean square") variations are just over 100 μK,[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-PlanckV-7) after subtracting a [dipole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dipole "Dipole") anisotropy from the [Doppler shift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doppler_shift "Doppler shift") of the background radiation. The latter is caused by the [peculiar velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peculiar_velocity "Peculiar velocity") of the Sun relative to the [comoving](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comoving_distance#Comoving_coordinates "Comoving distance") cosmic rest frame as it moves at 369.82±0.11 km/s towards the constellation [Crater](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crater_(constellation) "Crater (constellation)") near its boundary with the constellation [Leo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leo_(constellation) "Leo (constellation)")[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-PlanckI-8) The CMB dipole and [aberration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aberration_of_light "Aberration of light") at higher multipoles have been measured, consistent with galactic motion.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-PlanckXXVII-9) Despite the very small degree of anisotropy in the CMB, many aspects can be measured with high precision and such measurements are critical for cosmological theories.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-HuDodelsonReview-6)

In addition to temperature anisotropy, the CMB should have an angular variation in [polarization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polarization_(physics) "Polarization (physics)"). The polarisation at each direction in the sky has an orientation described in terms of E-mode and B-mode polarization. The E-mode signal is a factor of 10 less strong than the temperature anisotropy; it supplements the temperature data as they are correlated. The B-mode signal is even weaker but may contain additional cosmological data.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-HuDodelsonReview-6)

The anisotropy is related to physical origin of the polarisation. Excitation of an electron by linear polarised light generates polarized light at 90 degrees to the incident direction. If the incoming radiation is isotropic, different incoming directions create polarizations that cancel out. If the incoming radiation has quadrupole anisotropy, residual polarization will be seen.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-10)

Other than the temperature and polarization anisotropy, the CMB frequency spectrum is expected to feature tiny departures from the black-body law known as [spectral distortions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_spectral_distortions "Cosmic microwave background spectral distortions"). These are also at the focus of an active research effort with the hope of a first measurement within the forthcoming decades, as they contain a wealth of information about the primordial universe and the formation of structures at late time.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-Voyage2050-11)

The CMB contains the vast majority of photons in the universe by a factor of 400 to 1;[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-HistoryOfAlternatives-12): 5  the number density of photons in the CMB is one billion times (10 9) the number density of matter in the universe. The total energy density of the CMB exceeds that of all the photons emitted by all the stars in the history of the universe.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-Ryden-2016-13): 69  Without the expansion of the universe to cause the cooling of the CMB, the night sky would shine as brightly as the Sun.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-14) The energy density of the CMB is 0.260 eV/cm 3 (4.17×10−14 J/m 3), about 411 photons/cm 3.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-15)

In 1931, [Georges Lemaître](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georges_Lema%C3%AEtre "Georges Lemaître") speculated that remnants of the early universe may be observable as radiation, but his candidate was [cosmic rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_rays "Cosmic rays").[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-PeeblesPrinciples-16): 140 [Richard C. Tolman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_C._Tolman "Richard C. Tolman") showed in 1934 that expansion of the universe would cool blackbody radiation while maintaining a thermal spectrum. The cosmic microwave background was first predicted in 1948 by [Ralph Alpher](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ralph_Alpher "Ralph Alpher") and [Robert Herman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Herman "Robert Herman"), in a correction[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-17) they prepared for a paper by Alpher's PhD advisor [George Gamow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_Gamow "George Gamow").[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-18) Alpher and Herman were able to estimate the temperature of the cosmic microwave background to be 5 K.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-19)

The [Holmdel Horn Antenna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holmdel_Horn_Antenna "Holmdel Horn Antenna") on which Penzias and Wilson discovered the cosmic microwave background.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-NYT-20230905-20)

The first published recognition of the CMB radiation as a detectable phenomenon appeared in a brief paper by [Soviet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Union "Soviet Union") astrophysicists [A. G. Doroshkevich](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A._G._Doroshkevich "A. G. Doroshkevich") and [Igor Novikov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Igor_Dmitriyevich_Novikov "Igor Dmitriyevich Novikov"), in the spring of 1964.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-Penzias-Nobel-1979-21) In 1964, [David Todd Wilkinson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Todd_Wilkinson "David Todd Wilkinson") and Peter Roll, [Robert H. Dicke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_H._Dicke "Robert H. Dicke")'s colleagues at [Princeton University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Princeton_University "Princeton University"), began constructing a [Dicke radiometer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dicke_radiometer "Dicke radiometer") to measure the cosmic microwave background.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-22) In 1964, [Arno Penzias](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arno_Penzias "Arno Penzias") and [Robert Woodrow Wilson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Woodrow_Wilson "Robert Woodrow Wilson") at the [Crawford Hill](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crawford_Hill "Crawford Hill") location of [Bell Telephone Laboratories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bell_Telephone_Laboratories "Bell Telephone Laboratories") in nearby [Holmdel Township, New Jersey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holmdel_Township,_New_Jersey "Holmdel Township, New Jersey") had built a Dicke radiometer that they intended to use for radio astronomy and satellite communication experiments. The antenna was constructed in 1959 to support [Project Echo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Project_Echo "Project Echo")—the National Aeronautics and Space Administration's passive communications satellites, which used large Earth orbiting aluminized plastic balloons as reflectors to bounce radio signals from one point on the Earth to another.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-NYT-20230905-20) On 20 May 1964 they made their first measurement clearly showing the presence of the microwave background,[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-23) with their instrument having an excess 4.2K [antenna temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noise_temperature "Noise temperature") which they could not account for. After receiving a telephone call from Crawford Hill, Dicke said "Boys, we've been scooped."[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-Penzias&Wilson-24)[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-25)[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-26)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-PeeblesPrinciples-16): 140  A meeting between the Princeton and Crawford Hill groups determined that the antenna temperature was indeed due to the microwave background. Penzias and Wilson received the 1978 [Nobel Prize in Physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physics "Nobel Prize in Physics") for their discovery.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-PenziasWilsonNobelSummary-27)

The interpretation of the cosmic microwave background was a controversial issue in the late 1960s. Alternative explanations included energy from within the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System"), from galaxies, from intergalactic plasma and from multiple extragalactic radio sources. Two requirements would show that the microwave radiation was truly "cosmic". First, the intensity vs frequency or spectrum needed to be shown to match a thermal or blackbody source. This was accomplished by 1968 in a series of measurements of the radiation temperature at higher and lower wavelengths. Second, the radiation needed be shown to be isotropic, the same from all directions. This was also accomplished by 1970, demonstrating that this radiation was truly cosmic in origin.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-PartridgeReview-28)

In the 1970s numerous studies showed that tiny deviations from isotropy in the CMB could result from events in the early universe.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-PartridgeReview-28): 8.5.1  Harrison,[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-29) Peebles and Yu,[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-30) and Zel'dovich[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-31) realized that the early universe would require quantum inhomogeneities that would result in temperature anisotropy at the level of 10−4 or 10−5.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-PartridgeReview-28): 8.5.3.2 [Rashid Sunyaev](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rashid_Sunyaev "Rashid Sunyaev"), using the alternative name _relic radiation_, calculated the observable imprint that these inhomogeneities would have on the cosmic microwave background.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-32)

After a lull in the 1970s caused in part by the many experimental difficulties in measuring CMB at high precision,[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-PartridgeReview-28): 8.5.1  increasingly stringent limits on the anisotropy of the cosmic microwave background were set by ground-based experiments during the 1980s. [RELIKT-1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RELIKT-1 "RELIKT-1"), a Soviet cosmic microwave background anisotropy experiment on board the Prognoz 9 satellite (launched 1 July 1983), gave the first upper limits on the large-scale anisotropy.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-PartridgeReview-28): 8.5.3.2

The other key event in the 1980s was the proposal by [Alan Guth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alan_Guth "Alan Guth") for [cosmic inflation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_inflation "Cosmic inflation"). This theory of rapid spatial expansion gave an explanation for large-scale isotropy by allowing causal connection just before the epoch of last scattering.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-PartridgeReview-28): 8.5.4  With this and similar theories, detailed prediction encouraged larger and more ambitious experiments.

The [NASA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NASA "NASA") Cosmic Background Explorer ([COBE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_Background_Explorer "Cosmic Background Explorer")) satellite orbited Earth in 1989–1996 detected and quantified the large-scale anisotropies at the limit of its detection capabilities. The [NASA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NASA "NASA")[COBE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_Background_Explorer "Cosmic Background Explorer") mission clearly confirmed the primary anisotropy with the Differential Microwave Radiometer instrument, publishing their findings in 1992.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-33)[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-34) The team received the [Nobel Prize](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nobel_Prize "Nobel Prize") in physics for 2006 for this discovery.

### Precision cosmology

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cosmic_microwave_background&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Precision cosmology")]

Inspired by the COBE results, a series of ground and balloon-based experiments measured cosmic microwave background anisotropies on smaller angular scales over the[_[which?](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid\_weasel\_words "Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words")_] two decades. The sensitivity of the new experiments improved dramatically, with a reduction in internal noise by three orders of magnitude.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-Komatsu2022Review-1) The primary goal of these experiments was to measure the scale of the first acoustic peak, which COBE did not have sufficient resolution to resolve. This peak corresponds to large scale density variations in the early universe that are created by gravitational instabilities, resulting in acoustical oscillations in the plasma.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-35) The first peak in the anisotropy was tentatively detected by the [MAT/TOCO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mobile_Anisotropy_Telescope "Mobile Anisotropy Telescope") experiment[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-36) and the result was confirmed by the [BOOMERanG](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BOOMERanG_experiment "BOOMERanG experiment")[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-37) and [MAXIMA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Millimeter_Anisotropy_eXperiment_IMaging_Array "Millimeter Anisotropy eXperiment IMaging Array") experiments.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-38) These measurements demonstrated that the [geometry of the universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shape_of_the_universe "Shape of the universe") is approximately flat, rather than [curved](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curved_space "Curved space").[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-39) They ruled out [cosmic strings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_string "Cosmic string") as a major component of cosmic structure formation and suggested [cosmic inflation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_inflation "Cosmic inflation") was the right theory of structure formation.[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-40)

### Observations after COBE

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cosmic_microwave_background&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Observations after COBE")]

[![Image 4](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/PIA16874-CobeWmapPlanckComparison-20130321.jpg/250px-PIA16874-CobeWmapPlanckComparison-20130321.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:PIA16874-CobeWmapPlanckComparison-20130321.jpg)

Comparison of [CMB](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CMB "CMB") results from [COBE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_Background_Explorer "Cosmic Background Explorer"), [WMAP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WMAP "WMAP") and [Planck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_(spacecraft) "Planck (spacecraft)")

(March 21, 2013)

Inspired by the initial COBE results of an extremely isotropic and homogeneous background, a series of ground- and balloon-based experiments quantified CMB anisotropies on smaller angular scales over the next decade. The primary goal of these experiments was to measure the angular scale of the first acoustic peak, for which COBE did not have sufficient resolution. These measurements were able to rule out [cosmic strings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_string "Cosmic string") as the leading theory of cosmic structure formation, and suggested [cosmic inflation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_inflation "Cosmic inflation") was the right theory.

During the 1990s, the first peak was measured with increasing sensitivity and by 2000 the [BOOMERanG experiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BOOMERanG_experiment "BOOMERanG experiment") reported that the highest power fluctuations occur at scales of approximately one angular degree. Together with other cosmological data, these results implied that the geometry of the universe is [flat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flat_spacetime "Flat spacetime"). A number of ground-based [interferometers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interferometer "Interferometer") provided measurements of the fluctuations with higher accuracy over the next three years, including the [Very Small Array](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Very_Small_Array "Very Small Array"), [Degree Angular Scale Interferometer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_Angular_Scale_Interferometer "Degree Angular Scale Interferometer") (DASI), and the [Cosmic Background Imager](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_Background_Imager "Cosmic Background Imager") (CBI). DASI made the first detection of the polarization of the CMB and the CBI provided the first E-mode polarization spectrum with compelling evidence that it is out of phase with the T-mode spectrum.

### Wilkinson Microwave Anisotropy Probe

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cosmic_microwave_background&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Wilkinson Microwave Anisotropy Probe")]

In June 2001, [NASA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NASA "NASA") launched a second CMB space mission, [WMAP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WMAP "WMAP"), to make much more precise measurements of the large-scale anisotropies over the full sky. [WMAP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WMAP "WMAP") used symmetric, rapid-multi-modulated scanning, rapid switching radiometers at five frequencies to minimize non-sky signal noise.[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-FirstWMAP-41) The data from the mission was released in five installments, the last being the nine-year summary. The results are broadly consistent [Lambda CDM](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lambda_CDM "Lambda CDM") models based on 6 free parameters and fitting in to Big Bang cosmology with [cosmic inflation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_inflation "Cosmic inflation").[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-WMAP9Map-42)

### Atacama Cosmology Telescope

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cosmic_microwave_background&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Atacama Cosmology Telescope")]

A third space mission, the [ESA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Space_Agency "European Space Agency") (European Space Agency) [Planck Surveyor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_Surveyor "Planck Surveyor"), was launched in May 2009 and performed an even more detailed investigation until it was shut down in October 2013. Planck employed both [HEMT](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HEMT "HEMT") radiometers and [bolometer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bolometer "Bolometer") technology and measured the CMB at a smaller scale than WMAP. Its detectors were trialled in the Antarctic [Viper telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viper_telescope "Viper telescope") as ACBAR ([Arcminute Cosmology Bolometer Array Receiver](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arcminute_Cosmology_Bolometer_Array_Receiver "Arcminute Cosmology Bolometer Array Receiver")) experiment—which has produced the most precise measurements at small angular scales to date—and in the [Archeops](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archeops "Archeops") balloon telescope.

On 21 March 2013, the European-led research team behind the [_Planck_ cosmology probe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_(spacecraft) "Planck (spacecraft)") released the mission's all-sky map ([565x318 jpeg](https://web.archive.org/web/20131202233029/http://esacmt.esac.esa.int/science-e-media/img/61/51553_Planck_CMB_Mollweide_565.jpg), [3600x1800 jpeg](https://web.archive.org/web/20170215024745/https://www.nasa.gov/images/content/735683main_pia16873-full_full.jpg)) of the cosmic microwave background.[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-NASA-20130321-48)[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-NYT-20130321g-49) The map suggests the universe is slightly older than researchers expected. According to the map, subtle fluctuations in temperature were imprinted on the deep sky when the cosmos was about 370 000 years old. The imprint reflects ripples that arose as early, in the existence of the universe, as the first [nonillionth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nonillion#Standard_dictionary_numbers "Nonillion") (10−30) of a second. Apparently, these ripples gave rise to the present vast [cosmic web](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_Web#Large-scale_structure "Cosmic Web") of [galaxy clusters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_cluster "Galaxy cluster") and [dark matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter "Dark matter"). Based on the 2013 data, the universe contains 4.9% [ordinary matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matter "Matter"), 26.8% [dark matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter "Dark matter") and 68.3% [dark energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_energy "Dark energy"). On 5 February 2015, new data was released by the _Planck_ mission, according to which the age of the universe is 13.799±0.021[billion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1,000,000,000_(number) "1,000,000,000 (number)") years old and the [Hubble constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble_constant "Hubble constant") was measured to be 67.74±0.46(km/s)/Mpc.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-Planck_2015-50)

### South Pole Telescope

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cosmic_microwave_background&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: South Pole Telescope")]

The cosmic microwave background radiation and the [cosmological redshift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble%27s_law "Hubble's law")-distance relation are together regarded as the best available evidence for the [Big Bang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang "Big Bang") event. Measurements of the CMB have made the inflationary Big Bang model the [Standard Cosmological Model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Cosmological_Model "Standard Cosmological Model").[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-52) The discovery of the CMB in the mid-1960s curtailed interest in [alternatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-standard_cosmology "Non-standard cosmology") such as the [steady state theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steady_state_theory "Steady state theory").[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-53)

In the [Big Bang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang "Big Bang") model for the formation of the [universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe "Universe"), [inflationary cosmology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inflation_(cosmology) "Inflation (cosmology)") predicts that after about 10−37 seconds[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-54) the nascent universe underwent [exponential growth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponential_growth "Exponential growth") that smoothed out nearly all irregularities. The remaining irregularities were caused by quantum fluctuations in the [inflaton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inflaton "Inflaton") field that caused the inflation event.[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-55) Long before the formation of stars and planets, the early universe was more compact, much hotter and, starting 10−6 seconds after the Big Bang, filled with a uniform glow from its white-hot fog of interacting [plasma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasma_(physics) "Plasma (physics)") of [photons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon "Photon"), [electrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron "Electron"), and [baryons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baryon "Baryon").

As the universe [expanded](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_expansion_of_space "Metric expansion of space"), [adiabatic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adiabatic_process "Adiabatic process") cooling caused the energy density of the plasma to decrease until it became favorable for [electrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron "Electron") to combine with [protons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton "Proton"), forming [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen") atoms. This [recombination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recombination_(cosmology) "Recombination (cosmology)") event happened when the temperature was around 3000 K or when the universe was approximately 379,000 years old.[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-56) As photons did not interact with these electrically neutral atoms, the former began to travel [freely](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_streaming "Free streaming") through space, resulting in the [decoupling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoupling_(cosmology) "Decoupling (cosmology)") of matter and radiation.[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-57)

The [color temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Color_temperature "Color temperature") of the ensemble of decoupled photons has continued to diminish ever since; now down to 2.7260±0.0013 K,[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-apj707_2_916-4) it will continue to drop as the universe expands. The intensity of the radiation corresponds to black-body radiation at 2.726 K because red-shifted black-body radiation is just like black-body radiation at a lower temperature. According to the Big Bang model, the radiation from the sky we measure today comes from a spherical surface called _the surface of last scattering_. This represents the set of locations in space at which the decoupling event is estimated to have occurred[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-58)[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-59) and at a point in time such that the photons from that distance have just reached observers. Most of the radiation energy in the universe is in the cosmic microwave background,[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-60) making up a fraction of roughly 6×10−5 of the total density of the universe.[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-61)

Two of the greatest successes of the Big Bang theory are its prediction of the almost perfect black body spectrum and its detailed prediction of the anisotropies in the cosmic microwave background. The CMB spectrum has become the most precisely measured black body spectrum in nature.[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-dpf99-62)

### Predictions based on the Big Bang model

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cosmic_microwave_background&action=edit&section=16 "Edit section: Predictions based on the Big Bang model")]

In the late 1940s Alpher and Herman reasoned that if there was a Big Bang, the expansion of the universe would have stretched the high-energy radiation of the very early universe into the microwave region of the [electromagnetic spectrum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum "Electromagnetic spectrum"), and down to a temperature of about 5 K. They were slightly off with their estimate, but they had the right idea. They predicted the CMB. It took another 15 years for Penzias and Wilson to discover that the microwave background was actually there.[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-Apeiron2_3_79-63)

According to standard cosmology, the CMB gives a snapshot of the hot early [universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe "Universe") at the point in time when the temperature dropped enough to allow [electrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron "Electron") and [protons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton "Proton") to form [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen") atoms. This event made the universe nearly transparent to radiation because light was no longer being [scattered](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomson_scattering "Thomson scattering") off free electrons.[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-64) When this occurred some 380,000 years after the Big Bang, the temperature of the universe was about 3,000 K. This corresponds to an ambient energy of about 0.26[eV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronvolt "Electronvolt"), which is much less than the 13.6 eV ionization energy of hydrogen.[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-65) This epoch is generally known as the "time of last scattering" or the period of [recombination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recombination_(cosmology) "Recombination (cosmology)") or [decoupling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoupling_(cosmology) "Decoupling (cosmology)").[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-66)

Since decoupling, the color temperature of the background radiation has dropped by an average factor of 1,089[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-FirstWMAP-41) due to the expansion of the universe. As the universe expands, the CMB photons are [redshifted](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift "Redshift"), causing them to decrease in energy. The color temperature of this radiation stays [inversely proportional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inversely_proportional "Inversely proportional") to a parameter that describes the relative expansion of the universe over time, known as the [scale length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scale_factor_(universe) "Scale factor (universe)"). The color temperature _T_ r of the CMB as a function of redshift, _z_, can be shown to be proportional to the color temperature of the CMB as observed in the present day (2.725 K or 0.2348 meV):[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-67)

_T_ r = 2.725 K × (1 + _z_)
The high degree of uniformity throughout the [observable universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe "Observable universe") and its faint but measured anisotropy lend strong support for the Big Bang model in general and the [ΛCDM ("Lambda Cold Dark Matter") model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lambda-CDM_model "Lambda-CDM model") in particular. Moreover, the fluctuations are [coherent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coherence_(physics) "Coherence (physics)") on angular scales that are larger than the apparent [cosmological horizon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmological_horizon "Cosmological horizon") at recombination. Either such coherence is acausally [fine-tuned](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fine-tuning_(physics) "Fine-tuning (physics)"), or [cosmic inflation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_inflation "Cosmic inflation") occurred.[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-hep-ph/0309057-68)[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-69)

[![Image 5](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/PowerSpectrumExt.svg/330px-PowerSpectrumExt.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:PowerSpectrumExt.svg)

The power spectrum of the cosmic microwave background radiation temperature anisotropy in terms of the angular scale (or [multipole moment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multipole_moment "Multipole moment")). The data shown comes from the [WMAP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WMAP "WMAP") (2006), [Acbar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arcminute_Cosmology_Bolometer_Array_Receiver "Arcminute Cosmology Bolometer Array Receiver") (2004) [Boomerang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BOOMERanG_experiment "BOOMERanG experiment") (2005), [CBI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_Background_Imager "Cosmic Background Imager") (2004), and [VSA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Very_Small_Array "Very Small Array") (2004) instruments. Also shown is a theoretical model (solid line).

The [anisotropy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anisotropy "Anisotropy"), or directional dependency, of the cosmic microwave background is divided into two types: primary anisotropy, due to effects that occur at the surface of last scattering and before; and secondary anisotropy, due to effects such as interactions of the background radiation with intervening hot gas or gravitational potentials, which occur between the last scattering surface and the observer.

The structure of the cosmic microwave background anisotropies is principally determined by two effects: acoustic oscillations and [diffusion damping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffusion_damping "Diffusion damping") (also called collisionless damping or [Silk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Silk "Joseph Silk") damping). The acoustic oscillations arise because of a conflict in the [photon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon "Photon")–[baryon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baryon "Baryon") plasma in the early universe. The pressure of the photons tends to erase anisotropies, whereas the gravitational attraction of the baryons, moving at speeds much slower than light, makes them tend to collapse to form overdensities. These two effects compete to create acoustic oscillations, which give the microwave background its characteristic peak structure. The peaks correspond, roughly, to resonances in which the photons decouple when a particular mode is at its peak amplitude.

The peaks contain interesting physical signatures. The angular scale of the first peak determines the [curvature of the universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shape_of_the_universe "Shape of the universe") (but not the [topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology "Topology") of the universe). The next peak—ratio of the odd peaks to the even peaks—determines the reduced baryon density.[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-70) The third peak can be used to get information about the dark-matter density.[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-71)

The locations of the peaks give important information about the nature of the primordial density perturbations. There are two fundamental types of density perturbations called _adiabatic_ and _isocurvature_. A general density perturbation is a mixture of both, and different theories that purport to explain the primordial density perturbation spectrum predict different mixtures.

Adiabatic density perturbations In an adiabatic density perturbation, the fractional additional number density of each type of particle (baryons, [photons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon "Photon"), etc.) is the same. That is, if at one place there is a 1% higher number density of baryons than average, then at that place there is a 1% higher number density of photons (and a 1% higher number density in neutrinos) than average. [Cosmic inflation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_inflation "Cosmic inflation") predicts that the primordial perturbations are adiabatic.Isocurvature density perturbations In an isocurvature density perturbation, the sum (over different types of particle) of the fractional additional densities is zero. That is, a perturbation where at some spot there is 1% more energy in baryons than average, 1% more energy in photons than average, and 2% _less_ energy in neutrinos than average, would be a pure isocurvature perturbation. Hypothetical [cosmic strings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_string "Cosmic string") would produce mostly isocurvature primordial perturbations.
The CMB spectrum can distinguish between these two because these two types of perturbations produce different peak locations. Isocurvature density perturbations produce a series of peaks whose angular scales (_ℓ_ values of the peaks) are roughly in the ratio 1:3:5:..., while adiabatic density perturbations produce peaks whose locations are in the ratio 1:2:3:...[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-hu_white_1996-72) Observations are consistent with the primordial density perturbations being entirely adiabatic, providing key support for inflation, and ruling out many models of structure formation involving, for example, cosmic strings.

Collisionless damping is caused by two effects, when the treatment of the primordial plasma as [fluid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluid "Fluid") begins to break down:

*   the increasing [mean free path](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mean_free_path "Mean free path") of the photons as the primordial plasma becomes increasingly rarefied in an expanding universe,
*   the finite depth of the last scattering surface (LSS), which causes the mean free path to increase rapidly during decoupling, even while some Compton scattering is still occurring.

These effects contribute about equally to the suppression of anisotropies at small scales and give rise to the characteristic exponential damping tail seen in the very small angular scale anisotropies.

The depth of the LSS refers to the fact that the decoupling of the photons and baryons does not happen instantaneously, but instead requires an appreciable fraction of the age of the universe up to that era. One method of quantifying how long this process took uses the _photon visibility function_ (PVF). This function is defined so that, denoting the PVF by _P_(_t_), the probability that a CMB photon last scattered between time _t_ and _t_ + _dt_ is given by _P_(_t_)_dt_.

The maximum of the PVF (the time when it is most likely that a given CMB photon last scattered) is known quite precisely. The first-year [WMAP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe "Wilkinson Microwave Anisotropy Probe") results put the time at which _P_(_t_) has a maximum as 372,000 years.[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-WMAP_1_cosmo_params-73) This is often taken as the "time" at which the CMB formed. However, to figure out how _long_ it took the photons and baryons to decouple, we need a measure of the width of the PVF. The WMAP team finds that the PVF is greater than half of its maximal value (the "full width at half maximum", or FWHM) over an interval of 115,000 years.[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-WMAP_1_cosmo_params-73): 179  By this measure, decoupling took place over roughly 115,000 years, and thus when it was complete, the universe was roughly 487,000 years old.

#### Late time anisotropy

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cosmic_microwave_background&action=edit&section=18 "Edit section: Late time anisotropy")]

Since the CMB came into existence, it has apparently been modified by several subsequent physical processes, which are collectively referred to as late-time anisotropy, or secondary anisotropy. When the CMB photons became free to travel unimpeded, ordinary matter in the universe was mostly in the form of neutral hydrogen and helium atoms. However, observations of galaxies today seem to indicate that most of the volume of the [intergalactic medium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intergalactic_medium "Intergalactic medium") (IGM) consists of ionized material (since there are few absorption lines due to hydrogen atoms). This implies a period of [reionization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reionization "Reionization") during which some of the material of the universe was broken into hydrogen ions.

The CMB photons are scattered by free charges such as electrons that are not bound in atoms. In an ionized universe, such charged particles have been liberated from neutral atoms by ionizing (ultraviolet) radiation. Today these free charges are at sufficiently low density in most of the volume of the universe that they do not measurably affect the CMB. However, if the IGM was ionized at very early times when the universe was still denser, then there are two main effects on the CMB:

1.   Small scale anisotropies are erased. (Just as when looking at an object through fog, details of the object appear fuzzy.)
2.   The physics of how photons are scattered by free electrons ([Thomson scattering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomson_scattering "Thomson scattering")) induces polarization anisotropies on large angular scales. This broad angle polarization is correlated with the broad angle temperature perturbation.

Both of these effects have been observed by the WMAP spacecraft, providing evidence that the universe was ionized at very early times, at a [redshift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift "Redshift") around 10.[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-WMAP9Cosmo-74) The detailed provenance of this early ionizing radiation is still a matter of scientific debate. It may have included starlight from the very first population of stars ([population III](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Population_III "Population III") stars), supernovae when these first stars reached the end of their lives, or the ionizing radiation produced by the accretion disks of massive black holes.

The time following the emission of the cosmic microwave background—and before the observation of the first stars—is semi-humorously referred to by cosmologists as the [Dark Age](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_the_Big_Bang#Dark_Ages "Timeline of the Big Bang"), and is a period which is under intense study by astronomers (see [21 centimeter radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/21_centimeter_radiation "21 centimeter radiation")).

Two other effects which occurred between reionization and our observations of the cosmic microwave background, and which appear to cause anisotropies, are the [Sunyaev–Zel'dovich effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sunyaev%E2%80%93Zel%27dovich_effect "Sunyaev–Zel'dovich effect"), where a cloud of high-energy electrons scatters the radiation, transferring some of its energy to the CMB photons, and the [Sachs–Wolfe effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sachs%E2%80%93Wolfe_effect "Sachs–Wolfe effect"), which causes photons from the Cosmic Microwave Background to be gravitationally redshifted or blueshifted due to changing gravitational fields.

### Alternative theories

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cosmic_microwave_background&action=edit&section=19 "Edit section: Alternative theories")]

The standard cosmology that includes the Big Bang "enjoys considerable popularity among the practicing cosmologists"[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-NarlikarPadmanabhan-75): 211  However, there are challenges to the standard big bang framework for explaining CMB data. In particular standard cosmology requires [fine-tuning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fine-tuning_(physics) "Fine-tuning (physics)") of some free parameters, with different values supported by different experimental data.[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-NarlikarPadmanabhan-75): 245  As an example of the fine-tuning issue, standard cosmology cannot predict the present temperature of the relic radiation, ![Image 6: {\displaystyle T_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/55b9e7d7b96196b5a6a26f4349caa3ac82fd67e3).[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-NarlikarPadmanabhan-75): 229  This value of ![Image 7: {\displaystyle T_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/55b9e7d7b96196b5a6a26f4349caa3ac82fd67e3) is one of the best results of experimental cosmology and the [steady state model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steady_state_model "Steady state model") can predict it.[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-Apeiron2_3_79-63) However, alternative models have their own set of problems and they have only made post-facto explanations of existing observations.[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-NarlikarPadmanabhan-75): 239  Nevertheless, these alternatives have played an important historic role in providing ideas for and challenges to the standard explanation.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-HistoryOfAlternatives-12)

[![Image 8](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/CMB_power_spectra_-_TT%2C_EE%2C_BB.pdf/page1-400px-CMB_power_spectra_-_TT%2C_EE%2C_BB.pdf.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:CMB_power_spectra_-_TT,_EE,_BB.pdf)

Temperature power spectrum and E-mode and B-mode polarization power spectra of the cosmic microwave background

The cosmic microwave background is [polarized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polarization_(waves) "Polarization (waves)") at the level of a few microkelvin. There are two types of polarization, called E-mode (or gradient-mode) and B-mode (or curl mode).[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-Trippe2014-76) This is in analogy to [electrostatics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrostatics "Electrostatics"), in which the electric field (_E_-field) has a vanishing [curl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curl_(mathematics) "Curl (mathematics)") and the magnetic field (_B_-field) has a vanishing [divergence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divergence "Divergence").

The E-modes arise from [Thomson scattering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomson_scattering "Thomson scattering") in a heterogeneous plasma.[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-Trippe2014-76) E-modes were first seen in 2002 by the [Degree Angular Scale Interferometer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_Angular_Scale_Interferometer "Degree Angular Scale Interferometer") (DASI).[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-77)[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-78)

B-modes are expected to be an order of magnitude weaker than the E-modes. The former are not produced by standard scalar type perturbations, but are generated by [gravitational waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_wave "Gravitational wave") during [cosmic inflation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inflation_(cosmology) "Inflation (cosmology)") shortly after the big bang.[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-SeljakMeasuring-79)[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-SeljakSignature-80)[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-81) However, gravitational lensing of the stronger E-modes can also produce B-mode polarization.[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-SeljakGraviational-82)[[82]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-83) Detecting the original B-modes signal requires analysis of the contamination caused by lensing of the relatively strong E-mode signal.[[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-84)

#### Primordial gravitational waves

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cosmic_microwave_background&action=edit&section=23 "Edit section: Primordial gravitational waves")]

Models of "slow-roll" [cosmic inflation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_inflation "Cosmic inflation") in the [early universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Early_universe "Early universe") predicts primordial [gravitational waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_waves "Gravitational waves") that would impact the polarisation of the cosmic microwave background, creating a specific pattern of [B-mode polarization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polarization_(cosmology) "Polarization (cosmology)"). Detection of this pattern would support the theory of inflation and their strength can confirm and exclude different models of inflation.[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-SeljakSignature-80)[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-KamionkowskiReview-85) While claims that this characteristic pattern of B-mode polarization had been measured by [BICEP2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BICEP_and_Keck_Array "BICEP and Keck Array") instrument[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-NYT-20140922-86) were later attributed to [cosmic dust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_dust "Cosmic dust") due to new results of the [Planck experiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_(spacecraft) "Planck (spacecraft)"),[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-AXV-20140919-87)[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-KamionkowskiReview-85): 253  subsequent reanalysis with compensation for foreground dust show limits in agreement with results from [Lambda-CDM](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lambda-CDM "Lambda-CDM") models.[[87]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-88)

#### Gravitational lensing

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cosmic_microwave_background&action=edit&section=24 "Edit section: Gravitational lensing")]

[![Image 9](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Gravitational_lens-full.jpg/250px-Gravitational_lens-full.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Gravitational_lens-full.jpg)

Artist impression of the gravitational lensing effect of massive cosmic structures

The second type of B-modes was discovered in 2013 using the [South Pole Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Pole_Telescope "South Pole Telescope") with help from the [Herschel Space Observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herschel_Space_Observatory "Herschel Space Observatory").[[88]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-89) In October 2014, a measurement of the B-mode polarization at 150 GHz was published by the [POLARBEAR](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/POLARBEAR "POLARBEAR") experiment.[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-pc1-90) Compared to BICEP2, POLARBEAR focuses on a smaller patch of the sky and is less susceptible to dust effects. The team reported that POLARBEAR's measured B-mode polarization was of cosmological origin (and not just due to dust) at a 97.2% confidence level.[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-cs-91)

[![Image 10](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/WMAP_2008_TT_spectra.png/250px-WMAP_2008_TT_spectra.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:WMAP_2008_TT_spectra.png)

Example Multipole Power Spectrum. WMAP Data are represented as points, curves correspond to the best-fit LCDM model[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-WMAP9Cosmo-74)

The CMB angular anisotropies are usually presented in terms of power per multipole.[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-cmbreview-92) The map of temperature across the sky, ![Image 11: {\displaystyle T(\theta ,\varphi ),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/baf8bbf23c6683cd44387866e4afe8c0d20196b4) is written as coefficients of [spherical harmonics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_harmonics "Spherical harmonics"), ![Image 12: {\displaystyle T(\theta ,\varphi )=\sum _{\ell m}a_{\ell m}Y_{\ell m}(\theta ,\varphi )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/196ae4f532abaf7a6fd6ba7bf2893193b617f434) where the ![Image 13: {\displaystyle a_{\ell m}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/296c086e281bc8fcba62a723129d08e481f2a446) term measures the strength of the angular oscillation in ![Image 14: {\displaystyle Y_{\ell m}(\theta ,\varphi )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9316131b8f7e0791a44c24deaf5cd46323c16bc4), and _ℓ_ is the multipole number while _m_ is the azimuthal number. The azimuthal variation is not significant and is removed by applying the [angular correlation function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_correlation_function "Angular correlation function"), giving power spectrum term![Image 15: {\displaystyle C_{\ell }\equiv \langle |a_{\ell m}|^{2}\rangle .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3418a8af26ab4b480cc4d734a9970ec1e8b84c07) Increasing values of _ℓ_ correspond to higher multipole moments of CMB, meaning more rapid variation with angle.

### CMBR monopole term (_ℓ_ = 0)

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cosmic_microwave_background&action=edit&section=26 "Edit section: CMBR monopole term (ℓ = 0)")]

The monopole term, _ℓ_ = 0, is the constant isotropic mean temperature of the CMB, _T_ _γ_ = 2.7255±0.0006 K[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-cmbreview-92) with one standard deviation confidence. This term must be measured with absolute temperature devices, such as the FIRAS instrument on the [COBE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_Background_Explorer "Cosmic Background Explorer") satellite.[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-cmbreview-92): 499

### CMBR dipole anisotropy (_ℓ_ = 1)

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cosmic_microwave_background&action=edit&section=27 "Edit section: CMBR dipole anisotropy (ℓ = 1)")]

CMB dipole represents the largest anisotropy, which is in the first spherical harmonic (_ℓ_ = 1), a cosine function. The amplitude of CMB dipole is around 3.3621±0.0010 mK.[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-cmbreview-92) The CMB dipole moment is interpreted as the peculiar motion of the Earth relative to the CMB. Its amplitude depends on the time due to the Earth's orbit about the barycenter of the solar system. This enables us to add a time-dependent term to the dipole expression. The modulation of this term is 1 year,[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-cmbreview-92)[[92]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-cobe-93) which fits the observation done by COBE FIRAS.[[92]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-cobe-93)[[93]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-94) The dipole moment does not encode any primordial information.

From the CMB data, it is seen that the Sun appears to be moving at 369.82±0.11 km/s relative to the reference frame of the CMB (also called the CMB rest frame, or the frame of reference in which there is no motion through the CMB). The [Local Group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_Group "Local Group") — the galaxy group that includes our own Milky Way galaxy — appears to be moving at 620±15 km/s in the direction of [galactic longitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galactic_longitude "Galactic longitude")_ℓ_ = 271.9°±2°, _b_ = 30°±3°.[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-cmbreview-92) The dipole is now used to calibrate mapping studies.

The temperature variation in the CMB temperature maps at higher multipoles, or _ℓ_ ≥ 2, is considered to be the result of perturbations of the density in the early Universe, before the recombination epoch at a redshift of around _z_ ⋍ 1100. Before recombination, the Universe consisted of a hot, dense plasma of electrons and baryons. In such a hot dense environment, electrons and protons could not form any neutral atoms. The baryons in such early Universe remained highly ionized and so were tightly coupled with photons through the effect of Thompson scattering. These phenomena caused the pressure and gravitational effects to act against each other, and triggered fluctuations in the photon-baryon plasma. Quickly after the recombination epoch, the rapid expansion of the universe caused the plasma to cool down and these fluctuations are "frozen into" the CMB maps we observe today.[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-cmbreview-92)

With the increasingly precise data provided by WMAP, there have been a number of claims that the CMB exhibits anomalies, such as very large scale anisotropies, anomalous alignments, and non-Gaussian distributions.[[94]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-arXiv:0905.2854v2-95)[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-arXiv:astro-ph/0511666-96)[[96]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-arXiv:astro-ph/0503213-97) The most longstanding of these is the low-_ℓ_ multipole controversy. Even in the COBE map, it was observed that the [quadrupole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrupole "Quadrupole") (_ℓ_ = 2, spherical harmonic) has a low amplitude compared to the predictions of the Big Bang. In particular, the quadrupole and octupole (_ℓ_ = 3) modes appear to have an unexplained alignment with each other and with both the [ecliptic plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_of_the_ecliptic "Plane of the ecliptic") and [equinoxes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equinox "Equinox").[[97]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-98)[[98]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-99)[[99]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-100) A number of groups have suggested that this could be the signature of quantum corrections or new physics at the greatest observable scales; other groups suspect systematic errors in the data.[[100]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-101)[[101]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-arXiv:0907.2731v3-102)[[102]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-arXiv:1006.1270v1-103)[[103]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-arXiv:1009.2701v1-104)

Ultimately, due to the foregrounds and the [cosmic variance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_variance "Cosmic variance") problem, the greatest modes will never be as well measured as the small angular scale modes. The analyses were performed on two maps that have had the foregrounds removed as far as possible: the "internal linear combination" map of the WMAP collaboration and a similar map prepared by [Max Tegmark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Max_Tegmark "Max Tegmark") and others.[[104]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-hinshaw07-105)[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-FirstWMAP-41)[[105]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-106) Later analyses have pointed out that these are the modes most susceptible to foreground contamination from synchrotron, dust, and bremsstrahlung emission, and from experimental uncertainty in the monopole and dipole.

A full [Bayesian analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bayesian_analysis "Bayesian analysis") of the WMAP power spectrum demonstrates that the quadrupole prediction of [Lambda-CDM cosmology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lambda-CDM_model "Lambda-CDM model") is consistent with the data at the 10% level and that the observed octupole is not remarkable.[[106]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-107) Carefully accounting for the procedure used to remove the foregrounds from the full sky map further reduces the significance of the alignment by ~5%.[[107]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-108)[[108]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-109)[[109]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-110)[[110]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-111) Recent observations with the [Planck telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_(spacecraft) "Planck (spacecraft)"), which is very much more sensitive than WMAP and has a larger angular resolution, record the same anomaly, and so instrumental error (but not foreground contamination) appears to be ruled out.[[111]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-112) Coincidence is a possible explanation, chief scientist from [WMAP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WMAP "WMAP"), [Charles L. Bennett](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_L._Bennett "Charles L. Bennett") suggested coincidence and human psychology were involved, "I do think there is a bit of a psychological effect; people want to find unusual things."[[112]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-113)

Measurements of the density of quasars based on [Wide-field Infrared Survey Explorer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wide-field_Infrared_Survey_Explorer "Wide-field Infrared Survey Explorer") data finds a dipole significantly different from the one extracted from the CMB anisotropy.[[113]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-SuburSarkar-114) This difference is conflict with the [cosmological principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmological_principle "Cosmological principle").[[114]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-115)

Assuming the universe keeps expanding and it does not suffer a [Big Crunch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Crunch "Big Crunch"), a [Big Rip](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Rip "Big Rip"), or another similar fate, the cosmic microwave background will continue redshifting until it will no longer be detectable,[[115]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-endcosmology-116) and will be superseded first by the one produced by [starlight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Starlight "Starlight"), and perhaps, later by the background radiation fields of processes that may take place in the far future of the universe such as [proton decay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton_decay "Proton decay"), [evaporation of black holes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hawking_radiation "Hawking radiation"), and [positronium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positronium "Positronium") decay.[[116]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-fate-117)

### Thermal (non-microwave background) temperature predictions

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cosmic_microwave_background&action=edit&section=32 "Edit section: Thermal (non-microwave background) temperature predictions")]

*   1896 – [Charles Édouard Guillaume](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_%C3%89douard_Guillaume "Charles Édouard Guillaume") estimates the "radiation of the stars" to be 5–6[K](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvin "Kelvin").[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-Apeiron2_3_79-63)[[117]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-G_1896-118)
*   1926 – Sir [Arthur Eddington](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arthur_Eddington "Arthur Eddington") estimates the non-thermal radiation of [starlight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star#Radiation "Star") in the galaxy "... by the formula _E_ = _σT_ 4 the effective temperature corresponding to this density is 3.18° absolute ... black body".[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-Apeiron2_3_79-63)[[118]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-119)
*   1930s – [Cosmologist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmologist "Cosmologist")[Erich Regener](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erich_Regener "Erich Regener") calculates that the non-thermal spectrum of cosmic rays in the galaxy has an effective temperature of 2.8 K.[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-Apeiron2_3_79-63)
*   1931 – Term _microwave_ first used in print: "When trials with wavelengths as low as 18 cm. were made known, there was undisguised surprise+that the problem of the micro-wave had been solved so soon." _Telegraph & Telephone Journal_ XVII. 179/1
*   1934 – [Richard Tolman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Tolman "Richard Tolman") shows that [black-body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black-body "Black-body") radiation in an expanding universe cools but remains thermal.
*   1946 – [Robert Dicke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Dicke "Robert Dicke") predicts "... radiation from cosmic matter" at <20 K, but did not refer to background radiation.[[119]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-Kragh-120)
*   1946 – [George Gamow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_Gamow "George Gamow") calculates a temperature of 50 K (assuming a 3-billion year old universe),[[120]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-The_Creation_Of_The_Universe-121) commenting it "... is in reasonable agreement with the actual temperature of interstellar space", but does not mention background radiation.[[121]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-122)
*   1953 – [Erwin Finlay-Freundlich](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erwin_Finlay-Freundlich "Erwin Finlay-Freundlich") in support of his [tired light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tired_light "Tired light") theory, derives a blackbody temperature for intergalactic space of 2.3 K and in the following year values of 1.9K and 6.0K.[[122]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-123)

### Microwave background radiation predictions and measurements

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cosmic_microwave_background&action=edit&section=33 "Edit section: Microwave background radiation predictions and measurements")]

*   1941 – [Andrew McKellar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andrew_McKellar "Andrew McKellar") detected a "rotational" temperature of 2.3[K](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvin "Kelvin") for the interstellar medium by comparing the population of CN doublet lines measured by W. S. Adams in a B star.[[123]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-dao7-124)[[124]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-125)
*   1948 – [Ralph Alpher](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ralph_Alpher "Ralph Alpher") and [Robert Herman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Herman "Robert Herman") estimate "the temperature in the universe" at 5 K. Although they do not specifically mention microwave background radiation, it may be inferred.[[125]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-126)
*   1953 – [George Gamow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_Gamow "George Gamow") estimates 7 K based on a model that does not rely on a free parameter[[119]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-Kragh-120)[[126]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-127): 2181 
*   1955 – Émile Le Roux of the [Nançay Radio Observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nan%C3%A7ay_Radio_Observatory "Nançay Radio Observatory"), in a sky survey at _λ_ = 33 cm, initially reported a near-isotropic background radiation of 3 kelvins, plus or minus 2; he did not recognize the cosmological significance[[119]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-Kragh-120): 343 [[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-PartridgeReview-28): 8.3.1  and later revised the error bars to 20K.[[127]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-128)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-WrightUCLASite-5)
*   1957 – Tigran Shmaonov reports that "the absolute effective temperature of the radioemission background ... is 4±3 K".[[128]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-129) with radiation intensity was independent of either time or direction of observation. Although Shamonov did not recognize it at the time, it is now clear that Shmaonov did observe the cosmic microwave background at a wavelength of 3.2 cm[[129]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-130)
*   1964 – [A. G. Doroshkevich](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A._G._Doroshkevich "A. G. Doroshkevich") and [Igor Dmitrievich Novikov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Igor_Dmitrievich_Novikov "Igor Dmitrievich Novikov") publish a brief paper suggesting microwave searches for the black-body radiation predicted by Gamow, Alpher, and Herman, where they name the CMB radiation phenomenon as detectable.[[130]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-131)
*   1964–65 – [Arno Penzias](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arno_Penzias "Arno Penzias") and [Robert Woodrow Wilson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Woodrow_Wilson "Robert Woodrow Wilson") measure the temperature to be approximately 3 K. [Robert Dicke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Dicke "Robert Dicke"), [James Peebles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philip_James_Edwin_Peebles "Philip James Edwin Peebles"), P. G. Roll, and [D. T. Wilkinson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Todd_Wilkinson "David Todd Wilkinson") interpret this radiation as a signature of the Big Bang.
*   1966 – [Rainer K. Sachs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rainer_K._Sachs "Rainer K. Sachs") and [Arthur M. Wolfe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arthur_M._Wolfe "Arthur M. Wolfe") theoretically predict microwave background fluctuation amplitudes created by [gravitational potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_potential "Gravitational potential") variations between observers and the last scattering surface (see _[Sachs–Wolfe effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sachs%E2%80%93Wolfe\_effect "Sachs–Wolfe effect")_).
*   1968 – [Martin Rees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Martin_Rees "Martin Rees") and [Dennis Sciama](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dennis_Sciama "Dennis Sciama") theoretically predict microwave background fluctuation amplitudes created by photons traversing time-dependent wells of potential.
*   1969 – [R. A. Sunyaev](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/R._A._Sunyaev "R. A. Sunyaev") and [Yakov Zel'dovich](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yakov_Zel%27dovich "Yakov Zel'dovich") study the inverse [Compton scattering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compton_scattering "Compton scattering") of microwave background photons by hot electrons (see _[Sunyaev–Zel'dovich effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sunyaev%E2%80%93Zel%27dovich\_effect "Sunyaev–Zel'dovich effect")_).
*   1983 – Researchers from the [Cambridge Radio Astronomy Group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cavendish_Astrophysics_Group "Cavendish Astrophysics Group") and the [Owens Valley Radio Observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Owens_Valley_Radio_Observatory "Owens Valley Radio Observatory") first detect the [Sunyaev–Zel'dovich effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sunyaev%E2%80%93Zel%27dovich_effect "Sunyaev–Zel'dovich effect") from [clusters of galaxies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_cluster "Galaxy cluster").
*   1983 – [RELIKT-1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RELIKT-1 "RELIKT-1") Soviet CMB anisotropy experiment was launched.
*   1990 – FIRAS on the [Cosmic Background Explorer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_Background_Explorer "Cosmic Background Explorer") (COBE) satellite measures the black body form of the CMB spectrum with exquisite precision, and shows that the microwave background has a nearly perfect black-body spectrum with T = 2.73 K and thereby strongly constrains the density of the [intergalactic medium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intergalactic_medium "Intergalactic medium").
*   January 1992 – Scientists that analysed data from the [RELIKT-1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RELIKT-1 "RELIKT-1") report the discovery of [anisotropy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anisotropy "Anisotropy") in the cosmic microwave background at the Moscow astrophysical seminar.[[131]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-132)
*   1992 – Scientists that analysed data from [COBE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_Background_Explorer "Cosmic Background Explorer") DMR report the discovery of [anisotropy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anisotropy "Anisotropy") in the cosmic microwave background.[[132]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-133)
*   1995 – The [Cosmic Anisotropy Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_Anisotropy_Telescope "Cosmic Anisotropy Telescope") performs the first high resolution observations of the cosmic microwave background.
*   1999 – First measurements of acoustic oscillations in the CMB anisotropy angular power spectrum from the [MAT/TOCO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mobile_Anisotropy_Telescope "Mobile Anisotropy Telescope"), BOOMERANG, and Maxima Experiments. The [BOOMERanG experiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BOOMERanG_experiment "BOOMERanG experiment") makes higher quality maps at intermediate resolution, and confirms that the universe is "flat".
*   2002 – Polarization discovered by [DASI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_Angular_Scale_Interferometer "Degree Angular Scale Interferometer").[[133]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-134)
*   2003 – E-mode polarization spectrum obtained by the CBI.[[134]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-135) The [CBI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_Background_Imager "Cosmic Background Imager") and the [Very Small Array](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Very_Small_Array "Very Small Array") produces yet higher quality maps at high resolution (covering small areas of the sky).
*   2003 – The [Wilkinson Microwave Anisotropy Probe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe "Wilkinson Microwave Anisotropy Probe") spacecraft produces an even higher quality map at low and intermediate resolution of the whole sky (WMAP provides _no_ high-resolution data, but improves on the intermediate resolution maps from [BOOMERanG](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BOOMERanG_experiment "BOOMERanG experiment")).
*   2004 – E-mode polarization spectrum obtained by the [CBI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_Background_Imager "Cosmic Background Imager").[[135]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-136)
*   2004 – The [Arcminute Cosmology Bolometer Array Receiver](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arcminute_Cosmology_Bolometer_Array_Receiver "Arcminute Cosmology Bolometer Array Receiver") produces a higher quality map of the high resolution structure not mapped by WMAP.
*   2005 – The [Arcminute Microkelvin Imager](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arcminute_Microkelvin_Imager "Arcminute Microkelvin Imager") and the [Sunyaev–Zel'dovich Array](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sunyaev%E2%80%93Zel%27dovich_Array "Sunyaev–Zel'dovich Array") begin the first surveys for very high redshift [clusters of galaxies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_cluster "Galaxy cluster") using the [Sunyaev–Zel'dovich effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sunyaev%E2%80%93Zel%27dovich_effect "Sunyaev–Zel'dovich effect").
*   2005 – [Ralph A. Alpher](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ralph_A._Alpher "Ralph A. Alpher") is awarded the [National Medal of Science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Medal_of_Science "National Medal of Science") for his groundbreaking work in nucleosynthesis and prediction that the universe expansion leaves behind background radiation, thus providing a model for the Big Bang theory.
*   2006 – The long-awaited three-year [WMAP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WMAP "WMAP") results are released, confirming previous analysis, correcting several points, and including [polarization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#Polarization "Cosmic microwave background radiation") data.
*   2006 – Two of COBE's principal investigators, [George Smoot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_F._Smoot "George F. Smoot") and [John Mather](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_C._Mather "John C. Mather"), received the [Nobel Prize in Physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physics "Nobel Prize in Physics") in 2006 for their work on precision measurement of the CMBR.
*   2006–2011 – Improved measurements from [WMAP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WMAP "WMAP"), new supernova surveys ESSENCE and SNLS, and baryon acoustic oscillations from [SDSS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sloan_Digital_Sky_Survey "Sloan Digital Sky Survey") and [WiggleZ](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_survey#List_of_sky_surveys "Astronomical survey"), continue to be consistent with the standard [Lambda-CDM model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lambda-CDM_model "Lambda-CDM model").
*   2010 – The first all-sky map from the [Planck telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_(spacecraft) "Planck (spacecraft)") is released.
*   2013 – An improved all-sky map from the [Planck telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_(spacecraft) "Planck (spacecraft)") is released, improving the measurements of WMAP and extending them to much smaller scales.
*   2014 – On March 17, 2014, astrophysicists of the [BICEP2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BICEP_and_Keck_Array "BICEP and Keck Array") collaboration announced the detection of inflationary [gravitational waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_waves "Gravitational waves") in the [B-mode](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/B-modes "B-modes")[power spectrum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_spectrum "Power spectrum"), which if confirmed, would provide clear experimental evidence for the [theory of inflation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inflation_(cosmology) "Inflation (cosmology)").[[136]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-BICEP2-2014-137)[[137]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-NASA-20140317-138)[[138]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-NYT-20140317-139)[[139]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-NYT-20140324-140)[[140]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-PRL-20140619-141)[[141]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-142) However, on 19 June 2014, lowered confidence in confirming the [cosmic inflation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_inflation "Cosmic inflation") findings was reported.[[140]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-PRL-20140619-141)[[142]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-NYT-20140619-143)[[143]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-BBC-20140619-144)
*   2015 – On January 30, 2015, the same team of astronomers from BICEP2 withdrew the claim made on the previous year. Based on the combined data of BICEP2 and Planck, the [European Space Agency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Space_Agency "European Space Agency") announced that the signal can be entirely attributed to [dust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_dust "Cosmic dust") in the Milky Way.[[144]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-nature-20150130-145)
*   2018 – The final data and maps from the [Planck telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_(spacecraft) "Planck (spacecraft)") is released, with improved measurements of the polarization on large scales.[[145]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-146)
*   2019 – [Planck telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_(spacecraft) "Planck (spacecraft)") analyses of their final 2018 data continue to be released.[[146]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation#cite_note-147)
