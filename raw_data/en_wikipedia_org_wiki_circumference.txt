In [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry"), the **circumference** (from [Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin_language "Latin language")_\_[circumferēns](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/circumferens#Latin "wikt:circumferens")\__'carrying around, circling') is the [perimeter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perimeter "Perimeter") of a [circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle "Circle") or [ellipse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse "Ellipse"). The circumference is the [arc length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arc_length "Arc length") of the circle, as if it were opened up and straightened out to a [line segment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_segment "Line segment").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumference#cite_note-1) More generally, the perimeter is the [curve length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curve_length "Curve length") around any closed figure. Circumference may also refer to the circle itself, that is, the [locus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Locus_(geometry) "Locus (geometry)") corresponding to the [edge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edge_(geometry) "Edge (geometry)") of a [disk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disk_(geometry) "Disk (geometry)"). The _circumference of a sphere_ is the circumference, or length, of any one of its [great circles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_circle "Great circle").

The circumference of a circle is the distance around it, but if, as in many elementary treatments, distance is defined in terms of straight lines, this cannot be used as a definition. Under these circumstances, the circumference of a circle may be defined as the [limit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limit_(mathematics) "Limit (mathematics)") of the perimeters of inscribed [regular polygons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polygon "Regular polygon") as the number of sides increases without bound.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumference#cite_note-2) The term circumference is used when measuring physical objects, as well as when considering abstract geometric forms.

### Relationship with π

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Circumference&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Relationship with π")]

The circumference of a [circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle "Circle") is related to one of the most important [mathematical constants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_constant "Mathematical constant"). This [constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constant_(mathematics) "Constant (mathematics)"), [pi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi "Pi"), is represented by the [Greek letter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_letter "Greek letter")[![Image 6: {\displaystyle \pi .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c94b721b560eaa34cbf1e346505aca908d473be5)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi_(letter) "Pi (letter)") Its first few decimal digits are 3.141592653589793...[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumference#cite_note-3) Pi is defined as the [ratio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ratio "Ratio") of a circle's circumference ![Image 7: {\displaystyle C}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4fc55753007cd3c18576f7933f6f089196732029) to its [diameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diameter "Diameter")![Image 8: {\displaystyle d:}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7e5dcd1a25034416d879bf218d83365e09577fbf)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumference#cite_note-4)![Image 9: {\displaystyle \pi ={\frac {C}{d}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f98a23e73a342246e95838018afd6f157a859564)

Or, equivalently, as the ratio of the circumference to twice the [radius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radius "Radius"). The above formula can be rearranged to solve for the circumference: ![Image 10: {\displaystyle {C}=\pi \cdot {d}=2\pi \cdot {r}.\!}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1b508f32a126d7505d56e7ff1527c68924e7180e)

The ratio of the circle's circumference to its radius is equivalent to ![Image 11: {\displaystyle 2\pi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/73efd1f6493490b058097060a572606d2c550a06).[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumference#cite_note-9) This is also the number of [radians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radian "Radian") in one [turn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turn_(angle) "Turn (angle)"). The use of the mathematical constant π is ubiquitous in mathematics, engineering, and science.

In _[Measurement of a Circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Measurement\_of\_a\_Circle "Measurement of a Circle")_ written circa 250 BCE, [Archimedes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archimedes "Archimedes") showed that this ratio (written as ![Image 12: {\displaystyle C/d,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cc7c45eb63b007ae807b3de35dfee385d7c50c40) since he did not use the name π) was greater than 3⁠10/71⁠ but less than 3⁠1/7⁠ by calculating the perimeters of an inscribed and a circumscribed regular polygon of 96 sides.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumference#cite_note-10) This method for approximating π was used for centuries, obtaining more accuracy by using polygons of larger and larger number of sides. The last such calculation was performed in 1630 by [Christoph Grienberger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christoph_Grienberger "Christoph Grienberger") who used polygons with 10 40 sides.

[![Image 13](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Ellipses_same_circumference.png/250px-Ellipses_same_circumference.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Ellipses_same_circumference.png)

Circle, and ellipses with the same circumference

Some authors use circumference to denote the perimeter of an ellipse. There is no general formula for the circumference of an ellipse in terms of the [semi-major and semi-minor axes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semi-major_and_semi-minor_axes "Semi-major and semi-minor axes") of the ellipse that uses only elementary functions. However, there are approximate formulas in terms of these parameters. One such approximation, due to Euler (1773), for the [canonical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canonical_form "Canonical form") ellipse, ![Image 14: {\displaystyle {\frac {x^{2}}{a^{2}}}+{\frac {y^{2}}{b^{2}}}=1,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5e04cef1c6af3e391a7fe772f38ce56bd0a71cc5) is ![Image 15: {\displaystyle C_{\rm {ellipse}}\sim \pi {\sqrt {2\left(a^{2}+b^{2}\right)}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0736ef53e6581599bce93fb0aa214d4c81ba0284) Some lower and upper bounds on the circumference of the canonical ellipse with ![Image 16: {\displaystyle a\geq b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ed5d3957d5f94566507526017e4ebb67c02efe81) are:[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumference#cite_note-11)![Image 17: {\displaystyle 2\pi b\leq C\leq 2\pi a,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fc9945ec7e99abb22b9866e7f68bc06538d5b8cd)![Image 18: {\displaystyle \pi (a+b)\leq C\leq 4(a+b),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dbcd3d3ccc77b56e2153d8e2a992cd89ae1a5db7)![Image 19: {\displaystyle 4{\sqrt {a^{2}+b^{2}}}\leq C\leq \pi {\sqrt {2\left(a^{2}+b^{2}\right)}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/11d4d77488e086dd32a47e01702a604a29b5eb3f)

Here the upper bound ![Image 20: {\displaystyle 2\pi a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dcf68a5ac76f0d5a957464f181bf60d2807eda74) is the circumference of a [circumscribed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumscribed_circle "Circumscribed circle")[concentric circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concentric_circle "Concentric circle") passing through the endpoints of the ellipse's major axis, and the lower bound ![Image 21: {\displaystyle 4{\sqrt {a^{2}+b^{2}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e33305ba73b323001753e068840c1c1224d58638) is the [perimeter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perimeter "Perimeter") of an [inscribed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inscribed_figure "Inscribed figure")[rhombus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhombus "Rhombus") with [vertices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_(geometry) "Vertex (geometry)") at the endpoints of the major and minor axes.

The circumference of an ellipse can be expressed exactly in terms of the [complete elliptic integral of the second kind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complete_elliptic_integral_of_the_second_kind "Complete elliptic integral of the second kind").[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumference#cite_note-12) More precisely, ![Image 22: {\displaystyle C_{\rm {ellipse}}=4a\int _{0}^{\pi /2}{\sqrt {1-e^{2}\sin ^{2}\theta }}\ d\theta ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0425c115456e308396c883f7c42ae3b15ed48ebd) where ![Image 23: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc) is the length of the semi-major axis and ![Image 24: {\displaystyle e}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cd253103f0876afc68ebead27a5aa9867d927467) is the eccentricity ![Image 25: {\displaystyle {\sqrt {1-b^{2}/a^{2}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c9cdcb3fa7deb8fd41527b852dae712743b3c6dd)
