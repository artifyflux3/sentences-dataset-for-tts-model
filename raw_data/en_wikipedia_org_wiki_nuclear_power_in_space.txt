**Nuclear power in space** is the use of [nuclear power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power "Nuclear power") in [outer space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_space "Outer space"), typically either small [fission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_fission "Nuclear fission") systems or [radioactive decay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radioactive_decay "Radioactive decay"), for electricity or heat. Another use is for scientific observation, as in a [Mössbauer spectrometer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M%C3%B6ssbauer_spectrometer "Mössbauer spectrometer"). The most common type is a [radioisotope thermoelectric generator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radioisotope_thermoelectric_generator "Radioisotope thermoelectric generator"), which has been used on many space probes and on crewed lunar missions. Small fission reactors for Earth observation satellites, such as the [TOPAZ nuclear reactor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TOPAZ_nuclear_reactor "TOPAZ nuclear reactor"), have also been flown.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_space#cite_note-1) A [radioisotope heater unit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radioisotope_heater_unit "Radioisotope heater unit") is powered by radioactive decay, and can keep components from becoming too cold to function -- potentially over a span of decades.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_space#cite_note-doe1-2)

The United States tested the [SNAP-10A](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SNAP-10A "SNAP-10A") nuclear reactor in space for 43 days in 1965,[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_space#cite_note-zaitsev-3) with the next test of a nuclear reactor power system intended for space use occurring on 13 September 2012 with the Demonstration Using Flattop Fission (DUFF) test of the [Kilopower](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kilopower "Kilopower") reactor.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_space#cite_note-4)

After a ground-based test of the experimental 1965 [Romashka reactor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Romashka_reactor "Romashka reactor"), which used uranium and direct [thermoelectric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermoelectric "Thermoelectric") conversion to electricity,[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_space#cite_note-Romashka_reactor-converter-5) the USSR sent about 40 nuclear-electric [satellites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/US-A "US-A") into space, mostly powered by the [BES-5](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BES-5 "BES-5") reactor. The more powerful TOPAZ-II reactor produced 10 kilowatts of electricity.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_space#cite_note-zaitsev-3)

Examples of concepts that use nuclear power for space propulsion systems include the [nuclear electric rocket](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_electric_rocket "Nuclear electric rocket") (nuclear powered [ion thruster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ion_thruster "Ion thruster")(s)), the [radioisotope rocket](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radioisotope_rocket "Radioisotope rocket"), and radioisotope electric propulsion (REP).[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_space#cite_note-6) One of the more explored concepts is the [nuclear thermal rocket](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_thermal_rocket "Nuclear thermal rocket"), which was ground tested in the [NERVA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NERVA "NERVA") program. [Nuclear pulse propulsion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_pulse_propulsion "Nuclear pulse propulsion") was the subject of [Project Orion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Project_Orion_(nuclear_propulsion) "Project Orion (nuclear propulsion)").[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_space#cite_note-7)

Hazards and regulations
-----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_power_in_space&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Hazards and regulations")]

[![Image 4: Sodium-potassium (NaK) alloy droplets similar to those released in high Earth orbit by the Soviet RORSAT program's fission reactors.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/NaK_Alloy.jpg/250px-NaK_Alloy.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:NaK_Alloy.jpg)

Sodium-potassium (NaK) alloy droplets similar to those released in high Earth orbit by the Soviet RORSAT program's fission reactors.

After the ban of [nuclear weapons in space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Militarisation_of_space "Militarisation of space") by the [Outer Space Treaty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty "Outer Space Treaty") in 1967, nuclear power has been discussed at least since 1972 as a sensitive issue by states.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_space#cite_note-Space_Legal_Issues_2018-8) Space nuclear power sources may experience accidents during launch, operation, and end-of-service phases, resulting in the exposure of nuclear power sources to extreme physical conditions and the release of radioactive materials into the Earth's atmosphere and surface environment.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_space#cite_note-:0-9) For example, all Radioisotope Power Systems (RPS) used in space missions have utilized Pu-238. [Plutonium-238](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plutonium-238 "Plutonium-238") is a radioactive element that emits alpha particles. Although [NASA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NASA "NASA") states that it exists in spacecraft in a form that is not readily absorbed and poses little to no chemical or toxicological risk upon entering the human body (e.g. in the design of American spacecraft, plutonium dioxide exists in ceramic form to prevent inhalation or ingestion by humans, and it is placed within strict safety protection systems), it cannot be denied that it may be released and dispersed into the environment, posing hazards to both the environment and human health.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_space#cite_note-10) Pu-238 primarily accumulates in the lungs, liver, and bones through inhalation of powdered form, thereby posing risks to human health.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_space#cite_note-11)

#### Accidents within the atmosphere

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_power_in_space&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Accidents within the atmosphere")]

There have been several environmental accidents related to space nuclear power in history.

In 1964, a [Thor-Ablestar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thor-Ablestar "Thor-Ablestar") rocket carrying the [Transit 5BN-3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transit_(satellite) "Transit (satellite)") satellite failed to reach orbit, destroying the satellite in re-entry over the southern hemisphere. Its one kilogram of plutonium-238 fuel within the [SNAP-9A](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SNAP-9A "SNAP-9A") RTG was released into the stratosphere. A 1972 Department of Energy soil sample report attributed 13.4 [kilocuries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kilocurie "Kilocurie") of Pu-238 to the accident, from the one kilogram's 17 kilocuries total. This was contrasted to the 11,600 kilocuries of [strontium-90](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strontium-90 "Strontium-90") deposited by all nuclear weapons testing.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_space#cite_note-12)

In May 1968, a [Thor-Agena](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thor-Agena "Thor-Agena") rocket carrying the [Nimbus B](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nimbus_B "Nimbus B") satellite was destroyed by a guidance error. Its plutonium [SNAP-19](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SNAP-19 "SNAP-19") RTG was recovered intact, without leakage from the Pacific sea floor, refurbished, and flown on [Nimbus 3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nimbus_3 "Nimbus 3").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_space#cite_note-NASA_nssdc2-13)

In April 1970, the Apollo 13 lunar mission was aborted due to an oxygen tank explosion in the spacecraft's service module. Upon reentering the atmosphere, the lunar module equipped with the [SNAP-27](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SNAP-27 "SNAP-27") RTG exploded and crashed into the South Pacific Ocean, with no leakage of nuclear fuel.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_space#cite_note-:0-9) This is the only intact flown nuclear system that remains on Earth without recovery.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

In early 1978, the Soviet spacecraft [Kosmos 954](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kosmos_954 "Kosmos 954"), powered by a 45-kilogram highly enriched uranium reactor, went into an uncontrolled descent. Due to the unpredictable impact point, preparations were made for potential contamination of inhabited areas. This event underscored the potential danger of space objects containing radioactive materials, emphasizing the need for strict international emergency planning and information sharing in the event of space nuclear accidents. It also led to the intergovernmental formulation of emergency protocols, such as [Operation Morning Light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operation_Morning_Light "Operation Morning Light"), where Canada and the United States jointly recovered 80 radioactive fragments within a 600-kilometer range in the Canadian Northwest Territories. COSMOS 954 became the first example for global emergency preparedness and response arrangements for satellites carrying nuclear power sources.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_space#cite_note-14)

The majority of [nuclear power systems launched into space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_nuclear_power_systems_in_space "List of nuclear power systems in space") remain in [graveyard orbits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graveyard_orbit "Graveyard orbit") around Earth. Between 1980 and 1989, the [BES-5](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BES-5 "BES-5") and [TOPAZ-I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TOPAZ_nuclear_reactor "TOPAZ nuclear reactor") fission reactors of the Soviet [RORSAT](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RORSAT "RORSAT") program suffered leakages of their liquid [sodium–potassium alloy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium%E2%80%93potassium_alloy "Sodium–potassium alloy") coolant. Each reactor lost on average 5.3 kilograms of its 13 kilogram total coolant, totaling 85 kilograms across 16 reactors. A 2017 ESA paper calculated that, while smaller droplets quickly decay, 65 kilograms of coolant still remain in centimeter-sized droplets around 800 km altitude orbits, comprising 10% of the space debris in that size range.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_space#cite_note-u407-15)

#### Trapped-positron problem

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_power_in_space&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Trapped-positron problem")]

[![Image 5](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Structure_of_the_magnetosphere_mod.svg/250px-Structure_of_the_magnetosphere_mod.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Structure_of_the_magnetosphere_mod.svg)

The structure of Earth's magnetosphere. Fission-produced trapped positrons precess along the innermost red field lines and can interfere with instruments on satellites.

[![Image 6](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Illustration_of_Cosmos_1818_and_Cosmos_1867.jpg/250px-Illustration_of_Cosmos_1818_and_Cosmos_1867.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Illustration_of_Cosmos_1818_and_Cosmos_1867.jpg)

Diagram of Kosmos 1818 and Kosmos 1867, which carried the TOPAZ-I fission reactors

[![Image 7: Illustration of NASA's Solar Maximum Mission satellite](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Smm.jpg/250px-Smm.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Smm.jpg)

[![Image 8: Illustration of ISAS' Ginga X-ray astronomy satellite](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Astro-c_ginga.png/250px-Astro-c_ginga.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Astro-c_ginga.png)

Orbital fission reactors are a source of significant interference for orbital [gamma ray observatories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamma-ray_astronomy "Gamma-ray astronomy"). Unlike RTGs which largely rely on energy from [alpha decay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_decay "Alpha decay"), fission reactors produce significant [gamma radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamma_radiation "Gamma radiation"), with the uranium-235 chain releasing 6.3% of its total energy as prompt (shown below) and delayed (daughter product decay) gamma rays:[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_space#cite_note-kayelaby-16)

![Image 9: {\displaystyle {\begin{array}{r}^{1}_{0}{\text{n}}\ +\ _{92}^{235}{\text{U}}\longrightarrow \ _{56}^{141}{\text{Ba}}\ +\ _{36}^{92}{\text{Kr}}\ +\ 3\ _{0}^{1}{\text{n}}\ +\gamma \end{array}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/43e62c01ef2673f6439540fe5fa4a2a8eafa8f99)

[Pair production](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pair_production "Pair production") occurs as these gamma rays interact with reactor or adjacent material, ejecting electrons and [positrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positron "Positron") into space:

![Image 10: {\displaystyle \gamma +{\text{Z}}\longrightarrow \ e^{+}+e^{-}+{\text{Z}}\ }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35f11e7790ce294ce4614e3ebe04235cb12d11f1)

These electrons and positrons then become trapped in the magnetosphere's [flux tubes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flux_tube "Flux tube"), which carry them through a range of orbital altitudes, where the positrons can annihilate with the structure of other satellites, again producing gamma rays:

![Image 11: {\displaystyle e^{+}+e^{-}\longrightarrow \gamma \ +\gamma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e96ba3448961bd4e85f9ecd75adb553bdad9393e)

These gamma rays can interfere with satellite instruments. This most notably occurred in 1987, when the [TOPAZ-I nuclear reactors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TOPAZ_nuclear_reactor "TOPAZ nuclear reactor") (6–10 kWe) aboard the twin [RORSAT](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RORSAT "RORSAT") test vehicles [Kosmos 1818](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kosmos_1818 "Kosmos 1818") and [Kosmos 1867](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kosmos_1867 "Kosmos 1867") affected the gamma ray telescopes aboard [NASA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NASA "NASA")'s [Solar Maximum Mission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_Maximum_Mission "Solar Maximum Mission") and the [University of Tokyo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Tokyo "University of Tokyo")/[ISAS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Institute_of_Space_and_Astronautical_Science "Institute of Space and Astronautical Science")' [Ginga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ginga_(satellite) "Ginga (satellite)"). TOPAZ-I remains the most powerful fission reactor operated in space, with previous Soviet missions using the [BES-5](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BES-5 "BES-5") reactor (2–3 kWe) at altitudes well below gamma ray observatories.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_space#cite_note-u312-17)

#### National regulations

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_power_in_space&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: National regulations")]

The presence of space nuclear sources and the potential consequences of nuclear accidents on humans and the environment cannot be ignored. Therefore, there have been strict regulations for the application of nuclear power in outer space to mitigate the risks associated with the use of space nuclear power sources among governments.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_space#cite_note-18)

For instance, in the United States, safety considerations are integrated into every stage of the design, testing, manufacturing, and operation of space nuclear systems. The [NRC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_Regulatory_Commission "Nuclear Regulatory Commission") oversee the ownership, use, and production of nuclear materials and facilities. The [Department of Energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ministry_of_energy "Ministry of energy") is bound by the [National Environmental Policy Act](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Environmental_Policy_Act "National Environmental Policy Act") (NEPA) to consider the environmental impact of nuclear material handling, transportation, and storage.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_space#cite_note-:0-9)[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_space#cite_note-19) NASA, the Department of Energy, and other federal and local authorities develop comprehensive emergency plans for each launch, including timely public communication. In the event of an accident, monitoring teams equipped with highly specialized support equipment and automated stations are deployed around the launch site to identify potential radioactive material releases, quantify and describe the release scope, predict the quantity and distribution of dispersed material, and develop and recommend protective actions.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_space#cite_note-20)

#### International regulations

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_power_in_space&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: International regulations")]

At the global level, following the 1978 COSMOS 954 incident, the international community recognized the need to establish a set of principles and guidelines to ensure the safe use of nuclear power sources in outer space. Consequently, in 1992, the General Assembly adopted resolution 47/68, titled "Principles Relevant to the Use of Nuclear Power Sources in Outer Space."[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_space#cite_note-:1-21) These principles primarily address safety assessment, international information exchange and dialogue, responsibility, and compensation. It stipulates that the principles should be revisited by the [Committee on the Peaceful Uses of Outer Space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Committee_on_the_Peaceful_Uses_of_Outer_Space "United Nations Committee on the Peaceful Uses of Outer Space") no later than two years after adoption.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_space#cite_note-:1-21) After years of consultation and deliberation, in 2009, the International Safety Framework for Nuclear Power Source Applications in Outer Space was adopted to enhance safety for space missions involving nuclear power sources. It offers guidance for engineers and mission designers, although its effective implementation necessitates integration into existing processes.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_space#cite_note-:2-22)[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_space#cite_note-:3-23)

The "Safety Framework" asserts that each nation bears responsibility for the safety of its space nuclear power. Governments and international organizations must justify the necessity of space nuclear power applications compared to potential alternatives and demonstrate their usage based on comprehensive safety assessments, including probabilistic risk analysis, with particular attention to the risk of public exposure to harmful radiation or radioactive materials. Nations also need to establish and maintain robust safety oversight bodies, systems, and emergency preparedness to minimize the likelihood and mitigate the consequences of potential accidents.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_space#cite_note-:3-23) Unlike the 1992 "Principles," the "Safety Framework" applies to all types of space nuclear power source development and applications, not just the technologies existing at the time.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_space#cite_note-:2-22)

In the draft report on the implementation of the Safety Framework for Nuclear Power Source Applications in Outer Space published in 2023, the working group considers that the safety framework has been widely accepted and demonstrated to be helpful for member states in developing and/or implementing national systems and policies to ensure the safe use of nuclear power sources in outer space. Other member states and intergovernmental organizations not currently involved in the utilization of space nuclear power sources also acknowledge and accept the value of this framework, taking into account safety issues associated with such applications.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_space#cite_note-24)

[![Image 12](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Viking_Pre-Launch_Test_Flight_-_GPN-2003-00047.jpg/250px-Viking_Pre-Launch_Test_Flight_-_GPN-2003-00047.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Viking_Pre-Launch_Test_Flight_-_GPN-2003-00047.jpg)

Both the _[Viking 1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viking\_1 "Viking 1")_ and _[Viking 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viking\_2 "Viking 2")_ landers used RTGs for power on the surface of Mars. (Viking launch vehicle pictured)

Nuclear power systems function independently of sunlight, which is highly advantageous for [outer Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_Solar_System "Outer Solar System") exploration i.e. Jupiter and beyond. All [spacecraft leaving the Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_artificial_objects_leaving_the_Solar_System "List of artificial objects leaving the Solar System"), i.e. [_Pioneer_ _10_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pioneer_10 "Pioneer 10") and [_11_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pioneer_11 "Pioneer 11"), [_Voyager_ _1_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voyager_1 "Voyager 1") and [_2_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voyager_2 "Voyager 2"), and _[New Horizons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New\_Horizons "New Horizons")_ use NASA RTGs, as did the outer planet missions of [_Galileo_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galileo_(spacecraft) "Galileo (spacecraft)"), [_Cassini_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens "Cassini–Huygens"), and [_Ulysses_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ulysses_(spacecraft) "Ulysses (spacecraft)"). However, in part, due to the [global shortage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plutonium-238#U.S._production_ceases_and_resumes "Plutonium-238") of [plutonium-238](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plutonium-238 "Plutonium-238"),[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_space#cite_note-25)[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_space#cite_note-26)[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_space#cite_note-27)[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_space#cite_note-28) and advances in solar efficiency,[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_space#cite_note-29) the more recent Jupiter missions of [_Juno_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Juno_(spacecraft) "Juno (spacecraft)"), _[Jupiter Icy Moons Explorer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jupiter\_Icy\_Moons\_Explorer "Jupiter Icy Moons Explorer")_, and _[Europa Clipper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Europa\_Clipper "Europa Clipper")_, as well as the [Jupiter trojan asteroid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jupiter_trojan "Jupiter trojan") mission of [_Lucy_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lucy_(spacecraft) "Lucy (spacecraft)"), all opted for large solar arrays despite a relative 4% solar flux at Jupiter's orbit of 5.2 [AU](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_unit "Astronomical unit").

[Solar power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_power "Solar power") is much more commonly used for its low cost and efficiency, primarily in Earth and lunar orbit and for interplanetary missions within the [inner Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inner_Solar_System "Inner Solar System") i.e. missions to Mercury, Venus, Mars and the asteroid belt. However, nuclear power has been used for some of these missions such as the Apollo program's [SNAP-27](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SNAP-27 "SNAP-27") RTG for lunar surface use, and the [MMRTG](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MMRTG "MMRTG") on the Mars _[Curiosity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curiosity\_(rover) "Curiosity (rover)")_ and _[Perseverance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perseverance\_(rover) "Perseverance (rover)")_ rovers.

Nuclear-based systems can have less mass than solar cells of equivalent power, allowing more compact spacecraft that are easier to orient and direct in space. This makes them useful for radar satellites such as the [RORSAT](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RORSAT "RORSAT") program deployed by the Soviet Union. In the case of crewed spaceflight, nuclear power concepts that can power both life support and propulsion systems may reduce both cost and flight time.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_space#cite_note-30) Apollo 12 marked the first use of a nuclear power system on a crewed flight, carrying a [SNAP-27](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SNAP-27 "SNAP-27") RTG to power the [Apollo Lunar Surface Experiments Package](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apollo_Lunar_Surface_Experiments_Package "Apollo Lunar Surface Experiments Package").[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_space#cite_note-31)

### Powering radar systems

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_power_in_space&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Powering radar systems")]

As active electromagnetic detectors including radar observe a power-distance drop-off of ![Image 14: {\displaystyle P\propto R^{-4}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ea8bfc98af279ab5d504452f86e28f1915d87d33), comparatively low Earth orbits are desirable.

The Soviet Union did not launch interplanetary missions beyond Mars, and generally developed few RTGs.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_space#cite_note-e178-32) American RTGs in the 1970s supplied power in the 100 W range.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_space#cite_note-Atomic-33) For the [RORSAT](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RORSAT "RORSAT") military radar satellites (1967–1988), fission reactors, especially the [BES-5](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BES-5 "BES-5"), were developed to supply an average of 2 kW to the radar. At altitudes averaging 255.3 km, they would have rapidly decayed if they had used a large solar array instead.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_space#cite_note-u312-17)

The later United States [Lacrosse/Onyx](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lacrosse_(satellite) "Lacrosse (satellite)") radar satellite program, beginning launches in 1988, operated at altitudes of 420–718 km. To power radar at this range, a solar array reportedly 45 m in length was operated, speculated to supply 10–20 kW.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_space#cite_note-k222-34)

### Radioisotope systems

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_power_in_space&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Radioisotope systems")]

[![Image 25](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/SNAP-27_on_the_Moon.jpg/250px-SNAP-27_on_the_Moon.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:SNAP-27_on_the_Moon.jpg)

SNAP-27 on the Moon

For more than fifty years, [radioisotope thermoelectric generators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radioisotope_thermoelectric_generator "Radioisotope thermoelectric generator") (RTGs) have been the United States’ main nuclear power source in space. RTGs offer many benefits; they are relatively safe and maintenance-free, are resilient under harsh conditions, and can operate for decades. RTGs are particularly desirable for use in parts of space where solar power is not a viable power source. Dozens of RTGs have been implemented to power 25 different US spacecraft, some of which have been operating for more than 20 years. Over 40 radioisotope thermoelectric generators have been used globally (principally US and USSR) on space missions.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_space#cite_note-nasa/doe2010-45)

The [advanced Stirling radioisotope generator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Advanced_Stirling_radioisotope_generator "Advanced Stirling radioisotope generator") (ASRG, a model of [Stirling radioisotope generator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stirling_radioisotope_generator "Stirling radioisotope generator") (SRG)) produces roughly four times the electric power of an RTG per unit of nuclear fuel, but flight-ready units based on Stirling technology are not expected until 2028.[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_space#cite_note-Meeting_2015-46) NASA plans to utilize two ASRGs to explore [Titan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Titan_(moon) "Titan (moon)") in the distant future.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

[![Image 26](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/ASRG_Labeled_Cutaway_%28English%29.jpg/250px-ASRG_Labeled_Cutaway_%28English%29.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:ASRG_Labeled_Cutaway_(English).jpg)

Cutaway diagram of the advanced Stirling radioisotope generator.

Radioisotope power generators include:

*   SNAP-19, SNAP-27 ([Systems for Nuclear Auxiliary Power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Systems_for_Nuclear_Auxiliary_Power "Systems for Nuclear Auxiliary Power"))
*   [MHW-RTG](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MHW-RTG "MHW-RTG")
*   [GPHS-RTG](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GPHS-RTG "GPHS-RTG")
*   [MMRTG](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MMRTG "MMRTG")
*   [ASRG](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Advanced_Stirling_radioisotope_generator "Advanced Stirling radioisotope generator") (Advanced Stirling radioisotope generator)

[Radioisotope heater units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radioisotope_heater_unit "Radioisotope heater unit") (RHUs) are also used on spacecraft to warm scientific instruments to the proper temperature so they operate efficiently. A larger model of RHU called the [General Purpose Heat Source](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Purpose_Heat_Source "General Purpose Heat Source") (GPHS) is used to power RTGs and the ASRG.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

Extremely slow-decaying radioisotopes have been proposed for use on interstellar probes with multi-decade lifetimes.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_space#cite_note-Ref_t-47)

As of 2011, another direction for development was an RTG assisted by subcritical nuclear reactions.[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_space#cite_note-48)

Fission power systems may be utilized to power a spacecraft's heating or propulsion systems. In terms of heating requirements, when spacecraft require more than 100 kW for power, fission systems are much more cost effective than RTGs.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

In 1965, the [US](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federal_government_of_the_United_States "Federal government of the United States") launched a space reactor, the [SNAP-10A](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SNAP-10A "SNAP-10A"), which had been developed by [Atomics International](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomics_International "Atomics International"), then a division of [North American Aviation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_Aviation "North American Aviation").[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_space#cite_note-snap10a-49)

Over the past few decades, several fission reactors have been proposed, and the [Soviet Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Union "Soviet Union") launched 31 [BES-5](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BES-5 "BES-5") low power fission reactors in their [RORSAT](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/US-A "US-A") satellites utilizing thermoelectric converters between 1967 and 1988.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

In the 1960s and 1970s, the Soviet Union developed [TOPAZ reactors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TOPAZ_nuclear_reactor "TOPAZ nuclear reactor"), which utilize thermionic converters instead, although the first test flight was not until 1987.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

In 1983, NASA and other US government agencies began development of a next-generation space reactor, the [SP-100](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SP-100 "SP-100"), contracting with General Electric and others. In 1994, the SP-100 program was cancelled, largely for political reasons, with the idea of transitioning to the Russian [TOPAZ-II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TOPAZ_nuclear_reactor#TOPAZ-II "TOPAZ nuclear reactor") reactor system. Although some TOPAZ-II prototypes were ground-tested, the system was never deployed for US space missions.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_space#cite_note-topazii-50)

In 2008, NASA announced plans to utilize a small fission power system on the surface of the Moon and Mars, and began testing "key" technologies for it to come to fruition.[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_space#cite_note-51)

Proposed fission power system spacecraft and exploration systems have included [SP-100](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SP-100 "SP-100"), [JIMO nuclear electric propulsion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/JIMO_nuclear_electric_propulsion "JIMO nuclear electric propulsion"), and [Fission Surface Power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fission_Surface_Power "Fission Surface Power").[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_space#cite_note-nasa/doe2010-45)

[![Image 27](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/SAFE-30_Reactor.png/250px-SAFE-30_Reactor.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:SAFE-30_Reactor.png)

[SAFE-30](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Safe_Affordable_Fission_Engine "Safe Affordable Fission Engine") small experimental reactor

A number of [micro nuclear reactor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Micro_nuclear_reactor "Micro nuclear reactor") types have been developed or are in development for space applications:[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_space#cite_note-52)

*   [RAPID-L](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RAPID-L_(Nuclear_Reactor) "RAPID-L (Nuclear Reactor)")
*   closed cycle magnetohydrodynamic (CCMHD) power generation system
*   [SP-100](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SP-100 "SP-100")
*   [Alkali Metal Thermoelectric Converter (AMTEC)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkali-metal_thermal_to_electric_converter "Alkali-metal thermal to electric converter")
*   [Kilopower](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kilopower "Kilopower")

Nuclear thermal propulsion systems (NTR) are based on the heating power of a fission reactor, offering a more efficient propulsion system than one powered by chemical reactions. Current research focuses more on nuclear electric systems as the power source for providing thrust to propel spacecraft that are already in space.

Other space fission reactors for powering space vehicles include the [SAFE-400](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Safe_affordable_fission_engine "Safe affordable fission engine") reactor and the HOMER-15. In 2020, Roscosmos (the [Russian Federal Space Agency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_Federal_Space_Agency "Russian Federal Space Agency")) plans to launch a spacecraft utilizing nuclear-powered propulsion systems (developed at the [Keldysh Research Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Keldysh_Research_Center "Keldysh Research Center")), which includes a small gas-cooled fission reactor with 1 MWe.[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_space#cite_note-53)[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_space#cite_note-54)

[![Image 28](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Prometheus1.jpg/250px-Prometheus1.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Prometheus1.jpg)

Artists's Conception of Jupiter Icy Moons Orbiter mission for Prometheus, with the reactor on the right, providing power to ion engines and electronics.

In 2002, NASA announced an initiative towards developing nuclear systems, which later came to be known as [Project Prometheus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Project_Prometheus "Project Prometheus"). A major part of the Prometheus Project was to develop the Stirling Radioisotope Generator and the Multi-Mission Thermoelectric Generator, both types of RTGs. The project also aimed to produce a safe and long-lasting space fission reactor system for a spacecraft's power and propulsion, replacing the long-used RTGs. Budget constraints resulted in the effective halting of the project, but Project Prometheus has had success in testing new systems.[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_space#cite_note-55) After its creation, scientists successfully tested a [High Power Electric Propulsion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High_Power_Electric_Propulsion "High Power Electric Propulsion") (HiPEP) ion engine, which offered substantial advantages in fuel efficiency, thruster lifetime, and thruster efficiency over other power sources.[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_space#cite_note-56)

### Fission Surface Power System

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_power_in_space&action=edit&section=17 "Edit section: Fission Surface Power System")]

In September 2020, NASA and the [Department of Energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Energy "United States Department of Energy") (DOE) issued a formal request for proposals for a lunar nuclear power system, otherwise known as a Fission Surface Power System (FSPS).[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_space#cite_note-57) The desire for developing these systems is to assist the [Artemis Project](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Project "Artemis Project") in occupying the moon and provide a reliable energy source in areas that have weeks-long lunar night cycles. Furthermore, these systems can be extended to future Mars missions, which further increase design consideration complexity due to atmospheric events, such as dust storms. NASA is collaborating with the DOE Idaho National Laboratory to progress this mission forward.

Phase 1 of the project is focused on the development of different preliminary low enriched uranium material designs to determine the feasibility of the different concepts. The system is expected to have a 40 kW (54 hp) output at 120 Vdc lasting 10 years, weigh less than 6,000 kg (13,000 lb) while fitting on a lander module, and produce less than 5 rem per year at a minimum distance of 1 km (0.62 mi).[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_space#cite_note-58) Three $5 million dollar contracts were awarded in 2022 to [Lockheed Martin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lockheed_Martin "Lockheed Martin"), [Westinghouse Electric Corporation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Westinghouse_Electric_Corporation "Westinghouse Electric Corporation"), and IX (joint venture of [Intuitive Machines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intuitive_Machines "Intuitive Machines") and [X-energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-energy "X-energy")) to engage in industry developed reactor designs for power conversion, heat rejection, power management, and distribution systems.[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_space#cite_note-59)

As it currently stands, the initial base location will be located on the southern pole of the moon so there is an almost constant stream of sun light for solar cells to power habitation modules, estimating power limits to be reached at 20 kW (27 hp). The Fission Surface Power System will be at the core of the power flow system and provide the only stable method for power generation without environmental factors. The demo reactor is expected to supply 10 kW to the grid, while the full system will provide 40 kW. This will enable the use of [In situ resource utilization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/In_situ_resource_utilization "In situ resource utilization") (ISRU).[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_space#cite_note-60)
