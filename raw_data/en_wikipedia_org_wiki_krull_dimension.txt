From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

In [commutative algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_algebra "Commutative algebra"), the **Krull dimension** of a [commutative ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_ring "Commutative ring")_R_, named after [Wolfgang Krull](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Krull "Wolfgang Krull"), is the [supremum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supremum "Supremum") of the lengths of all [chains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chain_(order_theory) "Chain (order theory)") of [prime ideals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_ideals "Prime ideals"). The Krull dimension need not be finite even for a [Noetherian ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noetherian_ring "Noetherian ring"). More generally the Krull dimension can be defined for [modules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Module_(mathematics) "Module (mathematics)") over possibly [non-commutative rings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-commutative_ring "Non-commutative ring") as the [deviation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deviation_of_a_poset "Deviation of a poset") of the [poset](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poset "Poset") of submodules.

The Krull dimension was introduced to provide an algebraic definition of the [dimension of an algebraic variety](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension_of_an_algebraic_variety "Dimension of an algebraic variety"): the dimension of the [affine variety](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_variety "Affine variety") defined by an ideal _I_ in a [polynomial ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial_ring "Polynomial ring")_R_ is the Krull dimension of _R_/_I_.

A [field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)")_k_ has Krull dimension 0; more generally, _k_[_x_ 1, ..., _x_ _n_] has Krull dimension _n_. A [principal ideal domain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principal_ideal_domain "Principal ideal domain") that is not a field has Krull dimension 1. A [local ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_ring "Local ring") has Krull dimension 0 if and only if every element of its [maximal ideal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maximal_ideal "Maximal ideal") is [nilpotent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nilpotent "Nilpotent").

There are several other ways that have been used to define the dimension of a ring. Most of them coincide with the Krull dimension for Noetherian rings, but can differ for non-Noetherian rings.

We say that a chain of prime ideals of the form ![Image 1: {\displaystyle {\mathfrak {p}}_{0}\subsetneq {\mathfrak {p}}_{1}\subsetneq \ldots \subsetneq {\mathfrak {p}}_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fcb33c8d5a2f5108db7d78ae3bb0e0499b0f6794) has **length _n_**. That is, the length is the number of strict inclusions, not the number of primes; these differ by 1. We define the **Krull dimension** of ![Image 2: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) to be the supremum of the lengths of all chains of prime ideals in ![Image 3: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33).

Given a prime ideal ![Image 4: {\displaystyle {\mathfrak {p}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a14c125cdf81ac25d76edc2e8d557302c9f555a9) in _R_, we define the **height** of ![Image 5: {\displaystyle {\mathfrak {p}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a14c125cdf81ac25d76edc2e8d557302c9f555a9), written ![Image 6: {\displaystyle \operatorname {ht} ({\mathfrak {p}})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd8cf3c457ca0ccd7b77281d2f218190e528c3d), to be the supremum of the lengths of all chains of prime ideals contained in ![Image 7: {\displaystyle {\mathfrak {p}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a14c125cdf81ac25d76edc2e8d557302c9f555a9), meaning that ![Image 8: {\displaystyle {\mathfrak {p}}_{0}\subsetneq {\mathfrak {p}}_{1}\subsetneq \ldots \subsetneq {\mathfrak {p}}_{n}={\mathfrak {p}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a1527b6469b3be3748c4f908f2f40d40f4c2f95b).[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Krull_dimension#cite_note-matsumura30-1) In other words, the height of ![Image 9: {\displaystyle {\mathfrak {p}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a14c125cdf81ac25d76edc2e8d557302c9f555a9) is the Krull dimension of the [localization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Localization_of_a_ring "Localization of a ring") of _R_ at ![Image 10: {\displaystyle {\mathfrak {p}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a14c125cdf81ac25d76edc2e8d557302c9f555a9). A prime ideal has height zero if and only if it is a [minimal prime ideal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minimal_prime_ideal "Minimal prime ideal"). The Krull dimension of a ring is the supremum of the heights of all maximal ideals, or those of all prime ideals. The height is also sometimes called the codimension, rank, or altitude of a prime ideal.

In a [Noetherian ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noetherian_ring "Noetherian ring"), every prime ideal has finite height. Nonetheless, [Nagata](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Masayoshi_Nagata "Masayoshi Nagata") gave an example of a Noetherian ring of infinite Krull dimension.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Krull_dimension#cite_note-2) A ring is called **[catenary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catenary_ring "Catenary ring")** if any inclusion ![Image 11: {\displaystyle {\mathfrak {p}}\subset {\mathfrak {q}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0259430e2701755d4d93f9e35fa0a8c54c4f1258) of prime ideals can be extended to a maximal chain of prime ideals between ![Image 12: {\displaystyle {\mathfrak {p}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a14c125cdf81ac25d76edc2e8d557302c9f555a9) and ![Image 13: {\displaystyle {\mathfrak {q}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/79b0448036d3f2e513dd80d94095c45d81510269), and any two maximal chains between ![Image 14: {\displaystyle {\mathfrak {p}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a14c125cdf81ac25d76edc2e8d557302c9f555a9) and ![Image 15: {\displaystyle {\mathfrak {q}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/79b0448036d3f2e513dd80d94095c45d81510269) have the same length. A ring is called [universally catenary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universally_catenary "Universally catenary") if any finitely generated algebra over it is catenary. Nagata gave an example of a Noetherian ring which is not catenary.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Krull_dimension#cite_note-3)

In a Noetherian ring, a prime ideal has height at most _n_ if and only if it is a [minimal prime ideal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minimal_prime_ideal "Minimal prime ideal") over an ideal generated by _n_ elements ([Krull's height theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Krull%27s_principal_ideal_theorem "Krull's principal ideal theorem") and its converse).[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Krull_dimension#cite_note-4) It implies that the [descending chain condition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Descending_chain_condition "Descending chain condition") holds for prime ideals in such a way the lengths of the chains descending from a prime ideal are bounded by the number of generators of the prime.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Krull_dimension#cite_note-5)

More generally, the height of an ideal **I** is the infimum of the heights of all prime ideals containing **I**. In the language of [algebraic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_geometry "Algebraic geometry"), this is the [codimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Codimension "Codimension") of the subvariety of Spec(![Image 16: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33)) corresponding to **I**.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Krull_dimension#cite_note-matsumura30-1)

It follows readily from the definition of the [spectrum of a ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectrum_of_a_ring "Spectrum of a ring") Spec(_R_), the space of prime ideals of _R_ equipped with the [Zariski topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zariski_topology "Zariski topology"), that the Krull dimension of _R_ is equal to the dimension of its spectrum as a [topological space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_space "Topological space"), meaning the supremum of the lengths of all chains of [irreducible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irreducible_space "Irreducible space") closed subsets. This follows immediately from the [Galois connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galois_connection "Galois connection") between ideals of _R_ and closed subsets of Spec(_R_) and the observation that, by the definition of Spec(_R_), each prime ideal ![Image 17: {\displaystyle {\mathfrak {p}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a14c125cdf81ac25d76edc2e8d557302c9f555a9) of _R_ corresponds to a generic point of the closed subset associated to ![Image 18: {\displaystyle {\mathfrak {p}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a14c125cdf81ac25d76edc2e8d557302c9f555a9) by the Galois connection.

*   The dimension of a [polynomial ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial_ring "Polynomial ring") over a field _k_[_x_ 1, ..., _x_ _n_] is the number of variables _n_. In the language of [algebraic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_geometry "Algebraic geometry"), this says that the affine space of dimension _n_ over a field has dimension _n_, as expected. In general, if _R_ is a [Noetherian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noetherian_ring "Noetherian ring") ring of dimension _n_, then the dimension of _R_[_x_] is _n_ + 1. If the Noetherian hypothesis is dropped, then _R_[_x_] can have dimension anywhere between _n_ + 1 and 2 _n_ + 1.
*   For example, the ideal ![Image 19: {\displaystyle {\mathfrak {p}}=(y^{2}-x,y)\subset \mathbb {C} [x,y]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ae42e2b4c04eef3bca1ca372979781520bba40a0) has height 2 since we can form the maximal ascending chain of prime ideals![Image 20: {\displaystyle (0)={\mathfrak {p}}_{0}\subsetneq (y^{2}-x)={\mathfrak {p}}_{1}\subsetneq (y^{2}-x,y)={\mathfrak {p}}_{2}={\mathfrak {p}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b7aed013c213027d5f329df35f7efebdbf4f0ae).
*   Given an irreducible polynomial ![Image 21: {\displaystyle f\in \mathbb {C} [x,y,z]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d887b89127185ad52206e4d8b84919fb0e3899c4), the ideal ![Image 22: {\displaystyle I=(f^{3})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b4939af9d055429ce42517b6aea1d371adeb02f) is not prime (since ![Image 23: {\displaystyle f\cdot f^{2}\in I}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/434ee9b82a9867f100bc10abee129fc2191a0175), but neither of the factors are), but we can easily compute the height since the smallest prime ideal containing ![Image 24: {\displaystyle I}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/535ea7fc4134a31cbe2251d9d3511374bc41be9f) is just ![Image 25: {\displaystyle (f)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f8bea8bbec80317513538b12e2ec1ad420b9e1bb).
*   The ring of integers **Z** has dimension 1. More generally, any [principal ideal domain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principal_ideal_domain "Principal ideal domain") that is not a field has dimension 1.
*   An [integral domain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral_domain "Integral domain") is a field if and only if its Krull dimension is zero. [Dedekind domains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dedekind_domain "Dedekind domain") that are not fields (for example, [discrete valuation rings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discrete_valuation_ring "Discrete valuation ring")) have dimension one.
*   The Krull dimension of the [zero ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero_ring "Zero ring") is typically defined to be either ![Image 26: {\displaystyle -\infty }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ca2608c4b5fd3bffc73585f8c67e379b4e99b6f1) or ![Image 27: {\displaystyle -1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/704fb0427140d054dd267925495e78164fee9aac). The zero ring is the only ring with a negative dimension.
*   A ring is [Artinian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artinian_ring "Artinian ring") if and only if it is [Noetherian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noetherian_ring "Noetherian ring") and its Krull dimension is ≤0.
*   An [integral extension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral_extension "Integral extension") of a ring has the same dimension as the ring does.
*   Let _R_ be an algebra over a field _k_ that is an integral domain. Then the Krull dimension of _R_ is less than or equal to the transcendence degree of the field of fractions of _R_ over _k_.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Krull_dimension#cite_note-6) The equality holds if _R_ is finitely generated as an algebra (for instance by the [Noether normalization lemma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noether_normalization_lemma "Noether normalization lemma")).
*   Let _R_ be a Noetherian ring, _I_ an ideal and ![Image 28: {\displaystyle \operatorname {gr} _{I}(R)=\bigoplus _{k=0}^{\infty }I^{k}/I^{k+1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7af8096088b612e39e60135b2f020e10eb05b1ea) be the [associated graded ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Associated_graded_ring "Associated graded ring") (geometers call it the ring of the [normal cone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_cone "Normal cone") of _I_). Then ![Image 29: {\displaystyle \operatorname {dim} \operatorname {gr} _{I}(R)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9733e6876e3c7f8ba5bccbea42c171bf23dd2ef4) is the supremum of the heights of maximal ideals of _R_ containing _I_.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Krull_dimension#cite_note-7)
*   A commutative Noetherian ring of Krull dimension zero is a direct product of a finite number (possibly one) of [local rings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_ring "Local ring") of Krull dimension zero.
*   A Noetherian local ring is called a [Cohen–Macaulay ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cohen%E2%80%93Macaulay_ring "Cohen–Macaulay ring") if its dimension is equal to its [depth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Depth_(ring_theory) "Depth (ring theory)"). A [regular local ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_local_ring "Regular local ring") is an example of such a ring.
*   A Noetherian [integral domain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral_domain "Integral domain") is a [unique factorization domain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unique_factorization_domain "Unique factorization domain") if and only if every height 1 prime ideal is principal.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Krull_dimension#cite_note-8)
*   For a commutative Noetherian ring the three following conditions are equivalent: being a [reduced ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reduced_ring "Reduced ring") of Krull dimension zero, being a field or a [direct product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direct_product "Direct product") of fields, being [von Neumann regular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Von_Neumann_regular_ring "Von Neumann regular ring").

If _R_ is a commutative ring, and _M_ is an _R_-module, we define the Krull dimension of _M_ to be the Krull dimension of the quotient of _R_ making _M_ a [faithful module](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Faithful_module "Faithful module"). That is, we define it by the formula:

![Image 30: {\displaystyle \dim _{R}M:=\dim(R/{\operatorname {Ann} _{R}(M)})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e69c95d7d9faa458d47b6564f5740eefec40bf70)
where Ann _R_(_M_), the [annihilator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Annihilator_(ring_theory) "Annihilator (ring theory)"), is the kernel of the natural map R → End _R_(M) of _R_ into the ring of _R_-linear endomorphisms of _M_.

In the language of [schemes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scheme_(mathematics) "Scheme (mathematics)"), finitely generated modules are interpreted as [coherent sheaves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coherent_sheaves "Coherent sheaves"), or generalized finite rank [vector bundles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_bundles "Vector bundles").

For non-commutative rings
-------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Krull_dimension&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: For non-commutative rings")]

The Krull dimension of a module over a possibly non-commutative ring is defined as the [deviation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deviation_of_a_poset "Deviation of a poset") of the poset of submodules ordered by inclusion. For commutative Noetherian rings, this is the same as the definition using chains of prime ideals.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Krull_dimension#cite_note-9) The two definitions can be different for commutative rings which are not Noetherian.
