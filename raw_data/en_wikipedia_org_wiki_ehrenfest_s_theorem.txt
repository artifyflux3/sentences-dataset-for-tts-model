From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

The **Ehrenfest theorem**, named after Austrian theoretical physicist [Paul Ehrenfest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Ehrenfest "Paul Ehrenfest"), relates the time [derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative "Derivative") of the [expectation values](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expectation_value_(quantum_mechanics) "Expectation value (quantum mechanics)") of the position and momentum [operators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operator_(physics) "Operator (physics)")_x_ and _p_ to the expectation value of the force ![Image 1: {\displaystyle F=-V'(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/62bcebb1a3c4c25e10596c9d64d128eb6069c809) on a massive particle moving in a scalar potential ![Image 2: {\displaystyle V(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4ab3e825c2bf9c80d11d12e070a4626d48e03c61),[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ehrenfest's_theorem#cite_note-1)

![Image 3: {\displaystyle m{\frac {d}{dt}}\langle x\rangle =\langle p\rangle ,\;\;{\frac {d}{dt}}\langle p\rangle =-\left\langle V'(x)\right\rangle ~.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ee01d9842880c3d83982f76388a5bc953288daf9)

The Ehrenfest theorem is a special case of a more general relation between the expectation of any [quantum mechanical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics")[operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operator_(physics) "Operator (physics)") and the expectation of the [commutator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutator "Commutator") of that operator with the [Hamiltonian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamiltonian_(quantum_mechanics) "Hamiltonian (quantum mechanics)") of the system [[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ehrenfest's_theorem#cite_note-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ehrenfest's_theorem#cite_note-Smith-3)

![Image 4: {\displaystyle {\frac {d}{dt}}\langle A\rangle ={\frac {1}{i\hbar }}\langle [A,H]\rangle +\left\langle {\frac {\partial A}{\partial t}}\right\rangle ~,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/303dcdd57e0f1e0a9683a06fb1c2f764197c0d26)

where A is some quantum mechanical operator and ⟨_A_⟩ is its [expectation value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expectation_value "Expectation value").

It is most apparent in the [Heisenberg picture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heisenberg_picture "Heisenberg picture") of quantum mechanics, where it amounts to just the expectation value of the Heisenberg equation of motion. It provides mathematical support to the [correspondence principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Correspondence_principle "Correspondence principle").

The reason is that Ehrenfest's theorem is closely related to [Liouville's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liouville%27s_theorem_(Hamiltonian) "Liouville's theorem (Hamiltonian)") of [Hamiltonian mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamiltonian_mechanics "Hamiltonian mechanics"), which involves the [Poisson bracket](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poisson_bracket "Poisson bracket") instead of a commutator. [Dirac's rule of thumb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canonical_quantization#Classical_and_quantum_brackets "Canonical quantization") suggests that statements in quantum mechanics which contain a commutator correspond to statements in classical mechanics where the commutator is supplanted by a Poisson bracket multiplied by _iħ_. This makes the operator expectation values obey corresponding classical equations of motion, provided the Hamiltonian is at most quadratic in the coordinates and momenta. Otherwise, the evolution equations still may hold [approximately](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moyal_bracket "Moyal bracket"), provided fluctuations are small.

Relation to classical physics
-----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ehrenfest_theorem&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Relation to classical physics")]

Although, at first glance, it might appear that the Ehrenfest theorem is saying that the quantum mechanical expectation values obey Newton's classical equations of motion, this is not actually the case.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ehrenfest's_theorem#cite_note-Wheeler-4) If the pair ![Image 5: {\displaystyle (\langle x\rangle ,\langle p\rangle )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7af87ff955bfaf31fa1c178dbcb6cbe4d5a772a1) were to satisfy Newton's second law, the right-hand side of the second equation would have to be ![Image 6: {\displaystyle -V'\left(\left\langle x\right\rangle \right),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d82f18c98a8dcdf3a979a5f4f7b4d0571081c917) which is typically not the same as ![Image 7: {\displaystyle -\left\langle V'(x)\right\rangle .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b68ebe7bc0c70b096a9007683f396bde55272623) If for example, the potential ![Image 8: {\displaystyle V(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4ab3e825c2bf9c80d11d12e070a4626d48e03c61) is cubic, (i.e. proportional to ![Image 9: {\displaystyle x^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4e2d4389a3b6f20cb8a118506601a68c2263143a)), then ![Image 10: {\displaystyle V'}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ff31fe992a31b4c954a933f1be91e2739a1c0ae7) is quadratic (proportional to ![Image 11: {\displaystyle x^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cf0bf28fd28f45d07e1ceb909ce333c18c558c93)). This means, in the case of Newton's second law, the right side would be in the form of ![Image 12: {\displaystyle \langle x\rangle ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a66e16570ecabb99c397dda5c462c4289bb31c35), while in the Ehrenfest theorem it is in the form of ![Image 13: {\displaystyle \langle x^{2}\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/57cc6f0a3e9ee02cb182afe885c3602607fa413a). The difference between these two quantities is the square of the uncertainty in ![Image 14: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) and is therefore nonzero.

An exception occurs in case when the classical equations of motion are linear, that is, when ![Image 15: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) is quadratic and ![Image 16: {\displaystyle V'}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ff31fe992a31b4c954a933f1be91e2739a1c0ae7) is linear. In that special case, ![Image 17: {\displaystyle V'\left(\left\langle x\right\rangle \right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7636cdb2cd9d00f46ea9f6e30ca2205d7fa5493a) and ![Image 18: {\displaystyle \left\langle V'(x)\right\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d2be4590f5f6043003c5e7e871b60bbf264be23e) do agree. Thus, for the case of a quantum harmonic oscillator, the expected position and expected momentum do exactly follow the classical trajectories.

For general systems, if the wave function is highly concentrated around a point ![Image 19: {\displaystyle x_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/86f21d0e31751534cd6584264ecf864a6aa792cf), then ![Image 20: {\displaystyle V'\left(\left\langle x\right\rangle \right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7636cdb2cd9d00f46ea9f6e30ca2205d7fa5493a) and ![Image 21: {\displaystyle \left\langle V'(x)\right\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d2be4590f5f6043003c5e7e871b60bbf264be23e) will be _almost_ the same, since both will be approximately equal to ![Image 22: {\displaystyle V'(x_{0})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/597d8921d945c06cd6f9c31c4547c1fbc824b9ed). In that case, the expected position and expected momentum will _approximately_ follow the classical trajectories, at least for as long as the wave function remains localized in position.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ehrenfest's_theorem#cite_note-5)

Derivation in the Schrödinger picture
-------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ehrenfest_theorem&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Derivation in the Schrödinger picture")]

Suppose some system is presently in a [quantum state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_state "Quantum state")Φ. If we want to know the instantaneous time derivative of the expectation value of A, that is, by definition ![Image 23: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}{\frac {d}{dt}}\langle A\rangle &={\frac {d}{dt}}\int \Phi ^{*}A\Phi \,d^{3}x\\&=\int \left({\frac {\partial \Phi ^{*}}{\partial t}}\right)A\Phi \,d^{3}x+\int \Phi ^{*}\left({\frac {\partial A}{\partial t}}\right)\Phi \,d^{3}x+\int \Phi ^{*}A\left({\frac {\partial \Phi }{\partial t}}\right)\,d^{3}x\\&=\int \left({\frac {\partial \Phi ^{*}}{\partial t}}\right)A\Phi \,d^{3}x+\left\langle {\frac {\partial A}{\partial t}}\right\rangle +\int \Phi ^{*}A\left({\frac {\partial \Phi }{\partial t}}\right)\,d^{3}x\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/635bcb6659048dd7d9de78a19c1fc5a24f7cd9e5) where we are integrating over all of space. If we apply the [Schrödinger equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schr%C3%B6dinger_equation "Schrödinger equation"), we find that ![Image 24: {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial \Phi }{\partial t}}={\frac {1}{i\hbar }}H\Phi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e4caab1801affa3edfd06a799727e6cb8e28534a)

By taking the complex conjugate we find [[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ehrenfest's_theorem#cite_note-6)![Image 25: {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial \Phi ^{*}}{\partial t}}=-{\frac {1}{i\hbar }}\Phi ^{*}H^{*}=-{\frac {1}{i\hbar }}\Phi ^{*}H.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/543328b48b1d51d130608761d9df27df85d04b13)

Note _H_ = _H_∗, because the [Hamiltonian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamiltonian_(quantum_mechanics) "Hamiltonian (quantum mechanics)") is [Hermitian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermitian_operator "Hermitian operator"). Placing this into the above equation we have

![Image 26: {\displaystyle {\frac {d}{dt}}\langle A\rangle ={\frac {1}{i\hbar }}\int \Phi ^{*}(AH-HA)\Phi ~d^{3}x+\left\langle {\frac {\partial A}{\partial t}}\right\rangle ={\frac {1}{i\hbar }}\langle [A,H]\rangle +\left\langle {\frac {\partial A}{\partial t}}\right\rangle .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d1f00ea01ef7a2b29ad7544b22369195652c58b9)

Often (but not always) the operator A is time-independent so that its derivative is zero and we can ignore the last term.

Derivation in the Heisenberg picture
------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ehrenfest_theorem&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Derivation in the Heisenberg picture")]

In the [Heisenberg picture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heisenberg_picture "Heisenberg picture"), the derivation is straightforward. The Heisenberg picture moves the time dependence of the system to operators instead of state vectors. Starting with the Heisenberg equation of motion, ![Image 27: {\displaystyle {\frac {d}{dt}}A(t)={\frac {\partial A(t)}{\partial t}}+{\frac {1}{i\hbar }}[A(t),H],}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/82b9aa5e08a24dcd88b124df320196a634a5cf61) Ehrenfest's theorem follows simply upon projecting the Heisenberg equation onto ![Image 28: {\displaystyle |\Psi \rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e77f6b1e903837c5765c9683da41dd93199621c) from the right and ![Image 29: {\displaystyle \langle \Psi |}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3cad280544637db61aad581527433c6ce5ac1409) from the left, or taking the expectation value, so ![Image 30: {\displaystyle \left\langle \Psi \left|{\frac {d}{dt}}A(t)\right|\Psi \right\rangle =\left\langle \Psi \left|{\frac {\partial A(t)}{\partial t}}\right|\Psi \right\rangle +\left\langle \Psi \left|{\frac {1}{i\hbar }}[A(t),H]\right|\Psi \right\rangle ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f7b5c7c880e7301afdaf19b5828f551bbd9dfc55)

One may pull the ⁠_d_/_dt_⁠ out of the first term, since the state vectors are no longer time dependent in the Heisenberg Picture. Therefore, ![Image 31: {\displaystyle {\frac {d}{dt}}\langle A(t)\rangle =\left\langle {\frac {\partial A(t)}{\partial t}}\right\rangle +{\frac {1}{i\hbar }}\left\langle [A(t),H]\right\rangle .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e51bfa5eb4fd8d43ad1b27245f792d13770b345a)

For the very general example of a massive [particle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementary_particle "Elementary particle") moving in a [potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potential "Potential"), the Hamiltonian is simply ![Image 32: {\displaystyle H(x,p,t)={\frac {p^{2}}{2m}}+V(x,t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/680098420845c8051cd34cfa39ac56ee6a0d6f10) where x is the position of the particle.

Suppose we wanted to know the instantaneous change in the expectation of the momentum p. Using Ehrenfest's theorem, we have ![Image 33: {\displaystyle {\frac {d}{dt}}\langle p\rangle ={\frac {1}{i\hbar }}\langle [p,H]\rangle +\left\langle {\frac {\partial p}{\partial t}}\right\rangle ={\frac {1}{i\hbar }}\langle [p,V(x,t)]\rangle ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e314255a33c84294179b07e7d37f52583f210010)

since the operator p commutes with itself and has no time dependence.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ehrenfest's_theorem#cite_note-7) By expanding the right-hand-side, replacing p by −_iħ_∇, we get ![Image 34: {\displaystyle {\frac {d}{dt}}\langle p\rangle =\int \Phi ^{*}V(x,t){\frac {\partial }{\partial x}}\Phi ~dx-\int \Phi ^{*}{\frac {\partial }{\partial x}}(V(x,t)\Phi )~dx~.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/065c8cbe7918a488247462bacb6f0e5b6d582983)

After applying the [product rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Product_rule "Product rule") on the second term, we have ![Image 35: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}{\frac {d}{dt}}\langle p\rangle &=\int \Phi ^{*}V(x,t){\frac {\partial }{\partial x}}\Phi ~dx-\int \Phi ^{*}\left({\frac {\partial }{\partial x}}V(x,t)\right)\Phi ~dx-\int \Phi ^{*}V(x,t){\frac {\partial }{\partial x}}\Phi ~dx\\&=-\int \Phi ^{*}\left({\frac {\partial }{\partial x}}V(x,t)\right)\Phi ~dx\\&=\left\langle -{\frac {\partial }{\partial x}}V(x,t)\right\rangle =\langle F\rangle .\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e2059644e93e7fc8aee03443e68d1bc73622ff4f)

As explained in the introduction, this result does _not_ say that the pair ![Image 36: {\displaystyle (\langle X\rangle ,\langle P\rangle )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8a208b4fe89bcbccd675e91ada6ab2f734def8b4) satisfies [Newton's second law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_second_law "Newton's second law"), because the right-hand side of the formula is ![Image 37: {\displaystyle \langle F(x,t)\rangle ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d9141e166aa08dd0b03a2c67ad0e7c56c89f254a) rather than ![Image 38: {\displaystyle F(\langle X\rangle ,t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/397d87e04cb96a6a26f19564d27a635389ac5b25). Nevertheless, as explained in the introduction, for states that are highly localized in space, the expected position and momentum will _approximately_ follow classical trajectories, which may be understood as an instance of the [correspondence principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Correspondence_principle "Correspondence principle").

Similarly, we can obtain the instantaneous change in the position expectation value. ![Image 39: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}{\frac {d}{dt}}\langle x\rangle &={\frac {1}{i\hbar }}\langle [x,H]\rangle +\left\langle {\frac {\partial x}{\partial t}}\right\rangle \\[5pt]&={\frac {1}{i\hbar }}\left\langle \left[x,{\frac {p^{2}}{2m}}+V(x,t)\right]\right\rangle +0\\[5pt]&={\frac {1}{i\hbar }}\left\langle \left[x,{\frac {p^{2}}{2m}}\right]\right\rangle \\[5pt]&={\frac {1}{i\hbar 2m}}\left\langle [x,p]{\frac {d}{dp}}p^{2}\right\rangle \\[5pt]&={\frac {1}{i\hbar 2m}}\langle i\hbar 2p\rangle \\[5pt]&={\frac {1}{m}}\langle p\rangle \end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3e5b47a274ca180f57ede2f4b4916058cd27e08f)

This result is actually in exact accord with the classical equation.

Derivation of the Schrödinger equation from the Ehrenfest theorems
------------------------------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ehrenfest_theorem&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Derivation of the Schrödinger equation from the Ehrenfest theorems")]

It was established above that the Ehrenfest theorems are consequences of the [Schrödinger equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schr%C3%B6dinger_equation "Schrödinger equation"). However, the converse is also true: the Schrödinger equation can be inferred from the Ehrenfest theorems.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ehrenfest's_theorem#cite_note-Bondar2012-8) We begin from ![Image 40: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}m{\frac {d}{dt}}\left\langle \Psi (t)\right|{\hat {x}}\left|\Psi (t)\right\rangle &=\left\langle \Psi (t)\right|{\hat {p}}\left|\Psi (t)\right\rangle ,\\[5pt]{\frac {d}{dt}}\left\langle \Psi (t)\right|{\hat {p}}\left|\Psi (t)\right\rangle &=\left\langle \Psi (t)\right|-V'({\hat {x}})\left|\Psi (t)\right\rangle .\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5ea29c13d0cbe58ff2000dc979d938942c2ee3de)

Application of the [product rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Product_rule "Product rule") leads to ![Image 41: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\left\langle {\frac {d\Psi }{dt}}{\Big |}{\hat {x}}{\Big |}\Psi \right\rangle +\left\langle \Psi {\Big |}{\hat {x}}{\Big |}{\frac {d\Psi }{dt}}\right\rangle &=\left\langle \Psi {\Big |}{\frac {\hat {p}}{m}}{\Big |}\Psi \right\rangle ,\\[5pt]\left\langle {\frac {d\Psi }{dt}}{\Big |}{\hat {p}}{\Big |}\Psi \right\rangle +\left\langle \Psi {\Big |}{\hat {p}}{\Big |}{\frac {d\Psi }{dt}}\right\rangle &=\langle \Psi |-V'({\hat {x}})|\Psi \rangle ,\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/219a9a4edd81e2f997d83aeff3b90c23ac1b71eb) Here, apply [Stone's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stone%27s_theorem_on_one-parameter_unitary_groups "Stone's theorem on one-parameter unitary groups"), using Ĥ to denote the quantum generator of time translation. The next step is to show that this is the same as the Hamiltonian operator used in quantum mechanics. Stone's theorem implies ![Image 42: {\displaystyle i\hbar \left|{\frac {d\Psi }{dt}}\right\rangle ={\hat {H}}|\Psi (t)\rangle ~,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7872dd238370aff6e4c795b3f586d2daae98b671) where ħ was introduced as a normalization constant to the balance dimensionality. Since these identities must be valid for any initial state, the averaging can be dropped and the system of commutator equations for Ĥ are derived: ![Image 43: {\displaystyle im[{\hat {H}},{\hat {x}}]=\hbar {\hat {p}},\qquad i[{\hat {H}},{\hat {p}}]=-\hbar V'({\hat {x}}).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5a2449926ade577a5a42281524c6c3b83bf4178f)

Assuming that observables of the coordinate and momentum obey the [canonical commutation relation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canonical_commutation_relation "Canonical commutation relation")[_x̂_, _p̂_] = _iħ_. Setting ![Image 44: {\displaystyle {\hat {H}}=H({\hat {x}},{\hat {p}})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5951acec87bb711d611394ec34834e346536d0cd), the commutator equations can be converted into the differential equations[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ehrenfest's_theorem#cite_note-Bondar2012-8)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ehrenfest's_theorem#cite_note-Transtrum2005-9)![Image 45: {\displaystyle m{\frac {\partial H(x,p)}{\partial p}}=p,\qquad {\frac {\partial H(x,p)}{\partial x}}=V'(x),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/df42cedeb707454919521afee298897cd665ea46) whose solution is the familiar [quantum Hamiltonian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamiltonian_(quantum_mechanics) "Hamiltonian (quantum mechanics)")![Image 46: {\displaystyle {\hat {H}}={\frac {{\hat {p}}^{2}}{2m}}+V({\hat {x}}).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b61ab1e9d42529cff12833349fdc7dad133414d)

Whence, the [Schrödinger equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schr%C3%B6dinger_equation "Schrödinger equation") was derived from the Ehrenfest theorems by assuming the canonical commutation relation between the coordinate and momentum. If one assumes that the coordinate and momentum commute, the same computational method leads to the [Koopman–von Neumann classical mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Koopman%E2%80%93von_Neumann_classical_mechanics "Koopman–von Neumann classical mechanics"), which is the [Hilbert space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space "Hilbert space") formulation of [classical mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_mechanics "Classical mechanics").[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ehrenfest's_theorem#cite_note-Bondar2012-8) Therefore, this derivation as well as the [derivation of the Koopman–von Neumann mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Koopman%E2%80%93von_Neumann_classical_mechanics#Derivation_starting_from_operator_axioms "Koopman–von Neumann classical mechanics"), shows that the essential difference between quantum and classical mechanics reduces to the value of the commutator [_x̂_, _p̂_].

The implications of the Ehrenfest theorem for systems with classically chaotic dynamics are discussed at Scholarpedia article [Ehrenfest time and chaos](http://www.scholarpedia.org/article/Ehrenfest_time_and_chaos). Due to exponential instability of classical trajectories the Ehrenfest time, on which there is a complete correspondence between quantum and classical evolution, is shown to be logarithmically short being proportional to a logarithm of typical quantum number. For the case of integrable dynamics this time scale is much larger being proportional to a certain power of quantum number.

7.   **[^](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ehrenfest's_theorem#cite_ref-7)**Although the expectation value of the momentum ⟨_p_⟩, which is a [real-number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number")-valued function of time, will have time dependence, the momentum operator itself, p does not, in this picture: Rather, the momentum operator is a constant [linear operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_operator "Linear operator") on the [Hilbert space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space "Hilbert space") of the system. The time dependence of the expectation value, in this picture, is due to the [time evolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_evolution "Time evolution") of the wavefunction for which the expectation value is calculated. An [Ad hoc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ad_hoc "Ad hoc") example of an operator which does have time dependence is ⟨_xt_ 2⟩, where x is the ordinary position operator and t is just the (non-operator) time, a parameter.
