In green, confocal parabolae opening upwards, ![Image 2: {\displaystyle 2y={\frac {x^{2}}{\sigma ^{2}}}-\sigma ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/673e571139e5b22fccaf444c22145eb8d7e9f7d5) In red, confocal parabolae opening downwards, ![Image 3: {\displaystyle 2y=-{\frac {x^{2}}{\tau ^{2}}}+\tau ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e6a5c3e95d49388cafee36ab3252ee706cdec39d)

**Parabolic coordinates** are a two-dimensional [orthogonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_coordinates "Orthogonal coordinates")[coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_system "Coordinate system") in which the [coordinate lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_system#Coordinate_line "Coordinate system") are [confocal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confocal "Confocal")[parabolas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parabola "Parabola"). [A three-dimensional version](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parabolic_cylindrical_coordinates "Parabolic cylindrical coordinates") of parabolic coordinates is obtained by rotating the two-dimensional [system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_system "Coordinate system") about the symmetry axis of the parabolas.

Parabolic coordinates have found many applications, e.g., the treatment of the [Stark effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stark_effect "Stark effect") and the [potential theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potential_theory "Potential theory") of the edges.

The curves of constant ![Image 7: {\displaystyle \sigma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/59f59b7c3e6fdb1d0365a494b81fb9a696138c36) form confocal parabolae

![Image 8: {\displaystyle 2y={\frac {x^{2}}{\sigma ^{2}}}-\sigma ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/673e571139e5b22fccaf444c22145eb8d7e9f7d5)
that open upwards (i.e., towards ![Image 9: {\displaystyle +y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/05c6eafa49a9bcc5f53484e1e9e6f8a182c00aed)), whereas the curves of constant ![Image 10: {\displaystyle \tau }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38a7dcde9730ef0853809fefc18d88771f95206c) form confocal parabolae

-----------------------------

The scale factors for the parabolic coordinates ![Image 17: {\displaystyle (\sigma ,\tau )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c2d60596d44a9bc5043b60b76c1fac959eb65eb0) are equal

![Image 18: {\displaystyle h_{\sigma }=h_{\tau }={\sqrt {\sigma ^{2}+\tau ^{2}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1a46d762e5391efe1f62bdf5fd9bf0504c0b1650)
Hence, the infinitesimal element of area is

![Image 19: {\displaystyle dA=\left(\sigma ^{2}+\tau ^{2}\right)d\sigma d\tau }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cf76cc2c4440056b1d68d8a8949b51cc18e32d83)
and the [Laplacian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laplacian "Laplacian") equals

![Image 20: {\displaystyle \nabla ^{2}\Phi ={\frac {1}{\sigma ^{2}+\tau ^{2}}}\left({\frac {\partial ^{2}\Phi }{\partial \sigma ^{2}}}+{\frac {\partial ^{2}\Phi }{\partial \tau ^{2}}}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8fd25fc4b1495e6b13d0bea1674f63f3887aa7f4)
Other differential operators such as ![Image 21: {\displaystyle \nabla \cdot \mathbf {F} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1f4f5b9e6560d7fdaf1a69d9fdeff6beb1f327cc) and ![Image 22: {\displaystyle \nabla \times \mathbf {F} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c6e2429b7e3cb6965d1a6651703ff5ef0c88b378) can be expressed in the coordinates ![Image 23: {\displaystyle (\sigma ,\tau )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c2d60596d44a9bc5043b60b76c1fac959eb65eb0) by substituting the scale factors into the general formulae found in [orthogonal coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_coordinates "Orthogonal coordinates").

Three-dimensional parabolic coordinates
---------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Parabolic_coordinates&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Three-dimensional parabolic coordinates")]

[![Image 24](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Parabolic_coordinates_3D.png/330px-Parabolic_coordinates_3D.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Parabolic_coordinates_3D.png)

[Coordinate surfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_system#Coordinate_surface "Coordinate system") of the three-dimensional parabolic coordinates. The red paraboloid corresponds to τ=2, the blue paraboloid corresponds to σ=1, and the yellow half-plane corresponds to φ=−60°. The three surfaces intersect at the point **P** (shown as a black sphere) with [Cartesian coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system "Cartesian coordinate system") roughly (1.0, −1.732, 1.5).

The two-dimensional parabolic coordinates form the basis for two sets of three-dimensional [orthogonal coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_coordinates "Orthogonal coordinates"). The [parabolic cylindrical coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parabolic_cylindrical_coordinates "Parabolic cylindrical coordinates") are produced by projecting in the ![Image 25: {\displaystyle z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf368e72c009decd9b6686ee84a375632e11de98)-direction. Rotation about the symmetry axis of the parabolae produces a set of confocal paraboloids, the coordinate system of tridimensional parabolic coordinates. Expressed in terms of cartesian coordinates:

![Image 26: {\displaystyle x=\sigma \tau \cos \varphi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1bd5bf139358308d14aedd9f98d9675fe6e5478d)![Image 27: {\displaystyle y=\sigma \tau \sin \varphi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/69831d7d0663ab99a962a93116c32fcd38cb216d)![Image 28: {\displaystyle z={\frac {1}{2}}\left(\tau ^{2}-\sigma ^{2}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/12b052ed2e00ad3bf126ffcac71671f5659e3f56)
where the parabolae are now aligned with the ![Image 29: {\displaystyle z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf368e72c009decd9b6686ee84a375632e11de98)-axis, about which the rotation was carried out. Hence, the azimuthal angle ![Image 30: {\displaystyle \varphi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/33ee699558d09cf9d653f6351f9fda0b2f4aaa3e) is defined

![Image 31: {\displaystyle \tan \varphi ={\frac {y}{x}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c7f8914ff6a3dfd30ca46c217798e22620e36f70)
The surfaces of constant ![Image 32: {\displaystyle \sigma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/59f59b7c3e6fdb1d0365a494b81fb9a696138c36) form confocal paraboloids

![Image 33: {\displaystyle 2z={\frac {x^{2}+y^{2}}{\sigma ^{2}}}-\sigma ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/42b8529e6c0d07eaffca3355d2ffcaf79504059d)
that open upwards (i.e., towards ![Image 34: {\displaystyle +z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d128a5e034d5136437e671ed08b7188cead3f33f)) whereas the surfaces of constant ![Image 35: {\displaystyle \tau }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38a7dcde9730ef0853809fefc18d88771f95206c) form confocal paraboloids

![Image 36: {\displaystyle 2z=-{\frac {x^{2}+y^{2}}{\tau ^{2}}}+\tau ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bd943fbc56295946701568d29194e39300bbcbd3)
that open downwards (i.e., towards ![Image 37: {\displaystyle -z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/78c4571a4a3ceb7e7e55712372835ebe65d20f3e)). The foci of all these paraboloids are located at the origin.

The [Riemannian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_manifold "Riemannian manifold")[metric tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_tensor "Metric tensor") associated with this coordinate system is

![Image 38: {\displaystyle g_{ij}={\begin{bmatrix}\sigma ^{2}+\tau ^{2}&0&0\\0&\sigma ^{2}+\tau ^{2}&0\\0&0&\sigma ^{2}\tau ^{2}\end{bmatrix}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/357d25e58d200bed467671435854debc0b1a5343)

Three-dimensional scale factors
-------------------------------

The three dimensional scale factors are:
