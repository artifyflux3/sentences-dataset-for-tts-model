**Power** is the amount of [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy "Energy") transferred or converted per unit time. In the [International System of Units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Units "International System of Units"), the unit of power is the [watt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Watt "Watt"), equal to one [joule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joule "Joule") per second. Power is a [scalar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_(physics) "Scalar (physics)") quantity.

Specifying power in particular systems may require attention to other quantities; for example, the power involved in moving a ground vehicle is the product of the [aerodynamic drag](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aerodynamic_drag "Aerodynamic drag") plus [traction force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Traction_(engineering) "Traction (engineering)") on the wheels, and the [velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity "Velocity") of the vehicle. The output power of a [motor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engine "Engine") is the product of the [torque](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torque "Torque") that the motor generates and the [angular velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_velocity "Angular velocity") of its output shaft. Likewise, the power dissipated in an [electrical element](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrical_element "Electrical element") of a [circuit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrical_circuit "Electrical circuit") is the product of the [current](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_current "Electric current") flowing through the element and of the [voltage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voltage "Voltage") across the element.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_(physics)#cite_note-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_(physics)#cite_note-2)

Power is the [rate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rate_(mathematics) "Rate (mathematics)") with respect to time at which work is done or, more generally, the rate of change of total mechanical energy. It is given by: ![Image 2: {\displaystyle P={\frac {dE}{dt}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4153440d5e582dd37b968a08b0233792d1b33b5b) where P is power, E is the total mechanical energy (sum of kinetic and potential energy), and t is time.

For cases where only work is considered, power is also expressed as: ![Image 3: {\displaystyle P={\frac {dW}{dt}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1a63a64d60915ee3d2a890547a213f7c00966912) where W is the work done on the system. However, in systems where potential energy changes without explicit work being done (e.g., changing fields or conservative forces), the total energy definition is more general.

We will now show that the mechanical power generated by a force F on a body moving at the velocity v can be expressed as the product: ![Image 4: {\displaystyle P={\frac {dW}{dt}}=\mathbf {F} \cdot \mathbf {v} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4d786b9f8eb6e4dd503bf71a7a4bf04c78824df4)

If a _constant_ force **F** is applied throughout a [distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distance "Distance")**x**, the work done is defined as ![Image 5: {\displaystyle W=\mathbf {F} \cdot \mathbf {x} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fe3097d81155810c7915a4272ba11c47391f1969). In this case, power can be written as: ![Image 6: {\displaystyle P={\frac {dW}{dt}}={\frac {d}{dt}}\left(\mathbf {F} \cdot \mathbf {x} \right)=\mathbf {F} \cdot {\frac {d\mathbf {x} }{dt}}=\mathbf {F} \cdot \mathbf {v} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2da9c28cdb2391f67feefece9ad13deb82d27ed5)

If instead the force is _variable over a three-dimensional curve C_, then the work is expressed in terms of the line integral: ![Image 7: {\displaystyle W=\int _{C}\mathbf {F} \cdot d\mathbf {r} =\int _{\Delta t}\mathbf {F} \cdot {\frac {d\mathbf {r} }{dt}}\ dt=\int _{\Delta t}\mathbf {F} \cdot \mathbf {v} \,dt.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0577447a0a6481647aecfb7dafbee7d9f81447b5)

From the [fundamental theorem of calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_theorem_of_calculus "Fundamental theorem of calculus"), we know that ![Image 8: {\displaystyle P={\frac {dW}{dt}}={\frac {d}{dt}}\int _{\Delta t}\mathbf {F} \cdot \mathbf {v} \,dt=\mathbf {F} \cdot \mathbf {v} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8bf84537ae83c58a5b71691dd08c80d4e1424e0c) Hence the formula is valid for any general situation.

In older works, power is sometimes called _activity_.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_(physics)#cite_note-Smithsonian_Tables-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_(physics)#cite_note-Heron_Motors-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_(physics)#cite_note-Nature_1902-5)

The dimension of power is energy divided by time. In the [International System of Units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Units "International System of Units") (SI), the unit of power is the [watt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Watt "Watt") (W), which is equal to one [joule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joule "Joule") per second. Other common and traditional measures are [horsepower](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horsepower "Horsepower") (hp), comparing to the power of a horse; one [_mechanical horsepower_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horsepower#Mechanical_horsepower "Horsepower") equals about 745.7 watts. Other units of power include [ergs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erg "Erg") per second (erg/s), [foot-pounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foot-pound_force "Foot-pound force") per minute, [dBm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DBm "DBm"), a logarithmic measure relative to a reference of 1 milliwatt, [calories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calorie "Calorie") per hour, [BTU](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BTU "BTU") per hour (BTU/h), and [tons of refrigeration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ton_of_refrigeration "Ton of refrigeration").

Average power and instantaneous power
-------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Power_(physics)&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Average power and instantaneous power")]

As a simple example, burning one kilogram of [coal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coal "Coal") releases more energy than detonating a kilogram of [TNT](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TNT "TNT"),[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_(physics)#cite_note-6) but because the TNT reaction releases energy more quickly, it delivers more power than the coal. If Δ _W_ is the amount of [work](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanical_work "Mechanical work") performed during a period of [time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time "Time") of duration Δ _t_, the average power _P_ avg over that period is given by the formula ![Image 9: {\displaystyle P_{\mathrm {avg} }={\frac {\Delta W}{\Delta t}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/79a31696ee4286f17a9307a7e212cddb1d9f8abe) It is the average amount of work done or energy converted per unit of time. Average power is often called "power" when the context makes it clear.

Instantaneous power is the limiting value of the average power as the time interval Δ _t_ approaches zero. ![Image 10: {\displaystyle P=\lim _{\Delta t\to 0}P_{\mathrm {avg} }=\lim _{\Delta t\to 0}{\frac {\Delta W}{\Delta t}}={\frac {dW}{dt}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7077c02738abadfbb4ebf94c3d1e23ae7cec598d)

When power _P_ is constant, the amount of work performed in time period t can be calculated as ![Image 11: {\displaystyle W=Pt.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/50dcba5d53d1a2cec2d05e6de47bcacfa00fc79e)

In the context of energy conversion, it is more customary to use the symbol E rather than W.

[![Image 12](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Horsepower_plain.svg/250px-Horsepower_plain.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Horsepower_plain.svg)

One _metric horsepower_ is needed to lift 75[kilograms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kilogram "Kilogram") by 1[metre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metre "Metre") in 1[second](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second "Second").

Power in mechanical systems is the combination of forces and movement. In particular, power is the product of a force on an object and the object's velocity, or the product of a torque on a shaft and the shaft's angular velocity.

Mechanical power is also described as the time [derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative "Derivative") of work. In [mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanics "Mechanics"), the [work](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanical_work "Mechanical work") done by a force **F** on an object that travels along a curve C is given by the [line integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_integral "Line integral"): ![Image 13: {\displaystyle W_{C}=\int _{C}\mathbf {F} \cdot \mathbf {v} \,dt=\int _{C}\mathbf {F} \cdot d\mathbf {x} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9fb4d83d005f47b6c0ba4e3d7ed9ed72e039089a) where **x** defines the path C and **v** is the velocity along this path.

If the force **F** is derivable from a potential ([conservative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservative_force "Conservative force")), then applying the [gradient theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gradient_theorem "Gradient theorem") (and remembering that force is the negative of the [gradient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gradient "Gradient") of the potential energy) yields: ![Image 14: {\displaystyle W_{C}=U(A)-U(B),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3b39dd06cb773295e26811f5f1ce16dce929f87) where A and B are the beginning and end of the path along which the work was done.

The power at any point along the curve C is the time derivative: ![Image 15: {\displaystyle P(t)={\frac {dW}{dt}}=\mathbf {F} \cdot \mathbf {v} =-{\frac {dU}{dt}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d5de9211109aa9998e1b77e403cbf045e34f2a37)

In one dimension, this can be simplified to: ![Image 16: {\displaystyle P(t)=F\cdot v.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2227df6ce4046db4725421ce2330b7543ca1362e)

In rotational systems, power is the product of the [torque](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torque "Torque")**τ** and [angular velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_velocity "Angular velocity")**ω**, ![Image 17: {\displaystyle P(t)={\boldsymbol {\tau }}\cdot {\boldsymbol {\omega }},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7256f66346ba57d29da9872a2a941a8284cf1880) where _**ω**_ is [angular frequency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_frequency "Angular frequency"), measured in [radians per second](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radians_per_second "Radians per second"). The ![Image 18: {\displaystyle \cdot }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ba2c023bad1bd39ed49080f729cbf26bc448c9ba) represents [scalar product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_product "Scalar product").

In fluid power systems such as [hydraulic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydraulic "Hydraulic") actuators, power is given by ![Image 19: {\displaystyle P(t)=pQ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/69d8bc828906b7e595ab98344d924ad24a793c6c) where p is [pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pressure "Pressure") in [pascals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pascal_(unit) "Pascal (unit)") or N/m 2, and Q is [volumetric flow rate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volumetric_flow_rate "Volumetric flow rate") in m 3/s in SI units.

### Mechanical advantage

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Power_(physics)&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Mechanical advantage")]

If a mechanical system has no losses, then the input power must equal the output power. This provides a simple formula for the [mechanical advantage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanical_advantage "Mechanical advantage") of the system.

Let the input power to a device be a force _F_ A acting on a point that moves with velocity _v_ A and the output power be a force _F_ B acts on a point that moves with velocity _v_ B. If there are no losses in the system, then ![Image 20: {\displaystyle P=F_{\text{B}}v_{\text{B}}=F_{\text{A}}v_{\text{A}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8e91a77b4769aa8a0cbcfb9d3ea056e9a2e4459b) and the [mechanical advantage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanical_advantage "Mechanical advantage") of the system (output force per input force) is given by ![Image 21: {\displaystyle \mathrm {MA} ={\frac {F_{\text{B}}}{F_{\text{A}}}}={\frac {v_{\text{A}}}{v_{\text{B}}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1b34b8703492f2c81776b2ab7235bee133b3df14)

The similar relationship is obtained for rotating systems, where _T_ A and _ω_ A are the torque and angular velocity of the input and _T_ B and _ω_ B are the torque and angular velocity of the output. If there are no losses in the system, then ![Image 22: {\displaystyle P=T_{\text{A}}\omega _{\text{A}}=T_{\text{B}}\omega _{\text{B}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d833c08f7345f5f0b5e513ace05f9b38e6e31936) which yields the [mechanical advantage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanical_advantage "Mechanical advantage")![Image 23: {\displaystyle \mathrm {MA} ={\frac {T_{\text{B}}}{T_{\text{A}}}}={\frac {\omega _{\text{A}}}{\omega _{\text{B}}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf470a5687a7c35dc3545b6ffda7a62eafcee37f)

These relations are important because they define the maximum performance of a device in terms of [velocity ratios](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity_ratio "Velocity ratio") determined by its physical dimensions. See for example [gear ratios](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gear_ratio "Gear ratio").

[![Image 24: Ansel Adams photograph of electrical wires of the Boulder Dam Power Units](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Ansel_Adams_-_National_Archives_79-AAB-02.jpg/250px-Ansel_Adams_-_National_Archives_79-AAB-02.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Ansel_Adams_-_National_Archives_79-AAB-02.jpg)

[Ansel Adams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ansel_Adams "Ansel Adams") photograph of electrical wires of the Boulder Dam Power Units, 1941–1942

The instantaneous electrical power _P_ delivered to a component is given by ![Image 25: {\displaystyle P(t)=I(t)\cdot V(t),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8b12679760e20f0aff9ccdf4ffa9f710b8133865) where

If the component is a [resistor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resistor "Resistor") with time-invariant [voltage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voltage "Voltage") to [current](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_current "Electric current") ratio, then: ![Image 26: {\displaystyle P=I\cdot V=I^{2}\cdot R={\frac {V^{2}}{R}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/329da9bedd7ddd8090415faf6e652c26bc850b9e) where ![Image 27: {\displaystyle R={\frac {V}{I}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/227e3139fabbc01b096c1d14dab39609b04c412a) is the [electrical resistance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrical_resistance "Electrical resistance"), measured in [ohms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ohm "Ohm").

Peak power and duty cycle
-------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Power_(physics)&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Peak power and duty cycle")]

[![Image 28](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Peak-power-average-power-tau-T.png/250px-Peak-power-average-power-tau-T.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Peak-power-average-power-tau-T.png)

In a train of identical pulses, the instantaneous power is a periodic function of time. The ratio of the pulse duration to the period is equal to the ratio of the average power to the peak power. It is also called the duty cycle (see text for definitions).

In the case of a periodic signal ![Image 29: {\displaystyle s(t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c484de351ba40ccb9a5ad522c29c1aac5686c0df) of period ![Image 30: {\displaystyle T}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ec7200acd984a1d3a3d7dc455e262fbe54f7f6e0), like a train of identical pulses, the instantaneous power ![Image 31: {\textstyle p(t)=|s(t)|^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0b19738cdd60e4f712a402c9360989e8d18475a8) is also a periodic function of period ![Image 32: {\displaystyle T}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ec7200acd984a1d3a3d7dc455e262fbe54f7f6e0). The _peak power_ is simply defined by: ![Image 33: {\displaystyle P_{0}=\max[p(t)].}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/651ce48ebeae6bcfdbbcf14f27a6665e676eaee9)

The peak power is not always readily measurable, however, and the measurement of the average power ![Image 34: {\displaystyle P_{\mathrm {avg} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ee679bd7ad9a5cabddc68c33d503d16cfe7e4076) is more commonly performed by an instrument. If one defines the energy per pulse as ![Image 35: {\displaystyle \varepsilon _{\mathrm {pulse} }=\int _{0}^{T}p(t)\,dt}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c11cb404fe797c8f7963fecffa111a6d21695a34) then the average power is ![Image 36: {\displaystyle P_{\mathrm {avg} }={\frac {1}{T}}\int _{0}^{T}p(t)\,dt={\frac {\varepsilon _{\mathrm {pulse} }}{T}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dc4b34d67f5febf280fe05d0966af07e65415b32)

One may define the pulse length ![Image 37: {\displaystyle \tau }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38a7dcde9730ef0853809fefc18d88771f95206c) such that ![Image 38: {\displaystyle P_{0}\tau =\varepsilon _{\mathrm {pulse} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/44bb70fb1b8f2382b449d6db1d147cfaca02d290) so that the ratios ![Image 39: {\displaystyle {\frac {P_{\mathrm {avg} }}{P_{0}}}={\frac {\tau }{T}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ca290bcf651f9588f2ee3d589a4a43cdf6f4eb54) are equal. These ratios are called the _duty cycle_ of the pulse train.

Power is related to intensity at a radius ![Image 40: {\displaystyle r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d1ecb613aa2984f0576f70f86650b7c2a132538); the power emitted by a source can be written as:[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]![Image 41: {\displaystyle P(r)=I(4\pi r^{2}).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f26495b3f3a807a3c2fb069974d19edb84bd6022)

6.   **[^](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_(physics)#cite_ref-6)**Burning coal produces around 15-30 [megajoules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Megajoule "Megajoule") per kilogram, while detonating TNT produces about 4.7 megajoules per kilogram. For the coal value, see Fisher, Juliya (2003). ["Energy Density of Coal"](http://hypertextbook.com/facts/2003/JuliyaFisher.shtml). _The Physics Factbook_. Retrieved 30 May 2011. For the TNT value, see the article [TNT equivalent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TNT_equivalent "TNT equivalent"). Neither value includes the weight of oxygen from the air used during combustion.
