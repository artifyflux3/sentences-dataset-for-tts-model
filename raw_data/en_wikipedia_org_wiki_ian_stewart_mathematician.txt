**Ian Nicholas Stewart**[FRS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Society "Fellow of the Royal Society")[CMath](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chartered_Mathematician "Chartered Mathematician")[FIMA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Institute_of_Mathematics_and_its_Applications "Fellow of the Institute of Mathematics and its Applications") (born 24 September 1945)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ian_Stewart_(mathematician)#cite_note-3) is a British mathematician and a [popular-science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Popular-science "Popular-science") and science-fiction writer.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ian_Stewart_(mathematician)#cite_note-scopus-4) He is Emeritus Professor of Mathematics at the [University of Warwick](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Warwick "University of Warwick"), England.

Education and early life
------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ian_Stewart_(mathematician)&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Education and early life")]

Stewart was born in 1945 in [Folkestone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Folkestone "Folkestone"), England. While in the [sixth form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sixth_form "Sixth form") at [Harvey Grammar School](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Harvey_Grammar_School "The Harvey Grammar School") in Folkestone he came to the attention of the mathematics teacher. The teacher had Stewart sit mock [A-level](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Advanced_Level_(UK) "Advanced Level (UK)") examinations without any preparation along with the upper-sixth students; Stewart was placed first in the examination. He was awarded a scholarship to study at the [University of Cambridge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Cambridge "University of Cambridge") as an undergraduate student of [Churchill College, Cambridge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Churchill_College,_Cambridge "Churchill College, Cambridge"), where he studied the [Mathematical Tripos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_Tripos "Mathematical Tripos") and obtained a first-class [Bachelor of Arts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bachelor_of_Arts "Bachelor of Arts") degree in mathematics in 1966. Stewart then went to the [University of Warwick](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Warwick "University of Warwick") where his [PhD](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PhD "PhD") on [Lie algebras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_algebra "Lie algebra") was supervised by [Brian Hartley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brian_Hartley "Brian Hartley") and completed in 1969.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ian_Stewart_(mathematician)#cite_note-stewphd-5)

Career and research
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ian_Stewart_(mathematician)&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Career and research")]

After his PhD, Stewart was offered an academic position at Warwick. He is well known for his popular expositions of mathematics and his contributions to [catastrophe theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catastrophe_theory "Catastrophe theory").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ian_Stewart_(mathematician)#cite_note-6)

While at Warwick, Stewart edited the mathematical magazine _[Manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manifold\_(magazine) "Manifold (magazine)")_.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ian_Stewart_(mathematician)#cite_note-7) He also wrote a column called "Mathematical Recreations" for _[Scientific American](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific\_American "Scientific American")_ magazine from 1991 to 2001. This followed the work of past columnists like [Martin Gardner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Martin_Gardner "Martin Gardner"), [Douglas Hofstadter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Douglas_Hofstadter "Douglas Hofstadter"), and [A. K. Dewdney](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A._K._Dewdney "A. K. Dewdney"). Altogether, he wrote 96 columns for _Scientific American_, which were later reprinted in the books "Math Hysteria", "How to Cut a Cake: And Other Mathematical Conundrums" and "Cows in the Maze".

Stewart has held visiting academic positions in Germany (1974), New Zealand (1976), and the US ([University of Connecticut](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Connecticut "University of Connecticut") 1977–78, [University of Houston](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Houston "University of Houston") 1983–84).

Stewart has published more than 140 [scientific papers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Academic_publishing "Academic publishing"), including a series of influential papers co-authored with [Jim Collins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Collins_(bioengineer) "James Collins (bioengineer)") on [coupled oscillators and the symmetry of animal gaits](https://web.archive.org/web/20080106085632/http://www.bu.edu/abl/publications_2.html#cno).[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ian_Stewart_(mathematician)#cite_note-scopus-4)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ian_Stewart_(mathematician)#cite_note-8)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ian_Stewart_(mathematician)#cite_note-9)[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ian_Stewart_(mathematician)#cite_note-10)[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ian_Stewart_(mathematician)#cite_note-11)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ian_Stewart_(mathematician)#cite_note-12)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ian_Stewart_(mathematician)#cite_note-13)

Stewart has collaborated with [Jack Cohen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jack_Cohen_(biologist) "Jack Cohen (biologist)") and [Terry Pratchett](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terry_Pratchett "Terry Pratchett") on four [popular science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Popular_science "Popular science") books based on Pratchett's _[Discworld](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discworld "Discworld")_. In 1999 [Terry Pratchett](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terry_Pratchett "Terry Pratchett") made both [Jack Cohen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jack_Cohen_(scientist) "Jack Cohen (scientist)") and Professor Ian Stewart "Honorary Wizards of the Unseen University" at the same ceremony at which the University of Warwick gave Terry Pratchett an honorary degree.

In March 2014 Ian Stewart's iPad app, [Incredible Numbers by Professor Ian Stewart](https://www.incrediblenumbersapp.com/), launched in the App Store. The app was produced in partnership with [Profile Books](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Profile_Books "Profile Books") and [Touch Press](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Touch_Press "Touch Press").[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ian_Stewart_(mathematician)#cite_note-14)

In 1995 Stewart received the [Michael Faraday Medal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michael_Faraday_Medal "Michael Faraday Medal") and in 1997 he gave the [Royal Institution Christmas Lecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Institution_Christmas_Lectures "Royal Institution Christmas Lectures") on _The Magical Maze_. He was elected as a [Fellow of the Royal Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Society "Fellow of the Royal Society") in 2001.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ian_Stewart_(mathematician)#cite_note-ww-2) Stewart was the first recipient in 2008 of the [Christopher Zeeman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christopher_Zeeman "Christopher Zeeman") Medal, awarded jointly by the [London Mathematical Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/London_Mathematical_Society "London Mathematical Society") (LMS) and the [Institute of Mathematics and its Applications](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Institute_of_Mathematics_and_its_Applications "Institute of Mathematics and its Applications") (IMA) for his work on promoting mathematics.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ian_Stewart_(mathematician)#cite_note-17)

Stewart married Avril, in 1970.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ian_Stewart_(mathematician)#cite_note-ww-2) They met at a party at a house that Avril was renting while she trained as a nurse. They have two sons.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ian_Stewart_(mathematician)#cite_note-ww-2) He lists his recreations as science fiction, painting, guitar, keeping fish, geology, Egyptology and snorkelling.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ian_Stewart_(mathematician)#cite_note-ww-2)
