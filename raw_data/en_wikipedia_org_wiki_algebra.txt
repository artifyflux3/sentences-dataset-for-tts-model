[Elementary algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementary_algebra "Elementary algebra") studies which values solve equations formed using arithmetical operations.

[![Image 2: Signature of the ring of integers](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Ring_of_integers2.svg/500px-Ring_of_integers2.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ring_of_integers "Ring of integers")

**Algebra** is a branch of [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics") that deals with abstract systems, known as [algebraic structures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_structures "Algebraic structures"), and the manipulation of [expressions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expression_(mathematics) "Expression (mathematics)") within those systems. It is a generalization of [arithmetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic "Arithmetic") that introduces [variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variable_(mathematics) "Variable (mathematics)") and [algebraic operations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_operation "Algebraic operation") other than the standard arithmetic operations, such as [addition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Addition "Addition") and [multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication "Multiplication").

[Elementary algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementary_algebra "Elementary algebra") is the main form of algebra taught in schools. It examines mathematical statements using variables for unspecified values and seeks to determine for which values the statements are true. To do so, it uses different methods of transforming equations to isolate variables. [Linear algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_algebra "Linear algebra") is a closely related field that investigates [linear equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_equation "Linear equation") and combinations of them called _[systems of linear equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/System\_of\_linear\_equations "System of linear equations")_. It provides methods to find the values that solve all equations in the system at the same time, and to study the set of these solutions.

[Abstract algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abstract_algebra "Abstract algebra") studies algebraic structures, which consist of a [set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_(mathematics) "Set (mathematics)") of [mathematical objects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_objects "Mathematical objects") together with one or several [operations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operation_(mathematics) "Operation (mathematics)") defined on that set. It is a generalization of elementary and linear algebra since it allows mathematical objects other than numbers and non-arithmetic operations. It distinguishes between different types of algebraic structures, such as [groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_(mathematics) "Group (mathematics)"), [rings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ring_(mathematics) "Ring (mathematics)"), and [fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)"), based on the number of operations they use and the laws they follow, called [axioms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axioms "Axioms"). [Universal algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universal_algebra "Universal algebra") and [category theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category_theory "Category theory") provide general frameworks to investigate abstract patterns that characterize different classes of algebraic structures.

Algebraic methods were first studied in the [ancient period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_period "Ancient period") to solve specific problems in fields like [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry"). Subsequent mathematicians examined general techniques to solve equations independent of their specific applications. They described equations and their solutions using words and abbreviations until the 16th and 17th centuries when a rigorous symbolic formalism was developed. In the mid-19th century, the scope of algebra broadened beyond a [theory of equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theory_of_equations "Theory of equations") to cover diverse types of algebraic operations and structures. Algebra is relevant to many branches of mathematics, such as geometry, [topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology "Topology"), [number theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_theory "Number theory"), and [calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus "Calculus"), and other fields of inquiry, like [logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logic "Logic") and the [empirical sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empirical_sciences "Empirical sciences").

Definition and etymology
------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Algebra&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Definition and etymology")]

Algebra is the branch of mathematics that studies [algebraic structures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_structure "Algebraic structure") and the [operations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_operations "Algebraic operations") they use.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-1) An algebraic structure is a non-empty [set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_(mathematics) "Set (mathematics)") of [mathematical objects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_object "Mathematical object"), such as the [integers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer"), together with algebraic operations defined on that set, like [addition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Addition "Addition") and [multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication "Multiplication").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-2)[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-4) Algebra explores the laws, general characteristics, and types of algebraic structures. Within certain algebraic structures, it examines the use of [variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variable_(mathematics) "Variable (mathematics)") in [equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_equation "Algebraic equation") and how to manipulate these equations.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-5)[[b]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-9)

Algebra is often understood as a generalization of [arithmetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic "Arithmetic").[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-10) Arithmetic studies operations like addition, [subtraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subtraction "Subtraction"), multiplication, and [division](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_(mathematics) "Division (mathematics)"), in a particular domain of numbers, such as the real numbers.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-auto1-11)[Elementary algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementary_algebra "Elementary algebra") constitutes the first level of abstraction. Like arithmetic, it restricts itself to specific types of numbers and operations. It generalizes these operations by allowing indefinite quantities in the form of variables in addition to numbers.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-12) A higher level of abstraction is found in [abstract algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abstract_algebra "Abstract algebra"), which is not limited to a particular domain and examines algebraic structures such as [groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_(mathematics) "Group (mathematics)") and [rings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ring_(mathematics) "Ring (mathematics)"). It extends beyond typical arithmetic operations by also covering other types of operations.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-13) Universal algebra is still more abstract in that it is not interested in specific algebraic structures but investigates the characteristics of algebraic structures in general.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-14)

The word _algebra_ comes from the title of [al-Khwarizmi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Khwarizmi "Al-Khwarizmi")'s book _[Al-Jabr](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Jabr "Al-Jabr")_.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-15)

The term "algebra" is sometimes used in a more narrow sense to refer only to elementary algebra or only to abstract algebra.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-16) When used as a [countable noun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Countable_noun "Countable noun"), an algebra is [a specific type of algebraic structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra_over_a_field "Algebra over a field") that involves a [vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space") equipped with a certain type of binary operation, a [bilinear map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bilinear_map "Bilinear map").[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-17) Depending on the context, "algebra" can also refer to other algebraic structures, like a [Lie algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_algebra "Lie algebra") or an [associative algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Associative_algebra "Associative algebra").[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-18)

The word _algebra_ comes from the [Arabic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabic "Arabic") term الجبر (_al-jabr_), which originally referred to the surgical treatment of [bonesetting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bonesetting "Bonesetting"). In the 9th century, the term received a mathematical meaning when [Muhammad ibn Musa al-Khwarizmi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muhammad_ibn_Musa_al-Khwarizmi "Muhammad ibn Musa al-Khwarizmi") employed it to name a method for transforming equations and used it in the title of his treatise _[al-Kitāb al-Mukhtaṣar fī Ḥisāb al-Jabr wal-Muqābalah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Jabr "Al-Jabr")_ [_The Compendious Book on Calculation by Completion and Balancing_] which was translated into Latin as _Liber Algebrae et Almucabola_.[[c]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-20) The word entered the English language in the 16th century from [Italian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italian_language "Italian language"), [Spanish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spanish_language "Spanish language"), and medieval [Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin_language "Latin language").[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-21) Initially, its meaning was restricted to the [theory of equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theory_of_equations "Theory of equations"), that is, to the art of manipulating [polynomial equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial_equations "Polynomial equations") in view of solving them. This changed in the 19th century[[d]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-24) when the scope of algebra broadened to cover the study of diverse types of algebraic operations and structures together with their underlying [axioms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiom "Axiom"), the laws they follow.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-25)

Elementary algebra, also called school algebra, college algebra, and classical algebra,[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-26) is the oldest and most basic form of algebra. It is a generalization of [arithmetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic "Arithmetic") that relies on [variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variable_(mathematics) "Variable (mathematics)") and examines how mathematical [statements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statement_(logic) "Statement (logic)") may be transformed.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-auto4-27)

Arithmetic is the study of numerical operations and investigates how numbers are combined and transformed using the arithmetic operations of [addition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Addition "Addition"), [subtraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subtraction "Subtraction"), [multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication "Multiplication"), [division](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_(mathematics) "Division (mathematics)"), [exponentiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponentiation "Exponentiation"), extraction of [roots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nth_root "Nth root"), and [logarithm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logarithm "Logarithm"). For example, the operation of addition combines two numbers, called the addends, into a third number, called the sum, as in ![Image 8: {\displaystyle 2+5=7}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f13c2cc3632e995ba4ba4ebec919767b83cc002a).[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-auto1-11)

Elementary algebra relies on the same operations while allowing variables in addition to regular numbers. Variables are [symbols](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symbol "Symbol") for unspecified or unknown quantities. They make it possible to state relationships for which one does not know the exact values and to express general laws that are true, independent of which numbers are used. For example, the [equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equation "Equation")![Image 9: {\displaystyle 2\times 3=3\times 2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/161563545fb628ffd4a0e1fb5b7751ee29c68dc3) belongs to arithmetic and expresses an equality only for these specific numbers. By replacing the numbers with variables, it is possible to express a general law that applies to any possible combination of numbers, like the [commutative property of multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_property "Commutative property"), which is expressed in the equation ![Image 10: {\displaystyle a\times b=b\times a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/28d66fd09c6072e183e3395a174fd66dac99e514).[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-auto4-27)

[Algebraic expressions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_expression "Algebraic expression") are formed by using arithmetic operations to combine variables and numbers. By convention, the lowercase letters ⁠![Image 11: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4)⁠, ⁠![Image 12: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d)⁠, and ![Image 13: {\displaystyle z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf368e72c009decd9b6686ee84a375632e11de98) represent variables. In some cases, subscripts are added to distinguish variables, as in ⁠![Image 14: {\displaystyle x_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a8788bf85d532fa88d1fb25eff6ae382a601c308)⁠, ⁠![Image 15: {\displaystyle x_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d7af1b928f06e4c7e3e8ebfd60704656719bd766)⁠, and ⁠![Image 16: {\displaystyle x_{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/766d09a498699be10e276ad49145c921f8cbe335)⁠. The lowercase letters ⁠![Image 17: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc)⁠, ⁠![Image 18: {\displaystyle b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f11423fbb2e967f986e36804a8ae4271734917c3)⁠, and ![Image 19: {\displaystyle c}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/86a67b81c2de995bd608d5b2df50cd8cd7d92455) are usually used for [constants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constant_(mathematics) "Constant (mathematics)") and [coefficients](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coefficient "Coefficient").[[e]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-29) The expression ![Image 20: {\displaystyle 5x+3}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4f64f5f8e2e9d8602914304c7670bf842f85cfa6) is an algebraic expression created by multiplying the number 5 with the variable ![Image 21: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) and adding the number 3 to the result. Other examples of algebraic expressions are ![Image 22: {\displaystyle 32xyz}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dac6f9148904415002f9fc4a9649bdf737247f89) and ![Image 23: {\displaystyle 64x_{1}{}^{2}+7x_{2}-c}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fcb9629163efe438ab11c0c90334dff1190383f7).[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-30)

Some algebraic expressions take the form of statements that relate two expressions to one another. An equation is a statement formed by comparing two expressions, saying that they are equal. This can be expressed using the [equals sign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equals_sign "Equals sign") (⁠![Image 24: {\displaystyle =}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/505a4ceef454c69dffd23792c84b90f488543743)⁠), as in ⁠![Image 25: {\displaystyle 5x^{2}+6x=3y+4}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bedb5ef61b618f10dc5b6c38be1bfc726e8072f3)⁠. [Inequations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inequation "Inequation") involve a different type of comparison, saying that the two sides are different. This can be expressed using symbols such as the [less-than sign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Less-than_sign "Less-than sign") (⁠![Image 26: {\displaystyle <}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/33737c89a17785dacc8638b4d66db3d5c8670de1)⁠), the [greater-than sign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greater-than_sign "Greater-than sign") (⁠![Image 27: {\displaystyle >}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1b27b77ab4e3293ea9ce65cef60fea655c398423)⁠), and the inequality sign (⁠![Image 28: {\displaystyle \neq }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38cc3d8d8c60120bc2f905bae4d5e10d8ad6a3f4)⁠). Unlike other expressions, statements can be true or false, and their [truth value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Truth_value "Truth value") usually depends on the values of the variables. For example, the statement ![Image 29: {\displaystyle x^{2}=4}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/217ecaac737ee742f39cfaf66414e50119ec712f) is true if ![Image 30: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) is either 2 or −2 and false otherwise.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-31) Equations with variables can be divided into identity equations and conditional equations. Identity equations are true for all values that can be assigned to the variables, such as the equation ⁠![Image 31: {\displaystyle 2x+5x=7x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5e91adf3e22ef3ad426194f4d6a2b79aff273035)⁠. Conditional equations are only true for some values. For example, the equation ![Image 32: {\displaystyle x+4=9}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cac1c021412c4579727f8039314ec06bcd7fadfd) is only true if ![Image 33: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) is 5.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-32)

The main goal of elementary algebra is to determine the values for which a statement is true. This can be achieved by transforming and manipulating statements according to certain rules. A key principle guiding this process is that whatever operation is applied to one side of an equation also needs to be done to the other side. For example, if one subtracts 5 from the left side of an equation one also needs to subtract 5 from the right side to balance both sides. The goal of these steps is usually to isolate the variable one is interested in on one side, a process known as [solving the equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equation_solving "Equation solving") for that variable. For example, the equation ![Image 34: {\displaystyle x-7=4}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c749c532083aa735fcc55b9d4252140e1aaf58db) can be solved for ![Image 35: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) by adding 7 to both sides, which isolates ![Image 36: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) on the left side and results in the equation ⁠![Image 37: {\displaystyle x=11}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3c8b5e3ad3ed1ab128662a082f5bfb1ec94d3a2)⁠.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-33)

There are many other techniques used to solve equations. Simplification is employed to replace a complicated expression with an equivalent simpler one. For example, the expression ![Image 38: {\displaystyle 7x-3x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/57daea75d655cda833baf17f5dd2eff6910b26af) can be replaced with the expression ![Image 39: {\displaystyle 4x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/51fb5b15dfeeb171680fea02842c438db9395293) since ![Image 40: {\displaystyle 7x-3x=(7-3)x=4x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3e9ab4ea9e1826526805223484189b6f9e76fc90) by the distributive property.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-34) For statements with several variables, [substitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Substitution_(logic)#Algebra "Substitution (logic)") is a common technique to replace one variable with an equivalent expression that does not use this variable. For example, if one knows that ![Image 41: {\displaystyle y=3x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e95f1e0e2231c78102d73ef63385daa4b52c299e) then one can simplify the expression ![Image 42: {\displaystyle 7xy}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a51b6c57790d5e72a0730a9691ea110d5524f2d4) to arrive at ⁠![Image 43: {\displaystyle 21x^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0aa0f4ef9da8e29e1c1a954ce090b307a72565fd)⁠. In a similar way, if one knows the value of one variable one may be able to use it to determine the value of other variables.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-35)

Algebraic equations can be used to describe geometric figures. All values for ![Image 45: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) and ![Image 46: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d) that solve the equation are interpreted as points. They are drawn as a red, upward-sloping line in the graph above.

Algebraic equations can be interpreted [geometrically](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry") to describe spatial figures in the form of a [graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_of_a_function "Graph of a function"). To do so, the different variables in the equation are understood as [coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system "Cartesian coordinate system") and the values that solve the equation are interpreted as points of a graph. For example, if ![Image 47: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) is set to zero in the equation ⁠![Image 48: {\displaystyle y=0.5x-1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a7681cafe51fb336fd11349ce8eb821fe961b08d)⁠, then ![Image 49: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d) must be −1 for the equation to be true. This means that the ![Image 50: {\displaystyle (x,y)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/41cf50e4a314ca8e2c30964baa8d26e5be7a9386)-pair ![Image 51: {\displaystyle (0,-1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6982e7eb515f8a46400ff23c38a6f1430ae97278) is part of the graph of the equation. The ![Image 52: {\displaystyle (x,y)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/41cf50e4a314ca8e2c30964baa8d26e5be7a9386)-pair ⁠![Image 53: {\displaystyle (0,7)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cb31d15b53f2a53b8e297ef966df084d8fd55e12)⁠, by contrast, does not solve the equation and is therefore not part of the graph. The graph encompasses the totality of ![Image 54: {\displaystyle (x,y)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/41cf50e4a314ca8e2c30964baa8d26e5be7a9386)-pairs that solve the equation.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-36)

A polynomial is an expression consisting of one or more terms that are added or subtracted from each other, like ⁠![Image 55: {\displaystyle x^{4}+3xy^{2}+5x^{3}-1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf3d77f5ee871500a56d5e004ed302daae7e38c5)⁠. Each term is either a constant, a variable, or a product of a constant and variables. Each variable can be raised to a positive integer power. A monomial is a polynomial with one term while two- and three-term polynomials are called binomials and trinomials. The [degree of a polynomial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_of_a_polynomial "Degree of a polynomial") is the maximal value (among its terms) of the sum of the exponents of the variables (4 in the above example).[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-37) Polynomials of degree one are called _linear polynomials_. Linear algebra studies systems of linear polynomials.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-38) A polynomial is said to be _univariate_ or _multivariate_, depending on whether it uses one or more variables.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-39)

[Factorization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Factorization "Factorization") is a method used to simplify polynomials, making it easier to analyze them and determine the values for which they [evaluate to zero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero_of_a_function "Zero of a function"). Factorization consists of rewriting a polynomial as a product of several factors. For example, the polynomial ![Image 56: {\displaystyle x^{2}-3x-10}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5952ebf42559b584ec2aeb737cc7928fb198b18b) can be factorized as ⁠![Image 57: {\displaystyle (x+2)(x-5)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1dcef24e007fe5b6f6cbdf6600f8a8068c6e3621)⁠. The polynomial as a whole is zero if and only if one of its factors is zero, i.e., if ![Image 58: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) is either −2 or 5.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-40) Before the 19th century, much of algebra was devoted to [polynomial equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial_equation "Polynomial equation"), that is [equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equation "Equation") obtained by equating a polynomial to zero. The first attempts for solving polynomial equations were to express the solutions in terms of [n th roots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nth_root "Nth root"). The solution of a second-degree polynomial equation of the form ![Image 59: {\displaystyle ax^{2}+bx+c=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/23e70cfa003f402d108ec04d97983fb62f69536e) is given by the [quadratic formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadratic_formula "Quadratic formula")[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-41)![Image 60: {\displaystyle x={\frac {-b\pm {\sqrt {b^{2}-4ac\ }}}{2a}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/42efe5c6f302eb75186b3d5b782129850bfa04d1)

Solutions for the degrees 3 and 4 are given by the [cubic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cubic_formula "Cubic formula") and [quartic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quartic_formula "Quartic formula") formulas. There are no general solutions for higher degrees, as proven in the 19th century by the [Abel–Ruffini theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abel%E2%80%93Ruffini_theorem "Abel–Ruffini theorem").[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-auto3-42) Even when general solutions do not exist, approximate solutions can be found by numerical tools like the [Newton–Raphson method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%E2%80%93Raphson_method "Newton–Raphson method").[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-43)

The [fundamental theorem of algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_theorem_of_algebra "Fundamental theorem of algebra") asserts that every univariate polynomial equation of positive degree with [real](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number") or [complex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number") coefficients has at least one complex solution. Consequently, every polynomial of a positive degree can be [factorized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial_factorization "Polynomial factorization") into linear polynomials. This theorem was proved at the beginning of the 19th century, but this does not close the problem since the theorem does not provide any way for computing the solutions.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-44)

Linear algebra starts with the study of [systems of linear equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/System_of_linear_equations "System of linear equations").[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-45) An [equation is linear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_equation "Linear equation") if it can be expressed in the form ⁠![Image 61: {\displaystyle a_{1}x_{1}+a_{2}x_{2}+...+a_{n}x_{n}=b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b08bdb66eac99b3fa88db149b594aa9e76ae45bf)⁠, where ⁠![Image 62: {\displaystyle a_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bbf42ecda092975c9c69dae84e16182ba5fe2e07)⁠, ⁠![Image 63: {\displaystyle a_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/270580da7333505d9b73697417d0543c43c98b9f)⁠, ..., ![Image 64: {\displaystyle a_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/790f9209748c2dca7ed7b81932c37c02af1dbc31) and ![Image 65: {\displaystyle b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f11423fbb2e967f986e36804a8ae4271734917c3) are constants. Examples are ![Image 66: {\displaystyle x_{1}-7x_{2}+3x_{3}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/334db36e327f02fb06b1617244583008980e0889) and ⁠![Image 67: {\displaystyle \textstyle {\frac {1}{4}}x-y=4}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a728e70efa7722362122c5ebaea3a6662baa9a74)⁠. A _system of linear equations_ is a set of linear equations for which one is interested in common solutions.[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-46)

Under some conditions on the number of rows and columns, matrices can be [added](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_addition "Matrix addition"), [multiplied](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_multiplication "Matrix multiplication"), and sometimes [inverted](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverse_matrix "Inverse matrix"). Then, multiplying on the left both members of the above matrix equation by ![Image 77: {\displaystyle A^{-1},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cc5a7c51eddc55dce006ed7b1368fef589afb93c) one gets the solution of the system of linear equations as[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-48)![Image 78: {\displaystyle X=A^{-1}B.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eceaa1061d7b23d12de2e1a67b2f9006c689b0ce)

Methods of solving systems of linear equations range from the introductory, like substitution[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-49) and elimination,[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-50) to more advanced techniques using matrices, such as [Cramer's rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cramer%27s_rule "Cramer's rule"), the [Gaussian elimination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaussian_elimination "Gaussian elimination"), and [LU decomposition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LU_decomposition "LU decomposition").[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-51) Some systems of equations are [inconsistent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Consistent_and_inconsistent_equations "Consistent and inconsistent equations"), meaning that no solutions exist because the equations contradict each other.[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-auto10-52)[[f]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-53) Consistent systems have either one unique solution or an infinite number of solutions.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-auto11-54)[[g]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-55)

The study of [vector spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space") and [linear maps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_map "Linear map") form a large part of linear algebra. A vector space is an algebraic structure formed by a set with an addition that makes it an [abelian group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abelian_group "Abelian group") and a [scalar multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_multiplication "Scalar multiplication") that is compatible with addition (see [vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space") for details). A linear map is a function between vector spaces that is compatible with addition and scalar multiplication. In the case of [finite-dimensional vector spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite-dimensional_vector_space "Finite-dimensional vector space"), vectors and linear maps can be represented by matrices. It follows that the theories of matrices and finite-dimensional vector spaces are essentially the same. In particular, vector spaces provide a third way for expressing and manipulating systems of linear equations.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-56) From this perspective, a matrix is a representation of a linear map: if one chooses a particular [basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basis_(linear_algebra) "Basis (linear algebra)") to describe the vectors being transformed, then the entries in the matrix give the results of applying the linear map to the basis vectors.[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-57)

[![Image 79: Graph of two linear equations](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Linear_Function_Graph.svg/250px-Linear_Function_Graph.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Linear_Function_Graph.svg)

Linear equations with two variables can be interpreted geometrically as lines. The solution of a system of linear equations is where the lines intersect.

Systems of equations can be interpreted as geometric figures. For systems with two variables, each equation represents a [line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_(geometry) "Line (geometry)") in [two-dimensional space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two-dimensional_space "Two-dimensional space"). The point where the two lines intersect is the solution of the full system because this is the only point that solves both the first and the second equation. For inconsistent systems, the two lines run parallel, meaning that there is no solution since they never intersect. If two equations are not independent then they describe the same line, meaning that every solution of one equation is also a solution of the other equation. These relations make it possible to seek solutions graphically by plotting the equations and determining where they intersect.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-58) The same principles also apply to systems of equations with more variables, with the difference being that the equations do not describe lines but higher dimensional figures. For instance, equations with three variables correspond to [planes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_(mathematics) "Plane (mathematics)") in [three-dimensional space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_space "Three-dimensional space"), and the points where all planes intersect solve the system of equations.[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-59)

Abstract algebra, also called modern algebra,[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-60) is the study of [algebraic structures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_structures "Algebraic structures"). An algebraic structure is a framework for understanding [operations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operation_(mathematics) "Operation (mathematics)") on [mathematical objects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_object "Mathematical object"), like the addition of numbers. While elementary algebra and linear algebra work within the confines of particular algebraic structures, abstract algebra takes a more general approach that compares how algebraic structures differ from each other and what types of algebraic structures there are, such as [groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_(mathematics) "Group (mathematics)"), [rings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ring_(mathematics) "Ring (mathematics)"), and [fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)").[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-61) The key difference between these types of algebraic structures lies in the number of operations they use and the laws they obey.[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-auto9-62) In [mathematics education](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics_education "Mathematics education"), abstract algebra refers to an advanced [undergraduate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Undergraduate "Undergraduate") course that mathematics majors take after completing courses in linear algebra.[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-63)

[![Image 80: Diagram of binary operation](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Binary_operations_as_black_box.svg/250px-Binary_operations_as_black_box.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Binary_operations_as_black_box.svg)

Many algebraic structures rely on binary operations, which take two objects as their input and combine them into a single object as output, like addition and multiplication do.

On a formal level, an algebraic structure is a [set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_(mathematics) "Set (mathematics)")[[h]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-65) of mathematical objects, called the underlying set, together with one or several operations.[[i]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-67) Abstract algebra is primarily interested in [binary operations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_operation "Binary operation"),[[j]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-69) which take any two objects from the underlying set as inputs and map them to another object from this set as output.[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-70) For example, the algebraic structure ![Image 81: {\displaystyle \langle \mathbb {N} ,+\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b9b93f09e875ebb3a3f933f312396e249ee83cd) has the [natural numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_numbers "Natural numbers") (⁠![Image 82: {\displaystyle \mathbb {N} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fdf9a96b565ea202d0f4322e9195613fb26a9bed)⁠) as the underlying set and addition (⁠![Image 83: {\displaystyle +}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fe6ef363cd19902d1a7a71fb1c8b21e8ede52406)⁠) as its binary operation.[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-auto5-66) The underlying set can contain mathematical objects other than numbers, and the operations are not restricted to regular arithmetic operations.[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-71) For instance, the underlying set of the [symmetry group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry_group "Symmetry group") of a geometric object is made up of [geometric transformations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_transformation "Geometric transformation"), such as [rotations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation "Rotation"), under which the object remains [unchanged](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invariant_(mathematics) "Invariant (mathematics)"). Its binary operation is [function composition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_composition "Function composition"), which takes two transformations as input and has the transformation resulting from applying the first transformation followed by the second as its output.[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-72)

Abstract algebra classifies algebraic structures based on the laws or [axioms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiom "Axiom") that its operations obey and the number of operations it uses. One of the most basic types is a group, which has one operation and requires that this operation is [associative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Associative_property "Associative property") and has an [identity element](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Identity_element "Identity element") and [inverse elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverse_element "Inverse element"). An operation is associative if the order of several applications does not matter, i.e., if ![Image 84: {\displaystyle (a\circ b)\circ c}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/383dce17a5f966c20847f569d8572793651a37a5)[[k]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-74) is the same as ![Image 85: {\displaystyle a\circ (b\circ c)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e5e7227ec4d1e320dfd5d17efba38026d62d66d0) for all elements. An operation has an identity element or a neutral element if one element _e_ exists that does not change the value of any other element, i.e., if ⁠![Image 86: {\displaystyle a\circ e=e\circ a=a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ca3d48275016791a29668e36acd76f3fad262a14)⁠. An operation has inverse elements if for any element ![Image 87: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc) there exists a reciprocal element ![Image 88: {\displaystyle a^{-1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3f5709c8d86f7fec8fb86069bf5d15a9eabe564e) that undoes ⁠![Image 89: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc)⁠. If an element operates on its inverse then the result is the neutral element _e_, expressed formally as ⁠![Image 90: {\displaystyle a\circ a^{-1}=a^{-1}\circ a=e}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2eb407bf45d58acfe1ae9d433e9a762f53b1caf0)⁠. Every algebraic structure that fulfills these requirements is a group.[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-75) For example, ![Image 91: {\displaystyle \langle \mathbb {Z} ,+\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/325e5d3ba782da8b52e703f50b7ba480d5ab2c0f) is a group formed by the set of [integers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integers "Integers") together with the operation of addition. The neutral element is 0 and the inverse element of any number ![Image 92: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc) is ![Image 93: {\displaystyle -a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e0982b5868a66be1ed3ad7ef4bcd3d3db20f982).[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-76) The natural numbers with addition, by contrast, do not form a group since they contain only positive integers and therefore lack inverse elements.[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-77)

[Group theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_theory "Group theory") examines the nature of groups, with basic theorems such as the [fundamental theorem of finite abelian groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_theorem_of_finite_abelian_groups "Fundamental theorem of finite abelian groups") and the [Feit–Thompson theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feit%E2%80%93Thompson_theorem "Feit–Thompson theorem").[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-78) The latter was a key early step in one of the most important mathematical achievements of the 20th century: the collaborative effort, taking up more than 10,000 journal pages and mostly published between 1960 and 2004, that culminated in a complete [classification of finite simple groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classification_of_finite_simple_groups "Classification of finite simple groups").[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-79)

#### Ring theory and field theory

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Algebra&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Ring theory and field theory")]

A ring is an algebraic structure with two operations that work similarly to the addition and multiplication of numbers and are named and generally denoted similarly. A ring is a [commutative group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_group "Commutative group") under addition: the addition of the ring is associative, commutative, and has an identity element and inverse elements. The multiplication is associative and [distributive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distributivity "Distributivity") with respect to addition; that is, ![Image 94: {\displaystyle a(b+c)=ab+ac}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5aad41bae613e8522d4d598bcf9f52772f472c55) and ![Image 95: {\displaystyle (b+c)a=ba+ca.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cd2ca7ec5fba0da60f5bba7784d35582a6866de3) Moreover, multiplication is associative and has an [identity element](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Identity_element "Identity element") generally denoted as 1.[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-80)[[l]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-82) Multiplication needs not to be commutative; if it is commutative, one has a [commutative ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_ring "Commutative ring").[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-83) The [ring of integers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ring_of_integers "Ring of integers") (⁠![Image 96: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/449494a083e0a1fda2b61c62b2f09b6bee4633dc)⁠) is one of the simplest commutative rings.[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-84)

A _[field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field\_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)")_ is a commutative ring such that ⁠![Image 97: {\displaystyle 1\neq 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9bee82995e4ac724a7b5fdfb4b0d76560321e1d4)⁠[[m]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-86) and each nonzero element has a [multiplicative inverse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplicative_inverse "Multiplicative inverse").[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-87) The ring of integers does not form a field because it lacks multiplicative inverses. For example, the multiplicative inverse of ![Image 98: {\displaystyle 7}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ee716ec61382a6b795092c0edd859d12e64cbba8) is ⁠![Image 99: {\displaystyle {\tfrac {1}{7}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/afcab3a9c6842020657bf281789284c1a05b1ff8)⁠, which is not an integer. The [rational numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_numbers "Rational numbers"), the [real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_numbers "Real numbers"), and the [complex numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_numbers "Complex numbers") each form a field with the operations of addition and multiplication.[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-88)

[Ring theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ring_theory "Ring theory") is the study of rings, exploring concepts such as [subrings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subring "Subring"), [quotient rings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quotient_ring "Quotient ring"), [polynomial rings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial_ring "Polynomial ring"), and [ideals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ideal_(ring_theory) "Ideal (ring theory)") as well as theorems such as [Hilbert's basis theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert%27s_basis_theorem "Hilbert's basis theorem").[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-89) Field theory is concerned with fields, examining [field extensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_extension "Field extension"), [algebraic closures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_closure "Algebraic closure"), and [finite fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_fields "Finite fields").[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-90)[Galois theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galois_theory "Galois theory") explores the relation between field theory and group theory, relying on the [fundamental theorem of Galois theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_theorem_of_Galois_theory "Fundamental theorem of Galois theory").[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-91)

#### Theories of interrelations among structures

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Algebra&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Theories of interrelations among structures")]

[![Image 100: Diagram of relations between some algebraic structures](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Magma_to_group4.svg/250px-Magma_to_group4.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Magma_to_group4.svg)

Diagram of relations between some algebraic structures. For instance, its top right section shows that a [magma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magma_(algebra) "Magma (algebra)") becomes a [semigroup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semigroup "Semigroup") if its operation is associative.

Besides groups, rings, and fields, there are many other algebraic structures studied by algebra. They include [magmas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magma_(algebra) "Magma (algebra)"), [semigroups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semigroup "Semigroup"), [monoids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monoid "Monoid"), [abelian groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abelian_group "Abelian group"), [commutative rings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_ring "Commutative ring"), [modules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Module_(mathematics) "Module (mathematics)"), [lattices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lattice_(order) "Lattice (order)"), [vector spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space"), [algebras over a field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra_over_a_field "Algebra over a field"), and [associative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Associative "Associative") and [non-associative algebras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-associative_algebras "Non-associative algebras"). They differ from each other regarding the types of objects they describe and the requirements that their operations fulfill. Many are related to each other in that a basic structure can be turned into a more specialized structure by adding constraints.[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-auto9-62) For example, a magma becomes a semigroup if its operation is associative.[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-92)

[Homomorphisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homomorphism "Homomorphism") are tools to examine structural features by comparing two algebraic structures.[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-93) A homomorphism is a function from the underlying set of one algebraic structure to the underlying set of another algebraic structure that preserves certain structural characteristics. If the two algebraic structures use binary operations and have the form ![Image 101: {\displaystyle \langle A,\circ \rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5aae9852f755f997ff72ce293a16284f6cb37ccd) and ![Image 102: {\displaystyle \langle B,\star \rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87a95ecdc623a905bd9db20a2aa5f51f98baf9fc) then the function ![Image 103: {\displaystyle h:A\to B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d387dab07e2b7052cf00981f5943a73c940c2e0b) is a homomorphism if it fulfills the following requirement: ⁠![Image 104: {\displaystyle h(x\circ y)=h(x)\star h(y)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fe47e649eb7c6aa14d73a4a4947401bcf5b46691)⁠. The existence of a homomorphism reveals that the operation ![Image 105: {\displaystyle \star }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bd316a21eeb5079a850f223b1d096a06bfa788c0) in the second algebraic structure plays the same role as the operation ![Image 106: {\displaystyle \circ }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/99add39d2b681e2de7ff62422c32704a05c7ec31) does in the first algebraic structure.[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-94)[Isomorphisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isomorphisms "Isomorphisms") are a special type of homomorphism that indicates a high degree of similarity between two algebraic structures. An isomorphism is a [bijective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bijective "Bijective") homomorphism, meaning that it establishes a one-to-one relationship between the elements of the two algebraic structures. This implies that every element of the first algebraic structure is mapped to one unique element in the second structure without any unmapped elements in the second structure.[[82]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-95)

[![Image 107: Venn diagram of a set and its subset](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Venn_A_subset_B.svg/250px-Venn_A_subset_B.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Venn_A_subset_B.svg)

[Subalgebras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subalgebra "Subalgebra") restrict their operations to a subset of the underlying set of the original algebraic structure.

Another tool of comparison is the relation between an algebraic structure and its [subalgebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subalgebra#Subalgebras_in_universal_algebra "Subalgebra").[[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-auto8-96) The algebraic structure and its subalgebra use the same operations,[[n]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-98) which follow the same axioms. The only difference is that the underlying set of the subalgebra is a subset of the underlying set of the algebraic structure.[[o]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-100) All operations in the subalgebra are required to be [closed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closure_(mathematics) "Closure (mathematics)") in its underlying set, meaning that they only produce elements that belong to this set.[[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-auto8-96) For example, the set of [even integers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parity_(mathematics) "Parity (mathematics)") together with addition is a subalgebra of the full set of integers together with addition. This is the case because the sum of two even numbers is again an even number. But the set of odd integers together with addition is not a subalgebra because it is not closed: adding two odd numbers produces an even number, which is not part of the chosen subset.[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-auto6-97)

[Universal algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universal_algebra "Universal algebra") is the study of algebraic structures in general. As part of its general perspective, it is not concerned with the specific elements that make up the underlying sets and considers operations with more than two inputs, such as [ternary operations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ternary_operation "Ternary operation"). It provides a framework for investigating what structural features different algebraic structures have in common.[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-101)[[p]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-103) One of those structural features concerns the [identities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Identity_(mathematics)#Logic_and_universal_algebra "Identity (mathematics)") that are true in different algebraic structures. In this context, an identity is a [universal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universal_quantification "Universal quantification") equation or an equation that is true for all elements of the underlying set. For example, commutativity is a universal equation that states that ![Image 108: {\displaystyle a\circ b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6f270b24a930a6546b42f355ad905d2d7a26d4b3) is identical to ![Image 109: {\displaystyle b\circ a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c12766887e6a74b8b91e2df5be1132f75df723bf) for all elements.[[88]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-104) A [variety](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variety_(universal_algebra) "Variety (universal algebra)") is a class of all algebraic structures that satisfy certain identities. For example, if two algebraic structures satisfy commutativity then they are both part of the corresponding variety.[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-105)[[q]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-107)[[r]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-110)

[Category theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category_theory "Category theory") examines how mathematical objects are related to each other using the concept of [categories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category_(mathematics) "Category (mathematics)"). A category is a collection of objects together with a collection of [morphisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morphisms "Morphisms") or "arrows" between those objects. These two collections must satisfy certain conditions. For example, morphisms can be joined, or _composed_: if there exists a morphism from object ![Image 110: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc) to object ⁠![Image 111: {\displaystyle b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f11423fbb2e967f986e36804a8ae4271734917c3)⁠, and another morphism from object ![Image 112: {\displaystyle b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f11423fbb2e967f986e36804a8ae4271734917c3) to object ⁠![Image 113: {\displaystyle c}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/86a67b81c2de995bd608d5b2df50cd8cd7d92455)⁠, then there must also exist one from object ![Image 114: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc) to object ⁠![Image 115: {\displaystyle c}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/86a67b81c2de995bd608d5b2df50cd8cd7d92455)⁠. Composition of morphisms is required to be associative, and there must be an "identity morphism" for every object.[[93]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-111) Categories are widely used in contemporary mathematics since they provide a unifying framework to describe and analyze many fundamental mathematical concepts. For example, sets can be described with the [category of sets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category_of_sets "Category of sets"), and any group can be regarded as the morphisms of a category with just one object.[[94]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-112)

[![Image 116: Rhind Papyrus](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Rhind_Mathematical_Papyrus.jpg/250px-Rhind_Mathematical_Papyrus.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Rhind_Mathematical_Papyrus.jpg)

The [Rhind Mathematical Papyrus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhind_Mathematical_Papyrus "Rhind Mathematical Papyrus") from [ancient Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Egypt "Ancient Egypt"), dated c. 1650 BCE, is one of the earliest documents discussing algebraic problems.

The origin of algebra lies in attempts to solve mathematical problems involving arithmetic calculations and unknown quantities. These developments happened in the ancient period in [Babylonia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Babylonia "Babylonia"), [Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Egypt "Ancient Egypt"), [Greece](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greece "Ancient Greece"), [China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_China "Ancient China"), and [India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_India "Ancient India"). One of the earliest documents on algebraic problems is the [Rhind Mathematical Papyrus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhind_Mathematical_Papyrus "Rhind Mathematical Papyrus") from ancient Egypt, which was written around 1650 BCE.[[s]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-114) It discusses solutions to [linear equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_equations "Linear equations"), as expressed in problems like "A quantity; its fourth is added to it. It becomes fifteen. What is the quantity?" Babylonian clay tablets from around the same time explain methods to solve linear and [quadratic polynomial equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadratic_equation "Quadratic equation"), such as the method of [completing the square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Completing_the_square "Completing the square").[[96]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-115)

Many of these insights found their way to the ancient Greeks. Starting in the 6th century BCE, their main interest was geometry rather than algebra, but they employed algebraic methods to solve geometric problems. For example, they studied geometric figures while taking their lengths and areas as unknown quantities to be determined, as exemplified in [Pythagoras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras "Pythagoras")' formulation of the [difference of two squares](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Difference_of_two_squares "Difference of two squares") method and later in [Euclid's _Elements_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid%27s_Elements "Euclid's Elements").[[97]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-116) In the 3rd century CE, [Diophantus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diophantus "Diophantus") provided a detailed treatment of how to solve algebraic equations in a series of books called _[Arithmetica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetica "Arithmetica")_. He was the first to experiment with symbolic notation to express polynomials.[[98]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-117) Diophantus's work influenced Arab development of algebra with many of his methods reflected in the concepts and techniques used in medieval Arabic algebra.[[99]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-118) In ancient China, _[The Nine Chapters on the Mathematical Art](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The\_Nine\_Chapters\_on\_the\_Mathematical\_Art "The Nine Chapters on the Mathematical Art")_, a book composed over the period spanning from the 10th century BCE to the 2nd century CE,[[100]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-119) explored various techniques for solving algebraic equations, including the use of matrix-like constructs.[[101]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-120)

There is no unanimity of opinion as to whether these early developments are part of algebra or only precursors. They offered solutions to algebraic problems but did not conceive them in an abstract and general manner, focusing instead on specific cases and applications.[[102]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-121) This changed with the Persian mathematician [al-Khwarizmi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Khwarizmi "Al-Khwarizmi"),[[t]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-123) who published his _[The Compendious Book on Calculation by Completion and Balancing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The\_Compendious\_Book\_on\_Calculation\_by\_Completion\_and\_Balancing "The Compendious Book on Calculation by Completion and Balancing")_ in 825 CE. It presents the first detailed treatment of general methods that can be used to manipulate linear and quadratic equations by "reducing" and "balancing" both sides.[[104]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-124) Other influential contributions to algebra came from the Arab mathematician [Thābit ibn Qurra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Th%C4%81bit_ibn_Qurra "Thābit ibn Qurra") also in the 9th century and the Persian mathematician [Omar Khayyam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Omar_Khayyam "Omar Khayyam") in the 11th and 12th centuries.[[105]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-125)

In India, [Brahmagupta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brahmagupta "Brahmagupta") investigated how to solve quadratic equations and systems of equations with several variables in the 7th century CE. Among his innovations were the use of zero and negative numbers in algebraic equations.[[106]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-126) The Indian mathematicians [Mahāvīra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mah%C4%81v%C4%ABra_(mathematician) "Mahāvīra (mathematician)") in the 9th century and [Bhāskara II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bh%C4%81skara_II "Bhāskara II") in the 12th century further refined Brahmagupta's methods and concepts.[[107]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-127) In 1247, the Chinese mathematician [Qin Jiushao](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qin_Jiushao "Qin Jiushao") wrote the _[Mathematical Treatise in Nine Sections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical\_Treatise\_in\_Nine\_Sections "Mathematical Treatise in Nine Sections")_, which includes [an algorithm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horner%27s_method "Horner's method") for the [numerical evaluation of polynomials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial_evaluation "Polynomial evaluation"), including polynomials of higher degrees.[[108]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-128)

[![Image 117: Drawing of François Viète](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Francois_Viete.jpeg/250px-Francois_Viete.jpeg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Francois_Viete.jpeg)

[![Image 118: Painting of René Descartes](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Frans_Hals_-_Portret_van_Ren%C3%A9_Descartes_%28cropped%29.jpg/250px-Frans_Hals_-_Portret_van_Ren%C3%A9_Descartes_%28cropped%29.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Frans_Hals_-_Portret_van_Ren%C3%A9_Descartes_(cropped).jpg)

The Italian mathematician [Fibonacci](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fibonacci "Fibonacci") brought al-Khwarizmi's ideas and techniques to Europe in books including his _[Liber Abaci](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liber\_Abaci "Liber Abaci")_.[[109]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-129) In 1545, the Italian polymath [Gerolamo Cardano](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gerolamo_Cardano "Gerolamo Cardano") published his book _[Ars Magna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ars\_Magna\_(Cardano\_book) "Ars Magna (Cardano book)")_, which covered many topics in algebra, discussed [imaginary numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imaginary_numbers "Imaginary numbers"), and was the first to present general methods for solving [cubic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cubic_equation "Cubic equation") and [quartic equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quartic_equation "Quartic equation").[[110]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-130) In the 16th and 17th centuries, the French mathematicians [François Viète](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Vi%C3%A8te "François Viète") and [René Descartes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Descartes "René Descartes") introduced letters and symbols to denote variables and operations, making it possible to express equations in an concise and abstract manner. Their predecessors had relied on verbal descriptions of problems and solutions.[[111]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-131) Some historians see this development as a key turning point in the history of algebra and consider what came before it as the prehistory of algebra because it lacked the abstract nature based on symbolic manipulation.[[112]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-132)

In the 17th and 18th centuries, many attempts were made to find general solutions to polynomials of degree five and higher. All of them failed.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-auto3-42) At the end of the 18th century, the German mathematician [Carl Friedrich Gauss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Friedrich_Gauss "Carl Friedrich Gauss") proved the [fundamental theorem of algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_theorem_of_algebra "Fundamental theorem of algebra"), which describes the existence of [zeros](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero_of_a_function "Zero of a function") of polynomials of any degree without providing a general solution.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-auto7-22) At the beginning of the 19th century, the Italian mathematician [Paolo Ruffini](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paolo_Ruffini "Paolo Ruffini") and the Norwegian mathematician [Niels Henrik Abel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Niels_Henrik_Abel "Niels Henrik Abel") were [able to show](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abel%E2%80%93Ruffini_theorem "Abel–Ruffini theorem") that no general solution exists for polynomials of degree five and higher.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-auto3-42) In response to and shortly after their findings, the French mathematician [Évariste Galois](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%89variste_Galois "Évariste Galois") developed what came later to be known as [Galois theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galois_theory "Galois theory"), which offered a more in-depth analysis of the solutions of polynomials while also laying the foundation of [group theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_theory "Group theory").[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-auto2-23) Mathematicians soon realized the relevance of group theory to other fields and applied it to disciplines like geometry and number theory.[[113]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-133)

[![Image 119: Photo of Garrett Birkhoff](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Garrett_Birkhoff.jpeg/250px-Garrett_Birkhoff.jpeg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Garrett_Birkhoff.jpeg)

[Garrett Birkhoff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Garrett_Birkhoff "Garrett Birkhoff") developed many of the foundational concepts of universal algebra.

Starting in the mid-19th century, interest in algebra shifted from the study of polynomials associated with elementary algebra towards a more general inquiry into algebraic structures, marking the emergence of [abstract algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abstract_algebra "Abstract algebra"). This approach explored the axiomatic basis of arbitrary algebraic operations.[[114]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-134) The invention of new algebraic systems based on different operations and elements accompanied this development, such as [Boolean algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boolean_algebra "Boolean algebra"), [vector algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space"), and [matrix algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_algebra "Matrix algebra").[[115]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-135) Influential early developments in abstract algebra were made by the German mathematicians [David Hilbert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Hilbert "David Hilbert"), [Ernst Steinitz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ernst_Steinitz "Ernst Steinitz"), and [Emmy Noether](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emmy_Noether "Emmy Noether") as well as the Austrian mathematician [Emil Artin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emil_Artin "Emil Artin"). They researched different forms of algebraic structures and categorized them based on their underlying axioms into types, like groups, rings, and fields.[[116]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-136)

The idea of the even more general approach associated with universal algebra was conceived by the English mathematician [Alfred North Whitehead](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alfred_North_Whitehead "Alfred North Whitehead") in his 1898 book _A Treatise on Universal Algebra_. Starting in the 1930s, the American mathematician [Garrett Birkhoff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Garrett_Birkhoff "Garrett Birkhoff") expanded these ideas and developed many of the foundational concepts of this field.[[117]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-137) The invention of universal algebra led to the emergence of various new areas focused on the algebraization of mathematics—that is, the application of algebraic methods to other branches of mathematics. Topological algebra arose in the early 20th century, studying algebraic structures such as [topological groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_groups "Topological groups") and [Lie groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_groups "Lie groups").[[118]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-138) In the 1940s and 50s, [homological algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homological_algebra "Homological algebra") emerged, employing algebraic techniques to study [homology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homology_(mathematics) "Homology (mathematics)").[[119]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-139) Around the same time, [category theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category_theory "Category theory") was developed and has since played a key role in the [foundations of mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foundations_of_mathematics "Foundations of mathematics").[[120]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-140) Other developments were the formulation of [model theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Model_theory "Model theory") and the study of [free algebras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_algebra "Free algebra").[[121]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-141)

The influence of algebra is wide-reaching, both within mathematics and in its applications to other fields.[[122]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-142) The algebraization of mathematics is the process of applying algebraic methods and principles to other [branches of mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Branches_of_mathematics "Branches of mathematics"), such as [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry"), [topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology "Topology"), [number theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_theory "Number theory"), and [calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus "Calculus"). It happens by employing symbols in the form of variables to express mathematical insights on a more general level, allowing mathematicians to develop formal models describing how objects interact and relate to each other.[[123]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-143)

[![Image 120: Rendered image of a sphere](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Sphere_Quadric.png/250px-Sphere_Quadric.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Sphere_Quadric.png)

The algebraic equation ![Image 121: {\displaystyle x^{2}+y^{2}+z^{2}=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a5b5c3e3525a1604f9425edb80835d236fe7af82) describes a [sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere "Sphere") at the [origin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Origin_(mathematics) "Origin (mathematics)") with a radius of 1.

One application, found in geometry, is the use of algebraic statements to describe geometric figures. For example, the equation ![Image 122: {\displaystyle y=3x-7}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4857390656a28244d04425be9b9a743816c463c6) describes a line in two-dimensional space while the equation ![Image 123: {\displaystyle x^{2}+y^{2}+z^{2}=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a5b5c3e3525a1604f9425edb80835d236fe7af82) corresponds to a [sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere "Sphere") in three-dimensional space. Of special interest to [algebraic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_geometry "Algebraic geometry") are [algebraic varieties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_varieties "Algebraic varieties"),[[u]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-145) which are solutions to [systems of polynomial equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Systems_of_polynomial_equations "Systems of polynomial equations") that can be used to describe more complex geometric figures.[[125]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-146) Algebraic reasoning can also solve geometric problems. For example, one can determine whether and where the line described by ![Image 124: {\displaystyle y=x+1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/14d4839f41dc3d34ae434dfad1ab18479cc6a41b) intersects with the circle described by ![Image 125: {\displaystyle x^{2}+y^{2}=25}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/918951e8e7e900ca20e45513737f11296377aed5) by solving the system of equations made up of these two equations.[[126]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-147) Topology studies the properties of geometric figures or [topological spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_space "Topological space") that are preserved under operations of [continuous deformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_deformation "Continuous deformation"). [Algebraic topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_topology "Algebraic topology") relies on algebraic theories such as [group theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_theory "Group theory") to classify topological spaces. For example, [homotopy groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homotopy_groups "Homotopy groups") classify topological spaces based on the existence of [loops](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Loop_(topology) "Loop (topology)") or [holes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hole#In_mathematics "Hole") in them.[[127]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-148)

Number theory is concerned with the properties of and relations between integers. [Algebraic number theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_number_theory "Algebraic number theory") applies algebraic methods and principles to this field of inquiry. Examples are the use of algebraic expressions to describe general laws, like [Fermat's Last Theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermat%27s_Last_Theorem "Fermat's Last Theorem"), and of algebraic structures to analyze the behavior of numbers, such as the [ring of integers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ring_of_integers "Ring of integers").[[128]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-149) The related field of [combinatorics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Combinatorics "Combinatorics") uses algebraic techniques to solve problems related to counting, arrangement, and combination of discrete objects. An example in [algebraic combinatorics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_combinatorics "Algebraic combinatorics") is the application of group theory to analyze [graphs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_(discrete_mathematics) "Graph (discrete mathematics)") and symmetries.[[129]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-150) The insights of algebra are also relevant to calculus, which uses mathematical expressions to examine [rates of change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rates_of_change "Rates of change") and [accumulation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral "Integral"). It relies on algebra, for instance, to understand how these expressions can be transformed and what role variables play in them.[[130]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-151)[Algebraic logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_logic "Algebraic logic") employs the methods of algebra to describe and analyze the structures and patterns that underlie [logical reasoning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logical_reasoning "Logical reasoning"),[[131]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-152) exploring both the relevant mathematical structures themselves and their application to concrete problems of logic.[[132]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-153) It includes the study of [Boolean algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boolean_algebra "Boolean algebra") to describe [propositional logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propositional_logic "Propositional logic")[[133]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-154) as well as the formulation and analysis of algebraic structures corresponding to more complex [systems of logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logic#Systems_of_logic "Logic").[[134]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-155)

[![Image 126: Picture of Rubik's cube](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Rubik%27s_cube.svg/250px-Rubik%27s_cube.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Rubik%27s_cube.svg)

The faces of a [Rubik's Cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rubik%27s_Cube "Rubik's Cube") can be rotated to change the arrangement of colored patches. The resulting permutations form a group called the [Rubik's Cube group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rubik%27s_Cube_group "Rubik's Cube group").[[135]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-156)

Algebraic methods are also commonly employed in other areas, like the natural sciences. For example, they are used to express [scientific laws](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_laws "Scientific laws") and solve equations in [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics"), [chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemistry "Chemistry"), and [biology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology "Biology").[[136]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-157) Similar applications are found in fields like [economics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economics "Economics"), [geography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography "Geography"), [engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineering "Engineering") (including [electronics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronics "Electronics") and [robotics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robotics "Robotics")), and [computer science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_science "Computer science") to express relationships, solve problems, and model systems.[[137]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-158) Linear algebra plays a central role in [artificial intelligence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artificial_intelligence "Artificial intelligence") and [machine learning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Machine_learning "Machine learning"), for instance, by enabling the efficient processing and analysis of large [datasets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dataset "Dataset").[[138]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-159) Various fields rely on algebraic structures investigated by abstract algebra. For example, physical sciences like [crystallography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystallography "Crystallography") and [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics") make extensive use of group theory,[[139]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-160) which is also employed to study puzzles such as [Sudoku](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sudoku "Sudoku") and [Rubik's Cubes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rubik%27s_Cube "Rubik's Cube"),[[140]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-161) and [origami](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics_of_paper_folding "Mathematics of paper folding").[[141]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-162) Both [coding theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coding_theory "Coding theory") and [cryptology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cryptology "Cryptology") rely on abstract algebra to solve problems associated with [data transmission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_transmission "Data transmission"), like avoiding the effects of [noise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noise "Noise") and ensuring [data security](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_security "Data security").[[142]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-163)

[![Image 127: Diagram of a balance scale](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Balance_scale.svg/330px-Balance_scale.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Balance_scale.svg)

[Balance scales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Balance_scales "Balance scales") are used in algebra education to help students understand how equations can be transformed to determine unknown values.[[143]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-164)

Algebra education mostly focuses on elementary algebra, which is one of the reasons why elementary algebra is also called school algebra. It is usually not introduced until [secondary education](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Secondary_education "Secondary education") since it requires mastery of the fundamentals of arithmetic while posing new cognitive challenges associated with abstract reasoning and generalization.[[144]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-165) It aims to familiarize students with the formal side of mathematics by helping them understand mathematical symbolism, for example, how variables can be used to represent unknown quantities. An additional difficulty for students lies in the fact that, unlike arithmetic calculations, algebraic expressions are often difficult to solve directly. Instead, students need to learn how to transform them according to certain laws, often to determine an unknown quantity.[[145]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-166)

Some tools to introduce students to the abstract side of algebra rely on concrete models and visualizations of equations, including geometric analogies, manipulatives including sticks or cups, and "function machines" representing equations as [flow diagrams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flow_diagram "Flow diagram"). One method uses [balance scales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Balance_scales "Balance scales") as a pictorial approach to help students grasp basic problems of algebra. The mass of some objects on the scale is unknown and represents variables. Solving an equation corresponds to adding and removing objects on both sides in such a way that the sides stay in balance until the only object remaining on one side is the object of unknown mass.[[146]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-167)[Word problems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Word_problem_(mathematics_education) "Word problem (mathematics education)") are another tool to show how algebra is applied to real-life situations. For example, students may be presented with a situation in which Naomi's brother has twice as many apples as Naomi. Given that both together have twelve apples, students are then asked to find an algebraic equation that describes this situation (⁠![Image 128: {\displaystyle 2x+x=12}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/65d4cc374372ce276c0ae9d21cc5fd9ab0c35377)⁠) and to determine how many apples Naomi has (⁠![Image 129: {\displaystyle x=4}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/73bfdd8100b6a4ce07d011900560f102e3965064)⁠).[[147]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-168)

At the university level, mathematics students encounter advanced algebra topics from linear and abstract algebra. Initial [undergraduate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Undergraduate "Undergraduate") courses in linear algebra focus on matrices, vector spaces, and linear maps. Upon completing them, students are usually introduced to abstract algebra, where they learn about algebraic structures like groups, rings, and fields, as well as the relations between them. The curriculum typically also covers specific instances of algebraic structures, such as the systems of rational numbers, the real numbers, and the polynomials.[[148]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra#cite_note-169)

*   [Computer algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_algebra "Computer algebra")– Scientific area at the interface between computer science and mathematics
*   [Exterior algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_algebra "Exterior algebra")– Algebra associated to any vector space
