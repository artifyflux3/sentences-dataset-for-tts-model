Diagram of generic quasi-satellite orbit

A **quasi-satellite** is an object in a specific type of [co-orbital configuration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Co-orbital_configuration "Co-orbital configuration") (1:1 [orbital resonance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_resonance "Orbital resonance")) with a [planet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planet "Planet") (or [dwarf planet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dwarf_planet "Dwarf planet")) where the object stays close to that planet over many orbital periods.

A quasi-satellite's orbit around the [Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun "Sun") takes the same time as the planet's, but has a different eccentricity (usually greater), as shown in the diagram. When viewed from the perspective of the planet by an observer facing the Sun, the quasi-satellite will appear to travel in an oblong retrograde loop around the planet. (See [Analemma §Of quasi-satellites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analemma#Of_quasi-satellites "Analemma")).

In contrast to _true_ satellites, quasi-satellite orbits lie outside the planet's [Hill sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hill_sphere "Hill sphere"), and are unstable. Over time they tend to evolve to other types of resonant motion, where they no longer remain in the planet's neighborhood, then possibly later move back to a quasi-satellite orbit, etc.

Other types of orbit in a 1:1 resonance with the planet include [horseshoe orbits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horseshoe_orbit "Horseshoe orbit") and [tadpole orbits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tadpole_orbit "Tadpole orbit") around the [Lagrangian points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrangian_point "Lagrangian point"), but objects in these orbits do not stay near the planet's longitude over many revolutions about the star. Objects in horseshoe orbits are known to sometimes periodically transfer to a relatively short-lived quasi-satellite orbit,[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasi-satellite#cite_note-Connors02-1) and are sometimes confused with them. An example of such an object is [2002 AA 29](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2002_AA29 "2002 AA29").

A quasi-satellite is similar to an object in a [distant retrograde orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distant_retrograde_orbit "Distant retrograde orbit"), in a different context. The latter term is usually used for a space probe or artificial satellite in a retrograde orbit around a moon, and the period may be much shorter than that of the moon, whereas the term "quasi-satellite" usually refers to an object like an asteroid whose period is similar to that of the planet of which it is considered to be a quasi-satellite. But in both cases, the object (asteroid, space probe) viewed in a reference frame that rotates with the two main objects (once a year for Sun-Earth, once a month for Earth-Moon) appears to move retrograde compared to that rotation, thus lengthening its sidereal period. So a quasi-satellite (with low inclination) tends to stay in certain constellations rather than going through the whole zodiac. Quasi-satellites with high eccentricity can get quite far from their planet, more than an [astronomical unit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_unit "Astronomical unit") for quasi-satellites of [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth "Earth") such as [2014 OL 339](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2014_OL339 "2014 OL339").

The word "geosynchronous" is sometimes used to describe quasi-satellites of the Earth, because their motion around the Sun is synchronized with Earth's. However, this usage is unconventional and confusing. Conventionally, [geosynchronous satellites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_satellite "Geosynchronous satellite") revolve in the prograde sense around the Earth, with orbital periods that are synchronized to the Earth's rotation.

Venus has one known quasi-satellite, [524522 Zoozve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/524522_Zoozve "524522 Zoozve"). This asteroid is also a [Mercury-](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_Mercury-crossing_minor_planets "List of Mercury-crossing minor planets") and [Earth-crosser](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_Earth-crossing_minor_planets "List of Earth-crossing minor planets"); it seems to have been a "companion" to Venus for approximately the last 7,000 years only, and is destined to be ejected from this orbital arrangement about 500 years from now.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasi-satellite#cite_note-2)

On the longer term, asteroids can transfer between quasi-satellite orbits and [horseshoe orbits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horseshoe_orbit "Horseshoe orbit"), which circulate around [Lagrangian points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrangian_point "Lagrangian point") L4 and L5. By 2016, orbital calculations showed that all five of Earth's then known quasi-satellites repeatedly transfer between horseshoe and quasi-satellite orbits.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasi-satellite#cite_note-2016HO3-8)[3753 Cruithne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3753_Cruithne "3753 Cruithne"),[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasi-satellite#cite_note-9)[2002 AA 29](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2002_AA29 "2002 AA29"),[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasi-satellite#cite_note-Connors02-1)[2003 YN 107](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2003_YN107 "2003 YN107") and [2015 SO 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2015_SO2 "2015 SO2")[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasi-satellite#cite_note-FuenteSO2-5) are minor planets in horseshoe orbits that might evolve into a quasi-satellite orbit. The time spent in the quasi-satellite phase differs from asteroid to asteroid. Quasi-satellite 2016 HO 3 is predicted to be stable in this orbital state for several hundred years, in contrast to 2003 YN 107 which was a quasi-satellite from 1996 to 2006 but then departed Earth's vicinity on a horseshoe orbit.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasi-satellite#cite_note-2016HO3-8)[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasi-satellite#cite_note-jpl.nasa.gov-10)

[469219 Kamoʻoalewa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/469219_Kamo%CA%BBoalewa "469219 Kamoʻoalewa") (2016 HO 3) is thought to be one of the most stable quasi-satellites found yet of Earth. It stays between 38 and 100[lunar distances](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunar_distance_(astronomy) "Lunar distance (astronomy)") from the Earth.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasi-satellite#cite_note-jpl.nasa.gov-10)

The dwarf-planet asteroid [1 Ceres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1_Ceres "1 Ceres") is believed to have a quasi-satellite, the as-yet-unnamed [(76146) 2000 EU 16](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/(76146)_2000_EU16 "(76146) 2000 EU16").

[(309239) 2007 RW 10](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/(309239)_2007_RW10 "(309239) 2007 RW10") is a temporary quasi-satellite of [Neptune](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune "Neptune").[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasi-satellite#cite_note-quasiN-11) The object has been a quasi-satellite of Neptune for about 12,500 years and it will remain in that dynamical state for another 12,500 years.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasi-satellite#cite_note-quasiN-11)

Based on simulations, it is believed that [Uranus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uranus "Uranus") and [Neptune](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune "Neptune") could potentially hold quasi-satellites for up to the age of the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System") (about 4.5 billion years),[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasi-satellite#cite_note-Wiegert00-12) but a quasi-satellite's orbit would remain stable for only 10 million years near [Jupiter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jupiter "Jupiter") and 100,000 years near [Saturn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saturn "Saturn"). Jupiter and Saturn are known to have quasi-satellites.[_[clarification needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Please\_clarify "Wikipedia:Please clarify")_][2015 OL 106](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=2015_OL106&action=edit&redlink=1 "2015 OL106 (page does not exist)"), a co-orbital to Jupiter, intermittently becomes a quasi satellite of the planet, and will next become one between 2380 and 2480.

### Artificial quasi-satellites

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Quasi-satellite&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Artificial quasi-satellites")]

In early 1989, the Soviet _[Phobos 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phobos\_program#Phobos\_2 "Phobos program")_ spacecraft was injected into a quasi-satellite orbit around the Martian moon [Phobos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phobos_(moon) "Phobos (moon)"), with a mean orbital radius of about 100 kilometres (62 mi) from Phobos.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasi-satellite#cite_note-13) According to computations, it could have then stayed trapped in the vicinity of Phobos for many months. The spacecraft was lost due to a malfunction of the on-board control system.

In 2005, aerospace engineer Thomas Gangale proposed a quasi-satellite orbit for spacecraft to provide communications relays between Earth and crews on Mars when Mars is in solar conjunction and direct communication is blocked by the Sun for several weeks.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasi-satellite#cite_note-14)

### Accidental quasi-satellites

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Quasi-satellite&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Accidental quasi-satellites")]

Some objects are known to be accidental quasi-satellites, which means that they are not forced into the configuration by the gravitational influence of the body of which they are quasi-satellites.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasi-satellite#cite_note-PlutoQS1-15) The [dwarf planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dwarf_planet "Dwarf planet")[Ceres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ceres_(dwarf_planet) "Ceres (dwarf planet)") and [Pluto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pluto "Pluto") are known to have accidental quasi-satellites.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasi-satellite#cite_note-PlutoQS1-15) In the case of Pluto, the known accidental quasi-satellite, [15810 Arawn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/15810_Arawn "15810 Arawn"), is, like Pluto, a [plutino](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plutino "Plutino"), and is forced into this configuration by the gravitational influence of Neptune.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasi-satellite#cite_note-PlutoQS1-15) This dynamical behavior is recurrent where Arawn becomes a quasi-satellite of Pluto every 2.4 Myr and remains in that configuration for nearly 350,000 years.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasi-satellite#cite_note-PlutoQS1-15)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasi-satellite#cite_note-S&T-16)[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasi-satellite#cite_note-analemma-17)
