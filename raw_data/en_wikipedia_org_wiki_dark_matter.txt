Title: Dark matter

URL Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter

Published Time: 2001-11-18T18:01:13Z

Markdown Content:
Unsolved problem in physics

What is dark matter? How was it generated?

In [astronomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy "Astronomy") and [cosmology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmology "Cosmology"), **dark matter** is an invisible and hypothetical form of [matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matter "Matter") that does not interact with [light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light "Light") or other [electromagnetic radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation "Electromagnetic radiation"). Dark matter is implied by [gravitational](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity "Gravity") effects that cannot be explained by [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity") unless more matter is present than can be observed. Such effects occur in the context of [formation and evolution of galaxies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_formation_and_evolution "Galaxy formation and evolution"),[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-Siegfried-1)[gravitational lensing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_lensing "Gravitational lensing"),[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-Trimble_1987-2) the [observable universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe "Observable universe")'s current structure, mass position in [galactic collisions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galactic_collision "Galactic collision"),[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-3) the motion of galaxies within [galaxy clusters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_cluster "Galaxy cluster"), and [cosmic microwave background](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background "Cosmic microwave background")[anisotropies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anisotropy "Anisotropy"). Dark matter is thought to serve as gravitational scaffolding for cosmic structures.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-4) After the Big Bang, dark matter clumped into blobs along narrow filaments with superclusters of galaxies forming a cosmic web at scales on which entire galaxies appear like tiny particles.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-6)

In the standard [Lambda-CDM model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lambda-CDM_model "Lambda-CDM model") of cosmology, the [mass–energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass%E2%80%93energy_equivalence "Mass–energy equivalence") content of the universe is 5% ordinary matter, 26.8% dark matter, and 68.2% a form of energy known as [dark energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_energy "Dark energy").[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-NASA_Planck_Mission-7)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-NASA_Science_Dark_Matter-8)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-planck_overview-9)[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-wmap7parameters(a)-10) Thus, dark matter constitutes 85% of the total mass, while dark energy and dark matter constitute 95% of the total mass–energy content.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-planckcam-11)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-DarkMatter-12)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-13)[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-wmap7parameters-14) While the density of dark matter is significant in the halo around a galaxy, its local density in the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System") is much less than normal matter. The total of all the dark matter out to the orbit of Neptune would add up about 10 17 kg, the same as a large asteroid.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-15)

Dark matter is not known to interact with ordinary [baryonic matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baryonic_matter#Baryonic_matter "Baryonic matter") and [radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiation "Radiation") except through gravity, making it difficult to detect in the laboratory. The most prevalent explanation is that dark matter is some as-yet-undiscovered [subatomic particle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subatomic_particle "Subatomic particle"), such as either [weakly interacting massive particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weakly_interacting_massive_particle "Weakly interacting massive particle") (WIMPs) or [axions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axion "Axion").[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-ars_lensing-16) The other main possibility is that dark matter is composed of [primordial black holes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primordial_black_hole "Primordial black hole").[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-Carr24-17)[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-Bird-18)[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-Carr-19)

Dark matter is classified as "cold", "warm", or "hot" according to [velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity "Velocity") (more precisely, its [free streaming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_streaming "Free streaming") length). Recent models have favored a [cold dark matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cold_dark_matter "Cold dark matter") scenario, in which [structures emerge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Structure_formation "Structure formation") by the gradual accumulation of particles.

Although the astrophysics community generally accepts the existence of dark matter,[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-20) a minority of astrophysicists, intrigued by specific observations that are not well explained by ordinary dark matter, argue for various modifications of the standard laws of general relativity. These include [modified Newtonian dynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modified_Newtonian_dynamics "Modified Newtonian dynamics"), [tensor–vector–scalar gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor%E2%80%93vector%E2%80%93scalar_gravity "Tensor–vector–scalar gravity"), or [entropic gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropic_gravity "Entropic gravity"). So far none of the proposed modified gravity theories can describe [every piece of observational evidence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#Observational_evidence) at the same time, suggesting that even if gravity has to be modified, some form of dark matter will still be required.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-CarrollTrialogue-21)

History
-------

### Early history

The hypothesis of dark matter has an elaborate history.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-GianfracoHooperHistory-22)[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-23)[Wm. Thomson, Lord Kelvin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Thomson,_1st_Baron_Kelvin "William Thomson, 1st Baron Kelvin"), discussed the potential number of stars around the Sun in the appendices of a book based on a series of lectures given in 1884 in Baltimore.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-Kelvin-1904-24)[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-GianfracoHooperHistory-22) He inferred their density using the observed velocity dispersion of the stars near the Sun, assuming that the Sun was 20–100 million years old. He posed what would happen if there were a thousand million stars within 1[kiloparsec](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parsec "Parsec") of the Sun (at which distance their parallax would be 1[milli-arcsecond](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minute_and_second_of_arc "Minute and second of arc")). Kelvin concluded

> Many of our supposed thousand million stars – perhaps a great majority of them – may be dark bodies.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-Kelvin-1904-24)[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-ArsTech-2017-02-03-25)

In 1906, [Henri Poincaré](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Poincar%C3%A9 "Henri Poincaré")[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-Poincar%C3%A9-1906-26) used the [French](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_language "French language") term [_matière obscure_] ("dark matter") in discussing Kelvin's work.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-Poincar%C3%A9-1906-26)[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-ArsTech-2017-02-03-25) He found that the amount of dark matter would need to be less than that of visible matter, incorrectly, it turns out.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-ArsTech-2017-02-03-25)[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-GianfracoHooperHistory-22)

The second to suggest the existence of dark matter using stellar velocities was Dutch astronomer [Jacobus Kapteyn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacobus_Kapteyn "Jacobus Kapteyn") in 1922.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-27)[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-Patras2014-28)

A publication from 1930 by Swedish astronomer [Knut Lundmark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knut_Lundmark "Knut Lundmark") points to him being the first to realise that the universe must contain much more mass than can be observed.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-29) Dutch radio astronomy pioneer [Jan Oort](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jan_Oort "Jan Oort") also hypothesized the existence of dark matter in 1932.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-Patras2014-28)[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-30)[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-31) Oort was studying stellar motions in [the galactic neighborhood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_Group "Local Group") and found the mass in the galactic plane must be greater than what was observed, but this measurement was later determined to be incorrect.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-32)

In 1933, Swiss astrophysicist [Fritz Zwicky](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fritz_Zwicky "Fritz Zwicky") studied [galaxy clusters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_cluster "Galaxy cluster") while working at [Caltech](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/California_Institute_of_Technology "California Institute of Technology") and made a similar inference.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-zwicky1933-33)[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-34)[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-zwicky1937-35) Zwicky applied the [virial theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virial_theorem "Virial theorem") to the [Coma Cluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coma_Cluster "Coma Cluster") and obtained evidence of unseen mass he called _dunkle Materie_ ('dark matter'). Zwicky estimated its mass based on the motions of galaxies near its edge and compared that to an estimate based on its brightness and number of galaxies. He estimated the cluster had about 400 times more mass than was visually observable. The gravity effect of the visible galaxies was far too small for such fast orbits, thus mass must be hidden from view. Based on these conclusions, Zwicky inferred some unseen matter provided the mass and associated gravitational attraction to hold the cluster together.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-36) Zwicky's estimates were off by more than an order of magnitude, mainly due to an obsolete value of the [Hubble constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble_constant "Hubble constant");[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-37) the same calculation today shows a smaller fraction, using greater values for luminous mass. Nonetheless, Zwicky did correctly conclude from his calculation that most of the gravitational matter present was dark.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-ArsTech-2017-02-03-25) However, unlike modern theories, Zwicky considered "dark matter" to be non-luminous ordinary matter.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-GianfracoHooperHistory-22): III.A

Further indications of [mass-to-light ratio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass-to-light_ratio "Mass-to-light ratio") anomalies came from measurements of [galaxy rotation curves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_rotation_curve "Galaxy rotation curve"). In 1939, [H.W. Babcock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horace_W._Babcock "Horace W. Babcock") reported the rotation curve for the [Andromeda Nebula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andromeda_Galaxy "Andromeda Galaxy") (now called _the Andromeda Galaxy_), which suggested the mass-to-luminosity ratio increases radially.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-Babcock-1939-38) He attributed it to either light absorption within the galaxy or modified dynamics in the outer portions of the spiral, rather than to unseen matter. Following [Babcock's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horace_W._Babcock "Horace W. Babcock") 1939 report of unexpectedly rapid rotation in the outskirts of the Andromeda Galaxy and a mass-to-light ratio of 50; in 1940, [Oort](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jan_Oort "Jan Oort") discovered and wrote about the large non-visible halo of [NGC 3115](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NGC_3115 "NGC 3115").[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-39)

### 1970s

The hypothesis of dark matter largely took root in the 1970s. Several different observations were synthesized to argue that galaxies should be surrounded by halos of unseen matter. In two papers that appeared in 1974, this conclusion was drawn in tandem by independent groups: in [Princeton, New Jersey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Princeton,_New_Jersey "Princeton, New Jersey"), by [Jeremiah Ostriker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jeremiah_Ostriker "Jeremiah Ostriker"), [Jim Peebles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jim_Peebles "Jim Peebles"), and [Amos Yahil](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Amos_Yahil&action=edit&redlink=1 "Amos Yahil (page does not exist)"), and in Tartu, Estonia, by [Jaan Einasto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jaan_Einasto "Jaan Einasto"), [Enn Saar](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Enn_Saar&action=edit&redlink=1 "Enn Saar (page does not exist)"), and [Ants Kaasik](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ants_Kaasik&action=edit&redlink=1 "Ants Kaasik (page does not exist)").[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-DeSwart2024-40)

One of the observations that served as evidence for the existence of galactic halos of dark matter was the shape of [galaxy rotation curves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_rotation_curve "Galaxy rotation curve"). These observations were done in optical and radio astronomy. In optical astronomy, [Vera Rubin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vera_Rubin "Vera Rubin") and [Kent Ford](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kent_Ford_(astronomer) "Kent Ford (astronomer)") worked with a new [spectrograph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectrograph "Spectrograph") to measure the [velocity curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_rotation_curve "Galaxy rotation curve") of edge-on [spiral galaxies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spiral_galaxy "Spiral galaxy") with greater accuracy.[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-NYT-20161227-41)[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-42)[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-Rubin1970-43)

At the same time, radio astronomers were making use of new [radio telescopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radio_telescope "Radio telescope") to map the [21 cm line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/21_cm_line "21 cm line") of [atomic hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_hydrogen "Atomic hydrogen") in nearby galaxies. The radial distribution of interstellar atomic hydrogen ([H I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/H_I_region "H I region")) often extends to much greater galactic distances than can be observed as collective starlight, expanding the sampled distances for rotation curves – and thus of the total mass distribution – to a new dynamical regime. Early mapping of [Andromeda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andromeda_galaxy "Andromeda galaxy") with the 300-foot (91 m) telescope at [Green Bank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green_Bank_Observatory "Green Bank Observatory")[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-Roberts1966-44) and the 250-foot (76 m) dish at [Jodrell Bank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jodrell_Bank_Observatory "Jodrell Bank Observatory")[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-Gottesman1966-45) already showed the H I rotation curve did not trace the decline expected from Keplerian orbits.

As more sensitive receivers became available, Roberts & Whitehurst (1975)[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-Roberts1975-46) were able to trace the rotational velocity of Andromeda to 30 kpc, much beyond the optical measurements. Illustrating the advantage of tracing the gas disk at large radii; that paper's _Figure 16_[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-Roberts1975-46) combines the optical data[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-Rubin1970-43) (the cluster of points at radii of less than 15 kpc with a single point further out) with the H I data between 20 and 30 kpc, exhibiting the flatness of the outer galaxy rotation curve; the solid curve peaking at the center is the optical surface density, while the other curve shows the cumulative mass, still rising linearly at the outermost measurement. In parallel, the use of interferometric arrays for extragalactic H I spectroscopy was being developed. Rogstad &[Shostak](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seth_Shostak "Seth Shostak") (1972)[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-Rogstad1972-47) published H I rotation curves of five spirals mapped with the Owens Valley interferometer; the rotation curves of all five were very flat, suggesting very large values of mass-to-light ratio in the outer parts of their extended H I disks.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-Rogstad1972-47) In 1978, Albert Bosma showed further evidence of flat rotation curves using data from the [Westerbork Synthesis Radio Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Westerbork_Synthesis_Radio_Telescope "Westerbork Synthesis Radio Telescope").[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-48)

In 1978, [Steigman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gary_Steigman "Gary Steigman") et al.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-49) presented a study that extended earlier cosmological relic-density calculations to any hypothetical stable, electrically neutral, weak-scale lepton, showing how such a particle's abundance would "freeze out" in the [early Universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Early_universe "Early universe") and providing analytic expressions that linked its mass and weak interaction cross-section to the present-day matter density. By decoupling the analysis from specific [neutrino](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutrino "Neutrino") properties and treating the candidate generically, the authors set out a framework that later became the standard template for [weakly interacting massive particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weakly_interacting_massive_particle "Weakly interacting massive particle") (WIMPs)[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-50) and for comparing [particle-physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_physics "Particle physics") models with cosmological constraints. Though subsequent work has refined the methodology and explored many alternative candidates, this paper marked the first explicit, systematic treatment of dark matter as a new particle species beyond the [Standard Model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model "Standard Model").[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-51)

By the late 1970s the existence of dark matter halos around galaxies was widely recognized as real, and became a major unsolved problem in astronomy.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-DeSwart2024-40)

### 1980–1990s

A stream of observations in the 1980–1990s supported the presence of dark matter. [Persic, Salucci & Stel (1996)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#CITEREFPersicSalucciStel1996) is notable for the investigation of 967 spirals.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-52) The evidence for dark matter also included [gravitational lensing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_lensing "Gravitational lensing") of background objects by [galaxy clusters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_cluster "Galaxy cluster"),[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-Randall_2015-53)(pp 14–16) the temperature distribution of hot gas in galaxies and clusters, and the pattern of anisotropies in the [cosmic microwave background](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background "Cosmic microwave background").

According to the current consensus among cosmologists, dark matter is composed primarily of some type of not-yet-characterized [subatomic particle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subatomic_particle "Subatomic particle").[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-Copi_1995-54)[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-Bergstrom_2000-55) The search for this particle, by a variety of means, is one of the major efforts in [particle physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_physics "Particle physics").[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-bertone_hooper_silk-56)

Technical definition
--------------------

In standard cosmological calculations, _"matter"_ means any constituent of the universe whose energy density scales with the inverse cube of the [scale factor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scale_factor_(cosmology) "Scale factor (cosmology)"), i.e., _ρ_ ∝ _a_−3. This is in contrast to _"radiation"_, which scales as the inverse fourth power of the scale factor _ρ_ ∝ _a_−4, and a [cosmological constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmological_constant "Cosmological constant"), which does not change with respect to a (_ρ_ ∝ _a_ 0).[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-Baumann_lecture_notes-57) The different scaling factors for matter and radiation are a consequence of radiation [redshift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift "Redshift"). For example, after doubling the diameter of the observable Universe via [cosmic expansion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_expansion "Cosmic expansion"), the scale, a, has doubled. The energy of the [cosmic microwave background radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation "Cosmic microwave background radiation") has been halved (because the wavelength of each photon has doubled);[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-58) the energy of ultra-relativistic particles, such as early-era standard-model neutrinos, is similarly halved.[[b]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-59) The cosmological constant, as an intrinsic property of space, has a constant energy density regardless of the volume under consideration.[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-Baumann_lecture_notes-57)

In principle, "dark matter" means all components of the universe which are not visible but still obey _ρ_ ∝ _a_−3. In practice, the term "dark matter" is often used to mean only the non-baryonic component of dark matter, i.e., excluding "[missing baryons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Missing_baryon_problem "Missing baryon problem")".[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-60) Context will usually indicate which meaning is intended.

Observational evidence
----------------------

This section presents the observational evidence for dark matter from the smallest to the largest scales.

### Galaxy rotation curves

Animation of rotating disc galaxies. Dark matter– shown in red– is more concentrated near the center and it rotates more rapidly.

The arms of [spiral galaxies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spiral_galaxies "Spiral galaxies") rotate around their galactic center. The luminous mass density of a spiral galaxy decreases as one goes from the center to the outskirts. If luminous mass were all the matter, then the galaxy can be modelled as a point mass in the centre and test masses orbiting around it, similar to the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System").[[c]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-61) From [Kepler's Third Law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler%27s_Third_Law "Kepler's Third Law"), it is expected that the rotation velocities will decrease with distance from the center, similar to the Solar System. This is not observed.[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-62) Instead, the galaxy rotation curve remains flat or even increases as distance from the center increases.

If Kepler's laws are correct, then the obvious way to resolve this discrepancy is to conclude the mass distribution in spiral galaxies is not similar to that of the Solar System. In particular, there may be a lot of non-luminous matter (dark matter) in the outskirts of the galaxy.

### Velocity dispersions

Stars in bound systems must obey the [virial theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virial_theorem "Virial theorem"). The theorem, together with the measured velocity distribution, can be used to measure the mass distribution in a bound system, such as elliptical galaxies or globular clusters. With some exceptions, velocity dispersion estimates of elliptical galaxies[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-63) do not match the predicted velocity dispersion from the observed mass distribution, even assuming complicated distributions of stellar orbits.[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-64)

As with galaxy rotation curves, the obvious way to resolve the discrepancy is to postulate the existence of non-luminous matter.

### Galaxy clusters

[Galaxy clusters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_clusters "Galaxy clusters") are particularly important for dark matter studies since their masses can be estimated in three independent ways:

*   From the scatter in radial velocities of the galaxies within clusters
*   From [X-rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray "X-ray") emitted by hot gas in the clusters. From the X-ray energy spectrum and flux, the gas temperature and density can be estimated, hence giving the pressure; assuming pressure and gravity balance determines the cluster's mass profile.
*   [Gravitational lensing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_lens "Gravitational lens") (usually of more distant galaxies) can measure cluster masses without relying on observations of dynamics (e.g., velocity).

Generally, these three methods are in reasonable agreement that dark matter outweighs visible matter by approximately 5 to 1.[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-65)

### Bullet Cluster

The Bullet Cluster is the result of a recent collision of two galaxy clusters. It is of particular note because the location of the [center of mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Center_of_mass "Center of mass") as measured by gravitational lensing is different from the location of the center of mass of visible matter. This is difficult for modified gravity theories, which generally predict lensing around visible matter, to explain.[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-66)[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-67)[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-68)[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-69) Standard dark matter theory however has no issue: the hot, visible gas in each cluster would be cooled and slowed down by electromagnetic interactions, while dark matter (which does not interact electromagnetically) would not. This leads to the dark matter separating from the visible gas, producing the separate lensing peak as observed.[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-70)

### Gravitational lensing

One of the consequences of [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity") is the [gravitational lens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_lens "Gravitational lens"). Gravitational lensing occurs when massive objects between a source of light and the observer act as a lens to bend light from this source. Lensing does not depend on the properties of the mass; it only requires there to be a mass. The more massive an object, the more lensing is observed. An example is a [cluster of galaxies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_cluster "Galaxy cluster") lying between a more distant source such as a [quasar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasar "Quasar") and an observer. In this case, the galaxy cluster will lens the quasar.

Strong lensing is the observed distortion of background galaxies into arcs when their light passes through such a gravitational lens. It has been observed around many distant clusters including [Abell 1689](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abell_1689 "Abell 1689").[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-71) By measuring the distortion geometry, the mass of the intervening cluster can be obtained. In the [weak](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weak_gravitational_lensing "Weak gravitational lensing") regime, lensing does not distort background galaxies into arcs, causing minute distortions instead. By examining the apparent shear deformation of the adjacent background galaxies, the mean distribution of dark matter can be characterized. The measured mass-to-light ratios correspond to dark matter densities predicted by other large-scale structure measurements.[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-72)[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-73)

### Type Ia supernova distance measurements

Type Ia [supernovae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supernovae "Supernovae") can be used as [standard candles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_candles "Standard candles") to measure extragalactic distances, which can in turn be used to measure how fast the universe has expanded in the past.[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-74) Data indicates the universe is expanding at an accelerating rate, the cause of which is usually ascribed to [dark energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_energy "Dark energy").[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-75) Since observations indicate the universe is almost flat,[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-NASA_Shape-76)[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-Fermi_Flat-77)[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-78) it is expected the total energy density of everything in the universe should sum to 1 (Ω tot ≈ 1). The measured dark energy density is Ω Λ ≈ 0.690; the observed ordinary (baryonic) matter energy density is Ω b ≈ 0.0482 and the energy density of radiation is negligible. This leaves a missing Ω dm ≈ 0.258 which nonetheless behaves like matter (see technical definition section above)– dark matter.[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-planckesa2015-79)

### Redshift-space distortions

Large galaxy [redshift surveys](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift_survey "Redshift survey") may be used to make a three-dimensional map of the galaxy distribution. These maps are slightly distorted because distances are estimated from observed [redshifts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift "Redshift"); the redshift contains a contribution from the galaxy's so-called peculiar velocity in addition to the dominant Hubble expansion term. On average, superclusters are expanding more slowly than the cosmic mean due to their gravity, while voids are expanding faster than average. In a redshift map, galaxies in front of a supercluster have excess radial velocities towards it and have redshifts slightly higher than their distance would imply, while galaxies behind the supercluster have redshifts slightly low for their distance. This effect causes superclusters to appear squashed in the radial direction, and likewise voids are stretched. Their angular positions are unaffected. This effect is not detectable for any one structure since the true shape is not known, but can be measured by averaging over many structures. It was predicted quantitatively by Nick Kaiser in 1987, and first decisively measured in 2001 by the [2dF Galaxy Redshift Survey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2dF_Galaxy_Redshift_Survey "2dF Galaxy Redshift Survey").[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-80) Results are in agreement with the [Lambda-CDM model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lambda-CDM_model "Lambda-CDM model").

### Lyman-alpha forest

In [astronomical spectroscopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_spectroscopy "Astronomical spectroscopy"), the Lyman-alpha forest is the sum of the [absorption lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectral_line "Spectral line") arising from the [Lyman-alpha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lyman_series "Lyman series") transition of [neutral hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_line "Hydrogen line") in the spectra of distant [galaxies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy "Galaxy") and [quasars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasar "Quasar"). Lyman-alpha forest observations can also constrain cosmological models.[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-81) These constraints agree with those obtained from WMAP data.

### Cosmic microwave background

Although both dark matter and ordinary matter are matter, they do not behave in the same way. In particular, in the early universe, ordinary matter was ionized and interacted strongly with radiation via [Thomson scattering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomson_scattering "Thomson scattering"). Dark matter does not interact directly with radiation, but it does affect the cosmic microwave background (CMB) by its gravitational potential (mainly on large scales) and by its effects on the density and velocity of ordinary matter. Ordinary and dark matter perturbations, therefore, evolve differently with time and leave different imprints on the CMB.

The CMB is very close to a perfect blackbody but contains very small temperature anisotropies of a few parts in 100,000. A sky map of anisotropies can be decomposed into an angular power spectrum, which is observed to contain a series of acoustic peaks at near-equal spacing but different heights. The locations of these peaks depend on cosmological parameters. Matching theory to data, therefore, constrains cosmological parameters.[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-Wayne_Hu-82)

The CMB anisotropy was first discovered by [COBE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_Background_Explorer "Cosmic Background Explorer") in 1992, though this had too coarse resolution to detect the acoustic peaks. After the discovery of the first acoustic peak by the balloon-borne [BOOMERanG](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BOOMERanG "BOOMERanG") experiment in 2000, the power spectrum was precisely observed by [WMAP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WMAP "WMAP") in 2003–2012, and even more precisely by the [_Planck_ spacecraft](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_spacecraft "Planck spacecraft") in 2013–2015. The results support the Lambda-CDM model.[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-Hinshaw2009-83)[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-Planck15-84)

The observed CMB angular power spectrum provides powerful evidence in support of dark matter, as its precise structure is well fitted by the [Lambda-CDM model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lambda-CDM_model "Lambda-CDM model"),[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-Planck15-84) but difficult to reproduce with any competing model such as [modified Newtonian dynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modified_Newtonian_dynamics "Modified Newtonian dynamics") (MOND).[[82]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-85)

### Structure formation

[![Image 1](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Dark_matter_map_of_KiDS_survey_region_%28region_G12%29.jpg/250px-Dark_matter_map_of_KiDS_survey_region_%28region_G12%29.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Dark_matter_map_of_KiDS_survey_region_(region_G12).jpg)

Dark matter map for a patch of sky based on gravitational lensing analysis of a Kilo-Degree Survey[[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-86)

Structure formation refers to the period after the [Big Bang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang "Big Bang") when density perturbations collapsed to form stars, galaxies, and clusters. Prior to structure formation, the [Friedmann solutions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/FRW_metric "FRW metric") to general relativity describe a homogeneous universe. Later, small [anisotropies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anisotropy "Anisotropy") gradually grew and condensed the homogeneous universe into stars, galaxies and larger structures. Ordinary matter is affected by radiation, which is the dominant element of the universe at very early times. As a result, its density perturbations are washed out and unable to condense into structure.[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-Jaffe-87) If there were only ordinary matter in the universe, there would not have been enough time for density perturbations to grow into the galaxies and clusters currently seen.

Dark matter provides a solution to this problem because it is unaffected by radiation. Therefore, its density perturbations can grow first. The resulting gravitational potential acts as an attractive [potential well](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potential_well "Potential well") for ordinary matter collapsing later, speeding up the structure formation process.[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-Jaffe-87)[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-88)

### Sky surveys and baryon acoustic oscillations

Baryon acoustic oscillations (BAO) are fluctuations in the density of the visible baryonic matter (normal matter) of the universe on large scales. These are predicted to arise in the Lambda-CDM model due to acoustic oscillations in the photon–baryon fluid of the early universe and can be observed in the cosmic microwave background angular power spectrum. BAOs set up a preferred length scale for baryons. As the dark matter and baryons clumped together after recombination, the effect is much weaker in the galaxy distribution in the nearby universe, but is detectable as a subtle (~1%) preference for pairs of galaxies to be separated by 147 Mpc, compared to those separated by 130–160 Mpc. This feature was predicted theoretically in the 1990s and then discovered in 2005, in two large galaxy redshift surveys, the [Sloan Digital Sky Survey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sloan_Digital_Sky_Survey "Sloan Digital Sky Survey") and the [2dF Galaxy Redshift Survey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2dF_Galaxy_Redshift_Survey "2dF Galaxy Redshift Survey").[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-89) Combining the CMB observations with BAO measurements from galaxy [redshift surveys](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift_survey "Redshift survey") provides a precise estimate of the [Hubble constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble%27s_law "Hubble's law") and the average matter density in the Universe.[[87]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-Komatsu2009-90) The results support the Lambda-CDM model.

Theoretical classifications
---------------------------

Dark matter can be divided into _cold_, _warm_, and _hot_ categories.[[88]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-91) These categories refer to velocity rather than an actual temperature, and indicate how far corresponding objects moved due to random motions in the early universe, before they slowed due to cosmic expansion. This distance is called the _[free streaming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free\_streaming "Free streaming") length_. The categories of dark matter are set with respect to the size of the collection of mass prior to [structure formation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Structure_formation "Structure formation") that later collapses to form a dwarf galaxy. This collection of mass is sometimes called a [protogalaxy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protogalaxy "Protogalaxy"). Dark matter particles are classified as cold, warm, or hot if their free streaming length is much smaller (cold), similar to (warm), or much larger (hot) than the protogalaxy of a dwarf galaxy.[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-92)[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-93)[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-94) Mixtures of the above are also possible: a theory of [mixed dark matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mixed_dark_matter "Mixed dark matter") was popular in the mid-1990s, but was rejected following the discovery of [dark energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_energy "Dark energy").[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

The significance of the free streaming length is that the universe began with some primordial density fluctuations from the Big Bang (in turn arising from quantum fluctuations at the microscale). Particles from overdense regions will naturally spread to underdense regions, but because the universe is expanding quickly, there is a time limit for them to do so. Faster particles (hot dark matter) can beat the time limit while slower particles cannot. The particles travel a free streaming length's worth of distance within the time limit; therefore this length sets a minimum scale for later structure formation. Because galaxy-size density fluctuations get washed out by free-streaming, hot dark matter implies the first objects that can form are huge supercluster-size pancakes, which then fragment into galaxies, while the reverse is true for cold dark matter.

[Deep-field observations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_deep_fields "List of deep fields") show that galaxies formed first, followed by clusters and superclusters as galaxies clump together,[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-bertone_hooper_silk-56) and therefore that most dark matter is cold. This is also the reason why [neutrinos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutrinos "Neutrinos"), which move at nearly the speed of light and therefore would fall under hot dark matter, cannot make up the bulk of dark matter.[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-Jaffe-87)

### Baryonic matter

Dark matter can refer to any substance which interacts predominantly via gravity with visible matter (e.g., stars and planets). Hence in principle it need not be composed of a new type of fundamental particle but could, at least in part, be made up of standard [baryonic matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baryon "Baryon"), such as protons or neutrons. Most of the ordinary matter familiar to astronomers, including planets, brown dwarfs, red dwarfs, visible stars, white dwarfs, neutron stars, and black holes, fall into this category.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-GianfracoHooperHistory-22)[[106]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-BaryonicSource01-110) A black hole would ingest both baryonic and non-baryonic matter that comes close enough to its event horizon; afterwards, the distinction between the two is lost.[[107]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-111)

These massive objects that are hard to detect are collectively known as [MACHOs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Massive_compact_halo_object "Massive compact halo object"). Some scientists initially hoped that baryonic MACHOs could account for and explain all the dark matter.[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-Randall_2015-53): 286 [[108]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-112)

However, multiple lines of evidence suggest the majority of dark matter is not baryonic:

*   Sufficient diffuse, baryonic gas or dust would be visible when backlit by stars.
*   The theory of [Big Bang nucleosynthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_nucleosynthesis "Big Bang nucleosynthesis") predicts the observed [abundance of the chemical elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abundance_of_the_chemical_elements "Abundance of the chemical elements"). If there are more baryons, then there should also be more helium, lithium and heavier elements synthesized during the Big Bang.[[109]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-113)[[110]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-114) Agreement with observed abundances requires that baryonic matter makes up between 4–5% of the universe's [critical density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedmann_equations#Density_parameter "Friedmann equations"). In contrast, [large-scale structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Large-scale_structure_of_the_universe "Large-scale structure of the universe") and other observations indicate that the total matter density is about 30% of the critical density.[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-planckesa2015-79)
*   Astronomical searches for [gravitational microlensing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_microlensing "Gravitational microlensing") in the [Milky Way](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milky_Way "Milky Way") found at most only a small fraction of the dark matter may be in dark, compact, conventional objects (MACHOs, etc.); the excluded range of object masses is from half the Earth's mass up to 30 solar masses, which covers nearly all the plausible candidates.[[111]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-115)[[112]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-116)[[113]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-117)[[114]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-118)[[115]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-119)[[116]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-120)
*   Detailed analysis of the small irregularities (anisotropies) in the [cosmic microwave background](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background "Cosmic microwave background") by [WMAP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WMAP "WMAP") and [Planck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_(spacecraft) "Planck (spacecraft)") indicate that around five-sixths of the total matter is in a form that only interacts significantly with ordinary matter or [photons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon "Photon") through gravitational effects.[[117]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-121)

### Non-baryonic matter

There are two main candidates for non-baryonic dark matter: new particles and [primordial black holes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primordial_black_hole "Primordial black hole").

Unlike baryonic matter, nonbaryonic particles do not contribute to the formation of the [elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_element "Chemical element") in the early universe ([Big Bang nucleosynthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_nucleosynthesis "Big Bang nucleosynthesis"))[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-Copi_1995-54) and so its presence is felt only via its gravitational effects (such as [weak lensing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weak_lensing "Weak lensing")). In addition, some dark matter candidates can interact with themselves ([self-interacting dark matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-interacting_dark_matter "Self-interacting dark matter")) or with ordinary particles (e.g. [WIMPs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weakly_Interacting_Massive_Particle "Weakly Interacting Massive Particle") or Weakly Interacting Massive Particles), possibly resulting in observable by-products such as [gamma rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamma_rays "Gamma rays") and neutrinos (indirect detection).[[92]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-bertone_merritt-95) Candidates abound (see the table above), each with their own strengths and weaknesses.

#### Undiscovered massive particles

There exists no formal definition of a Weakly Interacting Massive Particle, but broadly, it is an [elementary particle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementary_particle "Elementary particle") which interacts via [gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity "Gravity") and any other force (or forces) which is as weak as or weaker than the [weak nuclear force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weak_nuclear_force "Weak nuclear force"), but also non-vanishing in strength. Many WIMP candidates are expected to have been produced thermally in the early Universe, similarly to the particles of the Standard Model[[118]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-122) according to [Big Bang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang "Big Bang") cosmology, and usually will constitute [cold dark matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cold_dark_matter "Cold dark matter"). Obtaining the correct abundance of dark matter today via [thermal production](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Thermal_production&action=edit&redlink=1 "Thermal production (page does not exist)") requires a self-[annihilation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Annihilation "Annihilation")[cross section](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_section_(physics) "Cross section (physics)") of ![Image 3: {\displaystyle \langle \sigma v\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c32624c6fcf974ef4aa9bfc7a9e691757fbd523f) ≃ 3×10−26 cm 3⋅s−1, which is roughly what is expected for a new particle in the 100[GeV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GeV "GeV")/_c_ 2 mass range that interacts via the [electroweak force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electroweak_force "Electroweak force").

Because [supersymmetric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supersymmetry "Supersymmetry") extensions of the Standard Model of particle physics readily predict a new particle with these properties, this apparent coincidence is known as the "**WIMP miracle**", and a stable supersymmetric partner has long been a prime explanation for dark matter.[[119]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-123) Experimental efforts to detect WIMPs include the search for products of WIMP annihilation, including [gamma rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamma_ray "Gamma ray"), [neutrinos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutrino "Neutrino") and [cosmic rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_ray "Cosmic ray") in nearby galaxies and galaxy clusters; direct detection experiments designed to measure the collision of WIMPs with [nuclei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_nucleus "Atomic nucleus") in the laboratory, as well as attempts to directly produce WIMPs in colliders, such as the [Large Hadron Collider](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Large_Hadron_Collider "Large Hadron Collider") at [CERN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CERN "CERN").

In the early 2010s, results from [direct-detection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#Direct_detection) experiments along with the lack of evidence for supersymmetry at the [Large Hadron Collider](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Large_Hadron_Collider "Large Hadron Collider") (LHC) experiment[[120]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-124)[[121]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-125) have cast doubt on the simplest WIMP hypothesis.[[122]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-126)

#### Undiscovered ultralight particles

Axions are hypothetical elementary particles originally theorized in 1978 independently by [Frank Wilczek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frank_Wilczek "Frank Wilczek") and [Steven Weinberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steven_Weinberg "Steven Weinberg") as the [Goldstone boson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goldstone_boson "Goldstone boson") of [Peccei–Quinn theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peccei%E2%80%93Quinn_theory "Peccei–Quinn theory"), which had been proposed in 1977 to solve the [strong CP problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strong_CP_problem "Strong CP problem") in [quantum chromodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_chromodynamics "Quantum chromodynamics") (QCD). QCD effects produce an effective periodic potential in which the axion field moves.[[123]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-peccei2008-127) Expanding the potential about one of its minima, one finds that the product of the axion mass with the axion decay constant is determined by the topological susceptibility of the QCD vacuum. An axion with mass that is much less than 60 keV/_c_ 2 is long-lived and weakly interacting: a perfect dark matter candidate.

The oscillations of the axion field about the minimum of the effective potential, the so-called misalignment mechanism, generate a cosmological population of cold axions with an abundance depending on the mass of the axion.[[124]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-auto-128)[[125]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-A_cosmological_bound_on_the_invisib-129)[[126]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-The_not-so-harmless_axion-130) With a mass above 5[μeV/c 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron-volt "Electron-volt") (10−11 times the [electron mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_mass "Electron mass")) axions could account for dark matter, and thus be both a dark-matter candidate and a solution to the strong CP problem. If inflation occurs at a low scale and lasts sufficiently long, the axion mass can be as low as 1 peV/_c_ 2.[[127]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-131)[[128]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-132)[[129]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-133)

Because axions have extremely low mass, their [de Broglie wavelength](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_Broglie_wavelength "De Broglie wavelength") is very large, in turn meaning that quantum effects could help resolve the small-scale problems of the [Lambda-CDM](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lambda-CDM "Lambda-CDM") model. A single ultralight axion with a decay constant at the [grand unified theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grand_unified_theory "Grand unified theory") scale provides the correct relic density without fine-tuning.[[130]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-134)

Axions as a dark matter candidate have gained in popularity in recent years, because of the non-detection of WIMPS.[[131]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-135)

#### Primordial black holes

Primordial black holes are hypothetical [black holes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_hole "Black hole") that formed soon after the [Big Bang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang "Big Bang"). In the [inflationary era](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inflationary_era "Inflationary era") and early [radiation-dominated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scale_factor_(cosmology)#Radiation-dominated_era "Scale factor (cosmology)") universe, extremely dense pockets of [subatomic matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subatomic_particle "Subatomic particle") may have been tightly packed to the point of [gravitational collapse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_collapse "Gravitational collapse"), creating primordial black holes without the [supernova](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supernova "Supernova") compression typically needed to make black holes today. Because the creation of primordial black holes would pre-date the first stars, they are not limited to the narrow mass range of [stellar black holes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_black_hole "Stellar black hole") and also not classified as baryonic dark matter.

The idea that black holes could form in the early universe was first suggested by [Yakov Zeldovich](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yakov_Zeldovich "Yakov Zeldovich") and [Igor Dmitriyevich Novikov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Igor_Dmitriyevich_Novikov "Igor Dmitriyevich Novikov") in 1967, and independently by [Stephen Hawking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stephen_Hawking "Stephen Hawking") in 1971. It quickly became clear that such black holes might account for at least part of dark matter. Primordial black holes as a dark matter candidate has the major advantage that it is based on a well-understood theory ([General Relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Relativity "General Relativity")) and objects ([black holes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_holes "Black holes")) that are already known to exist. However, producing primordial black holes requires exotic [cosmic inflation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_inflation "Cosmic inflation") or physics beyond the [standard model of particle physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_model_of_particle_physics "Standard model of particle physics"),[[132]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-136) and might also require fine-tuning.[[133]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-137) Primordial black holes can also span nearly the entire possible mass range, from atom-sized to supermassive.

The idea that primordial black holes make up dark matter gained prominence in 2015[[134]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-138) following results of [gravitational wave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_wave "Gravitational wave") measurements which detected the merger of intermediate-mass black holes. Black holes with about 30 solar masses are not predicted to form by either stellar collapse (typically less than 15 solar masses) or by the merger of black holes in galactic centers (millions or billions of solar masses), which suggests that the detected black holes might be primordial. A later survey of about a thousand supernovae detected no gravitational lensing events, when about eight would be expected if intermediate-mass primordial black holes above a certain mass range accounted for over 60% of dark matter.[[135]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-139) However, that study assumed that all black holes have the same or similar mass to the LIGO/Virgo mass range, which might not be the case (as suggested by subsequent [James Webb Space Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Webb_Space_Telescope "James Webb Space Telescope") observations).[[136]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-140)[[99]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-jwst-103)

The possibility that atom-sized primordial black holes account for a significant fraction of dark matter was ruled out by measurements of positron and electron fluxes outside the Sun's heliosphere by the _[Voyager 1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voyager\_1 "Voyager 1")_ spacecraft. Tiny black holes are theorized to emit [Hawking radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hawking_radiation "Hawking radiation"). However the detected fluxes were too low and did not have the expected energy spectrum, suggesting that tiny primordial black holes are not widespread enough to account for dark matter.[[137]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-141) Nonetheless, research and theories proposing dense dark matter accounts for dark matter continue as of 2018, including approaches to dark matter cooling,[[138]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-142)[[139]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-143) and the question remains unsettled. In 2019, the lack of microlensing effects in the observation of Andromeda suggests that tiny black holes do not exist.[[140]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-144)

Nonetheless, there still exists a largely unconstrained mass range smaller than that which can be limited by optical microlensing observations, where primordial black holes may account for all dark matter.[[141]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-145)[[142]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-146)

### Modified gravity

The last major possibility is that general relativity, the theory underpinning modern cosmology, is incorrect. General relativity is well-tested on Solar System scales, but its validity on galactic or cosmological scales has not been well proven.[[143]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-147) A suitable modification to general relativity can conceivably eliminate the need for dark matter. The best-known theories of this class are [MOND](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modified_Newtonian_dynamics "Modified Newtonian dynamics") and its relativistic generalization [tensor–vector–scalar gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor%E2%80%93vector%E2%80%93scalar_gravity "Tensor–vector–scalar gravity") (TeVeS),[[144]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-148)[f(R) gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/F(R)_gravity "F(R) gravity"),[[145]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-149)[negative mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Negative_mass "Negative mass"), [dark fluid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_fluid "Dark fluid"),[[146]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-150)[[147]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-151)[[148]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-Farnes-152) and [entropic gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropic_gravity "Entropic gravity").[[149]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-physorgnewtheory-153)[Alternative theories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alternatives_to_general_relativity "Alternatives to general relativity") abound.[[150]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-154)[[151]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-155)

A problem with modifying gravity is that observational evidence for dark matter – let alone general relativity – comes from so many independent approaches (see the "observational evidence" section above). Explaining any individual observation is possible but explaining all of them in the absence of dark matter is very difficult. Nonetheless, there have been some scattered successes for alternative hypotheses, such as a 2016 test of gravitational lensing in entropic gravity[[152]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-156)[[153]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-157)[[154]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-158) and a 2020 measurement of a unique MOND effect.[[155]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-159)[[156]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-160)

The prevailing opinion among most astrophysicists is that while modifications to general relativity can conceivably explain part of the observational evidence, there is probably enough data to conclude there must be some form of dark matter present in the universe.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-CarrollTrialogue-21)

Dark matter aggregation and dense dark matter objects
-----------------------------------------------------

If dark matter is composed of weakly interacting particles, then an obvious question is whether it can form objects equivalent to [planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planet "Planet"), [stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star "Star"), or [black holes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_hole "Black hole"). Historically, the answer has been it cannot,[[e]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-162)[[157]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-curio-161)[[158]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-cornell_ask-163)[[159]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-siegel-164) because of two factors:

It lacks an efficient means to lose energy[[157]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-curio-161)Ordinary matter forms dense objects because it has numerous ways to lose energy. Losing energy would be essential for object formation, because a particle that gains energy during compaction or falling "inward" under gravity, and cannot lose it any other way, will heat up and increase [velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity "Velocity") and [momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum "Momentum"). Dark matter appears to lack a means to lose energy, simply because it is not capable of interacting strongly in other ways except through gravity. The [virial theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virial_theorem "Virial theorem") suggests that such a particle would not stay bound to the gradually forming object – as the object began to form and compact, the dark matter particles within it would speed up and tend to escape.It lacks a diversity of interactions needed to form structures[[159]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-siegel-164)Ordinary matter interacts in many different ways, which allows the matter to form more complex structures. For example, stars form through gravity, but the particles within them interact and can emit energy in the form of [neutrinos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutrino "Neutrino") and [electromagnetic radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation "Electromagnetic radiation") through [fusion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_fusion "Nuclear fusion") when they become energetic enough. [Protons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton "Proton") and [neutrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron "Neutron") can bind via the [strong interaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strong_interaction "Strong interaction") and then form [atoms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atom "Atom") with [electrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron "Electron") largely through [electromagnetic interaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_interaction "Electromagnetic interaction"). There is no evidence that dark matter is capable of such a wide variety of interactions, since it seems to only interact through gravity (and possibly through some means no stronger than the [weak interaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weak_interaction "Weak interaction"), although until dark matter is better understood, this is only speculation).
Detection of dark matter particles
----------------------------------

If dark matter is made up of subatomic particles, then millions, possibly billions, of such particles must pass through every square centimeter of the Earth each second.[[160]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-Gaitskell_2004-165)[[161]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-Number_per_second-166) Many experiments aim to test this hypothesis. Although WIMPs have been the main search candidates,[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-bertone_hooper_silk-56)[axions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axion "Axion") have drawn renewed attention, with the [Axion Dark Matter Experiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axion_Dark_Matter_Experiment "Axion Dark Matter Experiment") (ADMX) searches for axions and many more planned in the future.[[162]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-Chadha-Day_et_al-167) Another candidate is heavy [hidden sector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hidden_sector "Hidden sector") particles which only interact with ordinary matter via gravity.

These experiments can be divided into two classes: direct detection experiments, which search for the scattering of dark matter particles off atomic nuclei within a detector; and indirect detection, which look for the products of dark matter particle annihilations or decays.[[92]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-bertone_merritt-95)

### Direct detection

Direct detection experiments aim to observe low-energy recoils of nuclei (typically a few [keV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/KeV "KeV")) induced by interactions with particles of dark matter, which (in theory) are passing through the Earth. After such a recoil, the nucleus will emit energy in the form of [scintillation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scintillation_(physics) "Scintillation (physics)") light or [phonons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phonon "Phonon") as they pass through sensitive detection apparatus. To do so effectively, it is crucial to maintain an extremely low background, which is the reason why such experiments typically operate deep underground, where interference from [cosmic rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_ray "Cosmic ray") is minimized. Examples of underground laboratories with direct detection experiments include the [Stawell mine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stawell_Underground_Physics_Laboratory "Stawell Underground Physics Laboratory"), the [Soudan mine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soudan_mine "Soudan mine"), the [SNOLAB](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SNOLAB "SNOLAB") underground laboratory at [Sudbury](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greater_Sudbury "Greater Sudbury"), the [Gran Sasso National Laboratory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gran_Sasso_National_Laboratory "Gran Sasso National Laboratory"), the [Canfranc Underground Laboratory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canfranc_Underground_Laboratory "Canfranc Underground Laboratory"), the [Boulby Underground Laboratory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boulby_Underground_Laboratory "Boulby Underground Laboratory"), the [Deep Underground Science and Engineering Laboratory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deep_Underground_Science_and_Engineering_Laboratory "Deep Underground Science and Engineering Laboratory") and the [China Jinping Underground Laboratory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_Jinping_Underground_Laboratory "China Jinping Underground Laboratory").

These experiments mostly use either cryogenic or noble liquid detector technologies. Cryogenic detectors operating at temperatures below 100 mK, detect the heat produced when a particle hits an atom in a crystal absorber such as [germanium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germanium "Germanium"). [Noble liquid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noble_gas "Noble gas") detectors detect [scintillation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scintillation_(physics) "Scintillation (physics)") produced by a particle collision in liquid [xenon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xenon "Xenon") or [argon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Argon "Argon"). Cryogenic detector experiments include such projects as [CDMS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cryogenic_Dark_Matter_Search "Cryogenic Dark Matter Search"), [CRESST](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cryogenic_Rare_Event_Search_with_Superconducting_Thermometers "Cryogenic Rare Event Search with Superconducting Thermometers"), [EDELWEISS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/EDELWEISS "EDELWEISS"), and [EURECA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Underground_Rare_Event_Calorimeter_Array "European Underground Rare Event Calorimeter Array"), while noble liquid experiments include [LZ](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LZ_experiment "LZ experiment"), [XENON](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/XENON "XENON"), [DEAP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DEAP "DEAP"), [ArDM](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ArDM "ArDM"), [WARP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WIMP_Argon_Programme "WIMP Argon Programme"), [DarkSide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DarkSide_(dark_matter_experiment) "DarkSide (dark matter experiment)"), [PandaX](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PandaX "PandaX"), and LUX, the [Large Underground Xenon experiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Large_Underground_Xenon_experiment "Large Underground Xenon experiment"). Both of these techniques focus strongly on their ability to distinguish background particles (which predominantly scatter off electrons) from dark matter particles (that scatter off nuclei). Other experiments include [SIMPLE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SIMPLE_(dark_matter) "SIMPLE (dark matter)") and [PICASSO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PICASSO_(dark_matter) "PICASSO (dark matter)"), which use alternative methods in their attempts to detect dark matter.

Currently there has been no well-established claim of dark matter detection from a direct detection experiment, leading instead to strong upper limits on the mass and interaction cross section with nucleons of such dark matter particles.[[163]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-168) The [DAMA/NaI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DAMA/NaI "DAMA/NaI") and more recent [DAMA/LIBRA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DAMA/LIBRA "DAMA/LIBRA") experimental collaborations have detected an annual modulation in the rate of events in their detectors,[[164]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-169)[[165]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-170) which they claim is due to dark matter. This results from the expectation that as the Earth orbits the Sun, the velocity of the detector relative to the [dark matter halo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter_halo "Dark matter halo") will vary by a small amount. This claim is so far unconfirmed and in contradiction with negative results from other experiments such as LUX, SuperCDMS[[166]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-171) and XENON100.[[167]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-172)

A special case of direct detection experiments covers those with directional sensitivity. This is a search strategy based on the motion of the Solar System around the [Galactic Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galactic_Center "Galactic Center").[[168]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-apssyn-173)[[169]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-samlee-174)[[170]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-dmgsheff-175)[[171]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-Kavli-176) A low-pressure [time projection chamber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_projection_chamber "Time projection chamber") makes it possible to access information on recoiling tracks and constrain WIMP-nucleus kinematics. WIMPs coming from the direction in which the Sun travels (approximately towards [Cygnus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cygnus_(constellation) "Cygnus (constellation)")) may then be separated from background, which should be isotropic. Directional dark matter experiments include [DMTPC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_Matter_Time_Projection_Chamber "Dark Matter Time Projection Chamber"), [DRIFT](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Directional_Recoil_Identification_From_Tracks "Directional Recoil Identification From Tracks"), Newage and MIMAC.

### Indirect detection

[![Image 4](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Collage_of_six_cluster_collisions_with_dark_matter_maps.jpg/500px-Collage_of_six_cluster_collisions_with_dark_matter_maps.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Collage_of_six_cluster_collisions_with_dark_matter_maps.jpg)

Collage of six cluster collisions with dark matter maps. The clusters were observed in a study of how dark matter in clusters of galaxies behaves when the clusters collide.[[172]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-177)

Video about the potential [gamma-ray detection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamma-ray_astronomy "Gamma-ray astronomy") of dark matter [annihilation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Annihilation "Annihilation") around [supermassive black holes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supermassive_black_hole "Supermassive black hole"). _(Duration 0:03:13, also see file description.)_

Indirect detection experiments search for the products of the self-annihilation or decay of dark matter particles in outer space. For example, in regions of high dark matter density (e.g., the [centre of the Milky Way](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galactic_Center "Galactic Center")) two dark matter particles could [annihilate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Annihilation "Annihilation") to produce [gamma rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamma_ray "Gamma ray") or Standard Model particle–antiparticle pairs.[[173]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-Bertone2010-178) Alternatively, if a dark matter particle is unstable, it could decay into Standard Model (or other) particles. These processes could be detected indirectly through an excess of gamma rays, [antiprotons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiproton "Antiproton") or [positrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positron "Positron") emanating from high density regions in the Milky Way and other galaxies.[[174]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-179) A major difficulty inherent in such searches is that various astrophysical sources can mimic the signal expected from dark matter, and so multiple signals are likely required for a conclusive discovery.[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-bertone_hooper_silk-56)[[92]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-bertone_merritt-95)

A few of the dark matter particles passing through the Sun or Earth may scatter off atoms and lose energy. Thus dark matter may accumulate at the center of these bodies, increasing the chance of collision/annihilation. This could produce a distinctive signal in the form of high-energy [neutrinos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutrino "Neutrino").[[175]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-180) Such a signal would be strong indirect proof of WIMP dark matter.[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-bertone_hooper_silk-56) High-energy neutrino telescopes such as [AMANDA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antarctic_Muon_And_Neutrino_Detector_Array "Antarctic Muon And Neutrino Detector Array"), [IceCube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IceCube "IceCube") and [ANTARES](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ANTARES_(telescope) "ANTARES (telescope)") are searching for this signal.[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-Randall_2015-53): 298  The detection by [LIGO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LIGO "LIGO") in [September 2015](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GW150914 "GW150914") of gravitational waves opens the possibility of observing dark matter in a new way, particularly if it is in the form of [primordial black holes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primordial_black_hole "Primordial black hole").[[176]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-181)[[177]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-182)[[178]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-183)

Many experimental searches have been undertaken to look for such emission from dark matter annihilation or decay, examples of which follow.

The [Energetic Gamma Ray Experiment Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energetic_Gamma_Ray_Experiment_Telescope "Energetic Gamma Ray Experiment Telescope") observed more gamma rays in 2008 than expected from the [Milky Way](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milky_Way "Milky Way"), but scientists concluded this was most likely due to incorrect estimation of the telescope's sensitivity.[[179]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-184)

The [Fermi Gamma-ray Space Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermi_Gamma-ray_Space_Telescope "Fermi Gamma-ray Space Telescope") is searching for similar gamma rays.[[180]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-185) In 2009, an as yet unexplained surplus of gamma rays from the Milky Way's galactic center was found in Fermi data. This [Galactic Center GeV excess](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galactic_Center_GeV_excess "Galactic Center GeV excess") might be due to dark matter annihilation or to a population of pulsars.[[181]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-186) In April 2012, an analysis of previously available data from Fermi's [Large Area Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermi_LAT "Fermi LAT") instrument produced statistical evidence of a 130 GeV signal in the gamma radiation coming from the center of the Milky Way.[[182]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-187) WIMP annihilation was seen as the most probable explanation.[[183]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-188)

At higher energies, [ground-based gamma-ray telescopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IACT "IACT") have set limits on the annihilation of dark matter in [dwarf spheroidal galaxies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dwarf_spheroidal_galaxy "Dwarf spheroidal galaxy")[[184]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-189) and in clusters of galaxies.[[185]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-190)

The [PAMELA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Payload_for_Antimatter_Matter_Exploration_and_Light-nuclei_Astrophysics "Payload for Antimatter Matter Exploration and Light-nuclei Astrophysics") experiment (launched in 2006) detected excess [positrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positron "Positron"). They could be from dark matter annihilation or from [pulsars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulsar "Pulsar"). No excess [antiprotons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiproton "Antiproton") were observed.[[186]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-191)

In 2013, results from the [Alpha Magnetic Spectrometer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Magnetic_Spectrometer "Alpha Magnetic Spectrometer") on the [International Space Station](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Space_Station "International Space Station") indicated excess high-energy [cosmic rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_ray "Cosmic ray") which could be due to dark matter annihilation.[[187]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-APS-20130403-192)[[188]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-AMS-20130403-193)[[189]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-AP-20130403-194)[[190]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-BBC-20130403-195)[[191]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-NASA-20130403-196)[[192]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-NYT-20130403-197)

### Collider searches for dark matter

An alternative approach to the detection of dark matter particles in nature is to produce them in a laboratory. Experiments with the [Large Hadron Collider](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Large_Hadron_Collider "Large Hadron Collider") (LHC) may be able to detect dark matter particles produced in collisions of the LHC [proton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton "Proton") beams. Because a dark matter particle should have negligible interactions with normal visible matter, it may be detected indirectly as (large amounts of) missing energy and momentum that escape the detectors, provided other (non-negligible) collision products are detected.[[193]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-kane_watson-198) Constraints on dark matter also exist from the [LEP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Large_Electron%E2%80%93Positron_Collider "Large Electron–Positron Collider") experiment using a similar principle, but probing the interaction of dark matter particles with electrons rather than quarks.[[194]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-199) Any discovery from collider searches must be corroborated by discoveries in the indirect or direct detection sectors to prove that the particle discovered is, in fact, dark matter.

In popular culture
------------------

Dark matter regularly appears as a topic in hybrid periodicals that cover both factual scientific topics and science fiction,[[195]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-200) and dark matter itself has been referred to as "the stuff of science fiction".[[196]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-201)

Mention of dark matter is made in works of fiction. In such cases, it is usually attributed extraordinary physical or magical properties, thus becoming inconsistent with the hypothesized properties of dark matter in physics and cosmology. For example:

*   Dark matter serves as a plot device in the 1995 _[X-Files](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The\_X-Files "The X-Files")_ episode "[Soft Light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soft_Light_(The_X-Files) "Soft Light (The X-Files)")".[[197]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-202)
*   A dark-matter-inspired substance known as _"Dust"_ features prominently in [Philip Pullman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philip_Pullman "Philip Pullman")'s _[His Dark Materials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/His\_Dark\_Materials "His Dark Materials")_ trilogy.[[198]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-203)
*   Beings made of dark matter are antagonists in [Stephen Baxter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stephen_Baxter_(author) "Stephen Baxter (author)")'s _[Xeelee Sequence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xeelee\_Sequence "Xeelee Sequence")_.[[199]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-204)

More broadly, the phrase "dark matter" is used metaphorically in fiction to evoke the unseen or invisible.[[200]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter#cite_note-205)

Related theories

*   [Conformal gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conformal_gravity "Conformal gravity")– Gravity theories that are invariant under Weyl transformations
*   [Density wave theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Density_wave_theory "Density wave theory") – A theory in which waves of compressed gas, which move slower than the galaxy, maintain galaxy's structure
*   [Entropic gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropic_gravity "Entropic gravity")– Theory in modern physics that describes gravity as an entropic force
*   [Dark radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_radiation "Dark radiation")– Postulated type of radiation that mediates interactions of dark matter
*   [Massive gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Massive_gravity "Massive gravity")– Theory of gravity in which the graviton has nonzero mass
*   [Unparticle physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unparticle_physics "Unparticle physics")– Speculative theory of non-particle matter

Experiments
*   [DEAP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DEAP "DEAP")– Dark matter search experiment, a search apparatus
*   [LZ experiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LZ_experiment "LZ experiment"), large underground dark matter detector
*   [Dark Matter Particle Explorer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_Matter_Particle_Explorer "Dark Matter Particle Explorer") (DAMPE)– Chinese science satellite, a space mission
*   [General antiparticle spectrometer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_antiparticle_spectrometer "General antiparticle spectrometer")
*   [MultiDark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MultiDark "MultiDark"), a research program
*   [Illustris project](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Illustris_project "Illustris project")– Computer-simulated universes, astrophysical simulations
*   [Future Circular Collider](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Future_Circular_Collider "Future Circular Collider")– Proposed particle accelerator, a particle accelerator research infrastructure

Dark matter candidates
*   [Feebly Interacting Particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feebly_Interacting_Particles "Feebly Interacting Particles")
*   [Light dark matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_dark_matter "Light dark matter")– Dark matter weakly interacting massive particles candidates with masses less than 1 GeV
*   [Mirror matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mirror_matter "Mirror matter")– Hypothetical counterpart to ordinary matter
*   [Exotic matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exotic_matter "Exotic matter")– Physics term for multiple concepts
*   [Neutralino](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutralino#Relationship_to_dark_matter "Neutralino")– Neutral mass eigenstate formed from superpartners of gauge and Higgs bosons
*   [Dark galaxy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_galaxy "Dark galaxy")– Hypothesized galaxy with no, or very few, stars
*   [Scalar field dark matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_field_dark_matter "Scalar field dark matter")– Classical, minimally coupled, scalar field postulated to account for the inferred dark matter
*   [Self-interacting dark matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-interacting_dark_matter "Self-interacting dark matter")– Hypothetical form of dark matter consisting of particles with strong self-interactions
*   [Weakly interacting massive particle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weakly_interacting_massive_particle "Weakly interacting massive particle") (WIMP)– Hypothetical particles that may constitute dark matter
*   [Weakly interacting slim particle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WISP_(particle_physics) "WISP (particle physics)") (WISP)– Low-mass counterpart to WIMP
*   [Strongly interacting massive particle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strongly_interacting_massive_particle "Strongly interacting massive particle") (SIMP)– Hypothetical particle
*   [Chameleon particle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chameleon_particle "Chameleon particle")– Hypothetical scalar particle that couples to matter more weakly than gravity

Other
*   [Galactic Center GeV excess](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galactic_Center_GeV_excess "Galactic Center GeV excess")– Unexplained gamma rays from the Galactic Center
*   [Luminiferous aether](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luminiferous_aether "Luminiferous aether") – A once theorized invisible and infinite material with no interaction with physical objects, used to explain how light could travel through a vacuum (now disproven)
