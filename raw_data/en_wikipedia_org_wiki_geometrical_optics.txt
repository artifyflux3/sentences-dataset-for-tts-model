Title: Geometrical optics

URL Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometrical_optics

Published Time: 2004-06-17T05:21:55Z

Markdown Content:
**Geometrical optics**, or **ray optics**, is a model of [optics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optics "Optics") that describes [light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light "Light")[propagation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wave_propagation "Wave propagation") in terms of _[rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ray\_(optics) "Ray (optics)")_. The ray in geometrical optics is an [abstraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abstract_object "Abstract object") useful for approximating the paths along which light propagates under certain circumstances.

The simplifying assumptions of geometrical optics include that light rays:

*   propagate in straight-line paths as they travel in a [homogeneous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homogeneity_(physics) "Homogeneity (physics)") medium
*   bend, and in particular circumstances may split in two, at the [interface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interface_(matter) "Interface (matter)") between two dissimilar [media](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optical_medium "Optical medium")
*   follow curved paths in a medium in which the [refractive index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Refractive_index "Refractive index") changes
*   may be absorbed or reflected.

Geometrical optics does not account for certain optical effects such as [diffraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffraction "Diffraction") and [interference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interference_(wave_propagation) "Interference (wave propagation)"), which are considered in [physical optics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_optics "Physical optics"). This simplification is useful in practice; it is an excellent approximation when the wavelength is small compared to the size of structures with which the light interacts. The techniques are particularly useful in describing geometrical aspects of [imaging](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Image "Image"), including [optical aberrations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optical_aberration "Optical aberration").

[![Image 1](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Plane_wave_wavefronts_3D.svg/250px-Plane_wave_wavefronts_3D.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Plane_wave_wavefronts_3D.svg)

As light travels through space, it [oscillates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oscillation "Oscillation") in [amplitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amplitude "Amplitude"). In this image, each maximum amplitude [crest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crest_(physics) "Crest (physics)") is marked with a [plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_(geometry) "Plane (geometry)") to illustrate the [wavefront](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wavefront "Wavefront"). The [ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ray_(optics) "Ray (optics)") is the arrow [perpendicular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular "Perpendicular") to these [parallel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_(geometry) "Parallel (geometry)") surfaces.

A light ray is a [line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_(geometry) "Line (geometry)") or [curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curve "Curve") that is [perpendicular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular "Perpendicular") to the light's [wavefronts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wavefront "Wavefront") (and is therefore [collinear](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/collinear "wiktionary:collinear") with the [wave vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wave_vector "Wave vector")). A slightly more rigorous definition of a light ray follows from [Fermat's principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermat%27s_principle "Fermat's principle"), which states that the path taken between two points by a ray of light is the path that can be traversed in the least time.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometrical_optics#cite_note-1)

Geometrical optics is often simplified by making the [paraxial approximation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paraxial_approximation "Paraxial approximation"), or "small angle approximation". The mathematical behavior then becomes [linear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_system "Linear system"), allowing optical components and systems to be described by simple matrices. This leads to the techniques of [Gaussian optics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaussian_optics "Gaussian optics") and _paraxial [ray tracing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ray\_tracing\_(physics) "Ray tracing (physics)")_, which are used to find basic properties of optical systems, such as approximate [image](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Image "Image") and object positions and [magnifications](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnification "Magnification").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometrical_optics#cite_note-Greivenkamp-2)

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Reflection_angles.svg/170px-Reflection_angles.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Reflection_angles.svg)

Diagram of [specular reflection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Specular_reflection "Specular reflection")

Glossy surfaces such as [mirrors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mirror "Mirror") reflect light in a simple, predictable way. This allows for production of reflected images that can be associated with an actual ([real](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_image "Real image")) or extrapolated ([virtual](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virtual_image "Virtual image")) location in space.

With such surfaces, the direction of the reflected ray is determined by the angle the incident ray makes with the [surface normal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_normal "Surface normal"), a line perpendicular to the surface at the point where the ray hits. The incident and reflected rays lie in a single plane, and the angle between the reflected ray and the surface normal is the same as that between the incident ray and the normal.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometrical_optics#cite_note-Geoptics-3) This is known as the [Law of Reflection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law_of_Reflection "Law of Reflection").

For [flat mirrors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_mirror "Plane mirror"), the law of reflection implies that images of objects are upright and the same distance behind the mirror as the objects are in front of the mirror. The image size is the same as the object size. (The [magnification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnification "Magnification") of a flat mirror is equal to one.) The law also implies that [mirror images](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mirror_image "Mirror image") are [parity inverted](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parity_(physics) "Parity (physics)"), which is perceived as a left-right inversion.

[Mirrors with curved surfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curved_mirror "Curved mirror") can be modeled by [ray tracing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ray_tracing_(physics) "Ray tracing (physics)") and using the law of reflection at each point on the surface. For [mirrors with parabolic surfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parabolic_reflector "Parabolic reflector"), parallel rays incident on the mirror produce reflected rays that converge at a common [focus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Focus_(optics) "Focus (optics)"). Other curved surfaces may also focus light, but with aberrations due to the diverging shape causing the focus to be smeared out in space. In particular, spherical mirrors exhibit [spherical aberration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_aberration "Spherical aberration"). Curved mirrors can form images with magnification greater than or less than one, and the image can be upright or inverted. An upright image formed by reflection in a mirror is always virtual, while an inverted image is real and can be projected onto a screen.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometrical_optics#cite_note-Geoptics-3)

[![Image 3](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Snells_law.svg/330px-Snells_law.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Snells_law.svg)

Illustration of Snell's Law

Refraction occurs when light travels through an area of space that has a changing index of refraction. The simplest case of refraction occurs when there is an interface between a uniform medium with index of refraction ![Image 4: {\displaystyle n_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ee784b70e772f55ede5e6e0bdc929994bff63413) and another medium with index of refraction ![Image 5: {\displaystyle n_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/840e456e3058bc0be28e5cf653b170cdbfcc3be4). In such situations, [Snell's Law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snell%27s_Law "Snell's Law") describes the resulting deflection of the light ray: ![Image 6: {\displaystyle n_{1}\sin \theta _{1}=n_{2}\sin \theta _{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b6b2a5ea2d5d1fdf92e3b13e58bd3f91a48236ff) where ![Image 7: {\displaystyle \theta _{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7f84b9443d095623e02fd287cd095123d70b0278) and ![Image 8: {\displaystyle \theta _{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0ed6ea624b20b153403979ffaf5434fc36de2990) are the angles between the normal (to the interface) and the incident and refracted waves, respectively. This phenomenon is also associated with a changing speed of light as seen from the definition of index of refraction provided above which implies: ![Image 9: {\displaystyle v_{1}\sin \theta _{2}\ =v_{2}\sin \theta _{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/67f709b46fbb8d6fd025424b4c93af66f9e2b2ef) where ![Image 10: {\displaystyle v_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/98d33f5d498d528bd8c10edc8ac8c34347f32b3a) and ![Image 11: {\displaystyle v_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eb04c423c2cb809c30cac725befa14ffbf4c85f3) are the wave velocities through the respective media.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometrical_optics#cite_note-Geoptics-3)

Various consequences of Snell's Law include the fact that for light rays traveling from a material with a high index of refraction to a material with a low index of refraction, it is possible for the interaction with the interface to result in zero transmission. This phenomenon is called [total internal reflection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Total_internal_reflection "Total internal reflection") and allows for [fiber optics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiber_optics "Fiber optics") technology. As light signals travel down a fiber optic cable, they undergo total internal reflection allowing for essentially no light lost over the length of the cable. It is also possible to produce [polarized light rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_polarization "Plane polarization") using a combination of reflection and refraction: When a refracted ray and the reflected ray form a [right angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_angle "Right angle"), the reflected ray has the property of "plane polarization". The angle of incidence required for such a scenario is known as [Brewster's angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brewster%27s_angle "Brewster's angle").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometrical_optics#cite_note-Geoptics-3)

Snell's Law can be used to predict the deflection of light rays as they pass through "linear media" as long as the indexes of refraction and the geometry of the media are known. For example, the propagation of light through a [prism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prism_(optics) "Prism (optics)") results in the light ray being deflected depending on the shape and orientation of the prism. Additionally, since different frequencies of light have slightly different indexes of refraction in most materials, refraction can be used to produce [dispersion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dispersion_(optics) "Dispersion (optics)")[spectra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectrum "Spectrum") that appear as rainbows. The discovery of this phenomenon when passing light through a prism is famously attributed to [Isaac Newton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaac_Newton "Isaac Newton").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometrical_optics#cite_note-Geoptics-3)

Some media have an index of refraction which varies gradually with position and, thus, light rays curve through the medium rather than travel in straight lines. This effect is what is responsible for [mirages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mirage "Mirage") seen on hot days where the changing index of refraction of the air causes the light rays to bend creating the appearance of specular reflections in the distance (as if on the surface of a pool of water). Material that has a varying index of refraction is called a gradient-index (GRIN) material and has many useful properties used in modern optical scanning technologies including [photocopiers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photocopiers "Photocopiers") and [scanners](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Image_scanner "Image scanner"). The phenomenon is studied in the field of [gradient-index optics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gradient-index_optics "Gradient-index optics").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometrical_optics#cite_note-4)

[![Image 12](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Lens3b.svg/360px-Lens3b.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Lens3b.svg)

A ray tracing diagram for a simple converging lens

A device which produces converging or diverging light rays due to refraction is known as a [lens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lens_(optics) "Lens (optics)"). Thin lenses produce focal points on either side that can be modeled using the [lensmaker's equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lensmaker%27s_equation "Lensmaker's equation").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometrical_optics#cite_note-hecht-5) In general, two types of lenses exist: [convex lenses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_lens "Convex lens"), which cause parallel light rays to converge, and [concave lenses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concave_lens "Concave lens"), which cause parallel light rays to diverge. The detailed prediction of how images are produced by these lenses can be made using ray-tracing similar to curved mirrors. Similarly to curved mirrors, thin lenses follow a simple equation that determines the location of the images given a particular focal length (![Image 13: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61)) and object distance (![Image 14: {\displaystyle S_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5bf84e7fd4fb8259a9b37f956afdf83ee2a020f9)):![Image 15: {\displaystyle {\frac {1}{S_{1}}}+{\frac {1}{S_{2}}}={\frac {1}{f}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3d5471b983b47c062376f96c793d2a5fb8b04761) where ![Image 16: {\displaystyle S_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1143e284d5f25cef778ab482edf6617a523ddd9f) is the distance associated with the image and is considered by convention to be negative if on the same side of the lens as the object and positive if on the opposite side of the lens.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometrical_optics#cite_note-hecht-5) The focal length f is considered negative for concave lenses.

Incoming parallel rays are focused by a convex lens into an inverted real image one focal length from the lens, on the far side of the lens.

[![Image 17](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/2015-05-25_0820Incoming_parallel_rays_are_focused_by_a_convex_lens_into_an_inverted_real_image_one_focal_length_from_the_lens%2C_on_the_far_side_of_the.png/250px-2015-05-25_0820Incoming_parallel_rays_are_focused_by_a_convex_lens_into_an_inverted_real_image_one_focal_length_from_the_lens%2C_on_the_far_side_of_the.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:2015-05-25_0820Incoming_parallel_rays_are_focused_by_a_convex_lens_into_an_inverted_real_image_one_focal_length_from_the_lens,_on_the_far_side_of_the.png)

Incoming parallel rays are focused by a convex lens into an inverted real image one focal length from the lens, on the far side of the lens.

Rays from an object at finite distance are focused further from the lens than the focal distance; the closer the object is to the lens, the further the image is from the lens. With concave lenses, incoming parallel rays diverge after going through the lens, in such a way that they seem to have originated at an upright virtual image one focal length from the lens, on the same side of the lens that the parallel rays are approaching on.

[![Image 18](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/2015-05-25_0836With_concave_lenses%2C_incoming_parallel_rays_diverge_after_going_through_the_lens%2C_in_such_a_way_that_they_seem_to_have_originated_at_an.png/250px-2015-05-25_0836With_concave_lenses%2C_incoming_parallel_rays_diverge_after_going_through_the_lens%2C_in_such_a_way_that_they_seem_to_have_originated_at_an.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:2015-05-25_0836With_concave_lenses,_incoming_parallel_rays_diverge_after_going_through_the_lens,_in_such_a_way_that_they_seem_to_have_originated_at_an.png)

With concave lenses, incoming parallel rays diverge after going through the lens, in such a way that they seem to have originated at an upright virtual image one focal length from the lens, on the same side of the lens that the parallel rays are approaching on.

Rays from an object at finite distance are associated with a virtual image that is closer to the lens than the focal length, and on the same side of the lens as the object. The closer the object is to the lens, the closer the virtual image is to the lens.

[![Image 19](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Virtualimageframerate1.gif/250px-Virtualimageframerate1.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Virtualimageframerate1.gif)

Rays from an object at finite distance are associated with a virtual image that is closer to the lens than the focal length, and on the same side of the lens as the object.

Likewise, the magnification of a lens is given by ![Image 20: {\displaystyle M=-{\frac {S_{2}}{S_{1}}}={\frac {f}{f-S_{1}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9ba81b22aed933e15e889507aa5b4a3f5d6bc0fd) where the negative sign is given, by convention, to indicate an upright object for positive values and an inverted object for negative values. Similar to mirrors, upright images produced by single lenses are virtual while inverted images are real.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometrical_optics#cite_note-Geoptics-3)

Lenses suffer from [aberrations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optical_aberration "Optical aberration") that distort images and focal points. These are due to both to geometrical imperfections and due to the changing index of refraction for different wavelengths of light ([chromatic aberration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chromatic_aberration "Chromatic aberration")).[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometrical_optics#cite_note-Geoptics-3)

Underlying mathematics
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Geometrical_optics&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Underlying mathematics")]

As a mathematical study, geometrical optics emerges as a short-[wavelength](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wavelength "Wavelength") limit for solutions to [hyperbolic partial differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_partial_differential_equation "Hyperbolic partial differential equation") (Sommerfeld–Runge method) or as a property of propagation of field discontinuities according to Maxwell's equations (Luneburg method). In this short-wavelength limit, it is possible to approximate the solution locally by ![Image 21: {\displaystyle u(t,x)\approx a(t,x)e^{i(k\cdot x-\omega t)}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/73ecac4272cb6730876ac017be47c590d457cd30) where ![Image 22: {\displaystyle k,\omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fc8fcd6413d09acb97893ca8cc6be3ed6ba6cedb) satisfy a [dispersion relation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dispersion_relation "Dispersion relation"), and the amplitude ![Image 23: {\displaystyle a(t,x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2e1d4ef4e319ce54aea3d49a712ac36a24111e3a) varies slowly. More precisely, the [leading order](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leading-order_term "Leading-order term") solution takes the form ![Image 24: {\displaystyle a_{0}(t,x)e^{i\varphi (t,x)/\varepsilon }.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2ec2990d7efefa744f40f115bb420112fbab2178) The phase ![Image 25: {\displaystyle \varphi (t,x)/\varepsilon }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d5fe3431316bde60669622137f4fe2cc2323e4df) can be linearized to recover large wavenumber ![Image 26: {\displaystyle k:=\nabla _{x}\varphi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81b5f76496990486d559e52059a183c047c118a4), and frequency ![Image 27: {\displaystyle \omega :=-\partial _{t}\varphi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0e5ab7b7d8d64d26d3d32cc0f9b17de13f4a563e). The amplitude ![Image 28: {\displaystyle a_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/693ad9f934775838bd72406b41ada4a59785d7ba) satisfies a [transport equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transport_equation "Transport equation"). The small parameter ![Image 29: {\displaystyle \varepsilon \,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/137e3f3e97dfca591286d3815815dd6470bdf77b) enters the scene due to highly oscillatory initial conditions. Thus, when initial conditions oscillate much faster than the coefficients of the differential equation, solutions will be highly oscillatory, and transported along rays. Assuming coefficients in the differential equation are smooth, the rays will be too. In other words, [refraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Refraction "Refraction") does not take place. The motivation for this technique comes from studying the typical scenario of light propagation where short wavelength light travels along rays that minimize (more or less) its travel time. Its full application requires tools from [microlocal analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microlocal_analysis "Microlocal analysis").

### Sommerfeld–Runge method

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Geometrical_optics&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Sommerfeld–Runge method")]

The method of obtaining equations of geometrical optics by taking the limit of zero wavelength was first described by [Arnold Sommerfeld](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arnold_Sommerfeld "Arnold Sommerfeld") and J. Runge in 1911.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometrical_optics#cite_note-6) Their derivation was based on an oral remark by [Peter Debye](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Debye "Peter Debye").[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometrical_optics#cite_note-7)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometrical_optics#cite_note-8) Consider a monochromatic scalar field ![Image 30: {\displaystyle \psi (\mathbf {r} ,t)=\phi (\mathbf {r} )e^{i\omega t}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c70010bd4431aa79c3be8725f315fedef4bc30c7), where ![Image 31: {\displaystyle \psi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/45e5789e5d9c8f7c79744f43ecaaf8ba42a8553a) could be any of the components of [electric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_field "Electric field") or [magnetic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_field "Magnetic field") and hence the function ![Image 32: {\displaystyle \phi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/72b1f30316670aee6270a28334bdf4f5072cdde4) satisfy the wave equation ![Image 33: {\displaystyle \nabla ^{2}\phi +k_{o}^{2}n(\mathbf {r} )^{2}\phi =0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0535f156611a553911a6267e55a12f63ce4756f8) where ![Image 34: {\displaystyle k_{o}=\omega /c=2\pi /\lambda _{o}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6dcd39037736a469d6079b925e28468c696d0f05) with ![Image 35: {\displaystyle c}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/86a67b81c2de995bd608d5b2df50cd8cd7d92455) being the [speed of light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light "Speed of light") in vacuum. Here, ![Image 36: {\displaystyle n(\mathbf {r} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d809ed046365b51548655ff862ddef471e75c837) is the [refractive index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Refractive_index "Refractive index") of the medium. Without loss of generality, let us introduce ![Image 37: {\displaystyle \phi =A(k_{o},\mathbf {r} )e^{ik_{o}S(\mathbf {r} )}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7b51ff8ac81d67cc520f8654bb85c1a5031129e4) to convert the equation to ![Image 38: {\displaystyle -k_{o}^{2}A[(\nabla S)^{2}-n^{2}]+2ik_{o}(\nabla S\cdot \nabla A)+ik_{o}A\nabla ^{2}S+\nabla ^{2}A=0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f8b472952e8003d9916152f34e2369eee0f63a95)

Since the underlying principle of geometrical optics lies in the limit ![Image 39: {\displaystyle \lambda _{o}\sim k_{o}^{-1}\rightarrow 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/aafd2c6490f745651d70b4641170c4da814e16a7), the following asymptotic series is assumed, ![Image 40: {\displaystyle A(k_{o},\mathbf {r} )=\sum _{m=0}^{\infty }{\frac {A_{m}(\mathbf {r} )}{(ik_{o})^{m}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f720666cf0f29800ccac5ac13ef8aa85734e01ee)

For large but finite value of ![Image 41: {\displaystyle k_{o}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c06248902993a3b78e87a0c2c7aa508312870f30), the series diverges, and one has to be careful in keeping only appropriate first few terms. For each value of ![Image 42: {\displaystyle k_{o}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c06248902993a3b78e87a0c2c7aa508312870f30), one can find an optimum number of terms to be kept and adding more terms than the optimum number might result in a poorer approximation.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometrical_optics#cite_note-9) Substituting the series into the equation and collecting terms of different orders, one finds ![Image 43: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}O(k_{o}^{2}):&\quad (\nabla S)^{2}=n^{2},\\[1ex]O(k_{o}):&\quad 2\nabla S\cdot \nabla A_{0}+A_{0}\nabla ^{2}S=0,\\[1ex]O(1):&\quad 2\nabla S\cdot \nabla A_{1}+A_{1}\nabla ^{2}S=-\nabla ^{2}A_{0},\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cb3606c5d5e65d16712ad728c4dae20cec772bc4) in general, ![Image 44: {\displaystyle O(k_{o}^{1-m}):\quad 2\nabla S\cdot \nabla A_{m}+A_{m}\nabla ^{2}S=-\nabla ^{2}A_{m-1}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/625b16cf514922cb1c67cc14b91b3651d956be81)

The first equation is known as the **[eikonal equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eikonal_equation "Eikonal equation")**, which determines the **eikonal**![Image 45: {\displaystyle S(\mathbf {r} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/257bcdb41aa0e6d1d0772f4c92a82a4baa4a9116) is a [Hamilton–Jacobi equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamilton%E2%80%93Jacobi_equation "Hamilton–Jacobi equation"), written for example in Cartesian coordinates becomes ![Image 46: {\displaystyle \left({\frac {\partial S}{\partial x}}\right)^{2}+\left({\frac {\partial S}{\partial y}}\right)^{2}+\left({\frac {\partial S}{\partial z}}\right)^{2}=n^{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ef5c8347fc6d6c8409afd70bff7363846afbb02a)

The remaining equations determine the functions ![Image 47: {\displaystyle A_{m}(\mathbf {r} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6aa5d805ebf604bddf726e5e6f6c2485d3e488f1).

The method of obtaining equations of geometrical optics by analysing surfaces of discontinuities of solutions to Maxwell's equations was first described by [Rudolf Karl Luneburg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rudolf_Luneburg "Rudolf Luneburg") in 1944.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometrical_optics#cite_note-10) It does not restrict the electromagnetic field to have a special form required by the Sommerfeld-Runge method which assumes the amplitude ![Image 48: {\displaystyle A(k_{o},\mathbf {r} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fa566b637906e1e668837bcc111b0d8b2f550c86) and phase ![Image 49: {\displaystyle S(\mathbf {r} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/257bcdb41aa0e6d1d0772f4c92a82a4baa4a9116) satisfy the equation ![Image 50: {\textstyle \lim _{k_{0}\to \infty }{\frac {1}{k_{0}}}\left({\frac {1}{A}}\,\nabla S\cdot \nabla A+{\frac {1}{2}}\nabla ^{2}S\right)=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b439cd05b7b46408ac194e904b623fc6c0176cfd). This condition is satisfied by e.g. plane waves but is not additive.

The main conclusion of Luneburg's approach is the following:

**Theorem.** Suppose the fields ![Image 51: {\displaystyle \mathbf {E} (x,y,z,t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/69562d727b73352500048436d0e82d2ee1b1a765) and ![Image 52: {\displaystyle \mathbf {H} (x,y,z,t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/19d41f4e271e71ec921605d8ea13b383b35f51f9) (in a linear isotropic medium described by dielectric constants ![Image 53: {\displaystyle \varepsilon (x,y,z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0f012f6b103fc2b3ec95a071ca76a8c899427142) and ![Image 54: {\displaystyle \mu (x,y,z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/56ea7df220cc40d21122e2563917cfd110ddad97)) have finite discontinuities along a (moving) surface in ![Image 55: {\displaystyle \mathbf {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/42ee047387e551a89e8481e1a9e974dcc5fd5acc) described by the equation ![Image 56: {\displaystyle \psi (x,y,z)-ct=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a58f0afeadfcf464e3c6ef5466b13297286161ef). Then Maxwell's equations in the integral form imply that ![Image 57: {\displaystyle \psi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/45e5789e5d9c8f7c79744f43ecaaf8ba42a8553a) satisfies the [eikonal equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eikonal_equation "Eikonal equation"): ![Image 58: {\displaystyle \psi _{x}^{2}+\psi _{y}^{2}+\psi _{z}^{2}=\varepsilon \mu =n^{2},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fbd589b013dcba6e820a77a6f89644d02cda6faf) where ![Image 59: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) is the index of refraction of the medium (Gaussian units).

An example of such surface of discontinuity is the initial wave front emanating from a source that starts radiating at a certain instant of time.

The surfaces of field discontinuity thus become geometrical optics wave fronts with the corresponding geometrical optics fields defined as: ![Image 60: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\mathbf {E} ^{*}(x,y,z)&=\mathbf {E} (x,y,z,\psi (x,y,z)/c)\\[1ex]\mathbf {H} ^{*}(x,y,z)&=\mathbf {H} (x,y,z,\psi (x,y,z)/c)\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5874fbdbc2c4ebc3b91ea38745cae8d08c140e68)

Those fields obey transport equations consistent with the transport equations of the Sommerfeld-Runge approach. Light rays in Luneburg's theory are defined as trajectories orthogonal to the discontinuity surfaces and can be shown to obey [Fermat's principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermat%27s_principle "Fermat's principle") of least time thus establishing the identity of those rays with light rays of standard optics.

The above developments can be generalised to anisotropic media.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometrical_optics#cite_note-11)

The proof of Luneburg's theorem is based on investigating how Maxwell's equations govern the propagation of discontinuities of solutions. The basic technical lemma is as follows:

**A technical lemma.** Let ![Image 61: {\displaystyle \varphi (x,y,z,t)=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/090de17e6d07b64b1ccbac5af128efef0335daa1) be a hypersurface (a 3-dimensional manifold) in spacetime ![Image 62: {\displaystyle \mathbf {R} ^{4}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bd186b8abaeed43c225609f9556d8ee783b6a7fd) on which one or more of: ![Image 63: {\displaystyle \mathbf {E} (x,y,z,t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/69562d727b73352500048436d0e82d2ee1b1a765), ![Image 64: {\displaystyle \mathbf {H} (x,y,z,t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/19d41f4e271e71ec921605d8ea13b383b35f51f9), ![Image 65: {\displaystyle \varepsilon (x,y,z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0f012f6b103fc2b3ec95a071ca76a8c899427142), ![Image 66: {\displaystyle \mu (x,y,z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/56ea7df220cc40d21122e2563917cfd110ddad97), have a finite discontinuity. Then at each point of the hypersurface the following formulas hold: ![Image 67: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\nabla \varphi \cdot [\varepsilon \mathbf {E} ]&=0\\[1ex]\nabla \varphi \cdot [\mu \mathbf {H} ]&=0\\[1ex]\nabla \varphi \times [\mathbf {E} ]+{\frac {1}{c}}\,\varphi _{t}\,[\mu \mathbf {H} ]&=0\\[1ex]\nabla \varphi \times [\mathbf {H} ]-{\frac {1}{c}}\,\varphi _{t}\,[\varepsilon \mathbf {E} ]&=0\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fa8b556c2167374be68145158bc6612579712a50) where the ![Image 68: {\displaystyle \nabla }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a3d0e93b78c50237f9ea83d027e4ebbdaef354b2) operator acts in the ![Image 69: {\displaystyle xyz}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4d3957b3fbfe29dfdb2f7cdad4e3d7fc2ea7be84)-space (for every fixed ![Image 70: {\displaystyle t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/65658b7b223af9e1acc877d848888ecdb4466560)) and the square brackets denote the difference in values on both sides of the discontinuity surface (set up according to an arbitrary but fixed convention, e.g. the gradient ![Image 71: {\displaystyle \nabla \varphi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7d135f308e43463a63104ad85008b3b072c3e938) pointing in the direction of the quantities being subtracted _from_).

**Sketch of proof.** Start with Maxwell's equations away from the sources (Gaussian units): ![Image 72: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\nabla \cdot \varepsilon \mathbf {E} =0\\[1ex]\nabla \cdot \mu \mathbf {H} =0\\[1ex]\nabla \times \mathbf {E} +{\tfrac {\mu }{c}}\,\mathbf {H} _{t}=0\\[1ex]\nabla \times \mathbf {H} -{\tfrac {\varepsilon }{c}}\,\mathbf {E} _{t}=0\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/03bda406cbd94f13a917d47352138314b2cf80ef)

Using Stokes' theorem in ![Image 73: {\displaystyle \mathbf {R} ^{4}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bd186b8abaeed43c225609f9556d8ee783b6a7fd) one can conclude from the first of the above equations that for any domain ![Image 74: {\displaystyle D}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f34a0c600395e5d4345287e21fb26efd386990e6) in ![Image 75: {\displaystyle \mathbf {R} ^{4}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bd186b8abaeed43c225609f9556d8ee783b6a7fd) with a piecewise smooth (3-dimensional) boundary ![Image 76: {\displaystyle \Gamma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4cfde86a3f7ec967af9955d0988592f0693d2b19) the following is true: ![Image 77: {\displaystyle \oint _{\Gamma }(\mathbf {M} \cdot \varepsilon \mathbf {E} )\,dS=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/520bccddf30a9746dbcbad51ee62be82b11f767c) where ![Image 78: {\displaystyle \mathbf {M} =(x_{N},y_{N},z_{N})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8630cc5b4d1a7c94d933c4198d9d0386b6769e9e) is the projection of the outward unit normal ![Image 79: {\displaystyle (x_{N},y_{N},z_{N},t_{N})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/712a60a503dea57be72e3ce2f54fc4fadec6533b) of ![Image 80: {\displaystyle \Gamma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4cfde86a3f7ec967af9955d0988592f0693d2b19) onto the 3D slice ![Image 81: {\displaystyle t={\rm {const}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3d172decd4c60b544d5974710f3039d7d2a57d7), and ![Image 82: {\displaystyle dS}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/66f6ab8a2a8b85df78efbea896fcfeb3d4ea39e4) is the volume 3-form on ![Image 83: {\displaystyle \Gamma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4cfde86a3f7ec967af9955d0988592f0693d2b19). Similarly, one establishes the following from the remaining Maxwell's equations: ![Image 84: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\oint _{\Gamma }\left(\mathbf {M} \cdot \mu \mathbf {H} \right)dS&=0\\[1.55ex]\oint _{\Gamma }\left(\mathbf {M} \times \mathbf {E} +{\frac {\mu }{c}}\,t_{N}\,\mathbf {H} \right)dS&=0\\[1.55ex]\oint _{\Gamma }\left(\mathbf {M} \times \mathbf {H} -{\frac {\varepsilon }{c}}\,t_{N}\,\mathbf {E} \right)dS&=0\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/be8704c5e24fd980e49c510d1418ff6c1f39af3c)

Now by considering arbitrary small sub-surfaces ![Image 85: {\displaystyle \Gamma _{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/44f27c3fa0660b68ef8fa747442140655cff65cd) of ![Image 86: {\displaystyle \Gamma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4cfde86a3f7ec967af9955d0988592f0693d2b19) and setting up small neighbourhoods surrounding ![Image 87: {\displaystyle \Gamma _{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/44f27c3fa0660b68ef8fa747442140655cff65cd) in ![Image 88: {\displaystyle \mathbf {R} ^{4}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bd186b8abaeed43c225609f9556d8ee783b6a7fd), and subtracting the above integrals accordingly, one obtains: ![Image 89: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\int _{\Gamma _{0}}(\nabla \varphi \cdot [\varepsilon \mathbf {E} ])\,{dS \over \|\nabla ^{4D}\varphi \|}&=0\\[1ex]\int _{\Gamma _{0}}(\nabla \varphi \cdot [\mu \mathbf {H} ])\,{dS \over \|\nabla ^{4D}\varphi \|}&=0\\[1ex]\int _{\Gamma _{0}}\left(\nabla \varphi \times [\mathbf {E} ]+{1 \over c}\,\varphi _{t}\,[\mu \mathbf {H} ]\right)\,{\frac {dS}{\|\nabla ^{4D}\varphi \|}}&=0\\[1ex]\int _{\Gamma _{0}}\left(\nabla \varphi \times [\mathbf {H} ]-{1 \over c}\,\varphi _{t}\,[\varepsilon \mathbf {E} ]\right)\,{\frac {dS}{\|\nabla ^{4D}\varphi \|}}&=0\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/36b6c0263cbfbc789c99ad82840379983c668293) where ![Image 90: {\displaystyle \nabla ^{4D}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5b40650f6e9aee5914af84d1e94b3e483f91e88c) denotes the gradient in the 4D ![Image 91: {\displaystyle xyzt}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/18208e4087d68de97248f1b32cd4c5f963d412f4)-space. And since ![Image 92: {\displaystyle \Gamma _{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/44f27c3fa0660b68ef8fa747442140655cff65cd) is arbitrary, the integrands must be equal to 0 which proves the lemma.

It's now easy to show that as they propagate through a continuous medium, the discontinuity surfaces obey the eikonal equation. Specifically, if ![Image 93: {\displaystyle \varepsilon }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a30c89172e5b88edbd45d3e2772c7f5e562e5173) and ![Image 94: {\displaystyle \mu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9fd47b2a39f7a7856952afec1f1db72c67af6161) are continuous, then the discontinuities of ![Image 95: {\displaystyle \mathbf {E} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d7f22b39d51f780fc02859059c1757c606b9de2) and ![Image 96: {\displaystyle \mathbf {H} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f017b876ed763037d8818ec5dfbbdc6703e0f683) satisfy: ![Image 97: {\displaystyle [\varepsilon \mathbf {E} ]=\varepsilon [\mathbf {E} ]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8e2919510029905936e2b8d8149e1f0a7c4cf97a) and ![Image 98: {\displaystyle [\mu \mathbf {H} ]=\mu [\mathbf {H} ]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/70b7d98f0ed5b58d0201a273be52bbcd79b288b6). In this case the last two equations of the lemma can be written as:

![Image 99: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\nabla \varphi \times [\mathbf {E} ]+{\mu  \over c}\,\varphi _{t}\,[\mathbf {H} ]&=0\\[1ex]\nabla \varphi \times [\mathbf {H} ]-{\varepsilon  \over c}\,\varphi _{t}\,[\mathbf {E} ]&=0\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d70f582d3447fcf917d75931ce5a535dec17cc9d)

Taking the cross product of the second equation with ![Image 100: {\displaystyle \nabla \varphi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7d135f308e43463a63104ad85008b3b072c3e938) and substituting the first yields: ![Image 101: {\displaystyle \nabla \varphi \times (\nabla \varphi \times [\mathbf {H} ])-{\varepsilon  \over c}\,\varphi _{t}\,(\nabla \varphi \times [\mathbf {E} ])=(\nabla \varphi \cdot [\mathbf {H} ])\,\nabla \varphi -\|\nabla \varphi \|^{2}\,[\mathbf {H} ]+{\varepsilon \mu  \over c^{2}}\varphi _{t}^{2}\,[\mathbf {H} ]=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c5b5c97784e35d3caadde1e799d60b9e70aaeb90)

The continuity of ![Image 102: {\displaystyle \mu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9fd47b2a39f7a7856952afec1f1db72c67af6161) and the second equation of the lemma imply: ![Image 103: {\displaystyle \nabla \varphi \cdot [\mathbf {H} ]=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b304b5ef3a9a1871057c81aca26b4a39dfd4f0f5), hence, for points lying on the surface ![Image 104: {\displaystyle \varphi =0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/192287b02f5764a18fe39f37b8199d72000aa220)_only_: ![Image 105: {\displaystyle \|\nabla \varphi \|^{2}={\varepsilon \mu  \over c^{2}}\varphi _{t}^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e05ee647c9c658987e241b787c1123942bc10b7f)

(Notice the presence of the discontinuity is essential in this step as we'd be dividing by zero otherwise.)

Because of the physical considerations one can assume without loss of generality that ![Image 106: {\displaystyle \varphi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/33ee699558d09cf9d653f6351f9fda0b2f4aaa3e) is of the following form: ![Image 107: {\displaystyle \varphi (x,y,z,t)=\psi (x,y,z)-ct}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/104d591df6962ae238ee14af4c038a2f97316e5b), i.e. a 2D surface moving through space, modelled as level surfaces of ![Image 108: {\displaystyle \psi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/45e5789e5d9c8f7c79744f43ecaaf8ba42a8553a). (Mathematically ![Image 109: {\displaystyle \psi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/45e5789e5d9c8f7c79744f43ecaaf8ba42a8553a) exists if ![Image 110: {\displaystyle \varphi _{t}\neq 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cf3a90f8dea29439d414ecc6df498ee26035f0e3) by the [implicit function theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Implicit_function_theorem "Implicit function theorem").) The above equation written in terms of ![Image 111: {\displaystyle \psi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/45e5789e5d9c8f7c79744f43ecaaf8ba42a8553a) becomes: ![Image 112: {\displaystyle \|\nabla \psi \|^{2}={\varepsilon \mu  \over c^{2}}\,(-c)^{2}=\varepsilon \mu =n^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/13c986fe266371dd3bd6a0560b52f43963836675) i.e., ![Image 113: {\displaystyle \psi _{x}^{2}+\psi _{y}^{2}+\psi _{z}^{2}=n^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/481d1a2c0efc7a5b1e39b328ac544236b8bb3a89) which is the eikonal equation and it holds for all ![Image 114: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4), ![Image 115: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d), ![Image 116: {\displaystyle z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf368e72c009decd9b6686ee84a375632e11de98), since the variable ![Image 117: {\displaystyle t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/65658b7b223af9e1acc877d848888ecdb4466560) is absent. Other laws of optics like [Snell's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snell%27s_law "Snell's law") and [Fresnel formulae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fresnel_equations#Complex_amplitude_reflection_and_transmission_coefficients "Fresnel equations") can be similarly obtained by considering discontinuities in ![Image 118: {\displaystyle \varepsilon }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a30c89172e5b88edbd45d3e2772c7f5e562e5173) and ![Image 119: {\displaystyle \mu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9fd47b2a39f7a7856952afec1f1db72c67af6161).

and the substitution ![Image 121: {\displaystyle \psi =Ae^{iS/\varepsilon }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/53fbc2afc93384253f4ece9aee62b0ae1e3f4f6e) leads to[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometrical_optics#cite_note-12)![Image 122: {\displaystyle -{\frac {A}{\varepsilon ^{2}}}{\frac {\partial S}{\partial x_{i}}}{\frac {\partial S}{\partial x^{i}}}+{\frac {2i}{\varepsilon }}{\frac {\partial A}{\partial x_{i}}}{\frac {\partial S}{\partial x^{i}}}+{\frac {iA}{\varepsilon }}{\frac {\partial ^{2}S}{\partial x_{i}\partial x^{i}}}+{\frac {\partial ^{2}A}{\partial x_{i}\partial x^{i}}}=0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/234226b235515e1768f833923eeeaa6638498d60)
