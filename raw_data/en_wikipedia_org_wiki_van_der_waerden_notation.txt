From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Notation used for Weyl spinors

In [theoretical physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theoretical_physics "Theoretical physics"), **Van der Waerden notation**[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_der_Waerden_notation#cite_note-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_der_Waerden_notation#cite_note-2) refers to the usage of two-component [spinors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spinor "Spinor") ([Weyl spinors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weyl_spinor "Weyl spinor")) in four spacetime dimensions. This is standard in [twistor theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Twistor_theory "Twistor theory") and [supersymmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supersymmetry "Supersymmetry"). It is named after [Bartel Leendert van der Waerden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bartel_Leendert_van_der_Waerden "Bartel Leendert van der Waerden").

Dotted indices
--------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Van_der_Waerden_notation&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Dotted indices")]

Undotted indices (chiral indices)
Spinors with lower undotted indices have a left-handed [chirality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chirality "Chirality"), and are called chiral indices.

Σ l e f t=(ψ α 0){\displaystyle \Sigma _{\mathrm {left} }={\begin{pmatrix}\psi _{\alpha }\\0\end{pmatrix}}}![Image 10: {\displaystyle \Sigma _{\mathrm {left} }={\begin{pmatrix}\psi _{\alpha }\\0\end{pmatrix}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/be7dfdc5a90e3d443e3a7b492fb7eb79bee34046)Dotted indices (anti-chiral indices)
Spinors with raised dotted indices, plus an overbar on the symbol (not index), are right-handed, and called anti-chiral indices.

Σ r i g h t=(0 χ¯α˙){\displaystyle \Sigma _{\mathrm {right} }={\begin{pmatrix}0\\{\bar {\chi }}^{\dot {\alpha }}\\\end{pmatrix}}}![Image 11: {\displaystyle \Sigma _{\mathrm {right} }={\begin{pmatrix}0\\{\bar {\chi }}^{\dot {\alpha }}\\\end{pmatrix}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e2bacdc93505cf21b29ce2e1be0d75558b2098de)
Without the indices, i.e. "index free notation", an overbar is retained on right-handed spinor, since ambiguity arises between chirality when no index is indicated.

Hatted indices
--------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Van_der_Waerden_notation&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Hatted indices")]

Indices which have hats are called Dirac indices, and are the set of dotted and undotted, or chiral and anti-chiral, indices. For example, if

α=1,2,α˙=1˙,2˙{\displaystyle \alpha =1,2\,,{\dot {\alpha }}={\dot {1}},{\dot {2}}}![Image 12: {\displaystyle \alpha =1,2\,,{\dot {\alpha }}={\dot {1}},{\dot {2}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d152b90bdd989c3fdfd088a88606009582d96494)
then a spinor in the chiral basis is represented as

Σ α^=(ψ α χ¯α˙){\displaystyle \Sigma _{\hat {\alpha }}={\begin{pmatrix}\psi _{\alpha }\\{\bar {\chi }}^{\dot {\alpha }}\\\end{pmatrix}}}![Image 13: {\displaystyle \Sigma _{\hat {\alpha }}={\begin{pmatrix}\psi _{\alpha }\\{\bar {\chi }}^{\dot {\alpha }}\\\end{pmatrix}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3446ebe7e2962d45cd75a74639f27eaee8cd51c2)
where

α^=(α,α˙)=1,2,1˙,2˙{\displaystyle {\hat {\alpha }}=(\alpha ,{\dot {\alpha }})=1,2,{\dot {1}},{\dot {2}}}![Image 14: {\displaystyle {\hat {\alpha }}=(\alpha ,{\dot {\alpha }})=1,2,{\dot {1}},{\dot {2}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/27ce572119a0ed7b3757653825ca4336dac8b0db)
In this notation the [Dirac adjoint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dirac_adjoint "Dirac adjoint") (also called the **Dirac conjugate**) is

Σ α^=(χ α ψ¯α˙){\displaystyle \Sigma ^{\hat {\alpha }}={\begin{pmatrix}\chi ^{\alpha }&{\bar {\psi }}_{\dot {\alpha }}\end{pmatrix}}}![Image 15: {\displaystyle \Sigma ^{\hat {\alpha }}={\begin{pmatrix}\chi ^{\alpha }&{\bar {\psi }}_{\dot {\alpha }}\end{pmatrix}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/764e4c54d611db66aabde681d2fc785c13dcd0fb)
