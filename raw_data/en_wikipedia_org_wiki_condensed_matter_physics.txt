Title: Condensed matter physics

URL Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics

Published Time: 2001-04-06T02:04:42Z

Markdown Content:
**Condensed matter physics** is the field of [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics") that deals with the macroscopic and microscopic physical properties of [matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matter "Matter"), especially the [solid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid "Solid") and [liquid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liquid "Liquid")[phases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_of_matter "State of matter"), that arise from [electromagnetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic "Electromagnetic") forces between [atoms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atom "Atom") and [electrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrons "Electrons"). More generally, the subject deals with condensed phases of matter: systems of many constituents with strong interactions among them. More exotic condensed phases include the [superconducting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superconductivity "Superconductivity") phase exhibited by certain materials at extremely low [cryogenic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cryogenic "Cryogenic")[temperatures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperature "Temperature"), the [ferromagnetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ferromagnet "Ferromagnet") and [antiferromagnetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiferromagnet "Antiferromagnet") phases of [spins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_(physics) "Spin (physics)") on [crystal lattices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystal_lattice "Crystal lattice") of atoms, the [Bose–Einstein condensates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bose%E2%80%93Einstein_condensates "Bose–Einstein condensates") found in [ultracold atomic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultracold_atom "Ultracold atom") systems, and [liquid crystals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liquid_crystals "Liquid crystals"). Condensed matter physicists seek to understand the behavior of these phases by experiments to measure various material properties, and by applying the [physical laws](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_law "Physical law") of [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics"), [electromagnetism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetism "Electromagnetism"), [statistical mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistical_mechanics "Statistical mechanics"), and other [physics theories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theoretical_physics "Theoretical physics") to develop mathematical models and predict the properties of extremely large groups of atoms.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-1)

The diversity of systems and phenomena available for study makes condensed matter physics the most active field of contemporary physics: one third of all American physicists self-identify as condensed matter physicists,[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-2) and the Division of Condensed Matter Physics is the largest division of the [American Physical Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Physical_Society "American Physical Society").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-aps-history-3) These include solid state and [soft matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soft_matter "Soft matter") physicists, who study [quantum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics") and non-quantum physical properties of matter respectively.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-4) Both types study a great range of materials, providing many research, funding and employment opportunities.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-5) The field overlaps with [chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemistry "Chemistry"), [materials science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Materials_science "Materials science"), [engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineering "Engineering") and [nanotechnology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nanotechnology "Nanotechnology"), and relates closely to [atomic physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_physics "Atomic physics") and [biophysics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biophysics "Biophysics"). The [theoretical physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theoretical_physics "Theoretical physics") of condensed matter shares important concepts and methods with that of [particle physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_physics "Particle physics") and [nuclear physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_physics "Nuclear physics").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-marvincohen2008-6)

A variety of topics in physics such as [crystallography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystallography "Crystallography"), [metallurgy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metallurgy "Metallurgy"), [elasticity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elasticity_(physics) "Elasticity (physics)"), [magnetism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetism "Magnetism"), etc., were treated as distinct areas until the 1940s, when they were grouped together as _[solid-state physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid-state\_physics "Solid-state physics")_. Around the 1960s, the study of physical properties of [liquids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liquid "Liquid") was added to this list, forming the basis for the more comprehensive specialty of condensed matter physics.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-rmp-7) The [Bell Telephone Laboratories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bell_Labs "Bell Labs") was one of the first institutes to conduct a research program in condensed matter physics.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-rmp-7) According to the founding director of the [Max Planck Institute for Solid State Research](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Max_Planck_Institute_for_Solid_State_Research "Max Planck Institute for Solid State Research"), physics professor Manuel Cardona, it was [Albert Einstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Einstein "Albert Einstein") who created the modern field of condensed matter physics starting with his seminal 1905 article on the [photoelectric effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect "Photoelectric effect") and [photoluminescence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoluminescence "Photoluminescence") which opened the fields of [photoelectron spectroscopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectron_spectroscopy "Photoelectron spectroscopy") and [photoluminescence spectroscopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoluminescence_spectroscopy "Photoluminescence spectroscopy"), and later his 1907 article on the [specific heat of solids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Specific_heat_of_solids "Specific heat of solids") which introduced, for the first time, the effect of lattice vibrations on the thermodynamic properties of crystals, in particular the [specific heat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Specific_heat "Specific heat").[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-Cardona-8) Deputy Director of the Yale Quantum Institute [A. Douglas Stone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A._Douglas_Stone "A. Douglas Stone") makes a similar priority case for Einstein in his work on the synthetic history of [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics").[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-Stone-9)

According to physicist [Philip Warren Anderson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philip_Warren_Anderson "Philip Warren Anderson"), the use of the term "condensed matter" to designate a field of study was coined by him and [Volker Heine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volker_Heine "Volker Heine"), when they changed the name of their group at the [Cavendish Laboratories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cavendish_Laboratories "Cavendish Laboratories"), [Cambridge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cambridge "Cambridge"), from _Solid state theory_ to _Theory of Condensed Matter_ in 1967,[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-pwa-princeton-10) as they felt it better included their interest in liquids, [nuclear matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_matter "Nuclear matter"), and so on.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-wsn-11)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-12) Although Anderson and Heine helped popularize the name "condensed matter", it had been used in Europe for some years, most prominently in the [Springer-Verlag](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Springer_Science%2BBusiness_Media "Springer Science+Business Media") journal _Physics of Condensed Matter_, launched in 1963.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-13) The name "condensed matter physics" emphasized the commonality of scientific problems encountered by physicists working on solids, liquids, plasmas, and other complex matter, whereas "solid state physics" was often associated with restricted industrial applications of metals and semiconductors. In the 1960s and 70s, some physicists felt the more comprehensive name better fit the funding environment and [Cold War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cold_War "Cold War") politics of the time.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-martin-pip-14)

References to "condensed" states can be traced to earlier sources. For example, in the introduction to his 1947 book _Kinetic Theory of Liquids_,[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-Frenkel-15)[Yakov Frenkel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yakov_Frenkel "Yakov Frenkel") proposed that "The kinetic theory of liquids must accordingly be developed as a generalization and extension of the kinetic theory of solid bodies. As a matter of fact, it would be more correct to unify them under the title of 'condensed bodies'".

[![Image 1](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Heike_Kamerlingh_Onnes_and_Johannes_Diderik_van_der_Waals.jpg/250px-Heike_Kamerlingh_Onnes_and_Johannes_Diderik_van_der_Waals.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Heike_Kamerlingh_Onnes_and_Johannes_Diderik_van_der_Waals.jpg)

[Heike Kamerlingh Onnes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heike_Kamerlingh_Onnes "Heike Kamerlingh Onnes") and [Johannes van der Waals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johannes_van_der_Waals "Johannes van der Waals") with the [helium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium "Helium")_liquefactor_ at Leiden in 1908

One of the first studies of condensed states of matter was by [English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People_of_England "People of England")[chemist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemist "Chemist")[Humphry Davy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Humphry_Davy "Humphry Davy"), in the first decades of the nineteenth century. Davy observed that of the forty [chemical elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_element "Chemical element") known at the time, twenty-six had [metallic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metal "Metal") properties such as [lustre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lustre_(mineralogy) "Lustre (mineralogy)"), [ductility](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ductility "Ductility") and high electrical and thermal conductivity.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-goodstein-16) This indicated that the atoms in [John Dalton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Dalton "John Dalton")'s [atomic theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_theory "Atomic theory") were not indivisible as Dalton claimed, but had inner structure. Davy further claimed that elements that were then believed to be gases, such as [nitrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen "Nitrogen") and [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen") could be liquefied under the right conditions and would then behave as metals.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-davy-1839-17)[[note 1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-19)

In 1823, [Michael Faraday](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michael_Faraday "Michael Faraday"), then an assistant in Davy's lab, successfully liquefied [chlorine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chlorine "Chlorine") and went on to liquefy all known gaseous elements, except for nitrogen, hydrogen, and [oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen "Oxygen").[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-goodstein-16) Shortly after, in 1869, [Irish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People_of_Ireland "People of Ireland") chemist [Thomas Andrews](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Andrews_(scientist) "Thomas Andrews (scientist)") studied the [phase transition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phase_transition "Phase transition") from a liquid to a gas and coined the term [critical point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Critical_point_(thermodynamics) "Critical point (thermodynamics)") to describe the condition where a gas and a liquid were indistinguishable as phases,[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-thomasandrews-20) and [Dutch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Netherlands "Netherlands") physicist [Johannes van der Waals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johannes_van_der_Waals "Johannes van der Waals") supplied the theoretical framework which allowed the prediction of critical behavior based on measurements at much higher temperatures.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-atkins-21): 35–38  By 1908, [James Dewar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Dewar "James Dewar") and [Heike Kamerlingh Onnes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heike_Kamerlingh_Onnes "Heike Kamerlingh Onnes") were successfully able to liquefy hydrogen and the then newly discovered [helium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium "Helium") respectively.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-goodstein-16)

[Paul Drude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Drude "Paul Drude") in 1900 proposed the first theoretical model for a classical [electron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron "Electron") moving through a metallic solid.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-marvincohen2008-6) Drude's model described properties of metals in terms of a gas of free electrons, and was the first microscopic model to explain empirical observations such as the [Wiedemann–Franz law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wiedemann%E2%80%93Franz_law "Wiedemann–Franz law").[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-Kittel_1996-22)[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-Hoddeson-1992-23): 27–29  However, despite the success of [Drude's model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drude_model "Drude model"), it had one notable problem: it was unable to correctly explain the electronic contribution to the [specific heat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Specific_heat "Specific heat") and magnetic properties of metals, and the temperature dependence of resistivity at low temperatures.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-Kragh2002-24): 366–368

In 1911, three years after helium was first liquefied, Onnes working at [University of Leiden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Leiden "University of Leiden") discovered [superconductivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superconductivity "Superconductivity") in [mercury](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mercury_(element) "Mercury (element)"), when he observed the electrical resistivity of mercury to vanish at temperatures below a certain value.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-vanDelft2010-25) The phenomenon completely surprised the best theoretical physicists of the time, and it remained unexplained for several decades.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-Slichter-AIP-supercond-26)[Albert Einstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Einstein "Albert Einstein"), in 1922, said regarding contemporary theories of superconductivity that "with our far-reaching ignorance of the quantum mechanics of composite systems we are very far from being able to compose a theory out of these vague ideas."[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-Schmalian-2010-27)

### Advent of quantum mechanics

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Condensed_matter_physics&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Advent of quantum mechanics")]

Drude's classical model was augmented by [Wolfgang Pauli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Pauli "Wolfgang Pauli"), [Arnold Sommerfeld](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arnold_Sommerfeld "Arnold Sommerfeld"), [Felix Bloch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Felix_Bloch "Felix Bloch") and other physicists. Pauli realized that the free electrons in metal must obey the [Fermi–Dirac statistics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermi%E2%80%93Dirac_statistics "Fermi–Dirac statistics"). Using this idea, he developed the theory of [paramagnetism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paramagnetism "Paramagnetism") in 1926. Shortly after, Sommerfeld incorporated the [Fermi–Dirac statistics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermi%E2%80%93Dirac_statistics "Fermi–Dirac statistics") into the [free electron model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_electron_model "Free electron model") and made it better to explain the heat capacity. Two years later, Bloch used [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics") to describe the motion of an electron in a periodic lattice.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-Kragh2002-24): 366–368

The mathematics of crystal structures developed by [Auguste Bravais](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Auguste_Bravais "Auguste Bravais"), [Yevgraf Fyodorov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yevgraf_Fyodorov "Yevgraf Fyodorov") and others was used to classify crystals by their [symmetry group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry_group "Symmetry group"), and tables of crystal structures were the basis for the series _International Tables of Crystallography_, first published in 1935.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-Aroyo-2006-28)[Band structure calculations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Band_theory "Band theory") were first used in 1930 to predict the properties of new materials, and in 1947 [John Bardeen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Bardeen "John Bardeen"), [Walter Brattain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Walter_Brattain "Walter Brattain") and [William Shockley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Shockley "William Shockley") developed the first [semiconductor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semiconductor "Semiconductor")-based [transistor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transistor "Transistor"), heralding a revolution in electronics.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-marvincohen2008-6)

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Replica-of-first-transistor.jpg/250px-Replica-of-first-transistor.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Replica-of-first-transistor.jpg)

A replica of the first [point-contact transistor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point-contact_transistor "Point-contact transistor") in [Bell labs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bell_labs "Bell labs")

In 1879, [Edwin Herbert Hall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edwin_Herbert_Hall "Edwin Herbert Hall") working at the [Johns Hopkins University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johns_Hopkins_University "Johns Hopkins University") discovered that a voltage developed across conductors which was transverse to both an electric current in the conductor and a magnetic field applied perpendicular to the current.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-29) This phenomenon, arising due to the nature of charge carriers in the conductor, came to be termed the [Hall effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hall_effect "Hall effect"), but it was not properly explained at the time because the electron was not experimentally discovered until 18 years later. After the advent of quantum mechanics, [Lev Landau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lev_Landau "Lev Landau") in 1930 developed the theory of [Landau quantization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Landau_quantization "Landau quantization") and laid the foundation for a theoretical explanation of the [quantum Hall effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_Hall_effect "Quantum Hall effect") which was discovered half a century later.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-30): 458–460 [[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-31)

Magnetism as a property of matter has been known in China since 4000 BC.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-mattis-magnetism-2006-32): 1–2  However, the first modern studies of magnetism only started with the development of [electrodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrodynamics "Electrodynamics") by Faraday, [Maxwell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Clerk_Maxwell "James Clerk Maxwell") and others in the nineteenth century, which included classifying materials as [ferromagnetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ferromagnetic "Ferromagnetic"), [paramagnetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paramagnetic "Paramagnetic") and [diamagnetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diamagnetic "Diamagnetic") based on their response to magnetization.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-Chatterjee-2004-ferromagnetism-33)[Pierre Curie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre_Curie "Pierre Curie") studied the dependence of magnetization on temperature and discovered the [Curie point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curie_point "Curie point") phase transition in ferromagnetic materials.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-mattis-magnetism-2006-32) In 1906, [Pierre Weiss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre_Weiss "Pierre Weiss") introduced the concept of [magnetic domains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_domain "Magnetic domain") to explain the main properties of ferromagnets.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-Visintin-domains-34): 9  The first attempt at a microscopic description of magnetism was by [Wilhelm Lenz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilhelm_Lenz "Wilhelm Lenz") and [Ernst Ising](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ernst_Ising "Ernst Ising") through the [Ising model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ising_model "Ising model") that described magnetic materials as consisting of a periodic lattice of [spins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_(physics) "Spin (physics)") that collectively acquired magnetization.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-mattis-magnetism-2006-32) The Ising model was solved exactly to show that [spontaneous magnetization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spontaneous_magnetization "Spontaneous magnetization") can occur in one dimension and it is possible in higher-dimensional lattices. Further research such as by Bloch on [spin waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_wave "Spin wave") and [Néel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/N%C3%A9el "Néel") on [antiferromagnetism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiferromagnetism "Antiferromagnetism") led to developing new magnetic materials with applications to [magnetic storage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_storage "Magnetic storage") devices.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-mattis-magnetism-2006-32): 36–38, g48

### Modern many-body physics

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Condensed_matter_physics&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Modern many-body physics")]

[![Image 3: A magnet levitating over a superconducting material.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Meissner_effect_p1390048.jpg/250px-Meissner_effect_p1390048.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Meissner_effect_p1390048.jpg)

A [magnet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnet "Magnet")[levitating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meissner_effect "Meissner effect") above a [high-temperature superconductor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-temperature_superconductor "High-temperature superconductor"). Today some physicists are working to understand high-temperature superconductivity using the AdS/CFT correspondence.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-35)

The Sommerfeld model and spin models for ferromagnetism illustrated the successful application of quantum mechanics to condensed matter problems in the 1930s. However, there still were several unsolved problems, most notably the description of [superconductivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superconductivity "Superconductivity") and the [Kondo effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kondo_effect "Kondo effect").[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-Coleman-2003-36) After [World War II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II "World War II"), several ideas from quantum field theory were applied to condensed matter problems. These included recognition of [collective excitation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collective_excitation "Collective excitation") modes of solids and the important notion of a quasiparticle. Soviet physicist [Lev Landau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lev_Landau "Lev Landau") used the idea for the [Fermi liquid theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermi_liquid_theory "Fermi liquid theory") wherein low energy properties of interacting fermion systems were given in terms of what are now termed Landau-quasiparticles.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-Coleman-2003-36) Landau also developed a [mean-field theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mean-field_theory "Mean-field theory") for continuous phase transitions, which described ordered phases as [spontaneous breakdown of symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spontaneous_symmetry_breaking "Spontaneous symmetry breaking"). The theory also introduced the notion of an [order parameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Order_parameter "Order parameter") to distinguish between ordered phases.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-Kadanoff-2009-37) Eventually in 1956, [John Bardeen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Bardeen "John Bardeen"), [Leon Cooper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leon_Cooper "Leon Cooper") and [Robert Schrieffer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Schrieffer "Robert Schrieffer") developed the so-called [BCS theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BCS_theory "BCS theory") of superconductivity, based on the discovery that arbitrarily small attraction between two electrons of opposite spin mediated by [phonons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phonon "Phonon") in the lattice can give rise to a bound state called a [Cooper pair](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cooper_pair "Cooper pair").[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-coleman-38)

[![Image 4](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Quantum_Hall_effect_-_Russian.png/250px-Quantum_Hall_effect_-_Russian.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Quantum_Hall_effect_-_Russian.png)

The [quantum Hall effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_Hall_effect "Quantum Hall effect"): Components of the Hall resistivity as a function of the external magnetic field[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-von_Klitzing-39): fig. 14 

The study of phase transitions and the critical behavior of observables, termed [critical phenomena](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Critical_phenomena "Critical phenomena"), was a major field of interest in the 1960s.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-Fisher-rmp-1998-40)[Leo Kadanoff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leo_Kadanoff "Leo Kadanoff"), [Benjamin Widom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benjamin_Widom "Benjamin Widom") and [Michael Fisher](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michael_Fisher "Michael Fisher") developed the ideas of [critical exponents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Critical_exponent "Critical exponent") and [widom scaling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Widom_scaling "Widom scaling"). These ideas were unified by [Kenneth G. Wilson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kenneth_G._Wilson "Kenneth G. Wilson") in 1972, under the formalism of the [renormalization group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Renormalization_group "Renormalization group") in the context of quantum field theory.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-Fisher-rmp-1998-40)

The [quantum Hall effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_Hall_effect "Quantum Hall effect") was discovered by [Klaus von Klitzing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Klaus_von_Klitzing "Klaus von Klitzing"), Dorda and Pepper in 1980 when they observed the Hall conductance to be integer multiples of a fundamental constant ![Image 5: {\displaystyle e^{2}/h}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6224e6d40f7748ccebba5199a177b7d978dc93e8).(see figure) The effect was observed to be independent of parameters such as system size and impurities.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-von_Klitzing-39) In 1981, theorist [Robert Laughlin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Laughlin "Robert Laughlin") proposed a theory explaining the unanticipated precision of the integral plateau. It also implied that the Hall conductance is proportional to a topological invariant, called [Chern number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chern_class#Chern_numbers "Chern class"), whose relevance for the band structure of solids was formulated by [David J. Thouless](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_J._Thouless "David J. Thouless") and collaborators.[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-Avron-hall-2003-41)[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-Thouless1998-42): 69, 74  Shortly after, in 1982, [Horst Störmer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horst_St%C3%B6rmer "Horst Störmer") and [Daniel Tsui](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Daniel_Tsui "Daniel Tsui") observed the [fractional quantum Hall effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractional_quantum_Hall_effect "Fractional quantum Hall effect") where the conductance was now a rational multiple of the constant ![Image 6: {\displaystyle e^{2}/h}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6224e6d40f7748ccebba5199a177b7d978dc93e8). Laughlin, in 1983, realized that this was a consequence of quasiparticle interaction in the Hall states and formulated a [variational method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variational_method "Variational method") solution, named the [Laughlin wavefunction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laughlin_wavefunction "Laughlin wavefunction").[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-wen-1992-43) The study of topological properties of the fractional Hall effect remains an active field of research.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-:0-44) Decades later, the aforementioned topological band theory advanced by [David J. Thouless](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_J._Thouless "David J. Thouless") and collaborators[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-45) was further expanded leading to the discovery of [topological insulators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_insulator "Topological insulator").[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-46)[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-47)

In 1986, [Karl Müller](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karl_Alexander_M%C3%BCller "Karl Alexander Müller") and [Johannes Bednorz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johannes_Bednorz "Johannes Bednorz") discovered the first [high temperature superconductor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High_temperature_superconductor "High temperature superconductor"), La 2-x Ba x CuO 4, which is superconducting at temperatures as high as 39 [kelvin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvin "Kelvin").[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-48) It was realized that the high temperature superconductors are examples of strongly correlated materials where the electron–electron interactions play an important role.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-physics-world_str-el-49) A satisfactory theoretical description of high-temperature superconductors is still not known and the field of [strongly correlated materials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strongly_correlated_material "Strongly correlated material") continues to be an active research topic.

In 2012, several groups released preprints which suggest that [samarium hexaboride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samarium#Samarium_hexaboride "Samarium") has the properties of a [topological insulator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_insulator "Topological insulator")[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-Nature-1-50) in accord with the earlier theoretical predictions.[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-TKI-51) Since samarium hexaboride is an established [Kondo insulator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kondo_insulator "Kondo insulator"), i.e. a strongly correlated electron material, it is expected that the existence of a topological Dirac surface state in this material would lead to a topological insulator with strong electronic correlations.

Theoretical condensed matter physics involves the use of theoretical models to understand properties of states of matter. These include models to study the electronic properties of solids, such as the [Drude model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drude_model "Drude model"), the [band structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronic_band_structure "Electronic band structure") and the [density functional theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Density_functional_theory "Density functional theory"). Theoretical models have also been developed to study the physics of [phase transitions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phase_transition "Phase transition"), such as the [Ginzburg–Landau theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ginzburg%E2%80%93Landau_theory "Ginzburg–Landau theory"), [critical exponents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Critical_exponent "Critical exponent") and the use of mathematical methods of [quantum field theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_field_theory "Quantum field theory") and the [renormalization group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Renormalization_group "Renormalization group"). Modern theoretical studies involve the use of [numerical computation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Numerical_computation "Numerical computation") of electronic structure and mathematical tools to understand phenomena such as [high-temperature superconductivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-temperature_superconductivity "High-temperature superconductivity"), [topological phases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_phase "Topological phase"), and [gauge symmetries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauge_symmetry "Gauge symmetry").

Theoretical understanding of condensed matter physics is closely related to the notion of [emergence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emergence "Emergence"), wherein complex assemblies of particles behave in ways dramatically different from their individual constituents.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-coleman-38)[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-:0-44) For example, a range of phenomena related to high temperature superconductivity are understood poorly, although the microscopic physics of individual electrons and lattices is well known.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-nsf-emergence-52) Similarly, models of condensed matter systems have been studied where [collective excitations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collective_excitation "Collective excitation") behave like [photons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon "Photon") and [electrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron "Electron"), thereby describing [electromagnetism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetism "Electromagnetism") as an emergent phenomenon.[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-levin-rmp-53) Emergent properties can also occur at the interface between materials: one example is the [lanthanum aluminate-strontium titanate interface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lanthanum_aluminate-strontium_titanate_interface "Lanthanum aluminate-strontium titanate interface"), where two band-insulators are joined to create conductivity and [superconductivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superconductivity "Superconductivity").

### Electronic theory of solids

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Condensed_matter_physics&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Electronic theory of solids")]

The metallic state has historically been an important building block for studying properties of solids.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-AshcroftMermin1976-54) The first theoretical description of metals was given by [Paul Drude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Drude "Paul Drude") in 1900 with the [Drude model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drude_model "Drude model"), which explained electrical and thermal properties by describing a metal as an [ideal gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ideal_gas "Ideal gas") of then-newly discovered [electrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron "Electron"). He was able to derive the empirical [Wiedemann-Franz law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wiedemann-Franz_law "Wiedemann-Franz law") and get results in close agreement with the experiments.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-Hoddeson-1992-23): 90–91  This classical model was then improved by [Arnold Sommerfeld](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arnold_Sommerfeld "Arnold Sommerfeld") who incorporated the [Fermi–Dirac statistics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermi%E2%80%93Dirac_statistics "Fermi–Dirac statistics") of electrons and was able to explain the anomalous behavior of the [specific heat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Specific_heat "Specific heat") of metals in the [Wiedemann–Franz law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wiedemann%E2%80%93Franz_law "Wiedemann–Franz law").[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-Hoddeson-1992-23): 101–103  In 1912, The structure of crystalline solids was studied by [Max von Laue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Max_von_Laue "Max von Laue") and Paul Knipping, when they observed the [X-ray diffraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray_diffraction "X-ray diffraction") pattern of crystals, and concluded that crystals get their structure from periodic [lattices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lattice_model_(physics) "Lattice model (physics)") of atoms.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-Hoddeson-1992-23): 48 [[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-55) In 1928, Swiss physicist [Felix Bloch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Felix_Bloch "Felix Bloch") provided a wave function solution to the [Schrödinger equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schr%C3%B6dinger_equation "Schrödinger equation") with a [periodic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Periodic_function "Periodic function") potential, known as [Bloch's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bloch%27s_theorem "Bloch's theorem").[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-han-2010-56)

Calculating electronic properties of metals by solving the many-body wavefunction is often computationally hard, and hence, approximation methods are needed to obtain meaningful predictions.[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-perdew-2010-57) The [Thomas–Fermi theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas%E2%80%93Fermi_model "Thomas–Fermi model"), developed in the 1920s, was used to estimate system energy and electronic density by treating the local electron density as a [variational parameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variational_method "Variational method"). Later in the 1930s, [Douglas Hartree](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Douglas_Hartree "Douglas Hartree"), [Vladimir Fock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vladimir_Fock "Vladimir Fock") and [John Slater](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_C._Slater "John C. Slater") developed the so-called [Hartree–Fock wavefunction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hartree%E2%80%93Fock_method "Hartree–Fock method") as an improvement over the Thomas–Fermi model. The Hartree–Fock method accounted for [exchange statistics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exchange_symmetry "Exchange symmetry") of single particle electron wavefunctions. In general, it is very difficult to solve the Hartree–Fock equation. Only the free electron gas case can be solved exactly.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-AshcroftMermin1976-54): 330–337  Finally in 1964–65, [Walter Kohn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Walter_Kohn "Walter Kohn"), [Pierre Hohenberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre_Hohenberg "Pierre Hohenberg") and [Lu Jeu Sham](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lu_Jeu_Sham "Lu Jeu Sham") proposed the [density functional theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Density_functional_theory "Density functional theory") (DFT) which gave realistic descriptions for bulk and surface properties of metals. The density functional theory has been widely used since the 1970s for band structure calculations of variety of solids.[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-perdew-2010-57)

Some states of matter exhibit _symmetry breaking_, where the relevant laws of physics possess some form of [symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry_(physics) "Symmetry (physics)") that is broken. A common example is [crystalline solids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystal "Crystal"), which break continuous [translational symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Translational_symmetry "Translational symmetry"). Other examples include magnetized [ferromagnets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ferromagnetism "Ferromagnetism"), which break [rotational symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_symmetry "Rotational symmetry"), and more exotic states such as the ground state of a [BCS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BCS_theory "BCS theory")[superconductor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superconductor "Superconductor"), that breaks [U(1)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U(1) "U(1)") phase rotational symmetry.[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-58)[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-59)

[Goldstone's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goldstone%27s_theorem "Goldstone's theorem") in [quantum field theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_field_theory "Quantum field theory") states that in a system with broken continuous symmetry, there may exist excitations with arbitrarily low energy, called the Goldstone [bosons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boson "Boson"). For example, in crystalline solids, these correspond to [phonons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phonon "Phonon"), which are quantized versions of lattice vibrations.[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-goldstone-60)

Phase transition refers to the change of phase of a system, which is brought about by change in an external parameter such as [temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperature "Temperature"), [pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pressure "Pressure"), or [molar composition](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Molar_composition&action=edit&redlink=1 "Molar composition (page does not exist)"). In a single-component system, a classical phase transition occurs at a temperature (at a specific pressure) where there is an abrupt change in the order of the system. For example, when ice melts and becomes water, the ordered hexagonal crystal structure of ice is modified to a hydrogen bonded, mobile arrangement of water molecules.

In [quantum phase transitions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_phase_transition "Quantum phase transition"), the temperature is set to [absolute zero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_zero "Absolute zero"), and the non-thermal control parameter, such as pressure or magnetic field, causes the phase transitions when order is destroyed by [quantum fluctuations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_fluctuation "Quantum fluctuation") originating from the [Heisenberg uncertainty principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heisenberg_uncertainty_principle "Heisenberg uncertainty principle"). Here, the different quantum phases of the system refer to distinct [ground states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ground_state "Ground state") of the [Hamiltonian matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamiltonian_matrix "Hamiltonian matrix"). Understanding the behavior of quantum phase transition is important in the difficult tasks of explaining the properties of rare-earth magnetic insulators, high-temperature superconductors, and other substances.[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-Vojta2003-61)

Two classes of phase transitions occur: _first-order transitions_ and _second-order_ or _continuous transitions_. For the latter, the two phases involved do not co-exist at the transition temperature, also called the [critical point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Critical_point_(thermodynamics) "Critical point (thermodynamics)"). Near the critical point, systems undergo critical behavior, wherein several of their properties such as [correlation length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Correlation_length "Correlation length"), [specific heat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Specific_heat "Specific heat"), and [magnetic susceptibility](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_susceptibility "Magnetic susceptibility") diverge exponentially.[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-Vojta2003-61) These critical phenomena present serious challenges to physicists because normal [macroscopic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macroscopic_scale "Macroscopic scale") laws are no longer valid in the region, and novel ideas and methods must be invented to find the new laws that can describe the system.[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-NRC1986-62): 75ff

The simplest theory that can describe continuous phase transitions is the [Ginzburg–Landau theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ginzburg%E2%80%93Landau_theory "Ginzburg–Landau theory"), which works in the so-called [mean-field approximation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mean-field_approximation "Mean-field approximation"). However, it can only roughly explain continuous phase transition for ferroelectrics and type I superconductors which involves long range microscopic interactions. For other types of systems that involves short range interactions near the critical point, a better theory is needed.[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-University1989-63): 8–11

Near the critical point, the fluctuations happen over broad range of size scales while the feature of the whole system is scale invariant. [Renormalization group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Renormalization_group "Renormalization group") methods successively average out the shortest wavelength fluctuations in stages while retaining their effects into the next stage. Thus, the changes of a physical system as viewed at different size scales can be investigated systematically. The methods, together with powerful computer simulation, contribute greatly to the explanation of the critical phenomena associated with continuous phase transition.[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-NRC1986-62): 11

Experimental condensed matter physics involves the use of experimental probes to try to discover new properties of materials. Such probes include effects of [electric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_field "Electric field") and [magnetic fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_field "Magnetic field"), measuring [response functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Response_function "Response function"), [transport properties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transport_theory_(statistical_physics) "Transport theory (statistical physics)") and [thermometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermometry "Thermometry").[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-exptcm-64) Commonly used experimental methods include [spectroscopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopy "Spectroscopy"), with probes such as [X-rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray_spectroscopy "X-ray spectroscopy"), [infrared light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infrared_spectroscopy "Infrared spectroscopy") and [inelastic neutron scattering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inelastic_neutron_scattering "Inelastic neutron scattering"); study of thermal response, such as [specific heat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Specific_heat "Specific heat") and measuring transport via thermal and heat [conduction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conduction_(heat) "Conduction (heat)").

[![Image 7](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Lysozym_diffraction.png/250px-Lysozym_diffraction.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Lysozym_diffraction.png)

Image of X-ray diffraction pattern from a [protein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protein "Protein") crystal

Several condensed matter experiments involve scattering of an experimental probe, such as [X-ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray "X-ray"), optical [photons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon "Photon"), [neutrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron "Neutron"), etc., on constituents of a material. The choice of scattering probe depends on the observation energy scale of interest. [Visible light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visible_light "Visible light") has energy on the scale of 1 [electron volt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_volt "Electron volt") (eV) and is used as a scattering probe to measure variations in material properties such as the [dielectric constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dielectric_constant "Dielectric constant") and [refractive index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Refractive_index "Refractive index"). X-rays have energies of the order of 10 [keV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_volt "Electron volt") and hence are able to probe atomic length scales, and are used to measure variations in electron charge density and crystal structure.[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-chaikin-lubensky-65): 33–34

[Neutrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron "Neutron") can also probe atomic length scales and are used to study the scattering off nuclei and electron [spins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_(physics) "Spin (physics)") and magnetization (as neutrons have spin but no charge). Coulomb and [Mott scattering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mott_scattering "Mott scattering") measurements can be made by using [electron beams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_beams "Electron beams") as scattering probes.[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-chaikin-lubensky-65): 33–34 [[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-Zhang2012-66): 39–43  Similarly, [positron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positron "Positron") annihilation can be used as an indirect measurement of local electron density.[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-siegel-1980-67)[Laser spectroscopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laser_spectroscopy "Laser spectroscopy") is an excellent tool for studying the microscopic properties of a medium, for example, to study [forbidden transitions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forbidden_transition "Forbidden transition") in media with [nonlinear optical spectroscopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-linear_optics "Non-linear optics").[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-NRC1986-62): 258–259

### External magnetic fields

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Condensed_matter_physics&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: External magnetic fields")]

In experimental condensed matter physics, external [magnetic fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_field "Magnetic field") act as [thermodynamic variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamic_variable "Thermodynamic variable") that control the state, phase transitions and properties of material systems.[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-iupap-report-68)[Nuclear magnetic resonance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_magnetic_resonance "Nuclear magnetic resonance") (NMR) is a method by which external [magnetic fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_fields "Magnetic fields") are used to find resonance modes of individual nuclei, thus giving information about the atomic, molecular, and bond structure of their environment. NMR experiments can be made in magnetic fields with strengths up to 60 [tesla](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tesla_(unit) "Tesla (unit)"). Higher magnetic fields can improve the quality of NMR measurement data.[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-StatesAstronomy2013-69): 69 [[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-70): 185 [Quantum oscillations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_oscillations "Quantum oscillations") is another experimental method where high magnetic fields are used to study material properties such as the geometry of the [Fermi surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermi_surface "Fermi surface").[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-doiron-leyraud2007-71) High magnetic fields will be useful in experimental testing of the various theoretical predictions such as the quantized [magnetoelectric effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetoelectric_effect "Magnetoelectric effect"), image [magnetic monopole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_monopole "Magnetic monopole"), and the half-integer [quantum Hall effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_Hall_effect "Quantum Hall effect").[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-StatesAstronomy2013-69): 57

### Magnetic resonance spectroscopy

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Condensed_matter_physics&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Magnetic resonance spectroscopy")]

The [local structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_structure "Local structure"), as well as the structure of the nearest neighbour atoms, can be investigated in condensed matter with magnetic resonance methods, such as [electron paramagnetic resonance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_paramagnetic_resonance "Electron paramagnetic resonance") (EPR) and [nuclear magnetic resonance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_magnetic_resonance "Nuclear magnetic resonance") (NMR), which are very sensitive to the details of the surrounding of nuclei and electrons by means of the hyperfine coupling. Both localized electrons and specific stable or unstable isotopes of the [nuclei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_nucleus "Atomic nucleus") become the probe of these [hyperfine interactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperfine_structure "Hyperfine structure")), which couple the electron or nuclear spin to the local electric and magnetic fields. These methods are suitable to study defects, diffusion, phase transitions and magnetic order. Common experimental methods include [NMR](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_magnetic_resonance "Nuclear magnetic resonance"), [nuclear quadrupole resonance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_quadrupole_resonance "Nuclear quadrupole resonance") (NQR), implanted radioactive probes as in the case of [muon spin spectroscopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muon_spin_spectroscopy "Muon spin spectroscopy") (![Image 8: {\displaystyle \mu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9fd47b2a39f7a7856952afec1f1db72c67af6161)SR), [Mössbauer spectroscopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M%C3%B6ssbauer_spectroscopy "Mössbauer spectroscopy"), ![Image 9: {\displaystyle \beta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7ed48a5e36207156fb792fa79d29925d2f7901e8)NMR and [perturbed angular correlation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perturbed_angular_correlation "Perturbed angular correlation") (PAC). PAC is especially ideal for the study of phase changes at extreme temperatures above 2000°C due to the temperature independence of the method.

[![Image 10](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Bose_Einstein_condensate.png/250px-Bose_Einstein_condensate.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Bose_Einstein_condensate.png)

The first [Bose–Einstein condensate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bose%E2%80%93Einstein_condensate "Bose–Einstein condensate") observed in a gas of ultracold [rubidium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rubidium "Rubidium") atoms. The blue and white areas represent higher density.

[Ultracold atom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultracold_atom "Ultracold atom") trapping in optical lattices is an experimental tool commonly used in condensed matter physics, and in [atomic, molecular, and optical physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic,_molecular,_and_optical_physics "Atomic, molecular, and optical physics"). The method involves using optical lasers to form an [interference pattern](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interference_(wave_propagation) "Interference (wave propagation)"), which acts as a _lattice_, in which ions or atoms can be placed at very low temperatures. Cold atoms in optical lattices are used as _quantum simulators_, that is, they act as controllable systems that can model behavior of more complicated systems, such as [frustrated magnets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometrical_frustration "Geometrical frustration").[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-buluta-science2009-72) In particular, they are used to engineer one-, two- and three-dimensional lattices for a [Hubbard model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubbard_model "Hubbard model") with pre-specified parameters, and to study phase transitions for [antiferromagnetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiferromagnetism "Antiferromagnetism") and [spin liquid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_liquid "Spin liquid") ordering.[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-greiner-nature2008-73)[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-jaksch-aop2005-74)[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-:0-44)

In 1995, a gas of [rubidium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rubidium "Rubidium") atoms cooled down to a temperature of 170 [nK](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvin "Kelvin") was used to experimentally realize the [Bose–Einstein condensate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bose%E2%80%93Einstein_condensate "Bose–Einstein condensate"), a novel state of matter originally predicted by [S. N. Bose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S._N._Bose "S. N. Bose") and [Albert Einstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Einstein "Albert Einstein"), wherein a large number of atoms occupy one [quantum state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_state "Quantum state").[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-nytimes-BEC-75)

[![Image 11](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Fullerene_Nanogears_-_GPN-2000-001535.jpg/250px-Fullerene_Nanogears_-_GPN-2000-001535.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Fullerene_Nanogears_-_GPN-2000-001535.jpg)

Computer simulation of _nanogears_ made of [fullerene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fullerene "Fullerene") molecules. It is hoped that advances in nanoscience will lead to machines working on the molecular scale.

Research in condensed matter physics[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-:0-44)[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-76) has given rise to several device applications, such as the development of the [semiconductor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semiconductor "Semiconductor")[transistor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transistor "Transistor"),[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-marvincohen2008-6)[laser](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laser "Laser") technology,[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-NRC1986-62)[magnetic storage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_storage "Magnetic storage"), [liquid crystals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liquid_crystals "Liquid crystals"), [optical fibres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optical_fibres "Optical fibres")[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-77) and several phenomena studied in the context of [nanotechnology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nanotechnology "Nanotechnology").[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-2010Committee2007-78): 111ff  Methods such as [scanning-tunneling microscopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scanning_tunneling_microscope "Scanning tunneling microscope") can be used to control processes at the [nanometer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nanometer "Nanometer") scale, and have given rise to the study of nanofabrication.[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-yeh-perspective-79) Such molecular machines were developed for example by Nobel laureates in chemistry [Ben Feringa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ben_Feringa "Ben Feringa"), [Jean-Pierre Sauvage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean-Pierre_Sauvage "Jean-Pierre Sauvage") and [Fraser Stoddart](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fraser_Stoddart "Fraser Stoddart"). Feringa and his team developed multiple molecular machines such as the [molecular car](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_car "Molecular car"), molecular windmill and many more.[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-80)

In [quantum computation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_computation "Quantum computation"), information is represented by quantum bits, or [qubits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qubit "Qubit"). The qubits may [decohere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_decoherence "Quantum decoherence") quickly before useful computation is completed. This serious problem must be solved before quantum computing may be realized. To solve this problem, several promising approaches are proposed in condensed matter physics, including [Josephson junction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Josephson_junction "Josephson junction") qubits, [spintronic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spintronic "Spintronic") qubits using the [spin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_(physics) "Spin (physics)") orientation of magnetic materials, and the topological non-Abelian [anyons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anyon "Anyon") from [fractional quantum Hall effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractional_quantum_Hall_effect "Fractional quantum Hall effect") states.[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-yeh-perspective-79)

Condensed matter physics also has important uses for [biomedicine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biomedicine "Biomedicine"). For example, [magnetic resonance imaging](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_resonance_imaging "Magnetic resonance imaging") is widely used in medical imaging of soft tissue and other physiological features which cannot be viewed with traditional x-ray imaging.[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics#cite_note-yeh-perspective-79)
