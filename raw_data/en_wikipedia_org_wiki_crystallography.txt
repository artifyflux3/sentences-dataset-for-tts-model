**Crystallography** is the branch of science devoted to the study of molecular and crystalline structure and properties.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystallography#cite_note-1) The word _crystallography_ is derived from the [Ancient Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek "Ancient Greek") word [κρύσταλλος](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%BA%CF%81%CF%8D%CF%83%CF%84%CE%B1%CE%BB%CE%BB%CE%BF%CF%82#Ancient_Greek "wikt:κρύσταλλος") (_krústallos_; "clear ice, rock-crystal"), and [γράφειν](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%B3%CF%81%CE%AC%CF%86%CE%B5%CE%B9%CE%BD#Ancient_Greek "wikt:γράφειν") (_gráphein_; "to write").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystallography#cite_note-2) In July 2012, the [United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations") recognised the importance of the science of crystallography by proclaiming 2014 the International Year of Crystallography.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystallography#cite_note-UN_Resolution-3)

Crystallography is a broad topic, and many of its subareas, such as [X-ray crystallography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray_crystallography "X-ray crystallography"), are themselves important scientific topics. Crystallography ranges from the fundamentals of [crystal structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystal_structure "Crystal structure") to the mathematics of [crystal geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystal_system "Crystal system"), including those that are [not periodic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aperiodic_crystal "Aperiodic crystal") or [quasicrystals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasicrystal "Quasicrystal"). At the atomic scale it can involve the use of [X-ray diffraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray_diffraction "X-ray diffraction") to produce experimental data that the tools of [X-ray crystallography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray_crystallography "X-ray crystallography") can convert into detailed positions of atoms, and sometimes electron density. At larger scales it includes experimental tools such as [orientational imaging](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orientation_imaging_microscopy "Orientation imaging microscopy") to examine the relative orientations at the [grain boundary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grain_boundary "Grain boundary") in materials. Crystallography plays a key role in many areas of biology, chemistry, and physics, as well new developments in these fields.

History and timeline
--------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Crystallography&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: History and timeline")]

Before the 20th century, the study of [crystals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystal "Crystal") was based on physical measurements of their geometry using a [goniometer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goniometer "Goniometer").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystallography#cite_note-4) This involved measuring the angles of crystal faces relative to each other and to theoretical reference axes (crystallographic axes), and establishing the [symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry_(physics) "Symmetry (physics)") of the crystal in question. The position in 3D space of each crystal face is plotted on a [stereographic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stereographic_projection "Stereographic projection") net such as a [Wulff net](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wulff_net "Wulff net") or [Lambert net](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lambert_azimuthal_equal-area_projection "Lambert azimuthal equal-area projection"). The [pole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pole_figure "Pole figure") to each face is plotted on the net. Each point is labelled with its [Miller index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Miller_index "Miller index"). The final plot allows the symmetry of the crystal to be established.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystallography#cite_note-:0-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystallography#cite_note-6)

The discovery of [X-rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray "X-ray") and [electrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron "Electron") in the last decade of the 19th century enabled the determination of crystal structures on the atomic scale, which brought about the modern era of crystallography. The first X-ray diffraction experiment was conducted in 1912 by [Max von Laue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Max_von_Laue "Max von Laue"),[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystallography#cite_note-L1912-7) while electron diffraction was first realized in 1927 in the [Davisson–Germer experiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Davisson%E2%80%93Germer_experiment "Davisson–Germer experiment")[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystallography#cite_note-8) and parallel work by [George Paget Thomson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_Paget_Thomson "George Paget Thomson") and Alexander Reid.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystallography#cite_note-9) These developed into the two main branches of crystallography, [X-ray crystallography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray_crystallography "X-ray crystallography") and [electron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_diffraction "Electron diffraction") diffraction. The quality and throughput of solving crystal structures greatly improved in the second half of the 20th century, with the developments of customized instruments and [phasing algorithms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phase_problem "Phase problem"). Nowadays, crystallography is an [interdisciplinary field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interdisciplinary_field "Interdisciplinary field"), supporting theoretical and experimental discoveries in various domains.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystallography#cite_note-10) Modern-day scientific instruments for crystallography vary from laboratory-sized equipment, such as [diffractometers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffractometer "Diffractometer") and [electron microscopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_microscope "Electron microscope"), to dedicated large facilities, such as [photoinjectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoinjector "Photoinjector"), [synchrotron light sources](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synchrotron_light_source "Synchrotron light source") and [free-electron lasers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free-electron_laser "Free-electron laser").

Crystallographic methods depend mainly on analysis of the [diffraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffraction "Diffraction") patterns of a sample targeted by a beam of some type. [X-rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray "X-ray") are most commonly used; other beams used include [electrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron "Electron") or [neutrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron "Neutron"). Crystallographers often explicitly state the type of beam used, as in the terms _[X-ray diffraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray\_diffraction "X-ray diffraction"), [neutron diffraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron\_diffraction "Neutron diffraction")_ and _[electron diffraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron\_diffraction "Electron diffraction")_. These three types of radiation interact with the specimen in different ways.

*   X-rays interact with the [spatial distribution of electrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_density "Electron density") in the sample.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystallography#cite_note-11)
*   Neutrons are scattered by the atomic nuclei through the [strong nuclear forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strong_nuclear_force "Strong nuclear force"), but in addition the [magnetic moment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_moment "Magnetic moment") of neutrons is non-zero, so they are also scattered by [magnetic fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_field "Magnetic field"). When neutrons are scattered from [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen")-containing materials, they produce diffraction patterns with high noise levels, which can sometimes be resolved by substituting [deuterium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deuterium "Deuterium") for hydrogen.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystallography#cite_note-12)
*   Electrons are [charged particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charged_particle "Charged particle") and therefore interact with the total [charge distribution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charge_density "Charge density") of both the [atomic nuclei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_nuclei "Atomic nuclei") and the electrons of the sample.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystallography#cite_note-13): Chpt 4 

It is hard to focus x-rays or neutrons, but since electrons are charged they can be focused and are used in [electron microscopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_microscopes "Electron microscopes") to produce magnified images. There are many ways that [transmission electron microscopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transmission_electron_microscopy "Transmission electron microscopy") and related techniques such as [scanning transmission electron microscopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scanning_transmission_electron_microscopy "Scanning transmission electron microscopy"), [high-resolution electron microscopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-resolution_electron_microscopy "High-resolution electron microscopy") can be used to obtain images with in many cases atomic resolution from which crystallographic information can be obtained. There are also other methods such as [low-energy electron diffraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Low-energy_electron_diffraction "Low-energy electron diffraction"), [low-energy electron microscopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Low-energy_electron_microscopy "Low-energy electron microscopy") and [reflection high-energy electron diffraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflection_high-energy_electron_diffraction "Reflection high-energy electron diffraction") which can be used to obtain crystallographic information about surfaces.

Applications in various areas
-----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Crystallography&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Applications in various areas")]

Crystallography is used by materials scientists to characterize different materials. In single crystals, the effects of the crystalline arrangement of atoms is often easy to see macroscopically because the natural shapes of crystals reflect the atomic structure. In addition, physical properties are often controlled by crystalline defects. The understanding of crystal structures is an important prerequisite for understanding [crystallographic defects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystallographic_defect "Crystallographic defect"). Most materials do not occur as a single crystal, but are poly-crystalline in nature (they exist as an aggregate of small crystals with different orientations). As such, [powder diffraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Powder_diffraction "Powder diffraction") techniques, which take diffraction patterns of samples with a large number of crystals, play an important role in structural determination.

Other physical properties are also linked to crystallography. For example, the minerals in [clay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clay "Clay") form small, flat, platelike structures. Clay can be easily deformed because the platelike particles can slip along each other in the plane of the plates, yet remain strongly connected in the direction perpendicular to the plates. Such mechanisms can be studied by crystallographic [texture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Texture_(crystalline) "Texture (crystalline)") measurements. Crystallographic studies help elucidate the relationship between a material's structure and its properties, aiding in developing new materials with tailored characteristics. This understanding is crucial in various fields, including metallurgy, geology, and materials science. Advancements in crystallographic techniques, such as electron diffraction and X-ray crystallography, continue to expand our understanding of material behavior at the atomic level.

In another example, [iron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron#Allotropes "Iron") transforms from a [body-centered cubic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Body-centered_cubic "Body-centered cubic") (bcc) structure called [ferrite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allotropes_of_iron#Alpha_iron_(%CE%B1-Fe) "Allotropes of iron") to a [face-centered cubic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Face-centered_cubic "Face-centered cubic") (fcc) structure called [austenite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Austenite "Austenite") when it is heated.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystallography#cite_note-14) The fcc structure is a close-packed structure unlike the bcc structure; thus the volume of the iron decreases when this transformation occurs.

Crystallography is useful in phase identification. When manufacturing or using a material, it is generally desirable to know what compounds and what phases are present in the material, as their composition, structure and proportions will influence the material's properties. Each phase has a characteristic arrangement of atoms. X-ray or neutron diffraction can be used to identify which structures are present in the material, and thus which compounds are present. Crystallography covers the enumeration of the symmetry patterns which can be formed by atoms in a crystal and for this reason is related to [group theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_theory#Chemistry_and_materials_science "Group theory").

[X-ray crystallography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray_crystallography "X-ray crystallography") is the primary method for determining the molecular conformations of biological [macromolecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macromolecule "Macromolecule"), particularly [protein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protein "Protein") and [nucleic acids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleic_acid "Nucleic acid") such as [DNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNA "DNA") and [RNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RNA "RNA"). The first crystal structure of a macromolecule was solved in 1958, a three-dimensional model of the myoglobin molecule obtained by X-ray analysis.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystallography#cite_note-15)[Neutron crystallography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron_crystallography "Neutron crystallography") is often used to help refine structures obtained by X-ray methods or to solve a specific bond; the methods are often viewed as complementary, as X-rays are sensitive to electron positions and scatter most strongly off heavy atoms, while neutrons are sensitive to nucleus positions and scatter strongly even off many light isotopes, including hydrogen and deuterium.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystallography#cite_note-16)[Electron diffraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_diffraction "Electron diffraction") has been used to determine some protein structures, most notably [membrane proteins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Membrane_protein "Membrane protein") and [viral capsids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viral_capsid "Viral capsid").

Macromolecular structures determined through X-ray crystallography (and other techniques) are housed in the [Protein Data Bank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protein_Data_Bank "Protein Data Bank") (PDB)–a freely accessible repository for the structures of proteins and other biological macromolecules.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystallography#cite_note-17) There are many [molecular graphics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_graphics "Molecular graphics") codes available for visualising these structures.

*   Coordinates in _square [brackets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket "Bracket")_ such as **[100]** denote a direction vector (in real space).
*   Coordinates in _angle brackets_ or _chevrons_ such as **<100>** denote a _family_ of directions which are related by symmetry operations. In the cubic [crystal system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystal_system "Crystal system") for example, **<100>** would mean [100], [010], [001] or the negative of any of those directions.
*   [Miller indices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Miller_Index "Miller Index") in _parentheses_ such as **(100)** denote a plane of the crystal structure, and regular repetitions of that plane with a particular spacing. In the cubic system, the [normal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_(geometry) "Normal (geometry)") to the (hkl) plane is the direction [hkl], but in lower-symmetry cases, the normal to (hkl) is not parallel to [hkl].
*   Indices in _curly brackets_ or _braces_ such as **{100}** denote a family of planes and their normals. In cubic materials the symmetry makes them equivalent, just as the way angle brackets denote a family of directions. In non-cubic materials, <hkl> is not necessarily perpendicular to {hkl}.

The _International Tables for Crystallography_[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystallography#cite_note-18) is an eight-book series that outlines the standard notations for formatting, describing and testing crystals. The series contains books that covers analysis methods and the mathematical procedures for determining organic structure through x-ray crystallography, electron diffraction, and neutron diffraction. The International tables are focused on procedures, techniques and descriptions and do not list the physical properties of individual crystals themselves. Each book is about 1000 pages and the titles of the books are:

Vol A - _Space Group Symmetry_,Vol A1 - _Symmetry Relations Between Space Groups_,Vol B - _Reciprocal Space_,Vol C - _Mathematical, Physical, and Chemical Tables_,Vol D - _Physical Properties of Crystals_,Vol E - _Subperiodic Groups_,Vol F - _Crystallography of Biological Macromolecules_, and Vol G - _Definition and Exchange of Crystallographic Data_.
