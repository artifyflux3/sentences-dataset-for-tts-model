From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

In [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics"), **semiclassical** refers to a theory in which one part of a [system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_system "Physical system") is described [quantum mechanically](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics"), whereas the other is treated [classically](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_mechanics "Classical mechanics"). For example, external [fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(physics) "Field (physics)") will be constant, or when changing will be classically described. In general, it incorporates a development in [powers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponent "Exponent") of the [Planck constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant "Planck constant"), resulting in the classical physics of power 0, and the first nontrivial approximation to the power of (−1). In this case, there is a clear link between the quantum-mechanical system and the associated semi-classical and classical approximations, as it is similar in appearance to the transition from [physical optics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_optics "Physical optics") to [geometric optics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_optics "Geometric optics").

[Max Planck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Max_Planck "Max Planck") was the first to introduce the idea of quanta of energy in 1900 while studying [black-body radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black-body_radiation "Black-body radiation"). In 1906, he was also the first to write that quantum theory should replicate classical mechanics at some limit, particularly if the [Planck constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant "Planck constant")_h_ were infinitesimal.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semiclassical_physics#cite_note-:0-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semiclassical_physics#cite_note-:1-2) With this idea he showed that [Planck's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck%27s_law "Planck's law") for thermal radiation leads to the [Rayleigh–Jeans law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rayleigh%E2%80%93Jeans_law "Rayleigh–Jeans law"), the classical prediction (valid for large [wavelength](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wavelength "Wavelength")).[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semiclassical_physics#cite_note-:0-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semiclassical_physics#cite_note-:1-2)

Some examples of a semiclassical approximation include:

*   [WKB approximation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WKB_approximation "WKB approximation"): electrons in classical external [electromagnetic fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_field "Electromagnetic field").
*   [semiclassical gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semiclassical_gravity "Semiclassical gravity"): [quantum field theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_field_theory "Quantum field theory") within a classical [curved](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curved_space "Curved space")[gravitational](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity "Gravity") background (see [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity")).
*   [quantum chaos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_chaos "Quantum chaos"): quantization of classical [chaotic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chaos_theory "Chaos theory") systems.
*   magnetic properties of materials and astrophysical bodies under the effect of large magnetic fields (see for example [De Haas–Van Alphen effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_Haas%E2%80%93Van_Alphen_effect "De Haas–Van Alphen effect"))
*   [quantum field theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_field_theory "Quantum field theory"): only [Feynman diagrams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feynman_diagram "Feynman diagram") with at most a single closed loop (see for example [one-loop Feynman diagram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One-loop_Feynman_diagram "One-loop Feynman diagram")) are considered, which corresponds to the powers of the Planck constant.

*   [Bohr model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bohr_model "Bohr model")
*   [Correspondence principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Correspondence_principle "Correspondence principle")
*   [Classical limit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_limit "Classical limit")
*   [Eikonal approximation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eikonal_approximation "Eikonal approximation")
*   [Einstein–Brillouin–Keller method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein%E2%80%93Brillouin%E2%80%93Keller_method "Einstein–Brillouin–Keller method")
*   [Old quantum theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Old_quantum_theory "Old quantum theory")
