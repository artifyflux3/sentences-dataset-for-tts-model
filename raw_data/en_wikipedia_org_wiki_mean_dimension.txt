From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), the **mean** (topological) **dimension** of a [topological dynamical system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_dynamical_system "Topological dynamical system") is a non-negative extended real number that is a measure of the complexity of the system. Mean dimension was first introduced in 1999 by [Gromov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Leonidovich_Gromov "Mikhail Leonidovich Gromov").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mean_dimension#cite_note-1) Shortly after it was developed and studied systematically by [Lindenstrauss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elon_Lindenstrauss "Elon Lindenstrauss") and [Weiss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benjamin_Weiss "Benjamin Weiss").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mean_dimension#cite_note-LindenstraussWeiss2000-2) In particular they proved the following key fact: a system with finite [topological entropy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_entropy "Topological entropy") has zero mean dimension. For various topological dynamical systems with infinite topological entropy, the mean dimension can be calculated or at least bounded from below and above. This allows mean dimension to be used to distinguish between systems with infinite topological entropy. Mean dimension is also related to the problem of [embedding topological dynamical systems in shift spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universal_space#Universal_spaces_in_topological_dynamics "Universal space") (over Euclidean cubes).

A topological dynamical system consists of a compact Hausdorff topological space ![Image 2: {\displaystyle \textstyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/90a4ada62f49160766cf8302e9351bc4438c482a) and a continuous self-map ![Image 3: {\displaystyle \textstyle T:X\rightarrow X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f181686e8748d495c5d3530acd7bb239564cfdb8). Let ![Image 4: {\displaystyle \textstyle {\mathcal {O}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2c29d267576cc3392f118bffe8b429c478f612cb) denote the collection of open finite covers of ![Image 5: {\displaystyle \textstyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/90a4ada62f49160766cf8302e9351bc4438c482a). For ![Image 6: {\displaystyle \textstyle \alpha \in {\mathcal {O}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c4dace9bd7b04dd05e9611b4cb9a69b4718898e7) define its order by

![Image 7: {\displaystyle \operatorname {ord} (\alpha )=\max _{x\in X}\sum _{U\in \alpha }1_{U}(x)-1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1780e89ebd108a6241f5e7a5fc61a6694101c333)
An open finite cover ![Image 8: {\displaystyle \textstyle \beta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fd583a2ffef371fd09f141575dbfbd307c46964c) refines ![Image 9: {\displaystyle \textstyle \alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/45728a083749011217ec42e1bcfbe76ebdbe8c18), denoted ![Image 10: {\displaystyle \textstyle \beta \succ \alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f565d2895f6e74c9eb843f8219887ed593e352e9), if for every ![Image 11: {\displaystyle \textstyle V\in \beta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b76fe064a52ae724ef2c1e04862d9617ba513b4e), there is ![Image 12: {\displaystyle \textstyle U\in \alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d9c3d4e43529397898dba086ff9c8824f7b3ef8a) so that ![Image 13: {\displaystyle \textstyle V\subset U}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/814b2cc1845d5e38fcc4abeb5efc41ffa3e1711d). Let

![Image 14: {\displaystyle D(\alpha )=\min _{\beta \succ \alpha }\operatorname {ord} (\beta )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/049f5d18d268e26dd6b3602eea6dce266ca4b3ab)
Note that in terms of this definition the [Lebesgue covering dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebesgue_covering_dimension "Lebesgue covering dimension") is defined by ![Image 15: {\displaystyle \dim _{\mathrm {Leb} }(X)=\sup _{\alpha \in {\mathcal {O}}}D(\alpha )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9b06696fd8b3e000f5cfac051a03d48c78d2dd4c).

Let ![Image 16: {\displaystyle \textstyle \alpha ,\beta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ce0fb85ead92b83f8fd356b68ef0d7bb2372a407) be open finite covers of ![Image 17: {\displaystyle \textstyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/90a4ada62f49160766cf8302e9351bc4438c482a). The join of ![Image 18: {\displaystyle \textstyle \alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/45728a083749011217ec42e1bcfbe76ebdbe8c18) and ![Image 19: {\displaystyle \textstyle \beta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fd583a2ffef371fd09f141575dbfbd307c46964c) is the open finite cover by all sets of the form ![Image 20: {\displaystyle \textstyle A\cap B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6b79c2e39089cb1bc831a1fb1d8ba89b7b2e8304) where ![Image 21: {\displaystyle \textstyle A\in \alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e88a3b0799eb5cba12849b939c99c26f89bb05e2), ![Image 22: {\displaystyle \textstyle B\in \beta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d78803a54da7eac7ec8ef527b7f925aaca5ea39f). Similarly one can define the join ![Image 23: {\displaystyle \textstyle \bigvee _{i=1}^{n}\alpha _{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47137e91138ca886222e8ad3f8eb4a5213aa7fec) of any finite collection of open covers of ![Image 24: {\displaystyle \textstyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/90a4ada62f49160766cf8302e9351bc4438c482a).

The mean dimension is the non-negative extended real number:

![Image 25: {\displaystyle \operatorname {mdim} (X,T)=\sup _{\alpha \in {\mathcal {\mathcal {O}}}}\lim _{n\rightarrow \infty }{\frac {D(\alpha ^{n})}{n}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/960957da66d65ac0655427d06b7a593e73a4a323)
where ![Image 26: {\displaystyle \textstyle \alpha ^{n}=\bigvee _{i=0}^{n-1}T^{-i}\alpha .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cc6603f902c0d5d156924f6983f5c93c5c6681c9)

Definition in the metric case
-----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Mean_dimension&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Definition in the metric case")]

If the compact Hausdorff topological space ![Image 27: {\displaystyle \textstyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/90a4ada62f49160766cf8302e9351bc4438c482a) is [metrizable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metrizable_space "Metrizable space") and ![Image 28: {\displaystyle \textstyle d}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c4a5f1130824df33e2aff957314f14b0f8745ee4) is a compatible metric, an equivalent definition can be given. For ![Image 29: {\displaystyle \textstyle \varepsilon >0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e97046fdc8131bd68400ea058df7ea1326b8f44), let ![Image 30: {\displaystyle \textstyle \operatorname {Widim} _{\varepsilon }(X,d)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/33263aaa7f28197fc38c9ccdef6b58bab4965ac0) be the minimal non-negative integer ![Image 31: {\displaystyle \textstyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6f305e136d6d5ea97d6abc8d333894f20f297ed4), such that there exists an open finite cover of ![Image 32: {\displaystyle \textstyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/90a4ada62f49160766cf8302e9351bc4438c482a) by sets of diameter less than ![Image 33: {\displaystyle \textstyle \varepsilon }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7133ce73ec025b79174c7655031030387cc34b06) such that any ![Image 34: {\displaystyle \textstyle n+2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b5b7210af434b9e424720c8bc889810b34fa2336) distinct sets from this cover have empty intersection. Note that in terms of this definition the [Lebesgue covering dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebesgue_covering_dimension "Lebesgue covering dimension") is defined by ![Image 35: {\displaystyle \textstyle \dim _{\mathrm {Leb} }(X)=\sup _{\varepsilon >0}\operatorname {Widim} _{\varepsilon }(X,d)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7fda45835388b9b5cda446fbf7710cdc1cbacd36). Let

![Image 36: {\displaystyle d_{n}(x,y)=\max _{0\leq i\leq n-1}d(T^{i}x,T^{i}y)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e34e90a1d9dca9d8e5c6437cab658af8bf04f15c)
The mean dimension is the non-negative extended real number:

![Image 37: {\displaystyle \operatorname {mdim} (X,d)=\sup _{\varepsilon >0}\lim _{n\rightarrow \infty }{\frac {\operatorname {Widim} _{\varepsilon }(X,d_{n})}{n}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6f6aa72aac6623fdf8ea8ed99f3ad31b2ec76c00)
Let ![Image 38: {\displaystyle \textstyle d\in \mathbb {N} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c8681a9af02c072b394c977c7a7a7d86ff3bd3f4). Let ![Image 39: {\displaystyle \textstyle X=([0,1]^{d})^{\mathbb {Z} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6ab3ea0a42547cbbd3bf926c5d1979cecbdb9549) and ![Image 40: {\displaystyle \textstyle T:X\rightarrow X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f181686e8748d495c5d3530acd7bb239564cfdb8) be the **shift** homeomorphism ![Image 41: {\displaystyle \textstyle (\ldots ,x_{-2},x_{-1},\mathbf {x_{0}} ,x_{1},x_{2},\ldots )\rightarrow (\ldots ,x_{-1},x_{0},\mathbf {x_{1}} ,x_{2},x_{3},\ldots )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e448e223168efde84ba9a9f88569b884c8e9a5d3), then ![Image 42: {\displaystyle \textstyle \operatorname {mdim} (X,T)=d}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/508eb20fbcc0fead0bd5d54c7c0e07a19a1160cd).
