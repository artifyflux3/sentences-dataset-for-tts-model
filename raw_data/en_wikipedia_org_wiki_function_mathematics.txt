In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), a **function** from a [set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_(mathematics) "Set (mathematics)")X to a set Y assigns to each element of X exactly one element of Y.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics)#cite_note-halmos-1) The set X is called the [domain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_of_a_function "Domain of a function") of the function[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics)#cite_note-2) and the set Y is called the [codomain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Codomain "Codomain") of the function.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics)#cite_note-codomain-3)

Functions were originally the idealization of how a varying quantity depends on another quantity. For example, the position of a [planet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planet "Planet") is a _function_ of time. [Historically](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_the_function_concept "History of the function concept"), the concept was elaborated with the [infinitesimal calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinitesimal_calculus "Infinitesimal calculus") at the end of the 17th century, and, until the 19th century, the functions that were considered were [differentiable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differentiable_function "Differentiable function") (that is, they had a high degree of regularity). The concept of a function was formalized at the end of the 19th century in terms of [set theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_theory "Set theory"), and this greatly increased the possible applications of the concept.

A function is often denoted by a letter such as f, g or h. The value of a function f at an element x of its domain (that is, the element of the codomain that is associated with x) is denoted by _f_(_x_); for example, the value of f at _x_ = 4 is denoted by _f_(4). Commonly, a specific function is defined by means of an [expression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expression_(mathematics) "Expression (mathematics)") depending on x, such as ![Image 1: {\displaystyle f(x)=x^{2}+1;}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b5cfd777bae6ce11f1c33372d52fa61a5bb2d0df) in this case, some computation, called **function evaluation**, may be needed for deducing the value of the function at a particular value; for example, if ![Image 2: {\displaystyle f(x)=x^{2}+1,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/31eec96067f891efa5a072d1781924daf9ed0003) then ![Image 3: {\displaystyle f(4)=4^{2}+1=17.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b38062c42739e5d2fdd7b53aa0c9a94617cdc881)

Given its domain and its codomain, a function is uniquely represented by the set of all [pairs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pair_(mathematics) "Pair (mathematics)")(_x_, _f_ (_x_)), called the _[graph of the function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph\_of\_a\_function "Graph of a function")_, a popular means of illustrating the function.[[note 1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics)#cite_note-4)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics)#cite_note-5) When the domain and the codomain are sets of real numbers, each such pair may be thought of as the [Cartesian coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinates "Cartesian coordinates") of a point in the plane.

Functions are widely used in [science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Science "Science"), [engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineering "Engineering"), and in most fields of mathematics. It has been said that functions are "the central objects of investigation" in most fields of mathematics.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics)#cite_note-FOOTNOTESpivak200839-6)

The concept of a function has evolved significantly over centuries, from its informal origins in ancient mathematics to its formalization in the 19th century. See [History of the function concept](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_the_function_concept "History of the function concept") for details.

A **function**f from a [set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_(mathematics) "Set (mathematics)")X to a set Y is an assignment of one element of Y to each element of X. The set X is called the [domain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_of_a_function "Domain of a function") of the function and the set Y is called the [codomain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Codomain "Codomain") of the function.

If the element y in Y is assigned to x in X by the function f, one says that f _maps_ x to y, and this is commonly written ![Image 6: {\displaystyle y=f(x).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ea153ca7e4a53e290c347ab731724265f98c0b60) In this notation, x is the _[argument](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Argument\_of\_a\_function "Argument of a function")_ or _[variable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variable\_(mathematics) "Variable (mathematics)")_ of the function.

A specific element x of X is a _value of the variable_, and the corresponding element of Y is the _value of the function_ at x, or the [image](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Image_(mathematics) "Image (mathematics)") of x under the function. The _image of a function_, sometimes called its [range](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Range_of_a_function "Range of a function"), is the set of the images of all elements in the domain.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics)#cite_note-EOM_Function-7)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics)#cite_note-T&K_Calc_p.3-8)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics)#cite_note-Trench_RA_pp.30-32-9)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics)#cite_note-TBB_RA_pp.A4-A5-10)

A function f, its domain X, and its codomain Y are often specified by the notation ![Image 7: {\displaystyle f:X\to Y.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b918aeefba8721a6732102a5848bd4238615ec55) One may write ![Image 8: {\displaystyle x\mapsto y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eb2452f2d32e5424f3db361de033fd49a73f9dcc) instead of ![Image 9: {\displaystyle y=f(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2311a6a75c54b0ea085a381ba472c31d59321514), where the symbol ![Image 10: {\displaystyle \mapsto }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bc09de045e7d82eef9fe078e7e7606576640c11b) (read '[maps to](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maps_to "Maps to")') is used to specify where a particular element x in the domain is mapped to by f. This allows the definition of a function without naming. For example, the [square function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_function "Square function") is the function ![Image 11: {\displaystyle x\mapsto x^{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/724da729a8df718591408d7bdfb38ce2b47035db)

The domain and codomain are not always explicitly given when a function is defined. In particular, it is common that one might only know, without some (possibly difficult) computation, that the domain of a specific function is contained in a larger set. For example, if ![Image 12: {\displaystyle f:\mathbb {R} \to \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1e3a10a3ad05781f5cf9c2d875a02227e21a8448) is a [real function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_function "Real function"), the determination of the domain of the function ![Image 13: {\displaystyle x\mapsto 1/f(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cde09bf037011b15f092d47999d05b417088dde1) requires knowing the [zeros](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero_of_a_function "Zero of a function") of f. This is one of the reasons for which, in [mathematical analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_analysis "Mathematical analysis"), "a function from X to Y " may refer to a function having a proper subset of X as a domain.[[note 2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics)#cite_note-11) For example, a "function from the reals to the reals" may refer to a [real-valued](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real-valued_function "Real-valued function") function of a [real variable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_of_a_real_variable "Function of a real variable") whose domain is a proper subset of the [real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number"), typically a subset that contains a non-empty [open interval](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_interval "Open interval"). Such a function is then called a [partial function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_function "Partial function").

A function f on a set S means a function from the domain S, without specifying a codomain. However, some authors use it as shorthand for saying that the function is _f_: _S_ → _S_.

The above definition of a function is essentially that of the founders of [calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus "Calculus"), [Leibniz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leibniz "Leibniz"), [Newton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaac_Newton "Isaac Newton") and [Euler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler "Euler"). However, it cannot be [formalized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formal_proof "Formal proof"), since there is no mathematical definition of an "assignment". It is only at the end of the 19th century that the first formal definition of a function could be provided, in terms of [set theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_theory "Set theory"). This set-theoretic definition is based on the fact that a function establishes a _relation_ between the elements of the domain and some (possibly all) elements of the codomain. Mathematically, a [binary relation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_relation "Binary relation") between two sets _X_ and _Y_ is a [subset](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subset "Subset") of the set of all [ordered pairs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordered_pair "Ordered pair")![Image 16: {\displaystyle (x,y)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/41cf50e4a314ca8e2c30964baa8d26e5be7a9386) such that ![Image 17: {\displaystyle x\in X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3e580967f68f36743e894aa7944f032dda6ea01d) and ![Image 18: {\displaystyle y\in Y.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b14bc7ecf2f86320b4a930f4961919ae45a34d9c) The set of all these pairs is called the [Cartesian product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_product "Cartesian product") of _X_ and _Y_ and denoted ![Image 19: {\displaystyle X\times Y.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/41f39ef78be28dc2b9015ff7f82e9a1ef719a9f4) Thus, the above definition may be formalized as follows.

Partial functions are defined similarly to ordinary functions, with the "total" condition removed. That is, a _partial function_ from X to Y is a binary relation R between X and Y such that, for every ![Image 26: {\displaystyle x\in X,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e3ebdb0a09f0721ccdd0b779e0a21caf386be82a) there is _at most one_ y in Y such that ![Image 27: {\displaystyle (x,y)\in R.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/421ae0833b9c96e25c691320faca968cfe9a3d4f)

Using functional notation, this means that, given ![Image 28: {\displaystyle x\in X,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e3ebdb0a09f0721ccdd0b779e0a21caf386be82a) either ![Image 29: {\displaystyle f(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/202945cce41ecebb6f643f31d119c514bec7a074) is in Y, or it is undefined.

The set of the elements of X such that ![Image 30: {\displaystyle f(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/202945cce41ecebb6f643f31d119c514bec7a074) is defined and belongs to Y is called the _domain of definition_ of the function. A partial function from X to Y is thus an ordinary function that has as its domain a subset of X called the domain of definition of the function. If the domain of definition equals X, one often says that the partial function is a _total function_.

In several areas of mathematics, the term "function" refers to partial functions rather than to ordinary (total) functions. This is typically the case when functions may be specified in a way that makes difficult or even impossible to determine their domain.

In [calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus "Calculus"), a _real-valued function of a real variable_ or _[real function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real\_function "Real function")_ is a partial function from the set ![Image 31: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc) of the [real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number") to itself. Given a real function ![Image 32: {\displaystyle f:x\mapsto f(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0c6abc5d2f5d3b6f1ed1d94c74c2b9f255fc118e) its [multiplicative inverse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplicative_inverse "Multiplicative inverse")![Image 33: {\displaystyle x\mapsto 1/f(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cde09bf037011b15f092d47999d05b417088dde1) is also a real function. The determination of the domain of definition of a multiplicative inverse of a (partial) function amounts to compute the [zeros](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero_of_a_function "Zero of a function") of the function, the values where the function is defined but not its multiplicative inverse.

Similarly, a _[function of a complex variable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function\_of\_a\_complex\_variable "Function of a complex variable")_ is generally a partial function whose domain of definition is a subset of the [complex numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number")![Image 34: {\displaystyle \mathbb {C} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f9add4085095b9b6d28d045fd9c92c2c09f549a7). The difficulty of determining the domain of definition of a [complex function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_function "Complex function") is illustrated by the multiplicative inverse of the [Riemann zeta function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_zeta_function "Riemann zeta function"): the determination of the domain of definition of the function ![Image 35: {\displaystyle z\mapsto 1/\zeta (z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/328b3ae5767865f93350e2f503cce630ec5e968b) is more or less equivalent to the proof or disproof of one of the major open problems in mathematics, the [Riemann hypothesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_hypothesis "Riemann hypothesis").

In [computability theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computability_theory "Computability theory"), a [general recursive function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_recursive_function "General recursive function") is a partial function from the integers to the integers whose values can be computed by an [algorithm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algorithm "Algorithm") (roughly speaking). The domain of definition of such a function is the set of inputs for which the algorithm does not run forever. A fundamental theorem of computability theory is that there cannot exist an algorithm that takes an arbitrary general recursive function as input and tests whether 0 belongs to its domain of definition (see [Halting problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halting_problem "Halting problem")).

### Multivariate functions

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Function_(mathematics)&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Multivariate functions")]

[![Image 36](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Binary_operations_as_black_box.svg/250px-Binary_operations_as_black_box.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Binary_operations_as_black_box.svg)

A binary operation is a typical example of a bivariate function which assigns to each pair ![Image 37: {\displaystyle (x,y)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/41cf50e4a314ca8e2c30964baa8d26e5be7a9386) the result ![Image 38: {\displaystyle x\circ y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c2ba86902ee98c41deb1275ddb8693977f27e1da).

A **multivariate function**, **multivariable function**, or **function of several variables** is a function that depends on several arguments. Such functions are commonly encountered. For example, the position of a car on a road is a function of the time travelled and its average speed.

Formally, a function of n variables is a function whose domain is a set of n-tuples.[[note 3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics)#cite_note-13) For example, multiplication of [integers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer") is a function of two variables, or **bivariate function**, whose domain is the set of all [ordered pairs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordered_pairs "Ordered pairs") (2-tuples) of integers, and whose codomain is the set of integers. The same is true for every [binary operation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_operation "Binary operation"). The graph of a bivariate surface over a two-dimensional real domain may be interpreted as defining a [parametric surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_(mathematics)#Graph_of_a_bivariate_function "Surface (mathematics)"), as used in, e.g., [bivariate interpolation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bivariate_interpolation "Bivariate interpolation").

Commonly, an n-tuple is denoted enclosed between parentheses, such as in ![Image 39: {\displaystyle (1,2,\ldots ,n).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38a956b235b4d9e7046aeabf286c020fa1020e0a) When using [functional notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functional_notation "Functional notation"), one usually omits the parentheses surrounding tuples, writing ![Image 40: {\displaystyle f(x_{1},\ldots ,x_{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/61d6311d8c66acc1a5755c4c7cb688d3b1fa0fcb) instead of ![Image 41: {\displaystyle f((x_{1},\ldots ,x_{n})).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/07ea486934809be3b7f5d97c159b9a116ef18837)

Given n sets ![Image 42: {\displaystyle X_{1},\ldots ,X_{n},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6b0ca82f7c1f167358d7122823318450410ae5d8) the set of all n-tuples ![Image 43: {\displaystyle (x_{1},\ldots ,x_{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7935f7983d8a5ae59fea84efe65415235fa7c47b) such that ![Image 44: {\displaystyle x_{1}\in X_{1},\ldots ,x_{n}\in X_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c6041cb619899bcfa55a26ff377e7eb55ffbfdae) is called the [Cartesian product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_product "Cartesian product") of ![Image 45: {\displaystyle X_{1},\ldots ,X_{n},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6b0ca82f7c1f167358d7122823318450410ae5d8) and denoted ![Image 46: {\displaystyle X_{1}\times \cdots \times X_{n}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b9332eafc39443cb3785081f0d3e889b64b0961)

Therefore, a multivariate function is a function that has a Cartesian product or a [proper subset](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proper_subset "Proper subset") of a Cartesian product as a domain.

where the domain U has the form

If all the ![Image 49: {\displaystyle X_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af4a0955af42beb5f85aa05fb8c07abedc13990d) are equal to the set ![Image 50: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc) of the [real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number") or to the set ![Image 51: {\displaystyle \mathbb {C} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f9add4085095b9b6d28d045fd9c92c2c09f549a7) of the [complex numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number"), one talks respectively of a [function of several real variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_of_several_real_variables "Function of several real variables") or of a [function of several complex variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_of_several_complex_variables "Function of several complex variables").

There are various standard ways for denoting functions. The most commonly used notation is functional notation, which is the first notation described below.

### Functional notation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Function_(mathematics)&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Functional notation")]

The functional notation requires that a name is given to the function, which, in the case of a unspecified function is often the letter f. Then, the application of the function to an argument is denoted by its name followed by its argument (or, in the case of a multivariate functions, its arguments) enclosed between parentheses, such as in

![Image 52: {\displaystyle f(x),\quad \sin(3),\quad {\text{or}}\quad f(x^{2}+1).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1c30f49866bcc96fc4b8daf973844abe48f0eee0)

The argument between the parentheses may be a [variable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variable_(mathematics) "Variable (mathematics)"), often x, that represents an arbitrary element of the domain of the function, a specific element of the domain (3 in the above example), or an [expression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expression_(mathematics) "Expression (mathematics)") that can be evaluated to an element of the domain (![Image 53: {\displaystyle x^{2}+1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/92a3a8d23f9f8123651e496dcf8490990c65cf9a) in the above example). The use of a unspecified variable between parentheses is useful for defining a function explicitly such as in "let ![Image 54: {\displaystyle f(x)=\sin(x^{2}+1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ba3b1c9d9e56b3327baceebfd1e530cc5b132a06)".

When the symbol denoting the function consists of several characters and no ambiguity may arise, the parentheses of functional notation might be omitted. For example, it is common to write sin _x_ instead of sin(_x_).

Functional notation was first used by [Leonhard Euler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leonhard_Euler "Leonhard Euler") in 1734.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics)#cite_note-14) Some widely used functions are represented by a symbol consisting of several letters (usually two or three, generally an abbreviation of their name). In this case, a [roman type](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_type "Roman type") is customarily used instead, such as "sin" for the [sine function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sine_function "Sine function"), in contrast to italic font for single-letter symbols.

The functional notation is often used colloquially for referring to a function and simultaneously naming its argument, such as in "let ![Image 55: {\displaystyle f(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/202945cce41ecebb6f643f31d119c514bec7a074) be a function". This is an [abuse of notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abuse_of_notation "Abuse of notation") that is useful for a simpler formulation.

Arrow notation defines the rule of a function inline, without requiring a name to be given to the function. It uses the ↦ arrow symbol, read as "[maps to](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maps_to "Maps to")". For example, ![Image 56: {\displaystyle x\mapsto x+1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/092c91a7a2ccb9481d3da9c5e59cd410c66dd87f) is the function which takes a real number as input and outputs that number plus 1. Again, a domain and codomain of ![Image 57: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc) is implied.

The domain and codomain can also be explicitly stated, for example:

![Image 58: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\operatorname {sqr} \colon \mathbb {Z} &\to \mathbb {Z} \\x&\mapsto x^{2}.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c83da8e3fd28ad40fade38a4a75b3de3be02e792)

This defines a function sqr from the integers to the integers that returns the square of its input.

As a common application of the arrow notation, suppose ![Image 59: {\displaystyle f:X\times X\to Y;\;(x,t)\mapsto f(x,t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8b4649a27b503cf52487d3e9252faf20b362db2a) is a function in two variables, and we want to refer to a [partially applied function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_application "Partial application")![Image 60: {\displaystyle X\to Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/290b16963d52e4a7995aae01ee854b97a6ea10c0) produced by fixing the second argument to the value _t_ 0 without introducing a new function name. The map in question could be denoted ![Image 61: {\displaystyle x\mapsto f(x,t_{0})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7063c8d624a28d6798322fb19c71a580adfcbd68) using the arrow notation. The expression ![Image 62: {\displaystyle x\mapsto f(x,t_{0})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7063c8d624a28d6798322fb19c71a580adfcbd68) (read: "the map taking x to f of x comma t nought") represents this new function with just one argument, whereas the expression _f_(_x_ 0, _t_ 0) refers to the value of the function f at the point (_x_ 0, _t_ 0).

Index notation may be used instead of functional notation. That is, instead of writing _f_ (_x_), one writes ![Image 63: {\displaystyle f_{x}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/04a7e56cbda7f3fa285ea6a6500af7901cbf58d9)

This is typically the case for functions whose domain is the set of the [natural numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number "Natural number"). Such a function is called a [sequence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sequence_(mathematics) "Sequence (mathematics)"), and, in this case the element ![Image 64: {\displaystyle f_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b2702450f0458a5e01a698e248af552a7fab2b50) is called the n th element of the sequence.

The index notation can also be used for distinguishing some variables called _[parameters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parameter\_(mathematics) "Parameter (mathematics)")_ from the "true variables". In fact, parameters are specific variables that are considered as being fixed during the study of a problem. For example, the map ![Image 65: {\displaystyle x\mapsto f(x,t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e82a8366e1505166fcef3257f1fec8f056140157) (see above) would be denoted ![Image 66: {\displaystyle f_{t}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/874c306411e808e8191e8aeb95e3440e1c68d6e9) using index notation, if we define the collection of maps ![Image 67: {\displaystyle f_{t}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/874c306411e808e8191e8aeb95e3440e1c68d6e9) by the formula ![Image 68: {\displaystyle f_{t}(x)=f(x,t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ee02b1f83705d1df5a9414b665d3e0eead9e786c) for all ![Image 69: {\displaystyle x,t\in X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d9b32f7879b9ed63837691bf63021ba6d450daca).

In the notation ![Image 70: {\displaystyle x\mapsto f(x),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3322446e350dec66a9be262d198979af3d1c0da0) the symbol x does not represent any value; it is simply a [placeholder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Placeholder_name "Placeholder name"), meaning that, if x is replaced by any value on the left of the arrow, it should be replaced by the same value on the right of the arrow. Therefore, x may be replaced by any symbol, often an [interpunct](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interpunct "Interpunct") " ⋅ ". This may be useful for distinguishing the function _f_ (⋅) from its value _f_ (_x_) at x.

For example, ![Image 71: {\displaystyle a(\cdot )^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8e8095cc4a24833a2d13ab6afe89cf29873c13b3) may stand for the function ![Image 72: {\displaystyle x\mapsto ax^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dab922de5eb858b22ba59ddfcab0ed41db3e259d), and ![Image 73: {\textstyle \int _{a}^{\,(\cdot )}f(u)\,du}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ba9e4e970c8a3275e4c0fcceb79a3a55815f73b8) may stand for a function defined by an [integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral "Integral") with variable upper bound: ![Image 74: {\textstyle x\mapsto \int _{a}^{x}f(u)\,du}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9fca324c6d96cbfbeb58d21833ee4321420a712c).

### Specialized notations

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Function_(mathematics)&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Specialized notations")]

There are other, specialized notations for functions in sub-disciplines of mathematics. For example, in [linear algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_algebra "Linear algebra") and [functional analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functional_analysis "Functional analysis"), [linear forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_form "Linear form") and the [vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_(mathematics_and_physics) "Vector (mathematics and physics)") they act upon are denoted using a [dual pair](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_pair "Dual pair") to show the underlying [duality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duality_(mathematics) "Duality (mathematics)"). This is similar to the use of [bra–ket notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bra%E2%80%93ket_notation "Bra–ket notation") in quantum mechanics. In [logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_logic "Mathematical logic") and the [theory of computation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theory_of_computation "Theory of computation"), the function notation of [lambda calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lambda_calculus "Lambda calculus") is used to explicitly express the basic notions of function [abstraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abstraction_(computer_science) "Abstraction (computer science)") and [application](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_application "Function application"). In [category theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category_theory "Category theory") and [homological algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homological_algebra "Homological algebra"), networks of functions are described in terms of how they and their compositions [commute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_property "Commutative property") with each other using [commutative diagrams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_diagram "Commutative diagram") that extend and generalize the arrow notation for functions described above.

### Functions of more than one variable

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Function_(mathematics)&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Functions of more than one variable")]

In some cases the argument of a function may be an ordered pair of elements taken from some set or sets. For example, a function f can be defined as mapping any pair of real numbers ![Image 75: {\displaystyle (x,y)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/41cf50e4a314ca8e2c30964baa8d26e5be7a9386) to the sum of their squares, ![Image 76: {\displaystyle x^{2}+y^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f455605b597282c27d7cf2238821bc331479a7e9). Such a function is commonly written as ![Image 77: {\displaystyle f(x,y)=x^{2}+y^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/62cd27ae17a5f4e156905fdcee054a41d15fa36f) and referred to as "a function of two variables". Likewise one can have a function of three or more variables, with notations such as ![Image 78: {\displaystyle f(w,x,y)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e55642fd8a3e6f2d566c81564b0a3481d4bfaf2f), ![Image 79: {\displaystyle f(w,x,y,z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/580ec13f912c2810fd8d118acc67daad3924b005).

| Term | Distinction from "function" |
| --- | --- |
| [Map/Mapping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Map_(mathematics) "Map (mathematics)") | None; the terms are synonymous.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics)#cite_note-15) |
| A map can have _any set_ as its codomain, while, in some contexts, typically in older books, the codomain of a function is specifically the set of [real](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number") or [complex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number") numbers.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics)#cite_note-Lang87p43-16) |
| Alternatively, a map is associated with a _special structure_ (e.g. by explicitly specifying a structured codomain in its definition). For example, a [linear map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_map "Linear map").[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics)#cite_note-Apostol81p35-17) |
| [Homomorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homomorphism "Homomorphism") | A function between two [structures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Structure_(mathematics) "Structure (mathematics)") of the same type that preserves the operations of the structure (e.g. a [group homomorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_homomorphism "Group homomorphism")).[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics)#cite_note-18) |
| [Morphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morphism "Morphism") | A generalisation of homomorphisms to any [category](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category_(mathematics) "Category (mathematics)"), even when the objects of the category are not sets (for example, a [group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_(mathematics) "Group (mathematics)") defines a category with only one object, which has the elements of the group as morphisms; see [Category (mathematics) §Examples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category_(mathematics)#Examples "Category (mathematics)") for this example and other similar ones).[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics)#cite_note-19) |

A function may also be called a **map** or a **mapping**, but some authors make a distinction between the term "map" and "function". For example, the term "map" is often reserved for a "function" with some sort of special structure (e.g. [maps of manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maps_of_manifolds "Maps of manifolds")). In particular _map_ may be used in place of _homomorphism_ for the sake of succinctness (e.g., [linear map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_map "Linear map") or _map from G to H_ instead of _[group homomorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group\_homomorphism "Group homomorphism") from G to H_). Some authors[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics)#cite_note-Apostol81p35-17) reserve the word _mapping_ for the case where the structure of the codomain belongs explicitly to the definition of the function.

Some authors, such as [Serge Lang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Serge_Lang "Serge Lang"),[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics)#cite_note-Lang87p43-16) use "function" only to refer to maps for which the [codomain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Codomain "Codomain") is a subset of the [real](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number") or [complex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number") numbers, and use the term _mapping_ for more general functions.

In the theory of [dynamical systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynamical_system "Dynamical system"), a map denotes an [evolution function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discrete-time_dynamical_system "Discrete-time dynamical system") used to create [discrete dynamical systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynamical_system#Maps "Dynamical system"). See also [Poincaré map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poincar%C3%A9_map "Poincaré map").

Whichever definition of _map_ is used, related terms like _[domain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain\_of\_a\_function "Domain of a function")_, _[codomain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Codomain "Codomain")_, _[injective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Injective\_function "Injective function")_, _[continuous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous\_function "Continuous function")_ have the same meaning as for a function.

Specifying a function
---------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Function_(mathematics)&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Specifying a function")]

Given a function ![Image 80: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61), by definition, to each element ![Image 81: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) of the domain of the function ![Image 82: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61), there is a unique element associated to it, the value ![Image 83: {\displaystyle f(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/202945cce41ecebb6f643f31d119c514bec7a074) of ![Image 84: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) at ![Image 85: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4). There are several ways to specify or describe how ![Image 86: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) is related to ![Image 87: {\displaystyle f(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/202945cce41ecebb6f643f31d119c514bec7a074), both explicitly and implicitly. Sometimes, a theorem or an [axiom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiom "Axiom") asserts the existence of a function having some properties, without describing it more precisely. Often, the specification or description is referred to as the definition of the function ![Image 88: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61).

### By listing function values

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Function_(mathematics)&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: By listing function values")]

On a finite set a function may be defined by listing the elements of the codomain that are associated to the elements of the domain. For example, if ![Image 89: {\displaystyle A=\{1,2,3\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/75709a44930c3f020087d434e9bb260a0c46a0c8), then one can define a function ![Image 90: {\displaystyle f:A\to \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/29b96eb73b42abe3f9b5fc1b9a94dece18f9abf5) by ![Image 91: {\displaystyle f(1)=2,f(2)=3,f(3)=4.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d48eb562511cd44c4a8d161d225c74e1a3cc626d)

Functions are often defined by an [expression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expression_(mathematics) "Expression (mathematics)") that describes a combination of [arithmetic operations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic_operations "Arithmetic operations") and previously defined functions; such a formula allows computing the value of the function from the value of any element of the domain. For example, in the above example, ![Image 92: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) can be defined by the formula ![Image 93: {\displaystyle f(n)=n+1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/13080ea3fb204650e60021bcd68f35b7cb82cd50), for ![Image 94: {\displaystyle n\in \{1,2,3\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a7fd153aab97fa5d1cd50604f5b61c15bda65a87).

When a function is defined this way, the determination of its domain is sometimes difficult. If the formula that defines the function contains divisions, the values of the variable for which a denominator is zero must be excluded from the domain; thus, for a complicated function, the determination of the domain passes through the computation of the [zeros](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero_of_a_function "Zero of a function") of auxiliary functions. Similarly, if [square roots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_root "Square root") occur in the definition of a function from ![Image 95: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc) to ![Image 96: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0522388d36b55de7babe4bbfc49475eaf590c2bd) the domain is included in the set of the values of the variable for which the arguments of the square roots are nonnegative.

For example, ![Image 97: {\displaystyle f(x)={\sqrt {1+x^{2}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b79c34c2d3762de89fa5bfe544076a6f7d8ca57e) defines a function ![Image 98: {\displaystyle f:\mathbb {R} \to \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1e3a10a3ad05781f5cf9c2d875a02227e21a8448) whose domain is ![Image 99: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0522388d36b55de7babe4bbfc49475eaf590c2bd) because ![Image 100: {\displaystyle 1+x^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ee93a0bc45fd86054d8ac256bb58866c0ffb6336) is always positive if x is a real number. On the other hand, ![Image 101: {\displaystyle f(x)={\sqrt {1-x^{2}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1b779387bbccf8dcb6c6a52a8e052ae04529956c) defines a function from the reals to the reals whose domain is reduced to the interval [−1, 1]. (In old texts, such a domain was called the _domain of definition_ of the function.)

Functions can be classified by the nature of formulas that define them:

*   A [quadratic function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadratic_function "Quadratic function") is a function that may be written ![Image 102: {\displaystyle f(x)=ax^{2}+bx+c,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3ba19534f962564659c4c764c520a9b1c2583290) where _a_, _b_, _c_ are [constants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constant_(mathematics) "Constant (mathematics)").
*   More generally, a [polynomial function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial_function "Polynomial function") is a function that can be defined by a formula involving only additions, subtractions, multiplications, and [exponentiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponentiation "Exponentiation") to nonnegative integer powers. For example, ![Image 103: {\displaystyle f(x)=x^{3}-3x-1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5f7a70ffe2450e80f1d0a91cb1279ecf82ffe0a) and ![Image 104: {\displaystyle f(x)=(x-1)(x^{3}+1)+2x^{2}-1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/329ca932140f4c467b17cf1426e063090257e515) are polynomial functions of ![Image 105: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4).
*   A [rational function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_function "Rational function") is the same, with divisions also allowed, such as ![Image 106: {\displaystyle f(x)={\frac {x-1}{x+1}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b1175bc3324fa6a9f39bdc801c5cfa9e59e0eace) and ![Image 107: {\displaystyle f(x)={\frac {1}{x+1}}+{\frac {3}{x}}-{\frac {2}{x-1}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6d4091d575d27d2f4262b6643c8cd73a0ec82547)
*   An [algebraic function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_function "Algebraic function") is the same, with [n th roots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nth_root "Nth root") and [roots of polynomials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero_of_a_function "Zero of a function") also allowed.
*   An [elementary function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementary_function "Elementary function")[[note 4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics)#cite_note-20) is the same, with [logarithms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logarithm "Logarithm") and [exponential functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponential_functions "Exponential functions") allowed.

### Inverse and implicit functions

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Function_(mathematics)&action=edit&section=16 "Edit section: Inverse and implicit functions")]

A function ![Image 108: {\displaystyle f:X\to Y,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/43b6b077a3059ca728f62c163fec3d93b8429769) with domain X and codomain Y, is [bijective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bijective "Bijective"), if for every y in Y, there is one and only one element x in X such that _y_ = _f_(_x_). In this case, the [inverse function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverse_function "Inverse function") of f is the function ![Image 109: {\displaystyle f^{-1}:Y\to X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e989122f1ec4c60658a0bb4b619b1473da820393) that maps ![Image 110: {\displaystyle y\in Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cee1c0ec36a82f33f5e3d7434d5667881b4ec323) to the element ![Image 111: {\displaystyle x\in X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3e580967f68f36743e894aa7944f032dda6ea01d) such that _y_ = _f_(_x_). For example, the [natural logarithm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_logarithm "Natural logarithm") is a bijective function from the positive real numbers to the real numbers. It thus has an inverse, called the [exponential function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponential_function "Exponential function"), that maps the real numbers onto the positive numbers.

If a function ![Image 112: {\displaystyle f:X\to Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/abd1e080abef4bbdab67b43819c6431e7561361c) is not bijective, it may occur that one can select subsets ![Image 113: {\displaystyle E\subseteq X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4359fae89639fe08a113bd9154bff6b52047c7c3) and ![Image 114: {\displaystyle F\subseteq Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d26bfc0333372b856bcce14daf66a1086ca46270) such that the [restriction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Restriction_of_a_function "Restriction of a function") of f to E is a bijection from E to F, and has thus an inverse. The [inverse trigonometric functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverse_trigonometric_functions "Inverse trigonometric functions") are defined this way. For example, the [cosine function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosine_function "Cosine function") induces, by restriction, a bijection from the [interval](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interval_(mathematics) "Interval (mathematics)")[0, _π_] onto the interval [−1, 1], and its inverse function, called [arccosine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arccosine "Arccosine"), maps [−1, 1] onto [0, _π_]. The other inverse trigonometric functions are defined similarly.

More generally, given a [binary relation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_relation "Binary relation")R between two sets X and Y, let E be a subset of X such that, for every ![Image 115: {\displaystyle x\in E,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ac74fb0c2e4233f6f7171ba8efbc87da28a8ff02) there is some ![Image 116: {\displaystyle y\in Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cee1c0ec36a82f33f5e3d7434d5667881b4ec323) such that _x R y_. If one has a criterion allowing selecting such a y for every ![Image 117: {\displaystyle x\in E,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ac74fb0c2e4233f6f7171ba8efbc87da28a8ff02) this defines a function ![Image 118: {\displaystyle f:E\to Y,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c9b5356c234a6fda8ca6a341339b83609ff327f0) called an [implicit function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Implicit_function "Implicit function"), because it is implicitly defined by the relation R.

For example, the equation of the [unit circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_circle "Unit circle")![Image 119: {\displaystyle x^{2}+y^{2}=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ec84b90236512e8d27ff1a8f7707b60b63327de1) defines a relation on real numbers. If −1 <_x_< 1 there are two possible values of y, one positive and one negative. For _x_ = ± 1, these two values become both equal to 0. Otherwise, there is no possible value of y. This means that the equation defines two implicit functions with domain [−1, 1] and respective codomains [0, +∞) and (−∞, 0].

In this example, the equation can be solved in y, giving ![Image 120: {\displaystyle y=\pm {\sqrt {1-x^{2}}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3d2200b08e3d1e0047706d8545856c4637fd3bac) but, in more complicated examples, this is impossible. For example, the relation ![Image 121: {\displaystyle y^{5}+y+x=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d9d82fbc2ad8b2a28d99505ba655143535160a9c) defines y as an implicit function of x, called the [Bring radical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bring_radical "Bring radical"), which has ![Image 122: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc) as domain and range. The Bring radical cannot be expressed in terms of the four arithmetic operations and [n th roots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nth_root "Nth root").

The [implicit function theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Implicit_function_theorem "Implicit function theorem") provides mild [differentiability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differentiability "Differentiability") conditions for existence and uniqueness of an implicit function in the neighborhood of a point.

### Using differential calculus

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Function_(mathematics)&action=edit&section=17 "Edit section: Using differential calculus")]

Many functions can be defined as the [antiderivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiderivative "Antiderivative") of another function. This is the case of the [natural logarithm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_logarithm "Natural logarithm"), which is the antiderivative of 1/_x_ that is 0 for _x_ = 1. Another common example is the [error function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Error_function "Error function").

More generally, many functions, including most [special functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_function "Special function"), can be defined as solutions of [differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_equation "Differential equation"). The simplest example is probably the [exponential function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponential_function "Exponential function"), which can be defined as the unique function that is equal to its derivative and takes the value 1 for _x_ = 0.

[Power series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_series "Power series") can be used to define functions on the domain in which they converge. For example, the [exponential function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponential_function "Exponential function") is given by ![Image 123: {\textstyle e^{x}=\sum _{n=0}^{\infty }{x^{n} \over n!}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a0933c92fd40ead20121e8de3ad48c04ff6fcfb1). However, as the coefficients of a series are quite arbitrary, a function that is the sum of a convergent series is generally defined otherwise, and the sequence of the coefficients is the result of some computation based on another definition. Then, the power series can be used to enlarge the domain of the function. Typically, if a function for a real variable is the sum of its [Taylor series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taylor_series "Taylor series") in some interval, this power series allows immediately enlarging the domain to a subset of the [complex numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number"), the [disc of convergence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disc_of_convergence "Disc of convergence") of the series. Then [analytic continuation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytic_continuation "Analytic continuation") allows enlarging further the domain for including almost the whole [complex plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_plane "Complex plane"). This process is the method that is generally used for defining the [logarithm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logarithm "Logarithm"), the [exponential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponential_function "Exponential function") and the [trigonometric functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trigonometric_functions "Trigonometric functions") of a complex number.

Functions whose domain are the nonnegative integers, known as [sequences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sequence "Sequence"), are sometimes defined by [recurrence relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recurrence_relation "Recurrence relation").

The [factorial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Factorial "Factorial") function on the nonnegative integers (![Image 124: {\displaystyle n\mapsto n!}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d43615cae2b2eb2b4402bcd0b2bd7642eb855e5e)) is a basic example, as it can be defined by the recurrence relation

![Image 125: {\displaystyle n!=n(n-1)!\quad {\text{for}}\quad n>0,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/12bc6067e14b24bf50711c08808b7b2b3b7387d0)

and the initial condition

![Image 126: {\displaystyle 0!=1.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/73b6aeeba784d2992f64addf82d9cbbf7962a722)

Representing a function
-----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Function_(mathematics)&action=edit&section=19 "Edit section: Representing a function")]

A [graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_of_a_function "Graph of a function") is commonly used to give an intuitive picture of a function. As an example of how a graph helps to understand a function, it is easy to see from its graph whether a function is increasing or decreasing. Some functions may also be represented by [bar charts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bar_chart "Bar chart").

[![Image 127](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Motor_vehicle_deaths_in_the_US.svg/250px-Motor_vehicle_deaths_in_the_US.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Motor_vehicle_deaths_in_the_US.svg)

The function mapping each year to its US motor vehicle death count, shown as a [line chart](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_chart "Line chart")

[![Image 128](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Motor_vehicle_deaths_in_the_US_histogram.svg/250px-Motor_vehicle_deaths_in_the_US_histogram.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Motor_vehicle_deaths_in_the_US_histogram.svg)

The same function, shown as a bar chart

Given a function ![Image 129: {\displaystyle f:X\to Y,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/43b6b077a3059ca728f62c163fec3d93b8429769) its _graph_ is, formally, the set

![Image 130: {\displaystyle G=\{(x,f(x))\mid x\in X\}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c0f60a64d69fdc91d64307936f7d9970d9f3ec6b)

In the frequent case where X and Y are subsets of the [real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number") (or may be identified with such subsets, e.g. [intervals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interval_(mathematics) "Interval (mathematics)")), an element ![Image 131: {\displaystyle (x,y)\in G}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/124f43d066e62e8deeb05b6a0c75922786573fad) may be identified with a point having coordinates _x_, _y_ in a 2-dimensional coordinate system, e.g. the [Cartesian plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_plane "Cartesian plane"). Parts of this may create a [plot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plot_(graphics) "Plot (graphics)") that represents (parts of) the function. The use of plots is so ubiquitous that they too are called the _graph of the function_. Graphic representations of functions are also possible in other coordinate systems. For example, the graph of the [square function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_function "Square function")

![Image 132: {\displaystyle x\mapsto x^{2},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/91ec217e885b4ff48e302d65301cf25c6fc7c447)

consisting of all points with coordinates ![Image 133: {\displaystyle (x,x^{2})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2f624b81d622794760acd0cd347602595fa2504f) for ![Image 134: {\displaystyle x\in \mathbb {R} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dc20967d7838bbfb6e9df31756dc24de3573ae3c) yields, when depicted in Cartesian coordinates, the well known [parabola](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parabola "Parabola"). If the same quadratic function ![Image 135: {\displaystyle x\mapsto x^{2},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/91ec217e885b4ff48e302d65301cf25c6fc7c447) with the same formal graph, consisting of pairs of numbers, is plotted instead in [polar coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_coordinates "Polar coordinates")![Image 136: {\displaystyle (r,\theta )=(x,x^{2}),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4d4a9e5ffd2cb2f9c274ea62034dc8e17d454e1) the plot obtained is [Fermat's spiral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermat%27s_spiral "Fermat's spiral").

A function can be represented as a table of values. If the domain of a function is finite, then the function can be completely specified in this way. For example, the multiplication function ![Image 137: {\displaystyle f:\{1,\ldots ,5\}^{2}\to \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/75c6d1fa74d7c5be5592115baba6e7311dc3fcc0) defined as ![Image 138: {\displaystyle f(x,y)=xy}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a3ee958d3d5aa49d9e3954ebf344a2bddd4f4485) can be represented by the familiar [multiplication table](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication_table "Multiplication table")

| y x | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 | 5 |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 | 5 |
| 2 | 2 | 4 | 6 | 8 | 10 |
| 3 | 3 | 6 | 9 | 12 | 15 |
| 4 | 4 | 8 | 12 | 16 | 20 |
| 5 | 5 | 10 | 15 | 20 | 25 |

On the other hand, if a function's domain is continuous, a table can give the values of the function at specific values of the domain. If an intermediate value is needed, [interpolation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interpolation "Interpolation") can be used to estimate the value of the function. For example, a portion of a table for the sine function might be given as follows, with values rounded to 6 decimal places:

| x | sin _x_ |
| --- | --- |
| 1.289 | 0.960557 |
| 1.290 | 0.960835 |
| 1.291 | 0.961112 |
| 1.292 | 0.961387 |
| 1.293 | 0.961662 |

Before the advent of handheld calculators and personal computers, such tables were often compiled and published for functions such as logarithms and trigonometric functions.

A bar chart can represent a function whose domain is a finite set, the [natural numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number "Natural number"), or the [integers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer"). In this case, an element x of the domain is represented by an [interval](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interval_(mathematics) "Interval (mathematics)") of the x-axis, and the corresponding value of the function, _f_(_x_), is represented by a [rectangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle "Rectangle") whose base is the interval corresponding to x and whose height is _f_(_x_) (possibly negative, in which case the bar extends below the x-axis).

This section describes general properties of functions, that are independent of specific properties of the domain and the codomain.

There are a number of standard functions that occur frequently:

*   For every set X, there is a unique function, called the **empty function**, or **empty map**, from the [empty set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empty_set "Empty set") to X. The graph of an empty function is the empty set.[[note 5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics)#cite_note-21) The existence of empty functions is needed both for the coherency of the theory and for avoiding exceptions concerning the empty set in many statements. Under the usual set-theoretic definition of a function as an [ordered triplet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tuple "Tuple") (or equivalent ones), there is exactly one empty function for each set, thus the empty function ![Image 139: {\displaystyle \varnothing \to X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8cbc0203001f6a0e2648aa18ed1500c6a7637ab9) is not equal to ![Image 140: {\displaystyle \varnothing \to Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b9dbe760456d1e87721560f1fa4a96e63db2407b) if and only if ![Image 141: {\displaystyle X\neq Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/be749f23597a5705f52b2c13f89cde4389ff7f0f), although their graphs are both the [empty set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empty_set "Empty set").
*   For every set X and every [singleton set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Singleton_set "Singleton set"){_s_}, there is a unique function from X to {_s_}, which maps every element of X to s. This is a surjection (see below) unless X is the empty set.
*   Given a function ![Image 142: {\displaystyle f:X\to Y,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/43b6b077a3059ca728f62c163fec3d93b8429769) the _canonical surjection_ of f onto its image ![Image 143: {\displaystyle f(X)=\{f(x)\mid x\in X\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ca3316d91530b49bc3304209486939bad604be85) is the function from X to _f_(_X_) that maps x to _f_(_x_).
*   For every [subset](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subset "Subset")A of a set X, the [inclusion map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inclusion_map "Inclusion map") of A into X is the injective (see below) function that maps every element of A to itself.
*   The [identity function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Identity_function "Identity function") on a set X, often denoted by id _X_, is the inclusion of X into itself.

### Function composition

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Function_(mathematics)&action=edit&section=25 "Edit section: Function composition")]

Given two functions ![Image 144: {\displaystyle f:X\to Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/abd1e080abef4bbdab67b43819c6431e7561361c) and ![Image 145: {\displaystyle g:Y\to Z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/44e7e6d2116baaa7aa88d1adbf796ade5997a237) such that the domain of g is the codomain of f, their _composition_ is the function ![Image 146: {\displaystyle g\circ f:X\rightarrow Z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68801eb2856ac23fc685a20fc4a4761873d9d9eb) defined by

![Image 147: {\displaystyle (g\circ f)(x)=g(f(x)).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/50410f65ec5a8654714720d54c21fcaa3d2d2916)

That is, the value of ![Image 148: {\displaystyle g\circ f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/10b5ad4985af48d0fb7efa3c8afa5ad7d42bfc92) is obtained by first applying _f_ to _x_ to obtain _y_ = _f_(_x_) and then applying _g_ to the result y to obtain _g_(_y_) = _g_(_f_(_x_)). In this notation, the function that is applied first is always written on the right.

The composition ![Image 149: {\displaystyle g\circ f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/10b5ad4985af48d0fb7efa3c8afa5ad7d42bfc92) is an [operation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operation_(mathematics) "Operation (mathematics)") on functions that is defined only if the codomain of the first function is the domain of the second one. Even when both ![Image 150: {\displaystyle g\circ f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/10b5ad4985af48d0fb7efa3c8afa5ad7d42bfc92) and ![Image 151: {\displaystyle f\circ g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b2f61ca7838709fbae07dce9c0d513770f10cfae) satisfy these conditions, the composition is not necessarily [commutative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_property "Commutative property"), that is, the functions ![Image 152: {\displaystyle g\circ f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/10b5ad4985af48d0fb7efa3c8afa5ad7d42bfc92) and ![Image 153: {\displaystyle f\circ g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b2f61ca7838709fbae07dce9c0d513770f10cfae) need not be equal, and may deliver different values for the same argument. For example, let _f_(_x_) = _x_ 2 and _g_(_x_) = _x_ + 1, then ![Image 154: {\displaystyle g(f(x))=x^{2}+1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9050d0a90f81df4de49249892dcbf3bffca33fbc) and ![Image 155: {\displaystyle f(g(x))=(x+1)^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2ea84d7ee047fd301242a46fbdf5c994605eb660) agree just for ![Image 156: {\displaystyle x=0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/71320d636c7a43546bf4e94edb94649c5b2e82b9)

The function composition is [associative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Associative_property "Associative property") in the sense that, if one of ![Image 157: {\displaystyle (h\circ g)\circ f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c227490c73a1fd0ed1ef9a3f9d989398e2aca797) and ![Image 158: {\displaystyle h\circ (g\circ f)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b0e05ca5e3aeb39bf0b062d3f4ebcfc2e339c16f) is defined, then the other is also defined, and they are equal, that is, ![Image 159: {\displaystyle (h\circ g)\circ f=h\circ (g\circ f).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/463113c07156c96f462e121f73e238e185ee0134) Therefore, it is usual to just write ![Image 160: {\displaystyle h\circ g\circ f.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3f903279fd25d031541feb82152f1592e2086598)

The [identity functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Identity_function "Identity function")![Image 161: {\displaystyle \operatorname {id} _{X}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/623b238853010f9319f926446bc6cb2253e53e02) and ![Image 162: {\displaystyle \operatorname {id} _{Y}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8f280c0425786426a2cac3d1df83e73a5cdb4d1a) are respectively a [right identity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_identity "Right identity") and a [left identity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Left_identity "Left identity") for functions from X to Y. That is, if f is a function with domain X, and codomain Y, one has ![Image 163: {\displaystyle f\circ \operatorname {id} _{X}=\operatorname {id} _{Y}\circ f=f.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ae14fa7c63c808ffaf93f6df9b16baf3f87f6cff)

*   [![Image 164: A composite function g(f(x)) can be visualized as the combination of two "machines".](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Function_machine5.svg/250px-Function_machine5.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Function_machine5.svg "A composite function g(f(x)) can be visualized as the combination of two \"machines\".")

Let ![Image 167: {\displaystyle f:X\to Y.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b918aeefba8721a6732102a5848bd4238615ec55) The _image_ under f of an element x of the domain X is _f_(_x_).[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics)#cite_note-EOM_Function-7) If _A_ is any subset of _X_, then the _image_ of A under f, denoted _f_(_A_), is the subset of the codomain _Y_ consisting of all images of elements of A,[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics)#cite_note-EOM_Function-7) that is,

![Image 168: {\displaystyle f(A)=\{f(x)\mid x\in A\}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/95793cb619ae7bd33c59c5d5de5fd02ee1b092d4)

The _image_ of _f_ is the image of the whole domain, that is, _f_(_X_).[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics)#cite_note-PCM_p.11-22) It is also called the [range](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Range_of_a_function "Range of a function") of f,[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics)#cite_note-EOM_Function-7)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics)#cite_note-T&K_Calc_p.3-8)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics)#cite_note-Trench_RA_pp.30-32-9)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics)#cite_note-TBB_RA_pp.A4-A5-10) although the term _range_ may also refer to the codomain.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics)#cite_note-TBB_RA_pp.A4-A5-10)[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics)#cite_note-PCM_p.11-22)[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics)#cite_note-standard-23)

On the other hand, the _[inverse image](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverse\_image "Inverse image")_ or _[preimage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Preimage "Preimage")_ under f of an element y of the codomain Y is the set of all elements of the domain _X_ whose images under f equal y.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics)#cite_note-EOM_Function-7) In symbols, the preimage of y is denoted by ![Image 169: {\displaystyle f^{-1}(y)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8b357745fa4a2178733a502b4432072be8222fd4) and is given by the equation

![Image 170: {\displaystyle f^{-1}(y)=\{x\in X\mid f(x)=y\}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ce7e4068e7e4675fb278d2098f3eeb0ec67c2881)

Likewise, the preimage of a subset _B_ of the codomain _Y_ is the set of the preimages of the elements of _B_, that is, it is the subset of the domain _X_ consisting of all elements of _X_ whose images belong to _B_.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics)#cite_note-EOM_Function-7) It is denoted by ![Image 171: {\displaystyle f^{-1}(B)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/572ddad8cd0a0758fb98e1c94c432dc2f7a06636) and is given by the equation

![Image 172: {\displaystyle f^{-1}(B)=\{x\in X\mid f(x)\in B\}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/485f68954316eb2c28391474d3df7c40fd589413)

For example, the preimage of ![Image 173: {\displaystyle \{4,9\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/44b235effd45a537b68a200facce95137756ab04) under the [square function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_function "Square function") is the set ![Image 174: {\displaystyle \{-3,-2,2,3\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132441f34cc733c4645d0fa80d8544fc90262d9a).

By definition of a function, the image of an element _x_ of the domain is always a single element of the codomain. However, the preimage ![Image 175: {\displaystyle f^{-1}(y)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8b357745fa4a2178733a502b4432072be8222fd4) of an element y of the codomain may be [empty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empty_set "Empty set") or contain any number of elements. For example, if f is the function from the integers to themselves that maps every integer to 0, then ![Image 176: {\displaystyle f^{-1}(0)=\mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/32720d89ba91ec2b5d5b706db20ee12420f187da).

If ![Image 177: {\displaystyle f:X\to Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/abd1e080abef4bbdab67b43819c6431e7561361c) is a function, _A_ and _B_ are subsets of _X_, and _C_ and _D_ are subsets of _Y_, then one has the following properties:

*   ![Image 178: {\displaystyle A\subseteq B\Longrightarrow f(A)\subseteq f(B)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/74aab63eb9c503ca1d6168cba3d4a956f1aaaf45)
*   ![Image 179: {\displaystyle C\subseteq D\Longrightarrow f^{-1}(C)\subseteq f^{-1}(D)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2e57c217e011000e3e367cf58c8f8911cec1fb7c)
*   ![Image 180: {\displaystyle A\subseteq f^{-1}(f(A))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b7745c86e833c00204c441b3420ecdf3a4e86bbb)
*   ![Image 181: {\displaystyle C\supseteq f(f^{-1}(C))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/20f0c665d4fd411c57fc15878a0407b5e74ec8c0)
*   ![Image 182: {\displaystyle f(f^{-1}(f(A)))=f(A)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b57dc6b4af65497aea87a51de455949fb31fdf6e)
*   ![Image 183: {\displaystyle f^{-1}(f(f^{-1}(C)))=f^{-1}(C)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5d05d156017b8ab2055e098ee863bc393efd2373)

The preimage by f of an element y of the codomain is sometimes called, in some contexts, the [fiber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiber_(mathematics) "Fiber (mathematics)") of _y_ under _f_.

If a function f has an inverse (see below), this inverse is denoted ![Image 184: {\displaystyle f^{-1}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/63d2b575826d75cfdbc1bea2f34ccfa71f1c59b7) In this case ![Image 185: {\displaystyle f^{-1}(C)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ecc65ae260d9fcb0f29161fe1d5a8301c7616721) may denote either the image by ![Image 186: {\displaystyle f^{-1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3e5cfa2f5c08d6fe7d046b73faa6e3f213acc802) or the preimage by f of C. This is not a problem, as these sets are equal. The notation ![Image 187: {\displaystyle f(A)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2711c647e5397c7016ee21bbcea53565480bd5e1) and ![Image 188: {\displaystyle f^{-1}(C)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ecc65ae260d9fcb0f29161fe1d5a8301c7616721) may be ambiguous in the case of sets that contain some subsets as elements, such as ![Image 189: {\displaystyle \{x,\{x\}\}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/532d1022eef8f5f427703de9c6dd5b8d38d86316) In this case, some care may be needed, for example, by using square brackets ![Image 190: {\displaystyle f[A],f^{-1}[C]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6d728b72b3681c1a33529ac867bc49952dc812a4) for images and preimages of subsets and ordinary parentheses for images and preimages of elements.

### Injective, surjective and bijective functions

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Function_(mathematics)&action=edit&section=27 "Edit section: Injective, surjective and bijective functions")]

Let ![Image 191: {\displaystyle f:X\to Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/abd1e080abef4bbdab67b43819c6431e7561361c) be a function.

The function f is _[injective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Injective\_function "Injective function")_ (or _one-to-one_, or is an _injection_) if _f_(_a_) ≠ _f_(_b_) for every two different elements _a_ and _b_ of X.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics)#cite_note-PCM_p.11-22)[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics)#cite_note-EOM_Injection-24) Equivalently, f is injective if and only if, for every ![Image 192: {\displaystyle y\in Y,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/75e1353f0febe9bcf693d849ec82ce8d94e5f7bc) the preimage ![Image 193: {\displaystyle f^{-1}(y)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8b357745fa4a2178733a502b4432072be8222fd4) contains at most one element. An empty function is always injective. If X is not the empty set, then f is injective if and only if there exists a function ![Image 194: {\displaystyle g:Y\to X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/10a9844999dbfd6d1ba60a6d5d37779df277a74f) such that ![Image 195: {\displaystyle g\circ f=\operatorname {id} _{X},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4f5645c27403915e3d21d6eca5fad99cdcd5561b) that is, if f has a [left inverse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Left_inverse_function "Left inverse function").[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics)#cite_note-EOM_Injection-24)_Proof_: If f is injective, for defining g, one chooses an element ![Image 196: {\displaystyle x_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/86f21d0e31751534cd6584264ecf864a6aa792cf) in X (which exists as X is supposed to be nonempty),[[note 6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics)#cite_note-25) and one defines g by ![Image 197: {\displaystyle g(y)=x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b60bb15cd66fb9d9f721d9892b67c01cddd153be) if ![Image 198: {\displaystyle y=f(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2311a6a75c54b0ea085a381ba472c31d59321514) and ![Image 199: {\displaystyle g(y)=x_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b5c58233f69bc7034d7b59fc99d4ada7ddc50243) if ![Image 200: {\displaystyle y\not \in f(X).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b2d355db1cec99772b7d696063b1ef77d712d48f) Conversely, if ![Image 201: {\displaystyle g\circ f=\operatorname {id} _{X},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4f5645c27403915e3d21d6eca5fad99cdcd5561b) and ![Image 202: {\displaystyle y=f(x),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c9867a6ecb3cc19e19e0af39fb46523e69e616c1) then ![Image 203: {\displaystyle x=g(y),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af2f479eab87e0bc784a0ed4246fa429c6164392) and thus ![Image 204: {\displaystyle f^{-1}(y)=\{x\}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4c6684307f77a0709f6a253a27a719b9d8bf8a5a)

The function f is _[surjective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surjective "Surjective")_ (or _onto_, or is a _surjection_) if its range ![Image 205: {\displaystyle f(X)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b884e2d65b3356219702968b6751485fb8f38570) equals its codomain ![Image 206: {\displaystyle Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/961d67d6b454b4df2301ac571808a3538b3a6d3f), that is, if, for each element ![Image 207: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d) of the codomain, there exists some element ![Image 208: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) of the domain such that ![Image 209: {\displaystyle f(x)=y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a5080a8b0a963407ea74ffa50702563771518d1) (in other words, the preimage ![Image 210: {\displaystyle f^{-1}(y)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8b357745fa4a2178733a502b4432072be8222fd4) of every ![Image 211: {\displaystyle y\in Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cee1c0ec36a82f33f5e3d7434d5667881b4ec323) is nonempty).[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics)#cite_note-PCM_p.11-22)[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics)#cite_note-EOM_Surjection-26) If, as usual in modern mathematics, the [axiom of choice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiom_of_choice "Axiom of choice") is assumed, then f is surjective if and only if there exists a function ![Image 212: {\displaystyle g:Y\to X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/10a9844999dbfd6d1ba60a6d5d37779df277a74f) such that ![Image 213: {\displaystyle f\circ g=\operatorname {id} _{Y},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/307ae8225c2aa1e6d08f0bc76bf698729263f9da) that is, if f has a [right inverse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_inverse_function "Right inverse function").[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics)#cite_note-EOM_Surjection-26) The axiom of choice is needed, because, if f is surjective, one defines g by ![Image 214: {\displaystyle g(y)=x,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a68c9ed4e962731675c415d078cad57281ae86b8) where ![Image 215: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) is an _arbitrarily chosen_ element of ![Image 216: {\displaystyle f^{-1}(y).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9041b1fe9d0edbeee6a144fc971a4d51b9e5dda9)

The function f is _[bijective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bijective "Bijective")_ (or is a _bijection_ or a _one-to-one correspondence_) if it is both injective and surjective.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics)#cite_note-PCM_p.11-22)[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics)#cite_note-EOM_Bijection-27) That is, f is bijective if, for every ![Image 217: {\displaystyle y\in Y,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/75e1353f0febe9bcf693d849ec82ce8d94e5f7bc) the preimage ![Image 218: {\displaystyle f^{-1}(y)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8b357745fa4a2178733a502b4432072be8222fd4) contains exactly one element. The function f is bijective if and only if it admits an [inverse function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverse_function "Inverse function"), that is, a function ![Image 219: {\displaystyle g:Y\to X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/10a9844999dbfd6d1ba60a6d5d37779df277a74f) such that ![Image 220: {\displaystyle g\circ f=\operatorname {id} _{X}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2935e82106a6aeb3c354d02246be16fe2ac7e4a0) and ![Image 221: {\displaystyle f\circ g=\operatorname {id} _{Y}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/340d648bc681ff51cbcd847e207a453baccd313b)[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics)#cite_note-EOM_Bijection-27) (Contrarily to the case of surjections, this does not require the axiom of choice; the proof is straightforward).

Every function ![Image 222: {\displaystyle f:X\to Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/abd1e080abef4bbdab67b43819c6431e7561361c) may be [factorized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Factorization "Factorization") as the composition ![Image 223: {\displaystyle i\circ s}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/34c1d5fd76a33e0efee96d289ac9422fcc23ef43) of a surjection followed by an injection, where s is the canonical surjection of X onto _f_(_X_) and i is the canonical injection of _f_(_X_) into Y. This is the _canonical factorization_ of f.

"One-to-one" and "onto" are terms that were more common in the older English language literature; "injective", "surjective", and "bijective" were originally coined as French words in the second quarter of the 20th century by the [Bourbaki group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nicolas_Bourbaki "Nicolas Bourbaki") and imported into English.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics)#cite_note-28) As a word of caution, "a one-to-one function" is one that is injective, while a "one-to-one correspondence" refers to a bijective function. Also, the statement "_f_ maps _X_ _onto_ _Y_" differs from "_f_ maps _X_ _into_ _B_", in that the former implies that _f_ is surjective, while the latter makes no assertion about the nature of _f_. In a complicated reasoning, the one letter difference can easily be missed. Due to the confusing nature of this older terminology, these terms have declined in popularity relative to the Bourbakian terms, which have also the advantage of being more symmetrical.

### Restriction and extension

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Function_(mathematics)&action=edit&section=28 "Edit section: Restriction and extension")]

If ![Image 224: {\displaystyle f:X\to Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/abd1e080abef4bbdab67b43819c6431e7561361c) is a function and _S_ is a subset of _X_, then the _restriction_ of ![Image 225: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) to _S_, denoted ![Image 226: {\displaystyle f|_{S}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8dd8f943a75adb4c382a271541c010eb0d60b3b3), is the function from _S_ to _Y_ defined by

![Image 227: {\displaystyle f|_{S}(x)=f(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dda9855e14e326136e924a70d0b0218a736bb7c1)

for all _x_ in _S_. Restrictions can be used to define partial [inverse functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverse_function "Inverse function"): if there is a [subset](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subset "Subset")_S_ of the domain of a function ![Image 228: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) such that ![Image 229: {\displaystyle f|_{S}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8dd8f943a75adb4c382a271541c010eb0d60b3b3) is injective, then the canonical surjection of ![Image 230: {\displaystyle f|_{S}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8dd8f943a75adb4c382a271541c010eb0d60b3b3) onto its image ![Image 231: {\displaystyle f|_{S}(S)=f(S)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2bac2730187f424f13f20378bbff79347e40dcea) is a bijection, and thus has an inverse function from ![Image 232: {\displaystyle f(S)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/99a865d36b32ea1d60f15dc3093f5b28093f192b) to _S_. One application is the definition of [inverse trigonometric functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverse_trigonometric_functions "Inverse trigonometric functions"). For example, the [cosine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosine "Cosine") function is injective when restricted to the [interval](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interval_(mathematics) "Interval (mathematics)")[0, _π_]. The image of this restriction is the interval [−1, 1], and thus the restriction has an inverse function from [−1, 1] to [0, _π_], which is called [arccosine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arccosine "Arccosine") and is denoted arccos.

Function restriction may also be used for "gluing" functions together. Let ![Image 233: {\textstyle X=\bigcup _{i\in I}U_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b87cdc2074a4c87a94344669b3d5d69c8228252a) be the decomposition of X as a [union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_union "Set union") of subsets, and suppose that a function ![Image 234: {\displaystyle f_{i}:U_{i}\to Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/767ac1e7aabcf452776449785782fd0f36fec9c9) is defined on each ![Image 235: {\displaystyle U_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b21a6f475b0e68475c6019abe1fed0b415e0e42) such that for each pair ![Image 236: {\displaystyle i,j}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f4cbf8bbc622154cda8208d6e339495fe16a1f9a) of indices, the restrictions of ![Image 237: {\displaystyle f_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/65da883ca3d16b461e46c94777b0d9c4aa010e79) and ![Image 238: {\displaystyle f_{j}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/acc195ab3f9d65994b47774eb013601d09217aee) to ![Image 239: {\displaystyle U_{i}\cap U_{j}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/be592e07a07b4ce92cc30774ae3a8d984eca426e) are equal. Then this defines a unique function ![Image 240: {\displaystyle f:X\to Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/abd1e080abef4bbdab67b43819c6431e7561361c) such that ![Image 241: {\displaystyle f|_{U_{i}}=f_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/92463d8f349ef5f9731495571ea43612abc5efd6) for all i. This is the way that functions on [manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manifold "Manifold") are defined.

An _extension_ of a function f is a function g such that f is a restriction of g. A typical use of this concept is the process of [analytic continuation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytic_continuation "Analytic continuation"), that allows extending functions whose domain is a small part of the [complex plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_plane "Complex plane") to functions whose domain is almost the whole complex plane.

Here is another classical example of a function extension that is encountered when studying [homographies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homography "Homography") of the [real line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_line "Real line"). A _homography_ is a function ![Image 242: {\displaystyle h(x)={\frac {ax+b}{cx+d}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/30ea2d0884deecb78aa8fcff23d688b6cc0f512d) such that _ad_ − _bc_ ≠ 0. Its domain is the set of all [real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number") different from ![Image 243: {\displaystyle -d/c,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b1fe216fd10ada1d567e72ed5f059bfe97f1a1ba) and its image is the set of all real numbers different from ![Image 244: {\displaystyle a/c.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ff6eba9778a4571d653a0173b97d104de1ef7216) If one extends the real line to the [projectively extended real line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projectively_extended_real_line "Projectively extended real line") by including ∞, one may extend h to a bijection from the extended real line to itself by setting ![Image 245: {\displaystyle h(\infty )=a/c}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/993cd2447b94a4bbf667f8524f89ac64114d7fb8) and ![Image 246: {\displaystyle h(-d/c)=\infty }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b0b4e44944640076eadf15cd9062568526a2f868).

The idea of function, starting in the 17th century, was fundamental to the new [infinitesimal calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinitesimal_calculus "Infinitesimal calculus"). At that time, only [real-valued](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real-valued_function "Real-valued function") functions of a [real variable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_of_a_real_variable "Function of a real variable") were considered, and all functions were assumed to be [smooth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smooth_function "Smooth function"). But the definition was soon extended to [functions of several variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics)#Multivariate_function) and to [functions of a complex variable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functions_of_a_complex_variable "Functions of a complex variable"). In the second half of the 19th century, the mathematically rigorous definition of a function was introduced, and functions with arbitrary domains and codomains were defined.

Functions are now used throughout all areas of mathematics. In introductory [calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus "Calculus"), when the word _function_ is used without qualification, it means a real-valued function of a single real variable. The more general definition of a function is usually introduced to second or third year college students with [STEM](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/STEM "STEM") majors, and in their senior year they are introduced to calculus in a larger, more rigorous setting in courses such as [real analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_analysis "Real analysis") and [complex analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_analysis "Complex analysis").

Graph of two trigonometric functions: [sine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sine "Sine") and [cosine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosine "Cosine").

A _real function_ is a [real-valued](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real-valued_function "Real-valued function")[function of a real variable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_of_a_real_variable "Function of a real variable"), that is, a function whose codomain is the [field of real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number") and whose domain is a set of [real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number") that contains an [interval](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interval_(mathematics) "Interval (mathematics)"). In this section, these functions are simply called _functions_.

The functions that are most commonly considered in mathematics and its applications have some regularity, that is they are [continuous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_function "Continuous function"), [differentiable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differentiable_function "Differentiable function"), and even [analytic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytic_function "Analytic function"). This regularity insures that these functions can be visualized by their [graphs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics)#Graph_and_plots). In this section, all functions are differentiable in some interval.

Functions enjoy [pointwise operations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pointwise_operation "Pointwise operation"), that is, if f and g are functions, their sum, difference and product are functions defined by

![Image 250: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}(f+g)(x)&=f(x)+g(x)\\(f-g)(x)&=f(x)-g(x)\\(f\cdot g)(x)&=f(x)\cdot g(x)\\\end{aligned}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e426d18a2bc5fb702b5ba0497477739c65f1a667)

The domains of the resulting functions are the [intersection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_intersection "Set intersection") of the domains of f and g. The quotient of two functions is defined similarly by

![Image 251: {\displaystyle {\frac {f}{g}}(x)={\frac {f(x)}{g(x)}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3f8c6b970bb8c30650eaea1922c3921aec7d659)

but the domain of the resulting function is obtained by removing the [zeros](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero_of_a_function "Zero of a function") of g from the intersection of the domains of f and g.

The [polynomial functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial_function "Polynomial function") are defined by [polynomials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial "Polynomial"), and their domain is the whole set of real numbers. They include [constant functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constant_function "Constant function"), [linear functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_function "Linear function") and [quadratic functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadratic_function "Quadratic function"). [Rational functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_function "Rational function") are quotients of two polynomial functions, and their domain is the real numbers with a finite number of them removed to avoid [division by zero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_by_zero "Division by zero"). The simplest rational function is the function ![Image 252: {\displaystyle x\mapsto {\frac {1}{x}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/669c4e870914fd443f1c2264450695a3b98fa040) whose graph is a [hyperbola](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbola "Hyperbola"), and whose domain is the whole [real line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_line "Real line") except for 0.

The [derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative "Derivative") of a real differentiable function is a real function. An [antiderivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiderivative "Antiderivative") of a continuous real function is a real function that has the original function as a derivative. For example, the function ![Image 253: {\textstyle x\mapsto {\frac {1}{x}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c4e512047f779008dd32f5b041baceb2c4dfa9a8) is continuous, and even differentiable, on the positive real numbers. Thus one antiderivative, which takes the value zero for _x_ = 1, is a differentiable function called the [natural logarithm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_logarithm "Natural logarithm").

A real function f is [monotonic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monotonic_function "Monotonic function") in an interval if the sign of ![Image 254: {\displaystyle {\frac {f(x)-f(y)}{x-y}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/857bf1f8402b34ead332926a1050dff7f421bc3d) does not depend of the choice of x and y in the interval. If the function is differentiable in the interval, it is monotonic if the sign of the derivative is constant in the interval. If a real function f is monotonic in an interval I, it has an [inverse function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverse_function "Inverse function"), which is a real function with domain _f_(_I_) and image I. This is how [inverse trigonometric functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverse_trigonometric_functions "Inverse trigonometric functions") are defined in terms of [trigonometric functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trigonometric_functions "Trigonometric functions"), where the trigonometric functions are monotonic. Another example: the natural logarithm is monotonic on the positive real numbers, and its image is the whole real line; therefore it has an inverse function that is a [bijection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bijection "Bijection") between the real numbers and the positive real numbers. This inverse is the [exponential function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponential_function "Exponential function").

Many other real functions are defined either by the [implicit function theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Implicit_function_theorem "Implicit function theorem") (the inverse function is a particular instance) or as solutions of [differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_equation "Differential equation"). For example, the [sine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sine "Sine") and the [cosine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosine "Cosine") functions are the solutions of the [linear differential equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_differential_equation "Linear differential equation")

such that

![Image 256: {\displaystyle \sin 0=0,\quad \cos 0=1,\quad {\frac {\partial \sin x}{\partial x}}(0)=1,\quad {\frac {\partial \cos x}{\partial x}}(0)=0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6bd7b575eed317bfdd3cbf64c5411ae3fcf72764)

### Vector-valued function

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Function_(mathematics)&action=edit&section=31 "Edit section: Vector-valued function")]

When the elements of the codomain of a function are [vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_(mathematics_and_physics) "Vector (mathematics and physics)"), the function is said to be a vector-valued function. These functions are particularly useful in applications, for example modeling physical properties. For example, the function that associates to each point of a fluid its [velocity vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity_vector "Velocity vector") is a vector-valued function.

Some vector-valued functions are defined on a subset of ![Image 257: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d) or other spaces that share geometric or [topological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological "Topological") properties of ![Image 258: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d), such as [manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manifolds "Manifolds"). These vector-valued functions are given the name _vector fields_.

In [mathematical analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_analysis "Mathematical analysis"), and more specifically in [functional analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functional_analysis "Functional analysis"), a **function space** is a set of [scalar-valued](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar-valued_function "Scalar-valued function") or [vector-valued functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector-valued_function "Vector-valued function"), which share a specific property and form a [topological vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_vector_space "Topological vector space"). For example, the real [smooth functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smooth_function "Smooth function") with a [compact support](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compact_support "Compact support") (that is, they are zero outside some [compact set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compact_set "Compact set")) form a function space that is at the basis of the theory of [distributions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distribution_(mathematics) "Distribution (mathematics)").

Function spaces play a fundamental role in advanced mathematical analysis, by allowing the use of their algebraic and [topological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology "Topology") properties for studying properties of functions. For example, all theorems of existence and uniqueness of solutions of [ordinary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordinary_differential_equation "Ordinary differential equation") or [partial differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_differential_equation "Partial differential equation") result of the study of function spaces.

Multi-valued functions
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Function_(mathematics)&action=edit&section=33 "Edit section: Multi-valued functions")]

[![Image 259](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Function_with_two_values_1.svg/250px-Function_with_two_values_1.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Function_with_two_values_1.svg)

Together, the two square roots of all nonnegative real numbers form a single smooth curve.

[![Image 260](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Xto3minus3x.svg/250px-Xto3minus3x.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Xto3minus3x.svg)

Several methods for specifying functions of real or complex variables start from a local definition of the function at a point or on a [neighbourhood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neighbourhood_(mathematics) "Neighbourhood (mathematics)") of a point, and then extend by continuity the function to a much larger domain. Frequently, for a starting point ![Image 261: {\displaystyle x_{0},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c8b35dd572e629881da4083ad1681bc7cf420304) there are several possible starting values for the function.

For example, in defining the [square root](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_root "Square root") as the inverse function of the square function, for any positive real number ![Image 262: {\displaystyle x_{0},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c8b35dd572e629881da4083ad1681bc7cf420304) there are two choices for the value of the square root, one of which is positive and denoted ![Image 263: {\displaystyle {\sqrt {x_{0}}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/43d17683413882a6695d00f6e2d49ba450ab4446) and another which is negative and denoted ![Image 264: {\displaystyle -{\sqrt {x_{0}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d50109ab6da5d52c015b69ef106459324ba333e3) These choices define two continuous functions, both having the nonnegative real numbers as a domain, and having either the nonnegative or the nonpositive real numbers as images. When looking at the graphs of these functions, one can see that, together, they form a single [smooth curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smooth_curve "Smooth curve"). It is therefore often useful to consider these two square root functions as a single function that has two values for positive x, one value for 0 and no value for negative x.

In the preceding example, one choice, the positive square root, is more natural than the other. This is not the case in general. For example, let consider the [implicit function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Implicit_function "Implicit function") that maps y to a [root](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Root_of_a_function "Root of a function")x of ![Image 265: {\displaystyle x^{3}-3x-y=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d2b2ac300ad197f4bf3deae55b4b2e95ff3a03e0) (see the figure on the right). For _y_ = 0 one may choose either ![Image 266: {\displaystyle 0,{\sqrt {3}},{\text{ or }}-{\sqrt {3}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c03884332a438b88000c08a603f510e1cb1afb7d) for x. By the [implicit function theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Implicit_function_theorem "Implicit function theorem"), each choice defines a function; for the first one, the (maximal) domain is the interval [−2, 2] and the image is [−1, 1]; for the second one, the domain is [−2, ∞) and the image is [1, ∞); for the last one, the domain is (−∞, 2] and the image is (−∞, −1]. As the three graphs together form a smooth curve, and there is no reason for preferring one choice, these three functions are often considered as a single _multi-valued function_ of y that has three values for −2 <_y_< 2, and only one value for _y_ ≤ −2 and _y_ ≥ −2.

Usefulness of the concept of multi-valued functions is clearer when considering complex functions, typically [analytic functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytic_function "Analytic function"). The domain to which a complex function may be extended by [analytic continuation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytic_continuation "Analytic continuation") generally consists of almost the whole [complex plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_plane "Complex plane"). However, when extending the domain through two different paths, one often gets different values. For example, when extending the domain of the square root function, along a path of complex numbers with positive imaginary parts, one gets i for the square root of −1; while, when extending through complex numbers with negative imaginary parts, one gets −_i_. There are generally two ways of solving the problem. One may define a function that is not [continuous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_function "Continuous function") along some curve, called a [branch cut](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Branch_cut "Branch cut"). Such a function is called the [principal value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principal_value "Principal value") of the function. The other way is to consider that one has a _multi-valued function_, which is analytic everywhere except for isolated singularities, but whose value may "jump" if one follows a closed loop around a singularity. This jump is called the [monodromy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monodromy "Monodromy").

In the foundations of mathematics
---------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Function_(mathematics)&action=edit&section=34 "Edit section: In the foundations of mathematics")]

The definition of a function that is given in this article requires the concept of [set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_(mathematics) "Set (mathematics)"), since the domain and the codomain of a function must be a set. This is not a problem in usual mathematics, as it is generally not difficult to consider only functions whose domain and codomain are sets, which are well defined, even if the domain is not explicitly defined. However, it is sometimes useful to consider more general functions.

For example, the [singleton set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Singleton_set "Singleton set") may be considered as a function ![Image 267: {\displaystyle x\mapsto \{x\}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9b393766c20d0e835b81a0f3fd5007f5746eb1a7) Its domain would include all sets, and therefore would not be a set. In usual mathematics, one avoids this kind of problem by specifying a domain, which means that one has many singleton functions. However, when establishing foundations of mathematics, one may have to use functions whose domain, codomain or both are not specified, and some authors, often logicians, give precise definitions for these weakly specified functions.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics)#cite_note-29)

These generalized functions may be critical in the development of a formalization of the [foundations of mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foundations_of_mathematics "Foundations of mathematics"). For example, [Von Neumann–Bernays–Gödel set theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Von_Neumann%E2%80%93Bernays%E2%80%93G%C3%B6del_set_theory "Von Neumann–Bernays–Gödel set theory"), is an extension of the set theory in which the collection of all sets is a [class](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Class_(set_theory) "Class (set theory)"). This theory includes the [replacement axiom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Von_Neumann%E2%80%93Bernays%E2%80%93G%C3%B6del_set_theory#NBG's_axiom_of_replacement "Von Neumann–Bernays–Gödel set theory"), which may be stated as: If X is a set and F is a function, then _F_[_X_] is a set.

In alternative formulations of the foundations of mathematics using [type theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Type_theory "Type theory") rather than set theory, functions are taken as [primitive notions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primitive_notion "Primitive notion") rather than defined from other kinds of object. They are the inhabitants of [function types](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_type "Function type"), and may be constructed using expressions in the [lambda calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lambda_calculus "Lambda calculus").[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics)#cite_note-30)

In computer science
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Function_(mathematics)&action=edit&section=35 "Edit section: In computer science")]

In [computer programming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_programming "Computer programming"), a [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(programming) "Function (programming)") is, in general, a [subroutine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subroutine "Subroutine") which [implements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Implementation "Implementation") the abstract concept of function. That is, it is a program unit that produces an output for each input. [Functional programming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functional_programming "Functional programming") is the [programming paradigm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Programming_paradigm "Programming paradigm") consisting of building programs by using only subroutines that behave like mathematical functions, meaning that they have no [side effects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Side_effect_(computer_science) "Side effect (computer science)") and depend only on their arguments: they are [referentially transparent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Referential_transparency "Referential transparency"). For example, `if_then_else` is a function that takes three ([nullary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nullary "Nullary")) functions as arguments, and, depending on the value of the first argument (_true_ or _false_), returns the value of either the second or the third argument. An important advantage of functional programming is that it makes easier [program proofs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Program_proof "Program proof"), as being based on a well founded theory, the [lambda calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lambda_calculus "Lambda calculus") (see below). However, side effects are generally necessary for practical programs, ones that perform [input/output](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Input/output "Input/output"). There is a class of _[purely functional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Purely\_functional\_programming "Purely functional programming")_ languages, such as [Haskell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haskell "Haskell"), which encapsulate the possibility of side effects in the type of a function. Others, such as the [ML](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ML_(programming_language) "ML (programming language)") family, simply allow side effects.

In many [programming languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Programming_language "Programming language"), every subroutine is called a function, even when there is no output but only side effects, and when the functionality consists simply of modifying some data in the [computer memory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_memory "Computer memory").

Outside the context of programming languages, "function" has the usual mathematical meaning in [computer science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_science "Computer science"). In this area, a property of major interest is the [computability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computable_function "Computable function") of a function. For giving a precise meaning to this concept, and to the related concept of [algorithm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algorithm "Algorithm"), several [models of computation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Models_of_computation "Models of computation") have been introduced, the old ones being [general recursive functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%CE%9C-recursive_function "Μ-recursive function"), [lambda calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lambda_calculus "Lambda calculus"), and [Turing machine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turing_machine "Turing machine"). The fundamental theorem of [computability theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computability_theory "Computability theory") is that these three models of computation define the same set of computable functions, and that all the other models of computation that have ever been proposed define the same set of computable functions or a smaller one. The [Church–Turing thesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Church%E2%80%93Turing_thesis "Church–Turing thesis") is the claim that every philosophically acceptable definition of a _computable function_ defines also the same functions.

General recursive functions are [partial functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_function "Partial function") from integers to integers that can be defined from

*   [constant functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constant_function "Constant function"),
*   [successor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Successor_function "Successor function"), and
*   [projection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projection_function "Projection function") functions

via the operators

*   [composition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics)#Function_composition),
*   [primitive recursion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primitive_recursion "Primitive recursion"), and
*   [minimization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%CE%9C_operator "Μ operator").

Although defined only for functions from integers to integers, they can model any computable function as a consequence of the following properties:

*   a computation is the manipulation of finite sequences of symbols (digits of numbers, formulas, etc.),
*   every sequence of symbols may be coded as a sequence of [bits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bit "Bit"),
*   a bit sequence can be interpreted as the [binary representation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_representation "Binary representation") of an integer.

[Lambda calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lambda_calculus "Lambda calculus") is a theory that defines computable functions without using [set theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_theory "Set theory"), and is the theoretical background of functional programming. It consists of _terms_ that are either variables, function definitions (_𝜆_-terms), or applications of functions to terms. Terms are manipulated by interpreting its [axioms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiom "Axiom") (the _α_-equivalence, the β-reduction, and the η-conversion) as [rewriting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rewriting "Rewriting") rules, which can be used for computation.

In its original form, lambda calculus does not include the concepts of domain and codomain of a function. Roughly speaking, they have been introduced in the theory under the name of _type_ in [typed lambda calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Typed_lambda_calculus "Typed lambda calculus"). Most kinds of typed lambda calculi can define fewer functions than untyped lambda calculus.

3.   **[^](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics)#cite_ref-13)**n may also be 1, thus subsuming functions as defined above. For _n_ = 0, each [constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constant_(mathematics) "Constant (mathematics)") is a special case of a multivariate function, too.
4.   **[^](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics)#cite_ref-20)**Here "elementary" has not exactly its common sense: although most functions that are encountered in elementary courses of mathematics are elementary in this sense, some elementary functions are not elementary for the common sense, for example, those that involve roots of polynomials of high degree.
5.   **[^](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics)#cite_ref-21)**By definition, the graph of the empty function to X is a subset of the Cartesian product ∅ × _X_, and this product is empty.
6.   **[^](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics)#cite_ref-25)**The [axiom of choice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiom_of_choice "Axiom of choice") is not needed here, as the choice is done in a single set.
