Chemical compound

**Hydrogen deuteride** is an [isotopologue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotopologue "Isotopologue") of [dihydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihydrogen "Dihydrogen") composed of two isotopes of [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen"): the majority isotope 1 H ([protium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen-1 "Hydrogen-1")) and 2 H ([deuterium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deuterium "Deuterium")). Its proper [molecular formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_formula "Molecular formula") is 1 H 2 H, but for simplification, it is usually written as HD.

Preparation and occurrence
--------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen_deuteride&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Preparation and occurrence")]

In the laboratory it is produced by treating [sodium hydride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_hydride "Sodium hydride") with [deuterated water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heavy_water "Heavy water"):[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_deuteride#cite_note-1)

NaH + D 2 O → HD + NaOD
Hydrogen deuteride is a minor component of naturally occurring molecular hydrogen. It is one of the minor but noticeable components of the atmospheres of all the [giant planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giant_planet "Giant planet"), with abundances from about 30 ppm to about 200 ppm. HD has also been found in [supernova remnants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supernova_remnant "Supernova remnant"),[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_deuteride#cite_note-2) dense [interstellar clouds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_cloud "Interstellar cloud"),[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_deuteride#cite_note-3) and [protoplanetary disks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protoplanetary_disk "Protoplanetary disk").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_deuteride#cite_note-McClure2016-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_deuteride#cite_note-Kama2020-5)

Occurrence of HD vs. H 2 in giant planets' atmospheres | Planet | HD | H 2 |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Jupiter | ~0.003% | 89.8% ±2.0%[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_deuteride#cite_note-6) |
| Uranus | ~0.015% | 82.5% ±3.3%[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_deuteride#cite_note-7) |
| Neptune | ~0.019% | 80.0% ±3.2%[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_deuteride#cite_note-8) |
[![Image 33](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/H2%26HDlowRes.tiff/lossless-page1-250px-H2%26HDlowRes.tiff.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:H2%26HDlowRes.tiff)

[H NMR spectrum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/H_NMR_spectrum "H NMR spectrum") of a solution of HD (labeled with red bars) and H 2 (blue bar). The 1:1:1 triplet arises from the coupling of the 1 H nucleus ([I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_spin "Nuclear spin") = 1/2) to the 2 H nucleus ( I = 1).

Radio emission spectra
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen_deuteride&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Radio emission spectra")]

HD and H 2 have very similar [emission spectra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emission_spectrum "Emission spectrum"), but the emission frequencies differ.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_deuteride#cite_note-9)

The frequency of the astronomically important J = 1-0 [rotational transition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_transition "Rotational transition") of HD at 2.7 THz has been measured with tunable [FIR](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Far_infrared "Far infrared") radiation with an accuracy of 150 kHz.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_deuteride#cite_note-10) In astronomy, this J = 1-0 line has been used to determine the masses of protoplanetary disks surrounding [T Tauri stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/T_Tauri_star "T Tauri star")[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_deuteride#cite_note-McClure2016-4) and [Herbig Ae/Be stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herbig_Ae/Be_star "Herbig Ae/Be star").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_deuteride#cite_note-Kama2020-5)

See also: [rotational spectroscopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy "Rotational spectroscopy")
