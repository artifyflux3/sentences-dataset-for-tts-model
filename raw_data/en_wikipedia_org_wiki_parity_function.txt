In [Boolean algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boolean_algebra_(logic) "Boolean algebra (logic)"), a **parity function** is a [Boolean function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boolean_function "Boolean function") whose value is one [if and only if](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/If_and_only_if "If and only if") the input vector has an odd number of ones. The parity function of two inputs is also known as the [XOR](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exclusive_or "Exclusive or") function.

The parity function is notable for its role in theoretical investigation of [circuit complexity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circuit_complexity "Circuit complexity") of Boolean functions.

The output of the parity function is the [parity bit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parity_bit "Parity bit").

The ![Image 1: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)-variable parity function is the [Boolean function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boolean_function "Boolean function")![Image 2: {\displaystyle f:\{0,1\}^{n}\to \{0,1\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/afccfbce5d1d3dc006c9b3f0dd905285a464d08a) with the property that ![Image 3: {\displaystyle f(x)=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ea78f54e69b72f398cf6077e61c50a05b532d4c0)[if and only if](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/If_and_only_if "If and only if") the number of ones in the vector ![Image 4: {\displaystyle x\in \{0,1\}^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a2fe5fa2f2f1720e40e59457a01bb8bf14e0e4fc) is odd. In other words, ![Image 5: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) is defined as follows:

![Image 6: {\displaystyle f(x)=x_{1}\oplus x_{2}\oplus \dots \oplus x_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7fc50144b6ea29e52a476aa9c710621283cc9bde)
where ![Image 7: {\displaystyle \oplus }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8b16e2bdaefee9eed86d866e6eba3ac47c710f60) denotes [exclusive or](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exclusive_or "Exclusive or").

Parity only depends on the number of ones and is therefore a [symmetric Boolean function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetric_Boolean_function "Symmetric Boolean function").

The _n_-variable parity function and its negation are the only Boolean functions for which all [disjunctive normal forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disjunctive_normal_form "Disjunctive normal form") have the maximal number of 2 _n_−1[monomials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monomial "Monomial") of length _n_ and all [conjunctive normal forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conjunctive_normal_form "Conjunctive normal form") have the maximal number of 2 _n_−1 clauses of length _n_.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parity_function#cite_note-wp-1)

Computational complexity
------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Parity_function&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Computational complexity")]

Some of the earliest work in computational complexity was 1961 bound of [Bella Subbotovskaya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bella_Subbotovskaya "Bella Subbotovskaya") showing the size of a [Boolean formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boolean_expression "Boolean expression") computing parity must be at least ![Image 8: {\displaystyle \Omega (n^{3/2})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786972cd5e88a26b002ccf7380f0cbe8eea06482). This work uses the method of random restrictions. This exponent of ![Image 9: {\displaystyle 3/2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9a94d2d02968e6cbbc338b54f7762ccd20d74805) has been increased through careful analysis to ![Image 10: {\displaystyle 1.63}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5c1e4a58a0a60b559b144a8b4c6646f59fbdc491) by [Paterson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mike_Paterson "Mike Paterson") and [Zwick](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uri_Zwick "Uri Zwick") (1993) and then to ![Image 11: {\displaystyle 2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/901fc910c19990d0dbaaefe4726ceb1a4e217a0f) by [Håstad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johan_H%C3%A5stad "Johan Håstad") (1998). [[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parity_function#cite_note-jukna-2)

In the early 1980s, [Merrick Furst](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Merrick_L._Furst&action=edit&redlink=1 "Merrick L. Furst (page does not exist)"), [James Saxe](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=James_Saxe&action=edit&redlink=1 "James Saxe (page does not exist)") and [Michael Sipser](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michael_Sipser "Michael Sipser")[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parity_function#cite_note-FSS-3) and independently [Miklós Ajtai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikl%C3%B3s_Ajtai "Miklós Ajtai")[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parity_function#cite_note-4) established super-polynomial [lower bounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lower_bound "Lower bound") on the size of constant-depth [Boolean circuits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boolean_circuits "Boolean circuits") for the parity function, i.e., they showed that polynomial-size constant-depth circuits cannot compute the parity function. Similar results were also established for the majority, multiplication and [transitive closure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transitive_closure "Transitive closure") functions, by reduction from the parity function.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parity_function#cite_note-FSS-3)

[Håstad (1987)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parity_function#CITEREFH%C3%A5stad1987) established tight exponential lower bounds on the size of constant-depth [Boolean circuits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boolean_circuits "Boolean circuits") for the parity function. [Håstad's Switching Lemma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/H%C3%A5stad%27s_Switching_Lemma "Håstad's Switching Lemma") is the key technical tool used for these lower bounds and [Johan Håstad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johan_H%C3%A5stad "Johan Håstad") was awarded the [Gödel Prize](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G%C3%B6del_Prize "Gödel Prize") for this work in 1994. The precise result is that depth-k circuits with AND, OR, and NOT gates require size ![Image 12: {\displaystyle \exp(\Omega (n^{\frac {1}{k-1}}))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35d5ba29c4d80fe409d6720c48d5a6940079615d) to compute the parity function. This is asymptotically almost optimal as there are depth-k circuits computing parity which have size ![Image 13: {\displaystyle \exp(O(n^{\frac {1}{k-1}})t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/50912368b94d31926984f49fb233cf44a746a5d1).

An infinite parity function is a function ![Image 14: {\displaystyle f\colon \{0,1\}^{\omega }\to \{0,1\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c7146f73539ea4285971f2f86d824a6c73cdff14) mapping every infinite binary string to 0 or 1, having the following property: if ![Image 15: {\displaystyle w}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/88b1e0c8e1be5ebe69d18a8010676fa42d7961e6) and ![Image 16: {\displaystyle v}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e07b00e7fc0847fbd16391c778d65bc25c452597) are infinite binary strings differing only on finite number of coordinates then ![Image 17: {\displaystyle f(w)=f(v)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bc74f1132f77014d1264a8ad2d72be1d03eb90b1) if and only if ![Image 18: {\displaystyle w}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/88b1e0c8e1be5ebe69d18a8010676fa42d7961e6) and ![Image 19: {\displaystyle v}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e07b00e7fc0847fbd16391c778d65bc25c452597) differ on [even number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parity_(mathematics) "Parity (mathematics)") of coordinates.

Assuming [axiom of choice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiom_of_choice "Axiom of choice") it can be proved that parity functions exist and there are ![Image 20: {\displaystyle 2^{2^{\aleph _{0}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/66fc0957bdedfac064b7111d0596e1ddd50e9405) many of them; as many as the number of all functions from ![Image 21: {\displaystyle \{0,1\}^{\omega }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/916edc0f57d58c8e1b2adbff3b2438fb8672089d) to ![Image 22: {\displaystyle \{0,1\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/28de5781698336d21c9c560fb1cbb3fb406923eb). It is enough to take one representative per [equivalence class](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equivalence_class "Equivalence class") of relation ![Image 23: {\displaystyle \approx }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6f58f4c2b73283ce8a5ad28fb3746f2a8c998789) defined as follows: ![Image 24: {\displaystyle w\approx v}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e73763846ec88489f9bd0f07a4b98d2000211efb) if ![Image 25: {\displaystyle w}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/88b1e0c8e1be5ebe69d18a8010676fa42d7961e6) and ![Image 26: {\displaystyle v}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e07b00e7fc0847fbd16391c778d65bc25c452597) differ at finite number of coordinates. Having such representatives, we can map all of them to ![Image 27: {\displaystyle 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2aae8864a3c1fec9585261791a809ddec1489950); the rest of ![Image 28: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) values are deducted unambiguously.

Another construction of an infinite parity function can be done using a non-principal [ultrafilter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultrafilter_on_a_set "Ultrafilter on a set")![Image 29: {\displaystyle U}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/458a728f53b9a0274f059cd695e067c430956025) on ![Image 30: {\displaystyle \omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/48eff443f9de7a985bb94ca3bde20813ea737be8). The existence of non-principal ultrafilters on ![Image 31: {\displaystyle \omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/48eff443f9de7a985bb94ca3bde20813ea737be8) follows from – and is strictly weaker than – the axiom of choice. For any ![Image 32: {\displaystyle w:\omega \to \{0,1\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3817c67b1e07119b3e6bb1f2234e867473ea2c41) we consider the set ![Image 33: {\displaystyle A_{w}=\{n\in \omega \mid \{k\leq n\mid w(k)=0\}{\text{ is even}}\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/77bf65ab89e6eabc9b489eea2485bf33d840e18d). The infinite parity function ![Image 34: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) is defined by mapping ![Image 35: {\displaystyle w}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/88b1e0c8e1be5ebe69d18a8010676fa42d7961e6) to ![Image 36: {\displaystyle 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2aae8864a3c1fec9585261791a809ddec1489950) if and only if ![Image 37: {\displaystyle A_{w}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2d674dcd694f0332eafd61a2d30f395b6268007d) is an element of the ultrafilter.

It is necessary to assume at least some amount of choice to prove that infinite parity functions exist. If ![Image 38: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) is an infinite parity function and we consider the inverse image ![Image 39: {\displaystyle f^{-1}[0]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9e6e10867d4537921fb1e5199e8dac6c575da042) as a subset of the [Cantor space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cantor_space "Cantor space")![Image 40: {\displaystyle \{0,1\}^{\omega }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/916edc0f57d58c8e1b2adbff3b2438fb8672089d), then ![Image 41: {\displaystyle f^{-1}[0]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9e6e10867d4537921fb1e5199e8dac6c575da042) is a [non-measurable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-measurable_set "Non-measurable set") set and does not have the [property of Baire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Property_of_Baire "Property of Baire"). Without the axiom of choice, it is consistent (relative to [ZF](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zermelo-Fraenkel_set_theory "Zermelo-Fraenkel set theory")) that all subsets of the Cantor space are measurable and have the property of Baire and thus that no infinite parity function exists; this holds in the [Solovay model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solovay_model "Solovay model"), for instance.
