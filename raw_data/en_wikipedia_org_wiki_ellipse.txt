Ellipses: examples with increasing eccentricity

In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), an **ellipse** is a [plane curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_curve "Plane curve") surrounding two [focal points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Focus_(geometry) "Focus (geometry)"), such that for all points on the curve, the sum of the two distances to the focal points is a constant. It generalizes a [circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle "Circle"), which is the special type of ellipse in which the two focal points are the same. The elongation of an ellipse is measured by its [eccentricity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eccentricity_(mathematics) "Eccentricity (mathematics)")![Image 3: {\displaystyle e}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cd253103f0876afc68ebead27a5aa9867d927467), a number ranging from ![Image 4: {\displaystyle e=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d9850169d70a5ab7df71c2126441a86cec93eec8) (the [limiting case](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limiting_case_(mathematics) "Limiting case (mathematics)") of a circle) to ![Image 5: {\displaystyle e=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9c2f5932668126c63c844dc00ca187bc58a29e5a) (the limiting case of infinite elongation, no longer an ellipse but a [parabola](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parabola "Parabola")).

An ellipse has a simple [algebraic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra "Algebra") solution for its area, but for [its perimeter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perimeter_of_an_ellipse "Perimeter of an ellipse") (also known as [circumference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumference "Circumference")), [integration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral "Integral") is required to obtain an exact solution.

The largest and smallest [diameters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diameter "Diameter") of an ellipse, also known as its width and height, are typically denoted 2a and 2b. An ellipse has four [extreme points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extreme_point "Extreme point"): two _[vertices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex\_(geometry) "Vertex (geometry)")_ at the endpoints of the [major axis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Major_axis "Major axis") and two _co-vertices_ at the endpoints of the minor axis.

The standard [parametric equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parametric_equation "Parametric equation") is: ![Image 11: {\displaystyle (x,y)=(a\cos(t),b\sin(t))\quad {\text{for}}\quad 0\leq t\leq 2\pi .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/379e0f793358b2105a5fc0a23cac22fe16fd76ce)

Ellipses are the [closed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_curve "Closed curve") type of [conic section](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conic_section "Conic section"): a plane curve tracing the intersection of a [cone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cone "Cone") with a [plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_(mathematics) "Plane (mathematics)") (see figure). Ellipses have many similarities with the other two forms of conic sections, parabolas and [hyperbolas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbola "Hyperbola"), both of which are [open](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_curve "Open curve") and [unbounded](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unbounded_set "Unbounded set"). An angled [cross section](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_section_(geometry) "Cross section (geometry)") of a right circular [cylinder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylinder_(geometry)#Cylindric_section "Cylinder (geometry)") is also an ellipse.

An ellipse may also be defined in terms of one focal point and a line outside the ellipse called the [_directrix_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse#Eccentricity_and_the_directrix_property): for all points on the ellipse, the ratio between the distance to the focus and the distance to the directrix is a constant, called the [_eccentricity_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse#Eccentricity_and_the_directrix_property): ![Image 12: {\displaystyle e={\frac {c}{a}}={\sqrt {1-{\frac {b^{2}}{a^{2}}}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/74cfe003f7f80d258931c31b5d4bd2ccfba2e24e)

Ellipses are common in [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics"), [astronomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy "Astronomy") and [engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineering "Engineering"). For example, the [orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler_orbit "Kepler orbit") of each planet in the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System") is approximately an ellipse with the Sun at one focus point (more precisely, the focus is the [barycenter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barycentric_coordinates_(astronomy) "Barycentric coordinates (astronomy)") of the Sun–planet pair). The same is true for moons orbiting planets and all other systems of two astronomical bodies. The shapes of planets and stars are often well described by [ellipsoids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipsoid "Ellipsoid"). A circle viewed from a side angle looks like an ellipse: that is, the ellipse is the image of a circle under [parallel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_projection "Parallel projection") or [perspective projection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perspective_projection "Perspective projection"). The ellipse is also the simplest [Lissajous figure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lissajous_figure "Lissajous figure") formed when the horizontal and vertical motions are [sinusoids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sine_wave "Sine wave") with the same frequency: a similar effect leads to [elliptical polarization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptical_polarization "Elliptical polarization") of light in [optics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optics "Optics").

The name, ἔλλειψις (_élleipsis_, "omission"), was given by [Apollonius of Perga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apollonius_of_Perga "Apollonius of Perga") in his _Conics_.

Ellipse: definition by focus and circular directrix

An ellipse can be defined geometrically as a set or [locus of points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Locus_of_points "Locus of points") in the Euclidean plane:

Given two fixed points ![Image 15: {\displaystyle F_{1},F_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ddbaefad7d000285a069031a623cdc454e4b79e4) called the foci and a distance ![Image 16: {\displaystyle 2a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d325c24be7d760207674a169b078892bdd5cbc76) which is greater than the distance between the foci, the ellipse is the set of points ![Image 17: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a) such that the sum of the distances ![Image 18: {\displaystyle |PF_{1}|,\ |PF_{2}|}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/11f9cd6112506b260df20f12b6c45035dee7692d) is equal to ![Image 19: {\displaystyle 2a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d325c24be7d760207674a169b078892bdd5cbc76): ![Image 20: {\displaystyle E=\left\{P\in \mathbb {R} ^{2}\,\mid \,\left|PF_{2}\right|+\left|PF_{1}\right|=2a\right\}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bdc50045c00f914dd8fffac2bd88d4932ca12876)

The midpoint ![Image 21: {\displaystyle C}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4fc55753007cd3c18576f7933f6f089196732029) of the line segment joining the foci is called the _center_ of the ellipse. The line through the foci is called the _major axis_, and the line perpendicular to it through the center is the _minor axis_. The major axis intersects the ellipse at two _[vertices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex\_(curve) "Vertex (curve)")_![Image 22: {\displaystyle V_{1},V_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/50193d5201b7ff4d46487f5d5e58ee9c1e6f29fc), which have distance ![Image 23: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc) to the center. The distance ![Image 24: {\displaystyle c}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/86a67b81c2de995bd608d5b2df50cd8cd7d92455) of the foci to the center is called the _focal distance_ or linear eccentricity. The quotient ![Image 25: {\displaystyle e={\tfrac {c}{a}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9eecd5578dc51638b2b9d79cf72d70b98cbb4197) is defined as the _eccentricity_.

The case ![Image 26: {\displaystyle F_{1}=F_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4a1387d67417e45de53b554e8b775be7ad3cb236) yields a circle and is included as a special type of ellipse.

The equation ![Image 27: {\displaystyle \left|PF_{2}\right|+\left|PF_{1}\right|=2a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f37d94229edd8c50e32aa06a23356b7d97ae9d4f) can be viewed in a different way (see figure):

If ![Image 28: {\displaystyle c_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0b30ba1b247fb8d334580cec68561e749d24aff2) is the circle with center ![Image 29: {\displaystyle F_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0fd17e0779153d765b40ebef91533489b87b2e37) and radius ![Image 30: {\displaystyle 2a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d325c24be7d760207674a169b078892bdd5cbc76), then the distance of a point ![Image 31: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a) to the circle ![Image 32: {\displaystyle c_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0b30ba1b247fb8d334580cec68561e749d24aff2) equals the distance to the focus ![Image 33: {\displaystyle F_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/100c7fbf174fe8b06eacc2a6b0bb2e1badd1c7ce): ![Image 34: {\displaystyle \left|PF_{1}\right|=\left|Pc_{2}\right|.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6a60747a23bdec8c1654b53f121edfb28fc249b2)

![Image 35: {\displaystyle c_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0b30ba1b247fb8d334580cec68561e749d24aff2) is called the _circular directrix_ (related to focus ![Image 36: {\displaystyle F_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0fd17e0779153d765b40ebef91533489b87b2e37)) of the ellipse.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse#cite_note-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse#cite_note-2) This property should not be confused with the definition of an ellipse using a directrix line below.

Using [Dandelin spheres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dandelin_spheres "Dandelin spheres"), one can prove that any section of a cone with a plane is an ellipse, assuming the plane does not contain the apex and has slope less than that of the lines on the cone.

The tangent at a point ![Image 71: {\displaystyle (x_{1},\,y_{1})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c9e693bb3fab45df3151c136a55b3419ed0f799b) of the ellipse ![Image 72: {\displaystyle {\tfrac {x^{2}}{a^{2}}}+{\tfrac {y^{2}}{b^{2}}}=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f6aa55c90da259c96a7894fe6e7555d2428cdec3) has the coordinate equation: ![Image 73: {\displaystyle {\frac {x_{1}}{a^{2}}}x+{\frac {y_{1}}{b^{2}}}y=1.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/994f2b6b5927b4db93c30b28c25a1d6003952169)

A vector [parametric equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parametric_equation "Parametric equation") of the tangent is: ![Image 74: {\displaystyle {\vec {x}}={\begin{pmatrix}x_{1}\\y_{1}\end{pmatrix}}+s\left({\begin{array}{r}-y_{1}a^{2}\\x_{1}b^{2}\end{array}}\right),\quad s\in \mathbb {R} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e2c37da74ce0608c17b2a97e6c0b2acc1678fdda)

**Proof:** Let ![Image 75: {\displaystyle (x_{1},\,y_{1})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c9e693bb3fab45df3151c136a55b3419ed0f799b) be a point on an ellipse and ![Image 76: {\textstyle {\vec {x}}={\begin{pmatrix}x_{1}\\y_{1}\end{pmatrix}}+s{\begin{pmatrix}u\\v\end{pmatrix}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/502a140a5d58126eafda333729f3f8087bbcd72a) be the equation of any line ![Image 77: {\displaystyle g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3556280e66fe2c0d0140df20935a6f057381d77) containing ![Image 78: {\displaystyle (x_{1},\,y_{1})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c9e693bb3fab45df3151c136a55b3419ed0f799b). Inserting the line's equation into the ellipse equation and respecting ![Image 79: {\textstyle {\frac {x_{1}^{2}}{a^{2}}}+{\frac {y_{1}^{2}}{b^{2}}}=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d5af6060ad79e43e38eba5c8c2f3b0d74273b8f5) yields: ![Image 80: {\displaystyle {\frac {\left(x_{1}+su\right)^{2}}{a^{2}}}+{\frac {\left(y_{1}+sv\right)^{2}}{b^{2}}}=1\ \quad \Longrightarrow \quad 2s\left({\frac {x_{1}u}{a^{2}}}+{\frac {y_{1}v}{b^{2}}}\right)+s^{2}\left({\frac {u^{2}}{a^{2}}}+{\frac {v^{2}}{b^{2}}}\right)=0\ .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8a1f5318d9c1ee7102a56ece447258162a149b42) There are then cases:

1.   ![Image 81: {\displaystyle {\frac {x_{1}}{a^{2}}}u+{\frac {y_{1}}{b^{2}}}v=0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dd90222636919d3b3bb29f503789629aba0a84e6) Then line ![Image 82: {\displaystyle g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3556280e66fe2c0d0140df20935a6f057381d77) and the ellipse have only point ![Image 83: {\displaystyle (x_{1},\,y_{1})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c9e693bb3fab45df3151c136a55b3419ed0f799b) in common, and ![Image 84: {\displaystyle g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3556280e66fe2c0d0140df20935a6f057381d77) is a tangent. The tangent direction has [perpendicular vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_(geometry) "Normal (geometry)")![Image 85: {\displaystyle {\begin{pmatrix}{\frac {x_{1}}{a^{2}}}&{\frac {y_{1}}{b^{2}}}\end{pmatrix}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2998c465c3d326d8c90db3f2eb61ed357fd6a868), so the tangent line has equation ![Image 86: {\textstyle {\frac {x_{1}}{a^{2}}}x+{\tfrac {y_{1}}{b^{2}}}y=k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ad1109488d3605c807c39dc1f806ffca4becb3ae) for some ![Image 87: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40). Because ![Image 88: {\displaystyle (x_{1},\,y_{1})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c9e693bb3fab45df3151c136a55b3419ed0f799b) is on the tangent and the ellipse, one obtains ![Image 89: {\displaystyle k=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6c035ffa69b5bca8bf2d16c3da3aaad79a8bcbfa).
2.   ![Image 90: {\displaystyle {\frac {x_{1}}{a^{2}}}u+{\frac {y_{1}}{b^{2}}}v\neq 0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6a3478011aa8d8687991a21c79f579cdc4e037d6) Then line ![Image 91: {\displaystyle g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3556280e66fe2c0d0140df20935a6f057381d77) has a second point in common with the ellipse, and is a secant.

Using (1) one finds that ![Image 92: {\displaystyle {\begin{pmatrix}-y_{1}a^{2}&x_{1}b^{2}\end{pmatrix}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7820c99cbf4021be7a0fd4117c8e01d5ad6f6dae) is a tangent vector at point ![Image 93: {\displaystyle (x_{1},\,y_{1})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c9e693bb3fab45df3151c136a55b3419ed0f799b), which proves the vector equation.

If ![Image 94: {\displaystyle (x_{1},y_{1})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7fc74086e56542bd28b46a84faaee3cebdd4a899) and ![Image 95: {\displaystyle (u,v)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eadf12294edccd7a29c99cfc1765e4a14bf47e58) are two points of the ellipse such that ![Image 96: {\textstyle {\frac {x_{1}u}{a^{2}}}+{\tfrac {y_{1}v}{b^{2}}}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1a7f64600f5647ec96e4cc969472704a74577f04), then the points lie on two _conjugate diameters_ (see [below](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse#Conjugate_diameters)). (If ![Image 97: {\displaystyle a=b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1956b03d1314c7071ac1f45ed7b1e29422dcfcc4), the ellipse is a circle and "conjugate" means "orthogonal".)

If the standard ellipse is shifted to have center ![Image 98: {\displaystyle \left(x_{\circ },\,y_{\circ }\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/594dd3c3be61b6829e3415a0c988565fd529e6b9), its equation is ![Image 99: {\displaystyle {\frac {\left(x-x_{\circ }\right)^{2}}{a^{2}}}+{\frac {\left(y-y_{\circ }\right)^{2}}{b^{2}}}=1\ .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fe592d041402e4b3e54a69b154f497af44fccda8)

The axes are still parallel to the _x_- and _y_-axes.

[![Image 100](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/General_ellipse.png/330px-General_ellipse.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:General_ellipse.png)

A general ellipse in the plane can be uniquely described as a bivariate quadratic equation of Cartesian coordinates, or using center, semi-major and semi-minor axes, and angle

In [analytic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytic_geometry "Analytic geometry"), the ellipse is defined as a [quadric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadratic_form "Quadratic form"): the set of points ![Image 101: {\displaystyle (x,\,y)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0e7afe5870de593e694568d1c4b0b95c6ac50777) of the [Cartesian plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_plane "Cartesian plane") that, in non-degenerate cases, satisfy the [implicit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Implicit_and_explicit_functions "Implicit and explicit functions") equation[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse#cite_note-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse#cite_note-6)![Image 102: {\displaystyle Ax^{2}+Bxy+Cy^{2}+Dx+Ey+F=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a694f3a0805f2f11868a2a69413866d64170409b) provided ![Image 103: {\displaystyle B^{2}-4AC<0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4f9b3184559e82578c79d0efc54045fd4982c5d5)

To distinguish the [degenerate cases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degenerate_conic "Degenerate conic") from the non-degenerate case, let _∆_ be the [determinant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Determinant "Determinant")![Image 104: {\displaystyle \Delta ={\begin{vmatrix}A&{\frac {1}{2}}B&{\frac {1}{2}}D\\{\frac {1}{2}}B&C&{\frac {1}{2}}E\\{\frac {1}{2}}D&{\frac {1}{2}}E&F\end{vmatrix}}=ACF+{\tfrac {1}{4}}BDE-{\tfrac {1}{4}}(AE^{2}+CD^{2}+FB^{2}).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c7667aecb971fe0f450b0ff40a0196f7fe729974)

Then the ellipse is a non-degenerate real ellipse if and only if _C∆_< 0. If _C∆_> 0, we have an imaginary ellipse, and if _∆_ = 0, we have a point ellipse.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse#cite_note-Lawrence-7): 63

The general equation's coefficients can be obtained from known semi-major axis ![Image 105: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc), semi-minor axis ![Image 106: {\displaystyle b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f11423fbb2e967f986e36804a8ae4271734917c3), center coordinates ![Image 107: {\displaystyle \left(x_{\circ },\,y_{\circ }\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/594dd3c3be61b6829e3415a0c988565fd529e6b9), and rotation angle ![Image 108: {\displaystyle \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e5ab2664b422d53eb0c7df3b87e1360d75ad9af) (the angle from the positive horizontal axis to the ellipse's major axis) using the formulae: ![Image 109: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}A&=a^{2}\sin ^{2}\theta +b^{2}\cos ^{2}\theta &B&=2\left(b^{2}-a^{2}\right)\sin \theta \cos \theta \\[1ex]C&=a^{2}\cos ^{2}\theta +b^{2}\sin ^{2}\theta &D&=-2Ax_{\circ }-By_{\circ }\\[1ex]E&=-Bx_{\circ }-2Cy_{\circ }&F&=Ax_{\circ }^{2}+Bx_{\circ }y_{\circ }+Cy_{\circ }^{2}-a^{2}b^{2}.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/83709b15a759e09332f42231c992ef5f97e6e2cb)

These expressions can be derived from the canonical equation ![Image 110: {\displaystyle {\frac {X^{2}}{a^{2}}}+{\frac {Y^{2}}{b^{2}}}=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d4893cd8abdbbe27d217784fcb73a7c7047c2736) by a Euclidean transformation of the coordinates ![Image 111: {\displaystyle (X,\,Y)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5f67132f4114622e831c92844f14318c05cb1213): ![Image 112: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}X&=\left(x-x_{\circ }\right)\cos \theta +\left(y-y_{\circ }\right)\sin \theta ,\\Y&=-\left(x-x_{\circ }\right)\sin \theta +\left(y-y_{\circ }\right)\cos \theta .\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fee05ae05b0c85ac26e036e1ce7151ba0907e6ea)

Conversely, the canonical form parameters can be obtained from the general-form coefficients by the equations:[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse#cite_note-mathworld-3)

![Image 113: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}a,b&={\frac {-{\sqrt {2{\big (}AE^{2}+CD^{2}-BDE+(B^{2}-4AC)F{\big )}{\big (}(A+C)\pm {\sqrt {(A-C)^{2}+B^{2}}}{\big )}}}}{B^{2}-4AC}},\\x_{\circ }&={\frac {2CD-BE}{B^{2}-4AC}},\\[5mu]y_{\circ }&={\frac {2AE-BD}{B^{2}-4AC}},\\[5mu]\theta &={\tfrac {1}{2}}\operatorname {atan2} (-B,\,C-A),\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/49c699db33888167e2c0c9fb5223b3b11d5124f9)

where [atan2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atan2 "Atan2") is the 2-argument arctangent function.

### Standard parametric representation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ellipse&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Standard parametric representation")]

Using [trigonometric functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trigonometric_function "Trigonometric function"), a parametric representation of the standard ellipse ![Image 117: {\displaystyle {\tfrac {x^{2}}{a^{2}}}+{\tfrac {y^{2}}{b^{2}}}=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f6aa55c90da259c96a7894fe6e7555d2428cdec3) is: ![Image 118: {\displaystyle (x,\,y)=(a\cos t,\,b\sin t),\ 0\leq t<2\pi \,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c6a8b6e45332f2527bada46aa09e100ef26c23de)

The parameter _t_ (called the _[eccentric anomaly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eccentric\_anomaly "Eccentric anomaly")_ in astronomy) is not the angle of ![Image 119: {\displaystyle (x(t),y(t))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b7b80868600db89fef84e4d41317b7c8a1e0d047) with the _x_-axis, but has a geometric meaning due to [Philippe de La Hire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philippe_de_La_Hire "Philippe de La Hire") (see _[§Drawing ellipses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse#Drawing\_ellipses)_ below).[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse#cite_note-8)

### Rational representation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ellipse&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Rational representation")]

With the substitution ![Image 120: {\textstyle u=\tan \left({\frac {t}{2}}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9cc670f0f67b5cab3b4f2c132355cd4d55d04e2b) and trigonometric formulae one obtains ![Image 121: {\displaystyle \cos t={\frac {1-u^{2}}{1+u^{2}}}\ ,\quad \sin t={\frac {2u}{1+u^{2}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5a7189fb575455b221bb999b1b5d5f12ba8fe5dd)

and the _rational_ parametric equation of an ellipse ![Image 122: {\displaystyle {\begin{cases}x(u)=a\,{\dfrac {1-u^{2}}{1+u^{2}}}\\[10mu]y(u)=b\,{\dfrac {2u}{1+u^{2}}}\\[10mu]-\infty <u<\infty \end{cases}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/338f80cb5063c4db7bb394618c0012575c6ae071)

which covers any point of the ellipse ![Image 123: {\displaystyle {\tfrac {x^{2}}{a^{2}}}+{\tfrac {y^{2}}{b^{2}}}=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f6aa55c90da259c96a7894fe6e7555d2428cdec3) except the left vertex ![Image 124: {\displaystyle (-a,\,0)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4fe512bfe9855846883a7e88318129516cc12151).

For ![Image 125: {\displaystyle u\in [0,\,1],}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c61b780db9ac550dd283876e16abe9c2cccdf8c3) this formula represents the right upper quarter of the ellipse moving counter-clockwise with increasing ![Image 126: {\displaystyle u.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/edd5636410da69bac33da075162221527401793c) The left vertex is the limit ![Image 127: {\textstyle \lim _{u\to \pm \infty }(x(u),\,y(u))=(-a,\,0)\;.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54df8d27bbc9cbe106710f10ee0f715961c7fbaf)

Alternately, if the parameter ![Image 128: {\displaystyle [u:v]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/297de5a93c52f13ef84add1d79d693fcda686176) is considered to be a point on the [real projective line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_projective_line "Real projective line")![Image 129: {\textstyle \mathbf {P} (\mathbf {R} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/09fcebc435e5694c8c37f590332fae766bd4aa32), then the corresponding rational parametrization is ![Image 130: {\displaystyle [u:v]\mapsto \left(a{\frac {v^{2}-u^{2}}{v^{2}+u^{2}}},b{\frac {2uv}{v^{2}+u^{2}}}\right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fa1c21205522d70966ce62b8e324b19ec4c90f41)

Then ![Image 131: {\textstyle [1:0]\mapsto (-a,\,0).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0da12e69b8138a30dcb6cfbeeb95bd63b890f2db)

Rational representations of conic sections are commonly used in [computer-aided design](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer-aided_design "Computer-aided design") (see [Bézier curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/B%C3%A9zier_curve#Rational_B%C3%A9zier_curves "Bézier curve")).

### Tangent slope as parameter

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ellipse&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: Tangent slope as parameter")]

A parametric representation, which uses the slope ![Image 132: {\displaystyle m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a07d98bb302f3856cbabc47b2b9016692e3f7bc) of the tangent at a point of the ellipse can be obtained from the derivative of the standard representation ![Image 133: {\displaystyle {\vec {x}}(t)=(a\cos t,\,b\sin t)^{\mathsf {T}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a4c9769759de5005863da43033923e06f79f450): ![Image 134: {\displaystyle {\vec {x}}'(t)=(-a\sin t,\,b\cos t)^{\mathsf {T}}\quad \rightarrow \quad m=-{\frac {b}{a}}\cot t\quad \rightarrow \quad \cot t=-{\frac {ma}{b}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1a5c36aa09bc0a56ae6fb72fe1a3604bc2fb119e)

With help of [trigonometric formulae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_trigonometric_identities#Pythagorean_identities "List of trigonometric identities") one obtains: ![Image 135: {\displaystyle \cos t={\frac {\cot t}{\pm {\sqrt {1+\cot ^{2}t}}}}={\frac {-ma}{\pm {\sqrt {m^{2}a^{2}+b^{2}}}}}\ ,\quad \quad \sin t={\frac {1}{\pm {\sqrt {1+\cot ^{2}t}}}}={\frac {b}{\pm {\sqrt {m^{2}a^{2}+b^{2}}}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ee220fb861e4f2af754be48014bf7d14fe43c08f)

Replacing ![Image 136: {\displaystyle \cos t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3798aceb2c56c130331f6fc331815306df597de5) and ![Image 137: {\displaystyle \sin t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b812327f691449768f64b4fe8b27c8468ec1ad8) of the standard representation yields: ![Image 138: {\displaystyle {\vec {c}}_{\pm }(m)=\left(-{\frac {ma^{2}}{\pm {\sqrt {m^{2}a^{2}+b^{2}}}}},\;{\frac {b^{2}}{\pm {\sqrt {m^{2}a^{2}+b^{2}}}}}\right),\,m\in \mathbb {R} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8dbfb90854a74813eaa3c976a52449276ec0969e)

Here ![Image 139: {\displaystyle m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a07d98bb302f3856cbabc47b2b9016692e3f7bc) is the slope of the tangent at the corresponding ellipse point, ![Image 140: {\displaystyle {\vec {c}}_{+}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b85590e6ed1728a79fdf8dae477b8f013612a4e) is the upper and ![Image 141: {\displaystyle {\vec {c}}_{-}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9c5b65dad8e65b6cd632157ffed5f6aceb0f6194) the lower half of the ellipse. The vertices![Image 142: {\displaystyle (\pm a,\,0)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7e8e23ad7d9cd0e871b043470943354c22b4a2fd), having vertical tangents, are not covered by the representation.

The equation of the tangent at point ![Image 143: {\displaystyle {\vec {c}}_{\pm }(m)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2a28b30737d3f9305e24285710f2ee477f51328c) has the form ![Image 144: {\displaystyle y=mx+n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0ae774e96bc6f9f2eb75c360415fe3096dd3e8e1). The still unknown ![Image 145: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) can be determined by inserting the coordinates of the corresponding ellipse point ![Image 146: {\displaystyle {\vec {c}}_{\pm }(m)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2a28b30737d3f9305e24285710f2ee477f51328c): ![Image 147: {\displaystyle y=mx\pm {\sqrt {m^{2}a^{2}+b^{2}}}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eb7fb21427332172d11a63969d63344d0da5b314)

This description of the tangents of an ellipse is an essential tool for the determination of the [orthoptic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthoptic_(geometry) "Orthoptic (geometry)") of an ellipse. The orthoptic article contains another proof, without differential calculus and trigonometric formulae.

[![Image 148](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Ellipse-aff.svg/300px-Ellipse-aff.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Ellipse-aff.svg)

Ellipse as an affine image of the unit circle

Another definition of an ellipse uses [affine transformations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_transformation "Affine transformation"):

Any _ellipse_ is an affine image of the unit circle with equation ![Image 149: {\displaystyle x^{2}+y^{2}=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ec84b90236512e8d27ff1a8f7707b60b63327de1).Parametric representation
An affine transformation of the Euclidean plane has the form ![Image 150: {\displaystyle {\vec {x}}\mapsto {\vec {f}}\!_{0}+A{\vec {x}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/765a6a8086cb3c5513f93e196e9e9bde509ca130), where ![Image 151: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3) is a regular [matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_(mathematics) "Matrix (mathematics)") (with non-zero [determinant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Determinant "Determinant")) and ![Image 152: {\displaystyle {\vec {f}}\!_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/775a1987137c53250767ed16f0b3cca2be1a40b1) is an arbitrary vector. In [polar coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_coordinates "Polar coordinates"), with the origin at the center of the ellipse and with the angular coordinate ![Image 191: {\displaystyle \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e5ab2664b422d53eb0c7df3b87e1360d75ad9af) measured from the major axis, the ellipse's equation is[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse#cite_note-Lawrence-7): 75 ![Image 192: {\displaystyle r(\theta )={\frac {ab}{\sqrt {(b\cos \theta )^{2}+(a\sin \theta )^{2}}}}={\frac {b}{\sqrt {1-(e\cos \theta )^{2}}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/703b7f26bb0a4a8f49a499d6d56b5e2cfbd407a7) where ![Image 193: {\displaystyle e}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cd253103f0876afc68ebead27a5aa9867d927467) is the eccentricity (not [Euler's number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler%27s_number "Euler's number")).

Polar coordinates centered at focus.

Eccentricity and the directrix property
---------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ellipse&action=edit&section=20 "Edit section: Eccentricity and the directrix property")]

[![Image 200](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Ellipse-ll-e.svg/300px-Ellipse-ll-e.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Ellipse-ll-e.svg)

Ellipse: directrix property

Each of the two lines parallel to the minor axis, and at a distance of ![Image 201: {\textstyle d={\frac {a^{2}}{c}}={\frac {a}{e}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/db40f2f0dd19d95c7e1d640caf63e1e135698773) from it, is called a _directrix_ of the ellipse (see diagram).

For an arbitrary point ![Image 202: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a) of the ellipse, the quotient of the distance to one focus and to the corresponding directrix (see diagram) is equal to the eccentricity: ![Image 203: {\displaystyle {\frac {\left|PF_{1}\right|}{\left|Pl_{1}\right|}}={\frac {\left|PF_{2}\right|}{\left|Pl_{2}\right|}}=e={\frac {c}{a}}\ .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f4d37d64e2187264b82540a41b4eb2ff7fa21c15)
The proof for the pair ![Image 204: {\displaystyle F_{1},l_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/13366c3fb9a07c61e03716c2fc53387a1d391626) follows from the fact that ![Image 205: {\textstyle \left|PF_{1}\right|^{2}=(x-c)^{2}+y^{2},\ \left|Pl_{1}\right|^{2}=\left(x-{\tfrac {a^{2}}{c}}\right)^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1aac284e537d1400031bda094a0b9d59294fa656) and ![Image 206: {\displaystyle y^{2}=b^{2}-{\tfrac {b^{2}}{a^{2}}}x^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2afecde9655b414988209c37cc31993dc47ff797) satisfy the equation ![Image 207: {\displaystyle \left|PF_{1}\right|^{2}-{\frac {c^{2}}{a^{2}}}\left|Pl_{1}\right|^{2}=0\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3ba7ed8d12c2904e22e0b35188212a1142988406)

The second case is proven analogously.

The converse is also true and can be used to define an ellipse (in a manner similar to the definition of a parabola):

For any point ![Image 208: {\displaystyle F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/545fd099af8541605f7ee55f08225526be88ce57) (focus), any line ![Image 209: {\displaystyle l}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/829091f745070b9eb97a80244129025440a1cfac) (directrix) not through ![Image 210: {\displaystyle F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/545fd099af8541605f7ee55f08225526be88ce57), and any real number ![Image 211: {\displaystyle e}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cd253103f0876afc68ebead27a5aa9867d927467) with ![Image 212: {\displaystyle 0<e<1,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/492ac9a910c6a74f38c4d19cafde2f25509caeb2) the ellipse is the locus of points for which the quotient of the distances to the point and to the line is ![Image 213: {\displaystyle e,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1cfc3e7aeae6ef256412eaa9f68d097804943b5a) that is: ![Image 214: {\displaystyle E=\left\{P\ \left|\ {\frac {|PF|}{|Pl|}}=e\right.\right\}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/abb7bfa9a0dbb4b0faade9e4e6618b4996c11ac2)
The extension to ![Image 215: {\displaystyle e=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d9850169d70a5ab7df71c2126441a86cec93eec8), which is the eccentricity of a circle, is not allowed in this context in the Euclidean plane. However, one may consider the directrix of a circle to be the [line at infinity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_at_infinity "Line at infinity") in the [projective plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_plane "Projective plane").

(The choice ![Image 216: {\displaystyle e=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9c2f5932668126c63c844dc00ca187bc58a29e5a) yields a parabola, and if ![Image 217: {\displaystyle e>1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9605ca17e3915b659685c0326fbbcbfb522f11b3), a hyperbola.)

Rays from one focus reflect off the ellipse to pass through the other focus.

An ellipse possesses the following property:

The normal at a point ![Image 253: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a) bisects the angle between the lines ![Image 254: {\displaystyle {\overline {PF_{1}}},\,{\overline {PF_{2}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cdfcc6ab1e2e9c3bfa4aee7e67bea0db349b7c13).Proof
Because the tangent line is perpendicular to the normal, an equivalent statement is that the tangent is the external angle bisector of the lines to the foci (see diagram). Let ![Image 255: {\displaystyle L}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/103168b86f781fe6e9a4a87b8ea1cebe0ad4ede8) be the point on the line ![Image 256: {\displaystyle {\overline {PF_{2}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/65b56a3496fbba215f25fe7877e73049d9e030df) with distance ![Image 257: {\displaystyle 2a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d325c24be7d760207674a169b078892bdd5cbc76) to the focus ![Image 258: {\displaystyle F_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0fd17e0779153d765b40ebef91533489b87b2e37), where ![Image 259: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc) is the semi-major axis of the ellipse. Let line ![Image 260: {\displaystyle w}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/88b1e0c8e1be5ebe69d18a8010676fa42d7961e6) be the external angle bisector of the lines ![Image 261: {\displaystyle {\overline {PF_{1}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1b64bd56812529ec71b4a0d441c8c7f8439477b5) and ![Image 262: {\displaystyle {\overline {PF_{2}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/36afb04e5f31bd164611ba0bd2fb1714742a1c53) Take any other point ![Image 263: {\displaystyle Q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8752c7023b4b3286800fe3238271bbca681219ed) on ![Image 264: {\displaystyle w.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d358cd6be4381ccfa44bd5702785437956d6e23f) By the [triangle inequality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle_inequality "Triangle inequality") and the [angle bisector theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle_bisector_theorem "Angle bisector theorem"), ![Image 265: {\displaystyle 2a=\left|LF_{2}\right|<{}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f9fc8c3b9edd787898e7fb1d790acb29760b8f78)![Image 266: {\displaystyle \left|QF_{2}\right|+\left|QL\right|={}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2ed6a454958d51e7dd1bc66a79a1a88c388d6a41)![Image 267: {\displaystyle \left|QF_{2}\right|+\left|QF_{1}\right|,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/58270708b6b9e35a23179d6132d98a022429a54a) so ![Image 268: {\displaystyle Q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8752c7023b4b3286800fe3238271bbca681219ed) must be outside the ellipse. As this is true for every choice of ![Image 269: {\displaystyle Q,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d4000165887f03a10c30d085c2bf26ec3af8d5b0)![Image 270: {\displaystyle w}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/88b1e0c8e1be5ebe69d18a8010676fa42d7961e6) only intersects the ellipse at the single point ![Image 271: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a) so must be the tangent line.

Application
The rays from one focus are reflected by the ellipse to the second focus. This property has optical and acoustic applications similar to the reflective property of a parabola (see [whispering gallery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whispering_gallery "Whispering gallery")). It also serves to formulate [Alhazen's problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alhazen%27s_problem "Alhazen's problem") of reflection on a circle tangent to the ellipse.

Additionally, because of the focus-to-focus reflection property of ellipses, if the rays are allowed to continue propagating, reflected rays will eventually align closely with the major axis.

Central projection of circles (gate)

Ellipses appear in [descriptive geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Descriptive_geometry "Descriptive geometry") as images (parallel or central projection) of circles. There exist various tools to draw an ellipse. Computers provide the fastest and most accurate method for drawing an ellipse. However, technical tools (_[ellipsographs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipsograph "Ellipsograph")_) to draw an ellipse without a computer exist. The principle was known to the 5th century mathematician [Proclus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proclus "Proclus"), and the tool now known as an [elliptical trammel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptical_trammel "Elliptical trammel") was invented by [Leonardo da Vinci](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci "Leonardo da Vinci").[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse#cite_note-11)

If there is no ellipsograph available, one can draw an ellipse using an [approximation by the four osculating circles at the vertices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse#Approximation_by_osculating_circles).

For any method described below, knowledge of the axes and the semi-axes is necessary (or equivalently: the foci and the semi-major axis). If this presumption is not fulfilled one has to know at least two conjugate diameters. With help of [Rytz's construction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rytz%27s_construction "Rytz's construction") the axes and semi-axes can be retrieved.

### Pins-and-string method

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ellipse&action=edit&section=28 "Edit section: Pins-and-string method")]

The characterization of an ellipse as the locus of points so that sum of the distances to the foci is constant leads to a method of drawing one using two [drawing pins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drawing_pin "Drawing pin"), a length of string, and a pencil. In this method, pins are pushed into the paper at two points, which become the ellipse's foci. A string is tied at each end to the two pins; its length after tying is ![Image 326: {\displaystyle 2a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d325c24be7d760207674a169b078892bdd5cbc76). The tip of the pencil then traces an ellipse if it is moved while keeping the string taut. Using two pegs and a rope, gardeners use this procedure to outline an elliptical flower bed—thus it is called the _gardener's ellipse_. The Byzantine architect [Anthemius of Tralles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anthemius_of_Tralles "Anthemius of Tralles") (c. 600) described how this method could be used to construct an elliptical reflector,[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse#cite_note-13) and it was elaborated in a now-lost 9th-century treatise by [Al-Ḥasan ibn Mūsā](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-%E1%B8%A4asan_ibn_M%C5%ABs%C4%81_ibn_Sh%C4%81kir "Al-Ḥasan ibn Mūsā ibn Shākir").[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse#cite_note-14)

A similar method for drawing [confocal ellipses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confocal_conic_sections#Graves's_theorem:_the_construction_of_confocal_ellipses_by_a_string "Confocal conic sections") with a _closed_ string is due to the Irish bishop [Charles Graves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Graves_(bishop) "Charles Graves (bishop)").

### Paper strip methods

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ellipse&action=edit&section=29 "Edit section: Paper strip methods")]

The two following methods rely on the parametric representation (see _[§Standard parametric representation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse#Standard\_parametric\_representation)_, above): ![Image 327: {\displaystyle (a\cos t,\,b\sin t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a3175cd5a4697744c8c0c56109906a89fe123902)

This representation can be modeled technically by two simple methods. In both cases center, the axes and semi axes ![Image 328: {\displaystyle a,\,b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c12ceaff955e1a98e48db7fa8fa43121630fa209) have to be known.

Method 1
The first method starts with

a strip of paper of length ![Image 329: {\displaystyle a+b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a2391acf09244b9dba74eb940e871a6be7e7973a).
The point, where the semi axes meet is marked by ![Image 330: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a). If the strip slides with both ends on the axes of the desired ellipse, then point ![Image 331: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a) traces the ellipse. For the proof one shows that point ![Image 332: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a) has the parametric representation ![Image 333: {\displaystyle (a\cos t,\,b\sin t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a3175cd5a4697744c8c0c56109906a89fe123902), where parameter ![Image 334: {\displaystyle t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/65658b7b223af9e1acc877d848888ecdb4466560) is the angle of the slope of the paper strip.

A technical realization of the motion of the paper strip can be achieved by a [Tusi couple](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tusi_couple "Tusi couple") (see animation). The device is able to draw any ellipse with a _fixed_ sum ![Image 335: {\displaystyle a+b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a2391acf09244b9dba74eb940e871a6be7e7973a), which is the radius of the large circle. This restriction may be a disadvantage in real life. More flexible is the second paper strip method.

The following method to construct single points of an ellipse relies on the [Steiner generation of a conic section](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steiner_conic "Steiner conic"):

Given two [pencils](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pencil_(mathematics) "Pencil (mathematics)")![Image 366: {\displaystyle B(U),\,B(V)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/10a1643ab25d5bc87278cf445bc1c1746bfd6468) of lines at two points ![Image 367: {\displaystyle U,\,V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9afc12d08e71dfd34a8da408e9030e202b080469) (all lines containing ![Image 368: {\displaystyle U}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/458a728f53b9a0274f059cd695e067c430956025) and ![Image 369: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845), respectively) and a projective but not perspective mapping ![Image 370: {\displaystyle \pi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9be4ba0bb8df3af72e90a0535fabcc17431e540a) of ![Image 371: {\displaystyle B(U)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5f40b0baca9532aa8c1106155a0cae6ec77a2ce) onto ![Image 372: {\displaystyle B(V)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3240b15b8d5cc8b968454438e642b397e0f4414c), then the intersection points of corresponding lines form a non-degenerate projective conic section.
For the generation of points of the ellipse ![Image 373: {\displaystyle {\tfrac {x^{2}}{a^{2}}}+{\tfrac {y^{2}}{b^{2}}}=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f6aa55c90da259c96a7894fe6e7555d2428cdec3) one uses the pencils at the vertices ![Image 374: {\displaystyle V_{1},\,V_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/93f4c299d2482c7b9f98653c6438358f0e1334b6). Let ![Image 375: {\displaystyle P=(0,\,b)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a09b79804a60cc91aad3aabeabe11d00fc92d724) be an upper co-vertex of the ellipse and ![Image 376: {\displaystyle A=(-a,\,2b),\,B=(a,\,2b)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e08adf372d78010d920b57653e05906e7d5e160a).

![Image 377: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a) is the center of the rectangle ![Image 378: {\displaystyle V_{1},\,V_{2},\,B,\,A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8c0d8884d7b07dd9cbbe6aa20ecf54eca0f1cf54). The side ![Image 379: {\displaystyle {\overline {AB}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/70f91c39c977790f3cc4e768d5aad89bb1696110) of the rectangle is divided into n equal spaced line segments and this division is projected parallel with the diagonal ![Image 380: {\displaystyle AV_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0ad09dac04f9df792979f555864314c2283c0a28) as direction onto the line segment ![Image 381: {\displaystyle {\overline {V_{1}B}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2717941417acc1c87dfab3dae8a1721814187284) and assign the division as shown in the diagram. The parallel projection together with the reverse of the orientation is part of the projective mapping between the pencils at ![Image 382: {\displaystyle V_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/adfdbc929f16cb00bb43289c223651b41f7b9f80) and ![Image 383: {\displaystyle V_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ceaa689a894f5020a7b46177d201cbce2d41122b) needed. The intersection points of any two related lines ![Image 384: {\displaystyle V_{1}B_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/19f1f0e5d776460a59d14b0721f922e4f9d7fe8a) and ![Image 385: {\displaystyle V_{2}A_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f1c83b91d0029c26a60932747cb3af85f10a9e69) are points of the uniquely defined ellipse. With help of the points ![Image 386: {\displaystyle C_{1},\,\dotsc }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c4ae79caeef98137b01a2b8400a26cbbb6de92cd) the points of the second quarter of the ellipse can be determined. Analogously one obtains the points of the lower half of the ellipse.

Steiner generation can also be defined for hyperbolas and parabolas. It is sometimes called a _parallelogram method_ because one can use other points rather than the vertices, which starts with a parallelogram instead of a rectangle.

Ellipse: pole-polar relation

Any ellipse can be described in a suitable coordinate system by an equation ![Image 439: {\displaystyle {\tfrac {x^{2}}{a^{2}}}+{\tfrac {y^{2}}{b^{2}}}=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f6aa55c90da259c96a7894fe6e7555d2428cdec3). The equation of the tangent at a point ![Image 440: {\displaystyle P_{1}=\left(x_{1},\,y_{1}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0b3dc7fedd66c71a7ce565744b134e669ca30ece) of the ellipse is ![Image 441: {\displaystyle {\tfrac {x_{1}x}{a^{2}}}+{\tfrac {y_{1}y}{b^{2}}}=1.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/91306bdbe9e1b2f3a4884a1cac67bf777b852101) If one allows point ![Image 442: {\displaystyle P_{1}=\left(x_{1},\,y_{1}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0b3dc7fedd66c71a7ce565744b134e669ca30ece) to be an arbitrary point different from the origin, then

point ![Image 443: {\displaystyle P_{1}=\left(x_{1},\,y_{1}\right)\neq (0,\,0)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/610ba12f66bced33a208f619cafa50326b0001a9) is mapped onto the line ![Image 444: {\displaystyle {\tfrac {x_{1}x}{a^{2}}}+{\tfrac {y_{1}y}{b^{2}}}=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0bbdaa9c0ad38b2780cbae59b7022c21145a9e0d), not through the center of the ellipse.
This relation between points and lines is a [bijection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bijection "Bijection").

The [inverse function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverse_function "Inverse function") maps

Such a relation between points and lines generated by a conic is called _[pole-polar relation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pole\_and\_polar "Pole and polar")_ or _polarity_. The pole is the point; the polar the line.

By calculation one can confirm the following properties of the pole-polar relation of the ellipse:

1.   The intersection point of two polars is the pole of the line through their poles.
2.   The foci ![Image 445: {\displaystyle (c,\,0)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/21f68286402c820877c4be1ca3e0e02f4d9be445) and ![Image 446: {\displaystyle (-c,\,0)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fd709b2dcf610893611518601114cf6c2d3e5f0c), respectively, and the directrices ![Image 447: {\displaystyle x={\tfrac {a^{2}}{c}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a970d8ac8f26c51f2f3e24329cef716641e664d3) and ![Image 448: {\displaystyle x=-{\tfrac {a^{2}}{c}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/856c6cd402cbd3b9598f8bf91bb8d577e380691f), respectively, belong to pairs of pole and polar. Because they are even polar pairs with respect to the circle ![Image 449: {\displaystyle x^{2}+y^{2}=a^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6463e4390061fac92c5c0241ed46eb742d983ab7), the directrices can be constructed by compass and straightedge (see [Inversive geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inversive_geometry "Inversive geometry")).

Pole-polar relations exist for hyperbolas and parabolas as well.

where again ![Image 482: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc) is the length of the semi-major axis, ![Image 483: {\textstyle e={\sqrt {1-b^{2}/a^{2}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c5e1b066a3a2be6f826ae8fb20287a3f505fd940) is the eccentricity, and the function ![Image 484: {\displaystyle E}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4232c9de2ee3eec0a9c0a19b15ab92daa6223f9b) is the [complete elliptic integral of the second kind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complete_elliptic_integral_of_the_second_kind "Complete elliptic integral of the second kind"), ![Image 485: {\displaystyle E(e)\,=\,\int _{0}^{\pi /2}{\sqrt {1-e^{2}\sin ^{2}\theta }}\ d\theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bcb6775742c535b22be98e46c57745816fea2742) which is in general not an [elementary function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementary_function "Elementary function").

The circumference of the ellipse may be evaluated in terms of ![Image 486: {\displaystyle E(e)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e8c3c60376e68f0651f4f927633006aadb64601e) using [Gauss's arithmetic-geometric mean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic-geometric_mean "Arithmetic-geometric mean");[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse#cite_note-19) this is a quadratically converging iterative method (see [here](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptic_integral#Computation "Elliptic integral") for details).

The exact [infinite series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinite_series "Infinite series") is: ![Image 487: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}{\frac {C}{2\pi a}}&=1-\left({\frac {1}{2}}\right)^{2}e^{2}-\left({\frac {1\cdot 3}{2\cdot 4}}\right)^{2}{\frac {e^{4}}{3}}-\left({\frac {1\cdot 3\cdot 5}{2\cdot 4\cdot 6}}\right)^{2}{\frac {e^{6}}{5}}-\cdots \\&=1-\sum _{n=1}^{\infty }\left({\frac {(2n-1)!!}{(2n)!!}}\right)^{2}{\frac {e^{2n}}{2n-1}}\\&=-\sum _{n=0}^{\infty }\left({\frac {(2n-1)!!}{(2n)!!}}\right)^{2}{\frac {e^{2n}}{2n-1}},\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fc89ffe697adef54905ae3b133a6870d63882b0a) where ![Image 488: {\displaystyle n!!}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/511717d541dba5357928e8d8631f1b4d4f8d5b31) is the [double factorial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double_factorial "Double factorial") (extended to negative odd integers in the usual way, giving ![Image 489: {\displaystyle (-1)!!=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/399bb0701ee77817613b0d3b197076f210923130) and ![Image 490: {\displaystyle (-3)!!=-1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/188a2d464a574aa2c61a21610fc7aba1228bd05a)).

This series converges, but by expanding in terms of ![Image 491: {\displaystyle h=(a-b)^{2}/(a+b)^{2},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/16a34465dd9ec33947b0be74eb173ebb86dc21ca)[James Ivory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Ivory_(mathematician) "James Ivory (mathematician)"),[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse#cite_note-20)[Bessel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Wilhelm_Bessel "Friedrich Wilhelm Bessel")[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse#cite_note-21) and [Kummer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ernst_Kummer "Ernst Kummer")[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse#cite_note-Linderholm95-22) derived a series that converges much more rapidly. It is most concisely written in terms of the [binomial coefficient with ![Image 492: {\displaystyle n=1/2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c1721524d24b6231bdffb2befeef0f3c4e021f96)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binomial_coefficient#Binomial_coefficient_with_n_=_1/2 "Binomial coefficient"): ![Image 493: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}{\frac {C}{\pi (a+b)}}&=\sum _{n=0}^{\infty }{{\frac {1}{2}} \choose n}^{2}h^{n}\\&=\sum _{n=0}^{\infty }\left({\frac {(2n-3)!!}{(2n)!!}}\right)^{2}h^{n}\\&=\sum _{n=0}^{\infty }\left({\frac {(2n-3)!!}{2^{n}n!}}\right)^{2}h^{n}\\&=\sum _{n=0}^{\infty }\left({\frac {1}{(2n-1)4^{n}}}{\binom {2n}{n}}\right)^{2}h^{n}\\&=1+{\frac {h}{4}}+{\frac {h^{2}}{64}}+{\frac {h^{3}}{256}}+{\frac {25\,h^{4}}{16384}}+{\frac {49\,h^{5}}{65536}}+{\frac {441\,h^{6}}{2^{20}}}+{\frac {1089\,h^{7}}{2^{22}}}+\cdots .\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2929ad69c32ee6de5afdd4a2181a6bd1053b1627) The coefficients are slightly smaller (by a factor of ![Image 494: {\displaystyle 2n-1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fb6ce8a02613283b1e60305814a1457335b44437)), but also ![Image 495: {\displaystyle e^{4}/16\leq h\leq e^{4}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/243cf1ccda25778bb45ce74c505e49350f9e5ccd) is numerically much smaller than ![Image 496: {\displaystyle e}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cd253103f0876afc68ebead27a5aa9867d927467) except at ![Image 497: {\displaystyle h=e=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a3898938a5e940cc810cd7a400b83e39f0e57afc) and ![Image 498: {\displaystyle h=e=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5eb775288cb14731716161964e71ecd1c8a26462). For eccentricities less than 0.5 (![Image 499: {\displaystyle h<0.005}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/57e6adcecb83826b2ad7afc3ad5000d7ea61705c)), the error is at the limits of [double-precision floating-point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double-precision_floating-point "Double-precision floating-point") after the ![Image 500: {\displaystyle h^{4}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/21db4c317590e3525f8ea8ad31f455d7af8ee8f3) term.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse#cite_note-Cook23-23)

Given an ellipse whose axes are drawn, we can construct the endpoints of a particular elliptic arc whose length is one eighth of the ellipse's circumference using only [straightedge and compass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Straightedge_and_compass_construction "Straightedge and compass construction") in a finite number of steps; for some specific shapes of ellipses, such as when the axes have a length ratio of ⁠![Image 521: {\displaystyle {\sqrt {2}}:1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fd734a2303c444bca42418ab6fc306645e2f596c)⁠, it is additionally possible to construct the endpoints of a particular arc whose length is one twelfth of the circumference.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse#cite_note-28) (The vertices and co-vertices are already endpoints of arcs whose length is one half or one quarter of the ellipse's circumference.) The division in special cases has been investigated by [Legendre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adrien-Marie_Legendre "Adrien-Marie Legendre") in his classical treatise.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse#cite_note-29)

In triangle geometry
--------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ellipse&action=edit&section=46 "Edit section: In triangle geometry")]

Ellipses appear in triangle geometry as

1.   [Steiner ellipse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steiner_ellipse "Steiner ellipse"): ellipse through the vertices of the triangle with center at the centroid,
2.   [inellipses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inellipse "Inellipse"): ellipses which touch the sides of a triangle. Special cases are the [Steiner inellipse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steiner_inellipse "Steiner inellipse") and the [Mandart inellipse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mandart_inellipse "Mandart inellipse").

#### Elliptical reflectors and acoustics

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ellipse&action=edit&section=50 "Edit section: Elliptical reflectors and acoustics")]

[![Image 535](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/%22Wave_pattern_of_a_little_droplet_dropped_into_mercury_in_one_focus_of_the_ellipse_%22_by_Weber_Bros..jpg/250px-%22Wave_pattern_of_a_little_droplet_dropped_into_mercury_in_one_focus_of_the_ellipse_%22_by_Weber_Bros..jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:%22Wave_pattern_of_a_little_droplet_dropped_into_mercury_in_one_focus_of_the_ellipse_%22_by_Weber_Bros..jpg)

Wave pattern of a little droplet dropped into mercury in the foci of the ellipse

If the water's surface is disturbed at one focus of an elliptical water tank, the circular waves of that disturbance, after [reflecting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflection_(physics) "Reflection (physics)") off the walls, converge simultaneously to a single point: the _second focus_. This is a consequence of the total travel length being the same along any wall-bouncing path between the two foci.

Similarly, if a light source is placed at one focus of an elliptic [mirror](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mirror "Mirror"), all light rays on the plane of the ellipse are reflected to the second focus. Since no other smooth curve has such a property, it can be used as an alternative definition of an ellipse. (In the special case of a circle with a source at its center all light would be reflected back to the center.) If the ellipse is rotated along its major axis to produce an ellipsoidal mirror (specifically, a [prolate spheroid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prolate_spheroid "Prolate spheroid")), this property holds for all rays out of the source. Alternatively, a cylindrical mirror with elliptical cross-section can be used to focus light from a linear [fluorescent lamp](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluorescent_lamp "Fluorescent lamp") along a line of the paper; such mirrors are used in some [document scanners](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Image_scanner "Image scanner").

Sound waves are reflected in a similar way, so in a large elliptical room a person standing at one focus can hear a person standing at the other focus remarkably well. The effect is even more evident under a [vaulted roof](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cupola "Cupola") shaped as a section of a prolate spheroid. Such a room is called a _[whisper chamber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whisper\_chamber "Whisper chamber")_. The same effect can be demonstrated with two reflectors shaped like the end caps of such a spheroid, placed facing each other at the proper distance. Examples are the [National Statuary Hall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Statuary_Hall "National Statuary Hall") at the [United States Capitol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Capitol "United States Capitol") (where [John Quincy Adams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Quincy_Adams "John Quincy Adams") is said to have used this property for eavesdropping on political matters); the [Mormon Tabernacle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mormon_Tabernacle "Mormon Tabernacle") at [Temple Square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temple_Square "Temple Square") in [Salt Lake City](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salt_Lake_City "Salt Lake City"), [Utah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Utah "Utah"); at an exhibit on sound at the [Museum of Science and Industry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Museum_of_Science_and_Industry_(Chicago) "Museum of Science and Industry (Chicago)") in [Chicago](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chicago "Chicago"); in front of the [University of Illinois at Urbana–Champaign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Illinois_at_Urbana%E2%80%93Champaign "University of Illinois at Urbana–Champaign") Foellinger Auditorium; and also at a side chamber of the Palace of Charles V, in the [Alhambra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alhambra "Alhambra").

In the 17th century, [Johannes Kepler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johannes_Kepler "Johannes Kepler") discovered that the orbits along which the planets travel around the Sun are ellipses with the Sun [approximately] at one focus, in his [first law of planetary motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler%27s_laws_of_planetary_motion "Kepler's laws of planetary motion"). Later, [Isaac Newton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaac_Newton "Isaac Newton") explained this as a corollary of his [law of universal gravitation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_law_of_universal_gravitation "Newton's law of universal gravitation").

More generally, in the gravitational [two-body problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two-body_problem "Two-body problem"), if the two bodies are bound to each other (that is, the total energy is negative), their orbits are [similar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Similarity_(geometry) "Similarity (geometry)") ellipses with the common [barycenter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barycenter "Barycenter") being one of the foci of each ellipse. The other focus of either ellipse has no known physical significance. The orbit of either body in the reference frame of the other is also an ellipse, with the other body at the same focus.

Keplerian elliptical orbits are the result of any radially directed attraction force whose strength is inversely proportional to the square of the distance. Thus, in principle, the motion of two oppositely charged particles in empty space would also be an ellipse. (However, this conclusion ignores losses due to [electromagnetic radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation "Electromagnetic radiation") and [quantum effects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics"), which become significant when the particles are moving at high speed.)

#### Harmonic oscillators

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ellipse&action=edit&section=52 "Edit section: Harmonic oscillators")]

The general solution for a [harmonic oscillator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harmonic_oscillator "Harmonic oscillator") in two or more [dimensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension "Dimension") is also an ellipse. Such is the case, for instance, of a long pendulum that is free to move in two dimensions; of a mass attached to a fixed point by a perfectly elastic [spring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spring_(mechanics) "Spring (mechanics)"); or of any object that moves under influence of an attractive force that is directly proportional to its distance from a fixed attractor. Unlike Keplerian orbits, however, these "harmonic orbits" have the center of attraction at the geometric center of the ellipse, and have fairly simple equations of motion.

#### Phase visualization

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ellipse&action=edit&section=53 "Edit section: Phase visualization")]

In [electronics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronics "Electronics"), the relative phase of two sinusoidal signals can be compared by feeding them to the vertical and horizontal inputs of an [oscilloscope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oscilloscope "Oscilloscope"). If the [Lissajous figure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lissajous_figure "Lissajous figure") display is an ellipse, rather than a straight line, the two signals are out of phase.

Two [non-circular gears](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-circular_gear "Non-circular gear") with the same elliptical outline, each pivoting around one focus and positioned at the proper angle, turn smoothly while maintaining contact at all times. Alternatively, they can be connected by a [link chain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Link_chain "Link chain") or [timing belt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toothed_belt "Toothed belt"), or in the case of a bicycle the main [chainring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chainwheel#ovoid_chainwheels "Chainwheel") may be elliptical, or an [ovoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ovoid "Ovoid") similar to an ellipse in form. Such elliptical gears may be used in mechanical equipment to produce variable [angular speed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_speed "Angular speed") or [torque](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torque "Torque") from a constant rotation of the driving axle, or in the case of a bicycle to allow a varying crank rotation speed with inversely varying [mechanical advantage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanical_advantage "Mechanical advantage").

Elliptical bicycle gears make it easier for the chain to slide off the cog when changing gears.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse#cite_note-30)

An example gear application would be a device that winds thread onto a conical [bobbin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bobbin "Bobbin") on a [spinning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spinning_(textiles) "Spinning (textiles)") machine. The bobbin would need to wind faster when the thread is near the apex than when it is near the base.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse#cite_note-31)

*   In a material that is optically [anisotropic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anisotropic "Anisotropic") ([birefringent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Birefringent "Birefringent")), the [refractive index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Refractive_index "Refractive index") depends on the direction of the light. The dependency can be described by an [index ellipsoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Index_ellipsoid "Index ellipsoid"). (If the material is optically [isotropic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotropic "Isotropic"), this ellipsoid is a sphere.)
*   In lamp-[pumped](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laser_pumping "Laser pumping") solid-state lasers, elliptical cylinder-shaped reflectors have been used to direct light from the pump lamp (coaxial with one ellipse focal axis) to the active medium rod (coaxial with the second focal axis).[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse#cite_note-32)
*   In laser-plasma produced [EUV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extreme_ultraviolet "Extreme ultraviolet") light sources used in microchip [lithography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extreme_ultraviolet_lithography "Extreme ultraviolet lithography"), EUV light is generated by plasma positioned in the primary focus of an ellipsoid mirror and is collected in the secondary focus at the input of the lithography machine.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse#cite_note-33)

### Statistics and finance

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ellipse&action=edit&section=56 "Edit section: Statistics and finance")]

In [statistics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistics "Statistics"), a bivariate [random vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Random_vector "Random vector")![Image 544: {\displaystyle (X,Y)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/41f29b9537685f499713112d6802e811cbf51bba) is [jointly elliptically distributed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptical_distribution "Elliptical distribution") if its iso-density contours—loci of equal values of the density function—are ellipses. The concept extends to an arbitrary number of elements of the random vector, in which case in general the iso-density contours are ellipsoids. A special case is the [multivariate normal distribution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multivariate_normal_distribution "Multivariate normal distribution"). The elliptical distributions are important in the financial field because if rates of return on assets are jointly elliptically distributed then all portfolios can be characterized completely by their mean and variance—that is, any two portfolios with identical mean and variance of portfolio return have identical distributions of portfolio return.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse#cite_note-34)[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse#cite_note-35)

Drawing an ellipse as a [graphics primitive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graphics_primitive "Graphics primitive") is common in standard display libraries, such as the MacIntosh [QuickDraw](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/QuickDraw "QuickDraw") API, and [Direct2D](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direct2D "Direct2D") on Windows. [Jack Bresenham](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jack_Bresenham "Jack Bresenham") at IBM is most famous for the invention of 2D drawing primitives, including line and circle drawing, using only fast integer operations such as addition and branch on carry bit. M. L. V. Pitteway extended Bresenham's algorithm for lines to conics in 1967.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse#cite_note-36) Another efficient generalization to draw ellipses was invented in 1984 by Jerry Van Aken.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse#cite_note-37)

In 1970 Danny Cohen presented at the "Computer Graphics 1970" conference in England a linear algorithm for drawing ellipses and circles. In 1971, L. B. Smith published similar algorithms for all conic sections and proved them to have good properties.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse#cite_note-38) These algorithms need only a few multiplications and additions to calculate each vector.

It is beneficial to use a parametric formulation in computer graphics because the density of points is greatest where there is the most curvature. Thus, the change in slope between each successive point is small, reducing the apparent "jaggedness" of the approximation.

Drawing with Bézier paths
[Composite Bézier curves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Composite_B%C3%A9zier_curve "Composite Bézier curve") may also be used to draw an ellipse to sufficient accuracy, since any ellipse may be construed as an [affine transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_transformation "Affine transformation") of a circle. The spline methods used to draw a circle may be used to draw an ellipse, since the constituent [Bézier curves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/B%C3%A9zier_curve "Bézier curve") behave appropriately under such transformations.
