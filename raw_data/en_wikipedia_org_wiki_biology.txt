**Biology** is the [scientific study](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_study "Scientific study") of [life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life "Life") and living [organisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism "Organism"). It is a broad [natural science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_science "Natural science") that encompasses a wide range of fields and unifying principles that explain the structure, function, growth, [origin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_life "History of life"), [evolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evolution "Evolution"), and distribution of life. Central to biology are five fundamental themes: the [cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_(biology) "Cell (biology)") as the basic unit of life, [genes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gene "Gene") and [heredity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heredity "Heredity") as the basis of inheritance, evolution as the driver of [biological diversity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biological_diversity "Biological diversity"), [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy "Energy") transformation for sustaining life processes, and the maintenance of internal stability ([homeostasis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homeostasis "Homeostasis")).[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-modell2015-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-davies2013-2)

Biology examines life across multiple [levels of organization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biological_organisation "Biological organisation"), from [molecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecule "Molecule") and cells to organisms, [populations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Population "Population"), and [ecosystems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem "Ecosystem"). Subdisciplines include [molecular biology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_biology "Molecular biology"), [physiology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physiology "Physiology"), [ecology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecology "Ecology"), [evolutionary biology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evolutionary_biology "Evolutionary biology"), [developmental biology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Developmental_biology "Developmental biology"), and [systematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Systematics "Systematics"), among others. Each of these fields applies a range of methods to investigate biological phenomena, including [observation, experimentation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_method "Scientific method"), and [mathematical modeling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_modeling "Mathematical modeling"). Modern biology is grounded in the theory of evolution by [natural selection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_selection "Natural selection"), first articulated by [Charles Darwin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Darwin "Charles Darwin"), and in the molecular understanding of genes encoded in [DNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNA "DNA"). The discovery of the [structure of DNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Structure_of_DNA "Structure of DNA") and advances in [molecular genetics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_genetics "Molecular genetics") have transformed many areas of biology, leading to applications in [medicine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medicine "Medicine"), [agriculture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agriculture "Agriculture"), [biotechnology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biotechnology "Biotechnology"), and [environmental science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Environmental_science "Environmental science").

Life on [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth "Earth") is believed to have originated over 3.7 billion years ago.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-Pearce_343%E2%80%93364-3) Today, it includes a vast diversity of organisms—from single-celled [archaea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archaea "Archaea") and [bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacteria "Bacteria") to complex multicellular [plants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant "Plant"), [fungi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fungi "Fungi"), and [animals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Animal "Animal"). [Biologists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biologist "Biologist") classify organisms based on shared characteristics and evolutionary relationships, using [taxonomic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taxonomy_(biology) "Taxonomy (biology)") and [phylogenetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phylogenetic "Phylogenetic") frameworks. These organisms interact with each other and with their environments in ecosystems, where they play roles in [energy flow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecological_energetics "Ecological energetics") and [nutrient cycling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nutrient_cycling "Nutrient cycling"). As a constantly evolving field, biology incorporates new discoveries and technologies that enhance the understanding of life and its processes, while contributing to solutions for challenges such as [disease](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disease "Disease"), [climate change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change "Climate change"), and [biodiversity loss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity_loss "Biodiversity loss").

Etymology
---------

From Greek _bios_, life, (from [Proto-Indo-European](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language "Proto-Indo-European language") root *gwei-, to live) and _logy_, study of. The compound was coined in 1800 by [Karl Friedrich Burdach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karl_Friedrich_Burdach "Karl Friedrich Burdach") and in 1802 used by both German naturalist [Gottfried Reinhold Treviranus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gottfried_Reinhold_Treviranus "Gottfried Reinhold Treviranus") and [Jean-Baptiste Lamarck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_Lamarck "Jean-Baptiste Lamarck").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-4)

History
-------

[![Image 7](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Neurology%2C_19th_century%3B_cell_of_Schwann._Wellcome_L0002015.jpg/250px-Neurology%2C_19th_century%3B_cell_of_Schwann._Wellcome_L0002015.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Neurology,_19th_century;_cell_of_Schwann._Wellcome_L0002015.jpg)

Drawing of what now are called [Schwann cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schwann_cells "Schwann cells") by one of the founders of [cell theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_theory "Cell theory"), [Theodor Schwann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theodor_Schwann "Theodor Schwann").

The earliest of roots of science, which included medicine, can be traced to [ancient Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Egypt "Ancient Egypt") and [Mesopotamia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mesopotamia "Mesopotamia") in around 3000 to 1200 [BCE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Common_Era "Common Era").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-Lindberg1-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-Grant2007a-6) Their contributions shaped ancient Greek [natural philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_philosophy "Natural philosophy").[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-7)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-Lindberg1-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-Grant2007a-6)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-8)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-9)[Ancient Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greece "Ancient Greece") philosophers such as [Aristotle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotle "Aristotle") (384–322 BCE) contributed extensively to the development of biological knowledge.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-10) He explored biological causation and the diversity of life. His successor, [Theophrastus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theophrastus "Theophrastus"), began the scientific study of plants.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-eb1911-11) Scholars of the [medieval Islamic world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Science_in_the_medieval_Islamic_world "Science in the medieval Islamic world") who wrote on biology included [al-Jahiz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Jahiz "Al-Jahiz") (781–869), [Al-Dīnawarī](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-D%C4%ABnawar%C4%AB "Al-Dīnawarī") (828–896), who wrote on botany,[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-Fahd-815-12) and [Rhazes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muhammad_ibn_Zakar%C4%ABya_R%C4%81zi "Muhammad ibn Zakarīya Rāzi") (865–925) who wrote on [anatomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anatomy "Anatomy") and [physiology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physiology "Physiology"). Medicine was especially well studied by [Islamic scholars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamic_scholars "Islamic scholars") working in Greek philosopher traditions, while natural history drew heavily on Aristotelian thought.

Biology began to quickly develop with [Anton van Leeuwenhoek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anton_van_Leeuwenhoek "Anton van Leeuwenhoek")'s dramatic improvement of the [microscope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microscope "Microscope"). It was then that scholars discovered [spermatozoa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spermatozoa "Spermatozoa"), bacteria, [infusoria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infusoria "Infusoria") and the diversity of microscopic life. Investigations by [Jan Swammerdam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jan_Swammerdam "Jan Swammerdam") led to new interest in [entomology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entomology "Entomology") and helped to develop techniques of microscopic [dissection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dissection "Dissection") and [staining](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Staining "Staining").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-13) Advances in microscopy had a profound impact on biological thinking. In the early 19th century, biologists pointed to the central importance of the [cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_(biology) "Cell (biology)"). In 1838, [Schleiden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matthias_Jakob_Schleiden "Matthias Jakob Schleiden") and [Schwann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theodor_Schwann "Theodor Schwann") began promoting the now universal ideas that (1) the basic unit of organisms is the cell and (2) that individual cells have all the characteristics of life, although they opposed the idea that (3) all cells come from the division of other cells, continuing to support [spontaneous generation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spontaneous_generation "Spontaneous generation"). However, [Robert Remak](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Remak "Robert Remak") and [Rudolf Virchow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rudolf_Virchow "Rudolf Virchow") were able to reify the third tenet, and by the 1860s most biologists accepted all three tenets which consolidated into [cell theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_theory "Cell theory").[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-14)[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-15)

Meanwhile, taxonomy and classification became the focus of natural historians. [Carl Linnaeus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus "Carl Linnaeus") published a basic [taxonomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taxonomy_(biology) "Taxonomy (biology)") for the natural world in 1735, and in the 1750s introduced [scientific names](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature "Binomial nomenclature") for all his species.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-16)[Georges-Louis Leclerc, Comte de Buffon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georges-Louis_Leclerc,_Comte_de_Buffon "Georges-Louis Leclerc, Comte de Buffon"), treated species as artificial categories and living forms as malleable—even suggesting the possibility of [common descent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Common_descent "Common descent").[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-17)

[![Image 8](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Charles_Darwin_aged_51.jpg/250px-Charles_Darwin_aged_51.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Charles_Darwin_aged_51.jpg)

In 1842, [Charles Darwin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Darwin "Charles Darwin") penned his first sketch of _[On the Origin of Species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/On\_the\_Origin\_of\_Species "On the Origin of Species")_.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-18)

Serious evolutionary thinking originated with the works of [Jean-Baptiste Lamarck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_Lamarck "Jean-Baptiste Lamarck"), who presented a coherent theory of evolution.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-Gould_2002_187-19) The British [naturalist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_history "Natural history")[Charles Darwin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Darwin "Charles Darwin"), combining the biogeographical approach of [Humboldt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_von_Humboldt "Alexander von Humboldt"), the uniformitarian geology of [Lyell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Lyell "Charles Lyell"), [Malthus's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Malthus "Thomas Malthus") writings on population growth, and his own morphological expertise and extensive natural observations, forged a more successful evolutionary theory based on [natural selection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_selection "Natural selection"); similar reasoning and evidence led [Alfred Russel Wallace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alfred_Russel_Wallace "Alfred Russel Wallace") to independently reach the same conclusions.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-20)[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-21)

The basis for modern genetics began with the work of [Gregor Mendel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gregor_Mendel "Gregor Mendel") in 1865.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-22) This outlined the principles of biological inheritance.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-miko2008a-23) However, the significance of his work was not realized until the early 20th century when evolution became a unified theory as the [modern synthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modern_synthesis_(20th_century) "Modern synthesis (20th century)") reconciled Darwinian evolution with [classical genetics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_genetics "Classical genetics").[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-Futuyma2017a-24) In the 1940s and early 1950s, a [series of experiments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hershey%E2%80%93Chase_experiment "Hershey–Chase experiment") by [Alfred Hershey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alfred_Hershey "Alfred Hershey") and [Martha Chase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Martha_Chase "Martha Chase") pointed to [DNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNA "DNA") as the component of [chromosomes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chromosome "Chromosome") that held the trait-carrying units that had become known as [genes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gene "Gene"). A focus on new kinds of model organisms such as [viruses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virus "Virus") and bacteria, along with the discovery of the double-helical structure of DNA by [James Watson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Watson "James Watson") and [Francis Crick](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Francis_Crick "Francis Crick") in 1953, marked the transition to the era of [molecular genetics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_genetics "Molecular genetics"). From the 1950s onwards, biology has been vastly extended in the [molecular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_Biology "Molecular Biology") domain. The [genetic code](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genetic_code "Genetic code") was cracked by [Har Gobind Khorana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Har_Gobind_Khorana "Har Gobind Khorana"), [Robert W. Holley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_W._Holley "Robert W. Holley") and [Marshall Warren Nirenberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marshall_Warren_Nirenberg "Marshall Warren Nirenberg") after DNA was understood to contain [codons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Codon "Codon"). The [Human Genome Project](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_Genome_Project "Human Genome Project") was launched in 1990 to map the human [genome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genome "Genome").[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-25)

Chemical basis
--------------

### Atoms and molecules

All organisms are made up of [chemical elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_element "Chemical element");[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-urry2017b-26)[oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen "Oxygen"), [carbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon "Carbon"), [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen"), and [nitrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen "Nitrogen") account for most (96%) of the mass of all organisms, with [calcium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calcium "Calcium"), [phosphorus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphorus "Phosphorus"), [sulfur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur "Sulfur"), [sodium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium "Sodium"), [chlorine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chlorine "Chlorine"), and [magnesium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnesium "Magnesium") constituting essentially all the remainder. Different elements can combine to form [compounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_compound "Chemical compound") such as water, which is fundamental to life.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-urry2017b-26)[Biochemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biochemistry "Biochemistry") is the study of [chemical processes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_process "Chemical process") within and relating to living [organisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism "Organism"). [Molecular biology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_biology "Molecular biology") is the branch of biology that seeks to understand the molecular basis of biological activity in and between cells, including [molecular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biomolecule "Biomolecule") synthesis, modification, mechanisms, and interactions.

### Water

[![Image 9](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/3D_model_hydrogen_bonds_in_water.svg/250px-3D_model_hydrogen_bonds_in_water.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:3D_model_hydrogen_bonds_in_water.svg)

Model of hydrogen bonds (1) between molecules of [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Properties_of_water "Properties of water")

Life arose from the Earth's first ocean, which formed some 3.8 billion years ago.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-freeman2017b-27) Since then, water continues to be the most abundant molecule in every organism. Water is important to life because it is an effective [solvent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solvent "Solvent"), capable of dissolving solutes such as sodium and [chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chloride "Chloride") ions or other small molecules to form an [aqueous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aqueous_solution "Aqueous solution")[solution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solution_(chemistry) "Solution (chemistry)"). Once dissolved in water, these solutes are more likely to come in contact with one another and therefore take part in [chemical reactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_reaction "Chemical reaction") that sustain life.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-freeman2017b-27) In terms of its [molecular structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Properties_of_water#Molecular_structure "Properties of water"), water is a small [polar molecule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_polarity#Polar_molecules "Chemical polarity") with a bent shape formed by the polar covalent bonds of two hydrogen (H) atoms to one oxygen (O) atom (H 2 O).[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-freeman2017b-27) Because the O–H bonds are polar, the oxygen atom has a slight negative charge and the two hydrogen atoms have a slight positive charge.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-freeman2017b-27) This polar [property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Properties_of_water "Properties of water") of water allows it to attract other water molecules via hydrogen bonds, which makes water [cohesive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cohesion_(chemistry) "Cohesion (chemistry)").[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-freeman2017b-27)[Surface tension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_tension "Surface tension") results from the cohesive force due to the attraction between molecules at the surface of the liquid.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-freeman2017b-27) Water is also [adhesive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adhesion "Adhesion") as it is able to adhere to the surface of any polar or charged non-water molecules.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-freeman2017b-27) Water is [denser](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Density "Density") as a [liquid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water#Liquid_water "Water") than it is as a solid (or ice).[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-freeman2017b-27) This unique property of water allows ice to float above liquid water such as ponds, lakes, and oceans, thereby [insulating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermal_insulation "Thermal insulation") the liquid below from the cold air above.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-freeman2017b-27) Water has the capacity to absorb energy, giving it a higher [specific heat capacity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Specific_heat_capacity "Specific heat capacity") than other solvents such as [ethanol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethanol "Ethanol").[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-freeman2017b-27) Thus, a large amount of energy is needed to break the hydrogen bonds between water molecules to convert liquid water into [water vapor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water_vapor "Water vapor").[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-freeman2017b-27) As a molecule, water is not completely stable as each water molecule continuously dissociates into hydrogen and [hydroxyl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroxyl "Hydroxyl") ions before reforming into a water molecule again.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-freeman2017b-27) In [pure water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Purified_water "Purified water"), the number of hydrogen ions balances (or equals) the number of hydroxyl ions, resulting in a [pH](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PH "PH") that is neutral.

### Organic compounds

[![Image 10](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Glucose_Haworth.png/250px-Glucose_Haworth.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Glucose_Haworth.png)

Organic compounds such as [glucose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glucose "Glucose") are vital to organisms.

[Organic compounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_compound "Organic compound") are molecules that contain carbon bonded to another element such as hydrogen.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-freeman2017b-27) With the exception of water, nearly all the molecules that make up each organism contain carbon.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-freeman2017b-27)[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-urry2017d-28) Carbon can form [covalent bonds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covalent_bond "Covalent bond") with up to four other atoms, enabling it to form diverse, large, and complex molecules.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-freeman2017b-27)[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-urry2017d-28) For example, a single carbon atom can form four single covalent bonds such as in [methane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane "Methane"), two [double covalent bonds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double_bond "Double bond") such as in [carbon dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_dioxide "Carbon dioxide") (CO 2), or a [triple covalent bond](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triple_bond "Triple bond") such as in [carbon monoxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide "Carbon monoxide") (CO). Moreover, carbon can form very long chains of interconnecting [carbon–carbon bonds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon%E2%80%93carbon_bond "Carbon–carbon bond") such as [octane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octane "Octane") or ring-like structures such as [glucose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glucose "Glucose").

The simplest form of an organic molecule is the [hydrocarbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrocarbon "Hydrocarbon"), which is a large family of organic compounds that are composed of hydrogen atoms bonded to a chain of carbon atoms. A hydrocarbon backbone can be substituted by other elements such as oxygen (O), hydrogen (H), phosphorus (P), and sulfur (S), which can change the chemical behavior of that compound.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-freeman2017b-27) Groups of atoms that contain these elements (O-, H-, P-, and S-) and are bonded to a central carbon atom or skeleton are called [functional groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functional_group "Functional group").[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-freeman2017b-27) There are six prominent functional groups that can be found in organisms: [amino group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amine "Amine"), [carboxyl group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carboxylic_acid "Carboxylic acid"), [carbonyl group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonyl_group "Carbonyl group"), [hydroxyl group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroxy_group "Hydroxy group"), [phosphate group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphate "Phosphate"), and [sulfhydryl group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thiol "Thiol").[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-freeman2017b-27)

In 1953, the [Miller–Urey experiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Miller%E2%80%93Urey_experiment "Miller–Urey experiment") showed that organic compounds could be synthesized abiotically within a closed system mimicking the conditions of [early Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Early_Earth "Early Earth"), thus suggesting that complex organic molecules could have arisen spontaneously in early Earth (see [abiogenesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abiogenesis "Abiogenesis")).[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-hillisetal2014d-29)[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-freeman2017b-27)

### Macromolecules

[![Image 11](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/225_Peptide_Bond-01.jpg/330px-225_Peptide_Bond-01.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:225_Peptide_Bond-01.jpg)

The (a) primary, (b) secondary, (c) tertiary, and (d) quaternary structures of a [hemoglobin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hemoglobin "Hemoglobin") protein

[Macromolecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macromolecule "Macromolecule") are large molecules made up of smaller subunits or [monomers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monomer "Monomer").[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-freeman2017c-30) Monomers include sugars, amino acids, and nucleotides.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-urry2017e-31)[Carbohydrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbohydrate "Carbohydrate") include monomers and polymers of sugars.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-freeman2017e-32) Lipids are the only class of macromolecules that are not made up of polymers. They include [steroids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steroid "Steroid"), [phospholipids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phospholipid "Phospholipid"), and fats,[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-urry2017e-31) largely nonpolar and hydrophobic (water-repelling) substances.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-freeman2017f-33) Proteins are the most diverse of the macromolecules. They include [enzymes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enzyme "Enzyme"), [transport proteins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transport_protein "Transport protein"), large [signaling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_signal "Cell signal") molecules, [antibodies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antibodies "Antibodies"), and [structural proteins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Structural_proteins "Structural proteins"). The basic unit (or monomer) of a protein is an [amino acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amino_acid "Amino acid").[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-freeman2017c-30) Twenty amino acids are used in proteins.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-freeman2017c-30) Nucleic acids are polymers of [nucleotides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleotide "Nucleotide").[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-freeman2017d-34) Their function is to store, transmit, and express hereditary information.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-urry2017e-31)

Cells
-----

[Cell theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_theory "Cell theory") states that [cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_(biology) "Cell (biology)") are the fundamental units of life, that all living things are composed of one or more cells, and that all cells arise from preexisting cells through [cell division](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_division "Cell division").[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-mazzarello1999-35) Most cells are very small, with diameters ranging from 1 to 100[micrometers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Micrometre "Micrometre") and are therefore only visible under a [light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_microscope "Light microscope") or [electron microscope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_microscope "Electron microscope").[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-36) There are generally two types of cells: [eukaryotic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eukaryotic "Eukaryotic") cells, which contain a [nucleus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_nucleus "Cell nucleus"), and [prokaryotic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prokaryotic "Prokaryotic") cells, which do not. Prokaryotes are [single-celled organisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unicellular_organism "Unicellular organism") such as [bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacterium "Bacterium"), whereas eukaryotes can be single-celled or multicellular. In [multicellular organisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multicellular_organisms "Multicellular organisms"), every cell in the organism's body is derived ultimately from a [single cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zygote "Zygote") in a fertilized [egg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egg_(biology) "Egg (biology)").

### Cell structure

[![Image 12](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Biological_cell.svg/250px-Biological_cell.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Biological_cell.svg)

Structure of an animal [cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_(biology) "Cell (biology)") depicting various [organelles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organelle "Organelle")

Every cell is enclosed within a [cell membrane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_membrane "Cell membrane") that separates its [cytoplasm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cytoplasm "Cytoplasm") from the [extracellular space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extracellular_space "Extracellular space").[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-urry2017g-37) A cell membrane consists of a [lipid bilayer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lipid_bilayer "Lipid bilayer"), including [cholesterols](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cholesterol "Cholesterol") that sit between phospholipids to maintain their [fluidity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Membrane_fluidity "Membrane fluidity") at various temperatures. Cell membranes are [semipermeable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semipermeable_membrane "Semipermeable membrane"), allowing small molecules such as oxygen, carbon dioxide, and water to pass through while restricting the movement of larger molecules and charged particles such as [ions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ion "Ion").[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-MBOC-38) Cell membranes also contain [membrane proteins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Membrane_protein "Membrane protein"), including [integral membrane proteins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral_membrane_protein "Integral membrane protein") that go across the membrane serving as [membrane transporters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Membrane_transporter "Membrane transporter"), and [peripheral proteins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peripheral_protein "Peripheral protein") that loosely attach to the outer side of the cell membrane, acting as enzymes shaping the cell.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-Tom_Herrmann_2019-39) Cell membranes are involved in various cellular processes such as [cell adhesion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_adhesion "Cell adhesion"), [storing electrical energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Membrane_potential "Membrane potential"), and [cell signalling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_signalling "Cell signalling") and serve as the attachment surface for several extracellular structures such as a [cell wall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_wall "Cell wall"), [glycocalyx](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycocalyx "Glycocalyx"), and [cytoskeleton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cytoskeleton "Cytoskeleton").

[![Image 13](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Plant_cell_structure-en.svg/330px-Plant_cell_structure-en.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Plant_cell_structure-en.svg)

Structure of a plant cell

Within the cytoplasm of a cell, there are many biomolecules such as [proteins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protein "Protein") and [nucleic acids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleic_acid "Nucleic acid").[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-Alberts2002-40) In addition to biomolecules, eukaryotic cells have specialized structures called [organelles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organelle "Organelle") that have their own lipid bilayers or are spatially units.[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-hillisetal2014f-41) These organelles include the [cell nucleus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_nucleus "Cell nucleus"), which contains most of the cell's DNA, or [mitochondria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mitochondrion "Mitochondrion"), which generate [adenosine triphosphate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate "Adenosine triphosphate") (ATP) to power cellular processes. Other organelles such as [endoplasmic reticulum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endoplasmic_reticulum "Endoplasmic reticulum") and [Golgi apparatus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golgi_apparatus "Golgi apparatus") play a role in the synthesis and packaging of proteins, respectively. Biomolecules such as proteins can be engulfed by [lysosomes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lysosome "Lysosome"), another specialized organelle. [Plant cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant_cell "Plant cell") have additional organelles that distinguish them from [animal cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Animal_cell "Animal cell") such as a cell wall that provides support for the plant cell, [chloroplasts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chloroplast "Chloroplast") that harvest sunlight energy to produce sugar, and [vacuoles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vacuole "Vacuole") that provide storage and structural support as well as being involved in reproduction and breakdown of plant seeds.[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-hillisetal2014f-41) Eukaryotic cells also have cytoskeleton that is made up of [microtubules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microtubule "Microtubule"), [intermediate filaments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intermediate_filament "Intermediate filament"), and [microfilaments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microfilament "Microfilament"), all of which provide support for the cell and are involved in the movement of the cell and its organelles.[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-hillisetal2014f-41) In terms of their structural composition, the microtubules are made up of [tubulin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tubulin "Tubulin") (e.g., [α-tubulin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tubulin#%CE%B1-Tubulin "Tubulin") and [β-tubulin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tubulin#%CE%B2-Tubulin "Tubulin")) whereas intermediate filaments are made up of fibrous proteins.[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-hillisetal2014f-41) Microfilaments are made up of [actin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Actin "Actin") molecules that interact with other strands of proteins.[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-hillisetal2014f-41)

### Metabolism

[![Image 14](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Activation2_updated.svg/250px-Activation2_updated.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Activation2_updated.svg)

Example of an enzyme-catalysed [exothermic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exothermic_reaction "Exothermic reaction") reaction

All cells require energy to sustain cellular processes. [Metabolism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metabolism "Metabolism") is the set of [chemical reactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_reactions "Chemical reactions") in an organism. The three main purposes of metabolism are: the conversion of food to energy to run cellular processes; the conversion of food/fuel to monomer building blocks; and the elimination of [metabolic wastes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metabolic_waste "Metabolic waste"). These enzyme-catalyzed reactions allow organisms to grow and reproduce, maintain their structures, and respond to their environments. Metabolic reactions may be categorized as [catabolic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catabolic "Catabolic")—the breaking down of compounds (for example, the breaking down of glucose to pyruvate by [cellular respiration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cellular_respiration "Cellular respiration")); or [anabolic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anabolic "Anabolic")—the building up ([synthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosynthesis "Biosynthesis")) of compounds (such as proteins, carbohydrates, lipids, and nucleic acids). Usually, catabolism releases energy, and anabolism consumes energy. The chemical reactions of metabolism are organized into [metabolic pathways](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metabolic_pathway "Metabolic pathway"), in which one chemical is transformed through a series of steps into another chemical, each step being facilitated by a specific enzyme. Enzymes are crucial to metabolism because they allow organisms to drive desirable reactions that require energy that will not occur by themselves, by [coupling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coupling_(physics) "Coupling (physics)") them to [spontaneous reactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spontaneous_process "Spontaneous process") that release energy. Enzymes act as [catalysts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catalysis "Catalysis")—they allow a reaction to proceed more rapidly without being consumed by it—by reducing the amount of [activation energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Activation_energy "Activation energy") needed to convert [reactants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reactant "Reactant") into [products](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Product_(chemistry) "Product (chemistry)"). Enzymes also allow the [regulation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metabolic_pathway#Regulation "Metabolic pathway") of the rate of a metabolic reaction, for example in response to changes in the cell's environment or to signals from other cells.

### Cellular respiration

[![Image 15](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/CellRespiration.svg/500px-CellRespiration.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:CellRespiration.svg)

Respiration in a [eukaryotic cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eukaryote "Eukaryote")

Cellular respiration is a set of metabolic reactions and processes that take place in cells to convert [chemical energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy#Energy_and_life "Energy") from [nutrients](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nutrients "Nutrients") into adenosine triphosphate (ATP), and then release waste products.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-42) The reactions involved in respiration are [catabolic reactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catabolism "Catabolism"), which break large molecules into smaller ones, releasing energy. Respiration is one of the key ways a cell releases chemical energy to fuel cellular activity. The overall reaction occurs in a series of biochemical steps, some of which are [redox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redox "Redox") reactions. Although cellular respiration is technically a [combustion reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Combustion_reaction "Combustion reaction"), it clearly does not resemble one when it occurs in a cell because of the slow, controlled release of energy from the series of reactions.

Sugar in the form of glucose is the main nutrient used by animal and plant cells in respiration. Cellular respiration involving oxygen is called aerobic respiration, which has four stages: [glycolysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycolysis "Glycolysis"), [citric acid cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Citric_acid_cycle "Citric acid cycle") (or Krebs cycle), [electron transport chain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_transport_chain "Electron transport chain"), and [oxidative phosphorylation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxidative_phosphorylation "Oxidative phosphorylation").[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-lodish2008l-43) Glycolysis is a metabolic process that occurs in the cytoplasm whereby glucose is converted into two [pyruvates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyruvic_acid "Pyruvic acid"), with two net molecules of ATP being produced at the same time.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-lodish2008l-43) Each pyruvate is then oxidized into [acetyl-CoA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetyl-CoA "Acetyl-CoA") by the [pyruvate dehydrogenase complex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyruvate_dehydrogenase_complex "Pyruvate dehydrogenase complex"), which also generates [NADH](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nicotinamide_adenine_dinucleotide "Nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide") and carbon dioxide. Acetyl-CoA enters the citric acid cycle, which takes places inside the mitochondrial matrix. At the end of the cycle, the total yield from 1 glucose (or 2 pyruvates) is 6 NADH, 2 FADH 2, and 2 ATP molecules. Finally, the next stage is oxidative phosphorylation, which in eukaryotes, occurs in the [mitochondrial cristae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crista "Crista"). Oxidative phosphorylation comprises the electron transport chain, which is a series of four [protein complexes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protein_complex "Protein complex") that transfer electrons from one complex to another, thereby releasing energy from NADH and FADH 2 that is coupled to the pumping of protons (hydrogen ions) across the inner mitochondrial membrane ([chemiosmosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemiosmosis "Chemiosmosis")), which generates a [proton motive force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemiosmosis#Proton-motive_force "Chemiosmosis").[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-lodish2008l-43) Energy from the proton motive force drives the enzyme [ATP synthase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ATP_synthase "ATP synthase") to synthesize more ATPs by [phosphorylating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphorylation "Phosphorylation")[ADPs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adenosine_diphosphate "Adenosine diphosphate"). The transfer of electrons terminates with molecular oxygen being the final [electron acceptor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_acceptor "Electron acceptor").

If oxygen were not present, pyruvate would not be metabolized by cellular respiration but undergoes a process of [fermentation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermentation "Fermentation"). The pyruvate is not transported into the mitochondrion but remains in the cytoplasm, where it is converted to [waste products](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cellular_waste_product "Cellular waste product") that may be removed from the cell. This serves the purpose of oxidizing the electron carriers so that they can perform glycolysis again and removing the excess pyruvate. Fermentation oxidizes NADH to NAD+ so it can be re-used in glycolysis. In the absence of oxygen, fermentation prevents the buildup of NADH in the cytoplasm and provides NAD+ for glycolysis. This waste product varies depending on the organism. In skeletal muscles, the waste product is [lactic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lactic_acid "Lactic acid"). This type of fermentation is called [lactic acid fermentation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lactic_acid_fermentation "Lactic acid fermentation"). In strenuous exercise, when energy demands exceed energy supply, the respiratory chain cannot process all of the hydrogen atoms joined by NADH. During anaerobic glycolysis, NAD+ regenerates when pairs of hydrogen combine with pyruvate to form lactate. Lactate formation is catalyzed by lactate dehydrogenase in a reversible reaction. Lactate can also be used as an indirect precursor for liver glycogen. During recovery, when oxygen becomes available, NAD+ attaches to hydrogen from lactate to form ATP. In yeast, the waste products are ethanol and carbon dioxide. This type of fermentation is known as alcoholic or [ethanol fermentation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethanol_fermentation "Ethanol fermentation"). The ATP generated in this process is made by [substrate-level phosphorylation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Substrate-level_phosphorylation "Substrate-level phosphorylation"), which does not require oxygen.

### Photosynthesis

[![Image 16](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Simple_photosynthesis_overview.svg/250px-Simple_photosynthesis_overview.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Simple_photosynthesis_overview.svg)

Photosynthesis changes sunlight into chemical energy, splits water to liberate O 2, and fixes CO 2 into sugar.

Photosynthesis is a process used by plants and other organisms to [convert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_transformation "Energy transformation")[light energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_energy "Light energy") into [chemical energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_energy "Chemical energy") that can later be released to fuel the organism's metabolic activities via cellular respiration. This chemical energy is stored in carbohydrate molecules, such as sugars, which are synthesized from carbon dioxide and water.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-OnlineEtDict_photosynthesis-44)[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-LSJ1-45)[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-LSJ2-46) In most cases, oxygen is released as a waste product. Most plants, [algae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algae "Algae"), and [cyanobacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyanobacteria "Cyanobacteria") perform photosynthesis, which is largely responsible for producing and maintaining the [oxygen content](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmospheric_oxygen "Atmospheric oxygen") of the Earth's atmosphere, and supplies most of the energy necessary for life on Earth.[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-bryantfrigaard-47)

Photosynthesis has four stages: [Light absorption](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_absorption "Light absorption"), electron transport, ATP synthesis, and [carbon fixation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_fixation "Carbon fixation").[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-lodish2008l-43) Light absorption is the initial step of photosynthesis whereby light energy is absorbed by [chlorophyll](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chlorophyll "Chlorophyll") pigments attached to proteins in the [thylakoid membranes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thylakoid#Membrane "Thylakoid"). The absorbed light energy is used to remove electrons from a donor (water) to a primary electron acceptor, a [quinone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quinone "Quinone") designated as Q. In the second stage, electrons move from the quinone primary electron acceptor through a series of electron carriers until they reach a final electron acceptor, which is usually the oxidized form of NADP+, which is reduced to NADPH, a process that takes place in a protein complex called [photosystem I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photosystem_I "Photosystem I") (PSI). The transport of electrons is coupled to the movement of protons (or hydrogen) from the stroma to the thylakoid membrane, which forms a pH gradient across the membrane as hydrogen becomes more concentrated in the lumen than in the stroma. This is analogous to the proton-motive force generated across the inner mitochondrial membrane in aerobic respiration.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-lodish2008l-43)

During the third stage of photosynthesis, the movement of protons down their [concentration gradients](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concentration_gradient "Concentration gradient") from the thylakoid lumen to the stroma through the ATP synthase is coupled to the synthesis of ATP by that same ATP synthase.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-lodish2008l-43) The NADPH and ATPs generated by the [light-dependent reactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light-dependent_reaction "Light-dependent reaction") in the second and third stages, respectively, provide the energy and electrons to drive the synthesis of glucose by fixing atmospheric carbon dioxide into existing organic carbon compounds, such as [ribulose bisphosphate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ribulose_bisphosphate "Ribulose bisphosphate") (RuBP) in a sequence of light-independent (or dark) reactions called the [Calvin cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calvin_cycle "Calvin cycle").[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-isbn0-321-73975-2-48)

### Cell signaling

Cell signaling (or communication) is the ability of [cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_(biology) "Cell (biology)") to receive, process, and transmit signals with its environment and with itself.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-neitzelrasband2021-49)[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-cellsignalling-50) Signals can be non-chemical such as light, [electrical impulses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_potential "Action potential"), and heat, or chemical signals (or [ligands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ligand "Ligand")) that interact with [receptors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Receptor_(biochemistry) "Receptor (biochemistry)"), which can be found [embedded](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_surface_receptor "Cell surface receptor") in the [cell membrane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_membrane "Cell membrane") of another cell or [located deep inside](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intracellular_receptor "Intracellular receptor") a cell.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-hillisetal2014e-51)[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-cellsignalling-50) There are generally four types of chemical signals: [autocrine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autocrine_signaling "Autocrine signaling"), [paracrine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paracrine_signaling "Paracrine signaling"), [juxtacrine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Juxtacrine_signalling "Juxtacrine signalling"), and [hormones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hormone "Hormone").[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-hillisetal2014e-51) In autocrine signaling, the ligand affects the same cell that releases it. [Tumor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neoplasm "Neoplasm") cells, for example, can reproduce uncontrollably because they release signals that initiate their own self-division. In paracrine signaling, the ligand diffuses to nearby cells and affects them. For example, brain cells called [neurons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neuron "Neuron") release ligands called [neurotransmitters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neurotransmitter "Neurotransmitter") that diffuse across a [synaptic cleft](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_synapse#Structure "Chemical synapse") to bind with a receptor on an adjacent cell such as another neuron or [muscle cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muscle_cell "Muscle cell"). In juxtacrine signaling, there is direct contact between the signaling and responding cells. Finally, hormones are ligands that travel through the [circulatory systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circulatory_system "Circulatory system") of animals or [vascular systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vascular_system "Vascular system") of plants to reach their target cells. Once a ligand binds with a receptor, it can influence the behavior of another cell, depending on the type of receptor. For instance, neurotransmitters that bind with an [inotropic receptor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ligand-gated_ion_channel "Ligand-gated ion channel") can alter the [excitability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_excitability "Cell excitability") of a target cell. Other types of receptors include [protein kinase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protein_kinase "Protein kinase") receptors (e.g., [receptor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Insulin_receptor "Insulin receptor") for the hormone [insulin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Insulin "Insulin")) and [G protein-coupled receptors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G_protein-coupled_receptor "G protein-coupled receptor"). Activation of G protein-coupled receptors can initiate [second messenger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_messenger_system "Second messenger system") cascades. The process by which a chemical or physical signal is transmitted through a cell as a [series of molecular events](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biochemical_cascade "Biochemical cascade") is called [signal transduction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Signal_transduction "Signal transduction").

### Cell cycle

[![Image 17: A diagram of the mitotic phases](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Mitosis_Stages.svg/960px-Mitosis_Stages.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Mitosis_Stages.svg "A diagram of the mitotic phases")

A diagram of the mitotic phases

The cell cycle is a series of events that take place in a [cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_(biology) "Cell (biology)") that cause it to divide into two daughter cells. These events include the [duplication of its DNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNA_replication "DNA replication") and some of its [organelles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organelle "Organelle"), and the subsequent partitioning of its cytoplasm into two daughter cells in a process called [cell division](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_division "Cell division").[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-52) In [eukaryotes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eukaryote "Eukaryote") (i.e., animal, plant, [fungal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fungal "Fungal"), and [protist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protist "Protist") cells), there are two distinct types of cell division: [mitosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mitosis "Mitosis") and [meiosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meiosis "Meiosis").[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-:0-53) Mitosis is part of the cell cycle, in which replicated [chromosomes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chromosomes "Chromosomes") are separated into two new nuclei. Cell division gives rise to genetically identical cells in which the total number of chromosomes is maintained. In general, mitosis (division of the nucleus) is preceded by the S stage of [interphase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interphase "Interphase") (during which the DNA is replicated) and is often followed by [telophase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Telophase "Telophase") and [cytokinesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cytokinesis "Cytokinesis"); which divides the [cytoplasm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cytoplasm "Cytoplasm"), [organelles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organelle "Organelle") and [cell membrane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_membrane "Cell membrane") of one cell into two new [cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_(biology) "Cell (biology)") containing roughly equal shares of these cellular components. The different stages of mitosis all together define the mitotic phase of an animal cell cycle—the division of the mother cell into two genetically identical daughter cells.[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-54) The cell cycle is a vital process by which a single-celled [fertilized egg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fertilized_egg "Fertilized egg") develops into a mature organism, as well as the process by which hair, skin, [blood cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_cell "Blood cell"), and some [internal organs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viscus "Viscus") are renewed. After cell division, each of the daughter cells begin the [interphase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interphase "Interphase") of a new cycle. In contrast to mitosis, meiosis results in four haploid daughter cells by undergoing one round of DNA replication followed by two divisions.[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-freeman2017m-55)[Homologous chromosomes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homologous_chromosome "Homologous chromosome") are separated in the first division ([meiosis I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meiosis#Meiosis_I "Meiosis")), and sister chromatids are separated in the second division ([meiosis II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meiosis#Meiosis_II "Meiosis")). Both of these cell division cycles are used in the process of sexual reproduction at some point in their life cycle. Both are believed to be present in the last eukaryotic common ancestor.

[![Image 18](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Meiosis_Overview_new.svg/250px-Meiosis_Overview_new.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Meiosis_Overview_new.svg)

In meiosis, the chromosomes duplicate and the [homologous chromosomes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homologous_chromosome "Homologous chromosome") exchange genetic information during meiosis I. The daughter cells divide again in meiosis II to form haploid [gametes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamete "Gamete").

[Prokaryotes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prokaryote "Prokaryote") (i.e., [archaea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archaea "Archaea") and bacteria) can also undergo cell division (or [binary fission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_fission "Binary fission")). Unlike the processes of [mitosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mitosis "Mitosis") and [meiosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meiosis "Meiosis") in eukaryotes, binary fission in prokaryotes takes place without the formation of a [spindle apparatus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spindle_apparatus "Spindle apparatus") on the cell. Before binary fission, DNA in the bacterium is tightly coiled. After it has uncoiled and duplicated, it is pulled to the separate poles of the bacterium as it increases the size to prepare for splitting. Growth of a new cell wall begins to separate the bacterium (triggered by [FtsZ](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/FtsZ "FtsZ") polymerization and "Z-ring" formation).[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-:32046082-56) The new cell wall ([septum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Septum_(cell_biology) "Septum (cell biology)")) fully develops, resulting in the complete split of the bacterium. The new daughter cells have tightly coiled DNA rods, [ribosomes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ribosome "Ribosome"), and [plasmids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasmid "Plasmid").

### Sexual reproduction and meiosis

Meiosis is a central feature of sexual reproduction in eukaryotes, and the most fundamental function of [meiosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meiosis "Meiosis") appears to be conservation of the integrity of the [genome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genome "Genome") that is passed on to progeny by parents.[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-57)[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-58) Two aspects of [sexual reproduction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sexual_reproduction "Sexual reproduction"), [meiotic recombination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genetic_recombination "Genetic recombination") and [outcrossing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outcrossing "Outcrossing"), are likely maintained respectively by the adaptive advantages of recombinational repair of genomic [DNA damage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNA_damage_(naturally_occurring) "DNA damage (naturally occurring)") and genetic [complementation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complementation_(genetics) "Complementation (genetics)") which masks the expression of deleterious recessive [mutations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mutation "Mutation").[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-Bernstein1985-59)

The beneficial effect of genetic complementation, derived from outcrossing (cross-fertilization) is also referred to as hybrid vigor or heterosis. Charles Darwin in his 1878 book _The Effects of Cross and Self-Fertilization in the Vegetable Kingdom_[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-60) at the start of chapter XII noted “The first and most important of the conclusions which may be drawn from the observations given in this volume, is that generally cross-fertilisation is beneficial and self-fertilisation often injurious, at least with the plants on which I experimented.” [Genetic variation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genetic_variation "Genetic variation"), often produced as a byproduct of sexual reproduction, may provide long-term advantages to those sexual lineages that engage in [outcrossing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outcrossing "Outcrossing").[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-Bernstein1985-59)

Genetics
--------

### Inheritance

[![Image 19](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Punnett_square_mendel_flowers.svg/250px-Punnett_square_mendel_flowers.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Punnett_square_mendel_flowers.svg)

[Punnett square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Punnett_square "Punnett square") depicting a cross between two pea plants heterozygous for purple (B) and white (b) blossoms

[Genetics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genetics "Genetics") is the scientific study of inheritance.[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-griffithsetal2014l-61)[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-griffiths2000sect60-62)[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-Hartl_and_Jones-63)[Mendelian inheritance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mendelian_inheritance "Mendelian inheritance"), specifically, is the process by which genes and traits are passed on from parents to offspring.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-miko2008a-23) It has several principles. The first is that genetic characteristics, [alleles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allele "Allele"), are discrete and have alternate forms (e.g., purple vs. white or tall vs. dwarf), each inherited from one of two parents. Based on the [law of dominance and uniformity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mendelian_inheritance#Law_of_Dominance_and_Uniformity "Mendelian inheritance"), which states that some alleles are [dominant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dominance_(genetics) "Dominance (genetics)") while others are [recessive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dominance_(genetics) "Dominance (genetics)"); an organism with at least one dominant allele will display the [phenotype](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phenotype "Phenotype") of that dominant allele. During gamete formation, the alleles for each gene segregate, so that each gamete carries only one allele for each gene. [Heterozygotic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heterozygous "Heterozygous") individuals produce gametes with an equal frequency of two alleles. Finally, the [law of independent assortment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mendelian_inheritance#Law_of_Independent_Assortment "Mendelian inheritance"), states that genes of different traits can segregate independently during the formation of gametes, i.e., genes are unlinked. An exception to this rule would include traits that are [sex-linked](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sex_linkage "Sex linkage"). [Test crosses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Test_cross "Test cross") can be performed to experimentally determine the underlying [genotype](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genotype "Genotype") of an organism with a dominant phenotype.[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-miko2008b-64) A [Punnett square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Punnett_square "Punnett square") can be used to predict the results of a test cross. The [chromosome theory of inheritance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boveri%E2%80%93Sutton_chromosome_theory "Boveri–Sutton chromosome theory"), which states that genes are found on chromosomes, was supported by [Thomas Morgans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Hunt_Morgan "Thomas Hunt Morgan")'s experiments with [fruit flies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drosophila_melanogaster "Drosophila melanogaster"), which established the [sex linkage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sex_linkage "Sex linkage") between eye color and sex in these insects.[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-miko2008c-65)

### Genes and DNA

Further information: [Gene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gene "Gene") and [DNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNA "DNA")

[![Image 20](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/0c/DNA_animation.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:DNA_animation.gif)

Bases lie between two spiraling DNA strands.

A gene is a unit of [heredity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heredity "Heredity") that corresponds to a region of deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA) that carries genetic information that controls form or function of an organism. DNA is composed of two [polynucleotide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynucleotide "Polynucleotide") chains that coil around each other to form a [double helix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleic_acid_double_helix "Nucleic acid double helix").[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-hillisetal2014i-66) It is found as linear [chromosomes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chromosome "Chromosome") in [eukaryotes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eukaryote "Eukaryote"), and circular chromosomes in [prokaryotes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prokaryote "Prokaryote"). The set of chromosomes in a cell is collectively known as its [genome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genome "Genome"). In eukaryotes, DNA is mainly in the [cell nucleus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_nucleus "Cell nucleus").[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-russell2001-67) In prokaryotes, the DNA is held within the [nucleoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleoid "Nucleoid").[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-68) The genetic information is held within genes, and the complete assemblage in an organism is called its [genotype](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genotype "Genotype").[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-69)[DNA replication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNA_replication "DNA replication") is a [semiconservative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semiconservative_replication "Semiconservative replication") process whereby each strand serves as a template for a new strand of DNA.[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-hillisetal2014i-66) Mutations are heritable changes in DNA.[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-hillisetal2014i-66) They can arise [spontaneously](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mutation#Spontaneous_mutation "Mutation") as a result of replication errors that were not corrected by proofreading or can be [induced](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mutation#Induced_mutation "Mutation") by an environmental [mutagen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mutagen "Mutagen") such as a chemical (e.g., [nitrous acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrous_acid "Nitrous acid"), [benzopyrene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzopyrene "Benzopyrene")) or radiation (e.g., [x-ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray "X-ray"), [gamma ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamma_ray "Gamma ray"), [ultraviolet radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultraviolet "Ultraviolet"), particles emitted by unstable isotopes).[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-hillisetal2014i-66) Mutations can lead to phenotypic effects such as loss-of-function, [gain-of-function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gain-of-function_research "Gain-of-function research"), and conditional mutations.[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-hillisetal2014i-66) Some mutations are beneficial, as they are a source of [genetic variation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genetic_variation "Genetic variation") for evolution.[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-hillisetal2014i-66) Others are harmful if they were to result in a loss of function of genes needed for survival.[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-hillisetal2014i-66)

### Gene expression

[![Image 21](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Extended_Central_Dogma_with_Enzymes.jpg/330px-Extended_Central_Dogma_with_Enzymes.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Extended_Central_Dogma_with_Enzymes.jpg)

The extended [central dogma of molecular biology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_dogma_of_molecular_biology "Central dogma of molecular biology") includes all the processes involved in the flow of genetic information.

Gene expression is the molecular process by which a [genotype](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genotype "Genotype") encoded in DNA gives rise to an observable [phenotype](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phenotype "Phenotype") in the proteins of an organism's body. This process is summarized by the [central dogma of molecular biology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_dogma_of_molecular_biology "Central dogma of molecular biology"), which was formulated by [Francis Crick](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Francis_Crick "Francis Crick") in 1958.[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-70)[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-71)[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-72) According to the Central Dogma, genetic information flows from DNA to RNA to protein. There are two gene expression processes: [transcription](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transcription_(genetics) "Transcription (genetics)") (DNA to RNA) and [translation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Translation_(genetics) "Translation (genetics)") (RNA to protein).[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-linelowitz2015-73)

### Gene regulation

The regulation of gene expression by environmental factors and during different stages of [development](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Developmental_biology "Developmental biology") can occur at each step of the process such as [transcription](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transcription_(biology) "Transcription (biology)"), [RNA splicing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RNA_splicing "RNA splicing"), [translation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Translation_(biology) "Translation (biology)"), and [post-translational modification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Post-translational_modification "Post-translational modification") of a protein.[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-hillisetal2014k-74) Gene expression can be influenced by positive or negative regulation, depending on which of the two types of regulatory proteins called [transcription factors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transcription_factor "Transcription factor") bind to the DNA sequence close to or at a promoter.[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-hillisetal2014k-74) A cluster of genes that share the same promoter is called an [operon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operon "Operon"), found mainly in prokaryotes and some lower eukaryotes (e.g., _[Caenorhabditis elegans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caenorhabditis\_elegans "Caenorhabditis elegans")_).[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-hillisetal2014k-74)[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-keeneandtenenbaum2002-75) In positive regulation of gene expression, the [activator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Activator_(genetics) "Activator (genetics)") is the transcription factor that stimulates transcription when it binds to the sequence near or at the promoter. Negative regulation occurs when another transcription factor called a [repressor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Repressor "Repressor") binds to a DNA sequence called an [operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operon#Operator "Operon"), which is part of an operon, to prevent transcription. Repressors can be inhibited by compounds called [inducers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inducer "Inducer") (e.g., [allolactose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allolactose "Allolactose")), thereby allowing transcription to occur.[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-hillisetal2014k-74) Specific genes that can be activated by inducers are called [inducible genes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gene_expression#Regulation_of_gene_expression "Gene expression"), in contrast to [constitutive genes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gene_expression#Regulation_of_gene_expression "Gene expression") that are almost constantly active.[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-hillisetal2014k-74) In contrast to both, [structural genes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Structural_gene "Structural gene") encode proteins that are not involved in gene regulation.[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-hillisetal2014k-74) In addition to regulatory events involving the promoter, gene expression can also be regulated by [epigenetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epigenetics "Epigenetics") changes to [chromatin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chromatin "Chromatin"), which is a complex of DNA and protein found in eukaryotic cells.[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-hillisetal2014k-74)

### Genes, development, and evolution

[Development](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Developmental_biology "Developmental biology") is the process by which a [multicellular organism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multicellular_organism "Multicellular organism") (plant or animal) goes through a series of changes, starting from a single cell, and taking on various forms that are characteristic of its life cycle.[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-hillisetal2014n-76) There are four key processes that underlie development: [Determination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_fate_determination "Cell fate determination"), [differentiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cellular_differentiation "Cellular differentiation"), [morphogenesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morphogenesis "Morphogenesis"), and growth. Determination sets the developmental fate of a cell, which becomes more restrictive during development. Differentiation is the process by which specialized cells arise from less specialized cells such as [stem cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stem_cell "Stem cell").[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-77)[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-78) Stem cells are [undifferentiated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cellular_differentiation "Cellular differentiation") or partially differentiated [cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_(biology) "Cell (biology)") that can differentiate into various [types of cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Types_of_cells "Types of cells") and [proliferate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_proliferation "Cell proliferation") indefinitely to produce more of the same stem cell.[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-:7-79) Cellular differentiation dramatically changes a cell's size, shape, [membrane potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Membrane_potential "Membrane potential"), [metabolic activity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metabolism "Metabolism"), and responsiveness to signals, which are largely due to highly controlled modifications in [gene expression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gene_expression "Gene expression") and [epigenetics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epigenetics "Epigenetics"). With a few exceptions, cellular differentiation almost never involves a change in the [DNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNA "DNA") sequence itself.[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-80) Thus, different cells can have very different physical characteristics despite having the same [genome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genome "Genome"). Morphogenesis, or the development of body form, is the result of spatial differences in gene expression.[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-hillisetal2014n-76) A small fraction of the genes in an organism's genome called the [developmental-genetic toolkit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evo-devo_gene_toolkit "Evo-devo gene toolkit") control the development of that organism. These toolkit genes are highly conserved among [phyla](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phylum "Phylum"), meaning that they are ancient and very similar in widely separated groups of animals. Differences in deployment of toolkit genes affect the body plan and the number, identity, and pattern of body parts. Among the most important toolkit genes are the [_Hox_ genes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hox_gene "Hox gene"). Hox genes determine where repeating parts, such as the many [vertebrae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertebra "Vertebra") of snakes, will grow in a developing embryo or larva.[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-CarrollNatHist-81)

Evolution
---------

### Evolutionary processes

[![Image 22](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Mutation_and_selection_diagram.svg/250px-Mutation_and_selection_diagram.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Mutation_and_selection_diagram.svg)

[Natural selection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_selection "Natural selection") for darker traits

[Evolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evolution "Evolution") is a central organizing concept in biology. It is the change in [heritable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heredity "Heredity")[characteristics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phenotypic_trait "Phenotypic trait") of populations over successive [generations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generation "Generation").[[82]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-82)[[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-83) In [artificial selection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artificial_selection "Artificial selection"), animals were selectively bred for specific traits. [[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-urry2017v-84) Given that traits are inherited, populations contain a varied mix of traits, and reproduction is able to increase any population, Darwin argued that in the natural world, it was nature that played the role of humans in selecting for specific traits.[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-urry2017v-84) Darwin inferred that individuals who possessed heritable traits better adapted to their environments are more likely to survive and produce more offspring than other individuals.[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-urry2017v-84) He further inferred that this would lead to the accumulation of favorable traits over successive generations, thereby increasing the match between the organisms and their environment.[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-lewontin70-85)[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-86)[[87]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-futuyma2017a-87)[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-urry2017v-84)[[88]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-hillisetal2014o-88)

### Speciation

A species is a group of organisms that mate with one another and speciation is the process by which one lineage splits into two lineages as a result of having evolved independently from each other.[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-hillisetal2014q-89) For speciation to occur, there has to be [reproductive isolation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reproductive_isolation "Reproductive isolation").[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-hillisetal2014q-89) Reproductive isolation can result from incompatibilities between genes as described by [Bateson–Dobzhansky–Muller model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bateson%E2%80%93Dobzhansky%E2%80%93Muller_model "Bateson–Dobzhansky–Muller model"). Reproductive isolation also tends to increase with [genetic divergence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genetic_divergence "Genetic divergence"). Speciation can occur when there are physical barriers that divide an ancestral species, a process known as [allopatric speciation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allopatric_speciation "Allopatric speciation").[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-hillisetal2014q-89)

### Phylogeny

![Image 23](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Phylogenetic_tree.svg/330px-Phylogenetic_tree.svg.png)

Phylogenetic tree showing the domains of [bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacterium "Bacterium"), [archaea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archaea "Archaea"), and [eukaryotes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eukaryote "Eukaryote")

A phylogeny is an evolutionary history of a specific group of organisms or their genes.[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-hillisetal2014p-90) It can be represented using a [phylogenetic tree](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phylogenetic_tree "Phylogenetic tree"), a diagram showing lines of descent among organisms or their genes. Each line drawn on the time axis of a tree represents a [lineage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lineage_(evolution) "Lineage (evolution)") of descendants of a particular species or population. When a lineage divides into two, it is represented as a fork or split on the phylogenetic tree.[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-hillisetal2014p-90) Phylogenetic trees are the basis for comparing and grouping different species.[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-hillisetal2014p-90) Different species that share a feature inherited from a common ancestor are described as having [homologous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homology_(biology) "Homology (biology)") features (or [synapomorphy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apomorphy_and_synapomorphy "Apomorphy and synapomorphy")).[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-kitchingetal2001-91)[[92]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-Futuyma2017p-92)[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-hillisetal2014p-90) Phylogeny provides the basis of biological classification.[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-hillisetal2014p-90) This classification system is rank-based, with the highest rank being the [domain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_(biology) "Domain (biology)") followed by [kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_(biology) "Kingdom (biology)"), [phylum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phylum "Phylum"), [class](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Class_(biology) "Class (biology)"), [order](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Order_(biology) "Order (biology)"), [family](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Family_(biology) "Family (biology)"), [genus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genus "Genus"), and [species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Species "Species").[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-hillisetal2014p-90) All organisms can be classified as belonging to one of [three domains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-domain_system "Three-domain system"): Archaea (originally Archaebacteria), Bacteria (originally eubacteria), or Eukarya (includes the fungi, plant, and animal kingdoms).[[93]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-domain-93)

### History of life

The history of life on Earth traces how organisms have evolved from the earliest emergence of life to present day. Earth formed about 4.5 billion years ago and all life on Earth, both living and extinct, descended from a [last universal common ancestor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Last_universal_common_ancestor "Last universal common ancestor") that lived about [3.5 billion years ago](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_evolution "Timeline of evolution").[[94]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-94)[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-deduve2002-95) Geologists have developed a [geologic time scale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geologic_time_scale "Geologic time scale") that divides the history of the Earth into major divisions, starting with four eons ([Hadean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hadean "Hadean"), [Archean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archean "Archean"), [Proterozoic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proterozoic "Proterozoic"), and [Phanerozoic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phanerozoic "Phanerozoic")), the first three of which are collectively known as the [Precambrian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Precambrian "Precambrian"), which lasted approximately 4 billion years.[[96]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-hillisetal2014r-96) Each eon can be divided into eras, with the Phanerozoic eon that began 539 million years ago[[97]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-97) being subdivided into [Paleozoic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paleozoic "Paleozoic"), [Mesozoic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mesozoic "Mesozoic"), and [Cenozoic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cenozoic "Cenozoic") eras.[[96]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-hillisetal2014r-96) These three eras together comprise eleven [periods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geologic_time_scale#Terminology "Geologic time scale") ([Cambrian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cambrian "Cambrian"), [Ordovician](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordovician "Ordovician"), [Silurian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silurian "Silurian"), [Devonian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Devonian "Devonian"), [Carboniferous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carboniferous "Carboniferous"), [Permian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permian "Permian"), [Triassic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triassic "Triassic"), [Jurassic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jurassic "Jurassic"), [Cretaceous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cretaceous "Cretaceous"), [Tertiary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tertiary_period "Tertiary period"), and [Quaternary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quaternary "Quaternary")).[[96]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-hillisetal2014r-96)

The similarities among all known present-day [species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Species "Species") indicate that they have diverged through the process of [evolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evolution "Evolution") from their common ancestor.[[98]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-98) Biologists regard the ubiquity of the [genetic code](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genetic_code "Genetic code") as evidence of universal [common descent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Common_descent "Common descent") for all [bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacterium "Bacterium"), [archaea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archaea "Archaea"), and [eukaryotes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eukaryote "Eukaryote").[[99]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-Futuyma-99)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-Pearce_343%E2%80%93364-3)[[100]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-Rosing_674%E2%80%93676-100)[[101]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-Ohtomo_25%E2%80%9328-101)[Microbial mats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microbial_mat "Microbial mat") of coexisting bacteria and archaea were the dominant form of life in the early [Archean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archean "Archean") eon and many of the major steps in early evolution are thought to have taken place in this environment.[[102]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-NisbetFowler1999-102) The earliest evidence of [eukaryotes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eukaryote "Eukaryote") dates from 1.85 billion years ago,[[103]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-103)[[104]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-fedonkin2003-104) and while they may have been present earlier, their diversification accelerated when they started using oxygen in their [metabolism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metabolism "Metabolism"). Later, around 1.7 billion years ago, [multicellular organisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multicellular_organism "Multicellular organism") began to appear, with [differentiated cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cellular_differentiation "Cellular differentiation") performing specialised functions.[[105]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-bonner1999-105)

Algae-like multicellular land plants are dated back to about 1 billion years ago,[[106]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-106) although evidence suggests that [microorganisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism "Microorganism") formed the earliest [terrestrial ecosystems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terrestrial_ecosystem "Terrestrial ecosystem"), at least 2.7 billion years ago.[[107]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-107) Microorganisms are thought to have paved the way for the inception of land plants in the [Ordovician](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordovician "Ordovician") period. Land plants were so successful that they are thought to have contributed to the [Late Devonian extinction event](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Late_Devonian_extinction "Late Devonian extinction").[[108]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-AlgeoScheckler1998-108)

[Ediacara biota](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ediacara_biota "Ediacara biota") appear during the [Ediacaran](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ediacaran "Ediacaran") period,[[109]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-109) while [vertebrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertebrate "Vertebrate"), along with most other modern [phyla](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phylum "Phylum") originated about 525 million years ago during the [Cambrian explosion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cambrian_explosion "Cambrian explosion").[[110]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-D-G.Shu_et_al._1999-110) During the Permian period, [synapsids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synapsid "Synapsid"), including the ancestors of [mammals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mammal "Mammal"), dominated the land,[[111]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-111) but most of this group became extinct in the [Permian–Triassic extinction event](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permian%E2%80%93Triassic_extinction_event "Permian–Triassic extinction event") 252 million years ago.[[112]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-112) During the recovery from this catastrophe, [archosaurs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archosaur "Archosaur") became the most abundant land vertebrates;[[113]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-TannerLucas2004-113) one archosaur group, the dinosaurs, dominated the Jurassic and Cretaceous periods.[[114]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-114) After the [Cretaceous–Paleogene extinction event](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cretaceous%E2%80%93Paleogene_extinction_event "Cretaceous–Paleogene extinction event") 66 million years ago killed off the non-avian dinosaurs,[[115]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-115) mammals [increased rapidly in size and diversity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adaptive_radiation "Adaptive radiation").[[116]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-116) Such [mass extinctions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction_event "Extinction event") may have accelerated evolution by providing opportunities for new groups of organisms to diversify.[[117]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-Van_Valkenburgh_1999_463%E2%80%93493-117)

### Bacteria and Archaea

[![Image 24](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Gemmatimonas_aurantiaca.jpg/250px-Gemmatimonas_aurantiaca.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Gemmatimonas_aurantiaca.jpg)

[![Image 25](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Halobacteria.jpg/250px-Halobacteria.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Halobacteria.jpg)

Bacteria are a type of [cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_(biology) "Cell (biology)") that constitute a large [domain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_(biology) "Domain (biology)") of [prokaryotic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prokaryotic "Prokaryotic")[microorganisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism "Microorganism"). Typically a few [micrometers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Micrometre "Micrometre") in length, bacteria have a [number of shapes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacterial_cell_structure#Cell_morphology "Bacterial cell structure"), ranging from [spheres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coccus "Coccus") to [rods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacillus_(shape) "Bacillus (shape)") and [spirals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spiral_bacteria "Spiral bacteria"). Bacteria were among the first life forms to appear on Earth, and are present in most of its [habitats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habitat "Habitat"). Bacteria inhabit soil, water, [acidic hot springs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hot_spring "Hot spring"), [radioactive waste](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radioactive_waste "Radioactive waste"),[[118]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-118) and the [deep biosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deep_biosphere "Deep biosphere") of the [Earth's crust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_crust "Earth's crust"). Bacteria also live in [symbiotic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symbiotic "Symbiotic") and [parasitic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parasitic "Parasitic") relationships with plants and animals. Most bacteria have not been characterised, and only about 27 percent of the [bacterial phyla](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacterial_phyla "Bacterial phyla") have species that can be [grown](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microbiological_culture "Microbiological culture") in the laboratory.[[119]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-Dudek-119)

[Archaea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archaea "Archaea") constitute the other domain of prokaryotic cells and were initially [classified](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taxonomy_(biology) "Taxonomy (biology)") as bacteria, receiving the name archaebacteria (in the Archaebacteria [kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_(biology) "Kingdom (biology)")), a term that has fallen out of use.[[120]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-120) Archaeal cells have unique properties separating them from the other [two domains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-domain_system "Three-domain system"), Bacteria and [Eukaryota](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eukaryote "Eukaryote"). Archaea are further divided into multiple recognized [phyla](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phylum "Phylum"). Archaea and bacteria are generally similar in size and shape, although a few archaea have very different shapes, such as the flat and square cells of _[Haloquadratum walsbyi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haloquadratum\_walsbyi "Haloquadratum walsbyi")_.[[121]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-121) Despite this [morphological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morphology_(biology) "Morphology (biology)") similarity to bacteria, archaea possess [genes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gene "Gene") and several [metabolic pathways](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metabolic_pathway "Metabolic pathway") that are more closely related to those of eukaryotes, notably for the [enzymes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enzyme "Enzyme") involved in [transcription](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transcription_(genetics) "Transcription (genetics)") and [translation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Translation_(biology) "Translation (biology)"). Other aspects of archaeal biochemistry are unique, such as their reliance on [ether lipids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ether_lipid "Ether lipid") in their [cell membranes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_membrane "Cell membrane"),[[122]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-122) including [archaeols](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archaeol "Archaeol"). Archaea use more energy sources than eukaryotes: these range from [organic compounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_compounds "Organic compounds"), such as sugars, to [ammonia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia "Ammonia"), [metal ions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ion "Ion") or even [hydrogen gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen"). [Salt-tolerant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halophile "Halophile") archaea (the [Haloarchaea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haloarchaea "Haloarchaea")) use sunlight as an energy source, and other species of archaea [fix carbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_fixation "Carbon fixation"), but unlike plants and [cyanobacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyanobacteria "Cyanobacteria"), no known species of archaea does both. Archaea [reproduce asexually](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asexual_reproduction "Asexual reproduction") by [binary fission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_fission "Binary fission"), [fragmentation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fragmentation_(reproduction) "Fragmentation (reproduction)"), or [budding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Budding "Budding"); unlike bacteria, no known species of Archaea form [endospores](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endospore "Endospore").

The first observed archaea were [extremophiles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extremophile "Extremophile"), living in extreme environments, such as [hot springs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hot_spring "Hot spring") and [salt lakes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salt_lake "Salt lake") with no other organisms. Improved molecular detection tools led to the discovery of archaea in almost every [habitat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habitat "Habitat"), including soil, oceans, and [marshlands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marshland "Marshland"). Archaea are particularly numerous in the oceans, and the archaea in [plankton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plankton "Plankton") may be one of the most abundant groups of organisms on the planet.

Archaea are a major part of [Earth's life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life "Life"). They are part of the [microbiota](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microbiota "Microbiota") of all organisms. In the [human microbiome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_microbiome "Human microbiome"), they are important in the [gut](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colon_(anatomy) "Colon (anatomy)"), mouth, and on the skin.[[123]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-Bang2015-123) Their morphological, metabolic, and geographical diversity permits them to play multiple ecological roles: carbon fixation; nitrogen cycling; organic compound turnover; and maintaining microbial symbiotic and [syntrophic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syntrophy "Syntrophy") communities, for example.[[124]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-124)

### Eukaryotes

[![Image 26](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Euglena_diagram.jpg/250px-Euglena_diagram.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Euglena_diagram.jpg)

_[Euglena](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euglena "Euglena")_, a single-celled eukaryote that can both move and photosynthesize

Eukaryotes are hypothesized to have split from archaea, which was followed by their [endosymbioses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endosymbiont "Endosymbiont") with bacteria (or [symbiogenesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symbiogenesis "Symbiogenesis")) that gave rise to mitochondria and chloroplasts, both of which are now part of modern-day eukaryotic cells.[[125]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-hillisetal2014t-125) The major lineages of eukaryotes diversified in the [Precambrian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Precambrian "Precambrian") about 1.5 billion years ago and can be classified into eight major [clades](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clade "Clade"): [alveolates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alveolate "Alveolate"), [excavates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Excavata "Excavata"), [stramenopiles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stramenopile "Stramenopile"), plants, [rhizarians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhizaria "Rhizaria"), [amoebozoans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amoebozoa "Amoebozoa"), [fungi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fungus "Fungus"), and animals.[[125]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-hillisetal2014t-125) Five of these clades are collectively known as [protists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protist "Protist"), which are mostly microscopic [eukaryotic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eukaryotic "Eukaryotic") organisms that are not plants, fungi, or animals.[[125]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-hillisetal2014t-125) While it is likely that protists share a [common ancestor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Common_descent "Common descent") (the [last eukaryotic common ancestor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Last_eukaryotic_common_ancestor "Last eukaryotic common ancestor")),[[126]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-O'Malley_Leger_Wideman_Ruiz-Trillo_pp._338%E2%80%93344-126) protists by themselves do not constitute a separate clade as some protists may be more closely related to plants, fungi, or animals than they are to other protists. Like groupings such as [algae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algae "Algae"), [invertebrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invertebrate "Invertebrate"), or [protozoans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protozoa "Protozoa"), the protist grouping is not a formal taxonomic group but is used for convenience.[[125]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-hillisetal2014t-125)[[127]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-Taylor_pp._1707%E2%80%931714-127) Most protists are unicellular; these are called microbial eukaryotes.[[125]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-hillisetal2014t-125)

Plants are mainly multicellular [organisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism "Organism"), predominantly [photosynthetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photosynthetic "Photosynthetic") eukaryotes of the [kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_(biology) "Kingdom (biology)") Plantae, which would exclude fungi and some [algae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algae "Algae"). Plant cells were derived by endosymbiosis of a [cyanobacterium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyanobacterium "Cyanobacterium") into an early eukaryote about one billion years ago, which gave rise to chloroplasts.[[128]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-hillisetal2014u-128) The first several clades that emerged following primary endosymbiosis were aquatic and most of the aquatic photosynthetic eukaryotic organisms are collectively described as algae, which is a term of convenience as not all algae are closely related.[[128]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-hillisetal2014u-128) Algae comprise several distinct clades such as [glaucophytes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glaucophyte "Glaucophyte"), which are microscopic freshwater algae that may have resembled in form to the early unicellular ancestor of Plantae.[[128]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-hillisetal2014u-128) Unlike glaucophytes, the other algal clades such as [red](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_algae "Red algae") and [green algae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green_algae "Green algae") are multicellular. Green algae comprise three major clades: [chlorophytes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chlorophyte "Chlorophyte"), [coleochaetophytes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coleochaetophyceae "Coleochaetophyceae"), and [stoneworts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stonewort "Stonewort").[[128]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-hillisetal2014u-128)

[Fungi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fungus "Fungus") are eukaryotes that digest foods outside their bodies,[[129]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-hillisetal2014v-129) secreting digestive enzymes that break down large food molecules before absorbing them through their cell membranes. Many fungi are also [saprobes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saprobe "Saprobe"), feeding on dead organic matter, making them important [decomposers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decomposer "Decomposer") in ecological systems.[[129]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-hillisetal2014v-129)

Animals are multicellular eukaryotes. With few exceptions, animals [consume organic material](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heterotroph "Heterotroph"), [breathe oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cellular_respiration#Aerobic_respiration "Cellular respiration"), are [able to move](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Motility "Motility"), can [reproduce sexually](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sexual_reproduction "Sexual reproduction"), and grow from a hollow sphere of [cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_(biology) "Cell (biology)"), the [blastula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blastula "Blastula"), during [embryonic development](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Embryogenesis "Embryogenesis"). Over 1.5 million [living](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extant_taxon "Extant taxon") animal [species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Species "Species") have been [described](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Species_description "Species description")—of which around 1 million are [insects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Insecta "Insecta")—but it has been estimated there are over 7 million animal species in total. They have [complex interactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecology "Ecology") with each other and their environments, forming intricate [food webs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Food_web "Food web").[[130]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-hillisetal2014w-130)

### Viruses

[![Image 27](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Phage.jpg/250px-Phage.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Phage.jpg)

[Bacteriophages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacteriophage "Bacteriophage") attached to a bacterial cell wall

Viruses are [submicroscopic](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/submicroscopic "wikt:submicroscopic")[infectious agents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infectious_agent "Infectious agent") that [replicate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viral_replication "Viral replication") inside the [cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_(biology) "Cell (biology)") of [organisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism "Organism").[[131]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-NG-20200415-131) Viruses infect all types of [life forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life_forms "Life forms"), from animals and plants to [microorganisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism "Microorganism"), including bacteria and [archaea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archaea "Archaea").[[132]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-pmid16984643-132)[[133]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-NYT-20210226-133) More than 6,000 [virus species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virus_species "Virus species") have been described in detail.[[134]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-ictv2019-134) Viruses are found in almost every [ecosystem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem "Ecosystem") on Earth and are the most numerous type of biological entity.[[135]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-Lawrence-135)[[136]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-136)

The origins of viruses in the evolutionary [history of life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_life "History of life") are unclear: some may have [evolved](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evolution "Evolution") from [plasmids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasmid "Plasmid")—pieces of DNA that can move between cells—while others may have evolved from bacteria. In evolution, viruses are an important means of [horizontal gene transfer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horizontal_gene_transfer "Horizontal gene transfer"), which increases [genetic diversity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genetic_diversity "Genetic diversity") in a way analogous to [sexual reproduction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sexual_reproduction "Sexual reproduction").[[137]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-Canchaya-137) Because viruses possess some but not all characteristics of life, they have been described as "organisms at the edge of life",[[138]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-ReferenceA-138) and as [self-replicators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-replication "Self-replication").[[139]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-kooninstarokadomskyy2016-139)

Ecology
-------

Ecology is the study of the distribution and abundance of life, the interaction between organisms and their [environment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_environment "Natural environment").[[140]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-140)

### Ecosystems

The [community](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Community_(ecology) "Community (ecology)") of living ([biotic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biotic_component "Biotic component")) organisms in conjunction with the nonliving ([abiotic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abiotic_component "Abiotic component")) components (e.g., water, light, radiation, temperature, [humidity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Humidity "Humidity"), [atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere "Atmosphere"), [acidity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acidity "Acidity"), and soil) of their environment is called an [ecosystem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem "Ecosystem").[[141]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-habitats_of_the_world-141)[[142]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-142)[[143]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-hillisetal2014ao-143) These biotic and abiotic components are linked together through [nutrient cycles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nutrient_cycle "Nutrient cycle") and energy flows.[[144]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-Odum1971-144) Energy from the sun enters the system through [photosynthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photosynthesis "Photosynthesis") and is incorporated into plant tissue. By feeding on plants and on one another, animals move [matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matter "Matter") and energy through the system. They also influence the quantity of plant and [microbial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microbe "Microbe")[biomass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biomass_(ecology) "Biomass (ecology)") present. By breaking down dead [organic matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_matter "Organic matter"), [decomposers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decomposer "Decomposer") release [carbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon "Carbon") back to the atmosphere and facilitate [nutrient cycling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nutrient_cycling "Nutrient cycling") by converting nutrients stored in dead biomass back to a form that can be readily used by plants and other microbes.[[145]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-chapinetal2002a-145)

### Populations

[![Image 28](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Logistic_Carrying_Capacity.svg/330px-Logistic_Carrying_Capacity.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Logistic_Carrying_Capacity.svg)

Reaching carrying capacity through a logistic growth curve

A population is the group of [organisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism "Organism") of the same [species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Species "Species") that occupies an [area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geographical_area "Geographical area") and [reproduce](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sexual_reproduction "Sexual reproduction") from generation to generation.[[146]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-hillisetal2014ap-146)[[147]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-urry2017ba-147)[[148]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-148)[[149]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-149)[[150]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-150)[Population size](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Population_size "Population size") can be estimated by multiplying population density by the area or volume. The [carrying capacity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carrying_capacity "Carrying capacity") of an [environment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_environment "Natural environment") is the maximum population size of a [species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Species "Species") that can be sustained by that specific environment, given the food, [habitat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habitat "Habitat"), [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drinking_water "Drinking water"), and other [resources](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resource "Resource") that are available.[[151]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-:32-151) The carrying capacity of a population can be affected by changing environmental conditions such as changes in the availability of resources and the cost of maintaining them. In [human populations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_population "Human population"), new [technologies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Technology "Technology") such as the [Green revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green_revolution "Green revolution") have helped increase the Earth's carrying capacity for humans over time, which has stymied the attempted predictions of impending population decline, the most famous of which was by [Thomas Malthus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Robert_Malthus "Thomas Robert Malthus") in the 18th century.[[146]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-hillisetal2014ap-146)

### Communities

[![Image 29](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/TrophicWeb.jpg/330px-TrophicWeb.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:TrophicWeb.jpg)

A (a) trophic pyramid and a (b) simplified food web. The trophic pyramid represents the biomass at each level.[[152]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-Odum05-152)

A community is a group of populations of species occupying the same geographical area at the same time.[[153]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-153) A [biological interaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biological_interaction "Biological interaction") is the effect that a pair of [organisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism "Organism") living together in a community have on each other. They can be either of the same species (intraspecific interactions), or of different species (interspecific interactions). These effects may be short-term, like [pollination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pollination "Pollination") and [predation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Predation "Predation"), or long-term; both often strongly influence the [evolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evolution "Evolution") of the species involved. A long-term interaction is called a [symbiosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symbiosis "Symbiosis"). Symbioses range from [mutualism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mutualism_(biology) "Mutualism (biology)"), beneficial to both partners, to [competition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Competition_(biology) "Competition (biology)"), harmful to both partners.[[154]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-154) Every species participates as a consumer, resource, or both in [consumer–resource interactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Consumer%E2%80%93resource_interactions "Consumer–resource interactions"), which form the core of [food chains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Food_chain "Food chain") or [food webs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Food_web "Food web").[[155]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-hillisetal2014aq-155) There are different [trophic levels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trophic_level "Trophic level") within any food web, with the lowest level being the primary producers (or [autotrophs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autotroph "Autotroph")) such as plants and algae that convert energy and inorganic material into [organic compounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_compound "Organic compound"), which can then be used by the rest of the community.[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-bryantfrigaard-47)[[156]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-156)[[157]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-157) At the next level are the [heterotrophs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heterotroph "Heterotroph"), which are the species that obtain energy by breaking apart organic compounds from other organisms.[[155]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-hillisetal2014aq-155) Heterotrophs that consume plants are primary consumers (or [herbivores](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herbivore "Herbivore")) whereas heterotrophs that consume herbivores are secondary consumers (or [carnivores](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carnivore "Carnivore")). And those that eat secondary consumers are tertiary consumers and so on. [Omnivorous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Omnivore "Omnivore") heterotrophs are able to consume at multiple levels. Finally, there are [decomposers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decomposer "Decomposer") that feed on the waste products or dead bodies of organisms.[[155]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-hillisetal2014aq-155) On average, the total amount of energy incorporated into the [biomass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biomass "Biomass") of a trophic level per unit of time is about one-tenth of the energy of the trophic level that it consumes. Waste and dead material used by decomposers as well as heat lost from metabolism make up the other ninety percent of energy that is not consumed by the next trophic level.[[158]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-hillisetal2014ar-158)

### Biosphere

[![Image 30](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Carbon_cycle.jpg/330px-Carbon_cycle.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Carbon_cycle.jpg)

[Fast carbon cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_cycle#Types_of_dynamics "Carbon cycle") showing the movement of carbon between land, atmosphere, and oceans in billions of tons per year. Yellow numbers are natural fluxes, red are human contributions, white are stored carbon. Effects of the [slow carbon cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_cycle#Types_of_dynamics "Carbon cycle"), such as volcanic and tectonic activity, are not included.[[159]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-nasacc-159)

In the global ecosystem or biosphere, matter exists as different interacting compartments, which can be biotic or abiotic as well as accessible or inaccessible, depending on their forms and locations.[[160]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-hillisetal2014as-160) For example, matter from terrestrial autotrophs are both biotic and accessible to other organisms whereas the matter in rocks and minerals are abiotic and inaccessible. A [biogeochemical cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biogeochemical_cycle "Biogeochemical cycle") is a pathway by which specific [elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_element "Chemical element") of matter are turned over or moved through the biotic ([biosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere "Biosphere")) and the abiotic ([lithosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lithosphere "Lithosphere"), [atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere "Atmosphere"), and [hydrosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrosphere "Hydrosphere")) compartments of Earth. There are biogeochemical cycles for [nitrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen_cycle "Nitrogen cycle"), [carbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_cycle "Carbon cycle"), and [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water_cycle "Water cycle").

### Conservation

Conservation biology is the study of the conservation of Earth's [biodiversity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity "Biodiversity") with the aim of protecting [species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Species "Species"), their [habitats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habitats "Habitats"), and [ecosystems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystems "Ecosystems") from excessive rates of [extinction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction "Extinction") and the erosion of biotic interactions.[[161]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-SahneyBenton2008RecoveryFromProfoundExtinction-161)[[162]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-ConsBiol80-162)[[163]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-163) It is concerned with factors that influence the maintenance, loss, and restoration of biodiversity and the science of sustaining evolutionary processes that engender [genetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genetics "Genetics"), population, [species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Species "Species"), and ecosystem diversity.[[164]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-Hunter96-164)[[165]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-Groom06-165)[[166]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-Dyke08-166)[[167]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-SahneyBentonFerry2010LinksDiversityVertebrates-167) The concern stems from estimates suggesting that up to 50% of all species on the planet will disappear within the next 50 years,[[168]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-Koh-168) which has contributed to poverty, starvation, and will reset the course of evolution on this planet.[[169]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-169)[[170]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-Jackson-170)[Biodiversity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity "Biodiversity") affects the functioning of ecosystems, which provide a variety of [services](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem_services "Ecosystem services") upon which people depend. Conservation biologists research and educate on the trends of [biodiversity loss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity_loss "Biodiversity loss"), species [extinctions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction "Extinction"), and the negative effect these are having on our capabilities to [sustain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sustainability "Sustainability") the well-being of human society. Organizations and citizens are responding to the [current biodiversity crisis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holocene_extinction "Holocene extinction") through conservation action plans that direct research, monitoring, and education programs that engage concerns at local through global scales.[[171]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-Soule86-171)[[164]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-Hunter96-164)[[165]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-Groom06-165)[[166]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology#cite_note-Dyke08-166)
