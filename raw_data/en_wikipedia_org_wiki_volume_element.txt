In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), a **volume element** provides a means for [integrating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral "Integral") a [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)") with respect to [volume](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume "Volume") in various coordinate systems such as [spherical coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_coordinate_system "Spherical coordinate system") and [cylindrical coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylindrical_coordinate_system "Cylindrical coordinate system"). Thus a volume element is an expression of the form ![Image 1: {\displaystyle \mathrm {d} V=\rho (u_{1},u_{2},u_{3})\,\mathrm {d} u_{1}\,\mathrm {d} u_{2}\,\mathrm {d} u_{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9ca8f66a3261d9b301caa732575a5eeb030f36b7) where the ![Image 2: {\displaystyle u_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/14f13cb025ff2e136dcbd2fc81ddf965b728e3d7) are the coordinates, so that the volume of any set ![Image 3: {\displaystyle B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47136aad860d145f75f3eed3022df827cee94d7a) can be computed by ![Image 4: {\displaystyle \operatorname {Volume} (B)=\int _{B}\rho (u_{1},u_{2},u_{3})\,\mathrm {d} u_{1}\,\mathrm {d} u_{2}\,\mathrm {d} u_{3}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b57731fcb94bade599732d584cb1d220e53b0c34) For example, in spherical coordinates ![Image 5: {\displaystyle \mathrm {d} V=u_{1}^{2}\sin u_{2}\,\mathrm {d} u_{1}\,\mathrm {d} u_{2}\,\mathrm {d} u_{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c5dc32ad557bbd48d23e9ce97de80cc65b4cb657), and so ![Image 6: {\displaystyle \rho =u_{1}^{2}\sin u_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6b1c7e12373c4cbd552b900cd2b9176be011630a).

The notion of a volume element is not limited to three dimensions: in two dimensions it is often known as the **area element**, and in this setting it is useful for doing [surface integrals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_integral "Surface integral"). Under changes of coordinates, the volume element changes by the absolute value of the [Jacobian determinant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacobian_determinant "Jacobian determinant") of the coordinate transformation (by the [change of variables formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integration_by_substitution#Substitution_for_multiple_variables "Integration by substitution")). This fact allows volume elements to be defined as a kind of [measure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Measure_(mathematics) "Measure (mathematics)") on a [manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manifold "Manifold"). On an [orientable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orientability "Orientability")[differentiable manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differentiable_manifold "Differentiable manifold"), a volume element typically arises from a [volume form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume_form "Volume form"): a top degree [differential form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_form "Differential form"). On a non-orientable manifold, the volume element is typically the [absolute value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_value "Absolute value") of a (locally defined) volume form: it defines a [1-density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Density_on_a_manifold "Density on a manifold").

Volume element in Euclidean space
---------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Volume_element&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Volume element in Euclidean space")]

In [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space"), the volume element is given by the product of the differentials of the Cartesian coordinates ![Image 7: {\displaystyle \mathrm {d} V=\mathrm {d} x\,\mathrm {d} y\,\mathrm {d} z.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/32835e39f230e48a4e92da2437fdb9a71cd61c85) In different coordinate systems of the form ![Image 8: {\displaystyle x=x(u_{1},u_{2},u_{3})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a0a5dbc76763c96edc3314f77323f2199a0ac80c), ![Image 9: {\displaystyle y=y(u_{1},u_{2},u_{3})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a0bb0e09f8a3c5c344e42263a93975fc0e7da454), ![Image 10: {\displaystyle z=z(u_{1},u_{2},u_{3})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3667c1ac2f5a1cbe58b5b896b5a184714cb7c21e), the volume element [changes by the Jacobian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacobian_matrix_and_determinant "Jacobian matrix and determinant") (determinant) of the coordinate change: ![Image 11: {\displaystyle \mathrm {d} V=\left|{\frac {\partial (x,y,z)}{\partial (u_{1},u_{2},u_{3})}}\right|\,\mathrm {d} u_{1}\,\mathrm {d} u_{2}\,\mathrm {d} u_{3}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8acb1370c0cfbba4463d8afb14582b83c5a6faa2) For example, in spherical coordinates (mathematical convention) ![Image 12: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}x&=\rho \cos \theta \sin \phi \\y&=\rho \sin \theta \sin \phi \\z&=\rho \cos \phi \end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/009f812c6649fca0f4c5ae8a0bb940a8ecdc39c8) the Jacobian determinant is ![Image 13: {\displaystyle \left|{\frac {\partial (x,y,z)}{\partial (\rho ,\phi ,\theta )}}\right|=\rho ^{2}\sin \phi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6073fde09c74d1f87c711af7c982bea16239fbd1) so that ![Image 14: {\displaystyle \mathrm {d} V=\rho ^{2}\sin \phi \,\mathrm {d} \rho \,\mathrm {d} \theta \,\mathrm {d} \phi .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7936548e0b5c75d5c12193ee08a82831d7828360) This can be seen as a special case of the fact that differential forms transform through a pullback ![Image 15: {\displaystyle F^{*}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c8ab6d9b2b48fcfd2f5c741de789391d4cdd0f13) as ![Image 16: {\displaystyle F^{*}(u\;dy^{1}\wedge \cdots \wedge dy^{n})=(u\circ F)\det \left({\frac {\partial F^{j}}{\partial x^{i}}}\right)\mathrm {d} x^{1}\wedge \cdots \wedge \mathrm {d} x^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2d354b5db18dbb58efb4706b9948f41621ed34f1)

Volume element of a linear subspace
-----------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Volume_element&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Volume element of a linear subspace")]

Consider the [linear subspace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_subspace "Linear subspace") of the _n_-dimensional [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space")**R**_n_ that is spanned by a collection of [linearly independent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linearly_independent "Linearly independent") vectors ![Image 17: {\displaystyle X_{1},\dots ,X_{k}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e5107c989e71d81dba6fd5c071149a0a26725853) To find the volume element of the subspace, it is useful to know the fact from [linear algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_algebra "Linear algebra") that the volume of the [parallelepiped](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelepiped "Parallelepiped") spanned by the ![Image 18: {\displaystyle X_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af4a0955af42beb5f85aa05fb8c07abedc13990d) is the square root of the [determinant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Determinant "Determinant") of the [Gramian matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gramian_matrix "Gramian matrix") of the ![Image 19: {\displaystyle X_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af4a0955af42beb5f85aa05fb8c07abedc13990d): ![Image 20: {\displaystyle {\sqrt {\det(X_{i}\cdot X_{j})_{i,j=1\dots k}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/73775627c977ab3aae5308ea55dc4b992812fb2e)

Any point _p_ in the subspace can be given coordinates ![Image 21: {\displaystyle (u_{1},u_{2},\dots ,u_{k})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cdf4b28d7b17cab7f5e0149c7cfd3955f57c4f09) such that ![Image 22: {\displaystyle p=u_{1}X_{1}+\cdots +u_{k}X_{k}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d0fc8f210affbee42bf8610c639617389f7e3706) At a point _p_, if we form a small parallelepiped with sides ![Image 23: {\displaystyle \mathrm {d} u_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cd2e793d6d6b247449777500fec90a085fb78fe1), then the volume of that parallelepiped is the square root of the determinant of the Grammian matrix ![Image 24: {\displaystyle {\sqrt {\det \left((du_{i}X_{i})\cdot (du_{j}X_{j})\right)_{i,j=1\dots k}}}={\sqrt {\det(X_{i}\cdot X_{j})_{i,j=1\dots k}}}\;\mathrm {d} u_{1}\,\mathrm {d} u_{2}\,\cdots \,\mathrm {d} u_{k}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/345954134ec1a21d4fc5452ef6f64cd5c5c55bc7) This therefore defines the volume form in the linear subspace.

Volume element of manifolds
---------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Volume_element&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Volume element of manifolds")]

On an _oriented_[Riemannian manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_manifold "Riemannian manifold") of dimension _n_, the volume element is a volume form equal to the [Hodge dual](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hodge_dual "Hodge dual") of the unit constant function, ![Image 25: {\displaystyle f(x)=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ea78f54e69b72f398cf6077e61c50a05b532d4c0): ![Image 26: {\displaystyle \omega =\star 1.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68ddf4516f71b32d242e5a5b56e39eca26ec0547) Equivalently, the volume element is precisely the [Levi-Civita tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_tensor "Levi-Civita tensor")![Image 27: {\displaystyle \epsilon }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3837cad72483d97bcdde49c85d3b7b859fb3fd2).[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume_element#cite_note-1) In coordinates, ![Image 28: {\displaystyle \omega =\epsilon ={\sqrt {\left|\det g\right|}}\,\mathrm {d} x^{1}\wedge \cdots \wedge \mathrm {d} x^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/266ae159e0f34afdbb658305b944fb5b5b2bd549) where ![Image 29: {\displaystyle \det g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b3a689ff5058ad7c943a6ac7c6d262c7fc77287c) is the [determinant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Determinant "Determinant") of the [metric tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_tensor "Metric tensor")_g_ written in the coordinate system.

### Area element of a surface

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Volume_element&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Area element of a surface")]

A simple example of a volume element can be explored by considering a two-dimensional surface embedded in _n_-dimensional [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space"). Such a volume element is sometimes called an _area element_. Consider a subset ![Image 30: {\displaystyle U\subset \mathbb {R} ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/88bf916836435eaac9f115120ab48ec6c558b4e2) and a mapping function ![Image 31: {\displaystyle \varphi :U\to \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b0ee82f15526d60ae0cf3d5f1ba6a71f05463f9) thus defining a surface embedded in ![Image 32: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d). In two dimensions, volume is just area, and a volume element gives a way to determine the area of parts of the surface. Thus a volume element is an expression of the form ![Image 33: {\displaystyle f(u_{1},u_{2})\,\mathrm {d} u_{1}\,\mathrm {d} u_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f83765f7652654c528cc1d4de342b66f310dfa0c) that allows one to compute the area of a set _B_ lying on the surface by computing the integral ![Image 34: {\displaystyle \operatorname {Area} (B)=\int _{B}f(u_{1},u_{2})\,\mathrm {d} u_{1}\,\mathrm {d} u_{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3c53595a78b5649705fea564ca653eb654c19daf)

Here we will find the volume element on the surface that defines area in the usual sense. The [Jacobian matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacobian_matrix "Jacobian matrix") of the mapping is ![Image 35: {\displaystyle J_{ij}={\frac {\partial \varphi _{i}}{\partial u_{j}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af5da1e3858664770664d58b5ae3a51a59913d30) with index _i_ running from 1 to _n_, and _j_ running from 1 to 2. The Euclidean [metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_(mathematics) "Metric (mathematics)") in the _n_-dimensional space induces a metric ![Image 36: {\displaystyle g=J^{T}J}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/28fe097305f712f3d16d198eb3c6b954e85e0f21) on the set _U_, with matrix elements ![Image 37: {\displaystyle g_{ij}=\sum _{k=1}^{n}J_{ki}J_{kj}=\sum _{k=1}^{n}{\frac {\partial \varphi _{k}}{\partial u_{i}}}{\frac {\partial \varphi _{k}}{\partial u_{j}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9043e5557085dc511dbffc8cfefef736cc15848d)

The [determinant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Determinant "Determinant") of the metric is given by ![Image 38: {\displaystyle \det g=\left|{\frac {\partial \varphi }{\partial u_{1}}}\wedge {\frac {\partial \varphi }{\partial u_{2}}}\right|^{2}=\det(J^{T}J)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b33971da8ebac73e0cff253a7073ee0c557b3c09)

For a regular surface, this determinant is non-vanishing; equivalently, the Jacobian matrix has rank 2.

Now consider a change of coordinates on _U_, given by a [diffeomorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffeomorphism "Diffeomorphism")![Image 39: {\displaystyle f\colon U\to U,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b58ffd9cca25499a2d09c2070bb9554b796026e2) so that the coordinates ![Image 40: {\displaystyle (u_{1},u_{2})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bb6764e9870c65f0289890bdb7edf20be77b802a) are given in terms of ![Image 41: {\displaystyle (v_{1},v_{2})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8e8ab5272bbaa8b9527fc9a9dde1296d18ce696c) by ![Image 42: {\displaystyle (u_{1},u_{2})=f(v_{1},v_{2})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3b5d321e111dd5ff706a7431beefac3426c8aa33). The Jacobian matrix of this transformation is given by ![Image 43: {\displaystyle F_{ij}={\frac {\partial f_{i}}{\partial v_{j}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b5c367c04227ace4de8cf67d7acdfb29c3373d4d)

In the new coordinates, we have ![Image 44: {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial \varphi _{i}}{\partial v_{j}}}=\sum _{k=1}^{2}{\frac {\partial \varphi _{i}}{\partial u_{k}}}{\frac {\partial f_{k}}{\partial v_{j}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f36312d9af0a5f26b0db9a5fbff6c2e6bbf365b6) and so the metric transforms as ![Image 45: {\displaystyle {\tilde {g}}=F^{T}gF}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/afa9ea68709e82ca4a2c07e801d581b4bf181752) where ![Image 46: {\displaystyle {\tilde {g}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bcf709e979316ee494a3f076f7e1d97be44a3f8f) is the pullback metric in the _v_ coordinate system. The determinant is ![Image 47: {\displaystyle \det {\tilde {g}}=\det g\left(\det F\right)^{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d9d1c30083bec9174815bb61ffd4b928578bc061)

Given the above construction, it should now be straightforward to understand how the volume element is invariant under an orientation-preserving change of coordinates.

In two dimensions, the volume is just the area. The area of a subset ![Image 48: {\displaystyle B\subset U}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f98fcc1dda3a96950248395514073a118036e215) is given by the integral ![Image 49: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}{\mbox{Area}}(B)&=\iint _{B}{\sqrt {\det g}}\;\mathrm {d} u_{1}\;\mathrm {d} u_{2}\\[1.6ex]&=\iint _{B}{\sqrt {\det g}}\left|\det F\right|\;\mathrm {d} v_{1}\;\mathrm {d} v_{2}\\[1.6ex]&=\iint _{B}{\sqrt {\det {\tilde {g}}}}\;\mathrm {d} v_{1}\;\mathrm {d} v_{2}.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/154e0086b523c6c208e2857a9f57dbaffb72c58f)

Thus, in either coordinate system, the volume element takes the same expression: the expression of the volume element is invariant under a change of coordinates.

Note that there was nothing particular to two dimensions in the above presentation; the above trivially generalizes to arbitrary dimensions.

For example, consider the sphere with radius _r_ centered at the origin in **R**3. This can be parametrized using [spherical coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_coordinates "Spherical coordinates") with the map ![Image 50: {\displaystyle \phi (u_{1},u_{2})=(r\cos u_{1}\sin u_{2},r\sin u_{1}\sin u_{2},r\cos u_{2}).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/447b00aaa9b9bde59955db43d04a4dee4ca47693) Then ![Image 51: {\displaystyle g={\begin{pmatrix}r^{2}\sin ^{2}u_{2}&0\\0&r^{2}\end{pmatrix}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68fbcdc97adc591a7d6a3ee7c43df1fe8f681a2f) and the area element is ![Image 52: {\displaystyle \omega ={\sqrt {\det g}}\;\mathrm {d} u_{1}\mathrm {d} u_{2}=r^{2}\sin u_{2}\,\mathrm {d} u_{1}\mathrm {d} u_{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6857c846d52ab61dee0ffe56556aac5b49ebc1fd)
