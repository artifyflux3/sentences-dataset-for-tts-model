In [vector calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_calculus "Vector calculus") and [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics"), a **vector field** is an assignment of a [vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_(mathematics_and_physics) "Vector (mathematics and physics)") to each point in a [space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_(mathematics) "Space (mathematics)"), most commonly [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space")![Image 2: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d).[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_field#cite_note-Galbis-2012-p12-1) A vector field on a [plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_(geometry) "Plane (geometry)") can be visualized as a collection of arrows with given magnitudes and directions, each attached to a point on the plane. Vector fields are often used to model, for example, the speed and direction of a moving fluid throughout [three dimensional space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three_dimensional_space "Three dimensional space"), such as the [wind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wind "Wind"), or the strength and direction of some [force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Force "Force"), such as the [magnetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_field "Magnetic field") or [gravitational](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity "Gravity") force, as it changes from one point to another point.

The elements of [differential and integral calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_and_integral_calculus "Differential and integral calculus") extend naturally to vector fields. When a vector field represents [force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Force "Force"), the [line integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_integral "Line integral") of a vector field represents the [work](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Work_(physics) "Work (physics)") done by a force moving along a path, and under this interpretation [conservation of energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservation_of_energy "Conservation of energy") is exhibited as a special case of the [fundamental theorem of calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_theorem_of_calculus "Fundamental theorem of calculus"). Vector fields can usefully be thought of as representing the velocity of a moving flow in space, and this physical intuition leads to notions such as the [divergence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divergence "Divergence") (which represents the rate of change of [volume](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume "Volume") of a flow) and [curl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curl_(mathematics) "Curl (mathematics)") (which represents the rotation of a flow).

A vector field is a special case of a _[vector-valued function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector-valued\_function "Vector-valued function")_, whose domain's dimension has no relation to the dimension of its range; for example, the [position vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Position_vector "Position vector") of a [space curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_curve "Space curve") is defined only for smaller subset of the ambient space. Likewise, n [coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_system "Coordinate system"), a vector field on a domain in _n_-dimensional Euclidean space ![Image 3: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d) can be represented as a vector-valued function that associates an _n_-tuple of real numbers to each point of the domain. This representation of a vector field depends on the coordinate system, and there is a well-defined transformation law (_[covariance and contravariance of vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covariance\_and\_contravariance\_of\_vectors "Covariance and contravariance of vectors")_) in passing from one coordinate system to the other.

Vector fields are often discussed on [open subsets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_set "Open set") of Euclidean space, but also make sense on other subsets such as [surfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_(topology) "Surface (topology)"), where they associate an arrow tangent to the surface at each point (a [tangent vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry_of_curves "Differential geometry of curves")). More generally, vector fields are defined on [differentiable manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differentiable_manifold "Differentiable manifold"), which are spaces that look like Euclidean space on small scales, but may have more complicated structure on larger scales. In this setting, a vector field gives a tangent vector at each point of the manifold (that is, a [section](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Section_(fiber_bundle) "Section (fiber bundle)") of the [tangent bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_bundle "Tangent bundle") to the manifold). Vector fields are one kind of [tensor field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_field "Tensor field").

Two representations of the same vector field: **v**(_x_, _y_) = −**r**. The arrows depict the field at discrete points, however, the field exists everywhere.

Given a subset _S_ of **R**_n_, a **vector field** is represented by a [vector-valued function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector-valued_function "Vector-valued function")_V_: _S_ → **R**_n_ in standard [Cartesian coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinates "Cartesian coordinates")(_x_ 1, …, _x_ _n_). If each component of _V_ is continuous, then _V_ is a continuous vector field. It is common to focus on **smooth** vector fields, meaning that each component is a [smooth function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smooth_function "Smooth function") (differentiable any number of times). A vector field can be visualized as assigning a vector to individual points within an _n_-dimensional space.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_field#cite_note-Galbis-2012-p12-1)

### Coordinate transformation law

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Vector_field&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Coordinate transformation law")]

In physics, a [vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_vector "Euclidean vector") is additionally distinguished by how its coordinates change when one measures the same vector with respect to a different background coordinate system. The [transformation properties of vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_vector#Vectors,_pseudovectors,_and_transformations "Euclidean vector") distinguish a vector as a geometrically distinct entity from a simple list of scalars, or from a [covector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covector "Covector").

Thus, suppose that (_x_ 1, ..., _x_ _n_) is a choice of Cartesian coordinates, in terms of which the components of the vector V are ![Image 20: {\displaystyle V_{x}=(V_{1,x},\dots ,V_{n,x})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5a68b37373763bbaf4536732a4f13724e83559e3) and suppose that (_y_ 1,...,_y_ _n_) are _n_ functions of the _x_ _i_ defining a different coordinate system. Then the components of the vector _V_ in the new coordinates are required to satisfy the transformation law

![Image 21: {\displaystyle V_{i,y}=\sum _{j=1}^{n}{\frac {\partial y_{i}}{\partial x_{j}}}V_{j,x}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9ba34807a0a319437f1251ed665782f7cc9f223a)1

Such a transformation law is called [contravariant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covariance_and_contravariance_of_vectors "Covariance and contravariance of vectors"). A similar transformation law characterizes vector fields in physics: specifically, a vector field is a specification of _n_ functions in each coordinate system subject to the transformation law (**[1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_field#math_1)**) relating the different coordinate systems.

Vector fields are thus contrasted with [scalar fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_field "Scalar field"), which associate a number or _scalar_ to every point in space, and are also contrasted with simple lists of scalar fields, which do not transform under coordinate changes.

*   A vector field for the movement of air on Earth will associate for every point on the surface of the Earth a vector with the wind speed and direction for that point. This can be drawn using arrows to represent the wind; the length ([magnitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnitude_(mathematics) "Magnitude (mathematics)")) of the arrow will be an indication of the wind speed. A "high" on the usual [barometric pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barometric_pressure "Barometric pressure") map would then act as a source (arrows pointing away), and a "low" would be a sink (arrows pointing towards), since air tends to move from high pressure areas to low pressure areas.
*   [Velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity "Velocity") field of a moving [fluid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluid "Fluid"). In this case, a [velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity "Velocity") vector is associated to each point in the fluid.
*   [Streamlines, streaklines and pathlines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Streamlines,_Streaklines_and_Pathlines "Streamlines, Streaklines and Pathlines") are 3 types of lines that can be made from (time-dependent) vector fields. They are: 
    *   streaklines: the line produced by particles passing through a specific fixed point over various times
    *   pathlines: showing the path that a given particle (of zero mass) would follow.
    *   streamlines (or fieldlines): the path of a particle influenced by the instantaneous field (i.e., the path of a particle if the field is held fixed).

*   [Magnetic fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_field "Magnetic field"). The fieldlines can be revealed using small [iron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron "Iron") filings.
*   [Maxwell's equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxwell%27s_equations "Maxwell's equations") allow us to use a given set of initial and boundary conditions to deduce, for every point in [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space"), a magnitude and direction for the [force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Force "Force") experienced by a charged test particle at that point; the resulting vector field is the [electric field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_field "Electric field").
*   A [gravitational field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_field "Gravitational field") generated by any massive object is also a vector field. For example, the gravitational field vectors for a spherically symmetric body would all point towards the sphere's center with the magnitude of the vectors reducing as radial distance from the body increases.

### Gradient field in Euclidean spaces

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Vector_field&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Gradient field in Euclidean spaces")]

[![Image 46](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Irrotationalfield.svg/330px-Irrotationalfield.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Irrotationalfield.svg)

A vector field that has circulation about a point cannot be written as the gradient of a function.

Vector fields can be constructed out of [scalar fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_field "Scalar field") using the [gradient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gradient "Gradient") operator (denoted by the [del](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Del "Del"): ∇).[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_field#cite_note-4)

A vector field _V_ defined on an open set _S_ is called a **gradient field** or a **[conservative field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservative_field "Conservative field")** if there exists a real-valued function (a scalar field) _f_ on _S_ such that ![Image 47: {\displaystyle V=\nabla f=\left({\frac {\partial f}{\partial x_{1}}},{\frac {\partial f}{\partial x_{2}}},{\frac {\partial f}{\partial x_{3}}},\dots ,{\frac {\partial f}{\partial x_{n}}}\right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e9072db2637fb193e4921e3516a4f8e75dd023b9)

The associated [flow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flow_(mathematics) "Flow (mathematics)") is called the **gradient flow**, and is used in the method of [gradient descent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gradient_descent "Gradient descent").

The [path integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_integral "Line integral") along any [closed curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_curve "Closed curve")_γ_ (_γ_(0) = _γ_(1)) in a conservative field is zero: ![Image 48: {\displaystyle \oint _{\gamma }V(\mathbf {x} )\cdot \mathrm {d} \mathbf {x} =\oint _{\gamma }\nabla f(\mathbf {x} )\cdot \mathrm {d} \mathbf {x} =f(\gamma (1))-f(\gamma (0)).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/318b853b664638637eedebd6f7196d1bb371d857)

### Central field in euclidean spaces

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Vector_field&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Central field in euclidean spaces")]

A _C_∞-vector field over **R**_n_ \ {0} is called a **central field** if ![Image 49: {\displaystyle V(T(p))=T(V(p))\qquad (T\in \mathrm {O} (n,\mathbb {R} ))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d62f18e5f584162d54952c0caaa4a18954a5e759) where O(_n_, **R**) is the [orthogonal group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_group "Orthogonal group"). We say central fields are [invariant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invariant_(mathematics) "Invariant (mathematics)") under [orthogonal transformations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_matrix "Orthogonal matrix") around 0.

The point 0 is called the **center** of the field.

Since orthogonal transformations are actually rotations and reflections, the invariance conditions mean that vectors of a central field are always directed towards, or away from, 0; this is an alternate (and simpler) definition. A central field is always a gradient field, since defining it on one semiaxis and integrating gives an antigradient.

Operations on vector fields
---------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Vector_field&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Operations on vector fields")]

A common technique in physics is to integrate a vector field along a [curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry_of_curves "Differential geometry of curves"), also called determining its [line integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_integral "Line integral"). Intuitively this is summing up all vector components in line with the tangents to the curve, expressed as their scalar products. For example, given a particle in a force field (e.g. gravitation), where each vector at some point in space represents the force acting there on the particle, the line integral along a certain path is the work done on the particle, when it travels along this path. Intuitively, it is the sum of the scalar products of the force vector and the small tangent vector in each point along the curve.

The line integral is constructed analogously to the [Riemann integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_integral "Riemann integral") and it exists if the curve is rectifiable (has finite length) and the vector field is continuous.

Given a vector field V and a curve γ, [parametrized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parametric_equation "Parametric equation") by t in [_a_, _b_] (where a and b are [real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number")), the line integral is defined as ![Image 50: {\displaystyle \int _{\gamma }V(\mathbf {x} )\cdot \mathrm {d} \mathbf {x} =\int _{a}^{b}V(\gamma (t))\cdot {\dot {\gamma }}(t)\,\mathrm {d} t.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ae806e0c23c74a4257467a26f1a81c2da365a9b9)

To show vector field topology one can use [line integral convolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_integral_convolution "Line integral convolution").

The [divergence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divergence "Divergence") of a vector field on Euclidean space is a function (or scalar field). In three-dimensions, the divergence is defined by ![Image 51: {\displaystyle \operatorname {div} \mathbf {F} =\nabla \cdot \mathbf {F} ={\frac {\partial F_{1}}{\partial x}}+{\frac {\partial F_{2}}{\partial y}}+{\frac {\partial F_{3}}{\partial z}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/07be0b6db45b09236c2ca48b40a2d0e96e1839a6)

with the obvious generalization to arbitrary dimensions. The divergence at a point represents the degree to which a small volume around the point is a [source or a sink](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sources_and_sinks "Sources and sinks") for the vector flow, a result which is made precise by the [divergence theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divergence_theorem "Divergence theorem").

The divergence can also be defined on a [Riemannian manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_manifold "Riemannian manifold"), that is, a manifold with a [Riemannian metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_metric "Riemannian metric") that measures the length of vectors.

### Curl in three dimensions

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Vector_field&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Curl in three dimensions")]

The [curl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curl_(mathematics) "Curl (mathematics)") is an operation which takes a vector field and produces another vector field. The curl is defined only in three dimensions, but some properties of the curl can be captured in higher dimensions with the [exterior derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_derivative "Exterior derivative"). In three dimensions, it is defined by ![Image 52: {\displaystyle \operatorname {curl} \mathbf {F} =\nabla \times \mathbf {F} =\left({\frac {\partial F_{3}}{\partial y}}-{\frac {\partial F_{2}}{\partial z}}\right)\mathbf {e} _{1}-\left({\frac {\partial F_{3}}{\partial x}}-{\frac {\partial F_{1}}{\partial z}}\right)\mathbf {e} _{2}+\left({\frac {\partial F_{2}}{\partial x}}-{\frac {\partial F_{1}}{\partial y}}\right)\mathbf {e} _{3}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e7817501699a78926548651742bf0424cdd4e283)

The curl measures the density of the [angular momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum "Angular momentum") of the vector flow at a point, that is, the amount to which the flow circulates around a fixed axis. This intuitive description is made precise by [Stokes' theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stokes%27_theorem "Stokes' theorem").

### Index of a vector field

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Vector_field&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Index of a vector field")]

The index of a vector field is an integer that helps describe its behaviour around an isolated zero (i.e., an isolated singularity of the field). In the plane, the index takes the value −1 at a saddle singularity but +1 at a source or sink singularity.

Let _n be_ the dimension of the manifold on which the vector field is defined. Take a closed surface (homeomorphic to the (n-1)-sphere) S around the zero, so that no other zeros lie in the interior of S. A map from this sphere to a unit sphere of dimension _n_−1 can be constructed by dividing each vector on this sphere by its length to form a unit length vector, which is a point on the unit sphere S _n_−1. This defines a continuous map from S to S _n_−1. The index of the vector field at the point is the [degree](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_of_a_continuous_mapping#Differential_topology "Degree of a continuous mapping") of this map. It can be shown that this integer does not depend on the choice of S, and therefore depends only on the vector field itself.

The index is not defined at any non-singular point (i.e., a point where the vector is non-zero). It is equal to +1 around a source, and more generally equal to (−1)_k_ around a saddle that has _k_ contracting dimensions and _n_−_k_ expanding dimensions.

**The index of the vector field** as a whole is defined when it has just finitely many zeroes. In this case, all zeroes are isolated, and the index of the vector field is defined to be the sum of the indices at all zeroes.

For an ordinary (2-dimensional) sphere in three-dimensional space, it can be shown that the index of any vector field on the sphere must be 2. This shows that every such vector field must have a zero. This implies the [hairy ball theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hairy_ball_theorem "Hairy ball theorem").

For a vector field on a compact manifold with finitely many zeroes, the [Poincaré-Hopf theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poincar%C3%A9-Hopf_theorem "Poincaré-Hopf theorem") states that the vector field’s index is the manifold’s [Euler characteristic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler_characteristic "Euler characteristic").

[![Image 53](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Magnet0873.png/250px-Magnet0873.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Magnet0873.png)

[Magnetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetism "Magnetism") field lines of an iron bar ([magnetic dipole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_dipole "Magnetic dipole"))

[Michael Faraday](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michael_Faraday "Michael Faraday"), in his concept of _[lines of force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lines\_of\_force "Lines of force"),_ emphasized that the field _itself_ should be an object of study, which it has become throughout physics in the form of [field theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_theory_(physics) "Field theory (physics)").

In addition to the magnetic field, other phenomena that were modeled by Faraday include the electrical field and [light field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_field "Light field").

In recent decades many phenomenological formulations of irreversible dynamics and evolution equations in physics, from the mechanics of complex fluids and solids to chemical kinetics and quantum thermodynamics, have converged towards the geometric idea of "steepest entropy ascent" or "gradient flow" as a consistent universal modeling framework that guarantees compatibility with the second law of thermodynamics and extends well-known near-equilibrium results such as Onsager reciprocity to the far-nonequilibrium realm.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_field#cite_note-5)

Consider the flow of a fluid through a region of space. At any given time, any point of the fluid has a particular velocity associated with it; thus there is a vector field associated to any flow. The converse is also true: it is possible to associate a flow to a vector field having that vector field as its velocity.

Given a vector field ![Image 54: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) defined on ![Image 55: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2), one defines curves ![Image 56: {\displaystyle \gamma (t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54fa4a5d64e164410e4a18106677bebefe1a1f1b) on ![Image 57: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2) such that for each ![Image 58: {\displaystyle t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/65658b7b223af9e1acc877d848888ecdb4466560) in an interval ![Image 59: {\displaystyle I}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/535ea7fc4134a31cbe2251d9d3511374bc41be9f), ![Image 60: {\displaystyle \gamma '(t)=V(\gamma (t))\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9b9da6888a814d3a6e6f38d89dcc8899fa7ced76)

By the [Picard–Lindelöf theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Picard%E2%80%93Lindel%C3%B6f_theorem "Picard–Lindelöf theorem"), if ![Image 61: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) is [Lipschitz continuous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lipschitz_continuity "Lipschitz continuity") there is a _unique_![Image 62: {\displaystyle C^{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bd24bae0d7570018e828e19851902c09c618af91)-curve ![Image 63: {\displaystyle \gamma _{x}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/caf2e6d6984dc3ef9963ec7e44329a8a2c755df5) for each point ![Image 64: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) in ![Image 65: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2) so that, for some ![Image 66: {\displaystyle \varepsilon >0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e04ec3670b50384a3ce48aca42e7cc5131a06b12), ![Image 67: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\gamma _{x}(0)&=x\\\gamma '_{x}(t)&=V(\gamma _{x}(t))\qquad \forall t\in (-\varepsilon ,+\varepsilon )\subset \mathbb {R} .\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3fb897220ccc6a4e28bae83e4d35e2b7cad53c5e)

The curves ![Image 68: {\displaystyle \gamma _{x}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/caf2e6d6984dc3ef9963ec7e44329a8a2c755df5) are called **integral curves** or **trajectories** (or less commonly, flow lines) of the vector field ![Image 69: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) and partition ![Image 70: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2) into [equivalence classes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equivalence_class "Equivalence class"). It is not always possible to extend the interval ![Image 71: {\displaystyle (-\varepsilon ,+\varepsilon )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a165d9d1ef3ef8f1a7b42d0065dbec019b82b491) to the whole [real number line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number_line "Real number line"). The flow may for example reach the edge of ![Image 72: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2) in a finite time. In two or three dimensions one can visualize the vector field as giving rise to a [flow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flow_(mathematics) "Flow (mathematics)") on ![Image 73: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2). If we drop a particle into this flow at a point ![Image 74: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) it will move along the curve ![Image 75: {\displaystyle \gamma _{p}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f8f10308dc5371da421a037c991f1c3a3b5d3b72) in the flow depending on the initial point ![Image 76: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36). If ![Image 77: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) is a stationary point of ![Image 78: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) (i.e., the vector field is equal to the zero vector at the point ![Image 79: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36)), then the particle will remain at ![Image 80: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36).

Typical applications are [pathline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Streamlines,_streaklines,_and_pathlines "Streamlines, streaklines, and pathlines") in [fluid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluid_flow "Fluid flow"), [geodesic flow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic_flow "Geodesic flow"), and [one-parameter subgroups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One-parameter_subgroup "One-parameter subgroup") and the [exponential map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponential_map_(Lie_theory) "Exponential map (Lie theory)") in [Lie groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_group "Lie group").

### Complete vector fields

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Vector_field&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: Complete vector fields")]

By definition, a vector field on ![Image 81: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) is called **complete** if each of its flow curves exists for all time.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_field#cite_note-6) In particular, [compactly supported](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compact_support "Compact support") vector fields on a manifold are complete. If ![Image 82: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) is a complete vector field on ![Image 83: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd), then the [one-parameter group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One-parameter_group "One-parameter group") of [diffeomorphisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffeomorphism "Diffeomorphism") generated by the flow along ![Image 84: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) exists for all time; it is described by a smooth mapping

![Image 85: {\displaystyle \mathbf {R} \times M\to M.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cbaf7d9f78e21fcbd0432fd8d7c7b38bc9756f2e)
On a compact manifold without boundary, every smooth vector field is complete. An example of an **incomplete** vector field ![Image 86: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) on the real line ![Image 87: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc) is given by ![Image 88: {\displaystyle V(x)=x^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8a39a6656cf7be1a5316a512378de02c38e43d3a). For, the differential equation ![Image 89: {\textstyle x'(t)=x^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/01649a7cdba43309bd732a878f8f1a0b6e399c27), with initial condition ![Image 90: {\displaystyle x(0)=x_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/20c92f17b65057cd0714e255b61557b6ba31f61b), has as its unique solution ![Image 91: {\textstyle x(t)={\frac {x_{0}}{1-tx_{0}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7e114b1e8060d85276e2b269aedf8715cce0f244) if ![Image 92: {\displaystyle x_{0}\neq 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/30c873d33292c8b8b4f57edbade8876f4894a5a9) (and ![Image 93: {\displaystyle x(t)=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/39d5a44241e71c6e25141d278126efa41a5de3dc) for all ![Image 94: {\displaystyle t\in \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/592bced0c39b10fc90e74c6a66223abfbfb029de) if ![Image 95: {\displaystyle x_{0}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9d18a96da37e1748deeb8d4c590dd4ad6629efef)). Hence for ![Image 96: {\displaystyle x_{0}\neq 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/30c873d33292c8b8b4f57edbade8876f4894a5a9), ![Image 97: {\displaystyle x(t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d54c275db3a1e620737b58e143b0818107fa5f5c) is undefined at ![Image 98: {\textstyle t={\frac {1}{x_{0}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f162bb98d148cdf8908b82acabeb79557f309794) so cannot be defined for all values of ![Image 99: {\displaystyle t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/65658b7b223af9e1acc877d848888ecdb4466560).

The flows associated to two vector fields need not [commute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_property "Commutative property") with each other. Their failure to commute is described by the [Lie bracket](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_bracket_of_vector_fields "Lie bracket of vector fields") of two vector fields, which is again a vector field. The Lie bracket has a simple definition in terms of the action of vector fields on smooth functions ![Image 100: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61):

![Image 101: {\displaystyle [X,Y](f):=X(Y(f))-Y(X(f)).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b6b7aff0e6f237662023631084e44441fab03d83)
Given a [smooth function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smooth_function "Smooth function") between manifolds, ![Image 102: {\displaystyle f:M\to N}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1dbd50e2de9728ee14a7c232441137f588b109f6), the [derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative "Derivative") is an induced map on [tangent bundles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_bundle "Tangent bundle"), ![Image 103: {\displaystyle f_{*}:TM\to TN}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4e65328656819eb7d35be618b249d4e41a7d8819). Given vector fields ![Image 104: {\displaystyle V:M\to TM}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b106611267ae14b545c2e59f43b668852e4a8a5) and ![Image 105: {\displaystyle W:N\to TN}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7ee2ab660c096451bfb970892f072c926a8279a2), we say that ![Image 106: {\displaystyle W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54a9c4c547f4d6111f81946cad242b18298d70b7) is ![Image 107: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61)-related to ![Image 108: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) if the equation ![Image 109: {\displaystyle W\circ f=f_{*}\circ V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2fe18aac870b908a34c871b0ca0570c21ecb9afe) holds.

If ![Image 110: {\displaystyle V_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f300b83673e961a9d48f3862216b167f94e5668c) is ![Image 111: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61)-related to ![Image 112: {\displaystyle W_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7301a4cfd04d4f5db4549fdf23746a0d2ce9f387), ![Image 113: {\displaystyle i=1,2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/608b3c5e448c465889913a88a105e38e7316fba7), then the Lie bracket ![Image 114: {\displaystyle [V_{1},V_{2}]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/51dc17a2963bc6c7ab701aa8dd43720214abe766) is ![Image 115: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61)-related to ![Image 116: {\displaystyle [W_{1},W_{2}]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/420dd448b9831f238dce99132b2aacd13122acae).

Replacing vectors by [_p_-vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/P-vector "P-vector") (_p_ th exterior power of vectors) yields _p_-vector fields; taking the [dual space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_space "Dual space") and exterior powers yields [differential _k_-forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_form "Differential form"), and combining these yields general [tensor fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_field "Tensor field").

Algebraically, vector fields can be characterized as [derivations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivation_(abstract_algebra) "Derivation (abstract algebra)") of the algebra of smooth functions on the manifold, which leads to defining a vector field on a commutative algebra as a derivation on the algebra, which is developed in the theory of [differential calculus over commutative algebras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_calculus_over_commutative_algebras "Differential calculus over commutative algebras").
