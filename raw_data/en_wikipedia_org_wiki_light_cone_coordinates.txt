Motivation [ edit ] A spacetime plane may be associated with the plane of split-complex numbers which is acted upon by elements of the unit hyperbola to effect Lorentz boosts. This number plane has axes corresponding to time and space. An alternative basis is the diagonal basis which corresponds to light-cone coordinates.

Light-cone coordinates in special relativity [ edit ] In a light-cone coordinate system, two of the coordinates are null vectors and all the other coordinates are spatial. The former can be denoted x + {\displaystyle x^{+}} and x − − {\displaystyle x^{-}} and the latter x ⊥ ⊥ {\displaystyle x_{\perp }} .

Assume we are working with a (d,1) Lorentzian signature.

Instead of the standard coordinate system (using Einstein notation ) d s 2 = − − d t 2 + δ δ i j d x i d x j {\displaystyle ds^{2}=-dt^{2}+\delta _{ij}dx^{i}dx^{j}} , with i , j = 1 , … … , d {\displaystyle i,j=1,\dots ,d} we have d s 2 = − − 2 d x + d x − − + δ δ i j d x i d x j {\displaystyle ds^{2}=-2dx^{+}dx^{-}+\delta _{ij}dx^{i}dx^{j}} with i , j = 1 , … … , d − − 1 {\displaystyle i,j=1,\dots ,d-1} , x + = t + x 2 {\displaystyle x^{+}={\frac {t+x}{\sqrt {2}}}} and x − − = t − − x 2 {\displaystyle x^{-}={\frac {t-x}{\sqrt {2}}}} .

Both x + {\displaystyle x^{+}} and x − − {\displaystyle x^{-}} can act as "time" coordinates.

A boost in the ( t , x ) {\displaystyle (t,x)} plane shows up as the squeeze mapping x + → → e + β β x + {\displaystyle x^{+}\to e^{+\beta }x^{+}} , x − − → → e − − β β x − − {\displaystyle x^{-}\to e^{-\beta }x^{-}} , x i → → x i {\displaystyle x^{i}\to x^{i}} . A rotation in the ( i , j ) {\displaystyle (i,j)} -plane only affects x ⊥ ⊥ {\displaystyle x_{\perp }} .

Another set of parabolic transformations show up as x + → → x + + δ δ i j α α i x j + α α 2 2 x − − {\displaystyle x^{+}\to x^{+}+\delta _{ij}\alpha ^{i}x^{j}+{\frac {\alpha ^{2}}{2}}x^{-}} , x − − → → x − − {\displaystyle x^{-}\to x^{-}} and x i → → x i + α α i x − − {\displaystyle x^{i}\to x^{i}+\alpha ^{i}x^{-}} .

Light cone coordinates can also be generalized to curved spacetime in general relativity. Sometimes calculations simplify using light cone coordinates. See Newman–Penrose formalism .
Light cone coordinates are sometimes used to describe relativistic collisions, especially if the relative velocity is very close to the speed of light. They are also used in the light cone gauge of string theory.
