From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

A **photonic metamaterial** (**PM**), also known as an **optical metamaterial**, is a type of [electromagnetic metamaterial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_metamaterial "Electromagnetic metamaterial"), that interacts with light, covering terahertz ([THz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terahertz_radiation "Terahertz radiation")), [infrared](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infrared "Infrared") (IR) or [visible wavelengths](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visible_wavelength "Visible wavelength").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-1) The materials employ a [periodic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Periodic_function "Periodic function"), cellular structure.

The [subwavelength](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subwavelength "Subwavelength") periodicity[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-2) distinguishes photonic metamaterials from [photonic band gap](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_band_gap "Photonic band gap") or [photonic crystal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_crystal "Photonic crystal") structures. The [cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Periodic_function "Periodic function") are on a scale that is magnitudes larger than the atom, yet much smaller than the radiated wavelength,[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-photonic-MM1-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-photonic-MM2-4) are on the order of [nanometers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nanometer "Nanometer").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-photonic-MM1-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-photonic-MM2-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-photonic-mm-evolve-5)

In a conventional material, the response to [electric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric "Electric") and [magnetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic "Magnetic") fields, and hence to [light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light "Light"), is determined by [atoms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dipole "Dipole").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-sunshine-MM-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-MM-review-12-06-7) In metamaterials, cells take the role of atoms in a material that is [homogeneous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homogeneity_(physics) "Homogeneity (physics)") at scales larger than the cells, yielding an _[effective medium model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Effective\_medium\_model "Effective medium model")_.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-photonic-MM1-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-photonic-MM2-4)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-8)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-sunshine-MM-6)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-9)

Some photonic metamaterials exhibit magnetism at high frequencies, resulting in strong magnetic coupling. This can produce a [negative index of refraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Negative_refractive_index "Negative refractive index") in the optical range.

Potential applications include [cloaking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metamaterial_cloaking "Metamaterial cloaking") and [transformation optics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transformation_optics "Transformation optics").[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-3-pt-lecture-10)

Photonic crystals differ from PM in that the size and periodicity of their scattering elements are larger, on the order of the wavelength. Also, a photonic crystal is not [homogeneous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homogeneity_(physics) "Homogeneity (physics)"), so it is not possible to define values of ε ([permittivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permittivity "Permittivity")) or _u_([permeability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permeability_(electromagnetism) "Permeability (electromagnetism)")).[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-PC-discuss-11)

While researching whether or not [matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matter "Matter") interacts with the [magnetic component](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_permeability "Magnetic permeability") of light, [Victor Veselago](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Victor_Veselago "Victor Veselago") (1967) envisioned the possibility of refraction with a negative sign, according to [Maxwell's equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxwell%27s_equations "Maxwell's equations"). A [refractive index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Refractive_index "Refractive index") with a negative sign is the result of [permittivity, ε < 0](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permittivity "Permittivity") (less than zero) and [magnetic permeability, μ < 0](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permeability_(electromagnetism) "Permeability (electromagnetism)") (less than zero).[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-photonic-mm-evolve-5)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-optical-NIM-MM-12) Veselago's analysis has been cited in over 1500 peer-reviewed articles and many books.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-Apps-Capolino-13)[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-reversing-light-14)[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-citations-for-article-15)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-Pos-future-DNG-16)

[![Image 1](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Metarefraction.svg/250px-Metarefraction.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Metarefraction.svg)

A comparison of refraction in a left-handed metamaterial to that in a normal material

In the mid-1990s, metamaterials were first seen as potential technologies for applications such as [nanometer-scale imaging](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subwavelength_imaging "Subwavelength imaging") and [cloaking objects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metamaterial_cloaking "Metamaterial cloaking"). For example, in 1995, Guerra[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-17) fabricated a transparent grating with 50 nm lines and spaces, and then coupled this (what would be later called) photonic metamaterial with an immersion objective to resolve a silicon grating having 50 nm lines and spaces, far beyond the diffraction limit for the 650 nm wavelength illumination in air. And in 2002, Guerra et al.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-18) published their demonstrated use of subwavelength nano-optics (photonic metamaterials) for optical data storage at densities well above the diffraction limit. As of 2015, metamaterial antennas were commercially available.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-19)[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-20)

Negative permeability was achieved with a [split-ring resonator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Split-ring_resonator "Split-ring resonator") (SRR) as part of the subwavelength cell. The SRR achieved negative permeability within a narrow frequency range. This was combined with a [symmetrically](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetrical "Symmetrical") positioned [electric conducting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrical_conductor "Electrical conductor") post, which created the first negative index metamaterial, operating in the microwave band. Experiments and simulations demonstrated the presence of a left-handed propagation band, a left-handed material. The first experimental confirmation of negative [index of refraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Index_of_refraction "Index of refraction") occurred soon after, also at microwave frequencies.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-photonic-mm-evolve-5)[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-Pendry-Essay-21)[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-2001-confirmation-22)

Negative permeability and negative permittivity
-----------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Photonic_metamaterial&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Negative permeability and negative permittivity")]

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/7/79/Photo_of_metamaterial_lattice_for_negative_refraction.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Photo_of_metamaterial_lattice_for_negative_refraction.jpg)

Photograph of the [metamaterial lattice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metamaterial_cloaking "Metamaterial cloaking") used to demonstrate [negative refraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Negative_index_metamaterials "Negative index metamaterials"). The array of square split-ring resonators gives the material a negative magnetic permeability, whereas the array of straight wires gives it a negative permittivity

[Natural materials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_material "Natural material"), such as [precious metals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Precious_metal "Precious metal"), can achieve ε < 0 up to the [visible frequencies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visible_frequencies "Visible frequencies"). However, at [terahertz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terahertz_radiation "Terahertz radiation"), infrared and visible frequencies, natural materials have a very weak magnetic coupling component, or permeability. In other words, susceptibility to the magnetic component of radiated light can be considered negligible.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-optical-NIM-MM-12)

Negative index metamaterials behave contrary to the conventional "right-handed" interaction of light found in conventional optical materials. Hence, these are dubbed [left-handed materials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Left-handed_material "Left-handed material") or [negative index materials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Negative_index_metamaterials "Negative index metamaterials") (NIMs), among other nomenclatures.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-photonic-mm-evolve-5)[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-Pendry-Essay-21)[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-2001-confirmation-22)

Only fabricated NIMs exhibit this capability. Photonic crystals, like many other known systems, can exhibit unusual propagation behavior such as reversal of [phase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phase_velocity "Phase velocity") and [group velocities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_velocities "Group velocities"). However, negative refraction does not occur in these systems.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-Pendry-Essay-21)[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-PRL-V85-2000-23)[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-PIERB-V2-2008-24)

Naturally occurring [ferromagnetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ferromagnetism "Ferromagnetism") and [antiferromagnetic materials](https://archive.today/20130212183609/http://www.dtu.dk/centre/NANOS/English/Research/Nanoparticles%20of%20antiferromagnetic%20materials.aspx) can achieve magnetic resonance, but with significant losses. In natural materials such as [natural magnets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ferrite_(magnet) "Ferrite (magnet)") and [ferrites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allotropes_of_iron "Allotropes of iron"), resonance for the [electric (coupling) response](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permittivity "Permittivity") and [magnetic (coupling) response](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_permeability "Magnetic permeability") do not occur at the same frequency.

Photonic metamaterial SRRs have reached scales below 100 nanometers, using [electron beam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_beam_lithography "Electron beam lithography") and [nanolithography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nanolithography "Nanolithography"). One nanoscale SRR cell has three small metallic rods that are physically connected. This is configured as a U shape and functions as a [nano-inductor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inductor "Inductor"). The gap between the tips of the U-shape function as a [nano-capacitor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capacitor "Capacitor"). Hence, it is an optical [nano-LC resonator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LC_circuit "LC circuit"). These "inclusions" create local [electric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_field "Electric field") and [magnetic fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_field "Magnetic field") when externally excited. These inclusions are usually ten times smaller than the vacuum wavelength of the [light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum "Electromagnetic spectrum") c 0 at the resonant frequency. The inclusions can then be evaluated by using an effective medium approximation.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-photonic-mm-evolve-5)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-Apps-Capolino-13)

PMs display a magnetic response with useful magnitude at optical frequencies. This includes negative permeability, despite the absence of magnetic materials. Analogous to ordinary optical material, PMs can be treated as an effective medium that is characterized by effective medium parameters ε(ω) and μ(ω), or similarly, ε eff and μ eff.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-Apps-Capolino-13)[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-Boltasseva-25)

The negative refractive index of PMs in the optical frequency range was experimentally demonstrated in 2005 by [Shalaev](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shalaev "Shalaev") et al. (at the telecom wavelength λ = 1.5 μm)[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-nanorod-26) and by Brueck et al. (at λ = 2 μm) at nearly the same time.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-Brueck-27)

Effective medium model
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Photonic_metamaterial&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Effective medium model")]

An [effective (transmission) medium approximation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Effective_medium_approximations "Effective medium approximations") describes material slabs that, when reacting to an [external excitation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation "Electromagnetic radiation"), are "effectively" homogeneous, with corresponding "effective" parameters that include "effective" ε and μ and apply to the slab as a whole. Individual inclusions or cells may have values different from the slab.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-Effective-medium-28)[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-effective-approximations-29) However, there are cases where the effective medium approximation does not hold [[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-30)[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-31) and one needs to be aware of its applicability.

Negative magnetic permeability was originally achieved in a left-handed medium at microwave frequencies by using arrays of split-ring resonators.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-32) In most natural materials, the magnetically coupled response starts to taper off at [frequencies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frequencies "Frequencies") in the [gigahertz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gigahertz "Gigahertz") range, which implies that significant magnetism does not occur at optical frequencies. The effective permeability of such materials is unity, μ eff = 1. Hence, the magnetic component of a radiated [electromagnetic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_field "Electromagnetic field") has virtually no effect on natural occurring materials at optical frequencies.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-Nanofabricated-media-33)

In metamaterials the cell acts as a meta-atom, a [larger scale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superposition_principle "Superposition principle") magnetic [dipole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dipole "Dipole"), analogous to the [picometer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Picometer "Picometer")-sized atom. For meta-atoms constructed from [gold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gold "Gold"), μ < 0 can be achieved at [telecommunication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Telecommunication "Telecommunication") frequencies but not at visible frequencies. The visible frequency has been elusive because the [plasma frequency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasma_frequency "Plasma frequency") of metals is the ultimate limiting condition.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-MM-review-12-06-7)

Design and fabrication
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Photonic_metamaterial&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Design and fabrication")]

Optical wavelengths are much shorter than microwaves, making subwavelength optical metamaterials more difficult to realize. Microwave metamaterials can be fabricated from [circuit board](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circuit_board "Circuit board") materials, while [lithography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nanolithography "Nanolithography") techniques must be employed to produce PMs.

Successful experiments used a periodic arrangement of short wires or metallic pieces with varied shapes. In a different study the whole slab was electrically connected.

Fabrication techniques include [electron beam lithography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_beam_lithography "Electron beam lithography"), nanostructuring with a [focused ion beam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Focused_ion_beam "Focused ion beam") and [interference lithography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interference_lithography "Interference lithography").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-Apps-Capolino-13)[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-fib-34)[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-fib2-35)[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-ion_beam_implantation-36)

In 2014 a [polarization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polarization_(waves) "Polarization (waves)")-insensitive metamaterial prototype was demonstrated to absorb energy over a broad band (a [super-octave](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Super-octave&action=edit&redlink=1 "Super-octave (page does not exist)")) of infrared wavelengths. The material displayed greater than 98% measured average absorptivity that it maintained over a wide ±45° field-of-view for mid-infrared wavelengths between 1.77 and 4.81 μm. One use is to conceal objects from infrared sensors. [Palladium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palladium "Palladium") provided greater bandwidth than silver or gold. A [genetic algorithm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genetic_algorithm "Genetic algorithm") randomly modified an initial candidate pattern, testing and eliminating all but the best. The process was repeated over multiple generations until the design became effective.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-37)[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-kurz14-38)

The metamaterial is made of four layers on a silicon substrate. The first layer is palladium, covered by [polyimide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyimide "Polyimide") (plastic) and a palladium screen on top. The screen has sub-wavelength cutouts that block the various wavelengths. A polyimide layer caps the whole absorber. It can absorb 90 percent of infrared radiation at up to a 55 degree angle to the screen. The layers do not need accurate alignment. The polyimide cap protects the screen and helps reduce any impedance mismatch that might occur when the wave crosses from the air into the device.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-kurz14-38)

### One-way transmission

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Photonic_metamaterial&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: One-way transmission")]

In 2015 visible light joined microwave and infrared NIMs in propagating light in only one direction. ("[mirrors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mirrors "Mirrors")" instead reduce light transmission in the reverse direction, requiring low light levels behind the mirror to work.)[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-mirror-39)

The material combined two optical nanostructures: a multi-layered block of alternating silver and glass sheets and metal grates. The silver-glass structure is a "hyperbolic" metamaterial, which treats light differently depending on which direction the waves are traveling. Each layer is tens of nanometers thick—much thinner than visible light's 400 to 700 nm wavelengths, making the block opaque to visible light, although light entering at certain angles can propagate inside the material.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-mirror-39)

Adding [chromium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chromium "Chromium") grates with sub-wavelength spacings bent incoming red or green light waves enough that they could enter and propagate inside the block. On the opposite side of the block, another set of grates allowed light to exit, angled away from its original direction. The spacing of the exit grates was different from that of the entrance grates, bending incident light so that external light could not enter the block from that side. Around 30 times more light passed through in the forward direction than in reverse. The intervening blocks reduced the need for precise alignment of the two grates with respect to each other.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-mirror-39)

Such structures hold potential for applications in optical communication—for instance, they could be integrated into photonic computer chips that split or combine signals carried by light waves. Other potential applications include biosensing using nanoscale particles to deflect light to angles steep enough to travel through the hyperbolic material and out the other side.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-mirror-39)

### Lumped circuit elements

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Photonic_metamaterial&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Lumped circuit elements")]

By employing a combination of [plasmonic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasmon "Plasmon") and non-plasmonic [nanoparticles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nanoparticle "Nanoparticle"), lumped circuit element nanocircuits at infrared and optical frequencies appear to be possible. Conventional lumped circuit elements are not available in a conventional way.[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-nano-optical-element-40)

Subwavelength [lumped circuit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lumped_element_model "Lumped element model") elements proved workable in the [microwave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microwave "Microwave") and [radio frequency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radio_frequency "Radio frequency") (RF) domain. The lumped element concept allowed for element simplification and circuit modularization. [Nanoscale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nanoscopic_scale "Nanoscopic scale") fabrication techniques exist to accomplish subwavelength geometries.[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-nano-optical-element-40)

Metals such as [gold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gold "Gold"), [silver](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silver "Silver"), [aluminum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aluminum "Aluminum") and [copper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Copper "Copper") conduct [currents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_current "Electric current") at RF and microwave frequencies. At optical frequencies characteristics of some noble metals are altered. Rather than normal current flow, [plasmonic resonances](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_plasmon_resonance "Surface plasmon resonance") occur as the real part of the [complex permittivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_permittivity "Complex permittivity") becomes negative. Therefore, the [main current flow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_current "Electric current") is actually the electric displacement [current density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Current_density "Current density") ∂D / ∂t, and can be termed as the “flowing optical current".[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-nano-optical-element-40)

At subwavelength scales the cell's [impedance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wave_impedance "Wave impedance") becomes dependent on [shape, size](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry"), material and the optical frequency illumination. The particle's orientation with the optical electric field may also help determine the impedance. Conventional [silicon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon "Silicon") dielectrics have the real permittivity component ε real> 0 at optical frequencies, causing the nanoparticle to act as a [capacitive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capacitive "Capacitive") impedance, a nanocapacitor. Conversely, if the material is a [noble metal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noble_metal "Noble metal") such as gold or silver, with ε real< 0, then it takes on [inductive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_induction "Electromagnetic induction") characteristics, becoming a nanoinductor. Material loss is represented as a nano-resistor.[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-nano-optical-element-40)[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-PRL-95-095504-2005-41)

The most commonly applied scheme to achieve a tunable index of refraction is electro-optical tuning. Here the change in refractive index is proportional to either the applied electric field, or is proportional to the [square modulus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_modulus "Square modulus") of the electric field. These are the [Pockels effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pockels_effect "Pockels effect") and [Kerr effects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kerr_effect "Kerr effect"), respectively.

An alternative is to employ a nonlinear optical material and depend on the optical field intensity to modify the refractive index or magnetic parameters.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-APL-91-2007-42)

Stacking layers produces NIMs at optical frequencies. However, the surface configuration (non-planar, bulk) of the SRR normally prevents stacking. Although a single-layer SRR structure can be constructed on a [dielectric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dielectric "Dielectric") surface, it is relatively difficult to stack these bulk structures due to alignment tolerance requirements.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-photonic-mm-evolve-5) A stacking technique for SRRs was published in 2007 that uses dielectric spacers to apply a planarization procedure to flatten the SRR layer.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-3D-PH-MM-optical-f-43) It appears that arbitrary many layers can be made this way, including any chosen number of unit cells and variant spatial arrangements of individual layers.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-photonic-mm-evolve-5)[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-3D-PH-MM-optical-f-43)[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-3D-fishnet-44)

In 2014 researchers announced a 400 nanometer thick frequency-doubling non-linear mirror that can be tuned to work at near-infrared to mid-infrared to terahertz frequencies. The material operates with much lower intensity light than traditional approaches. For a given input light intensity and structure thickness, the metamaterial produced approximately one million times higher intensity output. The mirrors do not require matching the [phase velocities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phase_velocity "Phase velocity") of the input and output waves.[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-nonl-45)

It can produce giant nonlinear response for multiple [nonlinear optical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nonlinear_optics "Nonlinear optics") processes, such as second harmonic, sum- and difference-frequency generation, as well a variety of four-wave mixing processes. The demonstration device converted light with a wavelength of 8000 to 4000 nanometers.[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-nonl-45)

The device is made of a stack of thin layers of [indium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indium "Indium"), [gallium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gallium "Gallium") and [arsenic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arsenic "Arsenic") or [aluminum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aluminum "Aluminum"), indium and arsenic. 100 of these layers, each between one and twelve nanometers thick, were faced on top by a pattern of asymmetrical, crossed gold nanostructures that form coupled [quantum wells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_well "Quantum well") and a layer of gold on the bottom.[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-nonl-45)

Potential applications include remote sensing and medical applications that call for compact laser systems.[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-nonl-45)

[Dyakonov surface waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dyakonov_surface_waves "Dyakonov surface waves")[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-DSW-46)[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-47)[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-48)[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-49)[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-50)[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-51)[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-52) (DSW) relate to [birefringence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Birefringence "Birefringence") related to photonic crystals, metamaterial anisotropy.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-surface-waves-53) Recently photonic metamaterial operated at 780 nanometer (near-infrared),[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-first-infrared-54)[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-780nm-55)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-optical-NIM-MM-12) 813 nm and 772 nm.[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-dual-NRI-56)[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photonic_metamaterial#cite_note-phase-engineer-57)
