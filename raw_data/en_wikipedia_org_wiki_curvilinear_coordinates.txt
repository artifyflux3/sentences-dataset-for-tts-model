**Curvilinear** (top), [**affine**](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_coordinate_system "Affine coordinate system") (right), and [**Cartesian**](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system "Cartesian coordinate system") (left) coordinates in two-dimensional space

In [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry"), **curvilinear coordinates** are a [coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_system "Coordinate system") for [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space") in which the [coordinate lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_line "Coordinate line") may be curved. These coordinates may be derived from a set of [Cartesian coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate "Cartesian coordinate") by using a transformation that is [locally invertible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invertible "Invertible") (a one-to-one map) at each point. This means that one can convert a point given in a Cartesian coordinate system to its curvilinear coordinates and back. The name _curvilinear coordinates_, coined by the French mathematician [Lamé](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gabriel_Lam%C3%A9 "Gabriel Lamé"), derives from the fact that the [coordinate surfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_surfaces "Coordinate surfaces") of the curvilinear systems are curved.

Well-known examples of curvilinear coordinate systems in three-dimensional Euclidean space (**R**3) are [cylindrical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylindrical_coordinate_system "Cylindrical coordinate system") and [spherical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_coordinates "Spherical coordinates") coordinates. A Cartesian coordinate surface in this space is a [coordinate plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_plane "Coordinate plane"); for example _z_ = 0 defines the _x_-_y_ plane. In the same space, the coordinate surface _r_ = 1 in spherical coordinates is the surface of a unit [sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere "Sphere"), which is curved. The formalism of curvilinear coordinates provides a unified and general description of the standard coordinate systems.

Curvilinear coordinates are often used to define the location or distribution of physical quantities which may be, for example, [scalars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_(mathematics) "Scalar (mathematics)"), [vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_(geometric) "Vector (geometric)"), or [tensors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor "Tensor"). Mathematical expressions involving these quantities in [vector calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_calculus "Vector calculus") and [tensor analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_analysis "Tensor analysis") (such as the [gradient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gradient "Gradient"), [divergence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divergence "Divergence"), [curl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curl_(mathematics) "Curl (mathematics)"), and [Laplacian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laplacian "Laplacian")) can be transformed from one coordinate system to another, according to transformation rules for scalars, vectors, and tensors. Such expressions then become valid for any curvilinear coordinate system.

A curvilinear coordinate system may be simpler to use than the Cartesian coordinate system for some applications. The motion of particles under the influence of [central forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_force "Central force") is usually easier to solve in [spherical coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_coordinates "Spherical coordinates") than in Cartesian coordinates; this is true of many physical problems with [spherical symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circular_symmetry "Circular symmetry") defined in **R**3. Equations with [boundary conditions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boundary_conditions "Boundary conditions") that follow coordinate surfaces for a particular curvilinear coordinate system may be easier to solve in that system. While one might describe the motion of a particle in a rectangular box using Cartesian coordinates, it is easier to describe the motion in a sphere with spherical coordinates. Spherical coordinates are the most common curvilinear coordinate systems and are used in [Earth sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth_sciences "Earth sciences"), [cartography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartography "Cartography"), [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics"), [relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theory_of_relativity "Theory of relativity"), and [engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineering "Engineering").

### Coordinates, basis, and vectors

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Curvilinear_coordinates&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Coordinates, basis, and vectors")]

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/General_curvilinear_coordinates_1.svg/500px-General_curvilinear_coordinates_1.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:General_curvilinear_coordinates_1.svg)

Fig. 1 - Coordinate surfaces, coordinate lines, and coordinate axes of general curvilinear coordinates.

[![Image 3](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Spherical_coordinate_elements.svg/500px-Spherical_coordinate_elements.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Spherical_coordinate_elements.svg)

Fig. 2 - Coordinate surfaces, coordinate lines, and coordinate axes of spherical coordinates. **Surfaces:**_r_ - spheres, θ - cones, Φ - half-planes; **Lines:**_r_ - straight beams, θ - vertical semicircles, Φ - horizontal circles; **Axes:**_r_ - straight beams, θ - tangents to vertical semicircles, Φ - tangents to horizontal circles

For now, consider [3-D space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_space "Three-dimensional space"). A point _P_ in 3-D space (or its [position vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Position_vector "Position vector")**r**) can be defined using Cartesian coordinates (_x_, _y_, _z_) [equivalently written (_x_ 1, _x_ 2, _x_ 3)], by ![Image 4: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} =x\mathbf {e} _{x}+y\mathbf {e} _{y}+z\mathbf {e} _{z}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1756983b1f93e4697ae0fcdae87708e011ec13e1), where **e**_x_, **e**_y_, **e**_z_ are the _[standard basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard\_basis "Standard basis") vectors_.

It can also be defined by its **curvilinear coordinates** (_q_ 1, _q_ 2, _q_ 3) if this triplet of numbers defines a single point in an unambiguous way. The relation between the coordinates is then given by the invertible transformation functions:

![Image 5: {\displaystyle x=f^{1}(q^{1},q^{2},q^{3}),\,y=f^{2}(q^{1},q^{2},q^{3}),\,z=f^{3}(q^{1},q^{2},q^{3})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a017c2e2c7921d5f5d787f40b1345562b0f0f50b)![Image 6: {\displaystyle q^{1}=g^{1}(x,y,z),\,q^{2}=g^{2}(x,y,z),\,q^{3}=g^{3}(x,y,z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2092771bd3a74552a677754e8c9b0cb505b34e94)
The surfaces _q_ 1 = constant, _q_ 2 = constant, _q_ 3 = constant are called the **coordinate surfaces**; and the space curves formed by their intersection in pairs are called the **[coordinate curves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_curves "Coordinate curves")**. The **coordinate axes** are determined by the [tangents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent "Tangent") to the coordinate curves at the intersection of three surfaces. They are not in general fixed directions in space, which happens to be the case for simple Cartesian coordinates, and thus there is generally no natural global basis for curvilinear coordinates.

In the Cartesian system, the standard basis vectors can be derived from the derivative of the location of point _P_ with respect to the local coordinate

![Image 7: {\displaystyle \mathbf {e} _{x}={\dfrac {\partial \mathbf {r} }{\partial x}};\;\mathbf {e} _{y}={\dfrac {\partial \mathbf {r} }{\partial y}};\;\mathbf {e} _{z}={\dfrac {\partial \mathbf {r} }{\partial z}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a1576a4810799a9392ce0ec5b48a7d4b0f7af785)
Applying the same derivatives to the curvilinear system locally at point _P_ defines the natural basis vectors:

![Image 8: {\displaystyle \mathbf {h} _{1}={\dfrac {\partial \mathbf {r} }{\partial q^{1}}};\;\mathbf {h} _{2}={\dfrac {\partial \mathbf {r} }{\partial q^{2}}};\;\mathbf {h} _{3}={\dfrac {\partial \mathbf {r} }{\partial q^{3}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/993e79edc4450f7ab8e8f837043067dc8c214fac)
Such a basis, whose vectors change their direction and/or magnitude from point to point is called a **local basis**. All bases associated with curvilinear coordinates are necessarily local. Basis vectors that are the same at all points are **global bases**, and can be associated only with linear or [affine coordinate systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_coordinate_system "Affine coordinate system").

For this article **e** is reserved for the [standard basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_basis "Standard basis") (Cartesian) and **h** or **b** is for the curvilinear basis.

These may not have unit length, and may also not be orthogonal. In the case that they _are_ orthogonal at all points where the derivatives are well-defined, we define the **[Lamé coefficients](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvilinear_coordinates#Relation_to_Lam%C3%A9_coefficients)** (after [Gabriel Lamé](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gabriel_Lam%C3%A9 "Gabriel Lamé")) by

![Image 9: {\displaystyle h_{1}=|\mathbf {h} _{1}|;\;h_{2}=|\mathbf {h} _{2}|;\;h_{3}=|\mathbf {h} _{3}|}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cbbd58321b9944bdf1e3181c9850da35b9a49e57)
and the curvilinear [orthonormal basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthonormal_basis "Orthonormal basis") vectors by

![Image 10: {\displaystyle \mathbf {b} _{1}={\dfrac {\mathbf {h} _{1}}{h_{1}}};\;\mathbf {b} _{2}={\dfrac {\mathbf {h} _{2}}{h_{2}}};\;\mathbf {b} _{3}={\dfrac {\mathbf {h} _{3}}{h_{3}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/df173ccb9e819b189bbe875f09728b11fa805d26)
These basis vectors may well depend upon the position of _P_; it is therefore necessary that they are not assumed to be constant over a region. (They technically form a basis for the [tangent bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_bundle "Tangent bundle") of ![Image 11: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5) at _P_, and so are local to _P_.)

In general, curvilinear coordinates allow the natural basis vectors **h**i not all mutually perpendicular to each other, and not required to be of unit length: they can be of arbitrary magnitude and direction. The use of an orthogonal basis makes vector manipulations simpler than for non-orthogonal. However, some areas of [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics") and [engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineering "Engineering"), particularly [fluid mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluid_mechanics "Fluid mechanics") and [continuum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuum_mechanics "Continuum mechanics"), require non-orthogonal bases to describe deformations and fluid transport to account for complicated directional dependences of physical quantities. A discussion of the general case appears later on this page.

### Differential elements

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Curvilinear_coordinates&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Differential elements")]

In orthogonal curvilinear coordinates, since the [total differential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Total_differential "Total differential") change in **r** is

![Image 12: {\displaystyle d\mathbf {r} ={\dfrac {\partial \mathbf {r} }{\partial q^{1}}}dq^{1}+{\dfrac {\partial \mathbf {r} }{\partial q^{2}}}dq^{2}+{\dfrac {\partial \mathbf {r} }{\partial q^{3}}}dq^{3}=h_{1}dq^{1}\mathbf {b} _{1}+h_{2}dq^{2}\mathbf {b} _{2}+h_{3}dq^{3}\mathbf {b} _{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7b57164fed127383fb7e1c18e8e221ae79d11324)
so scale factors are ![Image 13: {\displaystyle h_{i}=\left|{\frac {\partial \mathbf {r} }{\partial q^{i}}}\right|}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3095d7770d37f8598a7183789f1e033719e89040)

In non-orthogonal coordinates the length of ![Image 14: {\displaystyle d\mathbf {r} =dq^{1}\mathbf {h} _{1}+dq^{2}\mathbf {h} _{2}+dq^{3}\mathbf {h} _{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dc3f46ddcd07d8e155d8b2230a92f485cd9e99ca) is the positive square root of ![Image 15: {\displaystyle d\mathbf {r} \cdot d\mathbf {r} =dq^{i}dq^{j}\mathbf {h} _{i}\cdot \mathbf {h} _{j}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f3f472b8b787251b30516f1a114726205efb800d) (with [Einstein summation convention](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_summation_convention "Einstein summation convention")). The six independent scalar products _g ij_=**h**_i_.**h**_j_ of the natural basis vectors generalize the three scale factors defined above for orthogonal coordinates. The nine _g ij_ are the components of the [metric tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_tensor "Metric tensor"), which has only three non zero components in orthogonal coordinates: _g_ 11=_h_ 1 _h_ 1, _g_ 22=_h_ 2 _h_ 2, _g_ 33=_h_ 3 _h_ 3.

Covariant and contravariant bases
---------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Curvilinear_coordinates&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Covariant and contravariant bases")]

[![Image 16](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Vector_1-form.svg/500px-Vector_1-form.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Vector_1-form.svg)

 A vector **v** (**red**) represented by • a vector basis (**yellow, left:****e**1, **e**2, **e**3), tangent vectors to coordinate curves (**black**) and • a covector basis or cobasis (**blue, right:****e**1, **e**2, **e**3), normal vectors to coordinate surfaces (**grey**) in _general_ (not necessarily [orthogonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_coordinates "Orthogonal coordinates")) curvilinear coordinates (_q_ 1, _q_ 2, _q_ 3). The basis and cobasis do not coincide unless the coordinate system is orthogonal.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvilinear_coordinates#cite_note-1)

Spatial gradients, distances, time derivatives and scale factors are interrelated within a coordinate system by two groups of basis vectors:

1.   basis vectors that are locally tangent to their associated coordinate pathline: ![Image 17: {\displaystyle \mathbf {b} _{i}={\dfrac {\partial \mathbf {r} }{\partial q^{i}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f4d452ccd0ee21125547fb52f374cc8b27dbdd5f) are [contravariant vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covariance_and_contravariance_of_vectors "Covariance and contravariance of vectors") (denoted by lowered indices), and
2.   basis vectors that are locally normal to the isosurface created by the other coordinates: ![Image 18: {\displaystyle \mathbf {b} ^{i}=\nabla q^{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/42169bdfc6685c368603fc1c6eaf383102d8bd72) are [covariant vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covariance_and_contravariance_of_vectors "Covariance and contravariance of vectors") (denoted by raised indices), ∇ is the [del](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Del "Del")[operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_operator "Linear operator").

Note that, because of Einstein's summation convention, the position of the indices of the vectors is the opposite of that of the coordinates.

Consequently, a general curvilinear coordinate system has two sets of basis vectors for every point: {**b**1, **b**2, **b**3} is the contravariant basis, and {**b**1, **b**2, **b**3} is the covariant (a.k.a. reciprocal) basis. The covariant and contravariant basis vectors types have identical direction for orthogonal curvilinear coordinate systems, but as usual have inverted units with respect to each other.

Note the following important equality: ![Image 19: {\displaystyle \mathbf {b} ^{i}\cdot \mathbf {b} _{j}=\delta _{j}^{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/11fb43b44f089c4155a13074bb6ffac70ceb8046) wherein ![Image 20: {\displaystyle \delta _{j}^{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c2c2996dd3d4c7152c99cf5491fd3de9f01b4f09) denotes the [generalized Kronecker delta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kronecker_delta#Generalizations_of_the_Kronecker_delta "Kronecker delta").

**Proof**

In the Cartesian coordinate system ![Image 21: {\displaystyle (\mathbf {e} _{x},\mathbf {e} _{y},\mathbf {e} _{z})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d451d416ab9f530405a021f6e929000e8eb86089), we can write the dot product as:

![Image 22: {\displaystyle \mathbf {b} _{i}\cdot \mathbf {b} ^{j}=\left({\dfrac {\partial x}{\partial q_{i}}},{\dfrac {\partial y}{\partial q_{i}}},{\dfrac {\partial z}{\partial q_{i}}}\right)\cdot \left({\dfrac {\partial q_{j}}{\partial x}},{\dfrac {\partial q_{j}}{\partial y}},{\dfrac {\partial q_{j}}{\partial z}}\right)={\dfrac {\partial x}{\partial q_{i}}}{\dfrac {\partial q_{j}}{\partial x}}+{\dfrac {\partial y}{\partial q_{i}}}{\dfrac {\partial q_{j}}{\partial y}}+{\dfrac {\partial z}{\partial q_{i}}}{\dfrac {\partial q_{j}}{\partial z}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4fdffa7627c5a6a5bc8851136dca7bde47e4ecbc)
Consider an infinitesimal displacement ![Image 23: {\displaystyle d\mathbf {r} =dx\cdot \mathbf {e} _{x}+dy\cdot \mathbf {e} _{y}+dz\cdot \mathbf {e} _{z}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6dfc36792a514c8ba3117561723ecb1e89d91ec3). Let dq 1, dq 2 and dq 3 denote the corresponding infinitesimal changes in curvilinear coordinates q 1, q 2 and q 3 respectively.

By the chain rule, dq 1 can be expressed as:

![Image 24: {\displaystyle dq_{1}={\dfrac {\partial q_{1}}{\partial x}}dx+{\dfrac {\partial q_{1}}{\partial y}}dy+{\dfrac {\partial q_{1}}{\partial z}}dz={\dfrac {\partial q_{1}}{\partial x}}dx+{\dfrac {\partial q_{1}}{\partial y}}\left({\dfrac {\partial y}{\partial q_{1}}}dq_{1}+{\dfrac {\partial y}{\partial q_{2}}}dq_{2}+{\dfrac {\partial y}{\partial q_{3}}}dq_{3}\right)+{\dfrac {\partial q_{1}}{\partial z}}\left({\dfrac {\partial z}{\partial q_{1}}}dq_{1}+{\dfrac {\partial z}{\partial q_{2}}}dq_{2}+{\dfrac {\partial z}{\partial q_{3}}}dq_{3}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1a89e3830c31ddee3190b9006529715fbb0299cb)
If the displacement _d**r**_ is such that _dq_ 2 = _dq_ 3 = 0, i.e. the position vector **r** moves by an infinitesimal amount along the coordinate axis _q_ 2=const and _q_ 3=const, then:

![Image 25: {\displaystyle dq_{1}={\dfrac {\partial q_{1}}{\partial x}}dx+{\dfrac {\partial q_{1}}{\partial y}}{\dfrac {\partial y}{\partial q_{1}}}dq_{1}+{\dfrac {\partial q_{1}}{\partial z}}{\dfrac {\partial z}{\partial q_{1}}}dq_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47afac13b5812d25d4140f42c6d186f20ecb04b9)
Dividing by _dq_ 1, and taking the limit _dq_ 1 → 0:

![Image 26: {\displaystyle 1={\dfrac {\partial q_{1}}{\partial x}}{\dfrac {\partial x}{\partial q_{1}}}+{\dfrac {\partial q_{1}}{\partial y}}{\dfrac {\partial y}{\partial q_{1}}}+{\dfrac {\partial q_{1}}{\partial z}}{\dfrac {\partial z}{\partial q_{1}}}={\dfrac {\partial x}{\partial q_{1}}}{\dfrac {\partial q_{1}}{\partial x}}+{\dfrac {\partial y}{\partial q_{1}}}{\dfrac {\partial q_{1}}{\partial y}}+{\dfrac {\partial z}{\partial q_{1}}}{\dfrac {\partial q_{1}}{\partial z}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5a97c3f549c2a362cda7db6ebc2a799b21e2403e)
or equivalently:

![Image 27: {\displaystyle \mathbf {b} _{1}\cdot \mathbf {b} ^{1}=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eccd4113cc50c17433cc560dd8e53d924c15c54c)
Now if the displacement d**r** is such that _dq_ 1=_dq_ 3=0, i.e. the position vector **r** moves by an infinitesimal amount along the coordinate axis q 1=const and q 3=const, then:

![Image 28: {\displaystyle 0={\dfrac {\partial q_{1}}{\partial x}}dx+{\dfrac {\partial q_{1}}{\partial y}}{\dfrac {\partial y}{\partial q_{2}}}dq_{2}+{\dfrac {\partial q_{1}}{\partial z}}{\dfrac {\partial z}{\partial q_{2}}}dq_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7e960a5d264c8f12b63ec17246ceccfb1dad4f25)
Dividing by dq 2, and taking the limit dq 2 → 0:

![Image 29: {\displaystyle 0={\dfrac {\partial q_{1}}{\partial x}}{\dfrac {\partial x}{\partial q_{2}}}+{\dfrac {\partial q_{1}}{\partial y}}{\dfrac {\partial y}{\partial q_{2}}}+{\dfrac {\partial q_{1}}{\partial z}}{\dfrac {\partial z}{\partial q_{2}}}={\dfrac {\partial x}{\partial q_{2}}}{\dfrac {\partial q_{1}}{\partial x}}+{\dfrac {\partial y}{\partial q_{2}}}{\dfrac {\partial q_{1}}{\partial y}}+{\dfrac {\partial z}{\partial q_{2}}}{\dfrac {\partial q_{1}}{\partial z}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cf28da07ed95e6eb8decd2971ebee2a83bf545ca)
or equivalently:

![Image 30: {\displaystyle \mathbf {b} _{2}\cdot \mathbf {b} ^{1}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b78fac24db92262d2617e89f117b91af70783249)
And so forth for the other dot products.

**Alternative Proof:**

![Image 31: {\displaystyle \delta _{j}^{i}dq^{j}=dq^{i}=\nabla q^{i}\cdot d\mathbf {r} =\mathbf {b} ^{i}\cdot {\dfrac {\partial \mathbf {r} }{\partial q^{j}}}dq^{j}=\mathbf {b} ^{i}\cdot \mathbf {b} _{j}dq^{j}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bb6a08d51274430a7d34c2e5e9897facc273a271)
and the [Einstein summation convention](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_summation_convention "Einstein summation convention") is implied.

A vector **v** can be specified in terms of either basis, i.e.,

![Image 32: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} =v^{1}\mathbf {b} _{1}+v^{2}\mathbf {b} _{2}+v^{3}\mathbf {b} _{3}=v_{1}\mathbf {b} ^{1}+v_{2}\mathbf {b} ^{2}+v_{3}\mathbf {b} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b9c4016070901d1c94c02c86ecd0441edbd1abdb)
Using the Einstein summation convention, the basis vectors relate to the components by[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvilinear_coordinates#cite_note-Simmonds-2): 30–32

![Image 33: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} \cdot \mathbf {b} ^{i}=v^{k}\mathbf {b} _{k}\cdot \mathbf {b} ^{i}=v^{k}\delta _{k}^{i}=v^{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3fd7ecb597f9f6199e77ee6206079f82e7a11c74)![Image 34: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} \cdot \mathbf {b} _{i}=v_{k}\mathbf {b} ^{k}\cdot \mathbf {b} _{i}=v_{k}\delta _{i}^{k}=v_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/253af9e2f92db8ec9c31a7ece3d9ec96e3ad064c)
and

![Image 35: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} \cdot \mathbf {b} _{i}=v^{k}\mathbf {b} _{k}\cdot \mathbf {b} _{i}=g_{ki}v^{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/275240060c8bb003f53e4ed62ad039d7c77a780d)![Image 36: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} \cdot \mathbf {b} ^{i}=v_{k}\mathbf {b} ^{k}\cdot \mathbf {b} ^{i}=g^{ki}v_{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ecce65b8c8b3e2664bdf2612a517208b125b904c)
where _g_ is the metric tensor (see below).

A vector can be specified with covariant coordinates (lowered indices, written _v k_) or contravariant coordinates (raised indices, written _v k_). From the above vector sums, it can be seen that contravariant coordinates are associated with covariant basis vectors, and covariant coordinates are associated with contravariant basis vectors.

A key feature of the representation of vectors and tensors in terms of indexed components and basis vectors is _invariance_ in the sense that vector components which transform in a covariant manner (or contravariant manner) are paired with basis vectors that transform in a contravariant manner (or covariant manner).

### Constructing a covariant basis in one dimension

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Curvilinear_coordinates&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Constructing a covariant basis in one dimension")]

[![Image 37](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Local_basis_transformation.svg/340px-Local_basis_transformation.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Local_basis_transformation.svg)

Fig. 3 – Transformation of local covariant basis in the case of general curvilinear coordinates

Consider the one-dimensional curve shown in Fig. 3. At point _P_, taken as an [origin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Origin_(mathematics) "Origin (mathematics)"), _x_ is one of the Cartesian coordinates, and _q_ 1 is one of the curvilinear coordinates. The local (non-unit) basis vector is **b**1 (notated **h**1 above, with **b** reserved for unit vectors) and it is built on the _q_ 1 axis which is a tangent to that coordinate line at the point _P_. The axis _q_ 1 and thus the vector **b**1 form an angle ![Image 38: {\displaystyle \alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b79333175c8b3f0840bfb4ec41b8072c83ea88d3) with the Cartesian _x_ axis and the Cartesian basis vector **e**1.

It can be seen from triangle _PAB_ that

![Image 39: {\displaystyle \cos \alpha ={\cfrac {|\mathbf {e} _{1}|}{|\mathbf {b} _{1}|}}\quad \Rightarrow \quad |\mathbf {e} _{1}|=|\mathbf {b} _{1}|\cos \alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3b8e1a98531a4673bc7494ee78c67ea14684a441)
where |**e**1|, |**b**1| are the magnitudes of the two basis vectors, i.e., the scalar intercepts _PB_ and _PA_. _PA_ is also the projection of **b**1 on the _x_ axis.

However, this method for basis vector transformations using _directional cosines_ is inapplicable to curvilinear coordinates for the following reasons:

1.   By increasing the distance from _P_, the angle between the curved line _q_ 1 and Cartesian axis _x_ increasingly deviates from ![Image 40: {\displaystyle \alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b79333175c8b3f0840bfb4ec41b8072c83ea88d3).
2.   At the distance _PB_ the true angle is that which the tangent **at point C** forms with the _x_ axis and the latter angle is clearly different from ![Image 41: {\displaystyle \alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b79333175c8b3f0840bfb4ec41b8072c83ea88d3).

The angles that the _q_ 1 line and that axis form with the _x_ axis become closer in value the closer one moves towards point _P_ and become exactly equal at _P_.

Let point _E_ be located very close to _P_, so close that the distance _PE_ is infinitesimally small. Then _PE_ measured on the _q_ 1 axis almost coincides with _PE_ measured on the _q_ 1 line. At the same time, the ratio _PD/PE_ (_PD_ being the projection of _PE_ on the _x_ axis) becomes almost exactly equal to ![Image 42: {\displaystyle \cos \alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/277355f4e11bfdd8864cdb3eada54c1f9f736d0b).

Let the infinitesimally small intercepts _PD_ and _PE_ be labelled, respectively, as _dx_ and d _q_ 1. Then

![Image 43: {\displaystyle \cos \alpha ={\cfrac {dx}{dq^{1}}}={\frac {|\mathbf {e} _{1}|}{|\mathbf {b} _{1}|}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/deb62265652f39ef459fcd268917cf3e93445026).
Thus, the directional cosines can be substituted in transformations with the more exact ratios between infinitesimally small coordinate intercepts. It follows that the component (projection) of **b**1 on the _x_ axis is

![Image 44: {\displaystyle p^{1}=\mathbf {b} _{1}\cdot {\cfrac {\mathbf {e} _{1}}{|\mathbf {e} _{1}|}}=|\mathbf {b} _{1}|{\cfrac {|\mathbf {e} _{1}|}{|\mathbf {e} _{1}|}}\cos \alpha =|\mathbf {b} _{1}|{\cfrac {dx}{dq^{1}}}\quad \Rightarrow \quad {\cfrac {p^{1}}{|\mathbf {b} _{1}|}}={\cfrac {dx}{dq^{1}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e50c5f2e0fe54b10e293688be66687e909a83ae3).
If _q i_ = _q i_(_x_ 1, _x_ 2, _x_ 3) and _x i_ = _x i_(_q_ 1, _q_ 2, _q_ 3) are [smooth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smooth_function "Smooth function") (continuously differentiable) functions the transformation ratios can be written as ![Image 45: {\displaystyle {\cfrac {\partial q^{i}}{\partial x_{j}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/56591c653fcc2b62e2ef2208eadc88b7f62fa447) and ![Image 46: {\displaystyle {\cfrac {\partial x_{i}}{\partial q^{j}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4f56a1c692c3c88e0eeb2187d1ae83d6d3d8cf66). That is, those ratios are [partial derivatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_derivative "Partial derivative") of coordinates belonging to one system with respect to coordinates belonging to the other system.

### Constructing a covariant basis in three dimensions

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Curvilinear_coordinates&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Constructing a covariant basis in three dimensions")]

Doing the same for the coordinates in the other 2 dimensions, **b**1 can be expressed as:

![Image 47: {\displaystyle \mathbf {b} _{1}=p^{1}\mathbf {e} _{1}+p^{2}\mathbf {e} _{2}+p^{3}\mathbf {e} _{3}={\cfrac {\partial x_{1}}{\partial q^{1}}}\mathbf {e} _{1}+{\cfrac {\partial x_{2}}{\partial q^{1}}}\mathbf {e} _{2}+{\cfrac {\partial x_{3}}{\partial q^{1}}}\mathbf {e} _{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/668cda63eab3d77380db93fdd171e6c2790ce462)
Similar equations hold for **b**2 and **b**3 so that the standard basis {**e**1, **e**2, **e**3} is transformed to a local (ordered and _**normalised**_) basis {**b**1, **b**2, **b**3} by the following system of equations:

![Image 48: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\mathbf {b} _{1}&={\cfrac {\partial x_{1}}{\partial q^{1}}}\mathbf {e} _{1}+{\cfrac {\partial x_{2}}{\partial q^{1}}}\mathbf {e} _{2}+{\cfrac {\partial x_{3}}{\partial q^{1}}}\mathbf {e} _{3}\\\mathbf {b} _{2}&={\cfrac {\partial x_{1}}{\partial q^{2}}}\mathbf {e} _{1}+{\cfrac {\partial x_{2}}{\partial q^{2}}}\mathbf {e} _{2}+{\cfrac {\partial x_{3}}{\partial q^{2}}}\mathbf {e} _{3}\\\mathbf {b} _{3}&={\cfrac {\partial x_{1}}{\partial q^{3}}}\mathbf {e} _{1}+{\cfrac {\partial x_{2}}{\partial q^{3}}}\mathbf {e} _{2}+{\cfrac {\partial x_{3}}{\partial q^{3}}}\mathbf {e} _{3}\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/75a5afca3d216bed23aed5b042503d98c125646f)
By analogous reasoning, one can obtain the inverse transformation from local basis to standard basis:

![Image 49: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\mathbf {e} _{1}&={\cfrac {\partial q^{1}}{\partial x_{1}}}\mathbf {b} _{1}+{\cfrac {\partial q^{2}}{\partial x_{1}}}\mathbf {b} _{2}+{\cfrac {\partial q^{3}}{\partial x_{1}}}\mathbf {b} _{3}\\\mathbf {e} _{2}&={\cfrac {\partial q^{1}}{\partial x_{2}}}\mathbf {b} _{1}+{\cfrac {\partial q^{2}}{\partial x_{2}}}\mathbf {b} _{2}+{\cfrac {\partial q^{3}}{\partial x_{2}}}\mathbf {b} _{3}\\\mathbf {e} _{3}&={\cfrac {\partial q^{1}}{\partial x_{3}}}\mathbf {b} _{1}+{\cfrac {\partial q^{2}}{\partial x_{3}}}\mathbf {b} _{2}+{\cfrac {\partial q^{3}}{\partial x_{3}}}\mathbf {b} _{3}\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1d94c587cd33f75654766059904adaeed9fffbb0)

### Jacobian of the transformation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Curvilinear_coordinates&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Jacobian of the transformation")]

The above [systems of linear equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Systems_of_linear_equations "Systems of linear equations") can be written in matrix form using the Einstein summation convention as

![Image 50: {\displaystyle {\cfrac {\partial x_{i}}{\partial q^{k}}}\mathbf {e} _{i}=\mathbf {b} _{k},\quad {\cfrac {\partial q^{i}}{\partial x_{k}}}\mathbf {b} _{i}=\mathbf {e} _{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d85a39f932a0fcaa72aebd003e11fa4377910f2).
This [coefficient matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coefficient_matrix "Coefficient matrix") of the linear system is the [Jacobian matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacobian_matrix "Jacobian matrix") (and its inverse) of the transformation. These are the equations that can be used to transform a Cartesian basis into a curvilinear basis, and vice versa.

In three dimensions, the expanded forms of these matrices are

![Image 51: {\displaystyle \mathbf {J} ={\begin{bmatrix}{\cfrac {\partial x_{1}}{\partial q^{1}}}&{\cfrac {\partial x_{1}}{\partial q^{2}}}&{\cfrac {\partial x_{1}}{\partial q^{3}}}\\{\cfrac {\partial x_{2}}{\partial q^{1}}}&{\cfrac {\partial x_{2}}{\partial q^{2}}}&{\cfrac {\partial x_{2}}{\partial q^{3}}}\\{\cfrac {\partial x_{3}}{\partial q^{1}}}&{\cfrac {\partial x_{3}}{\partial q^{2}}}&{\cfrac {\partial x_{3}}{\partial q^{3}}}\\\end{bmatrix}},\quad \mathbf {J} ^{-1}={\begin{bmatrix}{\cfrac {\partial q^{1}}{\partial x_{1}}}&{\cfrac {\partial q^{1}}{\partial x_{2}}}&{\cfrac {\partial q^{1}}{\partial x_{3}}}\\{\cfrac {\partial q^{2}}{\partial x_{1}}}&{\cfrac {\partial q^{2}}{\partial x_{2}}}&{\cfrac {\partial q^{2}}{\partial x_{3}}}\\{\cfrac {\partial q^{3}}{\partial x_{1}}}&{\cfrac {\partial q^{3}}{\partial x_{2}}}&{\cfrac {\partial q^{3}}{\partial x_{3}}}\\\end{bmatrix}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81c476e79d268d80cc14e21e816899232a6c22ed)
In the inverse transformation (second equation system), the unknowns are the curvilinear basis vectors. For any specific location there can only exist one and only one set of basis vectors (else the basis is not well defined at that point). This condition is satisfied if and only if the equation system has a single solution. In [linear algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_algebra "Linear algebra"), a linear equation system has a single solution (non-trivial) only if the determinant of its system matrix is non-zero:

![Image 52: {\displaystyle \det(\mathbf {J} ^{-1})\neq 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5c8800469e8bcacd676d8d138b0c9febd14af756)
which shows the rationale behind the above requirement concerning the inverse Jacobian determinant.

Generalization to _n_ dimensions
--------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Curvilinear_coordinates&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Generalization to n dimensions")]

The formalism extends to any finite dimension as follows.

Consider the [real](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number")[Euclidean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space")_n_-dimensional space, that is **R**_n_ = **R** × **R** × ... × **R** (_n_ times) where **R** is the [set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_(mathematics) "Set (mathematics)") of [real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_numbers "Real numbers") and × denotes the [Cartesian product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_product "Cartesian product"), which is a [vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space").

The [coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinates "Coordinates") of this space can be denoted by: **x** = (_x_ 1, _x_ 2,...,_x n_). Since this is a vector (an element of the vector space), it can be written as:

![Image 53: {\displaystyle \mathbf {x} =\sum _{i=1}^{n}x_{i}\mathbf {e} ^{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d24c5f8556329e74dca93fcf84bdbae51cc1bf5d)
where **e**1 = (1,0,0...,0), **e**2 = (0,1,0...,0), **e**3 = (0,0,1...,0),...,**e**_n_ = (0,0,0...,1) is the _[standard basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard\_basis "Standard basis") set of vectors_ for the space **R**_n_, and _i_ = 1, 2,..._n_ is an index labelling components. Each vector has exactly one component in each dimension (or "axis") and they are mutually [orthogonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_vector "Orthogonal vector") ([perpendicular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular "Perpendicular")) and normalized (has [unit magnitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_vector "Unit vector")).

More generally, we can define basis vectors **b**_i_ so that they depend on **q** = (_q_ 1, _q_ 2,...,_q n_), i.e. they change from point to point: **b**_i_ = **b**_i_(**q**). In which case to define the same point **x** in terms of this alternative basis: the _[coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate\_vector "Coordinate vector")_ with respect to this basis _v i_ also necessarily depend on **x** also, that is _v i_ = _v i_(**x**). Then a vector **v** in this space, with respect to these alternative coordinates and basis vectors, can be expanded as a [linear combination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_combination "Linear combination") in this basis (which simply means to multiply each basis [vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_vector "Coordinate vector")**e**_i_ by a number _v_ _i_ – [scalar multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_multiplication "Scalar multiplication")):

![Image 54: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} =\sum _{j=1}^{n}{\bar {v}}^{j}\mathbf {b} _{j}=\sum _{j=1}^{n}{\bar {v}}^{j}(\mathbf {q} )\mathbf {b} _{j}(\mathbf {q} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fcf9832a258ef99ec5c316c19929370ae83df768)
The vector sum that describes **v** in the new basis is composed of different vectors, although the sum itself remains the same.

Transformation of coordinates
-----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Curvilinear_coordinates&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Transformation of coordinates")]

From a more general and abstract perspective, a curvilinear coordinate system is simply a [coordinate patch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atlas_(topology) "Atlas (topology)") on the [differentiable manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differentiable_manifold "Differentiable manifold")**E**n (n-dimensional [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space")) that is [diffeomorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffeomorphism "Diffeomorphism") to the [Cartesian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system "Cartesian coordinate system") coordinate patch on the manifold.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvilinear_coordinates#cite_note-3) Two diffeomorphic coordinate patches on a differential manifold need not overlap differentiably. With this simple definition of a curvilinear coordinate system, all the results that follow below are simply applications of standard theorems in [differential topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_topology "Differential topology").

The transformation functions are such that there's a one-to-one relationship between points in the "old" and "new" coordinates, that is, those functions are [bijections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bijection "Bijection"), and fulfil the following requirements within their [domains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_of_a_function "Domain of a function"):

1.   They are [smooth functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smooth_function "Smooth function"): q _i_ = q _i_(**x**)
2.   The inverse [Jacobian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacobian_matrix_and_determinant "Jacobian matrix and determinant") determinant ![Image 55: {\displaystyle J^{-1}={\begin{vmatrix}{\dfrac {\partial q^{1}}{\partial x_{1}}}&{\dfrac {\partial q^{1}}{\partial x_{2}}}&\cdots &{\dfrac {\partial q^{1}}{\partial x_{n}}}\\{\dfrac {\partial q^{2}}{\partial x_{1}}}&{\dfrac {\partial q^{2}}{\partial x_{2}}}&\cdots &{\dfrac {\partial q^{2}}{\partial x_{n}}}\\\vdots &\vdots &\ddots &\vdots \\{\dfrac {\partial q^{n}}{\partial x_{1}}}&{\dfrac {\partial q^{n}}{\partial x_{2}}}&\cdots &{\dfrac {\partial q^{n}}{\partial x_{n}}}\end{vmatrix}}\neq 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5c39f865830b53fc5b6b3d3291dd31b507f11ffd) is not zero; meaning the transformation is [invertible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invertible "Invertible"): _x i_(**q**) according to the [inverse function theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverse_function_theorem "Inverse function theorem"). The condition that the Jacobian determinant is not zero reflects the fact that three surfaces from different families intersect in one and only one point and thus determine the position of this point in a unique way.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvilinear_coordinates#cite_note-4)

Vector and tensor algebra in three-dimensional curvilinear coordinates
----------------------------------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Curvilinear_coordinates&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Vector and tensor algebra in three-dimensional curvilinear coordinates")]

Elementary vector and tensor algebra in curvilinear coordinates is used in some of the older scientific literature in [mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanics "Mechanics") and [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics") and can be indispensable to understanding work from the early and mid-1900s, for example the text by Green and Zerna.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvilinear_coordinates#cite_note-Green-5) Some useful relations in the algebra of vectors and second-order tensors in curvilinear coordinates are given in this section. The notation and contents are primarily from Ogden,[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvilinear_coordinates#cite_note-Ogden00-6) Naghdi,[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvilinear_coordinates#cite_note-Naghdi-7) Simmonds,[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvilinear_coordinates#cite_note-Simmonds-2) Green and Zerna,[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvilinear_coordinates#cite_note-Green-5) Basar and Weichert,[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvilinear_coordinates#cite_note-Basar-8) and Ciarlet.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvilinear_coordinates#cite_note-Ciarlet-9)

Tensors in curvilinear coordinates
----------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Curvilinear_coordinates&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Tensors in curvilinear coordinates")]

A second-order tensor can be expressed as

![Image 56: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {S}}=S^{ij}\mathbf {b} _{i}\otimes \mathbf {b} _{j}=S^{i}{}_{j}\mathbf {b} _{i}\otimes \mathbf {b} ^{j}=S_{i}{}^{j}\mathbf {b} ^{i}\otimes \mathbf {b} _{j}=S_{ij}\mathbf {b} ^{i}\otimes \mathbf {b} ^{j}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c69086f42bf8c0451110c35a77f96b7c86d51336)

The components _S ij_ are called the **contravariant** components, _S i j_ the **mixed right-covariant** components, _S i j_ the **mixed left-covariant** components, and _S ij_ the **covariant** components of the second-order tensor. The components of the second-order tensor are related by

### The metric tensor in orthogonal curvilinear coordinates

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Curvilinear_coordinates&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: The metric tensor in orthogonal curvilinear coordinates")]

At each point, one can construct a small line element d**x**, so the square of the length of the line element is the scalar product d**x** • d**x** and is called the [metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_(mathematics) "Metric (mathematics)") of the [space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space "Space"), given by:

![Image 59: {\displaystyle d\mathbf {x} \cdot d\mathbf {x} ={\cfrac {\partial x_{i}}{\partial q^{j}}}{\cfrac {\partial x_{i}}{\partial q^{k}}}dq^{j}dq^{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f52783e4a649c19512416cbbcd8ed9cebf980aeb).
The following portion of the above equation

![Image 60: {\displaystyle {\cfrac {\partial x_{k}}{\partial q^{i}}}{\cfrac {\partial x_{k}}{\partial q^{j}}}=g_{ij}(q^{i},q^{j})=\mathbf {b} _{i}\cdot \mathbf {b} _{j}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/725298f21b2ffd5956233a3e0955fc8c283fe891)
is a _symmetric_ tensor called the **[fundamental (or metric) tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_tensor "Metric tensor")** of the [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space") in curvilinear coordinates.

Indices can be [raised and lowered](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raising_and_lowering_indices "Raising and lowering indices") by the metric:

![Image 61: {\displaystyle v^{i}=g^{ik}v_{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e978a8c8a7140a4553204ec00444952a6d4a3473)

#### Example: Polar coordinates

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Curvilinear_coordinates&action=edit&section=16 "Edit section: Example: Polar coordinates")]

If we consider polar coordinates for **R**2,

### Christoffel symbols

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Curvilinear_coordinates&action=edit&section=18 "Edit section: Christoffel symbols")]

Vector and tensor calculus in three-dimensional curvilinear coordinates
-----------------------------------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Curvilinear_coordinates&action=edit&section=20 "Edit section: Vector and tensor calculus in three-dimensional curvilinear coordinates")]

Adjustments need to be made in the calculation of [line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_integral "Line integral"), [surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_integral "Surface integral") and [volume](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume_integral "Volume integral")[integrals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integration_(mathematics) "Integration (mathematics)"). For simplicity, the following restricts to three dimensions and orthogonal curvilinear coordinates. However, the same arguments apply for _n_-dimensional spaces. When the coordinate system is not orthogonal, there are some additional terms in the expressions.

Simmonds,[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvilinear_coordinates#cite_note-Simmonds-2) in his book on [tensor analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_analysis "Tensor analysis"), quotes [Albert Einstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Einstein "Albert Einstein") saying[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvilinear_coordinates#cite_note-Lanczos-10)

> The magic of this theory will hardly fail to impose itself on anybody who has truly understood it; it represents a genuine triumph of the method of absolute differential calculus, founded by Gauss, Riemann, Ricci, and Levi-Civita.

Vector and tensor calculus in general curvilinear coordinates is used in tensor analysis on four-dimensional curvilinear [manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manifold "Manifold") in [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity"),[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvilinear_coordinates#cite_note-Misner-11) in the [mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_mechanics "Solid mechanics") of curved [shells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plate_theory "Plate theory"),[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvilinear_coordinates#cite_note-Ciarlet-9) in examining the [invariance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invariant_(mathematics) "Invariant (mathematics)") properties of [Maxwell's equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxwell%27s_equations "Maxwell's equations") which has been of interest in [metamaterials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metamaterials "Metamaterials")[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvilinear_coordinates#cite_note-Greenleaf-12)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvilinear_coordinates#cite_note-Leonhardt-13) and in many other fields.

Some useful relations in the calculus of vectors and second-order tensors in curvilinear coordinates are given in this section. The notation and contents are primarily from Ogden,[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvilinear_coordinates#cite_note-14) Simmonds,[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvilinear_coordinates#cite_note-Simmonds-2) Green and Zerna,[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvilinear_coordinates#cite_note-Green-5) Basar and Weichert,[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvilinear_coordinates#cite_note-Basar-8) and Ciarlet.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvilinear_coordinates#cite_note-Ciarlet-9)

Fictitious forces in general curvilinear coordinates
----------------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Curvilinear_coordinates&action=edit&section=24 "Edit section: Fictitious forces in general curvilinear coordinates")]

By definition, if a particle with no forces acting on it has its position expressed in an inertial coordinate system, (_x_ 1,_x_ 2,_x_ 3,_t_), then there it will have no acceleration (d 2 _x_ _j_/d _t_ 2=0).[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvilinear_coordinates#cite_note-15) In this context, a coordinate system can fail to be "inertial" either due to non-straight time axis or non-straight space axes (or both). In other words, the basis vectors of the coordinates may vary in time at fixed positions, or they may vary with position at fixed times, or both. When equations of motion are expressed in terms of any non-inertial coordinate system (in this sense), extra terms appear, called Christoffel symbols. Strictly speaking, these terms represent components of the absolute acceleration (in classical mechanics), but we may also choose to continue to regard d 2 _x_ _j_/d _t_ 2 as the acceleration (as if the coordinates were inertial) and treat the extra terms as if they were forces, in which case they are called fictitious forces.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvilinear_coordinates#cite_note-16) The component of any such fictitious force normal to the path of the particle and in the plane of the path's curvature is then called [centrifugal force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centrifugal_force "Centrifugal force").[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvilinear_coordinates#cite_note-17)

This more general context makes clear the correspondence between the concepts of centrifugal force in [rotating coordinate systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotating_reference_frame "Rotating reference frame") and in stationary curvilinear coordinate systems. (Both of these concepts appear frequently in the literature.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvilinear_coordinates#cite_note-18)[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvilinear_coordinates#cite_note-19)[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvilinear_coordinates#cite_note-20)) For a simple example, consider a particle of mass _m_ moving in a circle of radius _r_ with angular speed _w_ relative to a system of polar coordinates rotating with angular speed _W_. The radial equation of motion is _mr_”=_F_ _r_+_mr_(_w_+_W_)2. Thus the centrifugal force is _mr_ times the square of the absolute rotational speed _A_=_w_+_W_ of the particle. If we choose a coordinate system rotating at the speed of the particle, then _W_=_A_ and _w_=0, in which case the centrifugal force is _mrA_ 2, whereas if we choose a stationary coordinate system we have _W_=0 and _w_=_A_, in which case the centrifugal force is again _mrA_ 2. The reason for this equality of results is that in both cases the basis vectors at the particle's location are changing in time in exactly the same way. Hence these are really just two different ways of describing exactly the same thing, one description being in terms of rotating coordinates and the other being in terms of stationary curvilinear coordinates, both of which are non-inertial according to the more abstract meaning of that term.

When describing general motion, the actual forces acting on a particle are often referred to the instantaneous [osculating circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osculating_circle "Osculating circle") tangent to the path of motion, and this circle in the general case is not centered at a fixed location, and so the decomposition into centrifugal and Coriolis components is constantly changing. This is true regardless of whether the motion is described in terms of stationary or rotating coordinates.
