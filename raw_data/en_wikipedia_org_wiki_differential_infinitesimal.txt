This article is about the mathematical notion derived from the historic concept of infinitesimal difference. For other uses, see [Differential (disambiguation)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_(disambiguation) "Differential (disambiguation)").

In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), **differential** refers to several related notions[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_(infinitesimal)#cite_note-1) derived from the early days of [calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus "Calculus"), put on a rigorous footing, such as [infinitesimal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinitesimal "Infinitesimal") differences and the [derivatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative "Derivative") of functions.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_(infinitesimal)#cite_note-2)

The term is used in various branches of mathematics such as [calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus "Calculus"), [differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry"), [algebraic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_geometry "Algebraic geometry") and [algebraic topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_topology "Algebraic topology").

The term **differential** is used nonrigorously in [calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus "Calculus") to refer to an [infinitesimal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinitesimal "Infinitesimal") ("infinitely small") change in some [varying quantity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variable_(mathematics) "Variable (mathematics)"). For example, if _x_ is a [variable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variable_(mathematics) "Variable (mathematics)"), then a change in the value of _x_ is often denoted Δ _x_ (pronounced _[delta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Delta\_(Greek) "Delta (Greek)") x_). The differential _dx_ represents an infinitely small change in the variable _x_. The idea of an infinitely small or infinitely slow change is, intuitively, extremely useful, and there are a number of ways to make the notion mathematically precise.

Using calculus, it is possible to relate the infinitely small changes of various variables to each other mathematically using [derivatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative "Derivative"). If _y_ is a function of _x_, then the differential _dy_ of _y_ is related to _dx_ by the formula ![Image 1: {\displaystyle dy={\frac {dy}{dx}}\,dx,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e8690f9df1066aadc3c2999e8633a040038558df) where ![Image 2: {\displaystyle {\frac {dy}{dx}}\,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4ff82c4b4be3ae608bf9eafa4aad43081c9bb56a)denotes not 'dy divided by dx' as one would intuitively read, but 'the [derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative "Derivative") of _y_ with respect to _x_ '. This formula summarizes the idea that the derivative of _y_ with respect to _x_ is the limit of the ratio of differences Δ _y_/Δ _x_ as Δ _x_ approaches zero:

*   In [calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus "Calculus"), the [differential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_of_a_function "Differential of a function") represents a change in the [linearization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linearization "Linearization") of a [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)"). 
    *   The [total differential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Total_differential "Total differential") is its generalization for functions of multiple variables.

*   In traditional approaches to calculus, differentials (e.g. _dx_, _dy_, _dt_, etc.) are interpreted as [infinitesimals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinitesimal "Infinitesimal"). There are several methods of defining infinitesimals rigorously, but it is sufficient to say that an infinitesimal number is smaller in absolute value than any positive real number, just as an infinitely large number is larger than any real number.
*   The [differential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Total_derivative "Total derivative") is another name for the [Jacobian matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacobian_matrix "Jacobian matrix") of [partial derivatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_derivative "Partial derivative") of a function from **R**_n_ to **R**_m_ (especially when this [matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_(mathematics) "Matrix (mathematics)") is viewed as a [linear map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_map "Linear map")).
*   More generally, the [differential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pushforward_(differential) "Pushforward (differential)") or _[pushforward](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pushforward\_(differential) "Pushforward (differential)")_ refers to the derivative of a map between [smooth manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smooth_manifold "Smooth manifold") and the pushforward operations it defines. The differential is also used to define the dual concept of [pullback](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pullback_(differential_geometry) "Pullback (differential geometry)").
*   [Stochastic calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stochastic_calculus "Stochastic calculus") provides a notion of [stochastic differential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stochastic_differential "Stochastic differential") and an associated calculus for [stochastic processes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stochastic_process "Stochastic process").
*   The [integrator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stieltjes_integral#Definition "Stieltjes integral") in a [Stieltjes integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stieltjes_integral "Stieltjes integral") is represented as the differential of a function. Formally, the differential appearing under the integral behaves exactly as a differential: thus, the [integration by substitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integration_by_substitution "Integration by substitution") and [integration by parts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integration_by_parts "Integration by parts") formulae for Stieltjes integral correspond, respectively, to the [chain rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chain_rule "Chain rule") and [product rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Product_rule "Product rule") for the differential.

[Infinitesimal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinitesimal "Infinitesimal") quantities played a significant role in the development of calculus. [Archimedes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archimedes "Archimedes") used them, even though he did not believe that arguments involving infinitesimals were rigorous.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_(infinitesimal)#cite_note-3)[Isaac Newton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaac_Newton "Isaac Newton") referred to them as [fluxions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Method_of_Fluxions "Method of Fluxions"). However, it was [Gottfried Leibniz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gottfried_Leibniz "Gottfried Leibniz") who coined the term _differentials_ for infinitesimal quantities and introduced the notation for them which is still used today.

In [Leibniz's notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leibniz%27s_notation "Leibniz's notation"), if _x_ is a variable quantity, then _dx_ denotes an infinitesimal change in the variable _x_. Thus, if _y_ is a function of _x_, then the [derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative "Derivative") of _y_ with respect to _x_ is often denoted _dy_/_dx_, which would otherwise be denoted (in the notation of Newton or [Lagrange](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph-Louis_Lagrange "Joseph-Louis Lagrange")) _ẏ_ or _y_′. The use of differentials in this form attracted much criticism, for instance in the famous pamphlet [The Analyst](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Analyst "The Analyst") by Bishop Berkeley. Nevertheless, the notation has remained popular because it suggests strongly the idea that the derivative of _y_ at _x_ is its [instantaneous rate of change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Instantaneous_rate_of_change "Instantaneous rate of change") (the [slope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slope_(mathematics) "Slope (mathematics)") of the graph's [tangent line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_line "Tangent line")), which may be obtained by taking the [limit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limit_(mathematics) "Limit (mathematics)") of the ratio Δ _y_/Δ _x_ as Δ _x_ becomes arbitrarily small. Differentials are also compatible with [dimensional analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimensional_analysis "Dimensional analysis"), where a differential such as _dx_ has the same dimensions as the variable _x_.

Calculus evolved into a distinct branch of mathematics during the 17th century CE, although there were antecedents going back to antiquity. The presentations of, e.g., Newton, Leibniz, were marked by non-rigorous definitions of terms like differential, [fluent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluent_(mathematics) "Fluent (mathematics)") and "infinitely small". While many of the arguments in [Bishop Berkeley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_Berkeley "George Berkeley")'s 1734 [The Analyst](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Analyst "The Analyst") are theological in nature, modern mathematicians acknowledge the validity of his argument against "[the Ghosts of departed Quantities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Analyst#Ghosts_of_departed_quantities "The Analyst")"; however, the modern approaches do not have the same technical issues. Despite the lack of rigor, immense progress was made in the 17th and 18th centuries. In the 19th century, Cauchy and others gradually developed the [Epsilon, delta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epsilon,_delta "Epsilon, delta") approach to continuity, limits and derivatives, giving a solid conceptual foundation for calculus.

In the 20th century, several new concepts in, e.g., multivariable calculus, differential geometry, seemed to encapsulate the intent of the old terms, especially _differential_; both differential and infinitesimal are used with new, more rigorous, meanings.

Differentials are also used in the notation for [integrals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral "Integral") because an integral can be regarded as an infinite sum of infinitesimal quantities: the area under a graph is obtained by subdividing the graph into infinitely thin strips and summing their areas. In an expression such as ![Image 10: {\displaystyle \int f(x)\,dx,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1bbd50daec1c6f3767a42b842a9b203d840e9934) the integral sign (which is a modified [long s](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Long_s "Long s")) denotes the infinite sum, _f_(_x_) denotes the "height" of a thin strip, and the differential _dx_ denotes its infinitely thin width.

There are several approaches for making the notion of differentials mathematically precise.

1.   Differentials as [linear maps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_map "Linear map"). This approach underlies the definition of the [derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Total_derivative "Total derivative") and the [exterior derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_derivative "Exterior derivative") in [differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_(infinitesimal)#cite_note-4)
2.   Differentials as [nilpotent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nilpotent "Nilpotent") elements of [commutative rings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_ring "Commutative ring"). This approach is popular in algebraic geometry.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_(infinitesimal)#cite_note-Harris1998-5)
3.   Differentials in smooth models of set theory. This approach is known as [synthetic differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synthetic_differential_geometry "Synthetic differential geometry") or [smooth infinitesimal analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smooth_infinitesimal_analysis "Smooth infinitesimal analysis") and is closely related to the algebraic geometric approach, except that ideas from [topos theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topos_theory "Topos theory") are used to _hide_ the mechanisms by which nilpotent infinitesimals are introduced.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_(infinitesimal)#cite_note-6)
4.   Differentials as infinitesimals in [hyperreal number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperreal_number "Hyperreal number") systems, which are extensions of the real numbers that contain invertible infinitesimals and infinitely large numbers. This is the approach of [nonstandard analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nonstandard_analysis "Nonstandard analysis") pioneered by [Abraham Robinson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abraham_Robinson "Abraham Robinson").[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_(infinitesimal)#cite_note-nonstd-7)

These approaches are very different from each other, but they have in common the idea of being _quantitative_, i.e., saying not just that a differential is infinitely small, but _how_ small it is.

### Differentials as linear maps

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Differential_(mathematics)&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Differentials as linear maps")]

There is a simple way to make precise sense of differentials, first used on the Real line by regarding them as [linear maps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_map "Linear map"). It can be used on ![Image 11: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc), ![Image 12: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d), a [Hilbert space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space "Hilbert space"), a [Banach space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Banach_space "Banach space"), or more generally, a [topological vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_vector_space "Topological vector space"). The case of the Real line is the easiest to explain. This type of differential is also known as a [covariant vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covariant_vector "Covariant vector") or [cotangent vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cotangent_vector "Cotangent vector"), depending on context.

#### Differentials as linear maps on R

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Differential_(mathematics)&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Differentials as linear maps on R")]

Then ![Image 19: {\displaystyle f(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/202945cce41ecebb6f643f31d119c514bec7a074) is the composite of ![Image 20: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) with ![Image 21: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4), whose value at ![Image 22: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) is ![Image 23: {\displaystyle f(x(p))=f(p)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9d0bf9340067d5395bcaee48429f6251cf4c6841). The differential ![Image 24: {\displaystyle \operatorname {d} f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/96c037e09e925f5c3080de37c5475ef610453129) (which of course depends on ![Image 25: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61)) is then a function whose value at ![Image 26: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) (usually denoted ![Image 27: {\displaystyle df_{p}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1142d069553f6a55274e87055720127c02d3ec02)) is not a number, but a linear map from ![Image 28: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc) to ![Image 29: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc). Since a linear map from ![Image 30: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc) to ![Image 31: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc) is given by a ![Image 32: {\displaystyle 1\times 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b4bf91a527dc01af9ef6ace81199becf1308e00)[matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_(mathematics) "Matrix (mathematics)"), it is essentially the same thing as a number, but the change in the point of view allows us to think of ![Image 33: {\displaystyle df_{p}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1142d069553f6a55274e87055720127c02d3ec02) as an infinitesimal and _compare_ it with the _standard infinitesimal_![Image 34: {\displaystyle dx_{p}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/29eb801f8265bc67ad79d20852df460a7b50b8c4), which is again just the identity map from ![Image 35: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc) to ![Image 36: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc) (a ![Image 37: {\displaystyle 1\times 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b4bf91a527dc01af9ef6ace81199becf1308e00)[matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_(mathematics) "Matrix (mathematics)") with entry ![Image 38: {\displaystyle 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/92d98b82a3778f043108d4e20960a9193df57cbf)). The identity map has the property that if ![Image 39: {\displaystyle \varepsilon }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a30c89172e5b88edbd45d3e2772c7f5e562e5173) is very small, then ![Image 40: {\displaystyle dx_{p}(\varepsilon )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b7725201829c09ebd77a3d6855a9229664fa4cd9) is very small, which enables us to regard it as infinitesimal. The differential ![Image 41: {\displaystyle df_{p}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1142d069553f6a55274e87055720127c02d3ec02) has the same property, because it is just a multiple of ![Image 42: {\displaystyle dx_{p}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/29eb801f8265bc67ad79d20852df460a7b50b8c4), and this multiple is the derivative ![Image 43: {\displaystyle f'(p)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/71159b6c94f0c2f8a4a88e0486a2bd0e95dafcbe) by definition. We therefore obtain that ![Image 44: {\displaystyle df_{p}=f'(p)\,dx_{p}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/02a29e57d4135aa64a8fd3df615d37b778cd6437), and hence ![Image 45: {\displaystyle df=f'\,dx}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/79c0c635464c5c6aee6ca78d32df5352f2745806). Thus we recover the idea that ![Image 46: {\displaystyle f'}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/258eaada38956fb69b8cb1a2eef46bcb97d3126b) is the ratio of the differentials ![Image 47: {\displaystyle df}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/53181e2067a93b6bbf150042723cb059d9d2d26f) and ![Image 48: {\displaystyle dx}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/845c817e348381a13f3fad5184169ce0e021c685).

This would just be a trick were it not for the fact that:

1.   it captures the idea of the derivative of ![Image 49: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) at ![Image 50: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) as the _best linear approximation_ to ![Image 51: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) at ![Image 52: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36);
2.   it has many generalizations.

We can now use the same trick as in the one-dimensional case and think of the expression ![Image 66: {\displaystyle f(x_{1},x_{2},\ldots ,x_{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1f25b8df72ba6491812f45e36e4938ad3385def1) as the composite of ![Image 67: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) with the standard coordinates ![Image 68: {\displaystyle x_{1},x_{2},\ldots ,x_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8694289524164f895d6665f163e14c4dc5ec648d) on ![Image 69: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d) (so that ![Image 70: {\displaystyle x_{j}(p)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffbc4509c3230bedf96b00363956aa7515d15597) is the ![Image 71: {\displaystyle j}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2f461e54f5c093e92a55547b9764291390f0b5d0)-th component of ![Image 72: {\displaystyle p\in \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c7d5b6202943d58e511d6616d84cd13ca0bc7547)). Then the differentials ![Image 73: {\displaystyle \left(dx_{1}\right)_{p},\left(dx_{2}\right)_{p},\ldots ,\left(dx_{n}\right)_{p}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1915bc551395809621a0904b4acda7aaab1efb3a) at a point ![Image 74: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) form a [basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basis_(linear_algebra) "Basis (linear algebra)") for the [vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space") of linear maps from ![Image 75: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d) to ![Image 76: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc) and therefore, if ![Image 77: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) is differentiable at ![Image 78: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36), we can write _![Image 79: {\displaystyle \operatorname {d} f\_{p}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest\_v1/media/math/render/svg/d57e5bfe93b171364c27e2a17df61d97eae34c69)_ as a [linear combination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_combination "Linear combination") of these basis elements: ![Image 80: {\displaystyle df_{p}=\sum _{j=1}^{n}D_{j}f(p)\,(dx_{j})_{p}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1b86b7d9325b42462abb1dfef5683590981c929b)

The coefficients ![Image 81: {\displaystyle D_{j}f(p)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3b0c00373ed02572aa7ebfaa00287e865c9ab290) are (by definition) the [partial derivatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_derivative "Partial derivative") of ![Image 82: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) at ![Image 83: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) with respect to ![Image 84: {\displaystyle x_{1},x_{2},\ldots ,x_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8694289524164f895d6665f163e14c4dc5ec648d). Hence, if ![Image 85: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) is differentiable on all of ![Image 86: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d), we can write, more concisely: ![Image 87: {\displaystyle \operatorname {d} f={\frac {\partial f}{\partial x_{1}}}\,dx_{1}+{\frac {\partial f}{\partial x_{2}}}\,dx_{2}+\cdots +{\frac {\partial f}{\partial x_{n}}}\,dx_{n}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/16690aa7b41df5c9565decd96906b979a26a10a4)

In the one-dimensional case this becomes ![Image 88: {\displaystyle df={\frac {df}{dx}}dx}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/65c642a7b555ece7647a9227b5da4e5714a544b3) as before.

This idea generalizes straightforwardly to functions from ![Image 89: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d) to ![Image 90: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{m}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6a87a024931038d1858dc22e8a194e5978c3412e). Furthermore, it has the decisive advantage over other definitions of the derivative that it is [invariant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invariant_(mathematics) "Invariant (mathematics)") under changes of coordinates. This means that the same idea can be used to define the [differential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pushforward_(differential) "Pushforward (differential)") of [smooth maps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smooth_map "Smooth map") between [smooth manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smooth_manifold "Smooth manifold").

Aside: Note that the existence of all the [partial derivatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_derivative "Partial derivative") of ![Image 91: {\displaystyle f(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/202945cce41ecebb6f643f31d119c514bec7a074) at ![Image 92: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) is a [necessary condition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Necessary_condition "Necessary condition") for the existence of a differential at ![Image 93: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4). However it is not a [sufficient condition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sufficient_condition "Sufficient condition"). For counterexamples, see [Gateaux derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gateaux_derivative "Gateaux derivative").

#### Differentials as linear maps on a vector space

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Differential_(mathematics)&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Differentials as linear maps on a vector space")]

The same procedure works on a vector space with a enough additional structure to reasonably talk about continuity. The most concrete case is a Hilbert space, also known as a [complete](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complete_metric_space "Complete metric space")[inner product space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inner_product_space "Inner product space"), where the inner product and its associated [norm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norm_(mathematics) "Norm (mathematics)") define a suitable concept of distance. The same procedure works for a Banach space, also known as a complete [Normed vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normed_vector_space "Normed vector space"). However, for a more general topological vector space, some of the details are more abstract because there is no concept of distance.

For the important case of a finite dimension, any inner product space is a Hilbert space, any normed vector space is a Banach space and any topological vector space is complete. As a result, you can define a coordinate system from an arbitrary basis and use the same technique as for ![Image 94: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d).

### Differentials as germs of functions

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Differential_(mathematics)&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Differentials as germs of functions")]

This approach works on any [differentiable manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differentiable_manifold "Differentiable manifold"). If

1.   U and V are open sets containing p
2.   ![Image 95: {\displaystyle f\colon U\to \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d71097bf5f00c4116453f42be6ed7f74e74b3be8) is continuous
3.   ![Image 96: {\displaystyle g\colon V\to \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/333c802782a87aa8dea28667684f72121d9c13a0) is continuous

then f is equivalent to g at p, denoted ![Image 97: {\displaystyle f\sim _{p}g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e2f57220ebbf6ef6bd770639d27f2363627fb61c), if and only if there is an open ![Image 98: {\displaystyle W\subseteq U\cap V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/308a6209e20e8e1d69e23e43419c3af9de07f24e) containing p such that ![Image 99: {\displaystyle f(x)=g(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/01d0bb8fc04dfc0d04d53ee9ada38c794e51ca78) for every x in W. The germ of f at p, denoted ![Image 100: {\displaystyle [f]_{p}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f77bc55904f55174e9e6411be57c5719a71fe2d8), is the set of all real continuous functions equivalent to f at p; if f is smooth at p then ![Image 101: {\displaystyle [f]_{p}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f77bc55904f55174e9e6411be57c5719a71fe2d8) is a smooth germ. If

1.   ![Image 102: {\displaystyle U_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bc9e7f892894bc50c32ce1b9f9a68a15562146ac), ![Image 103: {\displaystyle U_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/590fa6a550fbe2866a28243a733d54245d218b9d)![Image 104: {\displaystyle V_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/adfdbc929f16cb00bb43289c223651b41f7b9f80) and ![Image 105: {\displaystyle V_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ceaa689a894f5020a7b46177d201cbce2d41122b) are open sets containing p
2.   ![Image 106: {\displaystyle f_{1}\colon U_{1}\to \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/29926caaf9ef12db59b73a186ac1c306061881be), ![Image 107: {\displaystyle f_{2}\colon U_{2}\to \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d8a65163a5a0c5bd4e2d05b69ccae50a8ca3778), ![Image 108: {\displaystyle g_{1}\colon V_{1}\to \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffa08235836035f50d4fa519c1cbef688d8c71c7) and ![Image 109: {\displaystyle g_{2}\colon V_{2}\to \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e9e18014d19044ad4a8d27632cae9557b298014) are smooth functions
3.   ![Image 110: {\displaystyle f_{1}\sim _{p}g_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/85d0d4ad59dcf4a73b6707b9e9c777d60a1d466b)
4.   ![Image 111: {\displaystyle f_{2}\sim _{p}g_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/139fe310f3cf9ca5479c0013253e48f93429b0fe)
5.   r is a real number

then

1.   ![Image 112: {\displaystyle r*f_{1}\sim _{p}r*g_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c84990e5fdb3622991bae28e03035cfe7c27cecf)

This shows that the germs at p form an [algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra_over_a_field "Algebra over a field").

Define ![Image 115: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {I}}_{p}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0ed7ed4e7d9bda2118bf2b71311492f02f41e4cc) to be the set of all smooth germs vanishing at p and ![Image 116: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {I}}_{p}^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bc5a8fa0628e490fa40fff5f7a4b1e1aad3ca095) to be the [product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ideal_(ring_theory)#Ideal_operations "Ideal (ring theory)") of [ideals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ideal_(ring_theory) "Ideal (ring theory)")![Image 117: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {I}}_{p}{\mathcal {I}}_{p}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7922cac0ab13c9c8fca149784f0607fd1c4e00cc). Then a differential at p (cotangent vector at p) is an element of ![Image 118: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {I}}_{p}/{\mathcal {I}}_{p}^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4ebdc9240ad792f78b3bc5ec048020102cedb73). The differential of a smooth function f at p, denoted ![Image 119: {\displaystyle \mathrm {d} f_{p}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/df01051e1b972bf6ad289df346543a838018ec97), is ![Image 120: {\displaystyle [f-f(p)]_{p}/{\mathcal {I}}_{p}^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/01a7fbfd65b3fff3abeda692043a67fdc6153087).

A similar approach is to define differential equivalence of first order in terms of derivatives in an arbitrary coordinate patch. Then the differential of f at p is the set of all functions differentially equivalent to ![Image 121: {\displaystyle f-f(p)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/130d67b91b0d823388b721c773f8bb914ffda1e2) at p.

In [algebraic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_geometry "Algebraic geometry"), differentials and other infinitesimal notions are handled in a very explicit way by accepting that the [coordinate ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_ring "Coordinate ring") or [structure sheaf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Structure_sheaf "Structure sheaf") of a space may contain [nilpotent elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nilpotent_element "Nilpotent element"). The simplest example is the ring of [dual numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_number "Dual number")**R**[_ε_], where _ε_ 2 = 0.

This can be motivated by the algebro-geometric point of view on the derivative of a function _f_ from **R** to **R** at a point _p_. For this, note first that _f_− _f_(_p_) belongs to the [ideal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ideal_(ring_theory) "Ideal (ring theory)")_I_ _p_ of functions on **R** which vanish at _p_. If the derivative _f_ vanishes at _p_, then _f_− _f_(_p_) belongs to the square _I_ _p_ 2 of this ideal. Hence the derivative of _f_ at _p_ may be captured by the equivalence class [_f_− _f_(_p_)] in the [quotient space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quotient_space_(linear_algebra) "Quotient space (linear algebra)")_I_ _p_/_I_ _p_ 2, and the [1-jet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jet_(mathematics) "Jet (mathematics)") of _f_ (which encodes its value and its first derivative) is the equivalence class of _f_ in the space of all functions modulo _I_ _p_ 2. Algebraic geometers regard this equivalence class as the _restriction_ of _f_ to a _thickened_ version of the point _p_ whose coordinate ring is not **R** (which is the quotient space of functions on **R** modulo _I_ _p_) but **R**[_ε_] which is the quotient space of functions on **R** modulo _I_ _p_ 2. Such a thickened point is a simple example of a [scheme](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scheme_(mathematics) "Scheme (mathematics)").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_(infinitesimal)#cite_note-Harris1998-5)

#### Algebraic geometry notions

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Differential_(mathematics)&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Algebraic geometry notions")]

Differentials are also important in [algebraic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_geometry "Algebraic geometry"), and there are several important notions.

*   [Abelian differentials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abelian_differential "Abelian differential") usually mean differential one-forms on an [algebraic curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_curve "Algebraic curve") or [Riemann surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_surface "Riemann surface").
*   [Quadratic differentials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadratic_differential "Quadratic differential") (which behave like "squares" of abelian differentials) are also important in the theory of Riemann surfaces.
*   [Kähler differentials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K%C3%A4hler_differential "Kähler differential") provide a general notion of differential in algebraic geometry.

### Synthetic differential geometry

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Differential_(mathematics)&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Synthetic differential geometry")]

A fifth approach to infinitesimals is the method of [synthetic differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synthetic_differential_geometry "Synthetic differential geometry")[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_(infinitesimal)#cite_note-9) or [smooth infinitesimal analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smooth_infinitesimal_analysis "Smooth infinitesimal analysis").[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_(infinitesimal)#cite_note-10) This is closely related to the algebraic-geometric approach, except that the infinitesimals are more implicit and intuitive. The main idea of this approach is to replace the [category of sets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category_of_sets "Category of sets") with another [category](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category_(mathematics) "Category (mathematics)") of _smoothly varying sets_ which is a [topos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topos "Topos"). In this category, one can define the real numbers, smooth functions, and so on, but the real numbers _automatically_ contain nilpotent infinitesimals, so these do not need to be introduced by hand as in the algebraic geometric approach. However the [logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logic "Logic") in this new category is not identical to the familiar logic of the category of sets: in particular, the [law of the excluded middle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law_of_the_excluded_middle "Law of the excluded middle") does not hold. This means that set-theoretic mathematical arguments only extend to smooth infinitesimal analysis if they are _[constructive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constructive\_mathematics "Constructive mathematics")_ (e.g., do not use [proof by contradiction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proof_by_contradiction "Proof by contradiction")). [Constructivists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constructivism_(philosophy_of_mathematics) "Constructivism (philosophy of mathematics)") regard this disadvantage as a positive thing, since it forces one to find constructive arguments wherever they are available.

### Nonstandard analysis

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Differential_(mathematics)&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Nonstandard analysis")]

The final approach to infinitesimals again involves extending the real numbers, but in a less drastic way. In the [nonstandard analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nonstandard_analysis "Nonstandard analysis") approach there are no nilpotent infinitesimals, only invertible ones, which may be viewed as the [reciprocals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplicative_inverse "Multiplicative inverse") of infinitely large numbers.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_(infinitesimal)#cite_note-nonstd-7) Such extensions of the real numbers may be constructed explicitly using equivalence classes of sequences of [real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number"), so that, for example, the sequence (1,1/2,1/3,...,1/_n_,...) represents an infinitesimal. The [first-order logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First-order_logic "First-order logic") of this new set of [hyperreal numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperreal_number "Hyperreal number") is the same as the logic for the usual real numbers, but the [completeness axiom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Completeness_axiom "Completeness axiom") (which involves [second-order logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second-order_logic "Second-order logic")) does not hold. Nevertheless, this suffices to develop an elementary and quite intuitive approach to calculus using infinitesimals, see [transfer principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transfer_principle "Transfer principle").

Differential geometry
---------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Differential_(mathematics)&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Differential geometry")]

The notion of a differential motivates several concepts in [differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry") (and [differential topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_topology "Differential topology")).

*   The [differential (Pushforward)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pushforward_(differential) "Pushforward (differential)") of a map between manifolds.
*   [Differential forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_form "Differential form") provide a framework which accommodates multiplication and differentiation of differentials.
*   The [exterior derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_derivative "Exterior derivative") is a notion of differentiation of differential forms which generalizes the [differential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Total_derivative "Total derivative") of a function (which is a [differential 1-form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_1-form "Differential 1-form")).
*   [Pullback](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pullback_(differential_geometry) "Pullback (differential geometry)") is, in particular, a geometric name for the [chain rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chain_rule "Chain rule") for composing a map between manifolds with a differential form on the target manifold.
*   [Covariant derivatives or differentials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covariant_derivative "Covariant derivative") provide a general notion for differentiating of [vector fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_field "Vector field") and [tensor fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_field "Tensor field") on a manifold, or, more generally, sections of a [vector bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_bundle "Vector bundle"): see [Connection (vector bundle)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connection_(vector_bundle) "Connection (vector bundle)"). This ultimately leads to the general concept of a [connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connection_(mathematics) "Connection (mathematics)").

The term _differential_ has also been adopted in homological algebra and algebraic topology, because of the role the exterior derivative plays in de Rham cohomology: in a [cochain complex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cochain_complex "Cochain complex")![Image 122: {\displaystyle (C_{\bullet },d_{\bullet }),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/39a0618ff2da22715c4fc9b4693589dfac92489a) the maps (or _coboundary operators_) _d i_ are often called differentials. Dually, the boundary operators in a chain complex are sometimes called _codifferentials_.

The properties of the differential also motivate the algebraic notions of a _[derivation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivation\_(abstract\_algebra) "Derivation (abstract algebra)")_ and a _[differential algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential\_algebra "Differential algebra")_.

So, for example, the portion of calculus dealing with taking derivatives (i.e., differentiation), is known as differential calculus.

The word "differential" also has a more technical meaning in the theory of differential k-forms as a so-called one-form.
