Look up _**[brane](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/brane "wiktionary:brane")**_ in Wiktionary, the free dictionary.

In [string theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/String_theory "String theory") and related theories (such as [supergravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supergravity "Supergravity")), a **brane** is a physical object that generalizes the notion of a zero-[dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension "Dimension")[point particle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_particle "Point particle"), a one-dimensional [string](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/String_(physics) "String (physics)"), or a two-dimensional membrane to higher-dimensional objects. Branes are [dynamical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynamical_system "Dynamical system") objects which can propagate through [spacetime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spacetime "Spacetime") according to the rules of [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics"). They have [mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass "Mass") and can have other attributes such as [charge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charge_(physics) "Charge (physics)").

Mathematically, branes can be represented within [categories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category_(mathematics) "Category (mathematics)"), and are studied in [pure mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pure_mathematics "Pure mathematics") for insight into [homological mirror symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homological_mirror_symmetry "Homological mirror symmetry") and [noncommutative geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noncommutative_geometry "Noncommutative geometry").

The word "brane" originated in 1987 as a contraction of "[membrane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Membrane "Membrane")".[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brane#cite_note-1)

A point particle is a 0-brane, of dimension zero; a string, named after vibrating [musical strings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/String_(music) "String (music)"), is a 1-brane; a membrane, named after [vibrating membranes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acoustic_membrane "Acoustic membrane") such as [drumheads](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drumhead "Drumhead"), is a 2-brane.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brane#cite_note-2) The corresponding object of arbitrary dimension _p_ is called a _p_-brane, a term coined by [M. J. Duff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michael_Duff_(physicist) "Michael Duff (physicist)")_et al._ in 1988.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brane#cite_note-3)

A _p_-brane sweeps out a (_p_+1)-dimensional volume in spacetime called its **worldvolume**. Physicists often study [fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(physics) "Field (physics)") analogous to the [electromagnetic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_field "Electromagnetic field"), which live on the worldvolume of a brane.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brane#cite_note-4)

Open strings attached to a pair of [D-branes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D-brane "D-brane")

In [string theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/String_theory "String theory"), a [string](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/String_(physics) "String (physics)") may be open (forming a segment with two endpoints) or closed (forming a closed loop). [D-branes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D-brane "D-brane") are an important class of branes that arise when one considers open strings. As an open string propagates through spacetime, its endpoints are required to lie on a D-brane. The letter "D" in D-brane refers to the [Dirichlet boundary condition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dirichlet_boundary_condition "Dirichlet boundary condition"), which the D-brane satisfies.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brane#cite_note-5)

One crucial point about D-branes is that the dynamics on the D-brane worldvolume is described by a [gauge theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauge_theory "Gauge theory"), a kind of highly symmetric physical theory which is also used to describe the behavior of elementary particles in the [standard model of particle physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_model_of_particle_physics "Standard model of particle physics"). This connection has led to important insights into gauge theory and [quantum field theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_field_theory "Quantum field theory"). For example, it led to the discovery of the [AdS/CFT correspondence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AdS/CFT_correspondence "AdS/CFT correspondence"), a theoretical tool that physicists use to translate difficult problems in gauge theory into more mathematically tractable problems in string theory.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brane#cite_note-6)

Categorical description
-----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Brane&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Categorical description")]

Mathematically, branes can be described using the notion of a [category](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category_(mathematics) "Category (mathematics)").[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brane#cite_note-7) This is a mathematical structure consisting of _objects_, and for any pair of objects, a set of _[morphisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morphisms "Morphisms")_ between them. In most examples, the objects are mathematical structures (such as [sets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_(mathematics) "Set (mathematics)"), [vector spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_spaces "Vector spaces"), or [topological spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_spaces "Topological spaces")) and the morphisms are [functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)") between these structures.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brane#cite_note-8) One can likewise consider categories where the objects are D-branes and the morphisms between two branes ![Image 3: {\displaystyle \alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b79333175c8b3f0840bfb4ec41b8072c83ea88d3) and ![Image 4: {\displaystyle \beta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7ed48a5e36207156fb792fa79d29925d2f7901e8) are [states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wavefunction "Wavefunction") of open strings stretched between ![Image 5: {\displaystyle \alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b79333175c8b3f0840bfb4ec41b8072c83ea88d3) and ![Image 6: {\displaystyle \beta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7ed48a5e36207156fb792fa79d29925d2f7901e8).[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brane#cite_note-9)

[![Image 7: Visualization of a complex mathematical surface with many convolutions and self intersections.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Calabi_yau.jpg/250px-Calabi_yau.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Calabi_yau.jpg)

A cross section of a [Calabi–Yau manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calabi%E2%80%93Yau_manifold "Calabi–Yau manifold")

In one version of string theory known as the [topological B-model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_string_theory "Topological string theory"), the D-branes are [complex submanifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_manifold "Complex manifold") of certain six-dimensional shapes called [Calabi–Yau manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calabi%E2%80%93Yau_manifolds "Calabi–Yau manifolds"), together with additional data that arise physically from having [charges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charge_(physics) "Charge (physics)") at the endpoints of strings.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brane#cite_note-10) Intuitively, one can think of a submanifold as a surface embedded inside of a Calabi–Yau manifold, although submanifolds can also exist in dimensions different from two.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brane#cite_note-11) In mathematical language, the category having these branes as its objects is known as the [derived category](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derived_category "Derived category") of [coherent sheaves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coherent_sheaf "Coherent sheaf") on the Calabi–Yau.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brane#cite_note-12) In another version of string theory called the [topological A-model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_string_theory "Topological string theory"), the D-branes can again be viewed as submanifolds of a Calabi–Yau manifold. Roughly speaking, they are what mathematicians call [special Lagrangian submanifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_Lagrangian_submanifold "Special Lagrangian submanifold").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brane#cite_note-13) This means, among other things, that they have half the dimension of the space in which they sit, and they are length-, area-, or volume-minimizing.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brane#cite_note-14) The category having these branes as its objects is called the [Fukaya category](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fukaya_category "Fukaya category").[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brane#cite_note-15)

The derived category of coherent sheaves is constructed using tools from [complex geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_geometry "Complex geometry"), a branch of mathematics that describes geometric shapes in [algebraic terms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra "Algebra") and solves geometric problems using [algebraic equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_equation "Algebraic equation").[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brane#cite_note-16) On the other hand, the Fukaya category is constructed using [symplectic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symplectic_geometry "Symplectic geometry"), a branch of mathematics that arose from studies of [classical physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_physics "Classical physics"). Symplectic geometry studies spaces equipped with a [symplectic form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symplectic_form "Symplectic form"), a mathematical tool that can be used to compute [area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area "Area") in two-dimensional examples.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brane#cite_note-17)

The [homological mirror symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homological_mirror_symmetry "Homological mirror symmetry") conjecture of [Maxim Kontsevich](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxim_Kontsevich "Maxim Kontsevich") states that the derived category of coherent sheaves on one Calabi–Yau manifold is equivalent in a certain sense to the Fukaya category of a completely different Calabi–Yau manifold.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brane#cite_note-18) This equivalence provides an unexpected bridge between two branches of geometry, namely complex and symplectic geometry.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brane#cite_note-19)
