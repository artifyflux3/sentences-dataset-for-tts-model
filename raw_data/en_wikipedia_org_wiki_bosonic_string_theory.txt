**Bosonic string theory** is the original version of [string theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/String_theory "String theory"), developed in the late 1960s. It is so called because it contains only [bosons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boson "Boson") in the spectrum.

In the 1980s, [supersymmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supersymmetry "Supersymmetry") was discovered in the context of string theory, and a new version of string theory called [superstring theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superstring_theory "Superstring theory") (supersymmetric string theory) became the real focus. Nevertheless, bosonic string theory remains a very useful model to understand many general features of [perturbative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perturbative "Perturbative") string theory, and many theoretical difficulties of superstrings can actually already be found in the context of bosonic strings.

Problems
--------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bosonic_string_theory&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Problems")]

Although bosonic string theory has many attractive features, it falls short as a viable [physical model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_model "Physical model") in two significant areas.

First, it predicts only the existence of [bosons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bosons "Bosons") whereas many physical particles are [fermions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermions "Fermions").

Second, it predicts the existence of a mode of the string with [imaginary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imaginary_number "Imaginary number") mass, implying that the theory has an instability to a process known as "[tachyon condensation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tachyon_condensation "Tachyon condensation")".

In addition, bosonic string theory in a general spacetime dimension displays inconsistencies due to the [conformal anomaly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conformal_anomaly "Conformal anomaly"). But, as was first noticed by [Claud Lovelace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Claud_Lovelace "Claud Lovelace"),[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bosonic_string_theory#cite_note-PR-1) in a spacetime of 26 dimensions (25 dimensions of space and one of time), the [critical dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Critical_dimension "Critical dimension") for the theory, the anomaly cancels. This high dimensionality is not necessarily a problem for string theory, because it can be formulated in such a way that along the 22 excess dimensions spacetime is folded up to form a small [torus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torus "Torus") or other compact manifold. This would leave only the familiar four dimensions of spacetime visible to low energy experiments. The existence of a critical dimension where the anomaly cancels is a general feature of all string theories.

Types of bosonic strings
------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bosonic_string_theory&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Types of bosonic strings")]

There are four possible bosonic string theories, depending on whether [open strings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/String_(physics)#Closed_and_open_strings "String (physics)") are allowed and whether strings have a specified [orientation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orientability#Orientability_of_differentiable_manifolds "Orientability"). A theory of open strings must also include closed strings, because open strings can be thought of as having their endpoints fixed on a [D25-brane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D-brane "D-brane") that fills all of spacetime. A specific orientation of the string means that only interaction corresponding to an [orientable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orientability "Orientability")[worldsheet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Worldsheet "Worldsheet") are allowed (e.g., two strings can only merge with equal orientation). A sketch of the spectra of the four possible theories is as follows:

| Bosonic string theory | Non-positive M 2{\displaystyle M^{2}}![Image 43: {\displaystyle M^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ae9c3e3aa1aec0e135b5d342181d9ee7f9814f87) states |
| --- | --- |
| Open and closed, oriented | tachyon, [graviton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graviton "Graviton"), [dilaton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dilaton "Dilaton"), massless antisymmetric tensor |
| Open and closed, unoriented | tachyon, graviton, dilaton |
| Closed, oriented | tachyon, graviton, dilaton, antisymmetric tensor, [U(1)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U(1) "U(1)")[vector boson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_boson "Vector boson") |
| Closed, unoriented | tachyon, graviton, dilaton |

Note that all four theories have a negative energy tachyon (M 2=−1 α′{\displaystyle M^{2}=-{\frac {1}{\alpha '}}}![Image 44: {\displaystyle M^{2}=-{\frac {1}{\alpha '}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/24f68640ea5bc03a91cbf22f1f39dacb28eba35e)) and a massless graviton.

The rest of this article applies to the closed, oriented theory, corresponding to borderless, orientable worldsheets.

Mathematics
-----------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bosonic_string_theory&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Mathematics")]

### Path integral perturbation theory

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bosonic_string_theory&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Path integral perturbation theory")]

Bosonic string theory can be said[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bosonic_string_theory#cite_note-2) to be defined by the [path integral quantization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Path_integral_formulation "Path integral formulation") of the [Polyakov action](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyakov_action "Polyakov action"):

I 0[g,X]=T 8 π∫M d 2 ξ g g m n∂m x μ∂n x ν G μ ν(x){\displaystyle I_{0}[g,X]={\frac {T}{8\pi }}\int _{M}d^{2}\xi {\sqrt {g}}g^{mn}\partial _{m}x^{\mu }\partial _{n}x^{\nu }G_{\mu \nu }(x)}![Image 45: {\displaystyle I_{0}[g,X]={\frac {T}{8\pi }}\int _{M}d^{2}\xi {\sqrt {g}}g^{mn}\partial _{m}x^{\mu }\partial _{n}x^{\nu }G_{\mu \nu }(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ec86e41babcae6c6ab47e0d5dd06f409a09f4df5)
x μ(ξ){\displaystyle x^{\mu }(\xi )}![Image 46: {\displaystyle x^{\mu }(\xi )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3bbf3d7e8f8e65d574de2f55e0495cfac762803e) is the field on the [worldsheet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Worldsheet "Worldsheet") describing the most embedding of the string in 25 +1 spacetime; in the Polyakov formulation, g{\displaystyle g}![Image 47: {\displaystyle g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3556280e66fe2c0d0140df20935a6f057381d77) is not to be understood as the induced metric from the embedding, but as an independent dynamical field. G{\displaystyle G}![Image 48: {\displaystyle G}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5f3c8921a3b352de45446a6789b104458c9f90b) is the metric on the target spacetime, which is usually taken to be the [Minkowski metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minkowski_metric "Minkowski metric") in the perturbative theory. Under a [Wick rotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wick_rotation "Wick rotation"), this is brought to a Euclidean metric G μ ν=δ μ ν{\displaystyle G_{\mu \nu }=\delta _{\mu \nu }}![Image 49: {\displaystyle G_{\mu \nu }=\delta _{\mu \nu }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af65f80a0e317163024cf083c24cedec17509846). M is the worldsheet as a [topological manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_manifold "Topological manifold") parametrized by the ξ{\displaystyle \xi }![Image 50: {\displaystyle \xi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e0b461aaf61091abd5d2c808931c48b8ff9647db) coordinates. T{\displaystyle T}![Image 51: {\displaystyle T}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ec7200acd984a1d3a3d7dc455e262fbe54f7f6e0) is the string tension and related to the Regge slope as T=1 2 π α′{\displaystyle T={\frac {1}{2\pi \alpha '}}}![Image 52: {\displaystyle T={\frac {1}{2\pi \alpha '}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b64096a89cbecb3877b9453b78141c51514b8196).

I 0{\displaystyle I_{0}}![Image 53: {\displaystyle I_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/893d08e90ea73781dc133414d661529d0651ca80) has [diffeomorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffeomorphism_invariance "Diffeomorphism invariance") and [Weyl invariance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weyl_transformation "Weyl transformation"). Weyl symmetry is broken upon quantization ([Conformal anomaly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conformal_anomaly "Conformal anomaly")) and therefore this action has to be supplemented with a counterterm, along with a hypothetical purely topological term, proportional to the [Euler characteristic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler_characteristic "Euler characteristic"):

I=I 0+λ χ(M)+μ 0 2∫M d 2 ξ g{\displaystyle I=I_{0}+\lambda \chi (M)+\mu _{0}^{2}\int _{M}d^{2}\xi {\sqrt {g}}}![Image 54: {\displaystyle I=I_{0}+\lambda \chi (M)+\mu _{0}^{2}\int _{M}d^{2}\xi {\sqrt {g}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e0c7b8266c5ca0baf6fc4da8310ea659b3b65699)
The explicit breaking of Weyl invariance by the counterterm can be cancelled away in the [critical dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Critical_dimension "Critical dimension") 26.

Physical quantities are then constructed from the (Euclidean) [partition function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partition_function_(quantum_field_theory) "Partition function (quantum field theory)") and [N-point function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Correlation_function_(quantum_field_theory) "Correlation function (quantum field theory)"):

Z=∑h=0∞∫D g m n D X μ N exp⁡(−I[g,X]){\displaystyle Z=\sum _{h=0}^{\infty }\int {\frac {{\mathcal {D}}g_{mn}{\mathcal {D}}X^{\mu }}{\mathcal {N}}}\exp(-I[g,X])}![Image 55: {\displaystyle Z=\sum _{h=0}^{\infty }\int {\frac {{\mathcal {D}}g_{mn}{\mathcal {D}}X^{\mu }}{\mathcal {N}}}\exp(-I[g,X])}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/408aee3735e2a27d069ca0a2dd646525769b0d0e)⟨V i 1(k 1 μ)⋯V i p(k p μ)⟩=∑h=0∞∫D g m n D X μ N exp⁡(−I[g,X])V i 1(k 1 μ)⋯V i p(k p μ){\displaystyle \left\langle V_{i_{1}}(k_{1}^{\mu })\cdots V_{i_{p}}(k_{p}^{\mu })\right\rangle =\sum _{h=0}^{\infty }\int {\frac {{\mathcal {D}}g_{mn}{\mathcal {D}}X^{\mu }}{\mathcal {N}}}\exp(-I[g,X])V_{i_{1}}(k_{1}^{\mu })\cdots V_{i_{p}}(k_{p}^{\mu })}![Image 56: {\displaystyle \left\langle V_{i_{1}}(k_{1}^{\mu })\cdots V_{i_{p}}(k_{p}^{\mu })\right\rangle =\sum _{h=0}^{\infty }\int {\frac {{\mathcal {D}}g_{mn}{\mathcal {D}}X^{\mu }}{\mathcal {N}}}\exp(-I[g,X])V_{i_{1}}(k_{1}^{\mu })\cdots V_{i_{p}}(k_{p}^{\mu })}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f3f4a8f6392cefd08b4a3e41e97b10207ece8003)[![Image 57](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Sum_over_genera.png/250px-Sum_over_genera.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Sum_over_genera.png)

The perturbative series is expressed as a sum over topologies, indexed by the genus.

The discrete sum is a sum over possible topologies, which for euclidean bosonic orientable closed strings are compact orientable [Riemannian surfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_manifold "Riemannian manifold") and are thus identified by a genus h{\displaystyle h}![Image 58: {\displaystyle h}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b26be3e694314bc90c3215047e4a2010c6ee184a). A normalization factor N{\displaystyle {\mathcal {N}}}![Image 59: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {N}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b7551c7bed2cd2ee83e10536d157c94a5f8f72fd) is introduced to compensate overcounting from symmetries. While the computation of the partition function corresponds to the [cosmological constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmological_constant "Cosmological constant"), the N-point function, including p{\displaystyle p}![Image 60: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) vertex operators, describes the scattering amplitude of strings.

The symmetry group of the action actually reduces drastically the integration space to a finite dimensional manifold. The g{\displaystyle g}![Image 61: {\displaystyle g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3556280e66fe2c0d0140df20935a6f057381d77) path-integral in the partition function is _a priori_ a sum over possible Riemannian structures; however, [quotienting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quotient_space_(topology) "Quotient space (topology)") with respect to Weyl transformations allows us to only consider [conformal structures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conformal_structure "Conformal structure"), that is, equivalence classes of metrics under the identifications of metrics related by

g′(ξ)=e σ(ξ)g(ξ){\displaystyle g'(\xi )=e^{\sigma (\xi )}g(\xi )}![Image 62: {\displaystyle g'(\xi )=e^{\sigma (\xi )}g(\xi )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f29a88cfc8806661f5b7df03bbbf346508ee8cf2)
Since the world-sheet is two dimensional, there is a 1-1 correspondence between conformal structures and [complex structures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_manifold "Complex manifold"). One still has to quotient away diffeomorphisms. This leaves us with an integration over the space of all possible complex structures modulo diffeomorphisms, which is simply the [moduli space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moduli_space "Moduli space") of the given topological surface, and is in fact a finite-dimensional [complex manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_manifold "Complex manifold"). The fundamental problem of perturbative bosonic strings therefore becomes the parametrization of Moduli space, which is non-trivial for genus h≥4{\displaystyle h\geq 4}![Image 63: {\displaystyle h\geq 4}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/133c52a33660ce81b14994868db1169da614faa7).

#### h = 0

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bosonic_string_theory&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: h = 0")]

At tree-level, corresponding to genus 0, the cosmological constant vanishes: Z 0=0{\displaystyle Z_{0}=0}![Image 64: {\displaystyle Z_{0}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/834cb9e9a2c2b0f8c0d6d6ade030849160c2267c).

The four-point function for the scattering of four tachyons is the Shapiro-Virasoro amplitude:

Where k{\displaystyle k}![Image 66: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40) is the total momentum and s{\displaystyle s}![Image 67: {\displaystyle s}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/01d131dfd7673938b947072a13a9744fe997e632), t{\displaystyle t}![Image 68: {\displaystyle t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/65658b7b223af9e1acc877d848888ecdb4466560), u{\displaystyle u}![Image 69: {\displaystyle u}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3e6bb763d22c20916ed4f0bb6bd49d7470cffd8) are the [Mandelstam variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mandelstam_variables "Mandelstam variables").
