In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), a **fractal dimension** is a term invoked in the science of geometry to provide a rational statistical index of [complexity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complexity "Complexity") detail in a [pattern](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pattern "Pattern"). A [fractal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal "Fractal") pattern changes with the [scale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scaling_(geometry) "Scaling (geometry)") at which it is measured. It is also a measure of the [space-filling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space-filling_curve "Space-filling curve") capacity of a pattern and tells how a fractal scales differently, in a fractal (non-integer) dimension.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-Falconer-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-space_filling-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-vicsek-3)

The main idea of "fractured" [dimensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hausdorff_dimension "Hausdorff dimension") has a long history in mathematics, but the term itself was brought to the fore by [Benoit Mandelbrot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benoit_Mandelbrot "Benoit Mandelbrot") based on [his 1967 paper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/How_Long_Is_the_Coast_of_Britain%3F_Statistical_Self-Similarity_and_Fractional_Dimension "How Long Is the Coast of Britain? Statistical Self-Similarity and Fractional Dimension") on [self-similarity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-similarity "Self-similarity") in which he discussed _fractional dimensions_.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-coastline-4) In that paper, Mandelbrot cited previous work by [Lewis Fry Richardson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lewis_Fry_Richardson "Lewis Fry Richardson") describing the counter-intuitive notion that a coastline's measured length changes with the length of the measuring stick used (see [Fig.1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#coastline)). In terms of that notion, the fractal dimension of a coastline quantifies how the number of scaled measuring sticks required to measure the coastline changes with the scale applied to the stick.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-Mandelbrot1983-5) There are several formal mathematical definitions of fractal dimension that build on this basic concept of change in detail with change in scale, see [§Examples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#Examples) below.

Ultimately, the term _fractal dimension_ became the phrase with which Mandelbrot himself became most comfortable with respect to encapsulating the meaning of the word _fractal_, a term he created. After several iterations over years, Mandelbrot settled on this use of the language: "to use _fractal_ without a pedantic definition, to use _fractal dimension_ as a generic term applicable to _all_ the variants".[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-6)

One non-trivial example is the fractal dimension of a [Koch snowflake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Koch_snowflake "Koch snowflake"). It has a [topological dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_dimension "Topological dimension") of 1, but it is by no means [rectifiable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arc_length "Arc length"): the length of the curve between any two points on the Koch snowflake is [infinite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arc_length#Curves_with_infinite_length "Arc length"). No small piece of it is line-like, but rather it is composed of an infinite number of segments joined at different angles. The fractal dimension of a curve can be explained intuitively by thinking of a fractal line as an object too detailed to be one-dimensional, but too simple to be two-dimensional.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-7) Therefore, its dimension might best be described not by its usual topological dimension of 1 but by its fractal dimension, which is often a number between one and two; in the case of the Koch snowflake, it is approximately 1.2619.

Figure 2. A 32-segment quadric fractal scaled and viewed through boxes of different sizes. The pattern illustrates [self-similarity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-similarity "Self-similarity"). The theoretical fractal dimension for this fractal is 5/3 ≈ 1.67; its empirical fractal dimension from [box counting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Box_counting "Box counting") analysis is ±1%[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-empirical_fractal_dimension-8) using [fractal analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_analysis "Fractal analysis") software.

A **[fractal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal "Fractal") dimension** is an index for characterizing [fractal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal "Fractal") patterns or [sets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_(mathematics) "Set (mathematics)") by quantifying their [complexity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complexity "Complexity") as a ratio of the change in detail to the change in scale.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-Mandelbrot1983-5): 1  Several types of fractal dimension can be measured theoretically and [empirically](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_analysis "Fractal analysis") (see [Fig.2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#32seg)).[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-vicsek-3)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-medicine-9) Fractal dimensions are used to characterize a broad spectrum of objects ranging from the abstract[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-Falconer-1)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-vicsek-3) to practical phenomena, including turbulence,[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-Mandelbrot1983-5): 97–104  river networks,: 246–247  urban growth,[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-10)[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-11) human physiology,[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-doi10.1364/boe.1.000268-12)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-doi10.1007/s11682-008-9057-9-13) medicine,[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-medicine-9) and market trends.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-time_series-14) The essential idea of _fractional_ or _fractal_[dimensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hausdorff_dimension "Hausdorff dimension") has a long history in mathematics that can be traced back to the 1600s,[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-Mandelbrot1983-5): 19 [[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-classics-15) but the terms _fractal_ and _fractal dimension_ were coined by mathematician Benoit Mandelbrot in 1975.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-Falconer-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-space_filling-2)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-Mandelbrot1983-5)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-medicine-9)[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-time_series-14)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-Mandelbrot_quote-16)

_Fractal dimensions_ were first applied as an index characterizing complicated geometric forms for which the details seemed more important than the gross picture.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-Mandelbrot_quote-16) For sets describing ordinary geometric shapes, the theoretical fractal dimension equals the set's familiar [Euclidean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_geometry "Euclidean geometry") or [topological dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_dimension "Topological dimension"). Thus, it is 0 for sets describing points (0-dimensional sets); 1 for sets describing lines (1-dimensional sets having length only); 2 for sets describing surfaces (2-dimensional sets having length and width); and 3 for sets describing volumes (3-dimensional sets having length, width, and height). But this changes for fractal sets. If the theoretical fractal dimension of a set exceeds its topological dimension, the set is considered to have fractal geometry.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-Mandelbrot_Chaos-17)

Unlike topological dimensions, the fractal index can take non-[integer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer") values,[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-18) indicating that a set fills its space qualitatively and quantitatively differently from how an ordinary geometrical set does.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-Falconer-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-space_filling-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-vicsek-3) For instance, a curve with a fractal dimension very near to 1, say 1.10, behaves quite like an ordinary line, but a curve with fractal dimension 1.9 winds convolutedly through space very nearly like a surface. Similarly, a surface with fractal dimension of 2.1 fills space very much like an ordinary surface, but one with a fractal dimension of 2.9 folds and flows to fill space rather nearly like a volume.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-Mandelbrot_Chaos-17): 48 [[notes 1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-19) This general relationship can be seen in the two images of [fractal curves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_curves "Fractal curves") in [Fig.2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#32seg) and [Fig.3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#kline)– the 32-segment contour in Fig.2, convoluted and space-filling, has a fractal dimension of 1.67, compared to the perceptibly less complex Koch curve in Fig.3, which has a fractal dimension of approximately 1.2619.

[![Image 5: a Koch curve animation](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Blueklineani2.gif/250px-Blueklineani2.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Blueklineani2.gif)

Figure 3. The [Koch curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Koch_curve "Koch curve") is a classic [iterated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iteration "Iteration") fractal curve. It is made by starting from a line segment, and then iteratively scaling each segment by 1/3 into 4 new pieces laid end to end with 2 middle pieces leaning toward each other along an equilateral triangle, so that the whole new segment spans the distance between the endpoints of the original segment. The animation only shows a few iterations, but the theoretical curve is scaled in this way infinitely.

The relationship of an increasing fractal dimension with space-filling might be taken to mean fractal dimensions measure density, but that is not so; the two are not strictly correlated.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-empirical_fractal_dimension-8) Instead, a fractal dimension measures complexity, a concept related to certain key features of fractals: [self-similarity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-similarity "Self-similarity") and [detail or irregularity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#characteristics "Fractal").[[notes 2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-20) These features are evident in the two examples of fractal curves. Both are curves with [topological dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_dimension "Topological dimension") of 1, so one might hope to be able to measure their length and derivative in the same way as with ordinary curves. But we cannot do either of these things, because fractal curves have complexity in the form of self-similarity and detail that ordinary curves lack.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-Mandelbrot1983-5) The _self-similarity_ lies in the infinite scaling, and the _detail_ in the defining elements of each set. The [length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arc_length "Arc length") between any two points on these curves is infinite, no matter how close together the two points are, which means that it is impossible to approximate the length of such a curve by partitioning the curve into many small segments.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-von_Koch_paper-21) Every smaller piece is composed of an infinite number of scaled segments that look exactly like the first iteration. These are not [rectifiable curves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectifiable_curve "Rectifiable curve"), meaning that they cannot be measured by being broken down into many segments approximating their respective lengths. They cannot be meaningfully characterized by finding their lengths and derivatives. However, their fractal dimensions can be determined, which shows that both fill space more than ordinary lines but less than surfaces, and allows them to be compared in this regard.

The two fractal curves described above show a type of self-similarity that is exact with a repeating unit of detail that is readily visualized. This sort of structure can be extended to other spaces (e.g., a [fractal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_fractals_by_Hausdorff_dimension "List of fractals by Hausdorff dimension") that extends the Koch curve into 3D space has a theoretical _D_ = 2.5849). However, such neatly countable complexity is only one example of the self-similarity and detail that are present in fractals.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-vicsek-3)[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-time_series-14) The example of the coast line of Britain, for instance, exhibits self-similarity of an approximate pattern with approximate scaling.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-Mandelbrot1983-5): 26  Overall, [fractals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal "Fractal") show several [types and degrees of self-similarity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal#characteristics "Fractal") and detail that may not be easily visualized. These include, as examples, [strange attractors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Attractor "Attractor"), for which the detail has been described as in essence, smooth portions piling up,[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-Mandelbrot_Chaos-17): 49  the [Julia set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Julia_set "Julia set"), which can be seen to be complex swirls upon swirls, and heart rates, which are patterns of rough spikes repeated and scaled in time.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-heart-22) Fractal complexity may not always be resolvable into easily grasped units of detail and scale without complex analytic methods, but it is still quantifiable through fractal dimensions.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-Mandelbrot1983-5): 197, 262

The terms _fractal dimension_ and _fractal_ were coined by Mandelbrot in 1975,[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-Mandelbrot_quote-16) about a decade after he published his paper on self-similarity in the coastline of Britain. Various historical authorities credit him with also synthesizing centuries of complicated theoretical mathematics and engineering work and applying them in a new way to study complex geometries that defied description in usual linear terms.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-classics-15)[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-Gordon-23)[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-MacTutor-24) The earliest roots of what Mandelbrot synthesized as the fractal dimension have been traced clearly back to writings about nondifferentiable, infinitely self-similar functions, which are important in the mathematical definition of fractals, around the time that [calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus "Calculus") was discovered in the mid-1600s.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-Mandelbrot1983-5): 405  There was a lull in the published work on such functions for a time after that, then a renewal starting in the late 1800s with the publishing of mathematical functions and sets that are today called canonical fractals (such as the eponymous works of [von Koch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helge_von_Koch "Helge von Koch"),[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-von_Koch_paper-21)[Sierpiński](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sierpi%C5%84ski "Sierpiński"), and [Julia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaston_Julia "Gaston Julia")), but at the time of their formulation were often considered antithetical mathematical "monsters".[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-classics-15)[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-MacTutor-24) These works were accompanied by perhaps the most pivotal point in the development of the concept of a fractal dimension through the work of [Hausdorff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Felix_Hausdorff "Felix Hausdorff") in the early 1900s who defined a "fractional" [dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hausdorff_dimension "Hausdorff dimension") that has come to be named after him and is frequently invoked in defining modern [fractals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractals "Fractals").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-coastline-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-Mandelbrot1983-5): 44 [[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-Mandelbrot_Chaos-17)[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-Gordon-23)

The mathematical definition of fractal dimension can be derived by observing and then generalizing the effect of traditional dimension on measurement-changes under scaling.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-fil-26) For example, say you have a line and a measuring-stick of equal length. Now shrink the stick to 1/3 its size; you can now fit 3 sticks into the line. Similarly, in two dimensions, say you have a square and an identical "measuring-square". Now shrink the measuring-square's side to 1/3 its length; you can now fit 3^2 = 9 measuring-squares into the square. Such familiar scaling relationships obey equation(**[1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#math_1)**), where ![Image 16: {\displaystyle \varepsilon }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a30c89172e5b88edbd45d3e2772c7f5e562e5173) is the scaling factor, ![Image 17: {\displaystyle D}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f34a0c600395e5d4345287e21fb26efd386990e6) the dimension, and ![Image 18: {\displaystyle N}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5e3890c981ae85503089652feb48b191b57aae3) the resulting number of units (sticks, squares, etc.) in the measured object:

![Image 19: {\displaystyle N=\varepsilon ^{-D}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6c041812528ca449e172723e6da27d380aa5c3c7)1

In the line example, the dimension ![Image 20: {\displaystyle D=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/60927044e2d847d6ebf9949cb99244d0455069a2) because there are ![Image 21: {\displaystyle N=3}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4ab951d779fcdeea3ec188bb5c73518c46b19de) units when the scaling factor ![Image 22: {\displaystyle \varepsilon =1/3}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3918f4634bad80e2acc536083c245c0900f58e74). In the square example, ![Image 23: {\displaystyle D=2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1ac67967f48b21969a5fc88b231b13e11394278d) because ![Image 24: {\displaystyle N=9}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d730ee6bf71188959cced8eee34e210001f4a27f) when ![Image 25: {\displaystyle \varepsilon =1/3}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3918f4634bad80e2acc536083c245c0900f58e74).

[![Image 26: A fractal contour of a koch snowflake](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/KochFlake.svg/250px-KochFlake.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:KochFlake.svg)

The first four [iterations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iteration "Iteration") of the [Koch snowflake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Koch_snowflake "Koch snowflake"), which has a [Hausdorff dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hausdorff_dimension "Hausdorff dimension") of approximately 1.2619.

Fractal dimension generalizes traditional dimension in that it can be fractional, but it has exactly the same relationship with scaling that traditional dimension does; in fact, it is derived by simply rearranging equation (**[1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#math_1)**):

![Image 27: {\displaystyle D=-\log _{\varepsilon }N=-{\frac {\log N}{\log \varepsilon }}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f16208ea6540f2b5e787b9c453332586a96a9600)2

![Image 28: {\displaystyle D}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f34a0c600395e5d4345287e21fb26efd386990e6) can be thought of as the power of the scaling factor of an object's measure given some scaling of its "radius".

For example, the [Koch snowflake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Koch_snowflake "Koch snowflake") has ![Image 29: {\displaystyle D=1.26185\ldots }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0f5c163a735b86e3fd0bb1a046ea80aa1088b647), indicating that lengthening its radius grows its measure faster than if it were a one-dimensional shape (such as a polygon), but slower than if it were a two-dimensional shape (such as a filled polygon).[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-vicsek-3)

Of note, images shown in this page are not true fractals because the scaling described by ![Image 30: {\displaystyle D}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f34a0c600395e5d4345287e21fb26efd386990e6) cannot continue past the point of their smallest component, a pixel. However, the theoretical patterns that the images represent have no discrete pixel-like pieces, but rather are composed of an [infinite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinity "Infinity") number of infinitely scaled segments and do indeed have the claimed fractal dimensions.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-Mandelbrot1983-5)[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-fil-26)

_D_ is not a unique descriptor
------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Fractal_dimension&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: D is not a unique descriptor")]

Figure 6. Two [L-systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L-systems "L-systems") branching fractals that are made by producing 4 new parts for every 1/3 [scaling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self_similarity "Self similarity"), thus having the same theoretical ![Image 32: {\displaystyle D}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f34a0c600395e5d4345287e21fb26efd386990e6) as the Koch curve, and for which the empirical [box counting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Box_counting "Box counting")![Image 33: {\displaystyle D}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f34a0c600395e5d4345287e21fb26efd386990e6) has been demonstrated with 2% accuracy.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-empirical_fractal_dimension-8)

As is the case with dimensions determined for lines, squares, and cubes, fractal dimensions are general descriptors that do not uniquely define patterns.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-fil-26)[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-Vicsek-27) The value of _D_ for the Koch fractal discussed above, for instance, quantifies the pattern's inherent scaling, but does not uniquely describe nor provide enough information to reconstruct it. Many fractal structures or patterns could be constructed that have the same scaling relationship but are dramatically different from the Koch curve, as is illustrated in [Fig.6](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#statistical_koch_like_scaling_image).

For examples of how fractal patterns can be constructed, see [Fractal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal "Fractal"), [Sierpinski triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sierpinski_triangle "Sierpinski triangle"), [Mandelbrot set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mandelbrot_set "Mandelbrot set"), [Diffusion-limited aggregation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffusion-limited_aggregation "Diffusion-limited aggregation"), [L-system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L-system "L-system").

Fractal surface structures
--------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Fractal_dimension&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Fractal surface structures")]

[![Image 34](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Wiki_df_figure.png/500px-Wiki_df_figure.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Wiki_df_figure.png)

Figure 7: Illustration of increasing surface fractality. Self-affine surfaces (left) and corresponding surface profiles (right) showing increasing fractal dimension _D f_.

The concept of fractality is applied increasingly in the field of [surface science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_Science "Surface Science"), providing a bridge between surface characteristics and functional properties.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-28) Numerous surface descriptors are used to interpret the structure of nominally flat surfaces, which often exhibit self-affine features across multiple length-scales. Mean [surface roughness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_roughness "Surface roughness"), usually denoted R A, is the most commonly applied surface descriptor, however, numerous other descriptors including mean slope, [root-mean-square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Root-mean-square "Root-mean-square") roughness (R RMS) and others are regularly applied. It is found, however, that many physical surface phenomena cannot readily be interpreted with reference to such descriptors, thus fractal dimension is increasingly applied to establish correlations between surface structure in terms of scaling behavior and performance.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-29) The fractal dimensions of surfaces have been employed to explain and better understand phenomena in areas of [contact mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contact_mechanics "Contact mechanics"),[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-30)[frictional behavior](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friction "Friction"),[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-31)[electrical contact resistance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrical_contact_resistance "Electrical contact resistance")[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-32) and [transparent conducting oxides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transparent_conducting_oxide "Transparent conducting oxide").[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-33)

The concept of fractal dimension described in this article is a basic view of a complicated construct. The examples discussed here were chosen for clarity, and the scaling unit and ratios were known ahead of time. In practice, however, fractal dimensions can be determined using techniques that approximate scaling and detail from [limits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limit_(mathematics) "Limit (mathematics)") estimated from [regression lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regression_line "Regression line") over [log–log plots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Log%E2%80%93log_plot "Log–log plot") of size vs scale. Estimating from real-world data
-------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Fractal_dimension&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Estimating from real-world data")]

Many real-world phenomena exhibit limited or statistical fractal properties and fractal dimensions that have been estimated from [sampled](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sample_(statistics) "Sample (statistics)") data using computer-based [fractal analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_analysis "Fractal analysis") techniques. Practically, measurements of fractal dimension are affected by various methodological issues and are sensitive to numerical or experimental noise and limitations in the amount of data. Nonetheless, the field is rapidly growing as estimated fractal dimensions for statistically self-similar phenomena may have many practical applications in various fields, including astronomy,[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-37) acoustics,[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov-38)[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-39) architecture,[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-40)[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-41)[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-42) geology and earth sciences,[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-43) diagnostic imaging,[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-44)[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-45)[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-46) ecology,[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-47) electrochemical processes,[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-48) image analysis,[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-49)[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-50)[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-51)[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-52) biology and medicine,[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-53)[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-54)[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-55) neuroscience,[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-10.12688/f1000research.6590.1-56)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-doi10.1007/s11682-008-9057-9-13)[network analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension_on_networks "Fractal dimension on networks"), physiology,[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-doi10.1364/boe.1.000268-12) physics,[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-57)[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-58) and [Riemann zeta zeros](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_hypothesis "Riemann hypothesis").[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-Shanker-59) Fractal dimension estimates have also been shown to correlate with [Lempel–Ziv complexity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lempel%E2%80%93Ziv_complexity "Lempel–Ziv complexity") in real-world data sets from psychoacoustics and neuroscience.[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-10.12688/f1000research.6590.1-56)[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov-38)

An alternative to a direct measurement is considering a mathematical model that resembles formation of a real-world fractal object. In this case, a validation can also be done by comparing other than fractal properties implied by the model, with measured data. In [colloidal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colloid "Colloid") physics, systems composed of particles with various fractal dimensions arise. To describe these systems, it is convenient to speak about a [distribution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability_distribution "Probability distribution") of fractal dimensions and, eventually, a time evolution of the latter: a process that is driven by a complex interplay between [aggregation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_aggregation "Particle aggregation") and [coalescence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coalescence_(chemistry) "Coalescence (chemistry)").[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension#cite_note-60)
