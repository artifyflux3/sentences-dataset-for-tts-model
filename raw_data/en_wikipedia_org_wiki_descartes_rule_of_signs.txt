In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), **Descartes' rule of signs**, described by [René Descartes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Descartes "René Descartes") in his _[La Géométrie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/La\_G%C3%A9om%C3%A9trie "La Géométrie")_, counts the [roots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Root_of_a_function "Root of a function") of a [polynomial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial "Polynomial") by examining sign changes in its coefficients. The number of positive real roots is at most the number of sign changes in the sequence of the polynomial's coefficients (omitting zero coefficients), and the difference between the root count and the sign change count is always even. In particular, when the number of sign changes is zero or one, then there are exactly zero or one positive roots.

A [linear fractional transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_fractional_transformation "Linear fractional transformation") of the variable makes it possible to use the rule of signs to count roots in any interval. This is the basic idea of [Budan's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Budan%27s_theorem "Budan's theorem") and the [Budan–Fourier theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Budan%E2%80%93Fourier_theorem "Budan–Fourier theorem"). Repeated division of an interval in two results in a set of disjoint intervals, each containing one root, and together listing all the roots. This approach is used in the fastest algorithms today for computer computation of real roots of polynomials (see [real-root isolation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real-root_isolation "Real-root isolation")).

Descartes himself used the transformation _x_ → −_x_ for using his rule for getting information of the number of negative roots.

Descartes' rule of signs
------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Descartes%27_rule_of_signs&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Descartes' rule of signs")]

The rule states that if the nonzero terms of a single-variable [polynomial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial "Polynomial") with [real](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number")[coefficients](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coefficient "Coefficient") are ordered by descending variable exponent, then the number of positive [roots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Root_of_a_function "Root of a function") of the polynomial is either equal to the number of sign changes between consecutive (nonzero) coefficients, or is less than it by an even number. A root of [multiplicity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiple_root "Multiple root")k is counted as k roots.

In particular, if the number of sign changes is zero or one, the number of positive roots equals the number of sign changes.

As a [corollary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corollary "Corollary") of the rule, the number of negative roots is the number of sign changes after multiplying the coefficients of odd-power terms by −1, or fewer than it by an even number. This procedure is equivalent to substituting the negation of the variable for the variable itself. For example, the negative roots of ![Image 1: {\displaystyle ax^{3}+bx^{2}+cx+d}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7245863fa4abf24283dac1d221a034a327f9fe4d) are the positive roots of

![Image 2: {\displaystyle a(-x)^{3}+b(-x)^{2}+c(-x)+d=-ax^{3}+bx^{2}-cx+d.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6a0a00a35b97b18c16e243f85b2539fd96bf53ad)
Thus, applying Descartes' rule of signs to this polynomial gives the maximum number of negative roots of the original polynomial.

### Example: cubic polynomial

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Descartes%27_rule_of_signs&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Example: cubic polynomial")]

The polynomial

![Image 3: {\displaystyle f(x)=+x^{3}+x^{2}-x-1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0526e098a9fdf123e559810cdafc4f9946bd8217)
has one sign change between the second and third terms, as the sequence of signs is (+, +, −, −). Therefore, it has exactly one positive root. To find the number of negative roots, change the signs of the coefficients of the terms with odd exponents, i.e., apply Descartes' rule of signs to the polynomial ![Image 4: {\displaystyle f(-x)=-x^{3}+x^{2}+x-1.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fca1b700a70f204381c403c23c651b4c251f9474)

This polynomial has two sign changes, as the sequence of signs is (−, +, +, −), meaning that this second polynomial has two or zero positive roots; thus the original polynomial has two or zero negative roots.

In fact, the [factorization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Factorization_of_polynomials "Factorization of polynomials") of the first polynomial is

![Image 5: {\displaystyle f(x)=(x+1)^{2}(x-1),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6c397255a988d917ec487860927d931412c01d19)
so the roots are −1 (twice) and +1 (once).

The factorization of the second polynomial is

![Image 6: {\displaystyle f(-x)=-(x-1)^{2}(x+1).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/045212ca2979a76fd85b80e3ab6aad2e9e6ed786)
So here, the roots are +1 (twice) and −1 (once), the negation of the roots of the original polynomial.

The following is a rough outline of a proof.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Descartes'_rule_of_signs#cite_note-1) First, some preliminary definitions:

*   Let ![Image 7: {\displaystyle Z(f)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6a6775db9a50c99bdd14d0f066ccd36887eef226) be the number of strictly positive roots (counting multiplicity).

With these, we can formally state Descartes' rule as follows:

**Theorem**—The number of strictly positive roots (counting multiplicity) of ![Image 8: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) is equal to the number of sign changes in the coefficients of ![Image 9: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61), minus a nonnegative even number.

If ![Image 10: {\displaystyle b_{0}>0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b129a679cb37eb097685cc8df3b1318f8203dc1f), then we can divide the polynomial by ![Image 11: {\displaystyle x^{b_{0}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/778cec59ba2158dc5d5517dc2f6f4a9162ec4d7b), which would not change its number of strictly positive roots. Thus WLOG, let ![Image 12: {\displaystyle b_{0}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7ecd3a5bc848cc6a60f444229ad2781c71ed528a).

**Lemma**—If ![Image 13: {\displaystyle a_{n}a_{0}>0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/09d3a36367c6e2072362521a1c6cbd3dd237813c), then ![Image 14: {\displaystyle Z(f)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6a6775db9a50c99bdd14d0f066ccd36887eef226) is even. If ![Image 15: {\displaystyle a_{0}a_{n}<0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b581c94571ae8118a1bf13102adb2d5018e1db1), then ![Image 16: {\displaystyle Z(f)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6a6775db9a50c99bdd14d0f066ccd36887eef226) is odd.

**Proof of Lemma**

![Image 17: {\displaystyle f(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/202945cce41ecebb6f643f31d119c514bec7a074) starts at ![Image 18: {\displaystyle f(0)=a_{0}>0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b19bdda2ea6147885b00f3683ac8af55fbdc780f) and ends at ![Image 19: {\displaystyle f(+\infty )=+\infty >0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cbdb5dc2f075985b5d299f2954b47da452226ef4), so it must cross the positive x-axis an even number of times (each of which contributes an odd number of roots), and glance (without crossing) the positive x-axis an arbitrary number of times (each of which contributes an even number of roots).

The other case is similar.

**Proof of main theorem**

From the lemma, it follows that ![Image 20: {\displaystyle Z(f)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6a6775db9a50c99bdd14d0f066ccd36887eef226) and ![Image 21: {\displaystyle V(f)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a716ecee96f695ac9d7451ab772abd188102cccc) always have the same parity. It remains to show ![Image 22: {\displaystyle Z(f)\leq V(f)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b655f4240d77a28391dea9f171a56a6d52561934).

We induct on ![Image 23: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b). If ![Image 24: {\displaystyle n=0,1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e5b1a46f1c6cb1874214e180fecee01136aba394), then it is obvious. Now assume ![Image 25: {\displaystyle n\geq 2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e6bf67f9d06ca3af619657f8d20ee1322da77174).

By induction hypothesis, ![Image 26: {\displaystyle Z(f')=V(f')-2s}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ed1db230dcd664574d83f8961987426d43257356) for some integer ![Image 27: {\displaystyle s\geq 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/33eb0ad28ebb73afb1521fca14885b6301012792).

By [Rolle's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rolle%27s_theorem "Rolle's theorem"), there exists at least one positive root of ![Image 28: {\displaystyle f'}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/258eaada38956fb69b8cb1a2eef46bcb97d3126b) between any two different positive roots of ![Image 29: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61). Also, any ![Image 30: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40) -multiple positive root of ![Image 31: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) is a ![Image 32: {\displaystyle k-1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/21363ebd7038c93aae93127e7d910fc1b2e2c745) -multiple root of ![Image 33: {\displaystyle f'}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/258eaada38956fb69b8cb1a2eef46bcb97d3126b). Thus ![Image 34: {\displaystyle Z(f')\geq Z(f)-1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a50612f64e1fe0f2fcfa12a5134786bc10845630).

If ![Image 35: {\displaystyle a_{0}a_{1}>0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9b4b45f2bf605d51b8545afd19799854c84b2340), then ![Image 36: {\displaystyle V(f')=V(f)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2a6f976701f930ec9097959e497594f8009fcf3b), else ![Image 37: {\displaystyle V(f')=V(f)-1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a41d521703daa7176f8698ad4e1b225e57b0370c). In both cases, ![Image 38: {\displaystyle V(f')\leq V(f)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/40109cec2861f1b24cf15c8f83c0c36d696839bf)

Together, we have

![Image 39: {\displaystyle Z(f)\leq Z(f')+1=V(f')-2s+1\leq V(f)-2s+1\leq V(f)+1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/86967df5700b9a27b5d700747dd460c1f3005016) Further, since ![Image 40: {\displaystyle Z(f)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6a6775db9a50c99bdd14d0f066ccd36887eef226) and ![Image 41: {\displaystyle V(f)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a716ecee96f695ac9d7451ab772abd188102cccc) have the same parity, we have ![Image 42: {\displaystyle Z(f)\leq V(f)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b655f4240d77a28391dea9f171a56a6d52561934).

Any _n_ th degree polynomial has exactly _n_ roots in the [complex plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_plane "Complex plane"), if counted according to multiplicity. So if _f_(_x_) is a polynomial with real coefficients which does not have a root at 0 (that is a polynomial with a nonzero constant term) then the _minimum_ number of nonreal roots is equal to

![Image 43: {\displaystyle n-(p+q),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ba87331831d5538275f6a95603c55392c0adc936)
where _p_ denotes the maximum number of positive roots, _q_ denotes the maximum number of negative roots (both of which can be found using Descartes' rule of signs), and _n_ denotes the degree of the polynomial.

### Example: some zero coefficients and nonreal roots

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Descartes%27_rule_of_signs&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Example: some zero coefficients and nonreal roots")]

The polynomial

![Image 44: {\displaystyle f(x)=x^{3}-1,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7f0bb47b7efe618bce7334cf7b1239041ceccd79)
has one sign change; so the maximum number of positive real roots is one. As

![Image 45: {\displaystyle f(-x)=-x^{3}-1,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ed910f4d6de2d3c4493c569e1badabb2184439ac)
has no sign change, the original polynomial has no negative real roots. So the minimum number of nonreal roots is

![Image 46: {\displaystyle 3-(1+0)=2.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f7134b2ecb2bc05bfbe7be67db5b94e035ce5f3a)
Since nonreal roots of a polynomial with real coefficients must occur in conjugate pairs, it means that _x_ 3 − 1 has exactly two nonreal roots and one real root, which is positive.

The [even](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Even_number "Even number") difference between the number of sign changes and the number of positive roots is positive when the polynomial has pairs of [conjugate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_conjugate "Complex conjugate") nonreal roots with positive [real parts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_part "Real part"). Thus, if the polynomial is known to have all real roots, Descartes' rule allows one to find the exact number of positive and negative roots. Since it is easy to determine the multiplicity of zero as a root, the sign of all roots can be determined in this case.

If the real polynomial _P_ has _k_ real positive roots counted with multiplicity, then for every _a_> 0 there are at least _k_ changes of sign in the sequence of coefficients of the [Taylor series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taylor_series "Taylor series") of the function _e_ _ax_ _P_(_x_). For sufficiently large _a_, there are exactly _k_ such changes of sign.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Descartes'_rule_of_signs#cite_note-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Descartes'_rule_of_signs#cite_note-3)

In the 1970s [Askold Khovanskii](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Askold_Khovanskii "Askold Khovanskii") developed the theory of _[fewnomials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fewnomial "Fewnomial")_ that generalises Descartes' rule.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Descartes'_rule_of_signs#cite_note-4) The rule of signs can be thought of as stating that the number of real roots of a polynomial is dependent on the polynomial's complexity, and that this complexity is proportional to the number of monomials it has, not its degree. Khovanskiǐ showed that this holds true not just for polynomials but for algebraic combinations of many [transcendental functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transcendental_function "Transcendental function"), the so-called [Pfaffian functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pfaffian_function "Pfaffian function").
