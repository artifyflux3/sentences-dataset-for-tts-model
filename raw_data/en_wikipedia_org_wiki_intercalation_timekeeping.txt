Look up _**[intercalation](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/intercalation "wiktionary:intercalation")**_ or _**[embolism](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/embolism "wiktionary:embolism")**_ in Wiktionary, the free dictionary.

**Intercalation** or **embolism** in timekeeping is the insertion of a [leap day](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leap_day "Leap day"), [week](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leap_week_calendar "Leap week calendar"), or month into some [calendar years](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calendar_year "Calendar year") to make the calendar follow the seasons or moon phases.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intercalation_(timekeeping)#cite_note-1)[Lunisolar calendars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunisolar_calendar "Lunisolar calendar") may require intercalations of days or months.

Solar calendars
---------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Intercalation_(timekeeping)&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Solar calendars")]

Further information: [Intercalary month (Egypt)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intercalary_month_(Egypt) "Intercalary month (Egypt)")

The solar or [tropical year](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tropical_year "Tropical year") does not have a whole number of days (it is about 365.24 days), but a [calendar year](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calendar_year "Calendar year") must have a whole number of days. The most common way to reconcile the two is to vary the number of days in the calendar year.

In solar calendars, this is done by adding an extra day ("leap day" or "intercalary day") to a [common year](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Common_year "Common year") of 365 days, about once every four years, creating a [leap year](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leap_year "Leap year") that has 366 days ([Julian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Julian_calendar "Julian calendar"), [Gregorian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gregorian_calendar "Gregorian calendar") and [Indian national calendars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_national_calendar "Indian national calendar")).

The [Decree of Canopus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decree_of_Canopus "Decree of Canopus"), issued by the pharaoh [Ptolemy III Euergetes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ptolemy_III_Euergetes "Ptolemy III Euergetes") of [Ancient Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Egypt "Ancient Egypt") in 239 BC, decreed a solar leap day system; an Egyptian leap year was not adopted until 25 BC, when the Roman Emperor [Augustus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Augustus "Augustus") instituted a reformed [Alexandrian calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coptic_calendar "Coptic calendar").

In the [Julian calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Julian_calendar "Julian calendar"), as well as in the [Gregorian calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gregorian_calendar "Gregorian calendar"), which improved upon it, intercalation is done by adding an extra day to February in each leap year. In the Julian calendar this was done every four years. In the Gregorian, years divisible by 100 but not 400 were exempted in order to improve accuracy. Thus, 2000 was a leap year; 1700, 1800, and 1900 were not.

Epagomenal[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intercalation_(timekeeping)#cite_note-2) days are days within a solar calendar that are outside any regular month. Usually five epagomenal days are included within every year ([Egyptian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egyptian_calendar "Egyptian calendar"), [Coptic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coptic_calendar "Coptic calendar"), [Ethiopian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethiopian_calendar "Ethiopian calendar"), Mayan [Haab'](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haab%27 "Haab'") and [French Republican Calendars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Republican_Calendar "French Republican Calendar")), but a sixth epagomenal day is intercalated every four years in some (Coptic, Ethiopian and French Republican calendars).

The [Solar Hijri calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_Hijri_calendar "Solar Hijri calendar"), used in Iran, is based on solar calculations and is similar to the Gregorian calendar in its structure, and hence the intercalation, with the exception that its [epoch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epoch "Epoch") is the [Hijrah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hijrah "Hijrah").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intercalation_(timekeeping)#cite_note-3)

The [Bahá'í calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%27%C3%AD_calendar "Bahá'í calendar") includes enough epagomenal days (usually 4 or 5) before the last month (علاء, _ʿalāʾ_) to ensure that the following year starts on the [March equinox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/March_equinox "March equinox"). These are known as the [Ayyám-i-Há](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ayy%C3%A1m-i-H%C3%A1 "Ayyám-i-Há").

Lunisolar calendars
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Intercalation_(timekeeping)&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Lunisolar calendars")]

The solar year does not have a whole number of lunar months (it is about 365/29.5 = 12.37 [lunations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunar_month#Synodic_month "Lunar month")), so a lunisolar calendar must have a variable number of months per year. Regular years have 12 months, but embolismic years insert a 13th "intercalary" or "leap" month or "embolismic" month every second or third year. Whether to insert an intercalary month in a given year may be determined using regular cycles such as the 19-year [Metonic cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metonic_cycle "Metonic cycle") ([Hebrew calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hebrew_calendar "Hebrew calendar") and in the [determination of Easter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computus "Computus")) or using calculations of lunar phases ([Hindu lunisolar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hindu_calendar "Hindu calendar") and [Chinese calendars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_calendar "Chinese calendar")). The [Buddhist calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buddhist_calendar "Buddhist calendar") adds both an intercalary day and month on a usually regular cycle.

Lunar calendars
---------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Intercalation_(timekeeping)&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Lunar calendars")]

Further information: [Nasi'](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nasi%27 "Nasi'") and [Islamic calendar §Astronomical considerations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamic_calendar#Astronomical_considerations "Islamic calendar")

In principle, lunar calendars do not employ intercalation because they do not seek to synchronise with the seasons, and the motion of the moon is astronomically predictable. But religious lunar calendars rely on actual observation.

The [Lunar Hijri calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamic_calendar "Islamic calendar"), the purely lunar calendar observed by most of Islam, depends on actual observation of the first crescent of the moon and thus has no intercalation. Each month still has either 29 or 30 days, but due to the variable method of observations employed, there is usually no discernible order in the sequencing of 29- or 30-day month lengths. Traditionally, the first day of each month is the day (beginning at sunset) of the first sighting of the _hilal_ (crescent moon) shortly after sunset. If the _hilal_ is not observed immediately after the 29th day of a month (either because clouds block its view or because the western sky is still too bright when the moon sets), then the day that begins at that sunset is the 30th.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intercalation_(timekeeping)#cite_note-4)

The [tabular Islamic calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tabular_Islamic_calendar "Tabular Islamic calendar"), used in Iran, has 12 lunar months that usually alternate between 30 and 29 days every year, but an intercalary day is added to the last month of the year 12 times in a 33-year cycle. Some historians also linked the pre-Islamic practice of [Nasi'](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nasi%27 "Nasi'") to intercalation.

Leap seconds
------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Intercalation_(timekeeping)&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Leap seconds")]

The [International Earth Rotation and Reference Systems Service](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Earth_Rotation_and_Reference_Systems_Service "International Earth Rotation and Reference Systems Service") can insert or remove [leap seconds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leap_second "Leap second") from the last day of any month (June and December are preferred). These are sometimes described as intercalary.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intercalation_(timekeeping)#cite_note-5)

Other uses
----------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Intercalation_(timekeeping)&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Other uses")]

[ISO 8601](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO_8601 "ISO 8601") includes a [specification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO_week_date "ISO week date") for a 52/53-week year. Any year that has 53 Thursdays has 53 weeks; this extra week may be regarded as intercalary, i.e. a [leap week](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leap_week_calendar "Leap week calendar").

The _xiuhpōhualli_ (year count) system of the [Aztec calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aztec_calendar "Aztec calendar") had five intercalary days after the eighteenth and final month, the _[nēmontēmi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/N%C4%93mont%C4%93mi "Nēmontēmi")_, in which the people fasted and reflected on the past year.
