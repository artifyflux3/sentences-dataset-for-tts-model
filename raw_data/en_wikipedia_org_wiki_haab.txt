The **Haab º** (Mayan pronunciation:[[haÀê…ì]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/Mayan "Help:IPA/Mayan")) is part of the [Maya calendric system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maya_calendar "Maya calendar"). It was a [365-day calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/365-day_calendar "365-day calendar") used by many of the pre-Columbian cultures of [Mesoamerica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mesoamerica "Mesoamerica").

The Haab º comprises eighteen months of twenty days each, plus an additional period of five days ("nameless days") at the end of the year known as _Wayeb'_ (or _Uayeb_ in 16th-century orthography).

Bricker (1982) estimates that the Haab º was first used around 500 BCE with a starting point of the [winter solstice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Winter_solstice "Winter solstice").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haab%CA%BC#cite_note-2)

The Haab º month names are most commonly referred to by their names in colonial-era [Yucatec (Yukatek)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yucatec_Maya_language "Yucatec Maya language"). In sequence, these (in the revised orthography[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haab%CA%BC#cite_note-3)) are as seen on the right: Each day in the Haab º calendar was identified by a day number within the month followed by the name of the month. Day numbers began with a glyph translated as the "seating of" a named month, which is usually regarded as day 0 of that month, although a minority treat it as day 20 of the month preceding the named month. In the latter case, the seating of Pop is day 5 of Wayeb º. For the majority, the first day of the year was Seating Pop. This was followed by 1 Pop, 2 Pop ... 19 Pop, Seating Wo, 1 Wo and so on.

Inscriptions on The Temple of the Cross at Palenque shows clearly that the Maya were aware of the true length of the year, even though they did not employ the use of leap days in their system of calculations generally. J. Eric Thompson[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haab%CA%BC#cite_note-4) wrote that the Maya knew of the drift between the Haab º and the solar year and that they made "calculations as to the rate at which the error accumulated, but these were merely noted as corrections they were not used to change the calendar."

### Five unlucky days

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Haab%CA%BC&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Five unlucky days")]

The five nameless days at the end of the calendar, called Wayeb º, was thought to be a dangerous time. Foster (2002) writes "During Wayeb, portals between the mortal realm and the Underworld dissolved. No boundaries prevented the ill-intending deities from causing disasters." To ward off these evil spirits, the Mayans had customs and rituals they practised during Wayeb º. For example, the Mayans would not leave their homes and wash their hair.
