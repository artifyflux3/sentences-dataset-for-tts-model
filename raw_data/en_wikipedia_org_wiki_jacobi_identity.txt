In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), the **Jacobi identity** is a property of a [binary operation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_operation "Binary operation") that describes how the order of evaluation, the placement of parentheses in a multiple product, affects the result of the operation. By contrast, for operations with the [associative property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Associativity "Associativity"), any order of evaluation gives the same result (parentheses in a multiple product are not needed). The identity is named after the German mathematician [Carl Gustav Jacob Jacobi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Gustav_Jacob_Jacobi "Carl Gustav Jacob Jacobi"). He derived the Jacobi identity for [Poisson brackets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poisson_bracket "Poisson bracket") in his 1862 paper on differential equations.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacobi_identity#cite_note-Poisson1809-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacobi_identity#cite_note-Hawkins1991-2)

The [cross product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product "Cross product")![Image 1: {\displaystyle a\times b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/65b420244850c1a22be4c326f91e146db8b037f0) and the [Lie bracket operation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_algebra "Lie algebra")![Image 2: {\displaystyle [a,b]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9c4b788fc5c637e26ee98b45f89a5c08c85f7935) both satisfy the Jacobi identity.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacobi_identity#cite_note-3) In [analytical mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytical_mechanics "Analytical mechanics"), the Jacobi identity is satisfied by the [Poisson brackets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poisson_bracket "Poisson bracket"). In [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics"), it is satisfied by operator [commutators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutator#Ring_theory "Commutator") on a [Hilbert space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space "Hilbert space") and equivalently in the [phase space formulation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phase_space_formulation "Phase space formulation") of quantum mechanics by the [Moyal bracket](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moyal_bracket "Moyal bracket").

Let ![Image 3: {\displaystyle +}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fe6ef363cd19902d1a7a71fb1c8b21e8ede52406) and ![Image 4: {\displaystyle \times }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0ffafff1ad26cbe49045f19a67ce532116a32703) be two [binary operations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_operation "Binary operation"), and let ![Image 5: {\displaystyle 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2aae8864a3c1fec9585261791a809ddec1489950) be the [neutral element](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutral_element "Neutral element") for ![Image 6: {\displaystyle +}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fe6ef363cd19902d1a7a71fb1c8b21e8ede52406). The **Jacobi identity** is

![Image 7: {\displaystyle x\times (y\times z)\ +\ y\times (z\times x)\ +\ z\times (x\times y)\ =\ 0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c297956e402995708088d0abbe1c34a72da43f98)
Notice the pattern in the variables on the left side of this identity. In each subsequent expression of the form ![Image 8: {\displaystyle a\times (b\times c)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d48bea774c3c60817f327f396d1e3c083c53bc78), the variables ![Image 9: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4), ![Image 10: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d) and ![Image 11: {\displaystyle z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf368e72c009decd9b6686ee84a375632e11de98) are permuted according to the cycle ![Image 12: {\displaystyle x\mapsto y\mapsto z\mapsto x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daec050c8e16626953a89857b939eb13bf4b381). Alternatively, we may observe that the ordered triples ![Image 13: {\displaystyle (x,y,z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/22a8c93372e8f8b6e24d523bd5545aed3430baf4), ![Image 14: {\displaystyle (y,z,x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/56eb650ab735791b22f46a92fbf0eca1081feba8) and ![Image 15: {\displaystyle (z,x,y)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c17b62988c89d83ac7d092a30e0e287ce00b3425), are the [even permutations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permutation#Parity_of_a_permutation "Permutation") of the ordered triple ![Image 16: {\displaystyle (x,y,z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/22a8c93372e8f8b6e24d523bd5545aed3430baf4).

Commutator bracket form
-----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Jacobi_identity&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Commutator bracket form")]

The simplest informative example of a [Lie algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_algebra "Lie algebra") is constructed from the (associative) ring of ![Image 17: {\displaystyle n\times n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/59d2b4cb72e304526cf5b5887147729ea259da78) matrices, which may be thought of as infinitesimal motions of an _n_-dimensional vector space. The × operation is the [commutator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutator "Commutator"), which measures the failure of commutativity in matrix multiplication. Instead of ![Image 18: {\displaystyle X\times Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1613c1ff4b6fbfb6c80a8da83e90ad28f0ab3483), the Lie bracket notation is used:

![Image 19: {\displaystyle [X,Y]=XY-YX.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/649cef3a00d4024de15414fd6a37627571c9609e)
In that notation, the Jacobi identity is:

![Image 20: {\displaystyle [X,[Y,Z]]+[Y,[Z,X]]+[Z,[X,Y]]\ =\ 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7d77e8c02499d0bc3b2bdfe1a144ba07f6cc5048)
That is easily checked by computation.

More generally, if **A** is an associative algebra and **V** is a subspace of **A** that is closed under the bracket operation: ![Image 21: {\displaystyle [X,Y]=XY-YX}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/838f73010b4f791eeaf245317fb4b6e07c45d741) belongs to **V** for all ![Image 22: {\displaystyle X,Y\in V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/296fd06438d09cae93b3c2b879d5304b5fe7940e), the Jacobi identity continues to hold on **V**.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacobi_identity#cite_note-4) Thus, if a binary operation ![Image 23: {\displaystyle [X,Y]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/94470b44d283fde62130212956058ca6b727da37) satisfies the Jacobi identity, it may be said that it behaves as if it were given by ![Image 24: {\displaystyle XY-YX}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2d77f289bd8ea08d8990087aaaad8720e391c38d) in some associative algebra even if it is not actually defined that way.

Using the [antisymmetry property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anticommutativity "Anticommutativity")![Image 25: {\displaystyle [X,Y]=-[Y,X]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8c433e248c0c7e659ef3f2d9df64d8c7505630bc), the Jacobi identity may be rewritten as a modification of the [associative property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Associativity "Associativity"):

![Image 26: {\displaystyle [[X,Y],Z]=[X,[Y,Z]]-[Y,[X,Z]]~.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffe3847dca1561d17f643d4fd36a7851ff9c125d)
If ![Image 27: {\displaystyle [X,Z]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/366ab83f1ee0a471efb306249972deec6a5aad0f) is the action of the infinitesimal motion **X** on **Z**, that can be stated as:

> The action of _Y_ followed by _X_ (operator ![Image 28: {\displaystyle [X,[Y,\cdot \ ]]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ff0e9f50bf9ad3b7be2739a84cc0bf21337175fe)), minus the action of _X_ followed by _Y_ (operator ![Image 29: {\displaystyle ([Y,[X,\cdot \ ]]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ecebe6410bec49b9ed154b3d342a98ee11b73131)), is equal to the action of ![Image 30: {\displaystyle [X,Y]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/94470b44d283fde62130212956058ca6b727da37), (operator ![Image 31: {\displaystyle [[X,Y],\cdot \ ]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/40034a989220666e8f7cb0082d27133dad27cf6e)).

There is also a plethora of [graded Jacobi identities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_superalgebra#properties "Lie superalgebra") involving [anticommutators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anticommutator "Anticommutator")![Image 32: {\displaystyle \{X,Y\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2d8641d8086abfedadc49fc18a63419662acb184), such as:

![Image 33: {\displaystyle [\{X,Y\},Z]+[\{Y,Z\},X]+[\{Z,X\},Y]=0,\qquad [\{X,Y\},Z]+\{[Z,Y],X\}+\{[Z,X],Y\}=0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/815a6cdb80c32e53aeecd2ccccd07f4cb43dbeff)
Most common examples of the Jacobi identity come from the bracket multiplication ![Image 34: {\displaystyle [x,y]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1b7bd6292c6023626c6358bfd3943a031b27d663) on [Lie algebras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_algebra "Lie algebra") and [Lie rings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_ring "Lie ring"). The Jacobi identity is written as:

![Image 35: {\displaystyle [x,[y,z]]+[z,[x,y]]+[y,[z,x]]=0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a425004c6877ee5ecace799ca0396a3db1e4155b)
Because the bracket multiplication is [antisymmetric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anticommutativity "Anticommutativity"), the Jacobi identity admits two equivalent reformulations. Defining the [adjoint operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adjoint_representation_of_a_Lie_algebra "Adjoint representation of a Lie algebra")![Image 36: {\displaystyle \operatorname {ad} _{x}:y\mapsto [x,y]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9814bb34e60a706242e0a589bd22cbd90528f6fc), the identity becomes:

![Image 37: {\displaystyle \operatorname {ad} _{x}[y,z]=[\operatorname {ad} _{x}y,z]+[y,\operatorname {ad} _{x}z].}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68a463244ff920979eda9e6ce2d8f64c399d27e0)
Thus, the Jacobi identity for Lie algebras states that the action of any element on the algebra is a [derivation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivation_(abstract_algebra) "Derivation (abstract algebra)"). That form of the Jacobi identity is also used to define the notion of [Leibniz algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leibniz_algebra "Leibniz algebra").

Another rearrangement shows that the Jacobi identity is equivalent to the following identity between the operators of the adjoint representation:

![Image 38: {\displaystyle \operatorname {ad} _{[x,y]}=[\operatorname {ad} _{x},\operatorname {ad} _{y}].}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/41644db0d06bdb5555514ac8bf9e5f2bcd81dc3f)
There, the bracket on the left side is the operation of the original algebra, the bracket on the right is the commutator of the composition of operators, and the identity states that the ![Image 39: {\displaystyle \mathrm {ad} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4dfabb59497ffe0c094cac2720f0c6a67b33e205) map sending each element to its adjoint action is a [Lie algebra homomorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_algebra_homomorphism "Lie algebra homomorphism").
