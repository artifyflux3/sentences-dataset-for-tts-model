A **proton** is a stable [subatomic particle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subatomic_particle "Subatomic particle"), symbol p, [H+](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydron_(chemistry) "Hydron (chemistry)"), or 1 H+ with a positive [electric charge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_charge "Electric charge") of +1 _e_ ([elementary charge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementary_charge "Elementary charge")). Its mass is slightly less than the mass of a [neutron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron "Neutron") and approximately 1836 times the mass of an [electron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron "Electron") (the [proton-to-electron mass ratio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton-to-electron_mass_ratio "Proton-to-electron mass ratio")). Protons and neutrons, each with a mass of approximately one [dalton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dalton_(unit) "Dalton (unit)"), are jointly referred to as _[nucleons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleon "Nucleon")_ (particles present in atomic nuclei).

One or more protons are present in the [nucleus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_nucleus "Atomic nucleus") of every [atom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atom "Atom"). They provide the attractive electrostatic central force which binds the atomic electrons. The number of protons in the nucleus is the defining property of an element, and is referred to as the [atomic number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_number "Atomic number") (represented by the symbol _Z_). Since each [element](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_element "Chemical element") is identified by the number of protons in its nucleus, each element has its own atomic number, which determines the number of atomic electrons and consequently the chemical characteristics of the element.

The word _proton_ is [Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_language "Greek language") for "first", and the name was given to the hydrogen nucleus by [Ernest Rutherford](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ernest_Rutherford "Ernest Rutherford") in 1920. In previous years, Rutherford had discovered that the [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen") nucleus (known to be the lightest nucleus) could be extracted from the nuclei of [nitrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen "Nitrogen") by atomic collisions.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-Britannica-10) Protons were therefore a candidate to be a fundamental or [elementary particle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementary_particle "Elementary particle"), and hence a building block of nitrogen and all other heavier atomic nuclei.

Although protons were originally considered to be elementary particles, in the modern [Standard Model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model "Standard Model") of [particle physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_physics "Particle physics"), protons are known to be composite particles, containing three [valence quarks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Valence_quark "Valence quark"), and together with [neutrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron "Neutron") are now classified as [hadrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hadron "Hadron"). Protons are composed of two [up quarks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Up_quark "Up quark") of charge +⁠2/3⁠_e_ each, and one [down quark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Down_quark "Down quark") of charge −⁠1/3⁠_e_. The [rest masses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rest_mass "Rest mass") of quarks contribute only about 1% of a proton's mass.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-Mass-11) The remainder of a proton's mass is due to [quantum chromodynamics binding energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_chromodynamics_binding_energy "Quantum chromodynamics binding energy"), which includes the [kinetic energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_energy "Kinetic energy") of the quarks and the energy of the [gluon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gluon "Gluon") fields that bind the quarks together. The proton charge radius is around 0.841[fm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Femtometre "Femtometre") but two different kinds of measurements give slightly different values.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-PDG-2025-12)

At sufficiently low temperatures and kinetic energies, free protons will bind [electrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron "Electron") in any matter they traverse. Free protons are routinely used for accelerators for [proton therapy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton_therapy "Proton therapy") or various particle physics experiments, with the most powerful example being the [Large Hadron Collider](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Large_Hadron_Collider "Large Hadron Collider").

Unsolved problem in physics

How do the quarks and gluons carry the spin of protons?

Protons are [spin-⁠1/2⁠](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin-%C2%BD "Spin-½")[fermions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermion "Fermion") and are composed of three valence quarks,[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-Adair1989-13) making them [baryons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baryon "Baryon") (a sub-type of [hadrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hadron "Hadron")). The two [up quarks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Up_quark "Up quark") and one [down quark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Down_quark "Down quark") of a proton are held together by the [strong force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strong_interaction "Strong interaction"), mediated by [gluons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gluon "Gluon").[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-Cottingham1986-14): 21–22  A modern perspective has a proton composed of the valence quarks (up, up, down), the gluons, and transitory pairs of [sea quarks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sea_quark "Sea quark"). Protons have a positive charge distribution, which decays approximately exponentially, with a root mean square [charge radius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charge_radius "Charge radius") of about 0.8 fm.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-Basdevant2005-15)

Protons and [neutrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron "Neutron") are both [nucleons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleon "Nucleon"), which may be bound together by the [nuclear force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_force "Nuclear force") to form [atomic nuclei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_nuclei "Atomic nuclei"). The nucleus of the most common [isotope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotope "Isotope") of the [hydrogen atom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_atom "Hydrogen atom") (with the [chemical symbol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_symbol "Chemical symbol") "H") is a lone proton. The nuclei of the heavy hydrogen isotopes [deuterium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deuterium "Deuterium") and [tritium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tritium "Tritium") contain one proton bound to one and two neutrons, respectively. All other types of atomic nuclei are composed of two or more protons and various numbers of neutrons.

The concept of a hydrogen-like particle as a constituent of other atoms was developed over a long period. As early as 1815, [William Prout](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Prout "William Prout") proposed that all atoms are composed of hydrogen atoms (which he called "protyles"), based on a simplistic interpretation of early values of [atomic weights](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_weight "Atomic weight") (see [Prout's hypothesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prout%27s_hypothesis "Prout's hypothesis")), which was disproved when more accurate values were measured.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-Lecturer2006-16): 39–42

Proton detected in an [isopropanol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isopropanol "Isopropanol")[cloud chamber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cloud_chamber "Cloud chamber")

In 1886, [Eugen Goldstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eugen_Goldstein "Eugen Goldstein") discovered [canal rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canal_rays "Canal rays") (also known as anode rays) and showed that they were positively charged particles (ions) produced from gases. However, since particles from different gases had different values of [charge-to-mass ratio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charge-to-mass_ratio "Charge-to-mass ratio") (_q_/_m_), they could not be identified with a single particle, unlike the negative [electrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron "Electron") discovered by [J. J. Thomson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J._J._Thomson "J. J. Thomson"). [Wilhelm Wien](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilhelm_Wien "Wilhelm Wien") in 1898 identified the hydrogen ion as the particle with the highest charge-to-mass ratio in ionized gases.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-Wien1904-17)

Following the discovery of the atomic nucleus by [Ernest Rutherford](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ernest_Rutherford "Ernest Rutherford") in 1911, [Antonius van den Broek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antonius_van_den_Broek "Antonius van den Broek") proposed that the place of each element in the [periodic table](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Periodic_table "Periodic table") (its atomic number) is equal to its nuclear charge. This was confirmed experimentally by [Henry Moseley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry_Moseley "Henry Moseley") in 1913 using [X-ray spectra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray_spectroscopy "X-ray spectroscopy") (More details in [Atomic number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_number "Atomic number") under Moseley's 1913 experiment).

In 1919, after a long series of sporadic experiments interrupted by WWI, Rutherford discovered what he called [artificial disintegration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artificial_disintegration "Artificial disintegration") of [nitrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen "Nitrogen") atoms. Using [alpha particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_particles "Alpha particles") from [radium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radium "Radium") to strike air, Rutherford detected scintillation on a [zinc sulfide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zinc_sulfide "Zinc sulfide") screen at a distance, up to 28cm, well beyond the distance of alpha-particle range of travel but instead corresponding to the range of travel of hydrogen atoms. By 1920 he concluded that these hydrogen nuclei were a constituent part of the nitrogen nucleus. These hydrogen nuclei were soon called _protons_.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-Stuewer-1983-18): 23  This result is described as the discovery of protons.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-Petrucci2002-19)

Rutherford initially assumed that the alpha particle merely knocked a proton out of nitrogen, turning it into carbon. [Patrick Blackett](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patrick_Blackett "Patrick Blackett")'s cloud chamber images in 1925 demonstrated that the alpha particle was absorbed. If the alpha particle were not absorbed, then 3 charged particles, a negatively charged carbon, a proton, and an alpha particle, would be expected. The 3 charged particles would create three tracks in the cloud chamber, but only 2 tracks in the cloud chamber were observed. Blackett proposed that the alpha particle is absorbed by the nitrogen atom. Heavy oxygen (17 O), not carbon, was the product.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-20)[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-Stuewer-1983-18) This was the first reported [nuclear reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_reaction "Nuclear reaction"), 14 N + α → 17 O + p.

Rutherford knew hydrogen to be the simplest and lightest element and was influenced by [Prout's hypothesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prout%27s_hypothesis "Prout's hypothesis") that hydrogen was the building block of all elements. Discovery that the hydrogen nucleus is present in other nuclei as an elementary particle led Rutherford to give the hydrogen nucleus H+ a special name as a particle, since he suspected that hydrogen, the lightest element, contained only one of these particles. He named this new fundamental building block of the nucleus the _proton_, after the neuter singular of the Greek word for "first", πρῶτον. However, Rutherford also had in mind the word _protyle_ as used by Prout. Rutherford spoke at the [British Association for the Advancement of Science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Association_for_the_Advancement_of_Science "British Association for the Advancement of Science") at its [Cardiff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardiff "Cardiff") meeting beginning 24 August 1920.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-NatureScience-21) At the meeting, he was asked by [Oliver Lodge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oliver_Lodge "Oliver Lodge") for a new name for the positive hydrogen nucleus to avoid confusion with the neutral hydrogen atom. He initially suggested both _proton_ and _prouton_ (after Prout).[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-Romer1997-22) Rutherford later reported that the meeting had accepted his suggestion that the hydrogen nucleus be named the "proton", following Prout's word "protyle".[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-Footnote-23) The first use of the word "proton" in the scientific literature appeared in 1920.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-24)[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-Pais1986-25)

One or more bound protons are present in the nucleus of every atom. Free protons are found naturally in a number of situations in which energies or temperatures are high enough to separate them from electrons, for which they have some affinity. Free protons occur occasionally on Earth: [thunderstorms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thunderstorm "Thunderstorm") can produce protons with energies of up to several tens of [MeV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronvolt "Electronvolt").[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-Kohn2015-26)[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-Kohn2017-27) Free protons exist in [plasmas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasma_(physics) "Plasma (physics)") in which temperatures are too high to allow them to combine with [electrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron "Electron").[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-Ebeling2021-28) Free protons of high energy and velocity make up 90% of [cosmic rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_ray "Cosmic ray"), which propagate through the [interstellar medium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_medium "Interstellar medium").[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-goddard-2012-29) Free protons are [emitted directly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton_emission "Proton emission") from [atomic nuclei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_nucleus "Atomic nucleus") in some rare types of [radioactive decay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radioactive_decay "Radioactive decay").[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-30) Protons also result (along with electrons and [antineutrinos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antineutrino "Antineutrino")) from the [radioactive decay of free neutrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_neutron_decay "Free neutron decay"), which are unstable.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-Gonzalez-2021-31)

Unsolved problem in physics

Are protons fundamentally stable? Or do they decay with a finite lifetime as predicted by some extensions to the standard model?

The spontaneous decay of free protons has never been observed, and protons are therefore considered stable particles according to the Standard Model. However, some [grand unified theories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grand_unified_theory "Grand unified theory") (GUTs) of particle physics predict that [proton decay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton_decay "Proton decay") should take place with lifetimes between 10 31 and 10 36 years. Experimental searches have established lower bounds on the [mean lifetime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mean_lifetime "Mean lifetime") of a proton for various assumed decay products.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-Bucella1989-32)[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-Lee1995-33)[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-Kamioka-34)

Experiments at the [Super-Kamiokande](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Super-Kamiokande "Super-Kamiokande") detector in Japan gave lower limits for proton [mean lifetime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mean_lifetime "Mean lifetime") of 6.6×10 33 years for decay to an [antimuon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antimuon "Antimuon") and a neutral [pion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pion "Pion"), and 8.2×10 33 years for decay to a [positron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positron "Positron") and a neutral pion.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-Nishino2009-35) Another experiment at the [Sudbury Neutrino Observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sudbury_Neutrino_Observatory "Sudbury Neutrino Observatory") in Canada searched for [gamma rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamma_ray "Gamma ray") resulting from residual nuclei resulting from the decay of a proton from oxygen-16. This experiment was designed to detect decay to any product, and established a lower limit to a proton lifetime of 2.1×10 29 years.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-Ahmed2004-36)

However, protons are known to transform into [neutrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron "Neutron") through the process of [electron capture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_capture "Electron capture") (also called [inverse beta decay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverse_beta_decay "Inverse beta decay")). For free protons, this process does not occur spontaneously but only when energy is supplied. The equation is:

p+

 + [e−](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron "Electron") → [n](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron "Neutron") + [ν e](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_neutrino "Electron neutrino")
The process is reversible; neutrons can convert back to protons through [beta decay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beta_decay "Beta decay"), a common form of [radioactive decay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radioactive_decay "Radioactive decay"). In fact, a [free neutron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_neutron "Free neutron") decays this way, with a [mean lifetime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mean_lifetime "Mean lifetime") of about 15 minutes. A proton can also transform into a neutron through [beta plus decay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positron_emission "Positron emission") (β+ decay).

-------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Proton&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Quarks and the mass of a proton")]

In [quantum chromodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_chromodynamics "Quantum chromodynamics"), the modern theory of the nuclear force, most of the mass of protons and [neutrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron "Neutron") is explained by [special relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_relativity "Special relativity"). The mass of a proton is about 80–100 times greater than the sum of the rest masses of its three valence [quarks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quark "Quark"), while the [gluons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gluon "Gluon") have zero rest mass. The extra energy of the [quarks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quark "Quark") and [gluons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gluon "Gluon") in a proton, as compared to the rest energy of the quarks alone in the [QCD vacuum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/QCD_vacuum "QCD vacuum"), accounts for almost 99% of the proton's mass. The rest mass of a proton is, thus, the [invariant mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invariant_mass "Invariant mass") of the system of moving quarks and gluons that make up the particle, and, in such systems, even the energy of massless particles confined to a system is [still measured](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass%E2%80%93energy_equivalence#Composite_systems "Mass–energy equivalence") as part of the rest mass of the system.

Two terms are used in referring to the mass of the quarks that make up protons: _[current quark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Current\_quark "Current quark") mass_ refers to the mass of a quark by itself, while _[constituent quark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constituent\_quark "Constituent quark") mass_ refers to the current quark mass plus the mass of the [gluon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gluon "Gluon")[particle field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_field_theory "Quantum field theory") surrounding the quark.[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-Waston2004-41): 285–286 [[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-Smith2003-42): 150–151  These masses typically have very different values. The kinetic energy of the quarks that is a consequence of confinement is a contribution (see _[Mass in special relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass\_in\_special\_relativity "Mass in special relativity")_). Using [lattice QCD](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lattice_QCD "Lattice QCD") calculations, the contributions to the mass of the proton are the quark condensate (~9%, comprising the up and down quarks and a sea of virtual strange quarks), the quark kinetic energy (~32%), the gluon kinetic energy (~37%), and the anomalous gluonic contribution (~23%, comprising contributions from condensates of all quark flavors).[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-43)

The constituent quark model wavefunction for the proton is ![Image 10: {\displaystyle \mathrm {|p_{\uparrow }\rangle ={\tfrac {1}{\sqrt {18}}}\left(2|u_{\uparrow }d_{\downarrow }u_{\uparrow }\rangle +2|u_{\uparrow }u_{\uparrow }d_{\downarrow }\rangle +2|d_{\downarrow }u_{\uparrow }u_{\uparrow }\rangle -|u_{\uparrow }u_{\downarrow }d_{\uparrow }\rangle -|u_{\uparrow }d_{\uparrow }u_{\downarrow }\rangle -|u_{\downarrow }d_{\uparrow }u_{\uparrow }\rangle -|d_{\uparrow }u_{\downarrow }u_{\uparrow }\rangle -|d_{\uparrow }u_{\uparrow }u_{\downarrow }\rangle -|u_{\downarrow }u_{\uparrow }d_{\uparrow }\rangle \right)} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a1b2a951b566a7a37c81aca4dcb3b9c76bf935f6)

The internal dynamics of protons are complicated, because they are determined by the quarks' exchanging gluons, and interacting with various vacuum condensates. [Lattice QCD](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lattice_QCD "Lattice QCD") provides a way of calculating the mass of a proton directly from the theory to any accuracy, in principle. The most recent calculations[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-SMAnswers-44)[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-Fodor2008-45) claim that the mass is determined to better than 4% accuracy, even to 1% accuracy (see Figure S5 in Dürr _et al._[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-Fodor2008-45)). These claims are still controversial, because the calculations cannot yet be done with quarks as light as they are in the real world. This means that the predictions are found by a process of [extrapolation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extrapolation "Extrapolation"), which can introduce systematic errors.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-Perdrisat2007-46) It is hard to tell whether these errors are controlled properly, because the quantities that are compared to experiment are the masses of the [hadrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hadron "Hadron"), which are known in advance.

These recent calculations are performed by massive supercomputers, and, as noted by Boffi and Pasquini: "a detailed description of the nucleon structure is still missing because ... long-distance behavior requires a nonperturbative and/or numerical treatment..."[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-Boffi2007-47) More conceptual approaches to the structure of protons are: the [topological soliton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skyrmion "Skyrmion") approach originally due to [Tony Skyrme](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tony_Skyrme "Tony Skyrme") and the more accurate [AdS/QCD approach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AdS/QCD "AdS/QCD") that extends it to include a [string theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/String_theory "String theory") of gluons,[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-Erlich2008-48) various QCD-inspired models like the [bag model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bag_model "Bag model") and the [constituent quark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constituent_quark "Constituent quark") model, which were popular in the 1980s, and the [SVZ sum rules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SVZ_sum_rules "SVZ sum rules"), which allow for rough approximate mass calculations.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-Calangelo2000-49) These methods do not have the same accuracy as the more brute-force lattice QCD methods, at least not yet.

The [CODATA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CODATA "CODATA") recommended value of a proton's [charge radius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charge_radius "Charge radius") is 8.4075(64)×10−16 m.[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-physconst-rp-50) The radius of the proton measured by electron–proton scattering differs from the value measured via the [Lamb shift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lamb_shift "Lamb shift") in muonic hydrogen (an [exotic atom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exotic_atom "Exotic atom") made of a proton and a negatively charged [muon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muon "Muon")).[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-PDG-2025-12) As a muon is 200 times heavier than an electron, resulting in a smaller [atomic orbital](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_orbital "Atomic orbital"), it is much more sensitive to the proton's charge radius and thus allows a more precise measurement.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-Pohl2010-51) Subsequent improved scattering and electron-spectroscopy measurements agree with the new small radius. Work continues to refine and check this new value.[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-KarrReview-52)

A third kind of high precision measurement agrees most closely with the value given by the muonic hydrogen spectroscopy but unexplained differences remain. The exact nature of what these measurement mean has also been questioned.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-53)

### Pressure inside the proton

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Proton&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Pressure inside the proton")]

Since the proton is composed of quarks confined by gluons, an equivalent [pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pressure "Pressure") that acts on the quarks can be defined. The size of that pressure and other details about it are controversial.

In 2018 this pressure was reported to be on the order 10 35 Pa, which is greater than the pressure inside a [neutron star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron_star "Neutron star"). It was said to be maximum at the centre, positive (repulsive) to a radial distance of about 0.6 fm, negative (attractive) at greater distances, and very weak beyond about 2 fm. These numbers were derived by a combination of a theoretical model and experimental [Compton scattering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compton_scattering "Compton scattering") of high-energy electrons.[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-Burkert2018-54)[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-55)[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-56) However, these results have been challenged as also being consistent with zero pressure[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-57) and as effectively providing the pressure profile shape by selection of the model.[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-58)

### Charge radius in solvated proton, hydronium

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Proton&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Charge radius in solvated proton, hydronium")]

The radius of the hydrated proton appears in the [Born equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Born_equation "Born equation") for calculating the hydration enthalpy of [hydronium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydronium "Hydronium").

Interaction of free protons with ordinary matter
------------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Proton&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Interaction of free protons with ordinary matter")]

Although protons have affinity for oppositely charged electrons, this is a relatively low-energy interaction and so free protons must lose sufficient velocity (and [kinetic energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_energy "Kinetic energy")) in order to become closely associated and bound to electrons. High energy protons, in traversing ordinary matter, lose energy by collisions with [atomic nuclei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_nuclei "Atomic nuclei"), and by [ionization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ionization "Ionization") of atoms (removing electrons) until they are slowed sufficiently to be captured by the [electron cloud](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_cloud "Electron cloud") in a normal atom.

However, in such an association with an electron, the character of the bound proton is not changed, and it remains a proton. The attraction of low-energy free protons to any electrons present in normal matter (such as the electrons in normal atoms) causes free protons to stop and to form a new chemical bond with an atom. Such a bond happens at any sufficiently "cold" temperature (that is, comparable to temperatures at the surface of the Sun) and with any type of atom. Thus, in interaction with any type of normal (non-plasma) matter, low-velocity free protons do not remain free but are attracted to electrons in any atom or molecule with which they come into contact, causing the proton and molecule to combine. Such molecules are then said to be "[protonated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protonated "Protonated")", and chemically they are simply compounds of hydrogen, often positively charged. Often, as a result, they become so-called [Brønsted acids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Br%C3%B8nsted_acid "Brønsted acid"). For example, a proton captured by a water molecule in water becomes [hydronium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydronium "Hydronium"), the [aqueous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aqueous "Aqueous")[cation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cation "Cation")H 3 O+.

Proton in chemistry
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Proton&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Proton in chemistry")]

In [chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemistry "Chemistry"), the number of protons in the [nucleus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_nucleus "Atomic nucleus") of an atom is known as the [atomic number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_number "Atomic number"), which determines the [chemical element](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_element "Chemical element") to which the atom belongs. For example, the atomic number of [chlorine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chlorine "Chlorine") is 17; this means that each chlorine atom has 17 protons and that all atoms with 17 protons are chlorine atoms. The chemical properties of each atom are determined by the number of (negatively charged) [electrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron "Electron"), which for neutral atoms is equal to the number of (positive) protons so that the total charge is zero. For example, a neutral chlorine atom has 17 protons and 17 electrons, whereas a Cl−[anion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anion "Anion") has 17 protons and 18 electrons for a total charge of −1.

All atoms of a given element are not necessarily identical, however. The [number of neutrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_of_neutrons "Number of neutrons") may vary to form different [isotopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotope "Isotope"), and energy levels may differ, resulting in different [nuclear isomers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_isomer "Nuclear isomer"). For example, there are two stable [isotopes of chlorine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotopes_of_chlorine "Isotopes of chlorine"): 35

17 Cl with 35 − 17 = 18 neutrons and 37

17 Cl with 37 − 17 = 20 neutrons.

[![Image 11](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Hydrogen.svg/220px-Hydrogen.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Hydrogen.svg)

Protium, the most common isotope of hydrogen, consists of one proton and one electron (it has no neutrons). The term _hydrogen ion_ (H+

) implies that that H-atom has lost its one electron, causing only a proton to remain. Thus, in chemistry, the terms _proton_ and _hydrogen ion_ (for the protium isotope) are used synonymously.

> The proton is a unique chemical species, being a bare nucleus. As a consequence it has no independent existence in the condensed state and is invariably found bound by a pair of electrons to another atom.

Ross Stewart, _The Proton: Application to Organic Chemistry_ (1985, p. 1)

In chemistry, the term _proton_ refers to the hydrogen ion, H+

. Since the atomic number of hydrogen is 1, a hydrogen ion has no electrons and corresponds to a bare nucleus, consisting of a proton (and 0 neutrons for the most abundant isotope _protium_[1 1 H](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen-1 "Hydrogen-1")). The proton is a "bare charge" with only about 1/64,000 of the radius of a hydrogen atom, and so is extremely reactive chemically. The free proton, thus, has an extremely short lifetime in chemical systems such as liquids and it reacts immediately with the [electron cloud](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_cloud "Electron cloud") of any available molecule. In aqueous solution, it forms the [hydronium ion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydronium_ion "Hydronium ion"), H 3 O+, which in turn is further [solvated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solvation "Solvation") by water molecules in [clusters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_ion#Cation_(positively_charged) "Hydrogen ion") such as [H 5 O 2]+ and [H 9 O 4]+.[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-Headrick2005-59)

The transfer of H+

 in an [acid–base reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Br%C3%B8nsted%E2%80%93Lowry_acid%E2%80%93base_theory "Brønsted–Lowry acid–base theory") is usually referred to as "proton transfer". The [acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acid "Acid") is referred to as a proton donor and the [base](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Base_(chemistry) "Base (chemistry)") as a proton acceptor. Likewise, [biochemical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biochemistry "Biochemistry") terms such as _[proton pump](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton\_pump "Proton pump")_ and _[proton channel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton\_channel "Proton channel")_ refer to the movement of hydrated H+

 ions.

The ion produced by removing the electron from a [deuterium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deuterium "Deuterium") atom is known as a _deuteron_, not a proton. Likewise, removing an electron from a [tritium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tritium "Tritium") atom produces a _triton_.

### Proton nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR)

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Proton&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Proton nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR)")]

Also in chemistry, the term _[proton NMR](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton\_NMR "Proton NMR")_ refers to the observation of hydrogen-1 nuclei in (mostly [organic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_chemistry "Organic chemistry")) molecules by [nuclear magnetic resonance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_magnetic_resonance "Nuclear magnetic resonance"). This method uses the [quantized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantization_(physics) "Quantization (physics)")[spin magnetic moment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_magnetic_moment "Spin magnetic moment") of the proton, which is due to its angular momentum (or [spin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_(physics) "Spin (physics)")), which in turn has a magnitude of one-half the reduced [Planck constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant "Planck constant"). (![Image 12: {\displaystyle \hbar /2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/74e0713309590b82aed3a8247c30cfdeae9f456b)). The name refers to examination of protons as they occur in [protium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen-1 "Hydrogen-1") (hydrogen-1 atoms) in compounds, and does not imply that free protons exist in the compound being studied.

The [Apollo Lunar Surface Experiments Packages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apollo_Lunar_Surface_Experiments_Package "Apollo Lunar Surface Experiments Package") (ALSEP) determined that more than 95% of the particles in the [solar wind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_wind "Solar wind") are electrons and protons, in approximately equal numbers.[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-LPI2009-60)[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-BNL2007-61)

> Because the Solar Wind [Spectrometer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectrometer "Spectrometer") made continuous measurements, it was possible to measure how the [Earth's magnetic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_magnetic_field "Earth's magnetic field") affects arriving solar wind particles. For about two-thirds of each orbit, the [Moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moon "Moon") is outside of the Earth's magnetic field. At these times, a typical proton density was 10 to 20 per cubic centimeter, with most protons having velocities between 400 and 650 kilometers per second. For about five days of each month, the Moon is inside the Earth's geomagnetic tail, and typically no solar wind particles were detectable. For the remainder of each lunar orbit, the Moon is in a transitional region known as the [magnetosheath](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetosheath "Magnetosheath"), where the Earth's magnetic field affects the solar wind, but does not completely exclude it. In this region, the particle flux is reduced, with typical proton velocities of 250 to 450 kilometers per second. During the lunar night, the spectrometer was shielded from the solar wind by the Moon and no solar wind particles were measured.[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-LPI2009-60)

Protons also have extrasolar origin from galactic [cosmic rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_ray "Cosmic ray"), where they make up about 90% of the total particle flux. These protons often have higher energy than solar wind protons, and their intensity is far more uniform and less variable than protons coming from the Sun, the production of which is heavily affected by [solar proton events](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_proton_event "Solar proton event") such as [coronal mass ejections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coronal_mass_ejection "Coronal mass ejection").

Research has been performed on the dose-rate effects of protons, as typically found in [space travel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_spaceflight "Human spaceflight"), on human health.[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-BNL2007-61)[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-Shukitt-Hale-62) To be more specific, there are hopes to identify what specific chromosomes are damaged, and to define the damage, during [cancer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cancer "Cancer") development from proton exposure.[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-BNL2007-61) Another study looks into determining "the effects of exposure to proton irradiation on neurochemical and behavioral endpoints, including [dopaminergic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dopaminergic "Dopaminergic") functioning, [amphetamine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amphetamine "Amphetamine")-induced conditioned taste aversion learning, and spatial learning and memory as measured by the [Morris water maze](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morris_water_maze "Morris water maze").[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-Shukitt-Hale-62) Electrical charging of a spacecraft due to interplanetary proton bombardment has also been proposed for study.[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-Green2006-63) There are many more studies that pertain to space travel, including [galactic cosmic rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galactic_cosmic_rays "Galactic cosmic rays") and their [possible health effects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Health_threat_from_cosmic_rays "Health threat from cosmic rays"), and [solar proton event](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_proton_event "Solar proton event") exposure.

The [American Biostack and Soviet Biorack](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/STS-65#Mission_parameters "STS-65") space travel experiments have demonstrated the severity of molecular damage induced by heavy ions on [microorganisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism "Microorganism") including [Artemia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemia "Artemia") cysts.[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-Planel2004-64)

[CPT-symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CPT-symmetry "CPT-symmetry") puts strong constraints on the relative properties of particles and [antiparticles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiparticle "Antiparticle") and, therefore, is open to stringent tests. For example, the charges of a proton and antiproton must sum to exactly zero. This equality has been tested to one part in 10 8. The equality of their masses has also been tested to better than one part in 10 8. By holding antiprotons in a [Penning trap](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Penning_trap "Penning trap"), the equality of the charge-to-mass ratio of protons and antiprotons has been tested to one part in 6×10 9.[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-Gabrielse2006-65) The [magnetic moment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_moment "Magnetic moment") of antiprotons has been measured with an error of 8×10−3 nuclear [Bohr magnetons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bohr_magneton "Bohr magneton"), and is found to be equal and opposite to that of a proton.[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton#cite_note-66)
