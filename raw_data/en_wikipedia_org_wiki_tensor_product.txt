In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), the **tensor product**![Image 1: {\displaystyle V\otimes W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a0d8e48e05a95d9c68f80f49e3d509ba9de064c9) of two [vector spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space")![Image 2: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) and ![Image 3: {\displaystyle W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54a9c4c547f4d6111f81946cad242b18298d70b7) (over the same [field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)")) is a vector space to which is associated a [bilinear map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bilinear_map "Bilinear map")![Image 4: {\displaystyle V\times W\rightarrow V\otimes W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c0864d8c23a683ad82d218fcd55b0420020d0a1d) that maps a pair ![Image 5: {\displaystyle (v,w),\ v\in V,w\in W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/28e4a3d044cb0366591939c8edd535698615dff7) to an element of ![Image 6: {\displaystyle V\otimes W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a0d8e48e05a95d9c68f80f49e3d509ba9de064c9) denoted ⁠![Image 7: {\displaystyle v\otimes w}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/710c0ffa6ca79aa8934b9a477f5e675068d63e9c)⁠.

An element of the form ![Image 8: {\displaystyle v\otimes w}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/710c0ffa6ca79aa8934b9a477f5e675068d63e9c) is called the **tensor product** of ![Image 9: {\displaystyle v}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e07b00e7fc0847fbd16391c778d65bc25c452597) and ![Image 10: {\displaystyle w}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/88b1e0c8e1be5ebe69d18a8010676fa42d7961e6). An element of ![Image 11: {\displaystyle V\otimes W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a0d8e48e05a95d9c68f80f49e3d509ba9de064c9) is a [tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor "Tensor"), and the tensor product of two vectors is sometimes called an _elementary tensor_ or a _decomposable tensor_. The elementary tensors [span](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_span "Linear span")![Image 12: {\displaystyle V\otimes W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a0d8e48e05a95d9c68f80f49e3d509ba9de064c9) in the sense that every element of ![Image 13: {\displaystyle V\otimes W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a0d8e48e05a95d9c68f80f49e3d509ba9de064c9) is a sum of elementary tensors. If [bases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basis_(linear_algebra) "Basis (linear algebra)") are given for ![Image 14: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) and ![Image 15: {\displaystyle W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54a9c4c547f4d6111f81946cad242b18298d70b7), a basis of ![Image 16: {\displaystyle V\otimes W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a0d8e48e05a95d9c68f80f49e3d509ba9de064c9) is formed by all tensor products of a basis element of ![Image 17: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) and a basis element of ![Image 18: {\displaystyle W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54a9c4c547f4d6111f81946cad242b18298d70b7).

The tensor product of two vector spaces captures the properties of all bilinear maps in the sense that a bilinear map from ![Image 19: {\displaystyle V\times W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6b9b65a794c51f2c6b0396c9d01d3bbe48f54fa4) into another vector space ![Image 20: {\displaystyle Z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1cc6b75e09a8aa3f04d8584b11db534f88fb56bd) factors uniquely through a [linear map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_map "Linear map")![Image 21: {\displaystyle V\otimes W\to Z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ebf4730159213f281f21fd5b9d5aae5990f1fb11) (see the section below titled 'Universal property'), i.e. the bilinear map is associated to a unique linear map from the tensor product ![Image 22: {\displaystyle V\otimes W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a0d8e48e05a95d9c68f80f49e3d509ba9de064c9) to ![Image 23: {\displaystyle Z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1cc6b75e09a8aa3f04d8584b11db534f88fb56bd).

Tensor products are used in many application areas, including physics and engineering. For example, in [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity"), the [gravitational field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_field "Gravitational field") is described through the [metric tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_tensor "Metric tensor"), which is a [tensor field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_field "Tensor field") with one tensor at each point of the [space-time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space-time "Space-time")[manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manifold "Manifold"), and each belonging to the tensor product of the [cotangent space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cotangent_space "Cotangent space") at the point with itself.

Definitions and constructions
-----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tensor_product&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Definitions and constructions")]

The _tensor product_ of two vector spaces is a vector space that is defined [up to](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Up_to "Up to") an [isomorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isomorphism "Isomorphism"). There are several equivalent ways to define it. Most consist of defining explicitly a vector space that is called a tensor product, and, generally, the equivalence proof results almost immediately from the basic properties of the vector spaces that are so defined.

The tensor product can also be defined through a [universal property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universal_property "Universal property"); see [§Universal property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_product#Universal_property), below. As for every universal property, all [objects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Object_(category_theory) "Object (category theory)") that satisfy the property are isomorphic through a unique isomorphism that is compatible with the universal property. When this definition is used, the other definitions may be viewed as constructions of objects satisfying the universal property and as proofs that there are objects satisfying the universal property, that is that tensor products exist.

Let V and W be two [vector spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space") over a [field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)")F, with respective [bases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basis_(linear_algebra) "Basis (linear algebra)")![Image 24: {\displaystyle B_{V}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35b80d6fe956b7021e86e9ae1237a40cbfb0ae59) and ⁠![Image 25: {\displaystyle B_{W}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/528896b93850ee641fe416a3005e7ef2330c9b65)⁠.

The _tensor product_![Image 26: {\displaystyle V\otimes W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a0d8e48e05a95d9c68f80f49e3d509ba9de064c9) of V and W is a vector space that has as a basis the set of all ![Image 27: {\displaystyle v\otimes w}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/710c0ffa6ca79aa8934b9a477f5e675068d63e9c) with ![Image 28: {\displaystyle v\in B_{V}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/90f1d4c4e0eca9cd495c4f9fde95a176e5d14599) and ⁠![Image 29: {\displaystyle w\in B_{W}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b316ee42002eb557edb8fc563dfca41278575ecb)⁠. This definition can be formalized in the following way (this formalization is rarely used in practice, as the preceding informal definition is generally sufficient): ![Image 30: {\displaystyle V\otimes W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a0d8e48e05a95d9c68f80f49e3d509ba9de064c9) is the set of the [functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)") from the [Cartesian product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_product "Cartesian product")![Image 31: {\displaystyle B_{V}\times B_{W}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7134a83d4657b4a8032f2d7e634c8b87004f7cb3) to F that have a finite number of nonzero values. The [pointwise operations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pointwise_operation "Pointwise operation") make ![Image 32: {\displaystyle V\otimes W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a0d8e48e05a95d9c68f80f49e3d509ba9de064c9) a vector space. The function that maps ![Image 33: {\displaystyle (v,w)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fe1ba70e3b2d314bc63be62afe1e5c3a80b95834) to 1 and the other elements of ![Image 34: {\displaystyle B_{V}\times B_{W}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7134a83d4657b4a8032f2d7e634c8b87004f7cb3) to 0 is denoted ⁠![Image 35: {\displaystyle v\otimes w}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/710c0ffa6ca79aa8934b9a477f5e675068d63e9c)⁠.

The set ![Image 36: {\displaystyle \{v\otimes w\mid v\in B_{V},w\in B_{W}\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0bd51d2cf98273ddc2f02f3fc1e82dcb21ea827d) is then straightforwardly a basis of ⁠![Image 37: {\displaystyle V\otimes W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a0d8e48e05a95d9c68f80f49e3d509ba9de064c9)⁠, which is called the _tensor product_ of the bases ![Image 38: {\displaystyle B_{V}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35b80d6fe956b7021e86e9ae1237a40cbfb0ae59) and ⁠![Image 39: {\displaystyle B_{W}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/528896b93850ee641fe416a3005e7ef2330c9b65)⁠.

We can equivalently define ![Image 40: {\displaystyle V\otimes W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a0d8e48e05a95d9c68f80f49e3d509ba9de064c9) to be the set of [bilinear forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bilinear_form "Bilinear form") on ![Image 41: {\displaystyle V\times W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6b9b65a794c51f2c6b0396c9d01d3bbe48f54fa4) that are nonzero at only a finite number of elements of ⁠![Image 42: {\displaystyle B_{V}\times B_{W}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7134a83d4657b4a8032f2d7e634c8b87004f7cb3)⁠. To see this, given ![Image 43: {\displaystyle (x,y)\in V\times W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0df749f05c622482858e4cbce82f8896d69d7d44) and a bilinear form ⁠![Image 44: {\displaystyle B:V\times W\to F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1596db44c6655e9f0849ccd47bf1afd65cb03081)⁠, we can decompose ![Image 45: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) and ![Image 46: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d) in the bases ![Image 47: {\displaystyle B_{V}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35b80d6fe956b7021e86e9ae1237a40cbfb0ae59) and ![Image 48: {\displaystyle B_{W}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/528896b93850ee641fe416a3005e7ef2330c9b65) as: ![Image 49: {\displaystyle x=\sum _{v\in B_{V}}x_{v}\,v\quad {\text{and}}\quad y=\sum _{w\in B_{W}}y_{w}\,w,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/69afb1915044f348338a6021f77926ce4a43253d) where only a finite number of ![Image 50: {\displaystyle x_{v}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/971dcbf2d12e5533b22c0a7b979d7b9040f35982)'s and ![Image 51: {\displaystyle y_{w}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/67bb3f7963029d9c3e179e91f221e7d97cb8aad9)'s are nonzero, and find by the bilinearity of ![Image 52: {\displaystyle B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47136aad860d145f75f3eed3022df827cee94d7a) that: ![Image 53: {\displaystyle B(x,y)=\sum _{v\in B_{V}}\sum _{w\in B_{W}}x_{v}y_{w}\,B(v,w)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/aed2eb62701e49214c32db90522e11cda922021a)

Hence, we see that the value of ![Image 54: {\displaystyle B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47136aad860d145f75f3eed3022df827cee94d7a) for any ![Image 55: {\displaystyle (x,y)\in V\times W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0df749f05c622482858e4cbce82f8896d69d7d44) is uniquely and totally determined by the values that it takes on ⁠![Image 56: {\displaystyle B_{V}\times B_{W}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7134a83d4657b4a8032f2d7e634c8b87004f7cb3)⁠. This lets us extend the maps ![Image 57: {\displaystyle v\otimes w}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/710c0ffa6ca79aa8934b9a477f5e675068d63e9c) defined on ![Image 58: {\displaystyle B_{V}\times B_{W}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7134a83d4657b4a8032f2d7e634c8b87004f7cb3) as before into bilinear maps ![Image 59: {\displaystyle v\otimes w:V\times W\to F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5460c8c0cd34e186254b6504568fb9ecce4515e2) , by letting: ![Image 60: {\displaystyle (v\otimes w)(x,y):=\sum _{v'\in B_{V}}\sum _{w'\in B_{W}}x_{v'}y_{w'}\,(v\otimes w)(v',w')=x_{v}\,y_{w}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c46aa50a052401b35fd469e42f94fe221074a182)

Then we can express any bilinear form ![Image 61: {\displaystyle B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47136aad860d145f75f3eed3022df827cee94d7a) as a (potentially infinite) formal linear combination of the ![Image 62: {\displaystyle v\otimes w}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/710c0ffa6ca79aa8934b9a477f5e675068d63e9c) maps according to: ![Image 63: {\displaystyle B=\sum _{v\in B_{V}}\sum _{w\in B_{W}}B(v,w)(v\otimes w)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dac230b4f5150a1de9e87643f16b596d7ad02cb4) making these maps similar to a [Schauder basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schauder_basis "Schauder basis") for the vector space ![Image 64: {\displaystyle {\text{Hom}}(V,W;F)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f6c8473917d556f04bc75cc07968f0cb63bc851e) of all bilinear forms on ⁠![Image 65: {\displaystyle V\times W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6b9b65a794c51f2c6b0396c9d01d3bbe48f54fa4)⁠. To instead have it be a proper Hamel [basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basis_(linear_algebra) "Basis (linear algebra)"), it only remains to add the requirement that ![Image 66: {\displaystyle B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47136aad860d145f75f3eed3022df827cee94d7a) is nonzero at an only a finite number of elements of ⁠![Image 67: {\displaystyle B_{V}\times B_{W}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7134a83d4657b4a8032f2d7e634c8b87004f7cb3)⁠, and consider the subspace of such maps instead.

In either construction, the _tensor product of two vectors_ is defined from their decomposition on the bases. More precisely, taking the basis decompositions of ![Image 68: {\displaystyle x\in V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fa374e20b2db7f6b8caa71ff1865f7f84f215c9f) and ![Image 69: {\displaystyle y\in W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b816fb5c62d31397feac7b027bafe54b43f0e301) as before: ![Image 70: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}x\otimes y&={\biggl (}\sum _{v\in B_{V}}x_{v}\,v{\biggr )}\otimes {\biggl (}\sum _{w\in B_{W}}y_{w}\,w{\biggr )}\\[5mu]&=\sum _{v\in B_{V}}\sum _{w\in B_{W}}x_{v}y_{w}\,v\otimes w.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ec33afd3c07c2c62f39c18eb9f9ad12451cf6ee0)

This definition is quite clearly derived from the coefficients of ![Image 71: {\displaystyle B(v,w)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a056a6c0fa4cdee6c0bca98cb8d2b0b953904727) in the expansion by bilinearity of ![Image 72: {\displaystyle B(x,y)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2717c82252c5f53393feb9457796768a531b238e) using the bases ![Image 73: {\displaystyle B_{V}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35b80d6fe956b7021e86e9ae1237a40cbfb0ae59) and ⁠![Image 74: {\displaystyle B_{W}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/528896b93850ee641fe416a3005e7ef2330c9b65)⁠, as done above. It is then straightforward to verify that with this definition, the map ![Image 75: {\displaystyle {\otimes }:(x,y)\mapsto x\otimes y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/14e73551e38608bcb283edfe046f3e8ed6f1d6ca) is a bilinear map from ![Image 76: {\displaystyle V\times W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6b9b65a794c51f2c6b0396c9d01d3bbe48f54fa4) to ![Image 77: {\displaystyle V\otimes W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a0d8e48e05a95d9c68f80f49e3d509ba9de064c9) satisfying the [universal property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universal_property "Universal property") that any construction of the tensor product satisfies (see below).

If arranged into a rectangular array, the [coordinate vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_vector "Coordinate vector") of ![Image 78: {\displaystyle x\otimes y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/56efedc83f0f00d1a89451a753d743db31ceb74c) is the [outer product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_product "Outer product") of the coordinate vectors of ![Image 79: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) and ⁠![Image 80: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d)⁠. Therefore, the tensor product is a generalization of the outer product, that is, an abstraction of it beyond coordinate vectors.

A limitation of this definition of the tensor product is that, if one changes bases, a different tensor product is defined. However, the decomposition on one basis of the elements of the other basis defines a [canonical isomorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canonical_isomorphism "Canonical isomorphism") between the two tensor products of vector spaces, which allows identifying them. Also, contrarily to the two following alternative definitions, this definition cannot be extended into a definition of the [tensor product of modules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_product_of_modules "Tensor product of modules") over a [ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ring_(mathematics) "Ring (mathematics)").

### As a quotient space

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tensor_product&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: As a quotient space")]

A construction of the tensor product that is basis independent can be obtained in the following way.

Let V and W be two [vector spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space") over a [field F](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)").

One considers first a vector space L that has the [Cartesian product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_product "Cartesian product")![Image 81: {\displaystyle V\times W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6b9b65a794c51f2c6b0396c9d01d3bbe48f54fa4) as a [basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basis_(linear_algebra) "Basis (linear algebra)"). That is, the basis elements of L are the [pairs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordered_pair "Ordered pair")![Image 82: {\displaystyle (v,w)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fe1ba70e3b2d314bc63be62afe1e5c3a80b95834) with ![Image 83: {\displaystyle v\in V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/99886ebbde63daa0224fb9bf56fa11b3c8a6f4fb) and ⁠![Image 84: {\displaystyle w\in W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ff0b62771fb4b163c4f1260be1c39768e7173e3e)⁠. To get such a vector space, one can define it as the vector space of the [functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)")![Image 85: {\displaystyle V\times W\to F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d606ece59cf97d582b87f3a42c6f434ecea7bf78) that have a finite number of nonzero values and identifying ![Image 86: {\displaystyle (v,w)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fe1ba70e3b2d314bc63be62afe1e5c3a80b95834) with the function that takes the value 1 on ![Image 87: {\displaystyle (v,w)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fe1ba70e3b2d314bc63be62afe1e5c3a80b95834) and 0 otherwise.

Let R be the [linear subspace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_subspace "Linear subspace") of L that is spanned by the relations that the tensor product must satisfy. More precisely, R is [spanned by](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_span "Linear span") the elements of one of the forms:

![Image 88: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}(v_{1}+v_{2},w)&-(v_{1},w)-(v_{2},w),\\(v,w_{1}+w_{2})&-(v,w_{1})-(v,w_{2}),\\(sv,w)&-s(v,w),\\(v,sw)&-s(v,w),\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f9c90ef0f373180cce677409245fb199b660a2e4)
where ⁠![Image 89: {\displaystyle v,v_{1},v_{2}\in V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b5cf22b959f54f0f868baf34ab6bdf6633e627de)⁠, ![Image 90: {\displaystyle w,w_{1},w_{2}\in W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/210adc891b0f26e11b248c82111dfd588385d0ba) and ⁠![Image 91: {\displaystyle s\in F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f6fe9319682deb1cd7944bfec2a95227c49045ce)⁠.

Then, the tensor product is defined as the [quotient space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quotient_space_(linear_algebra) "Quotient space (linear algebra)"):

![Image 92: {\displaystyle V\otimes W=L/R,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2797160599f624278cb55593ebdd5b227669f9f4)
and the image of ![Image 93: {\displaystyle (v,w)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fe1ba70e3b2d314bc63be62afe1e5c3a80b95834) in this quotient is denoted ⁠![Image 94: {\displaystyle v\otimes w}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/710c0ffa6ca79aa8934b9a477f5e675068d63e9c)⁠.

It is straightforward to prove that the result of this construction satisfies the [universal property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universal_property "Universal property") considered below. (A very similar construction can be used to define the [tensor product of modules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_product_of_modules "Tensor product of modules").)

[![Image 95](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Another_universal_tensor_prod.svg/200px-Another_universal_tensor_prod.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Another_universal_tensor_prod.svg)

Universal property of tensor product: if _h_ is bilinear, there is a unique linear map ~_h_ that makes the diagram [commutative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_diagram "Commutative diagram") (that is, _h_ = ~_h_ ∘ _φ_).

In this section, the [universal property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universal_property "Universal property") satisfied by the tensor product is described. As for every universal property, two objects that satisfy the property are related by a unique [isomorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isomorphism "Isomorphism"). It follows that this is a (non-constructive) way to define the tensor product of two vector spaces. In this context, the preceding constructions of tensor products may be viewed as proofs of existence of the tensor product so defined.

A consequence of this approach is that every property of the tensor product can be deduced from the universal property, and that, in practice, one may forget the method that has been used to prove its existence.

The "universal-property definition" of the tensor product of two vector spaces is the following (recall that a [bilinear map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bilinear_map "Bilinear map") is a function that is _separately_[linear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_map "Linear map") in each of its arguments):

The _tensor product_ of two vector spaces V and W is a vector space denoted as ⁠![Image 96: {\displaystyle V\otimes W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a0d8e48e05a95d9c68f80f49e3d509ba9de064c9)⁠, together with a bilinear map ![Image 97: {\displaystyle {\varphi }:(v,w)\mapsto v\otimes w}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7bdf7c42170d395423819452e7cd1ccaa5f0470c) from ![Image 98: {\displaystyle V\times W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6b9b65a794c51f2c6b0396c9d01d3bbe48f54fa4) to ⁠![Image 99: {\displaystyle V\otimes W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a0d8e48e05a95d9c68f80f49e3d509ba9de064c9)⁠, such that, for every bilinear map ⁠![Image 100: {\displaystyle h:V\times W\to Z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81315535e8a4319d084e6ba1d703c24cea03797a)⁠, there is a _unique_ linear map ⁠![Image 101: {\displaystyle {\tilde {h}}:V\otimes W\to Z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c83c80a0846b42c700792b1a7edd17d2ee247d97)⁠, such that ![Image 102: {\displaystyle h={\tilde {h}}\circ {\varphi }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/23055a97eaa6e71ce6da674c2cd41d377297334a) (that is, ![Image 103: {\displaystyle h(v,w)={\tilde {h}}(v\otimes w)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/642b30b5db855fa84ab416d0207522e175bf1875) for every ![Image 104: {\displaystyle v\in V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/99886ebbde63daa0224fb9bf56fa11b3c8a6f4fb) and ⁠![Image 105: {\displaystyle w\in W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ff0b62771fb4b163c4f1260be1c39768e7173e3e)⁠).

this map ![Image 114: {\displaystyle T}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ec7200acd984a1d3a3d7dc455e262fbe54f7f6e0) is denoted by ![Image 115: {\displaystyle \,\otimes This allows omitting parentheses in the tensor product of more than two vector spaces or vectors.

### Commutativity as vector space operation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tensor_product&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Commutativity as vector space operation")]

The tensor product of two vector spaces ![Image 142: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) and ![Image 143: {\displaystyle W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54a9c4c547f4d6111f81946cad242b18298d70b7) is [commutative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative "Commutative") in the sense that there is a canonical isomorphism:

![Image 144: {\displaystyle V\otimes W\cong W\otimes V,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3a40672b664741a2e2538e837e009a642c6c8316)
that maps ![Image 145: {\displaystyle v\otimes w}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/710c0ffa6ca79aa8934b9a477f5e675068d63e9c) to ⁠![Image 146: {\displaystyle w\otimes v}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2bc3f5c4fdc28db3aceee46aa974395aec5fa95e)⁠.

On the other hand, even when ⁠![Image 147: {\displaystyle V=W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/740038d36bd79466d6938d73b83fe737161fa1c6)⁠, the tensor product of vectors is not commutative; that is ⁠![Image 148: {\displaystyle v\otimes w\neq w\otimes v}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/83b0a04a2855ad3cb70d103d2a87d28f7d1a0006)⁠, in general.

The map ![Image 149: {\displaystyle x\otimes y\mapsto y\otimes x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d21bf565f1c3ba12901ea01a9520905135d4ac94) from ![Image 150: {\displaystyle V\otimes V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/52a1c6efb27fb1c61db37781404942628dca7f8b) to itself induces a linear [automorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Automorphism "Automorphism") that is called a **braiding map**. More generally and as usual (see [tensor algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_algebra "Tensor algebra")), let ![Image 151: {\displaystyle V^{\otimes n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ae04a5f6f3c7b6bc96ecbcac5f4eb67bc6e8221a) denote the tensor product of n copies of the vector space V. For every [permutation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permutation "Permutation")s of the first n positive integers, the map:

![Image 152: {\displaystyle x_{1}\otimes \cdots \otimes x_{n}\mapsto x_{s(1)}\otimes \cdots \otimes x_{s(n)}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b63214afa8de33e42cc02c8c8904d8846c8887a8)
induces a linear automorphism of ⁠![Image 153: {\displaystyle V^{\otimes n}\to V^{\otimes n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/17ffa48f1ac2a97e25d78f346f0e67fcbad49cf5)⁠, which is called a braiding map.

Tensor product of linear maps
-----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tensor_product&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Tensor product of linear maps")]

Given a linear map ⁠![Image 154: {\displaystyle f:U\to V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/99d431c81a43bf61447672a1c6e62d56fc73026a)⁠, and a vector space W, the _tensor product:_

![Image 155: {\displaystyle f\otimes W:U\otimes W\to V\otimes W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/239f9a64897baeda0443d8c773d45bcd782366e8)
is the unique linear map such that:

![Image 156: {\displaystyle (f\otimes W)(u\otimes w)=f(u)\otimes w.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5e8bef20149a7c5371471c16fbd8acdfd957e3d0)
The tensor product ![Image 157: {\displaystyle W\otimes f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/794b3cedab1ce671849bb03bab9c61e7cac1ebd6) is defined similarly.

Given two linear maps ![Image 158: {\displaystyle f:U\to V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/99d431c81a43bf61447672a1c6e62d56fc73026a) and ⁠![Image 159: {\displaystyle g:W\to Z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/19fbd35b76b3d44d7fef6fade25658a2a3a17e29)⁠, their tensor product:

![Image 160: {\displaystyle f\otimes g:U\otimes W\to V\otimes Z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dc6c069fb26b88c4fd3d4042d700d3d99428213b)
is the unique linear map that satisfies:

![Image 161: {\displaystyle (f\otimes g)(u\otimes w)=f(u)\otimes g(w).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a0d7003ec6f8b31273bd0694cd06c19492474559)
One has:

![Image 162: {\displaystyle f\otimes g=(f\otimes Z)\circ (U\otimes g)=(V\otimes g)\circ (f\otimes W).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5e600afbf088c312d5935b386e5063605ee19e8e)
In terms of [category theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category_theory "Category theory"), this means that the tensor product is a [bifunctor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bifunctor "Bifunctor") from the [category](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category_(mathematics) "Category (mathematics)") of vector spaces to itself.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_product#cite_note-3)

If f and g are both [injective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Injective "Injective") or [surjective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surjective "Surjective"), then the same is true for all above defined linear maps. In particular, the tensor product with a vector space is an [exact functor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exact_functor "Exact functor"); this means that every [exact sequence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exact_sequence "Exact sequence") is mapped to an exact sequence ([tensor products of modules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_product_of_modules "Tensor product of modules") do not transform injections into injections, but they are [right exact functors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_exact_functor "Right exact functor")).

By choosing bases of all vector spaces involved, the linear maps _f_ and _g_ can be represented by [matrices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_(mathematics) "Matrix (mathematics)"). Then, depending on how the tensor ![Image 163: {\displaystyle v\otimes w}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/710c0ffa6ca79aa8934b9a477f5e675068d63e9c) is vectorized, the matrix describing the tensor product ![Image 164: {\displaystyle f\otimes g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4c61cd98c51da2ab230a9183da0e52217702e474) is the [Kronecker product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kronecker_product "Kronecker product") of the two matrices. For example, if _V_, _X_, _W_, and _U_ above are all two-dimensional and bases have been fixed for all of them, and _f_ and _g_ are given by the matrices: ![Image 165: {\displaystyle A={\begin{bmatrix}a_{1,1}&a_{1,2}\\a_{2,1}&a_{2,2}\\\end{bmatrix}},\qquad B={\begin{bmatrix}b_{1,1}&b_{1,2}\\b_{2,1}&b_{2,2}\\\end{bmatrix}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0744d170063a09c7b2b5ea760320cb38f5b93045) respectively, then the tensor product of these two matrices is: ![Image 166: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}{\begin{bmatrix}a_{1,1}&a_{1,2}\\a_{2,1}&a_{2,2}\\\end{bmatrix}}\otimes {\begin{bmatrix}b_{1,1}&b_{1,2}\\b_{2,1}&b_{2,2}\\\end{bmatrix}}&={\begin{bmatrix}a_{1,1}{\begin{bmatrix}b_{1,1}&b_{1,2}\\b_{2,1}&b_{2,2}\\\end{bmatrix}}&a_{1,2}{\begin{bmatrix}b_{1,1}&b_{1,2}\\b_{2,1}&b_{2,2}\\\end{bmatrix}}\\[3pt]a_{2,1}{\begin{bmatrix}b_{1,1}&b_{1,2}\\b_{2,1}&b_{2,2}\\\end{bmatrix}}&a_{2,2}{\begin{bmatrix}b_{1,1}&b_{1,2}\\b_{2,1}&b_{2,2}\\\end{bmatrix}}\\\end{bmatrix}}\\&={\begin{bmatrix}a_{1,1}b_{1,1}&a_{1,1}b_{1,2}&a_{1,2}b_{1,1}&a_{1,2}b_{1,2}\\a_{1,1}b_{2,1}&a_{1,1}b_{2,2}&a_{1,2}b_{2,1}&a_{1,2}b_{2,2}\\a_{2,1}b_{1,1}&a_{2,1}b_{1,2}&a_{2,2}b_{1,1}&a_{2,2}b_{1,2}\\a_{2,1}b_{2,1}&a_{2,1}b_{2,2}&a_{2,2}b_{2,1}&a_{2,2}b_{2,2}\\\end{bmatrix}}.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1223968534d5f18d34806ac28c6d1faea6744a50)

The resultant rank is at most 4, and thus the resultant dimension is 4. _rank_ here denotes the [tensor rank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_rank "Tensor rank") i.e. the number of requisite indices (while the [matrix rank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_rank "Matrix rank") counts the number of degrees of freedom in the resulting array). ⁠![Image 167: {\displaystyle \operatorname {Tr} A\otimes B=\operatorname {Tr} A\times \operatorname {Tr} B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0c6c227794c530ad89597434c67623b9c64ececb)⁠.

A [dyadic product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dyadic_product "Dyadic product") is the special case of the tensor product between two vectors of the same dimension.

For non-negative integers _r_ and _s_ a type ![Image 168: {\displaystyle (r,s)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2f3e3778daed31bfa6149169529acb5d9fcc8b49)[tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor "Tensor") on a vector space _V_ is an element of: ![Image 169: {\displaystyle T_{s}^{r}(V)=\underbrace {V\otimes \cdots \otimes V} _{r}\otimes \underbrace {V^{*}\otimes \cdots \otimes V^{*}} _{s}=V^{\otimes r}\otimes \left(V^{*}\right)^{\otimes s}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/59b1483be00da7a07bf5db7ddd75455c3d132e1f) Here ![Image 170: {\displaystyle V^{*}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5910e6a94f4f7ee2ee85ceed9dacef3eff7a6242) is the [dual vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_vector_space "Dual vector space") (which consists of all [linear maps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_map "Linear map")_f_ from _V_ to the ground field _K_).

There is a product map, called the _(tensor) product of tensors_:[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_product#cite_note-4)![Image 171: {\displaystyle T_{s}^{r}(V)\otimes _{K}T_{s'}^{r'}(V)\to T_{s+s'}^{r+r'}(V).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cac4b3bf67084a55500c2cd732c90c7183f0aab3)

It is defined by grouping all occurring "factors" _V_ together: writing ![Image 172: {\displaystyle v_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7dffe5726650f6daac54829972a94f38eb8ec127) for an element of _V_ and ![Image 173: {\displaystyle f_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/65da883ca3d16b461e46c94777b0d9c4aa010e79) for an element of the dual space: ![Image 174: {\displaystyle (v_{1}\otimes f_{1})\otimes (v'_{1})=v_{1}\otimes v'_{1}\otimes f_{1}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2708549aff74a87ebfa1b7f82beea6d19e67bbc3)

If _V_ is finite dimensional, then picking a basis of _V_ and the corresponding [dual basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_basis "Dual basis") of ![Image 175: {\displaystyle V^{*}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5910e6a94f4f7ee2ee85ceed9dacef3eff7a6242) naturally induces a basis of ![Image 176: {\displaystyle T_{s}^{r}(V)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/416a199c05b7343b65fec02a4757f17f01cc5b86) (this basis is described in the [article on Kronecker products](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kronecker_product#Abstract_properties "Kronecker product")). In terms of these bases, the [components](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_vector "Coordinate vector") of a (tensor) product of two (or more) [tensors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor "Tensor") can be computed. For example, if _F_ and _G_ are two [covariant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covariance_and_contravariance_of_vectors "Covariance and contravariance of vectors") tensors of orders _m_ and _n_ respectively (i.e. ![Image 177: {\displaystyle F\in T_{m}^{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2ed5accf25b55affe7a7141bcc7cc362bd598bab) and ⁠![Image 178: {\displaystyle G\in T_{n}^{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d95b742d4e8edc0c8b156a92acd546fdb6d5858c)⁠), then the components of their tensor product are given by:[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_product#cite_note-5)![Image 179: {\displaystyle (F\otimes G)_{i_{1}i_{2}\cdots i_{m+n}}=F_{i_{1}i_{2}\cdots i_{m}}G_{i_{m+1}i_{m+2}i_{m+3}\cdots i_{m+n}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5e508e2ecef18653383a1c202df2eb172da9bb0b)

Thus, the components of the tensor product of two tensors are the ordinary product of the components of each tensor. Another example: let **U** be a tensor of type (1, 1) with components ⁠![Image 180: {\displaystyle U_{\beta }^{\alpha }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/906cfc2ffa78e377030bf42f8453ac02f7a79fa0)⁠, and let **V** be a tensor of type ![Image 181: {\displaystyle (1,0)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5b53cc1773694affcc1d4d6c2c778d43156a1206) with components ⁠![Image 182: {\displaystyle V^{\gamma }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9faa0b310bc0e75c305db3299e09ec3b123a5b31)⁠. Then: ![Image 183: {\displaystyle \left(U\otimes V\right)^{\alpha }{}_{\beta }{}^{\gamma }=U^{\alpha }{}_{\beta }V^{\gamma }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1a57ee3ece74d7c24ae36cea6089e339aa1c2b00) and: ![Image 184: {\displaystyle (V\otimes U)^{\mu \nu }{}_{\sigma }=V^{\mu }U^{\nu }{}_{\sigma }.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4076f809a24f1406f6aed3759f7789e9086a1e14)

Tensors equipped with their product operation form an [algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra_over_a_field "Algebra over a field"), called the [tensor algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_algebra "Tensor algebra").

### Evaluation map and tensor contraction

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tensor_product&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Evaluation map and tensor contraction")]

For tensors of type (1, 1) there is a canonical **evaluation map:**![Image 185: {\displaystyle V\otimes V^{*}\to K}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2196377b3587c6a51c6c52178777eeda01e0ad55) defined by its action on pure tensors: ![Image 186: {\displaystyle v\otimes f\mapsto f(v).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f30bf8db945794fb67cc9c5bada8f29049eb358f)

More generally, for tensors of type ⁠![Image 187: {\displaystyle (r,s)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2f3e3778daed31bfa6149169529acb5d9fcc8b49)⁠, with _r_, _s_> 0, there is a map, called [tensor contraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_contraction "Tensor contraction"): ![Image 188: {\displaystyle T_{s}^{r}(V)\to T_{s-1}^{r-1}(V).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8ec7dd67d34bc64545cc09f55dbc53c14240fa66) (The copies of ![Image 189: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) and ![Image 190: {\displaystyle V^{*}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5910e6a94f4f7ee2ee85ceed9dacef3eff7a6242) on which this map is to be applied must be specified.)

On the other hand, if ![Image 191: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) is _finite-dimensional_, there is a canonical map in the other direction (called the **coevaluation map**): ![Image 192: {\displaystyle {\begin{cases}K\to V\otimes V^{*}\\\lambda \mapsto \sum _{i}\lambda v_{i}\otimes v_{i}^{*}\end{cases}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4bc1b8cbddb0ffcd5e4784bb1219235b89bc7752) where ![Image 193: {\displaystyle v_{1},\ldots ,v_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fb40a91abab8b7bfb0e84b074732b2f044fd56ef) is any basis of ⁠![Image 194: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845)⁠, and ![Image 195: {\displaystyle v_{i}^{*}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/97661cc042bf758736628eec83f2755015d70941) is its [dual basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_basis "Dual basis"). This map does not depend on the choice of basis.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_product#cite_note-6)

The interplay of evaluation and coevaluation can be used to characterize finite-dimensional vector spaces without referring to bases.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_product#cite_note-7)

### Adjoint representation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tensor_product&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Adjoint representation")]

The tensor product ![Image 196: {\displaystyle T_{s}^{r}(V)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/416a199c05b7343b65fec02a4757f17f01cc5b86) may be naturally viewed as a module for the [Lie algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_algebra "Lie algebra")![Image 197: {\displaystyle \mathrm {End} (V)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f7708ca3ed32f63af36fb62f848320792dace0cc) by means of the diagonal action: for simplicity let us assume ⁠![Image 198: {\displaystyle r=s=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b564af89f85bfd4382d1286f496b75f4a60a8c2a)⁠, then, for each ⁠![Image 199: {\displaystyle u\in \mathrm {End} (V)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0c93af1c44dfa4f28eb0840bfab772425693af25)⁠, ![Image 200: {\displaystyle u(a\otimes b)=u(a)\otimes b-a\otimes u^{*}(b),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0fd9281b4ecb7721503f67446b0cf6d4239bb5fa) where ![Image 201: {\displaystyle u^{*}\in \mathrm {End} \left(V^{*}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b867953c7e74e45236457aa400c6c5863a939263) is the [transpose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transpose "Transpose") of _u_, that is, in terms of the obvious pairing on ⁠![Image 202: {\displaystyle V\otimes V^{*}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7357cc6d99957d5b1c38160284567b2410a9b45a)⁠, ![Image 203: {\displaystyle \langle u(a),b\rangle =\langle a,u^{*}(b)\rangle .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3afabe32dff82e1585480b9e5d70028451eab378)

There is a canonical isomorphism ![Image 204: {\displaystyle T_{1}^{1}(V)\to \mathrm {End} (V)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cbd8f28d159372eb5bab3acfd186ff247cfb02d9) given by: ![Image 205: {\displaystyle (a\otimes b)(x)=\langle x,b\rangle a.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dfd1b403749da42b13ffe5674db618b8356462c0)

Under this isomorphism, every _u_ in ![Image 206: {\displaystyle \mathrm {End} (V)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f7708ca3ed32f63af36fb62f848320792dace0cc) may be first viewed as an endomorphism of ![Image 207: {\displaystyle T_{1}^{1}(V)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2d1e7bf8b87de34a86f6a286e25e904edfc49968) and then viewed as an endomorphism of ⁠![Image 208: {\displaystyle \mathrm {End} (V)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f7708ca3ed32f63af36fb62f848320792dace0cc)⁠. In fact it is the [adjoint representation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adjoint_representation_of_a_Lie_algebra "Adjoint representation of a Lie algebra")ad(_u_) of ⁠![Image 209: {\displaystyle \mathrm {End} (V)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f7708ca3ed32f63af36fb62f848320792dace0cc)⁠.

Linear maps as tensors
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tensor_product&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Linear maps as tensors")]

Given two finite dimensional vector spaces _U_, _V_ over the same field _K_, denote the [dual space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_space "Dual space") of _U_ as _U*_, and the _K_-vector space of all linear maps from _U_ to _V_ as Hom(_U_,_V_). There is an isomorphism: ![Image 210: {\displaystyle U^{*}\otimes V\cong \mathrm {Hom} (U,V),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/433b10d0aedda611c199ddd4ef654a2a721cc387) defined by an action of the pure tensor ![Image 211: {\displaystyle f\otimes v\in U^{*}\otimes V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4f45f508b3063ef12dece19aad0363099828cc5c) on an element of ⁠![Image 212: {\displaystyle U}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/458a728f53b9a0274f059cd695e067c430956025)⁠, ![Image 213: {\displaystyle (f\otimes v)(u)=f(u)v.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/58dc3f096132a3de8d818a897da8ff1e19a6ba25)

Its "inverse" can be defined using a basis ![Image 214: {\displaystyle \{u_{i}\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6dac7cc188229221066ed26cb35f89b53c333309) and its dual basis ![Image 215: {\displaystyle \{u_{i}^{*}\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/43958e3f84c69a9c2dce93f1df50a574827269b6) as in the section "[Evaluation map and tensor contraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_product#Evaluation_map_and_tensor_contraction)" above: ![Image 216: {\displaystyle {\begin{cases}\mathrm {Hom} (U,V)\to U^{*}\otimes V\\F\mapsto \sum _{i}u_{i}^{*}\otimes F(u_{i}).\end{cases}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/193fd7113ec96da2b656ca0b0b7a118e56bccf3c)

This result implies: ![Image 217: {\displaystyle \dim(U\otimes V)=\dim(U)\dim(V),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/72e9005dbe8f6e7ebe3d99a6ddbf57d38bea8a6e) which automatically gives the important fact that ![Image 218: {\displaystyle \{u_{i}\otimes v_{j}\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d65e1e5fcf74aea3ebde7367bcced3efefbcbda3) forms a basis of ![Image 219: {\displaystyle U\otimes V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a637c45a41613a06b812766e4401393a8c71f7b7) where ![Image 220: {\displaystyle \{u_{i}\},\{v_{j}\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7287145cc6a7e48bc7eb1a792a8d89590b981ae6) are bases of _U_ and _V_.

Furthermore, given three vector spaces _U_, _V_, _W_ the tensor product is linked to the vector space of _all_ linear maps, as follows: ![Image 221: {\displaystyle \mathrm {Hom} (U\otimes V,W)\cong \mathrm {Hom} (U,\mathrm {Hom} (V,W)).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cb982012b19d885e385fa1ea3aa4b1719f7ef4eb) This is an example of [adjoint functors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adjoint_functor "Adjoint functor"): the tensor product is "left adjoint" to Hom.

Tensor products of modules over a ring
--------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tensor_product&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: Tensor products of modules over a ring")]

The tensor product of two [modules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Module_(mathematics) "Module (mathematics)")_A_ and _B_ over a _[commutative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative\_ring "Commutative ring")_[ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ring_(mathematics) "Ring (mathematics)")_R_ is defined in exactly the same way as the tensor product of vector spaces over a field: ![Image 222: {\displaystyle A\otimes _{R}B:=F(A\times B)/G,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2da2f5c62a69c04d56f607f654798ec82a622cd4) where now ![Image 223: {\displaystyle F(A\times B)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47579f90cf77660d7ae54c8aabe55a90d67be499) is the [free _R_-module](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_module "Free module") generated by the cartesian product and _G_ is the _R_-module generated by [these relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_product_of_modules#Balanced_product "Tensor product of modules").

More generally, the tensor product can be defined even if the ring is [non-commutative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noncommutative_ring "Noncommutative ring"). In this case _A_ has to be a right-_R_-module and _B_ is a left-_R_-module, and instead of the last two relations above, the relation: ![Image 224: {\displaystyle (ar,b)\sim (a,rb)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3b58cb7581d3b2931508740313b795927cb6358d) is imposed. If _R_ is non-commutative, this is no longer an _R_-module, but just an [abelian group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abelian_group "Abelian group").

The universal property also carries over, slightly modified: the map ![Image 225: {\displaystyle \varphi :A\times B\to A\otimes _{R}B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/482ca8097b92d8d76efe9f8a4a680a9d7bd6f7e0) defined by ![Image 226: {\displaystyle (a,b)\mapsto a\otimes b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/604f62cdbe22c5d0ce60a0791678f7c388d7c23b) is a [middle linear map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_product_of_modules#Balanced_product "Tensor product of modules") (referred to as "the canonical middle linear map"[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_product#cite_note-8)); that is, it satisfies:[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_product#cite_note-chen-9)![Image 227: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\varphi (a+a',b)&=\varphi (a,b)+\varphi (a',b)\\\varphi (a,b+b')&=\varphi (a,b)+\varphi (a,b')\\\varphi (ar,b)&=\varphi (a,rb)\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/936a76302627b601d4ee6c94a4cb9c2693ddc820)

The first two properties make _φ_ a bilinear map of the [abelian group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abelian_group "Abelian group")⁠![Image 228: {\displaystyle A\times B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/65f31ae45b0098f06b5d22c38d317eb097a88fa9)⁠. For any middle linear map ![Image 229: {\displaystyle \psi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/45e5789e5d9c8f7c79744f43ecaaf8ba42a8553a) of ⁠![Image 230: {\displaystyle A\times B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/65f31ae45b0098f06b5d22c38d317eb097a88fa9)⁠, a unique group homomorphism _f_ of ![Image 231: {\displaystyle A\otimes _{R}B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e4773c5f4d6541ec487737f1c3962e1aafb7fe55) satisfies ⁠![Image 232: {\displaystyle \psi =f\circ \varphi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c6d130fae30ff9a54f93d0dbd6cbf5cb42db4aa4)⁠, and this property determines ![Image 233: {\displaystyle \varphi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/33ee699558d09cf9d653f6351f9fda0b2f4aaa3e) within group isomorphism. See the [main article](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_product_of_modules "Tensor product of modules") for details.

### Tensor product of modules over a non-commutative ring

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tensor_product&action=edit&section=16 "Edit section: Tensor product of modules over a non-commutative ring")]

Let _A_ be a right _R_-module and _B_ be a left _R_-module. Then the tensor product of _A_ and _B_ is an abelian group defined by: ![Image 234: {\displaystyle A\otimes _{R}B:=F(A\times B)/G}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7c1c3ce6801a11dfb13a5632f6811c1a7fcffd62) where ![Image 235: {\displaystyle F(A\times B)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47579f90cf77660d7ae54c8aabe55a90d67be499) is a [free abelian group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_abelian_group "Free abelian group") over ![Image 236: {\displaystyle A\times B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/65f31ae45b0098f06b5d22c38d317eb097a88fa9) and G is the subgroup of ![Image 237: {\displaystyle F(A\times B)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47579f90cf77660d7ae54c8aabe55a90d67be499) generated by relations: ![Image 238: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}&\forall a,a_{1},a_{2}\in A,\forall b,b_{1},b_{2}\in B,{\text{ for all }}r\in R:\\&(a_{1},b)+(a_{2},b)-(a_{1}+a_{2},b),\\&(a,b_{1})+(a,b_{2})-(a,b_{1}+b_{2}),\\&(ar,b)-(a,rb).\\\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/30a4cbdaec6f8b6119930c15412aa635d027fe31)

The universal property can be stated as follows. Let _G_ be an abelian group with a map ![Image 239: {\displaystyle q:A\times B\to G}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5e1a3d0418e5509918a2f5d185adf013043b2d88) that is bilinear, in the sense that: ![Image 240: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}q(a_{1}+a_{2},b)&=q(a_{1},b)+q(a_{2},b),\\q(a,b_{1}+b_{2})&=q(a,b_{1})+q(a,b_{2}),\\q(ar,b)&=q(a,rb).\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/94eca7a547a44c064bbaa53f14651f9a9bffb35d)

Then there is a unique map ![Image 241: {\displaystyle {\overline {q}}:A\otimes B\to G}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/10b7fe22a266dc863590c724df3795ef678cc716) such that ![Image 242: {\displaystyle {\overline {q}}(a\otimes b)=q(a,b)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af75c0f7cf4a0ea832328190dbc96b1dcd3cdfe7) for all ![Image 243: {\displaystyle a\in A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a97387981adb5d65f74518e20b6785a284d7abd5) and ⁠![Image 244: {\displaystyle b\in B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/61dbfba9ff608c8700a30596649d98dcc6147d86)⁠.

Furthermore, we can give ![Image 245: {\displaystyle A\otimes _{R}B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e4773c5f4d6541ec487737f1c3962e1aafb7fe55) a module structure under some extra conditions:

1.   If _A_ is a (_S_,_R_)-bimodule, then ![Image 246: {\displaystyle A\otimes _{R}B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e4773c5f4d6541ec487737f1c3962e1aafb7fe55) is a left _S_-module, where ⁠![Image 247: {\displaystyle s(a\otimes b):=(sa)\otimes b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/647f34b759836bf3602c265ead097edd811713d4)⁠.
2.   If _B_ is a (_R_,_S_)-bimodule, then ![Image 248: {\displaystyle A\otimes _{R}B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e4773c5f4d6541ec487737f1c3962e1aafb7fe55) is a right _S_-module, where ⁠![Image 249: {\displaystyle (a\otimes b)s:=a\otimes (bs)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/264aeffe5ca1dd9aa7dc5cfebd7446397c28d3d3)⁠.
3.   If _A_ is a (_S_,_R_)-bimodule and _B_ is a (_R_,_T_)-bimodule, then ![Image 250: {\displaystyle A\otimes _{R}B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e4773c5f4d6541ec487737f1c3962e1aafb7fe55) is a (_S_,_T_)-bimodule, where the left and right actions are defined in the same way as the previous two examples.
4.   If _R_ is a commutative ring, then _A_ and _B_ are (_R_,_R_)-bimodules where ![Image 251: {\displaystyle ra:=ar}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/875d15bddd7e03be8f5cc467d80597efce29f8d9) and ⁠![Image 252: {\displaystyle br:=rb}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2a0481d885e5160861723f27549b4608f483e95f)⁠. By 3), we can conclude ![Image 253: {\displaystyle A\otimes _{R}B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e4773c5f4d6541ec487737f1c3962e1aafb7fe55) is a (_R_,_R_)-bimodule.

### Computing the tensor product

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tensor_product&action=edit&section=17 "Edit section: Computing the tensor product")]

For vector spaces, the tensor product ![Image 254: {\displaystyle V\otimes W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a0d8e48e05a95d9c68f80f49e3d509ba9de064c9) is quickly computed since bases of _V_ of _W_ immediately determine a basis of ⁠![Image 255: {\displaystyle V\otimes W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a0d8e48e05a95d9c68f80f49e3d509ba9de064c9)⁠, as was mentioned above. For modules over a general (commutative) ring, not every module is free. For example, **Z**/_n_**Z** is not a free abelian group (**Z**-module). The tensor product with **Z**/_n_**Z** is given by: ![Image 256: {\displaystyle M\otimes _{\mathbf {Z} }\mathbf {Z} /n\mathbf {Z} =M/nM.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/31a1a5bb61d2818e30d042650d2734d3da90f430)

More generally, given a [presentation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Presentation_of_a_module "Presentation of a module") of some _R_-module _M_, that is, a number of generators ![Image 257: {\displaystyle m_{i}\in M,i\in I}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c9efefee55791571f25fb4ad614434298f1ef50d) together with relations: ![Image 258: {\displaystyle \sum _{j\in J}a_{ji}m_{i}=0,\qquad a_{ij}\in R,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5d7d9e88fd731c9ece7c1ea080b5770450bff693) the tensor product can be computed as the following [cokernel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cokernel "Cokernel"): ![Image 259: {\displaystyle M\otimes _{R}N=\operatorname {coker} \left(N^{J}\to N^{I}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d765f70b5f00206131f360f7f0d2116a96887a7e)

Here ⁠![Image 260: {\displaystyle N^{J}=\oplus _{j\in J}N}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/78d946817704012c3610420ee896550fb972d571)⁠, and the map ![Image 261: {\displaystyle N^{J}\to N^{I}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5afaa551050313719925b839e64126806253b3c) is determined by sending some ![Image 262: {\displaystyle n\in N}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/aedc99ee9cd35f9c34b0f743f8c26b34359ca2fe) in the _j_ th copy of ![Image 263: {\displaystyle N^{J}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f0e7f97e6fd7046926f442622c3c920a2bc2f0f0) to ![Image 264: {\displaystyle a_{ij}n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8e39cefbd0daec72fd915219badaf6d44c6bc32b) (in ⁠![Image 265: {\displaystyle N^{I}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c59ab034da8c647a5bab7e473ca7762a5c3785f0)⁠). Colloquially, this may be rephrased by saying that a presentation of _M_ gives rise to a presentation of ⁠![Image 266: {\displaystyle M\otimes _{R}N}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1946b1034181e2db2825dca869b8ef0753e4831f)⁠. This is referred to by saying that the tensor product is a [right exact functor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_exact_functor "Right exact functor"). It is not in general left exact, that is, given an injective map of _R_-modules ⁠![Image 267: {\displaystyle M_{1}\to M_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ca47dd77589421aca3b91efd9eb686a36d9eb523)⁠, the tensor product: ![Image 268: {\displaystyle M_{1}\otimes _{R}N\to M_{2}\otimes _{R}N}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0882505c9e5d88444612083f0cc1bc909524aaad) is not usually injective. For example, tensoring the (injective) map given by multiplication with _n_, _n_: **Z** → **Z** with **Z**/_n_**Z** yields the zero map 0: **Z**/_n_**Z** → **Z**/_n_**Z**, which is not injective. Higher [Tor functors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tor_functor "Tor functor") measure the defect of the tensor product being not left exact. All higher Tor functors are assembled in the [derived tensor product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derived_tensor_product "Derived tensor product").

Tensor product of algebras
--------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tensor_product&action=edit&section=18 "Edit section: Tensor product of algebras")]

Let _R_ be a commutative ring. The tensor product of _R_-modules applies, in particular, if _A_ and _B_ are [_R_-algebras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra_(ring_theory) "Algebra (ring theory)"). In this case, the tensor product ![Image 269: {\displaystyle A\otimes _{R}B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e4773c5f4d6541ec487737f1c3962e1aafb7fe55) is an _R_-algebra itself by putting: ![Image 270: {\displaystyle (a_{1}\otimes b_{1})\cdot (a_{2}\otimes b_{2})=(a_{1}\cdot a_{2})\otimes (b_{1}\cdot b_{2}).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9f80e8b41755e53bc7d3ee378427d8ac43eb4c8d) For example: ![Image 271: {\displaystyle R[x]\otimes _{R}R[y]\cong R[x,y].}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ec90fcc36d25e63c6826603a7c001a9769f0b6d7)

A particular example is when _A_ and _B_ are fields containing a common subfield _R_. The [tensor product of fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_product_of_fields "Tensor product of fields") is closely related to [Galois theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galois_theory "Galois theory"): if, say, _A_ = _R_[_x_] / _f_(_x_), where _f_ is some [irreducible polynomial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irreducible_polynomial "Irreducible polynomial") with coefficients in _R_, the tensor product can be calculated as: ![Image 272: {\displaystyle A\otimes _{R}B\cong B[x]/f(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7ee63790e6b1781257fb2d994254582369474f23) where now _f_ is interpreted as the same polynomial, but with its coefficients regarded as elements of _B_. In the larger field _B_, the polynomial may become reducible, which brings in Galois theory. For example, if _A_ = _B_ is a [Galois extension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galois_extension "Galois extension") of _R_, then: ![Image 273: {\displaystyle A\otimes _{R}A\cong A[x]/f(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bdb0e3d4bf455da8e9b8c7fd62052e8dbbbdc804) is isomorphic (as an _A_-algebra) to the ⁠![Image 274: {\displaystyle A^{\operatorname {deg} (f)}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/60b5521e3cd1fb82faca2616ae8667414225de64)⁠.

Other examples of tensor products
---------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tensor_product&action=edit&section=20 "Edit section: Other examples of tensor products")]

### Topological tensor products

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tensor_product&action=edit&section=21 "Edit section: Topological tensor products")]

[Hilbert spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space "Hilbert space") generalize finite-dimensional vector spaces to arbitrary dimensions. There is [an analogous operation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_product_of_Hilbert_spaces "Tensor product of Hilbert spaces"), also called the "tensor product," that makes Hilbert spaces a [symmetric monoidal category](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetric_monoidal_category "Symmetric monoidal category"). It is essentially constructed as the [metric space completion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complete_metric_space#Completion "Complete metric space") of the algebraic tensor product discussed above. However, it does not satisfy the obvious analogue of the universal property defining tensor products;[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_product#cite_note-11) the morphisms for that property must be restricted to [Hilbert–Schmidt operators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert%E2%80%93Schmidt_operator "Hilbert–Schmidt operator").[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_product#cite_note-12)

In situations where the imposition of an inner product is inappropriate, one can still attempt to complete the algebraic tensor product, as a [topological tensor product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_tensor_product "Topological tensor product"). However, such a construction is no longer uniquely specified: in many cases, there are multiple natural topologies on the algebraic tensor product.

### Tensor product of graded vector spaces

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tensor_product&action=edit&section=22 "Edit section: Tensor product of graded vector spaces")]

Some vector spaces can be decomposed into [direct sums](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direct_sum "Direct sum") of subspaces. In such cases, the tensor product of two spaces can be decomposed into sums of products of the subspaces (in analogy to the way that multiplication distributes over addition).

### Tensor product of representations

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tensor_product&action=edit&section=23 "Edit section: Tensor product of representations")]

Vector spaces endowed with an additional multiplicative structure are called [algebras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra_over_a_field "Algebra over a field"). The tensor product of such algebras is described by the [Littlewood–Richardson rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Littlewood%E2%80%93Richardson_rule "Littlewood–Richardson rule").

### Tensor product of multilinear forms

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tensor_product&action=edit&section=25 "Edit section: Tensor product of multilinear forms")]

Given two [multilinear forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multilinear_form "Multilinear form")![Image 306: {\displaystyle f(x_{1},\dots ,x_{k})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ff3f1c85797a56eecffc2c87c00063220f492941) and ![Image 307: {\displaystyle g(x_{1},\dots ,x_{m})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/584360c0d6821488e235193c8f2454ccf202a868) on a vector space ![Image 308: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) over the field ![Image 309: {\displaystyle K}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b76fce82a62ed5461908f0dc8f037de4e3686b0) their tensor product is the multilinear form: ![Image 310: {\displaystyle (f\otimes g)(x_{1},\dots ,x_{k+m})=f(x_{1},\dots ,x_{k})g(x_{k+1},\dots ,x_{k+m}).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6d4788c169ce7de77af0496855de6e6fb5bf65a6)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_product#cite_note-An_Introduction_to_Manifolds-13)

This is a special case of the [product of tensors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_product#General_tensors) if they are seen as multilinear maps (see also [tensors as multilinear maps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor#As_multilinear_maps "Tensor")). Thus the components of the tensor product of multilinear forms can be computed by the [Kronecker product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kronecker_product "Kronecker product").

### Tensor product of graphs

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tensor_product&action=edit&section=29 "Edit section: Tensor product of graphs")]

It should be mentioned that, though called "tensor product", this is not a tensor product of graphs in the above sense; actually it is the [category-theoretic product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Product_(category_theory) "Product (category theory)") in the category of graphs and [graph homomorphisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_homomorphism "Graph homomorphism"). ### Monoidal categories

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tensor_product&action=edit&section=30 "Edit section: Monoidal categories")]

The most general setting for the tensor product is the [monoidal category](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monoidal_category "Monoidal category"). It captures the algebraic essence of tensoring, without making any specific reference to what is being tensored. Thus, all tensor products can be expressed as an application of the monoidal category to some particular setting, acting on some particular objects.

A number of important subspaces of the [tensor algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_algebra "Tensor algebra") can be constructed as [quotients](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quotient_space_(linear_algebra) "Quotient space (linear algebra)"): these include the [exterior algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_algebra "Exterior algebra"), the [symmetric algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetric_algebra "Symmetric algebra"), the [Clifford algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_algebra "Clifford algebra"), the [Weyl algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weyl_algebra "Weyl algebra"), and the [universal enveloping algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universal_enveloping_algebra "Universal enveloping algebra") in general.

The exterior algebra is constructed from the [exterior product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_product "Exterior product"). Given a vector space _V_, the exterior product ![Image 311: {\displaystyle V\wedge V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3c450fa28ed57a421a6f4fc3cbc0f8b84156298f) is defined as: ![Image 312: {\displaystyle V\wedge V:=V\otimes V{\big /}\{v\otimes v\mid v\in V\}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/036199f2f6741062e24bf3e0acfd1dc695bacb54)

When the underlying field of _V_ does not have characteristic 2, then this definition is equivalent to: ![Image 313: {\displaystyle V\wedge V:=V\otimes V{\big /}{\bigl \{}v_{1}\otimes v_{2}+v_{2}\otimes v_{1}\mid (v_{1},v_{2})\in V^{2}{\bigr \}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a756951c7513f16ca01ec6f801cf5d4a5906494d)

The image of ![Image 314: {\displaystyle v_{1}\otimes v_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/907438c6e0da275ad0e78a882f4c2894204a5f47) in the exterior product is usually denoted ![Image 315: {\displaystyle v_{1}\wedge v_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c74315e03dae6a6a126b718bba7c7c4eb877242e) and satisfies, by construction, ⁠![Image 316: {\displaystyle v_{1}\wedge v_{2}=-v_{2}\wedge v_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/581eadcf16f5ca6ead01fd4f9136bdeee67c3b8b)⁠. Similar constructions are possible for ![Image 317: {\displaystyle V\otimes \dots \otimes V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8cd0cd1d09e915186133f6fe268dcf6d925ae54e) (_n_ factors), giving rise to ⁠![Image 318: {\displaystyle \Lambda ^{n}V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bea28f2771f7ac7f5a7e58b291c634258e057625)⁠, the _n_ th [exterior power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_power "Exterior power") of _V_. The latter notion is the basis of [differential _n_-forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_form "Differential form").

The symmetric algebra is constructed in a similar manner, from the [symmetric product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetric_tensor#symmetric_product "Symmetric tensor"): ![Image 319: {\displaystyle V\odot V:=V\otimes V{\big /}{\bigl \{}v_{1}\otimes v_{2}-v_{2}\otimes v_{1}\mid (v_{1},v_{2})\in V^{2}{\bigr \}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9ba7c2d748b047928c88ac3736ded4d6cb5b682a)

More generally: ![Image 320: {\displaystyle \operatorname {Sym} ^{n}V:=\underbrace {V\otimes \dots \otimes V} _{n}{\big /}(\dots \otimes v_{i}\otimes v_{i+1}\otimes \dots -\dots \otimes v_{i+1}\otimes v_{i}\otimes \dots )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ed0981a5de0c88c7f77c4de9d1b484ea84fdf909)

That is, in the symmetric algebra two adjacent vectors (and therefore all of them) can be interchanged. The resulting objects are called [symmetric tensors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetric_tensor "Symmetric tensor").

Tensor product in programming
-----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tensor_product&action=edit&section=32 "Edit section: Tensor product in programming")]

### Array programming languages

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tensor_product&action=edit&section=33 "Edit section: Array programming languages")]

[Array programming languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Array_programming_languages "Array programming languages") may have this pattern built in. For example, in [APL](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/APL_programming_language "APL programming language") the tensor product is expressed as `○.×` (for example `A ○.× B` or `A ○.× B ○.× C`). In [J](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J_programming_language "J programming language") the tensor product is the dyadic form of `*/` (for example `a */ b` or `a */ b */ c`).

J's treatment also allows the representation of some tensor fields, as `a` and `b` may be functions instead of constants. This product of two functions is a derived function, and if `a` and `b` are [differentiable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differentiable_function "Differentiable function"), then `a */ b` is differentiable.

However, these kinds of notation are not universally present in array languages. Other array languages may require explicit treatment of indices (for example, [MATLAB](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MATLAB "MATLAB")), and/or may not support [higher-order functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Higher-order_function "Higher-order function") such as the [Jacobian derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacobian_matrix_and_determinant "Jacobian matrix and determinant") (for example, [Fortran](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fortran "Fortran")/APL).
