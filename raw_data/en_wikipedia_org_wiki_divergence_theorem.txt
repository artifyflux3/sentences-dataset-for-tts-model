In [vector calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_calculus "Vector calculus"), the **divergence theorem**, also known as **Gauss's theorem** or **Ostrogradsky's theorem**,[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divergence_theorem#cite_note-Katz-1) is a [theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theorem "Theorem") relating the _[flux](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flux "Flux")_ of a [vector field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_field "Vector field") through a closed [surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_(mathematics) "Surface (mathematics)") to the _[divergence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divergence "Divergence")_ of the field in the volume enclosed.

More precisely, the divergence theorem states that the [surface integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_integral "Surface integral") of a vector field over a closed surface, which is called the "flux" through the surface, is equal to the [volume integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume_integral "Volume integral") of the divergence over the region enclosed by the surface. Intuitively, it states that "the sum of all sources of the field in a region (with sinks regarded as negative sources) gives the net flux out of the region".

The divergence theorem is an important result for the mathematics of [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics") and [engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineering "Engineering"), particularly in [electrostatics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrostatics "Electrostatics") and [fluid dynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluid_dynamics "Fluid dynamics"). In these fields, it is usually applied in three dimensions. However, it [generalizes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generalization "Generalization") to any number of dimensions. In one dimension, it is equivalent to the [fundamental theorem of calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_theorem_of_calculus "Fundamental theorem of calculus"). In two dimensions, it is equivalent to [Green's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green%27s_theorem "Green's theorem").

Explanation using liquid flow
-----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Divergence_theorem&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Explanation using liquid flow")]

[Vector fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_field "Vector field") are often illustrated using the example of the [velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity "Velocity") field of a [fluid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluid "Fluid"), such as a gas or liquid. A moving liquid has a velocity—a speed and a direction—at each point, which can be represented by a [vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_(mathematics_and_physics) "Vector (mathematics and physics)"), so that the velocity of the liquid at any moment forms a vector field. Consider an imaginary closed surface _S_ inside a body of liquid, enclosing a volume of liquid. The [flux](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flux "Flux") of liquid out of the volume at any time is equal to the volume rate of fluid crossing this surface, i.e., the [surface integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_integral "Surface integral") of the velocity over the surface.

Since liquids are incompressible, the amount of liquid inside a closed volume is constant; if there are no [sources or sinks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sources_and_sinks "Sources and sinks") inside the volume then the flux of liquid out of _S_ is zero. If the liquid is moving, it may flow into the volume at some points on the surface _S_ and out of the volume at other points, but the amounts flowing in and out at any moment are equal, so the _net_ flux of liquid out of the volume is zero.

However, if a _source_ of liquid is inside the closed surface, such as a pipe through which liquid is introduced, the additional liquid will exert pressure on the surrounding liquid, causing an outward flow in all directions. This will cause a net outward flow through the surface _S_. The flux outward through _S_ equals the volume rate of flow of fluid into _S_ from the pipe. Similarly if there is a _sink_ or drain inside _S_, such as a pipe which drains the liquid off, the external pressure of the liquid will cause a velocity throughout the liquid directed inward toward the location of the drain. The volume rate of flow of liquid inward through the surface _S_ equals the rate of liquid removed by the sink.

If there are multiple sources and sinks of liquid inside _S_, the flux through the surface can be calculated by adding up the volume rate of liquid added by the sources and subtracting the rate of liquid drained off by the sinks. The volume rate of flow of liquid through a source or sink (with the flow through a sink given a negative sign) is equal to the _[divergence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divergence "Divergence")_ of the velocity field at the pipe mouth, so adding up (integrating) the divergence of the liquid throughout the volume enclosed by _S_ equals the volume rate of flux through _S_. This is the divergence theorem.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divergence_theorem#cite_note-2)

The divergence theorem is employed in any [conservation law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservation_law "Conservation law") which states that the total volume of all sinks and sources, that is the volume integral of the divergence, is equal to the net flow across the volume's boundary.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divergence_theorem#cite_note-3)

Informal derivation
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Divergence_theorem&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Informal derivation")]

The divergence theorem follows from the fact that if a volume V is partitioned into separate parts, the [flux](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flux "Flux") out of the original volume is equal to the algebraic sum of the flux out of each component volume.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divergence_theorem#cite_note-Benford-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divergence_theorem#cite_note-Purcell-7) This is true despite the fact that the new subvolumes have surfaces that were not part of the original volume's surface, because these surfaces are just partitions between two of the subvolumes and the flux through them just passes from one volume to the other and so cancels out when the flux out of the subvolumes is summed.

[![Image 13](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Divergence_theorem_1_-_split_volume.png/500px-Divergence_theorem_1_-_split_volume.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Divergence_theorem_1_-_split_volume.png)

A volume divided into two subvolumes. At right the two subvolumes are separated to show the flux out of the different surfaces.

See the diagram. A closed, bounded volume V is divided into two volumes _V_ 1 and _V_ 2 by a surface _S_ 3 _(green)_. The flux Φ(_V_ i) out of each component region _V_ i is equal to the sum of the flux through its two faces, so the sum of the flux out of the two parts is

![Image 14: {\displaystyle \Phi (V_{\text{1}})+\Phi (V_{\text{2}})=\Phi _{\text{1}}+\Phi _{\text{31}}+\Phi _{\text{2}}+\Phi _{\text{32}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3db9d059ab698a626c39dee4770478381a0de6d)
where Φ 1 and Φ 2 are the flux out of surfaces _S_ 1 and _S_ 2, Φ 31 is the flux through _S_ 3 out of volume 1, and Φ 32 is the flux through _S_ 3 out of volume 2. The point is that surface _S_ 3 is part of the surface of both volumes. The "outward" direction of the [normal vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_vector "Normal vector")![Image 15: {\displaystyle \mathbf {\hat {n}} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4eb84e133d15551d660800ec29b44783ff36e19d) is opposite for each volume, so the flux out of one through _S_ 3 is equal to the negative of the flux out of the other so these two fluxes cancel in the sum.

![Image 16: {\displaystyle \Phi _{\text{31}}=\iint _{S_{3}}\mathbf {F} \cdot \mathbf {\hat {n}} \;\mathrm {d} S=-\iint _{S_{3}}\mathbf {F} \cdot (-\mathbf {\hat {n}} )\;\mathrm {d} S=-\Phi _{\text{32}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/90214bdca30a1626e95d3b3f76f512b140cfc08a)
Therefore:

![Image 17: {\displaystyle \Phi (V_{\text{1}})+\Phi (V_{\text{2}})=\Phi _{\text{1}}+\Phi _{\text{2}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2ce5cc9779224d66840b8677c4bd0d5c0b8cb64f)
Since the union of surfaces _S_ 1 and _S_ 2 is S

![Image 18: {\displaystyle \Phi (V_{\text{1}})+\Phi (V_{\text{2}})=\Phi (V)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7b4422a82eb95f9d88f4dda287921c3360841205)[![Image 19](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Divergence_theorem_2_-_volume_partition.png/500px-Divergence_theorem_2_-_volume_partition.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Divergence_theorem_2_-_volume_partition.png)

The volume can be divided into any number of subvolumes and the flux out of _V_ is equal to the sum of the flux out of each subvolume, because the flux through the green surfaces cancels out in the sum. In (b) the volumes are shown separated slightly, illustrating that each green partition is part of the boundary of two adjacent volumes

This principle applies to a volume divided into any number of parts, as shown in the diagram.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divergence_theorem#cite_note-Purcell-7) Since the integral over each internal partition _(green surfaces)_ appears with opposite signs in the flux of the two adjacent volumes they cancel out, and the only contribution to the flux is the integral over the external surfaces _(grey)_. Since the external surfaces of all the component volumes equal the original surface.

The goal is to divide the original volume into infinitely many infinitesimal volumes. As the volume is divided into smaller and smaller parts, the surface integral on the right, the flux out of each subvolume, approaches zero because the surface area _S_(_V_ i) approaches zero. However, from the definition of [divergence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divergence "Divergence"), the ratio of flux to volume, ![Image 26: {\displaystyle {\frac {\Phi (V_{\text{i}})}{|V_{\text{i}}|}}={\frac {1}{|V_{\text{i}}|}}\iint _{S(V_{\text{i}})}\mathbf {F} \cdot \mathbf {\hat {n}} \;\mathrm {d} S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8eb2835b68f2a214551c41217b9d388aa4149268), the part in parentheses below, does not in general vanish but approaches the [divergence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divergence "Divergence")div **F** as the volume approaches zero.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divergence_theorem#cite_note-Purcell-7)

![Image 27: {\displaystyle \iint _{S(V)}\mathbf {F} \cdot \mathbf {\hat {n}} \;\mathrm {d} S=\sum _{V_{\text{i}}\subset V}\left({\frac {1}{|V_{\text{i}}|}}\iint _{S(V_{\text{i}})}\mathbf {F} \cdot \mathbf {\hat {n}} \;\mathrm {d} S\right)|V_{\text{i}}|}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/feda9276efaab3dc962d010ae85ad939d6104397)
As long as the vector field **F**(**x**) has continuous derivatives, the sum above holds even in the [limit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limit_(mathematics) "Limit (mathematics)") when the volume is divided into infinitely small increments

![Image 28: {\displaystyle \iint _{S(V)}\mathbf {F} \cdot \mathbf {\hat {n}} \;\mathrm {d} S=\lim _{|V_{\text{i}}|\to 0}\sum _{V_{\text{i}}\subset V}\left({\frac {1}{|V_{\text{i}}|}}\iint _{S(V_{\text{i}})}\mathbf {F} \cdot \mathbf {\hat {n}} \;\mathrm {d} S\right)|V_{\text{i}}|}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/df9a48b138a04afc42f3da898d8bbee5c9d76165)
As ![Image 29: {\displaystyle |V_{\text{i}}|}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/686bdaf1fa1c108b76e9d784190c11561bc70914) approaches zero volume, it becomes the infinitesimal _dV_, the part in parentheses becomes the divergence, and the sum becomes a [volume integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume_integral "Volume integral") over V

![Image 30: {\displaystyle \;\iint _{S(V)}\mathbf {F} \cdot \mathbf {\hat {n}} \;\mathrm {d} S=\iiint _{V}\operatorname {div} \mathbf {F} \;\mathrm {d} V\;}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/373cf173e4a8d1b242adffcc0909ce89a8c47875)

Since this derivation is coordinate free, it shows that the divergence does not depend on the coordinates used.

### For compact Riemannian manifolds with boundary

**Theorem**—Let ![Image 121: {\displaystyle {\overline {\Omega }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/86f6b4b87fefc0caa4b0619fb9aa46d617a12359) be a ![Image 122: {\displaystyle C^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4fd6a5946b7e916352b0afc557f992328bac85e8) compact manifold with boundary with ![Image 123: {\displaystyle C^{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bd24bae0d7570018e828e19851902c09c618af91) metric tensor ![Image 124: {\displaystyle g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3556280e66fe2c0d0140df20935a6f057381d77). ### Differential and integral forms of physical laws

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Divergence_theorem&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Differential and integral forms of physical laws")]

As a result of the divergence theorem, a host of physical laws can be written in both a differential form (where one quantity is the divergence of another) and an integral form (where the flux of one quantity through a closed surface is equal to another quantity). Three examples are [Gauss's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauss%27s_law "Gauss's law") (in [electrostatics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrostatics "Electrostatics")), [Gauss's law for magnetism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauss%27s_law_for_magnetism "Gauss's law for magnetism"), and [Gauss's law for gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauss%27s_law_for_gravity "Gauss's law for gravity").

#### Continuity equations

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Divergence_theorem&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Continuity equations")]

[Continuity equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuity_equation "Continuity equation") offer more examples of laws with both differential and integral forms, related to each other by the divergence theorem. In [fluid dynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluid_dynamics "Fluid dynamics"), [electromagnetism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetism "Electromagnetism"), [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics"), [relativity theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativity_theory "Relativity theory"), and a number of other fields, there are [continuity equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuity_equation "Continuity equation") that describe the conservation of mass, momentum, energy, probability, or other quantities. Generically, these equations state that the divergence of the flow of the conserved quantity is equal to the distribution of _sources_ or _sinks_ of that quantity. The divergence theorem states that any such continuity equation can be written in a differential form (in terms of a divergence) and an integral form (in terms of a flux).[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divergence_theorem#cite_note-C.B._Parker_1994-12)

### Inverse-square laws

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Divergence_theorem&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Inverse-square laws")]

Any _[inverse-square law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverse-square\_law "Inverse-square law")_ can instead be written in a _Gauss's law_-type form (with a differential and integral form, as described above). Two examples are [Gauss's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauss%27s_law "Gauss's law") (in electrostatics), which follows from the inverse-square [Coulomb's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coulomb%27s_law "Coulomb's law"), and [Gauss's law for gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauss%27s_law_for_gravity "Gauss's law for gravity"), which follows from the inverse-square [Newton's law of universal gravitation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_law_of_universal_gravitation "Newton's law of universal gravitation"). The derivation of the Gauss's law-type equation from the inverse-square formulation or vice versa is exactly the same in both cases; see either of those articles for details.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divergence_theorem#cite_note-C.B._Parker_1994-12)

[Joseph-Louis Lagrange](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph-Louis_Lagrange "Joseph-Louis Lagrange") introduced the notion of surface integrals in 1760 and again in more general terms in 1811, in the second edition of his _[Mécanique Analytique](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M%C3%A9canique\_analytique "Mécanique analytique")._ Lagrange employed surface integrals in his work on fluid mechanics.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divergence_theorem#cite_note-:0-13) He discovered the divergence theorem in 1762.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divergence_theorem#cite_note-14)

[Carl Friedrich Gauss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Friedrich_Gauss "Carl Friedrich Gauss") was also using surface integrals while working on the gravitational attraction of an elliptical spheroid in 1813, when he proved special cases of the divergence theorem.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divergence_theorem#cite_note-15)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divergence_theorem#cite_note-:0-13) He proved additional special cases in 1833 and 1839.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divergence_theorem#cite_note-:32-16) But it was [Mikhail Ostrogradsky](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Ostrogradsky "Mikhail Ostrogradsky"), who gave the first proof of the general theorem, in 1826, as part of his investigation of heat flow.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divergence_theorem#cite_note-17) Special cases were proven by [George Green](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_Green_(mathematician) "George Green (mathematician)") in 1828 in _[An Essay on the Application of Mathematical Analysis to the Theories of Electricity and Magnetism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/An\_Essay\_on\_the\_Application\_of\_Mathematical\_Analysis\_to\_the\_Theories\_of\_Electricity\_and\_Magnetism "An Essay on the Application of Mathematical Analysis to the Theories of Electricity and Magnetism")_,[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divergence_theorem#cite_note-18)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divergence_theorem#cite_note-:32-16)[Siméon Denis Poisson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sim%C3%A9on_Denis_Poisson "Siméon Denis Poisson") in 1824 in a paper on elasticity, and [Frédéric Sarrus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre_Fr%C3%A9d%C3%A9ric_Sarrus "Pierre Frédéric Sarrus") in 1828 in his work on floating bodies.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divergence_theorem#cite_note-19)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divergence_theorem#cite_note-:32-16)

At a point ![Image 228: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a) on ![Image 229: {\displaystyle C}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4fc55753007cd3c18576f7933f6f089196732029):

![Image 230: {\displaystyle P=(\cos(s),\,\sin(s))\,\Rightarrow \,\mathbf {F} =2\sin(s)\mathbf {i} +5\cos(s)\mathbf {j} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ec2d84385b7cc901e8b7285ea8c987c003b46315)
Therefore,

![Image 231: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\oint _{C}\mathbf {F} \cdot \mathbf {n} \,\mathrm {d} s&=\int _{0}^{2\pi }(2\sin(s)\mathbf {i} +5\cos(s)\mathbf {j} )\cdot (\cos(s)\mathbf {i} +\sin(s)\mathbf {j} )\,\mathrm {d} s\\&=\int _{0}^{2\pi }(2\sin(s)\cos(s)+5\sin(s)\cos(s))\,\mathrm {d} s\\&=7\int _{0}^{2\pi }\sin(s)\cos(s)\,\mathrm {d} s\\&=0.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f556f50ae639c0e9c62d2f9dff26462f39f3ed82)
Because ![Image 232: {\displaystyle M={\mathfrak {Re}}(\mathbf {F} )=2y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fb9d8ef750c375c7e03823bab2aab1c7ffcb5116), we can evaluate ![Image 233: {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial M}{\partial x}}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a458fcd7893bc38bc1706f0e11d99db35aa89a22), and because ![Image 234: {\displaystyle N={\mathfrak {Im}}(\mathbf {F} )=5x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/05941815b046b4d79480dbbe9ecb39598bb87bd9),![Image 235: {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial N}{\partial y}}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/349f440899b6346be3c3eb659a4a69c5afe114d1). Thus

![Image 236: {\displaystyle \iint _{R}\,\mathbf {\nabla } \cdot \mathbf {F} \,\mathrm {d} A=\iint _{R}\left({\frac {\partial M}{\partial x}}+{\frac {\partial N}{\partial y}}\right)\,\mathrm {d} A=0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/de70831b69cb27cea3db4df31bfc56f2a9bd4ae9)
Let's say we wanted to evaluate the flux of the following [vector field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_field "Vector field") defined by ![Image 237: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} =2x^{2}{\textbf {i}}+2y^{2}{\textbf {j}}+2z^{2}{\textbf {k}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/643c1926ce41a83bf5b1ad6fe596f5286f1f5061) bounded by the following inequalities:

![Image 238: {\displaystyle \left\{0\leq x\leq 3\right\},\left\{-2\leq y\leq 2\right\},\left\{0\leq z\leq 2\pi \right\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ab725e9f494481c4d1cee8471f8b41ea86f88154)
By the divergence theorem,

![Image 239: {\displaystyle \iiint _{V}\left(\mathbf {\nabla } \cdot \mathbf {F} \right)\mathrm {d} V=}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f6676abdf7280a77ad1bc9c1ed9485e85d4c189d)![Image 240: \oiint](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/OiintLaTeX.svg/40px-OiintLaTeX.svg.png)![Image 241: {\displaystyle \scriptstyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/62e91bf22a258bad807b5a9ae8068ef38202ca30)![Image 242: {\displaystyle (\mathbf {F} \cdot \mathbf {n} )\,\mathrm {d} S.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/51e3c7b399d285d34e0e0d3ebccbd547d0c2669d)
We now need to determine the divergence of ![Image 243: {\displaystyle {\textbf {F}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ae8dfd3569cacb065e8fe86487cfddefd5bfda8c). If ![Image 244: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/da18bef8c979f3548bb0d8976f5844012d7b8256) is a three-dimensional vector field, then the divergence of ![Image 245: {\displaystyle {\textbf {F}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ae8dfd3569cacb065e8fe86487cfddefd5bfda8c) is given by ![Image 246: {\textstyle \nabla \cdot {\textbf {F}}=\left({\frac {\partial }{\partial x}}{\textbf {i}}+{\frac {\partial }{\partial y}}{\textbf {j}}+{\frac {\partial }{\partial z}}{\textbf {k}}\right)\cdot {\textbf {F}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b9afbbedacf78c642c0fc00259461847c315f1fd).

Thus, we can set up the following flux integral ![Image 247: {\displaystyle I=}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/705a4b880769ca31b1b5e6692b7ec32de96ce88b)![Image 248: \oiint](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/OiintLaTeX.svg/40px-OiintLaTeX.svg.png)![Image 249: {\displaystyle {\scriptstyle S}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1dca5757c655f5ab53abd4b0d246dcfb478810e5)![Image 250: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} \cdot \mathbf {n} \,\mathrm {d} S,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ae2ee7324a804a4fde749c850d5e04dcbaf16391) as follows:

![Image 251: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}I&=\iiint _{V}\nabla \cdot \mathbf {F} \,\mathrm {d} V\\[6pt]&=\iiint _{V}\left({\frac {\partial \mathbf {F_{x}} }{\partial x}}+{\frac {\partial \mathbf {F_{y}} }{\partial y}}+{\frac {\partial \mathbf {F_{z}} }{\partial z}}\right)\mathrm {d} V\\[6pt]&=\iiint _{V}(4x+4y+4z)\,\mathrm {d} V\\[6pt]&=\int _{0}^{3}\int _{-2}^{2}\int _{0}^{2\pi }(4x+4y+4z)\,\mathrm {d} V\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d474d797d9611bde7396b5ad3c477fc3954e40fb)
Now that we have set up the integral, we can evaluate it.

![Image 252: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\int _{0}^{3}\int _{-2}^{2}\int _{0}^{2\pi }(4x+4y+4z)\,\mathrm {d} V&=\int _{-2}^{2}\int _{0}^{2\pi }(12y+12z+18)\,\mathrm {d} y\,\mathrm {d} z\\[6pt]&=\int _{0}^{2\pi }24(2z+3)\,\mathrm {d} z\\[6pt]&=48\pi (2\pi +3)\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/620b3ddd2e22372f036668159bdb83c2be45446e)

### Multiple dimensions

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Divergence_theorem&action=edit&section=18 "Edit section: Multiple dimensions")]

One can use the [generalised Stokes' theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generalized_Stokes%27_theorem "Generalized Stokes' theorem") to equate the n-dimensional volume integral of the divergence of a vector field **F** over a region U to the (_n_ − 1)-dimensional surface integral of **F** over the boundary of U:

![Image 253: {\displaystyle \underbrace {\int \cdots \int _{U}} _{n}\nabla \cdot \mathbf {F} \,\mathrm {d} V=\underbrace {\oint _{}\cdots \oint _{\partial U}} _{n-1}\mathbf {F} \cdot \mathbf {n} \,\mathrm {d} S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8bde99ec8e7048a35f35cb8eaeec290fb781b3f8)
This equation is also known as the divergence theorem.

When _n_ = 2, this is equivalent to [Green's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green%27s_theorem "Green's theorem").

When _n_ = 1, it reduces to the [fundamental theorem of calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_theorem_of_calculus "Fundamental theorem of calculus"), part 2.

Writing the theorem in [Einstein notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_notation "Einstein notation"):

![Image 254: {\displaystyle \iiint _{V}{\dfrac {\partial \mathbf {F} _{i}}{\partial x_{i}}}\mathrm {d} V=}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9509eb1937881c3f261ff92fe5edd2e1d3b3a987)![Image 255: \oiint](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/OiintLaTeX.svg/40px-OiintLaTeX.svg.png)![Image 256: {\displaystyle \scriptstyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/62e91bf22a258bad807b5a9ae8068ef38202ca30)![Image 257: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} _{i}n_{i}\,\mathrm {d} S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/59cf3e8937766f60033f44c6b20b2cf98cddaef8)
suggestively, replacing the vector field **F** with a rank-n tensor field T, this can be generalized to:[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divergence_theorem#cite_note-20)

![Image 258: {\displaystyle \iiint _{V}{\dfrac {\partial T_{i_{1}i_{2}\cdots i_{q}\cdots i_{n}}}{\partial x_{i_{q}}}}\mathrm {d} V=}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a98108e7f8a8b67c88d38d5738ed2585823cca1c)![Image 259: \oiint](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/OiintLaTeX.svg/40px-OiintLaTeX.svg.png)![Image 260: {\displaystyle \scriptstyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/62e91bf22a258bad807b5a9ae8068ef38202ca30)![Image 261: {\displaystyle T_{i_{1}i_{2}\cdots i_{q}\cdots i_{n}}n_{i_{q}}\,\mathrm {d} S.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/63be79e5156d609aa4dfc1ae5ffc772a42700489)
where on each side, [tensor contraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_contraction "Tensor contraction") occurs for at least one index. This form of the theorem is still in 3d, each index takes values 1, 2, and 3. It can be generalized further still to higher (or lower) dimensions (for example to 4d [spacetime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spacetime "Spacetime") in [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity")[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divergence_theorem#cite_note-21)).
