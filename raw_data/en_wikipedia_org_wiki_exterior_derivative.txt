On a [differentiable manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differentiable_manifold "Differentiable manifold"), the **exterior derivative** extends the concept of the [differential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pushforward_(differential) "Pushforward (differential)") of a function to [differential forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_form "Differential form") of higher degree. The exterior derivative was first described in its current form by [Élie Cartan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%89lie_Cartan "Élie Cartan") in 1899. The resulting calculus, known as [exterior calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_calculus "Exterior calculus"), allows for a natural, metric-independent generalization of [Stokes' theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stokes%27_theorem "Stokes' theorem"), [Gauss's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauss%27s_theorem "Gauss's theorem"), and [Green's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green%27s_theorem "Green's theorem") from vector calculus.

If a differential _k_-form is thought of as measuring the [flux](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flux "Flux") through an infinitesimal _k_-[parallelotope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelepiped#Parallelotope "Parallelepiped") at each point of the manifold, then its exterior derivative can be thought of as measuring the net flux through the boundary of a (_k_ + 1)-parallelotope at each point.

Definition
----------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Exterior_derivative&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Definition")]

The exterior derivative of a [differential form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_form "Differential form") of degree _k_ (also differential _k_-form, or just _k_-form for brevity here) is a differential form of degree _k_ + 1.

If _f_ is a [smooth function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smoothness "Smoothness") (a 0-form), then the exterior derivative of _f_ is the [differential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pushforward_(differential) "Pushforward (differential)") of _f_. That is, _df_ is the unique [1-form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1-form "1-form") such that for every smooth [vector field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_field#Vector_fields_on_manifolds "Vector field")_X_, _df_ (_X_) = _d_ _X_ _f_, where _d_ _X_ _f_ is the [directional derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Directional_derivative "Directional derivative") of _f_ in the direction of _X_.

The exterior product of differential forms (denoted with the same symbol ∧) is defined as their [pointwise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pointwise "Pointwise")[exterior product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_product "Exterior product").

There are a variety of equivalent definitions of the exterior derivative of a general _k_-form.

### In terms of local coordinates

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Exterior_derivative&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: In terms of local coordinates")]

Alternatively, one can work entirely in a [local coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_coordinate_system "Local coordinate system")(_x_ 1, ..., _x_ _n_). The coordinate differentials _dx_ 1, ..., _dx_ _n_ form a basis of the space of one-forms, each associated with a coordinate. Given a [multi-index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multi-index "Multi-index")_I_ = (_i_ 1, ..., _i_ _k_) with 1 ≤ _i_ _p_ ≤ _n_ for 1 ≤ _p_ ≤ _k_ (and denoting _dx_ _i_ 1 ∧ ... ∧ _dx_ _i_ _k_ with _dx_ _I_), the exterior derivative of a (simple) _k_-form

φ=g d x I=g d x i 1∧d x i 2∧⋯∧d x i k{\displaystyle \varphi =g\,dx^{I}=g\,dx^{i_{1}}\wedge dx^{i_{2}}\wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{i_{k}}}![Image 77: {\displaystyle \varphi =g\,dx^{I}=g\,dx^{i_{1}}\wedge dx^{i_{2}}\wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{i_{k}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/191277e0b4a0587a0d76e89fb02f5b15e6773552)
over ℝ _n_ is defined as

d φ=d g∧d x i 1∧d x i 2∧⋯∧d x i k=∂g∂x j d x j∧d x i 1∧d x i 2∧⋯∧d x i k{\displaystyle d{\varphi }=dg\wedge dx^{i_{1}}\wedge dx^{i_{2}}\wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{i_{k}}={\frac {\partial g}{\partial x^{j}}}\,dx^{j}\wedge \,dx^{i_{1}}\wedge dx^{i_{2}}\wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{i_{k}}}![Image 78: {\displaystyle d{\varphi }=dg\wedge dx^{i_{1}}\wedge dx^{i_{2}}\wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{i_{k}}={\frac {\partial g}{\partial x^{j}}}\,dx^{j}\wedge \,dx^{i_{1}}\wedge dx^{i_{2}}\wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{i_{k}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/21dd0ea6ad128cd93bf8172450977e05c388e686)
(using the [Einstein summation convention](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_notation "Einstein notation")). The definition of the exterior derivative is extended [linearly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear "Linear") to a general _k_-form (which is expressible as a linear combination of basic simple k{\displaystyle k}![Image 79: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40)-forms)

ω=f I d x I,{\displaystyle \omega =f_{I}\,dx^{I},}![Image 80: {\displaystyle \omega =f_{I}\,dx^{I},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2a6691871fb523818f532d1c47df2bcea29b9b4f)
where each of the components of the multi-index _I_ run over all the values in {1, ..., _n_}. Note that whenever _j_ equals one of the components of the multi-index _I_ then _dx_ _j_ ∧ _dx_ _I_ = 0 (see _[Exterior product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior\_product "Exterior product")_).

The definition of the exterior derivative in local coordinates follows from the preceding [definition in terms of axioms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_derivative#In_terms_of_axioms). Indeed, with the _k_-form _φ_ as defined above,

d φ=d(g d x i 1∧⋯∧d x i k)=d g∧(d x i 1∧⋯∧d x i k)+g d(d x i 1∧⋯∧d x i k)=d g∧d x i 1∧⋯∧d x i k+g∑p=1 k(−1)p−1 d x i 1∧⋯∧d x i p−1∧d 2 x i p∧d x i p+1∧⋯∧d x i k=d g∧d x i 1∧⋯∧d x i k=∂g∂x i d x i∧d x i 1∧⋯∧d x i k{\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}d{\varphi }&=d\left(g\,dx^{i_{1}}\wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{i_{k}}\right)\\&=dg\wedge \left(dx^{i_{1}}\wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{i_{k}}\right)+g\,d\left(dx^{i_{1}}\wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{i_{k}}\right)\\&=dg\wedge dx^{i_{1}}\wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{i_{k}}+g\sum _{p=1}^{k}(-1)^{p-1}\,dx^{i_{1}}\wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{i_{p-1}}\wedge d^{2}x^{i_{p}}\wedge dx^{i_{p+1}}\wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{i_{k}}\\&=dg\wedge dx^{i_{1}}\wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{i_{k}}\\&={\frac {\partial g}{\partial x^{i}}}\,dx^{i}\wedge dx^{i_{1}}\wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{i_{k}}\\\end{aligned}}}![Image 81: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}d{\varphi }&=d\left(g\,dx^{i_{1}}\wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{i_{k}}\right)\\&=dg\wedge \left(dx^{i_{1}}\wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{i_{k}}\right)+g\,d\left(dx^{i_{1}}\wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{i_{k}}\right)\\&=dg\wedge dx^{i_{1}}\wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{i_{k}}+g\sum _{p=1}^{k}(-1)^{p-1}\,dx^{i_{1}}\wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{i_{p-1}}\wedge d^{2}x^{i_{p}}\wedge dx^{i_{p+1}}\wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{i_{k}}\\&=dg\wedge dx^{i_{1}}\wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{i_{k}}\\&={\frac {\partial g}{\partial x^{i}}}\,dx^{i}\wedge dx^{i_{1}}\wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{i_{k}}\\\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cfcb4b648effdd5caa14ada6b3f4eabe30595848)
Here, we have interpreted _g_ as a 0-form, and then applied the properties of the exterior derivative.

This result extends directly to the general _k_-form _ω_ as

d ω=∂f I∂x i d x i∧d x I.{\displaystyle d\omega ={\frac {\partial f_{I}}{\partial x^{i}}}\,dx^{i}\wedge dx^{I}.}![Image 82: {\displaystyle d\omega ={\frac {\partial f_{I}}{\partial x^{i}}}\,dx^{i}\wedge dx^{I}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/09b6a62f0c64ab4e269481209ad6dbd87bd3eec0)
In particular, for a 1-form _ω_, the components of _dω_ in [local coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_coordinate_system "Local coordinate system") are

(d ω)i j=∂i ω j−∂j ω i.{\displaystyle (d\omega )_{ij}=\partial _{i}\omega _{j}-\partial _{j}\omega _{i}.}![Image 83: {\displaystyle (d\omega )_{ij}=\partial _{i}\omega _{j}-\partial _{j}\omega _{i}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9d0ce9471f7dc6ced68c1dcc333bd1f6b95cf8c3)
_Caution_: There are two conventions regarding the meaning of d x i 1∧⋯∧d x i k{\displaystyle dx^{i_{1}}\wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{i_{k}}}![Image 84: {\displaystyle dx^{i_{1}}\wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{i_{k}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e8ba56d6cd59851a7f0d7ba20b5dba51938d6b00). Most current authors[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] have the convention that

(d x i 1∧⋯∧d x i k)(∂∂x i 1,…,∂∂x i k)=1.{\displaystyle \left(dx^{i_{1}}\wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{i_{k}}\right)\left({\frac {\partial }{\partial x^{i_{1}}}},\ldots ,{\frac {\partial }{\partial x^{i_{k}}}}\right)=1.}![Image 85: {\displaystyle \left(dx^{i_{1}}\wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{i_{k}}\right)\left({\frac {\partial }{\partial x^{i_{1}}}},\ldots ,{\frac {\partial }{\partial x^{i_{k}}}}\right)=1.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bc4a628d897281f04eae35d8858d4e49af907bc0)
while in older texts like Kobayashi and Nomizu or Helgason

(d x i 1∧⋯∧d x i k)(∂∂x i 1,…,∂∂x i k)=1 k!.{\displaystyle \left(dx^{i_{1}}\wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{i_{k}}\right)\left({\frac {\partial }{\partial x^{i_{1}}}},\ldots ,{\frac {\partial }{\partial x^{i_{k}}}}\right)={\frac {1}{k!}}.}![Image 86: {\displaystyle \left(dx^{i_{1}}\wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{i_{k}}\right)\left({\frac {\partial }{\partial x^{i_{1}}}},\ldots ,{\frac {\partial }{\partial x^{i_{k}}}}\right)={\frac {1}{k!}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c0f2b7fedda4963d659d3c7e4d87c9c6b59a0f20)

### In terms of invariant formula

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Exterior_derivative&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: In terms of invariant formula")]

Alternatively, an explicit formula can be given [[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_derivative#cite_note-1) for the exterior derivative of a _k_-form _ω_, when paired with _k_ + 1 arbitrary smooth [vector fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_field "Vector field")_V_ 0, _V_ 1, ..., _V_ _k_:

d ω(V 0,…,V k)=∑i(−1)i V i(ω(V 0,…,V^i,…,V k))+∑i<j(−1)i+j ω([V i,V j],V 0,…,V^i,…,V^j,…,V k){\displaystyle d\omega (V_{0},\ldots ,V_{k})=\sum _{i}(-1)^{i}V_{i}(\omega (V_{0},\ldots ,{\widehat {V}}_{i},\ldots ,V_{k}))+\sum _{i<j}(-1)^{i+j}\omega ([V_{i},V_{j}],V_{0},\ldots ,{\widehat {V}}_{i},\ldots ,{\widehat {V}}_{j},\ldots ,V_{k})}![Image 87: {\displaystyle d\omega (V_{0},\ldots ,V_{k})=\sum _{i}(-1)^{i}V_{i}(\omega (V_{0},\ldots ,{\widehat {V}}_{i},\ldots ,V_{k}))+\sum _{i<j}(-1)^{i+j}\omega ([V_{i},V_{j}],V_{0},\ldots ,{\widehat {V}}_{i},\ldots ,{\widehat {V}}_{j},\ldots ,V_{k})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f97e58ff8e6f8ba4c1e1c782e48e96865dcd416c)
where [_V i_, _V j_] denotes the [Lie bracket](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_bracket_of_vector_fields "Lie bracket of vector fields") and a hat denotes the omission of that element:

ω(V 0,…,V^i,…,V k)=ω(V 0,…,V i−1,V i+1,…,V k).{\displaystyle \omega (V_{0},\ldots ,{\widehat {V}}_{i},\ldots ,V_{k})=\omega (V_{0},\ldots ,V_{i-1},V_{i+1},\ldots ,V_{k}).}![Image 88: {\displaystyle \omega (V_{0},\ldots ,{\widehat {V}}_{i},\ldots ,V_{k})=\omega (V_{0},\ldots ,V_{i-1},V_{i+1},\ldots ,V_{k}).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fbbd57f8ef2101e13f52f208e0a3252acf115b0b)
In particular, when _ω_ is a 1-form we have that _dω_(_X_, _Y_) = _d_ _X_(_ω_(_Y_)) − _d_ _Y_(_ω_(_X_)) − _ω_([_X_, _Y_]).

d ω(V 0,…,V k)=1 k+1∑i(−1)i V i(ω(V 0,…,V^i,…,V k))+1 k+1∑i<j(−1)i+j ω([V i,V j],V 0,…,V^i,…,V^j,…,V k).{\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}d\omega (V_{0},\ldots ,V_{k})={}&{1 \over k+1}\sum _{i}(-1)^{i}\,V_{i}(\omega (V_{0},\ldots ,{\widehat {V}}_{i},\ldots ,V_{k}))\\&{}+{1 \over k+1}\sum _{i<j}(-1)^{i+j}\omega ([V_{i},V_{j}],V_{0},\ldots ,{\widehat {V}}_{i},\ldots ,{\widehat {V}}_{j},\ldots ,V_{k}).\end{aligned}}}![Image 89: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}d\omega (V_{0},\ldots ,V_{k})={}&{1 \over k+1}\sum _{i}(-1)^{i}\,V_{i}(\omega (V_{0},\ldots ,{\widehat {V}}_{i},\ldots ,V_{k}))\\&{}+{1 \over k+1}\sum _{i<j}(-1)^{i+j}\omega ([V_{i},V_{j}],V_{0},\ldots ,{\widehat {V}}_{i},\ldots ,{\widehat {V}}_{j},\ldots ,V_{k}).\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/40e205fa277b68a1d588ce1f07424caab7cce6c1)

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Exterior_derivative&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Examples")]

**Example 1.** Consider _σ_ = _u_ _dx_ 1 ∧ _dx_ 2 over a 1-form basis _dx_ 1, ..., _dx_ _n_ for a scalar field _u_. The exterior derivative is:

d σ=d u∧d x 1∧d x 2=(∑i=1 n∂u∂x i d x i)∧d x 1∧d x 2=∑i=3 n(∂u∂x i d x i∧d x 1∧d x 2){\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}d\sigma &=du\wedge dx^{1}\wedge dx^{2}\\&=\left(\sum _{i=1}^{n}{\frac {\partial u}{\partial x^{i}}}\,dx^{i}\right)\wedge dx^{1}\wedge dx^{2}\\&=\sum _{i=3}^{n}\left({\frac {\partial u}{\partial x^{i}}}\,dx^{i}\wedge dx^{1}\wedge dx^{2}\right)\end{aligned}}}![Image 90: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}d\sigma &=du\wedge dx^{1}\wedge dx^{2}\\&=\left(\sum _{i=1}^{n}{\frac {\partial u}{\partial x^{i}}}\,dx^{i}\right)\wedge dx^{1}\wedge dx^{2}\\&=\sum _{i=3}^{n}\left({\frac {\partial u}{\partial x^{i}}}\,dx^{i}\wedge dx^{1}\wedge dx^{2}\right)\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/86686f7a43c5173451db6ab4311934dfc67704b5)
The last formula, where summation starts at _i_ = 3, follows easily from the properties of the [exterior product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_product "Exterior product"). Namely, _dx_ _i_ ∧ _dx_ _i_ = 0.

**Example 2.** Let _σ_ = _u_ _dx_ + _v_ _dy_ be a 1-form defined over ℝ 2. By applying the above formula to each term (consider _x_ 1 = _x_ and _x_ 2 = _y_) we have the sum

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Exterior_derivative&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Stokes' theorem on manifolds")]

Main article: [Generalized Stokes' theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generalized_Stokes%27_theorem "Generalized Stokes' theorem")

If _M_ is a compact smooth orientable _n_-dimensional manifold with boundary, and _ω_ is an (_n_ − 1)-form on _M_, then [the generalized form of Stokes' theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generalized_Stokes%27_theorem "Generalized Stokes' theorem") states that

∫M d ω=∫∂M ω{\displaystyle \int _{M}d\omega =\int _{\partial {M}}\omega }![Image 92: {\displaystyle \int _{M}d\omega =\int _{\partial {M}}\omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/945945319b3949e79bb9d23b58f0937c41141b63)
Intuitively, if one thinks of _M_ as being divided into infinitesimal regions, and one adds the flux through the boundaries of all the regions, the interior boundaries all cancel out, leaving the total flux through the boundary of _M_.

Further properties
------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Exterior_derivative&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Further properties")]

### Closed and exact forms

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Exterior_derivative&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Closed and exact forms")]

Main article: [Closed and exact forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_and_exact_forms "Closed and exact forms")

A _k_-form _ω_ is called _closed_ if _dω_ = 0; closed forms are the [kernel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kernel_(algebra) "Kernel (algebra)") of _d_. _ω_ is called _exact_ if _ω_ = _dα_ for some (_k_ − 1)-form _α_; exact forms are the [image](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Image_(mathematics) "Image (mathematics)") of _d_. Because _d_ 2 = 0, every exact form is closed. The [Poincaré lemma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poincar%C3%A9_lemma "Poincaré lemma") states that in a contractible region, the converse is true.

### de Rham cohomology

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Exterior_derivative&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: de Rham cohomology")]

Because the exterior derivative _d_ has the property that _d_ 2 = 0, it can be used as the [differential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cochain_complex "Cochain complex") (coboundary) to define [de Rham cohomology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_Rham_cohomology "De Rham cohomology") on a manifold. The _k_-th de Rham cohomology (group) is the vector space of closed _k_-forms modulo the exact _k_-forms; as noted in the previous section, the Poincaré lemma states that these vector spaces are trivial for a contractible region, for _k_> 0. For [smooth manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smooth_manifold "Smooth manifold"), integration of forms gives a natural homomorphism from the de Rham cohomology to the singular cohomology over ℝ. The theorem of de Rham shows that this map is actually an isomorphism, a far-reaching generalization of the Poincaré lemma. As suggested by the generalized Stokes' theorem, the exterior derivative is the "dual" of the [boundary map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chain_complex#Formal_definition "Chain complex") on singular simplices.

### Naturality

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Exterior_derivative&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Naturality")]

The exterior derivative is natural in the technical sense: if _f_: _M_ → _N_ is a smooth map and Ω _k_ is the contravariant smooth [functor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functor "Functor") that assigns to each manifold the space of _k_-forms on the manifold, then the following diagram commutes

[![Image 93](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/bf/Exteriorderivnatural.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Exteriorderivnatural.png)

so _d_( _f_∗_ω_) = _f_∗_dω_, where _f_∗ denotes the [pullback](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pullback_(differential_geometry) "Pullback (differential geometry)") of _f_. This follows from that _f_∗_ω_(·), by definition, is _ω_( _f_∗(·)), _f_∗ being the [pushforward](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pushforward_(differential) "Pushforward (differential)") of _f_. Thus _d_ is a [natural transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_transformation "Natural transformation") from Ω _k_ to Ω _k_+1.

Exterior derivative in vector calculus
--------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Exterior_derivative&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Exterior derivative in vector calculus")]

Most [vector calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_calculus "Vector calculus") operators are special cases of, or have close relationships to, the notion of exterior differentiation.

### Gradient

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Exterior_derivative&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Gradient")]

A [smooth function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smooth_function "Smooth function")_f_: _M_ → ℝ on a real differentiable manifold _M_ is a 0-form. The exterior derivative of this 0-form is the 1-form _df_.

When an inner product ⟨·,·⟩ is defined, the [gradient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gradient "Gradient")∇_f_ of a function _f_ is defined as the unique vector in _V_ such that its inner product with any element of _V_ is the directional derivative of _f_ along the vector, that is such that

⟨∇f,⋅⟩=d f=∑i=1 n∂f∂x i d x i.{\displaystyle \langle \nabla f,\cdot \rangle =df=\sum _{i=1}^{n}{\frac {\partial f}{\partial x^{i}}}\,dx^{i}.}![Image 94: {\displaystyle \langle \nabla f,\cdot \rangle =df=\sum _{i=1}^{n}{\frac {\partial f}{\partial x^{i}}}\,dx^{i}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1f75d8a5905d837fe167e995f37848fc4d43cdeb)
That is,

∇f=(d f)♯=∑i=1 n∂f∂x i(d x i)♯,{\displaystyle \nabla f=(df)^{\sharp }=\sum _{i=1}^{n}{\frac {\partial f}{\partial x^{i}}}\,\left(dx^{i}\right)^{\sharp },}![Image 95: {\displaystyle \nabla f=(df)^{\sharp }=\sum _{i=1}^{n}{\frac {\partial f}{\partial x^{i}}}\,\left(dx^{i}\right)^{\sharp },}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e8382ed383109a5a90ea12bfceb4a270e4147c1)
where ♯ denotes the [musical isomorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Musical_isomorphism "Musical isomorphism")♯: _V_∗ → _V_ mentioned earlier that is induced by the inner product.

The 1-form _df_ is a section of the [cotangent bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cotangent_bundle "Cotangent bundle"), that gives a local linear approximation to _f_ in the cotangent space at each point.

### Divergence

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Exterior_derivative&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Divergence")]

A vector field _V_ = (_v_ 1, _v_ 2, ..., _v n_) on ℝ _n_ has a corresponding (_n_ − 1)-form

ω V=v 1(d x 2∧⋯∧d x n)−v 2(d x 1∧d x 3∧⋯∧d x n)+⋯+(−1)n−1 v n(d x 1∧⋯∧d x n−1)=∑i=1 n(−1)(i−1)v i(d x 1∧⋯∧d x i−1∧d x i^∧d x i+1∧⋯∧d x n){\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\omega _{V}&=v_{1}\left(dx^{2}\wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{n}\right)-v_{2}\left(dx^{1}\wedge dx^{3}\wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{n}\right)+\cdots +(-1)^{n-1}v_{n}\left(dx^{1}\wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{n-1}\right)\\&=\sum _{i=1}^{n}(-1)^{(i-1)}v_{i}\left(dx^{1}\wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{i-1}\wedge {\widehat {dx^{i}}}\wedge dx^{i+1}\wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{n}\right)\end{aligned}}}![Image 96: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\omega _{V}&=v_{1}\left(dx^{2}\wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{n}\right)-v_{2}\left(dx^{1}\wedge dx^{3}\wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{n}\right)+\cdots +(-1)^{n-1}v_{n}\left(dx^{1}\wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{n-1}\right)\\&=\sum _{i=1}^{n}(-1)^{(i-1)}v_{i}\left(dx^{1}\wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{i-1}\wedge {\widehat {dx^{i}}}\wedge dx^{i+1}\wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{n}\right)\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cea77f7a8c54c5c3b84746b31429813e98489ef3)
where d x i^{\displaystyle {\widehat {dx^{i}}}}![Image 97: {\displaystyle {\widehat {dx^{i}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b67eae31c334c40f99ef1b2e7fcf93442c8315c3) denotes the omission of that element.

(For instance, when _n_ = 3, i.e. in three-dimensional space, the 2-form _ω V_ is locally the [scalar triple product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_triple_product "Scalar triple product") with _V_.) The integral of _ω V_ over a hypersurface is the [flux](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flux "Flux") of _V_ over that hypersurface.

The exterior derivative of this (_n_ − 1)-form is the _n_-form

d ω V=div⁡V(d x 1∧d x 2∧⋯∧d x n).{\displaystyle d\omega _{V}=\operatorname {div} V\left(dx^{1}\wedge dx^{2}\wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{n}\right).}![Image 98: {\displaystyle d\omega _{V}=\operatorname {div} V\left(dx^{1}\wedge dx^{2}\wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{n}\right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/28bc1c1e4a173031d36f8cbcbd50055607d449c5)

### Curl

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Exterior_derivative&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Curl")]

A vector field _V_ on ℝ _n_ also has a corresponding 1-form

η V=v 1 d x 1+v 2 d x 2+⋯+v n d x n.{\displaystyle \eta _{V}=v_{1}\,dx^{1}+v_{2}\,dx^{2}+\cdots +v_{n}\,dx^{n}.}![Image 99: {\displaystyle \eta _{V}=v_{1}\,dx^{1}+v_{2}\,dx^{2}+\cdots +v_{n}\,dx^{n}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a7a8b22045be59289907281fd5ee1c19d93b9b30)
Locally, _η V_ is the [dot product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dot_product "Dot product") with _V_. The integral of _η V_ along a path is the [work](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanical_work "Mechanical work") done against −_V_ along that path.

When _n_ = 3, in three-dimensional space, the exterior derivative of the 1-form _η V_ is the 2-form

d η V=ω curl⁡V.{\displaystyle d\eta _{V}=\omega _{\operatorname {curl} V}.}![Image 100: {\displaystyle d\eta _{V}=\omega _{\operatorname {curl} V}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d8ee630651aa93ae4878e019c589be49cf5870d0)

### Invariant formulations of operators in vector calculus

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Exterior_derivative&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: Invariant formulations of operators in vector calculus")]

The standard [vector calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_calculus "Vector calculus") operators can be generalized for any [pseudo-Riemannian manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudo-Riemannian_manifold "Pseudo-Riemannian manifold"), and written in coordinate-free notation as follows:

grad⁡f≡∇f=(d f)♯div⁡F≡∇⋅F=⋆d⋆(F♭)curl⁡F≡∇×F=(⋆d(F♭))♯Δ f≡∇2 f=⋆d⋆d f∇2 F=(d⋆d⋆(F♭)−⋆d⋆d(F♭))♯,{\displaystyle {\begin{array}{rcccl}\operatorname {grad} f&\equiv &\nabla f&=&\left(df\right)^{\sharp }\\\operatorname {div} F&\equiv &\nabla \cdot F&=&{\star d{\star }{\mathord {\left(F^{\flat }\right)}}}\\\operatorname {curl} F&\equiv &\nabla \times F&=&\left({\star }d{\mathord {\left(F^{\flat }\right)}}\right)^{\sharp }\\\Delta f&\equiv &\nabla ^{2}f&=&{\star }d{\star }df\\&&\nabla ^{2}F&=&\left(d{\star }d{\star }{\mathord {\left(F^{\flat }\right)}}-{\star }d{\star }d{\mathord {\left(F^{\flat }\right)}}\right)^{\sharp },\\\end{array}}}![Image 101: {\displaystyle {\begin{array}{rcccl}\operatorname {grad} f&\equiv &\nabla f&=&\left(df\right)^{\sharp }\\\operatorname {div} F&\equiv &\nabla \cdot F&=&{\star d{\star }{\mathord {\left(F^{\flat }\right)}}}\\\operatorname {curl} F&\equiv &\nabla \times F&=&\left({\star }d{\mathord {\left(F^{\flat }\right)}}\right)^{\sharp }\\\Delta f&\equiv &\nabla ^{2}f&=&{\star }d{\star }df\\&&\nabla ^{2}F&=&\left(d{\star }d{\star }{\mathord {\left(F^{\flat }\right)}}-{\star }d{\star }d{\mathord {\left(F^{\flat }\right)}}\right)^{\sharp },\\\end{array}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a3e50370cdcc920aabe57346d3dc6299622045d6)
where ⋆ is the [Hodge star operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hodge_dual "Hodge dual"), ♭ and ♯ are the [musical isomorphisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Musical_isomorphism "Musical isomorphism"), _f_ is a [scalar field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_field "Scalar field") and _F_ is a [vector field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_field "Vector field").

Note that the expression for curl requires ♯ to act on ⋆_d_(_F_♭), which is a form of degree _n_ − 2. A natural generalization of ♯ to _k_-forms of arbitrary degree allows this expression to make sense for any _n_.
