In [algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra "Algebra"), the **length** of a [module](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Module_(mathematics) "Module (mathematics)") over a ring ![Image 1: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) is a generalization of the [dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension_(vector_space) "Dimension (vector space)") of a [vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space") which measures its size.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Length_of_a_module#cite_note-:0-1)page 153 It is defined to be the length of the longest chain of [submodules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Submodule "Submodule"). For vector spaces (modules over a field), the length equals the dimension. If ![Image 2: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) is an algebra over a field ![Image 3: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40), the length of a module is at most its dimension as a ![Image 4: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40)-vector space.

In [commutative algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_algebra "Commutative algebra") and [algebraic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_geometry "Algebraic geometry"), a module over a [Noetherian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noetherian_ring "Noetherian ring") commutative ring ![Image 5: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) can have finite length only when the module has [Krull dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Krull_dimension "Krull dimension") zero. Modules of finite length are [finitely generated modules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finitely_generated_module "Finitely generated module"), but most finitely generated modules have infinite length. Modules of finite length are [Artinian modules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artinian_module "Artinian module") and are fundamental to the theory of [Artinian rings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artinian_ring "Artinian ring").

The [degree of an algebraic variety](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_of_an_algebraic_variety "Degree of an algebraic variety") inside an affine or projective space is the length of the coordinate ring of the zero-dimensional intersection of the variety with a [generic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_position "General position") linear subspace of complementary dimension. More generally, the [intersection multiplicity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intersection_multiplicity "Intersection multiplicity") of several varieties is defined as the length of the coordinate ring of the zero-dimensional intersection.

Let ![Image 6: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) be a (left or right) module over some [ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ring_(mathematics) "Ring (mathematics)")![Image 7: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33). Given a chain of submodules of ![Image 8: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) of the form

![Image 9: {\displaystyle M_{0}\subsetneq M_{1}\subsetneq \cdots \subsetneq M_{n},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/14386af1c7abc174a44780dbd6745f0b46a38fcb)
one says that ![Image 10: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) is the _length_ of the chain.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Length_of_a_module#cite_note-:0-1) The _length_ of ![Image 11: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) is the largest length of any of its chains. If no such largest length exists, we say that ![Image 12: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) has _infinite length_. Clearly, if the length of a chain equals the length of the module, one has ![Image 13: {\displaystyle M_{0}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1ca9fd6d09577ac07580d071a5194b6e4f8e0439) and ![Image 14: {\displaystyle M_{n}=M.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1aa496a8dbcbbdc438654746b447dec72034b3e3)

The length of a ring ![Image 15: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) is the length of the longest chain of [ideals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ideal_(ring_theory) "Ideal (ring theory)"); that is, the length of ![Image 16: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) considered as a module over itself by left multiplication. By contrast, the [Krull dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Krull_dimension "Krull dimension") of ![Image 17: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) is the length of the longest chain of [_prime_ ideals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_ideal "Prime ideal").

### Finite length and finite modules

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Length_of_a_module&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Finite length and finite modules")]

If an ![Image 18: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33)-module ![Image 19: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) has finite length, then it is [finitely generated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finitely_generated_module "Finitely generated module").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Length_of_a_module#cite_note-2) If _R_ is a field, then the converse is also true.

### Relation to Artinian and Noetherian modules

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Length_of_a_module&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Relation to Artinian and Noetherian modules")]

An ![Image 20: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33)-module ![Image 21: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) has finite length if and only if it is both a [Noetherian module](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noetherian_module "Noetherian module") and an [Artinian module](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artinian_module "Artinian module")[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Length_of_a_module#cite_note-:0-1) (cf. [Hopkins' theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hopkins%27_theorem "Hopkins' theorem")). Since all Artinian rings are Noetherian, this implies that a ring has finite length if and only if it is Artinian.

### Behavior with respect to short exact sequences

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Length_of_a_module&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Behavior with respect to short exact sequences")]

Suppose![Image 22: {\displaystyle 0\rightarrow L\rightarrow M\rightarrow N\rightarrow 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3765e7a3848b469b926746efebcb66eb7905c864)is a [short exact sequence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Short_exact_sequence "Short exact sequence") of ![Image 23: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33)-modules. Then M has finite length if and only if _L_ and _N_ have finite length, and we have ![Image 24: {\displaystyle {\text{length}}_{R}(M)={\text{length}}_{R}(L)+{\text{length}}_{R}(N)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a3c24104e74642f3a7d701ed3c86bca7430cabf5) In particular, it implies the following two properties

*   The direct sum of two modules of finite length has finite length
*   The submodule of a module with finite length has finite length, and its length is less than or equal to its parent module.

### Jordan–Hölder theorem

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Length_of_a_module&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Jordan–Hölder theorem")]

A [composition series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Composition_series "Composition series") of the module _M_ is a chain of the form

![Image 25: {\displaystyle 0=N_{0}\subsetneq N_{1}\subsetneq \cdots \subsetneq N_{n}=M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1d7934c386d2ede82c2032f818d3745f84ffc668)
such that

![Image 26: {\displaystyle N_{i+1}/N_{i}{\text{ is simple for }}i=0,\dots ,n-1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7f4330b30477ca632d209e32808f8ea82c75827a)
A module _M_ has finite length if and only if it has a (finite) composition series, and the length of every such composition series is equal to the length of _M_.

### Finite dimensional vector spaces

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Length_of_a_module&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Finite dimensional vector spaces")]

Any finite dimensional vector space ![Image 27: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) over a field ![Image 28: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40) has a finite length. Given a basis ![Image 29: {\displaystyle v_{1},\ldots ,v_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fb40a91abab8b7bfb0e84b074732b2f044fd56ef) there is the chain![Image 30: {\displaystyle 0\subset {\text{Span}}_{k}(v_{1})\subset {\text{Span}}_{k}(v_{1},v_{2})\subset \cdots \subset {\text{Span}}_{k}(v_{1},\ldots ,v_{n})=V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/89893776729efacabd24b5b90a452ec74e67c23c)which is of length ![Image 31: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b). It is maximal because given any chain,![Image 32: {\displaystyle V_{0}\subset \cdots \subset V_{m}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4d05ef317287d57749aef455a5f58f5065f77ccc)the dimension of each inclusion will increase by at least ![Image 33: {\displaystyle 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/92d98b82a3778f043108d4e20960a9193df57cbf). Therefore, its length and dimension coincide.

Over a base ring ![Image 34: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33), [Artinian modules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artinian_module "Artinian module") form a class of examples of finite modules. In fact, these examples serve as the basic tools for defining the order of vanishing in [intersection theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intersection_theory "Intersection theory").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Length_of_a_module#cite_note-:1-3)

The zero module is the only one with length 0.

Modules with length 1 are precisely the [simple modules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simple_module "Simple module").

#### Artinian modules over Z

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Length_of_a_module&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Artinian modules over Z")]

The length of the [cyclic group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclic_group "Cyclic group")![Image 35: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} /n\mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f2120ebbc85f91df66c6de5446367bf9fd620844) (viewed as a module over the [integers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer")**Z**) is equal to the number of [prime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_number "Prime number") factors of ![Image 36: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b), with multiple prime factors counted multiple times. This follows from the fact that the submodules of ![Image 37: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} /n\mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f2120ebbc85f91df66c6de5446367bf9fd620844) are in one to one correspondence with the positive divisors of ![Image 38: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b), this correspondence resulting itself from the fact that ![Image 39: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/449494a083e0a1fda2b61c62b2f09b6bee4633dc) is a [principal ideal ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principal_ideal_ring "Principal ideal ring").

Use in multiplicity theory
--------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Length_of_a_module&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: Use in multiplicity theory")]

For the needs of [intersection theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intersection_theory "Intersection theory"), [Jean-Pierre Serre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean-Pierre_Serre "Jean-Pierre Serre") introduced a general notion of the [multiplicity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplicity_(mathematics) "Multiplicity (mathematics)") of a point, as the length of an [Artinian local ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artinian_local_ring "Artinian local ring") related to this point.

The first application was a complete definition of the [intersection multiplicity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intersection_multiplicity "Intersection multiplicity"), and, in particular, a statement of [Bézout's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/B%C3%A9zout%27s_theorem "Bézout's theorem") that asserts that the sum of the multiplicities of the intersection points of n[algebraic hypersurfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypersurface "Hypersurface") in a n-dimensional [projective space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_space "Projective space") is either infinite or is _exactly_ the product of the degrees of the hypersurfaces.

This definition of multiplicity is quite general, and contains as special cases most of previous notions of algebraic multiplicity.

#### Zero and poles of an analytic function

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Length_of_a_module&action=edit&section=18 "Edit section: Zero and poles of an analytic function")]

The order of vanishing is a generalization of the order of zeros and poles for [meromorphic functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meromorphic_function "Meromorphic function") in [complex analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_analysis "Complex analysis"). For example, the function![Image 72: {\displaystyle {\frac {(z-1)^{3}(z-2)}{(z-1)(z-4i)}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b7fe7b678a1a21e7f2977a4a781433e3797b4684)has zeros of order 2 and 1 at ![Image 73: {\displaystyle 1,2\in \mathbb {C} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a43d6267a8c7a7c48fa1cc6af4cb32d58069f2ea) and a pole of order ![Image 74: {\displaystyle 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/92d98b82a3778f043108d4e20960a9193df57cbf) at ![Image 75: {\displaystyle 4i\in \mathbb {C} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/297b9fcf7ea44ebf83b52ecd20fc15e64fab57cb). This kind of information can be encoded using the length of modules. V=V(z-1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/79c4c67368b9134a5e98e04e98aba7b6cac22204), there is the associated local ring ![Image 78: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {O}}_{V,X}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b01902ca91d62991961f3ea0a9f8558d1aaf5ca5) is ![Image 79: {\displaystyle \mathbb {C} [z]_{(z-1)}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1d4c86b7f9f95e6ec7746daeb03587c5f03085d2) and the quotient module ![Image 80: {\displaystyle {\frac {\mathbb {C} [z]_{(z-1)}}{((z-4i)(z-1)^{2})}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/23be5657f8a4da7e578d251471523f082257777f)Note that ![Image 81: {\displaystyle {\mathbb {C} [z]_{(z-1)}}{((z-1)^{2})}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c90212fe948d3fe1baf629aa41ff14d0d6343dd5)Its length is ![Image 83: {\displaystyle denominator.
