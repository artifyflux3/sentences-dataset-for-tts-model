Continuum limit in lattice models

[](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:2D_Random_Walk_400x400.ogv "Play video")Duration: 11 minutes and 7 seconds.11:07[](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:2D_Random_Walk_400x400.ogv)

An animated example of a [Brownian motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brownian_motion "Brownian motion")-like [random walk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Random_walk "Random walk") on a [torus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torus "Torus"). In the scaling limit, random walk approaches the [Wiener process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wiener_process "Wiener process") according to [Donsker's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Donsker%27s_theorem "Donsker's theorem").

In [mathematical physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_physics "Mathematical physics") and [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), the **continuum limit** or **scaling limit** of a [lattice model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lattice_model_(physics) "Lattice model (physics)") characterizes its behaviour in the limit as the lattice spacing goes to zero. It is often useful to use lattice models to approximate real-world processes, such as [Brownian motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brownian_motion "Brownian motion"). Indeed, according to [Donsker's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Donsker%27s_theorem "Donsker's theorem"), the discrete [random walk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Random_walk "Random walk") would, in the scaling limit, approach the true [Brownian motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brownian_motion "Brownian motion").

Terminology
-----------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Continuum_limit&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Terminology")]

The term _continuum limit_ mostly finds use in the physical sciences, often in reference to models of aspects of [quantum physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_physics "Quantum physics"), while the term _scaling limit_ is more common in mathematical use.

Application in quantum field theory
-----------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Continuum_limit&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Application in quantum field theory")]

[![Image 5](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/60px-Question_book-new.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg)This section **does not [cite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources "Wikipedia:Citing sources") any [sources](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability "Wikipedia:Verifiability")**. Please help [improve this section](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:EditPage/Continuum_limit "Special:EditPage/Continuum limit") by [adding citations to reliable sources](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:Referencing_for_beginners "Help:Referencing for beginners"). Unsourced material may be challenged and [removed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Burden_of_evidence "Wikipedia:Verifiability")._(December 2022)_ _([Learn how and when to remove this message](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:Maintenance\_template\_removal "Help:Maintenance template removal"))_

A lattice model that approximates a [continuum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuum_(theory) "Continuum (theory)")[quantum field theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_field_theory "Quantum field theory") in the limit as the lattice spacing goes to zero may correspond to finding a [second order phase transition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_order_phase_transition "Second order phase transition") of the model. This is the scaling limit of the model.
