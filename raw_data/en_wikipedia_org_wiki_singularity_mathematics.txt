In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), a **singularity** is a point at which a given mathematical object is not defined, or a point where the mathematical object ceases to be [well-behaved](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Well-behaved "Well-behaved") in some particular way, such as by lacking [differentiability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative "Derivative") or [analyticity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analyticity_of_holomorphic_functions "Analyticity of holomorphic functions").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Singularity_(mathematics)#cite_note-:1-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Singularity_(mathematics)#cite_note-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Singularity_(mathematics)#cite_note-mathworld-3)

For example, the [reciprocal function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reciprocal_function "Reciprocal function")![Image 1: {\displaystyle f(x)=1/x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/049b4bda6d9e222e496f2670248d9ecfb75841d1) has a singularity at ![Image 2: {\displaystyle x=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/953917eaf52f2e1baad54c8c9e3d6f9bb3710cdc), where the value of the [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)") is not defined, as involving a [division by zero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_by_zero "Division by zero"). The [absolute value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_value "Absolute value") function ![Image 3: {\displaystyle g(x)=|x|}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3d8c28dbc1b6ded52d3af96b517fcf1d1ae5cb48) also has a singularity at ![Image 4: {\displaystyle x=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/953917eaf52f2e1baad54c8c9e3d6f9bb3710cdc), since it is not [differentiable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differentiable_function "Differentiable function") there.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Singularity_(mathematics)#cite_note-4)

The [algebraic curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_curve "Algebraic curve") defined by ![Image 5: {\displaystyle \left\{(x,y):y^{3}-x^{2}=0\right\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/077fdca2a956c842f126559b1442da42e0ebdce8) in the ![Image 6: {\displaystyle (x,y)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/41cf50e4a314ca8e2c30964baa8d26e5be7a9386) coordinate system has a singularity (called a [cusp](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cusp_(singularity) "Cusp (singularity)")) at ![Image 7: {\displaystyle (0,0)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5d630d3e781a53b0a3559ae7e5b45f9479a3141a). For singularities in [algebraic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_geometry "Algebraic geometry"), see [singular point of an algebraic variety](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Singular_point_of_an_algebraic_variety "Singular point of an algebraic variety"). For singularities in [differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry"), see [singularity theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Singularity_theory "Singularity theory").

In [real analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_analysis "Real analysis"), singularities are either [discontinuities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classification_of_discontinuities "Classification of discontinuities"), or discontinuities of the [derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative "Derivative") (sometimes also discontinuities of higher order derivatives). There are four kinds of discontinuities: **type I**, which has two subtypes, and **type II**, which can also be divided into two subtypes (though usually is not).

In real analysis, a singularity or discontinuity is a property of a function alone. Any singularities that may exist in the derivative of a function are considered as belonging to the derivative, not to the original function.

### Coordinate singularities

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Singularity_(mathematics)&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Coordinate singularities")]

A **coordinate singularity** occurs when an apparent singularity or discontinuity occurs in one coordinate frame, which can be removed by choosing a different frame. An example of this is the apparent singularity at the 90 degree latitude in [spherical coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_coordinates "Spherical coordinates"). An object moving due north (for example, along the line 0 degrees longitude) on the surface of a sphere will suddenly experience an instantaneous change in longitude at the pole (in the case of the example, jumping from longitude 0 to longitude 180 degrees). This discontinuity, however, is only apparent; it is an artifact of the coordinate system chosen, which is singular at the poles. A different coordinate system would eliminate the apparent discontinuity (e.g., by replacing the latitude/longitude representation with an [n-vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/N-vector "N-vector") representation).

In [complex analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_analysis "Complex analysis"), there are several classes of singularities. These include the isolated singularities, the nonisolated singularities, and the branch points.

### Nonisolated singularities

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Singularity_(mathematics)&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Nonisolated singularities")]

Other than isolated singularities, complex functions of one variable may exhibit other singular behaviour. These are termed nonisolated singularities, of which there are two types:

*   **Cluster points**: [limit points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limit_points "Limit points") of isolated singularities. If they are all poles, despite admitting [Laurent series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laurent_series "Laurent series") expansions on each of them, then no such expansion is possible at its limit.
*   **Natural boundaries**: any non-isolated set (e.g. a curve) on which functions cannot be [analytically continued](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytic_continuation "Analytic continuation") around (or outside them if they are closed curves in the [Riemann sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_sphere "Riemann sphere")).

[Branch points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Branch_point "Branch point") are generally the result of a [multi-valued function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multi-valued_function "Multi-valued function"), such as ![Image 35: {\displaystyle {\sqrt {z}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a681c3407545aa71422f71b09d2ec10d1ad116a3) or ![Image 36: {\displaystyle \log(z),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5e369bc6c1a24495deaf57e3784ced14dcfc1bbc) which are defined within a certain limited domain so that the function can be made single-valued within the domain. The cut is a line or curve excluded from the domain to introduce a technical separation between discontinuous values of the function. When the cut is genuinely required, the function will have distinctly different values on each side of the branch cut. The shape of the branch cut is a matter of choice, even though it must connect two different branch points (such as ![Image 37: {\displaystyle z=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b92bfc06485cc90286474b14a516a68d8bfdd7b3) and ![Image 38: {\displaystyle z=\infty }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ce32f3cbf3b98a02776b78bd6991867897112b4a) for ![Image 39: {\displaystyle \log(z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6febb7119dbf902cadafa39f3dcc3bd446ef1a50)) which are fixed in place.

Finite-time singularity
-----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Singularity_(mathematics)&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Finite-time singularity")]

[![Image 40](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Rectangular_hyperbola.svg/250px-Rectangular_hyperbola.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Rectangular_hyperbola.svg)

The [reciprocal function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reciprocal_function "Reciprocal function"), exhibiting [hyperbolic growth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_growth "Hyperbolic growth").

A **finite-time singularity** occurs when one input variable is time, and an output variable increases towards infinity at a finite time. These are important in [kinematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic "Kinematic") and [Partial Differential Equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_Differential_Equation "Partial Differential Equation") – infinites do not occur physically, but the behavior near the singularity is often of interest. Mathematically, the simplest finite-time singularities are [power laws](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_law "Power law") for various exponents of the form ![Image 41: {\displaystyle x^{-\alpha },}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/59785b2ecc7ee1f9579dd05a1c7e5413a132f306) of which the simplest is [hyperbolic growth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_growth "Hyperbolic growth"), where the exponent is (negative) 1: ![Image 42: {\displaystyle x^{-1}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a9d686441acca0e660bc3343d83f1083adfb3dc3) More precisely, in order to get a singularity at positive time as time advances (so the output grows to infinity), one instead uses ![Image 43: {\displaystyle (t_{0}-t)^{-\alpha }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/96c49aae3a820498beb24a8e04db64d6f3f73212) (using _t_ for time, reversing direction to ![Image 44: {\displaystyle -t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/198aea65d6b8a474eebd6271c9499a3d92096bdb) so that time increases to infinity, and shifting the singularity forward from 0 to a fixed time ![Image 45: {\displaystyle t_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/02d3006c4190b1939b04d9b9bb21006fb4e6fa4a)).

An example would be the bouncing motion of an inelastic ball on a plane. If idealized motion is considered, in which the same fraction of [kinetic energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_energy "Kinetic energy") is lost on each bounce, the [frequency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frequency "Frequency") of bounces becomes infinite, as the ball comes to rest in a finite time. Other examples of finite-time singularities include the various forms of the [Painlevé paradox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Painlev%C3%A9_paradox "Painlevé paradox") (for example, the tendency of a chalk to skip when dragged across a blackboard), and how the [precession](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Precession "Precession") rate of a [coin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coin "Coin") spun on a flat surface accelerates towards infinite—before abruptly stopping (as studied using the [Euler's Disk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler%27s_Disk "Euler's Disk") toy).

Hypothetical examples include [Heinz von Foerster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heinz_von_Foerster "Heinz von Foerster")'s facetious "[Doomsday's equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heinz_von_Foerster#Doomsday_equation "Heinz von Foerster")" (simplistic models yield infinite human population in finite time).

Algebraic geometry and commutative algebra
------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Singularity_(mathematics)&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Algebraic geometry and commutative algebra")]

In [algebraic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_geometry "Algebraic geometry"), a [singularity of an algebraic variety](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Singular_point_of_an_algebraic_variety "Singular point of an algebraic variety") is a point of the variety where the [tangent space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_space "Tangent space") may not be regularly defined. The simplest example of singularities are curves that cross themselves. But there are other types of singularities, like [cusps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cusp_(singularity) "Cusp (singularity)"). For example, the equation _y_ 2 − _x_ 3 = 0 defines a curve that has a cusp at the origin _x_ = _y_ = 0. One could define the _x_-axis as a tangent at this point, but this definition can not be the same as the definition at other points. In fact, in this case, the _x_-axis is a "double tangent."

For [affine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_variety "Affine variety") and [projective varieties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_variety "Projective variety"), the singularities are the points where the [Jacobian matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacobian_matrix "Jacobian matrix") has a [rank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rank_(linear_algebra) "Rank (linear algebra)") which is lower than at other points of the variety.

An equivalent definition in terms of [commutative algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_algebra "Commutative algebra") may be given, which extends to [abstract varieties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abstract_variety "Abstract variety") and [schemes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scheme_(mathematics) "Scheme (mathematics)"): A point is _singular_ if the [local ring at this point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Localization_of_a_ring "Localization of a ring") is not a [regular local ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_local_ring "Regular local ring").
